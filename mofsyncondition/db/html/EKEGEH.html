Synthesis, crystal structure and properties of novel isostructural two-dimensional lanthanide-based coordination polymers with 2,3,5,6-tetrafluoro-1,4-benzenedicarboxylic acid

https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ica.2010.09.013Get rights and content

Abstract

Five new isostructural two-dimensional lanthanide-based coordination polymers with the formula Ln2(F4BDC)3(DEF)2(EtOH)2-2(DEF) (Ln = Tb, 1; Gd, 2; Eu, 3; La, 4; Nd, 5; F4BDC2− = 2,3,5,6-tetrafluoro-1,4-benzenedicarboxylate ligands; DEF = N,N′-diethylformamide), have been synthesized by reaction of Ln(NO3)3 and F4BDC in a DEF/EtOH solvent mixture. The compounds were characterized by single-crystal and powder X-ray diffraction. In all cases, the metal ion is coordinated by nine oxygen atoms from two bidentate μ2-F4BDC2− ligands, two bridging μ2-F4BDC2− ligands, one μ3-F4BDC2− ligand, one DEF molecule and one EtOH molecule forming a tri-capped trigonal prism. The Ln(1) metal ion is linked to an adjacent Ln(1) metal ion through the oxygen atoms of two bridging μ2-F4BDC2− ligands and two μ3-F4BDC2− bridging ligands to form a Ln2O18 dimer. The Ln2O18 polyhedra building units are linked to each other through four F4BDC2− ligands to form 2D sheets that are held together by hydrogen bonding to form a 3D framework. Compounds 1–5 were further characterized using thermogravimetric analysis and infrared spectroscopy. Studies of the photoluminescence properties of compounds 1 and 3, as well as the catalytic activity of compounds 3–5 in the Biginelli reaction, are presented.

1. Introduction

In recent years, the design and construction of hybrid inorganic organic materials (HIOMs) derived from metal ions or metal clusters with organic linkers has attracted much interest due to their chemical and physical properties that make them potentially useful for applications in catalysis, magnetism, luminescence, gas storage, and drug release [1], [2], [3], [4], [5]. Among more than 15,000 HIOMs reported, most are based on transition metal ions. Only a relatively small number of lanthanide-based frameworks has been reported even though they are potentially important owing to their larger coordination number and numerous coordination environments giving rise to less predictable geometries.

Materials containing lanthanide ions are mainly used in electroluminescent devices because of their photophysical properties [6]. Unlike most organic light emitting devices, lanthanides have sharp emission bands that range over the entire visible spectrum (Eu, Tb and Tm) to the near IR region (Nd, Er, Yb) [7], [8]. In some cases, lanthanide emissions can be very weak due to the parity forbidden f–f transitions. However, aromatic ligands can sensitize the emission. In addition, suitable ligands act to shield the metal ion from its environment which prevents quenching of the excited state [9]. Lanthanide ions are hard acids, and, thus, organic ligands, which are hard bases, are required. Oxygen-donating ligands, such as carboxylates, are used for this purpose, and the versatility of their coordination modes makes them attractive linkers for bridging lanthanide centers. Lanthanide frameworks, bound to carboxylate linkers such as 1,4-benzenedicarboxylic acid and 2,2-bipyridine-4,4′-dicarboxylic acid, have been studied in terms of their photophysical properties [7], [10].

Linkers with electron withdrawing groups, such as 2,3,5,6-tetrafluoro-1,4-benzenedicarboxylylate (F4BDC), have been used to build coordination polymers with transition metals [11]. However, it has been reported that transition metal hybrids do not crystallize well in acidic environments, and the acidity of the parent acid requires a basic co-ligand such as a nitrogen base in order for the HIOM to crystallize [11a]. The acidity of the metal site is of paramount importance in catalysis, and our object was to utilize the more weakly basic F4BDC ligand as a secondary building unit without the assistance of a basic co-ligand, which might be accomplished by removing protons from the parent acid using triethylamine in a vapor diffusion process. It was hoped that acidity of the metal ion in the framework would be enhanced considerably by this. To our knowledge, only one lanthanide HIOM in which erbium metal ions are linked by F4BDC ligands has been reported; it was studied for its photophysical properties [12]. This material was prepared by a different method, had a different composition, and a different structure in which two different types of Eu atoms were present in a distorted square antiprism geometry [12].

HIOMs have been studied for their heterogeneous catalytic activity in a variety of reactions. One such reaction, the Biginelli reaction, is significant in organic chemistry and involves the cyclocondensation of an aldehyde, a dicarbonyl compound and urea in the presence of a catalytic amount of a Lewis acid to give a substituted dihydropyrimidinone. Dihydropyrimidinones and their derivatives have long been known to have important pharmacological properties as anti-virals [13] as well as other related medicinal applications [14]. The Biginelli reaction has been used to assess the catalytic properties of rare earth frameworks in a few studies [15]; however, in some of these, an acidic ligand was present [15](a), [16] which makes it unclear whether the metal ion (Lewis acid) or the proton in the acidic group was responsible for the catalysis. This manuscript contains the first reported catalytic study of a lanthanide HIOM containing a fluorinated aromatic compound without acidic species in the lattice structure.

The synthesis, crystal structure determination, infrared spectroscopy and thermal stability of five isostructural 2D lanthanide-based metal–organic frameworks with 2,3,5,6-tetrafluoro-1.4-benzenedicarboxylic acid are reported in this work. Studies of the photoluminescence properties of compounds 1 and 3, as well as the catalytic activity of compounds 3–5 in the Biginelli reaction, are also presented.

2. Experimental

2.1. Materials and methods

Eu(NO3)3·6H2O was purchased from Johnson Matthey. La(NO3)3·6H2O was purchased from J.T. Baker Chemical Co. Nd(NO3)3·5H2O, Tb(NO3)3·5H2O, Gd(NO3)3·6H2O, triethylamine, benzaldehyde and ethyl acetoacetate were obtained from Aldrich Chemical Co. 2,3,5,6-Tetrafluoro-1,4-benzenedicarboxylic acid was obtained from Matrix Scientific Co. All chemicals were used as received. Urea and N,N-diethylformamide was obtained from Acros Chemical Co., while ethanol was purchased from Aaper Alcohol and Chemical Co. Toluene and methanol were purchased from Fisher Chemical Co. All solvents were used as received without further purification.

2.2. Physical measurements

Crystals suitable for single crystal X-ray diffraction were microscopically examined and selected from solution held on a slide cooled with dry ice to minimize loss of solvent. Intensity data for all compounds were measured on a Bruker SMART APEX CCD area detector system at 193 K using Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.7107 Å). Absorption effects were corrected using the sadabs program [17]. All structures were determined and refined using the Bruker shelxtl software package (Version 6.1) [18]. Crystal and structure refinement data for compounds 1–5 as well as selected bond lengths and angles are reported in Table 1, Table 2. X-ray powder diffraction data were collected at room temperature using a BRUKER P4 general-purpose four-circle X-ray diffractometer modified with a GADDS/Hi-Star detector with Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5406 Å). The goniometer was controlled using gadds software suite [19]. The samples were mounted on tape and two frames were measured at 2θ = 5 and 26° with exposure time of 180 s/frame. Infrared spectra were recorded either on a Mattson Genesis II FT-IR spectrometer or on a Perkin–Elmer Spectrum 100 spectrophotometer with an ATR sampling accessory equipped with a diamond anvil. Thermogravimetric analysis of compounds 1–5 was performed by heating 10–30 mg samples at a rate of 5 °C/min from 40 to 900 °C under air (40 mL/min) using a Perkin–Elmer Pyris 1 TGA system. Elemental analyses were carried out by Atlantic Microlabs, Inc. Solid state photoluminescence spectra were recorded at room temperature on a Perkin–Elmer LS50B luminescence spectrophotometer using a solid state attachment. GC–MS data were recorded on an Agilent 6850 Series GC system coupled to an Agilent 5973 Mass Selective Detector run in electron impact mode. A 50-split method was chosen to run on an Agilent column (13 × 0.25 mm × 0.25 μm) at a ramp rate of 13 °C/min from 50 °C to 280 °C.

2.3. Synthesis of Tb2(F4BDC)3(DEF)2(EtOH)2-2(DEF) (1)

Tb(NO3)3·5H2O (54.2 mg, 0.125 mmol) and H2F4BDC acid (41.8 mg, 0.176 mmol) were added to a 10 mL vial containing DEF (6 mL) and ethanol (1 mL). The vial was covered with parafilm which was pierced several times with a needle and set inside a beaker containing ethanol (5 mL) and triethylamine (0.2 mL). The beaker was then covered with parafilm and the mixture of ethanol and triethylamine vapor was allowed to diffuse into the solution for a period of 48 h. A white crystalline product formed which was washed with DEF and acetone and allowed to dry in air. The purity of the product was checked by PXRD, which can be seen in the Supporting information Fig. S1. (Yield: 44.7 mg, 52%, based on F4BDC). Anal. 2.8.1. Synthesis of 5-(ethoxycarbonyl)-6-methyl-4-phenyl-3,4-dihydropyrimidin-2(1H)-one (6a)

2.8.1.1. Heterogeneous catalysis

Benzaldehyde (30 μL, 0.29 mmol), ethyl acetoacetate (35 μL, 0.28 mmol), urea (30.0 mg, 0.49 mmol), 3 (21.8 mg, 0.014 mmol) and toluene (0.2 mL) were added to a 10 mL round-bottom flask equipped with a reflux condenser. The mixture was held at 80 °C for 1.5 h, then cooled to RT and diluted with ethyl acetate. The catalyst was filtered off and the ethyl acetate solution was washed with water (2 × 15 mL). The organic phase was dried over MgSO4, filtered, and evaporated to give the crude product. The product was purified by filtration of an ethyl acetate/petroleum ether solution to give 6a. Yield: 40.5 mg, 50%. 3. Results and discussion

3.1. Synthesis

The synthesis of compounds 1–5 was carried out by a vapor diffusion method which resulted in precipitation of high purity samples as confirmed by elemental analyses and powder X-ray diffraction. Initially, a solvothermal method was employed at varying temperatures (100, 120 and 145 °C) from 18–48 h in an array of different solvent systems without adjustment of pH, but no crystals suitable for single-crystal X-ray diffraction were obtained. Stoichiometric ratios of 1:1 lanthanide metal to H2F4BDC were utilized for all syntheses, and, in all cases, the same framework resulted, as indicated by single crystal and powder X-ray diffraction. DEF was the preferred solvent since use of DMF resulted in microcrystalline products. Ethanol was chosen as a co-solvent because enhanced crystal growth resulted compared to use of DEF/H2O, DEF/MeOH, and DEF/n-propanol mixtures. Powder diffraction patterns of the synthesized compounds compared to the simulated powder pattern are reported in the supporting information (Fig. S1). Since all of the compounds studied are isostructural, only the structure of 1 is described in detail (Ln2(F4BDC)3(DEF)2(EtOH)2-2(DEF); Ln = Tb, 1; Gd, 2; Eu, 3; La, 4; and Nd, 5). The bound solvent cannot be removed at RT, but the compounds tend to lose lattice DEF upon exposure to air and under vacuum. This is reflected in the analytical results which show varying amounts of remaining DEF in the lattice. One sample (Eu) was heated to 150 °C to remove any lattice and bound DEF, cooled to room temperature and then pumped at high vacuum overnight. IR analysis (Supporting information Fig. S4) showed essentially no remaining DEF in the compound. Studies of other MOFs have shown complete removal of DEF under vacuum at temperatures above 140 °C [20].

3.2. Structure of Tb2(F4BDC)3(DEF)2EtOH2-2(DEF) (1)

Compound 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/c with Z = 4. The framework has an overall two-dimensional structure. The asymmetric unit contains one terbium TbIII ion, one and a half F4BDC2− ligands, one DEF molecule, one EtOH molecule, and one lattice DEF molecule (Fig. 1a). Each terbium ion is 9-coordinated by the oxygen atoms of two bridging F4BDC2− ligands (O(1) and O(2)), two bidentate F4BDC2− ligands (O(3), O(4)), one μ3-F4BDC2− ligand (O(5), O(6)), one oxygen (O(7)) from a DEF molecule and one oxygen (O(8)) from an EtOH molecule to form a TbO9 polyhedron. The lattice DEF oxygen (O(9)) is hydrogen-bonded to the hydrogen on the sp2 carbon of the EtOH molecule (O(8)–H(O8)⋯O(9) 2.699(4) Å). The TbO9 polyhedron can be described as a tri-capped trigonal prism. The TbO9 polyhedron is linked to its adjacent terbium atom (Tb1–Tb1 distance is 4.1450(4) Å) through oxygen atoms from two bridging F4BDC2− ligands (O(1), O(2)), and two μ3-F4BDC2− ligands (O(5), O(6)) to form Tb2O16 dimers, due to sharing of the O(6) atom between TbIII ions (Fig. 1b and c). The Tb–O (from F4BDC2−) bond lengths of the dimer range from 2.319(2) Å to 2.478(2) Å, which corresponds well to known terbium–oxygen bond distances [21]. The two TbIII ions are related by a two-fold rotation along the b-axis. The dimers result from slightly offset face to face pi interactions between the benzene rings within the 1D chain, which have a distance of 3.981 Å.

The dimers are connected to each other by the oxygen atoms of four F4BDC2− linking units O(3), O(4), O(5) and O(6) to form a 1D chain along the c-axis (Fig. 2a). The 1D chains are connected through the oxygen atoms of two F4BDC2− ligands (O(1) and O(2)). This connection forms an infinite 2D sheet along the bc-plane with cavities where the hydrogen bonded lattice DEF molecules lie (Fig. 2b). The distances between dimers along the b- and c-directions are 11.212 and 10.212 Å, respectively [22]. The layers stack along the crystallographic a-axis and are held together by EtOH and DEF solvent molecules located between the sheets (Fig. 3a and b). The separation between planes along the a-axis is 11.045 Å.

The powder X-ray diffraction data comparing the simulated pattern to the as-synthesized pattern for this compound can be seen in Fig. 4b.

3.3. Thermal stability study

The thermal stability of compounds 1–5 was investigated using thermogravimetric analysis by heating the samples in air to 900 °C at a rate of 5 °C/min. In the case of compound 1 (Fig. 4a), there was a loss of two coordinated EtOH molecules between 40 and 115 °C, followed by loss of two lattice DEF molecules from 115 to 200 °C (calc. 19.3% exp. 19.2%). A final weight loss of two coordinated DEF molecules occurred between 250 and 325 °C (calc. 13.2% exp. 13.0%). The compound is stable to 250 °C and begins to decompose at 325 °C to give terbium oxyfluoride (TbOF) at 900 °C as determined by PXRD [23]. Similar solvent losses were observed for compounds 2–5 and can be seen in the Supporting information Fig. S2 and Table S1.

3.4. Infrared spectroscopy

Infrared spectra of compounds 1–5 were measured between 400 and 4000 cm−1 (Fig. S3). The IR spectrum of 1 shows a broad band at 3450 cm−1 which corresponds to the O–H stretch of the ethanol. The weak vibrations at 2989 cm−1 and 2950 cm−1 correspond to the DEF C–H methyl and DEF C–H aldehyde stretching modes. The stretching frequency for the carbonyl group on the benzene ring, normally in the range of 1680–1720 cm−1, was lowered after coordination to the lanthanide metal ion. The asymmetric and symmetric stretching modes for the bound carboxylate groups occur at 1623 cm−1 and 1411 cm−1. The DEF CO stretch can be seen at 1660 cm−1.

3.5. Solid state photoluminescence

The room temperature solid state photoluminescence spectra for 1 and 3 were taken and can be seen in Fig. 5. Upon excitation, 1 emits a green luminescence (Fig. 5a). The transitions appear at 489, 544, 585 and 620 nm and are attributed to the 5D4–7Fj (J = 6, 5, 4, 3) bands [7], [24]. The 5D4–7F5 transition (hypersensitive) is the most intense peak and the 5D4–7F6 transition is second in intensity consistent with other Tb-based systems [3a]. Compound 3 emits a red luminescence. The emission peaks which appear at 590, 615, 650 and 695 nm are attributed to the 5D0–7Fj, (J = 1, 2, 3, 4) transitions respectively, with the strongest emission attributed to the 5D0–7F2 transition at 615 nm (Fig. 5d). The appearance of the broad, low intensity, forbidden 5D0–7F0 transition indicates that the metal is in a non-centrosymmetric ligand field. The 5D0–7F2 electric-dipole transition is sensitive to the coordination environment around the europium ion and is almost twice the intensity of the 5D0–7F1 transition (a magnetic dipole transition relatively insensitive to the coordination environment) which again indicates that the europium ion does not sit on an inversion center. This information was very helpful in the crystal structure analysis [25].

3.6. Catalytic properties

Lanthanide triflate salts are the most widely used homogeneous Lewis acid catalyst giving moderate yields in a number of solvents, under a range of conditions, and using a variety of different starting reagents [26]. Lanthanide triflates, however, are difficult to recover because excess solvent must be removed and the triflates must be dried under vacuum for several hours in order to reuse them. The Biginelli reaction has been used to assess the Lewis acid catalytic properties of HIOMs by other groups [15], [16]. However; their syntheses were carried out at room temperature, some contained potentially catalytic acid groups, and most required 2–3 days for moderate yields. In light of this fact, we explored the catalytic activity of the compounds reported here under a shorter time period (1.5 h) and a longer period (3 days) under different conditions of temperature. Compounds 3–5 all gave similar yields so we chose to focus on compound 3 for this study (Scheme 1).

The catalytic activity of 3 and its activated catalyst 3a, from which solvent had been removed, were compared and the results are presented in Table 3. Compound 3 is interesting in that it is insoluble in everything except water, so its homogeneous catalytic activity in water as well as its heterogeneous activity in toluene and methanol was investigated. The europium catalyst gave greater than 50% of the yield observed for Yb(OTf)3 but at a lower temperature (70°) in about 30% of the time. In the case of heterogeneous reactions, the catalyst was easily recovered and re-used without loss of activity (Supporting information, Figs. S5 and S6). At room temperature over three days, reactions in water give higher yields of dihydropyrimidinone, comparable to those of Yb(OTf)3, as would be expected for homogeneous catalysis. To determine whether catalysis was occurring in the layers or on the surface of the framework, we compared the catalytic activity of the activated catalyst 3a to 3. The activated catalyst gave somewhat higher yields at 70° than did the unactivated catalyst, but the difference may be due to better access at the surface rather than access to voids in the framework. There was no significant difference at room temperature. Aldehyde reactants of different sizes (3-thio-phenecarboxaldehyde, 5.7 Å; benzaldehyde 6.1 Å; 1-napthylcarboxaldehyde, 7.9 Å) [27] were chosen to see if catalytic activity decreased as the size of the reactant increased. The distance between the layers in 3 is 7.4 Å (based on the Eu(1)–Eu(1) distance). The yields of the corresponding dihydropyrimidinones at 70° were similar for the different aldehydes (6b, 13%; 6c, 12%) indicating a constant activity, regardless of size. These results suggest that catalysis occurs at the surface and not between the layers. Finally, the catalyst was finely ground to increase surface area. If catalysis were taking place on the surface, as the surface area increased, the yield of the Biginelli product should also have increased. A modest increase in Biginelli product was observed (7–10%) consistent with the conclusion that catalysis occurs on the surface. Compounds 4 and 5 gave yields comparable to those obtained for 3 at 70 °C for 1.5 h.

4. Conclusions

Five new 2D lanthanide-based coordination polymers incorporating 2,3,5,6-tetrafluoro-1,4-benzenedicarboxylate have been synthesized using a vapor diffusion method and characterized by single crystal X-ray diffraction, powder diffraction, infrared spectroscopy and thermogravimetric analysis. The frameworks consist of lanthanide metal ions linked by [F4BDC]2− ligands to form 2D sheets, which stack to form a layered topology with solvent molecules between the sheets. DEF and EtOH can be removed from the layers with full retention of the framework upon heating to 150 °C. Compounds 1 and 3 showed photoluminescent properties upon excitation at 392 nm and 350 nm, respectively. Compounds 3–5 showed catalytic activity in the Biginelli reaction, which provided a more rapid synthesis compared to previous methods [28]. Evidence suggests that the catalysis is taking place at the surface of the framework and not within the layers of the framework.
