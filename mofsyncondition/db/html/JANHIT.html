Introduction

Luminescent1 coordination polymers (CPs) and metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have experienced growing interest in the past decades due to their range of potential applications including sensing,2 photocatalysis,3 or applications in organic light emitting displays.4 Dealing with sensing applications, luminescent sensors offer significant advantages over other sensing devices. Indeed, the output signal is a fast and easily detected colorimetric response to an external stimulus with relatively high sensitivity. Also, when the analyte and the chromophore interact together, emission wavelength shifts may be detected and are characteristic of the sensed material. To achieve a functional sensor, some prerequisites should be fulfilled: (i) capability of trapping/adsorbing analyte molecules to yield a local concentration and improve sensitivity possibly by host–guest interactions; (ii) physical and chemical stability; (iii) potential insensitivity towards quenching. Coordination polymers are therefore well-suited for such applications. Indeed, CPs are well-defined crystalline structures constructed by self-assembly processes through interactions between consecutive coordinating organic entities, referred to also as tectons,5 and metal ions, complexes or clusters acting as metallic nodes. The resulting periodic extended architectures may be of different dimensionalities (1D, 2D, and 3D) and may be porous. They are then referred to as porous coordination polymers (PCPs) or metal–organic frameworks (MOFs).6 Different strategies have been followed in order to generate luminescent coordination polymers including the use of luminescent (metallo)organic bridging linkers and/or of lanthanide ions. The benefits of luminescent CPs for chemical sensing lie on their well-defined and readily tailorable structures as well as on their adjustable functionality by proper choice of the (metallo)organic entities. Modulation of the emission properties in addition to the ease of functionalisation of the (metallo)ligands, or (metalla)tectons,7 without tedious synthetic procedures make them candidates of choice for designing sensors. However, tuning the emission characteristics of CPs to be adaptable to the analyte molecules implies that the distinct CPs studied are isostructural upon small changes on the (metalla)tectons or metal ions. Therefore, a good understanding of the overall structure of the CPs is beneficial. Finally, multiple cycles and easy reactivation processes may be foreseen with such crystalline materials.

We have developed a family of metallatectons based on tris-chelate luminescent Ir(iii) complexes able to self-assemble upon coordination with distinct metal ions to yield luminescent heterometallic coordination polymers.8 The choice of the cyclometallated Ir(iii) complex as the metalloligand was based on its rich photophysical properties.9 In the present study, cationic [Ir(Xppy)(L1)]+ metallatectons were designed and studied (Scheme 1). Those Ir complexes are equipped with two cyclometalating units (Xppy) and a bipyridyl chelate L1 (bpy) possessing two peripheral coordinating sites able to bind metal cations. In previous works, using 4-pyridyl terminal units, 1D and 2D heterometallic coordination polymers were obtained and studied in the solid state.8 In this work, in view of exploring the chemical diversity of the architectures formed, and in order to vary the angle of coordination, the peripheral coordinating units are 3-pyridyl derivatives. Those units are connected to the central 2,2′-bipyridine backbone at positions 5 and 5′. As cyclometalating units, either 2-phenylpyridine (ppyH), 2-(4-fluorophenyl)pyridine (FppyH) or 2-(2,4-difluorophenyl)pyridine (dFppyH) were used. Indeed, by adding fluorine atoms on the phenyl units, a hypsochromic shift of the emission wavelength is observed.10 Thus, modulation of the emission wavelength of the final coordination polymers is expected.

Herein, we report the synthesis and characterisation of three new luminescent cyclometalated Ir(iii) cationic discrete complexes and their combination with cadmium halide salts leading to the formation of luminescent isostructural 1D coordination polymers.

Results and discussion

Synthesis and crystal structures of the discrete Ir complexes

The three discrete iridium complexes [Ir(Xppy)2(L1)][PF6] (Xppy = ppy, Fppy or dFppy) were obtained in three steps starting from iridium(iii) chloride and the corresponding phenylpyridine ligands XppyH (Scheme 1). Thus, the three chloro-bridged dimers [Ir(ppy)2(μ-Cl)]2,11 [Ir(Fppy)2(μ-Cl)]2 and [Ir(dFppy)2(μ-Cl)]212 were obtained by the reaction of IrCl3·xH2O with the corresponding XppyH ligands in 71% to 76% yield. It should be noted that, while 2-phenylpyridine (ppyH) and 2-(2,4-difluorophenyl)pyridine (dFppyH) were commercially available, 2-(4-fluorophenyl)pyridine (FppyH) was prepared in 97% yield by a Suzuki–Miyaura coupling reaction starting from 4-fluorophenylboronic acid and 2-bromopyridine adapting the previously published procedure.13 Reaction of the respective Ir dimers with 4,4′-dibromo-2,2′-bipyridine L214 afforded the three dibromo-functionalised Ir complexes [Ir(Xppy)2(L2)][PF6] in ca. 60 to 90% yield, adapting the procedure reported for the synthesis of [Ir(ppy)2(bpy)][PF6] (bpy = 2,2′-bipyridine).15 In addition to characterisation in solution by NMR and UV/Vis techniques, the crystal structure of [Ir(Fppy)2(L2)][PF6] (Fig. 1a) could be determined (see Table S1 in the ESI† for crystallographic data). Single crystals suitable for X-ray diffraction were grown by vapour diffusion of Et2O into an acetonitrile solution containing the mono-fluorinated complex. The crystal (monoclinic, space group P21/c) contains the cationic Ir complex, one PF6− anion and one CH3CN solvent molecule and is of the general formula [Ir(Fppy)2(L2)][PF6]·CH3CN. Its geometrical parameters are akin to the ones exhibited by similar cationic bis-cyclometalated Ir complexes containing two Fppy ligands and one bpy ligand16 or two dFppy ligands and one 5,5′-dibromo bpy ligand8b (see the ESI, Table S2† for Ir–N and Ir–C bond lengths and NL2–Ir–NL2, Nppy–Ir–Cppy, and Nppy–Ir–Nppy angles). The Ir atom is surrounded by two Fppy units and one L2 bidentate ligand and adopts a slightly distorted octahedral geometry. The dihedral angles of the two aromatic rings of all bidentate ligands are close to 0° and the three ligands are almost planar. No specific intermolecular interactions are observed.

Finally, the three discrete complexes were synthesised in 77% to 95% yield via a Pd-catalysed Suzuki–Miyaura coupling reaction between 3-pyridylboronic acid and the respective dibromo-substituted Ir complexes. Indeed, the use of cationic tris-bidentate10b,17 or bis-tridentate18 cyclometalated Ir(iii) complexes as substrates for Pd-catalyzed Suzuki coupling reactions has already proven to be an efficient synthetic approach. It is important to note that another synthetic pathway has also been explored in parallel starting from the synthesis of L1 followed by its coordination to the Ir dimer. However, this pathway afforded poorer yields and is less versatile. Thus, depending on the Xppy ligands used, by-products may form during the last synthetic step. Those impurities were revealed to be difficult to remove. Similar observations were already reported in the case of closely related compounds.8b All iridium complexes were characterised by 1H and 13C NMR spectroscopy, mass spectrometry and UV/Vis absorption spectroscopy. 19F NMR was also performed to confirm the presence of fluorine atoms on the fluorinated phenylpyridine parts, Fppy and dFppy, and ascertain the purity of the complexes. Furthermore, all three complexes [Ir(Xppy)2(L1)][PF6] could be characterised in the crystalline phase using X-ray diffraction on single crystals obtained, in all three cases, upon vapour diffusion of diethylether into an acetonitrile solution containing the desired complex (Fig. 1b–d) (see Table S1 in the ESI† for crystallographic data).

Despite similar crystallisation conditions, the three crystal structures display slight differences (see selected crystallographic data, Table 1). For all three complexes, the structural parameters are similar to those observed for the structure of [Ir(Fppy)2(L2)][PF6]·CH3CN and similar cationic iridium complexes bearing 5,5′-mono-20 or di-substituted21 bipyridine ligands and phenylpyridine derivatives (see Table S3 in the ESI† for Ir–N and Ir–C bond lengths and NL1–Ir–NL1, Nppy–Ir–Cppy, Nppy–Ir–Nppy angles). The Ir center adopts a distorted octahedral geometry and is surrounded by ligand L1 and two Xppy moieties that display the expected trans arrangement of the N donor atoms and cis arrangement of the C atoms.9a,22 The two aromatic rings of the cyclometalated ligand and the central bipyridine moiety respectively are almost coplanar (see dihedral angles in the ESI, Table S3†). Furthermore, the two terminal 3-pyridyl units are slightly tilted with respect to the central bipyridine core with an average angle between the planes defined by the 3-pyridyl unit and the central bipyridine moiety of ca. 21.3° (see Table S3 in the ESI,† “3py-bpy” angle). Ligand L1 is slightly bent towards the Ir center (“curvature angle” of ca. 172°, see Table S3 in the ESI†). It should be noted that, for [Ir(dFppy)2(L1)][PF6]·0.5C4H10O, a 3-pyridyl unit of one crystallographically non-equivalent Ir complex displays a “3py-bpy” angle of only ca. 2.5° with respect to the bipyridine moiety. This could be rather attributed to the crystal packing than to a propensity of the ligand L1 itself to be planar.

Because of the free rotation around the C–C bonds connecting the bpy core with the 3-pyridyl terminal coordination sites, those three cationic metallatectons may adopt two extreme conformations named hereafter as U- or S-shaped conformations, that may be of interest for the formation of coordination polymers. Indeed, if the two nitrogen atoms of the terminal 3-pyridyl coordinating sites are in syn orientation relative to the bpy backbone, those metallatectons may be regarded as distorted U-shaped building units (Fig. 2a). This conformation is observed in the case of [Ir(ppy)2(L1)][PF6]·0.7C4H10O (squeezed). On the other hand, when the two nitrogen atoms are divergently oriented as exemplified by the crystal structure of [Ir(Fppy)2(L1)][PF6] (Fig. 2b), the metalloligand may be considered as a distorted S-shaped tecton. Obviously, the three metallatectons may adopt both conformations in solution and both conformations are accessible for the foreseen formation of coordination polymers.

Finally, the three complexes display π–π interactions between bpy units of distinct complexes with the shortest C–C distances ranging from 3.41 to 3.68 Å depending on the complex considered (see Fig. S2 in the ESI†). In addition, short H–F distances are observed implying weak CH⋯F interactions‡ within the crystal.23 Thus, for all three complexes, interesting non-classical weak CH⋯F interactions are observed for F atoms located on the PF6 anion and H atoms belonging either to L1 or to the Xppy ligands (see Table 2 for H–F distance and C–H⋯·F angle ranges, Fig. 3 and Fig. S3–S5†). Such non-classical hydrogen bonds to the hexafluorophosphate anion have already been reported for a tris-chelate Ru complex.24 Furthermore, in the case of [Ir(Fppy)2(L1)][PF6], a second type of CH⋯F interactions appears within the crystal due to the presence of fluorine atoms on the Xppy ligand. Accordingly, two Fppy ligands belonging to two adjacent Ir complexes exhibit rather short HPy–FPh distances (see Fig. 3 and Table 2). Such an interaction between Xppy ligands is not observed for the difluorinated analogue [Ir(dFppy)2(L1)][PF6]·0.5C4H10O.

Synthesis and crystal structures of the Cd-based coordination polymers

The three metallatectons were further reacted with CdX2 salts (X = Cl, Br, I) to generate (Ir,Cd)-based heterometallic CPs. Cd(ii) was chosen as the metallic node because of its adaptability and versatility. Indeed, when combined with metallatectons possessing divergently oriented pyridyl coordinating sites, the Cd(ii) cation may adopt distinct coordination geometries yielding monometallic Cd species, acting as square-planar,25 T-shaped,26 V-shaped27 or linear8b,24 connecting nodes and leading to the formation of 1D,28 2D28b,29,25,30 or 3D architectures.31 In addition, examples of heterometallic coordination polymers have been reported where the CdX2 salts rearrange into polynuclear species. Chloride-bridged Cd oligomers of formula [Cd2(μ-Cl)2Cl2]n ( n ≥ 1) may then behave as a finite square planar node (see for example ref. 29b) or infinite 1D inorganic pillars (see for example ref. 28 and 32). The formation of mono- or polynuclear nodes may be favoured depending on the solvent used28a or on the halide considered.33 Thus, predicting the nuclearity of the CdX2 node still remains difficult28b and its chemistry is rather versatile and adaptable to the environment when generating CPs. Most of the examples cited above deal with 4-pyridyl coordinating units. An even greater versatility is expected when using 3-pyridyl units. Hereafter, Cd ions rearrange into an unusual halide-bridged 4-connecting Cd trinuclear finite node. Few examples of coordination polymers based on trinuclear Cd complexes and pyridyl-substituted ligands have been described so far.34 Focus has generally been on the different reactivities between Cd(ii) ions compared to other d10 metal ions or on the influence of polycarboxylates on the final architectures in the presence of Cd(ii) and the pyridyl-based ligand. In the present work, a systematic study was done proving the formation of the more favourable anionic trinuclear Cd(ii) node compared to monometallic or infinite inorganic pillars under the present reaction conditions. Thus, three CdX2 halide salts (X = Cl, Br, I) were employed using the three Ir-based metallatectons described above ending in nine combinations, assuming the combination of the tectons and Cd salts results in the same architecture. The introduction of an increasing number of fluorine atoms on the metallatectons not only permits tuning of the metallatecton emission wavelength, as mentioned before, but also to consider its possible impact on the formation of the final crystalline materials. Variation of the halide atoms enabled us to study the potential influence of the halide counter ion (X = Cl, Br, I) of the Cd salt over the final extended architectures. For the three metallatectons [Ir(Xppy)2(L1)][PF6], a solution of the corresponding CdX2 salt (X = Cl, Br and I) in EtOH was slowly diffused through a 1/1 CHCl3/EtOH layer deposited onto a CHCl3 solution containing the desired Ir metallatecton. It was possible to obtain single crystals suitable for X-ray diffraction in seven cases out of the nine possible combinations. Only the combination of [Ir(ppy)2(L1)]+ with either CdCl2 or CdI2 salts failed in forming crystals of sufficient quality. For the other combinations of metallatectons and Cd salts, crystal structure determination revealed the formation of isostructural 1D coordination polymers, named CP1X, CP2X and CP3X§ containing the [Ir(ppy)2(L1)]+, [Ir(Fppy)2(L1)]+ or [Ir(dFppy)2(L1)]+ metallatectons respectively and the corresponding Cd2+ and X− ions (Fig. 4 and Fig. S6 to S9 in the ESI,† for crystallographic data, see Table S4†). It is worth noting that the Cd salt rearranges during the self-assembly process into a trinuclear species of formula [Cd3X8]2− that will be described afterwards. All CPs crystallised in the P triclinic space group. Besides the iridium complex, Cd2+ cations and X− anions, all crystals contain chloroform solvent molecules within the lattice. However, the number of CHCl3 molecules per unit cell varies as a consequence of the site occupancy of the Xppy ligands in the coordination polymers as will be detailed afterwards.¶ No other solvent molecules are present. Furthermore, no hexafluorophosphate ions are present as a result of the anion exchange between the PF6 and halide anions during the self-assembly process.

Again, all seven coordination polymers are isostructural. As specified above, a trinuclear dianionic Cd node [Cd3X8]2− formed upon self-assembly with the Ir metallatecton (Fig. 4d–f). All CPs consist of 1D coordination polymers with a 1 : 1 ratio of metallatecton to trimetallic node.

The iridium centre adopts a distorted octahedral coordination geometry with bond lengths and angles similar to the one observed for the discrete metallatectons (see the ESI, Table S5†). The conformation adopted by the metallatecton is of a distorted U-shaped type (see Fig. 4) with the two terminal coordinating sites in syn orientation. As for the discrete complexes, the two 3-pyridyl units are slightly bent with respect to the central bipyridyl moiety with similar curvature angles of ca. 170°. However, the tilt angle displayed between the two 3-pyridyl moieties and the central bpy is more pronounced in the case of the coordination polymers ranging from ca. 34° to 48°, probably as a consequence of the Cd coordination (see “3py-bpy angle”, Table S5 in the ESI†). Interestingly, the atoms belonging to the two Xppy units are disordered over two positions as illustrated by the site occupancy values (see the ESI, Table S6†). Thus, along one coordination polymer, the two Λ and Δ enantiomers of one metallatecton are distributed randomly. Hence, the coordination polymer may be described as an atactic polymer resulting in an achiral polymer. Because of this disorder, the solvent molecules are also distributed in a random manner, explaining why the number of solvent molecules varies per unit cell for distinct CPs.

Dealing with the Cd metallic node [Cd3X8]2−, two types of Cd atoms may be distinguished (Fig. 4d–f). Hence, the central Cd atom, Cdint, adopts a distorted octahedral geometry with four distinct bridging halide atoms occupying the equatorial positions and two nitrogen atoms belonging to two distinct Ir metallatectons at axial positions (see the ESI for bond lengths and angles, Table S5†). The geometry of the two other Cd atoms, Cdext, is a distorted trigonal bipyramid. Two terminal halide ligands and one μ-bridging halide occupy the equatorial positions of the bipyramid while another μ-bridging halide and a nitrogen atom belonging to an Ir complex are at axial positions (see the ESI for bond lengths and angles, Table S5†). Thus, the trinuclear Cd node is connected to four distinct Ir metallatectons with each external Cd atom Cdext linked to one metallatecton and the central Cd atom Cdint connected to two distinct metallatectons. It behaves thus as a four-distorted square planar connecting node. The distance between two consecutive trinuclear nodes is ca. 20.5 Å (Cdint–Cdint distance). It is worth noting that in the case of CdCl2 salts as precursors, a trinuclear anion of formula [Cd3Cl8]2− is always obtained. However, when using CdBr2 or CdI2 as metallic salts, because of the use of chloroform as a solvent for the self-assembly process, some bromide or iodide anions may be exchanged with chlorine atoms in the trinuclear node. In the case of CP1Br, CP2Br and CP3Br, in order to fully assign electron density, a mixture of Br and Cl atoms may be modelled at two bridging and two ending X positions of the Cd trinuclear node [Cd3X8]2−. This Cl/Br disorder was modelled for CP1Br and CP2Br (see Fig. S6 in the ESI† and Fig. 4 respectively). For both CP2I and CP3I, the X-ray study also revealed formation of a mixed halide anionic node [Cd3Cl(2+m)I(6−m)]2− (m = 0 to 4) where two out of the four bridging halides of the node are Cl− ions and a mixture of Cl/I atoms is found on the two other bridging positions and two ending ones (Fig. 4f and S9†). Along the c axis, coordination polymers are aligned in a parallel displaced manner forming a 2D plane (Fig. 5 and Fig. S10, S11 in the ESI†). The distance between parallel coordination polymers, dCP–CP, is ca. 17.6 Å (see Table S5 in the ESI†). Two consecutive planes are stacked along the a axis with no notable intermolecular interactions and an interplanar distance, dplanes, of ca. 9.7 Å (Fig. 5 and Fig. S10, S11 in the ESI†).

In order to verify the purity of the microcrystalline powders, PXRD (powder X-ray diffraction) was performed on the bromide derivatives, CP1Br, CP2Br and CP3Br (Fig. 6). This revealed a good match between patterns simulated from the single crystal and experimental data. This also highlighted the relatively good air stability of the coordination polymers enabling the photophysical study of those materials as exposed below.

Photophysical characterisation of the metallatectons and the coordination polymers

Firstly, the photophysical properties of the discrete Ir(iii) complexes were studied in the solid state (Table 3 and Fig. 7). A strong absorption band between 300 and ca. 500–600 nm is observed for the three metallatectons respectively. Those bands may be attributed to a mixture of MLCT (metal-to-ligand charge transfer), LC (ligand-centered) and LLCT (ligand-to-ligand charge transfer) transitions. Interestingly, and as expected, an hypsochromic shift of the absorption bands of ca. 40 nm is seen upon the consecutive introduction of fluorine atoms on the ppy scaffold. The emission properties were then investigated in the solid state.|| Depending on the number of F atoms on the ppy moieties, distinct behaviour may be observed in the emission spectra. Indeed, the lowest-lying triplet state may consist of contributions from charge transfer (MLCT) or ligand-centered (LC) triplet states. Preliminary DFT calculations show that, depending on the Xppy ligand used, the CT and LC states are energetically close-lying: emission can thus arise from either or both of them. Thus, for the three discrete complexes, distinct states may contribute to the emission spectra. In the case of [Ir(dFppy)2(L1)][PF6], a mixture of a LC transition centered on the entire bpy unit and a pseudo-MLCT transition is presumably responsible for the transition. According to previous reports on similar complexes and to preliminary DFT calculations, the pseudo-MLCT transition implies a HOMO orbital, centered both on the metal and the phenyl unit belonging to the Xppy moieties, and a LUMO orbital expanded over the whole bpy unit. Contribution of the LC state is supported by the emission spectrum of the difluorinated complex in solution which shows a structured band with two emission maxima at 540 and 573 nm related to vibrational relaxation. Indeed, the energy difference between these two maxima is of 975 cm−1 and corresponds to the vibration range of CC bonds. On the other hand, the two other discrete complexes exhibit broad and unstructured emission bands in the solid state and in solution, with maxima at 595 and 639 nm for complexes [Ir(Fppy)2(L1)][PF6] and [Ir(ppy)2(L1)][PF6] respectively, indicating a higher charge transfer character of the emissive state. Because of the difference in the emission band profile, compared to the dFppy complex described above, the transition is attributed mainly to a MLCT transition, as confirmed by the DFT calculations. As observed in the reflectance spectra, the successive introduction of F atoms on the ppy moieties induces a hypsochromic shift in emission as well. Because of a greater energy difference, the LC state does not compete with the MLCT state. The absolute photoluminescence quantum yields (PLQY) of the three complexes were measured in the solid state (powder samples) using an integrating sphere under air-equilibrated conditions at RT (Table 3). They were found to be around 10% for the non-fluorinated and difluorinated complexes, while a lower PLQY of ca. 2% was measured for [Ir(Fppy)2(L1)][PF6]. Excited-state lifetimes were also measured (powder samples): biexponential fits were used for τ of all three complexes (Table 3). Such behaviour has already been described for similar discrete cationic Ir(iii) complexes (see for example ref. 35). [Ir(Fppy)2(L1)][PF6] exhibits a shorter lifetime and a lower QY compared to the other complexes. Luminescence quenching in the solid state may be explained by intermolecular interactions. Moreover, as evidenced by the X-ray structure of [Ir(Fppy)2(L1)][PF6] (Table 2 and Fig. 3), CH⋯F interactions between two adjacent cyclometalating Fppy ligands of two distinct Ir complexes are observed. Those interactions are absent for the difluorinated analogue and obviously for the ppy derivative. A more detailed photophysical investigation accompanied by DFT calculations is currently underway to ascertain the nature of the excited states and will be the subject of another article.

In order to investigate the impact of the Cd coordination to the pyridyl moieties on the photophysical properties of the metallatecton, those properties were studied for the three brominated coordination polymers, i.e.CP1Br, CP2Br, CP3Br, in the solid state (Table 3 and Fig. 8).** A broad absorption band ranging from 300 to ca. 500–550 nm was observed for the three CPs. As for the discrete metallatectons, a bathochromic shift is observed in the reflectance spectra going from CP3Br to CP1Br, i.e. when decreasing the number of fluorine atoms on the Xppy units. The same behaviour is evidenced in the emission spectra. Compared to the respective discrete metallatectons, a broadening of the emission peaks is observed for all three CPs. A hypsochromic shift is seen in emission for CP1Br (Δλ = −32 nm) and CP3Br (Δλ = −25 nm), while a slight red-shift is displayed by CP2Br (Δλ = +8 nm). Such observations may be rationalised considering the fact that coordination of the Cd atom to the terminal 3-pyridyl unit of L1 implies electronic changes in the environment of the whole metallatecton. As mentioned above, depending on the Xppy derivatives, either MLCT or a mixture of MLCT and LC transitions may be encountered implying in all cases a LUMO orbital centered on the bpy moiety. Furthermore, DFT calculations on the discrete complexes show notably that the LUMO is partially localised on the terminal pyridyl units of L1, those units being the coordination sites for the Cd atoms in the final CPs. In the case of a LC transition, the HOMO orbital is also localised on L1, and in particular on the terminal pyridine moieties. Thus, binding of the Cd2+ cation to the periphery of L1 will modify in any case the LUMO energy of the metalloligand. In the meantime, for an LC transition, the HOMO energy may also vary, impacting further the emission energy. Moreover, because the LC and MLCT states are energetically close-lying, coordination of the Cd atom to those two peripheral pyridyl coordination sites of the bpy unit may also further perturb the orbital ordering and induce competition between emitting states. No quantum yield could be measured due to the low emission intensity.

Conclusions

In conclusion, three new luminescent octahedral tris-chelate Ir(iii) complexes have been synthesised and studied. Combination of those cationic U-shaped metalloligands with cadmium halide, CdX2, yielded peculiar 1-D heterometallic coordination polymers having [Cd3X8]2− as the metallic distorted square planar 4-connecting node (X = Cl, Br or I). The three metallatectons are luminescent with red-shifted emission upon removal of fluorine atoms on the cyclometalating ligands. Interestingly, the luminescence property is maintained in the resulting coordination polymers. Efforts toward the formation of porous crystalline luminescence materials using other metallic nodes and/or other peripheral coordination sites are underway.

Experimental

General

NMR spectra were recorded on Bruker Avance AV300 (300 MHz for 1H, 75 MHz for 13C, 282 MHz for 19F), Bruker Avance AV400 (400 MHz for 1H, 100 MHz for 13C) or Bruker Avance AV500 (500 MHz for 1H, 125 MHz for 13C, 470 MHz for 19F) spectrometers at 20 °C with complete proton decoupling for nuclei other than 1H. Chemicals shifts (in ppm) were determined relative to the residual undeuterated solvent as the internal reference (CDCl3: 7.26 ppm for 1H and 77.2 ppm for 13C; CD3CN: 1.94 ppm for 1H and 118.3 ppm and 1.3 ppm for 13C). Spin multiplicities are given with the following abbreviations: s (singlet), br s (broad singlet), d (doublet), dd (doublet of doublets), t (triplet), q (quadruplet), m (multiplet) and coupling constants (J) quoted in Hz. 1H NMR spectra were assigned by standard methods combined with COSY and NOESY/ROESY experiments. 13C spectra were assigned by standard methods combined with DEPT, HMQC and HMBC experiments.

Mass spectrometry was performed at the “Service de spectrométrie de masse”, Université de Strasbourg. Low and high-resolution mass spectra (positive mode ESI: Electro Spray Ionization) were recorded on a Thermoquest AQA Navigator® with a time of flight detector.

Elemental analyses were performed on a Thermo Scientific Flash 2000 by the “Service d'analyses, de mesures physiques et de spectroscopie optique”, Université de Strasbourg.

UV/vis spectra in solution and in the solid state were recorded on a PerkinElmer Lambda 650S spectrophotometer (spectra in the solid state were recorded in the reflection mode, using a 150 mm integrating sphere and Spectralon© as the light spectral reference for the reflection corrections). Wavelengths are given in nm and molar absorption coefficients (ε) are given in L mol−1 cm−1. Spectrophotometric grade solvents were used for measurements.

Steady state emission spectra of the discrete complexes were recorded in the solid state (powder samples) on a PicoQuant FluoTime 300 (PicoQuant GmbH, Germany) and corrected by standard correction curves. Time-resolved measurements were performed using either the time-correlated single-photon counting (TCSPC) electronics PicoHarp300 or the Multi-Channel Scaling (MCS) electronics NanoHarp 250 of the PicoQuant FluoTime 300 (PicoQuant GmbH, Germany), equipped with a PDL 820 laser pulse driver. A pulsed laser diode LDH-P-C-405 (λ = 405 nm, pulse FWHM < 50 ps, repetition rate 200 kHz–40 MHz) was used to excite the sample and mounted directly on the sample chamber at 90°. The photons were collected by a PMA-C-192 photomultiplier (PMT) single-photon-counting detector. The data were acquired by using the commercially available software EasyTau (PicoQuant GmbH, Germany), while data analysis was performed using the commercially available software FluoFit (PicoQuant GmbH, Germany). The absolute photoluminescence quantum yields (PLQYs) of solids were measured on a Hamamatsu Quantaurus-QY integrating sphere under air-equilibrated conditions at room temperature using scan mode (300–400 nm, 10 nm step). Steady-state emission spectra of the coordination polymers were recorded in the solid state (powder samples) on a PerkinElmer LS55 spectrometer equipped with a Hamamatsu R928 photomultiplier tube. Emission and excitation spectra were corrected for source intensity (lamp and grating) and emission spectral response (detector and grating) by standard correction curves.

Crystallography

X-ray single crystal structure data were collected on a Bruker SMART CCD diffractometer with Mo-Kα radiation. The structures were solved and refined using the Bruker SHELXTL Software Package using SHELXS-97 (Sheldrick, 2008) and refined by full matrix least squares on F2 using SHELXL-97 (Sheldrick, 2014) with anisotropic thermal parameters for all non-hydrogen atoms.36 The hydrogen atoms were introduced at calculated positions and not refined (riding model). CCDC 1980611–1980615 and 1980617–1980621† contains the supplementary crystallographic data for this paper.

Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns were recorded on a Bruker D8 AV diffractometer using Cu-Kα radiation (λ = 1.5406 Å) operating at 40 kV and 40 mA with a scanning range between 3.8 and 50° and a scan step size of 2° min−1. For comparison, simulated patterns were calculated using the Mercury software.

Synthetic procedures

All air sensitive and anhydrous reactions were carried out under argon. Light sensitive reactions were protected from light by covering with aluminium foil. The glassware was oven dried at 100 °C and cooled under an argon flow. Commercially available chemicals were used without further purification. Anhydrous toluene, CH2Cl2 and CH3CN were used as supplied by commercial sources without further purification. Ligand L2,14 dimer [Ir(ppy)2(μ-Cl)]2 11 and dimer [Ir(dFppy)2(μ-Cl)]2 12 were synthesised as previously described. Detailed synthetic procedures for all other compounds are given in the ESI.†

General procedure for the Suzuki-coupled pyridyl derivatives

To a solution of [Ir(Xppy)2(L2)][PF6] (Xppy = ppy, Fppy or dFppy) (1eq) in a mixture of toluene/EtOH/H2O/CH3CN (1/1/1/1) was added 3-pyridinylboronic acid (2.2 eq.). After degassing the resulting yellow solution with argon, Pd(PPh3)4 (0.22 eq.) and K2CO3 (5.22 eq.) were added and the solution was heated at 100 °C overnight. After filtration, CH2Cl2 (20 to 40 mL) was added and the organic layer was washed with water (2 × 10 to 20 mL) and evaporated to dryness. Recrystallization by vapor diffusion of Et2O into a CH3CN or CHCl3 solution containing the desired product afforded the different complexes as red to yellow solids.
