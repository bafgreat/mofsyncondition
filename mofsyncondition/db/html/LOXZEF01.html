Since the discovery of slow relaxation of magnetization in sandwiched bis-phthalocyanine compounds (Bu4N)Pc2Ln (Ln = Tb, Dy),1 considerable efforts have been focused on the design and syntheses of lanthanide-based single-molecule magnets (Ln-SMMs),2 some of which display rather high energy barriers and blocking temperatures.3 The high energy barriers originate from the large single-ion magnetic anisotropies for 4f ions due to the strong spin–orbit couplings. However, the anisotropy of lanthanide ions is not easy to control, and is highly dependent on the coordination enviornment around the 4f ions.4 The lanthanide ions with high local symmetry may be achieved in mononuclear species through particular ligand design. But the situation can be more complicated in polynuclear or extended systems because in these cases the effects of the exchange coupling as well as the intra- and inter-molecular dipolar interactions could also play a non-trivial role in the magnetic behaviors.2,5 In order to get insight into the key factor in the magnetic relaxation of lanthanide based complexes, it is essential to elucidate the magneto-structural relationships based on the structurally related systems.

Polymorphism, an important structural phenomenon, concerns the crystallization of a compound in more than one form, which is often related to the molecular geometry, interaction and crystal packing.6 This phenomenon can offer unique opportunities in studying the structure–property relationships,7,8 as demonstrated in polymorphic Fe(ii) spin-crossover systems.9 However, although a number of polymorphic coordination compounds have been obtained by subtle variation of reaction conditions, challenges remain in isolating different polymorphs because crystallization is a kinetic process and the weak intra- and inter-molecular interactions are difficult to control.6,10 As far as we are aware, polymorphic lanthanide compounds showing slow magnetization relaxation have not been documented in the literature except one example.4h

In this communication, we report polymorphic layered dysprosium phosphonates, namely, α-Dy(2-qpH)(SO4)(H2O)2 (α-Dy) and β-Dy(2-qpH)(SO4)(H2O)2 (β-Dy) (2-qpH2 = 2-quinolinephosphonic acid, C9H6NPO3H2). Both show field-induced slow magnetization relaxation at low temperatures. The impact of polymorphism on the magnetic dynamics is discussed.

Hydrothermal reaction of 2-qpH211 with Dy(NO3)3·6H2O and ZnSO4·7H2O in a 1 : 1 : 2 molar ratio (pH = 1.10) at 140 °C resulted in a mixture of rod-like crystals of α-Dy and plate-like crystals of β-Dy. Pure phases of α-Dy and β-Dy can be obtained when the pH of the reaction mixtures is 1.5 and 0.7, respectively, as confirmed by the powder XRD patterns. Single crystal structural analysis reveals that α-Dy crystallizes in monoclinic space group P21/c. The asymmetric unit contains one Dy3+, one 2-qpH−, one SO42− and two coordination water molecules (Fig. 1a). The Dy atom adopts a distorted dodecahedron geometry, surrounded by three phosphonate O atoms (O1, O2A, O3B), three sulfate O atoms (O4, O5, O5C) and two water molecules (O1W, O2W). The Dy–O bond lengths are 2.292(2)–2.472(2) Å. Each 2-qpH− is coordinated to three equivalent Dy3+via its three phosphonate oxygen atoms. The quinoline nitrogen is protonated. The SO42− anion behaves as a bidentate ligand, chelating and bridging two Dy atoms through two oxygen atoms (O4 and O5). The O5 serves as a μ3–O bridge linking two equivalent Dy atoms into a dimer of {Dy2O2}. Within the dimer, the Dy–O–Dy bond angle is 115.6(1)° and the Dy⋯Dy distance is 4.144(1) Å. The dimers are further connected through O–P–O units, forming a two-dimensional layer in the bc plane which contains 4-, 8-, and 12-membered rings (Fig. 1c). The Dy⋯Dy distances over the O–P–O bridges are 5.334(1) and 6.770(1) Å. The coordination water (O1W, O2W), phosphonate oxygen (O1, O2), sulfate oxygen (O6, O7) and quinoline nitrogen (N1) form hydrogen bonds within the layer (Table S3 and Fig. S4, ESI†). The quinoline groups are grafted on both sides of the inorganic layers, which are also involved in the inter-layer C–H⋯O hydrogen bond interactions (Table S3, ESI†). The parallel quinoline rings from the adjacent layers are π–π stacked along the b axis, with an average centroid–centroid distance between two benzene rings of 3.608 Å (Fig. S5–S6, ESI†). Thus, a three-dimensional supramolecular network structure is constructed.

Compound β-Dy crystallizes in the triclinic system space group P. It also shows a layer structure built up from {Dy2O2} dimers and phosphonate linkages (Fig. 1). In this case, the Dy–O bond lengths are 2.265(4)–2.482(4) Å. The Dy–O–Dy angle within the dimer [114.4(1)°] is slightly smaller than that in α-Dy. The Dy⋯Dy distances over μ3–O [4.146(1) Å] and O–P–O bridges [5.222(2), 5.486(2) and 6.792(3) Å] are comparable to those in α-Dy. Hydrogen bond interactions are also found within the layer and between the layers (Table S3, ESI†). Again, interlayer π–π stacking is found with the average distance between benzene rings of the quinoline groups being 3.626 Å.

Although compounds α-Dy and β-Dy display similar layer structures with the quinoline groups filling in the interlayer spaces, a careful analysis reveals that the arrangement of the {Dy2O2} dimers within the layer is different. In α-Dy, the dimers are arranged in a zigzag form along the c-axis. While in β-Dy, the dimers are arranged in parallel throughout the layer (Fig. 1c and d). Such a difference also poses influences on some structural parameters. For example, the Dy–O⋯O–Dy torsion angles over the O–P–O bridges are quite different in α-Dy (30.0, 66.3 and 67.7°) and β-Dy (24.8, 63.6 and 83.0°). The O1W–Dy1–O2W angle in α-Dy [140.1(1)°] is significantly reduced in β-Dy [130.1(1)°]. Thus the hydrogen bonds involving O1W are quite different, although those involving O2W remain similar in the two cases (Fig. S4, ESI†). All these structural differences will be reflected by their magnetic behaviors.

The direct-current (dc) magnetic susceptibility data for compounds α-Dy and β-Dy were collected in the temperature range of 1.8–300 K at 1 kOe. As shown in Fig. 2, the room temperature χMT values are 14.00 and 14.81 cm3 K mol−1 per Dy for α-Dy and β-Dy, respectively, which are close to the expected value of 14.17 cm3 K mol−1 for an isolated Dy3+ (6H15/2, S = 5/2, L = 5, J = 15/2, g = 4/3).12 Upon cooling, χMT values decrease gradually reaching minimum values of 10.93 (at ca. 22 K) and 12.72 cm3 K mol−1 (at ca. 16 K) for α-Dy and β-Dy, respectively, owing to the progressive depopulation of the excited MJ sublevels split by the crystal field.13 Below these temperatures, the χMT values increase sharply and approach 17.24 and 14.25 cm3 K mol−1, respectively, at 1.8 K. The upturn of χMT could be due to the ferromagnetic interactions between Dy3+ ions through the μ3–O and/or O–P–O bridges within the layer. In order to elucidate the exact origin of the increase of χMT, we prepared the diluted samples of α-Dy0.10Y0.90(2-qpH)(SO4)(H2O)2 (α-Dy0.10Y0.90) and β-Dy0.12Y0.88(2-qpH)(SO4)(H2O)2 (β-Dy0.12Y0.88) in which the Dy3+ is doped in the diamagnetic yttrium analogue α-Y(2-qpH)(SO4)(H2O)2 (α-Y) and β-Y(2-qpH)(SO4)(H2O)2 (β-Y), respectively, the phase purity of which was confirmed by PXRD measurements. Magnetic study shows that the χMT values of both α-Dy0.10Y0.90 and β-Dy0.12Y0.88 (per Dy) decrease monotonously upon cooling (Fig. 2), confirming the presence of weak ferromagnetic exchange couplings in α-Dy and β-Dy.14 Ferromagnetic interactions were also observed in a few other dysprosium complexes containing μ–O bridges.15

The magnetization of α-Dy and β-Dy, measured at 1.8 K, shows rapid increases at low field and then slowly reaches the values of 6.21 and 7.26 Nβ at 70 kOe (Fig. S7, ESI†). These values are lower than the expected saturation value of 10 Nβ for an isolated Dy3+. The unsaturated magnetization together with the non-superimposed M vs. H/T curves suggests the presence of magnetic anisotropy and/or low-lying excited states in the two polymorphic compounds.16 In addition, a small opening of the butterfly-shaped hysteresis loops is found for both compounds at 1.8 K (Fig. S8, ESI†). Interestingly, an evident hysteresis loop is observed in the diluted sample of β-Dy0.12Y0.88 at 1.8 K with the coercive field of ca. 400 Oe (Fig. 2, inset).

To explore the magnetic dynamics, the alternating current (ac) susceptibility data were measured under zero dc field. Neither peaks nor frequency dependence of the in-phase (χM′) and out-of-phase (χM′′) signals are observed for α-Dy down to 1.8 K. For β-Dy, only tails of χM′′ signals appear below ca. 3 K (Fig. S10, ESI†). The absence of ac signals could be attributed to the fast quantum tunneling of magnetization (QTM) and/or exchange couplings that destroy the anisotropy of Dy ions. The QTM effect can be suppressed by applying an external dc field.17 Thus, the ac susceptibility measurements were performed at 5.0 K under variable dc fields (0.5–3.0 kOe) for both compounds (Fig. S11, ESI†). 2 kOe dc field was chosen as the optimum field because it induces almost the slowest relaxation in both compounds.

Fig. 3a shows the χM′ and χM′′ vs. T curves of compound α-Dy, measured at Hdc = 2 kOe and frequencies 72–999 Hz. Both χM′ and χM′′ components show strong frequency dependence below 12 K, indicating slow relaxation of magnetization. The single peaks of χM′′ signals appear around 6 K, and shift to higher temperature with increasing frequency. Notably, additional shoulders are also present at ca. 8 K in the χM′′ vs. T curves, possibly due to the presence of a very small amount of β-Dy in the system which cannot be detected in the PXRD patterns. Indeed, these shoulders disappear in the χM′′ vs. T curves of the doped sample α-Dy0.10Y0.90 (Fig. 3b). Noting that the diluted sample shows the same field-induced relaxation as non-diluted sample (Fig. 3a and b), the ferromagnetic coupling must be weak enough and the relaxation is from the single-ion magnetic behavior of the Dy ion.

The frequency-dependent ac susceptibilities were also measured for α-Dy under a 2 kOe dc field (Fig. S13a, ESI†). Two relaxation processes in the high frequency (relaxation A, dominate) and low frequency region (relaxation B) are clearly identified in the Cole–Cole plots in the temperature range 2.0–7.0 K (Fig. S13b, ESI†). By fitting the data with a linear combination of two modified Debye models,18 the distribution coefficients αΑ and αB are obtained as 0.06–0.72 and 0.07–0.34, respectively. The energy barrier for relaxation A is obtained according to the Arrhenius law as 32.0 K (τ0A = 7.5 × 10−7 s) (Fig. 3f). For relaxation B, the linearity of the curve associated with an Orbach mechanism of relaxation is observed only in a small temperature range. Thus the data in the whole temperature range (2.0–7.0 K) were reproduced by equation τ−1 = CTn + τ0−1exp(−Δ′/kBT) assuming that the relaxation rate is given by the Orbach plus an alternative (Raman) mechanism.19,20 The best fit gives Δ′/kB = 107.5 K (τ0B = 3.6 × 10−10 s) with n = 3 (Fig. S13c, ESI†). The value agrees well with those for β-Dy and β-Dy0.12Y0.88 (discussed latter), suggesting the contamination of a small amount of β-Dy in α-Dy. In contrast, a single relaxation process is found for the dilute sample α-Dy0.10Y0.90 at 2 kOe dc field with Δ/kB = 29.3 K (τ0 = 5.4 × 10−6 s) (Fig. S15, ESI†), in accordance with that for the relaxation A in α-Dy.

For β-Dy, the χM′′(T) signals measured at Hdc = 2 kOe exhibit well-shaped peaks with one maximum in the temperature range of 7.0–9.0 K, depending on the specific frequency (Fig. 3c). For the dilute sample β-Dy0.12Y0.88, the peaks appear in the χM′′ vs. T curves at zero dc field, characteristic of a single molecule magnet (Fig. 3d). The tails below 6 K are attributed to the QTM effect. Indeed, when applying a 2 kOe dc field, the QTM effect is largely suppressed and a set of clear frequency-dependent peaks is observed (Fig. 3e).

Fig. S16a (ESI†) shows the variable frequency dependent ac susceptibilities of β-Dy. Both χM′ and χM′′ signals are frequency dependent below 10 K. In the range of 3.5–10.0 K, the magnetization relaxation time τ can be extracted by fitting the Cole–Cole plots using the generalized Debye model for the single relaxation process (Fig. S16b, ESI†). The α values are in the range of 0.03–0.59. The ln τ values increase linearly with 1/T above 7 K and deviate from linear behavior below 7 K. The high temperature data follow the Arrhenius law, resulting in the effective anisotropy barrier of 83.0 K and τ0 of 1.3 × 10−8 s (Fig. S16c (ESI†) and Fig. 3f). The observed curvature could be attributed to the non-negligible influence of direct and/or Raman processes at low temperature. For β-Dy0.12Y0.88, χM′′(ν) data show obvious frequency dependence below 10 K at zero dc field with Δ/kB = 89.5 K (τ0 = 2.9 × 10−9 s) (Fig. S17 (ESI†) and Fig. 3f). At 2 kOe dc field, the QTM effect is suppressed. The energy barrier associated with the Orbach mechanism is 110.2 K (τ0 = 4.6 × 10−10 s) (Fig. S19 (ESI†) and Fig. 3f).

Clearly, the magnetization relaxation barrier of β-Dy is much higher than that of α-Dy. Considering that the exchange couplings are weak ferromagnetic and the dipolar interactions are similar in the two polymorphs, the siginificant change in the energy barriers must be related to the aniostropy in the two systems. It is well known that the single-ion anisotropy of lanthanide is highly dependent on the coordination environment. As described above, the coordination sphere around the Dy3+ is slightly different in the two compounds. In order to estimate the orientation of the magnetic anisotropy, we use the electrostatic optimization of the aspherical electron density distribution method, proposed by Chilton et al. in MAGELLAN software, assuming that MJ = ±15/2 is the ground state.21 As shown in Fig. S20 (ESI†), the orientation of the anisotropy axis is indeed slightly different in the two polymorphs with the angles between the Dy1–O1W bond and the anisotropy axis being 61.6° (for α-Dy) and 54.1° (for β-Dy), respectively. It should be mentioned, however, that the estimation is rough because the real ground states may possess MJ values other than ±15/2. The orientation of the easy-axis could also be affected by the orientation of the H–O–H plane of the coordinated water due to the different hydrogen bond network involving O1W.22 Furthermore, noting that the ferromagnetically coupled {Dy2O2} dimers are arranged in a zigzag form along the c-axis in α-Dy while in parallel throughout the layer in β-Dy, the overall anisotropy could be largely reduced in the former case due to the non-parallel arrangement of the easy-axes (Fig. 1c and d). All these factors may contribute to the much higher energy barrier observed for β-Dy.

In conclusion, two polymorphs of layered dysprosium phosphonate Dy(2-qpH)(SO4)(H2O)2 have been successfully isolated by controlling the pH of the reaction mixtures. Field-induced slow relaxation of magnetization is observed with energy barriers of ca. 32.0 K for α-Dy and ca. 83.0 K for β-Dy, respectively. The remarkable difference in the energy barriers could originate from the different arrangements of the {Dy2O2} dimers within the layer, the slightly different coordination environment as well as the orientation of the H–O–H planes of the coordination water molecules. This work demonstrates a rare example on the impact of polymorphism on the magnetic dynamics of Ln-SMMs. Further work is in progress to explore new polymorphic lanthanide systems and elucidate their magneto-structural relationships.
