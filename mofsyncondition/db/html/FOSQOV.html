Introduction

Polyoxometalates (POMs) are one of the most widely used inorganic polydentate building blocks not only due to their extreme variability of compositions and molecular characteristics, but also to their application in catalysis, photochemistry, gas storage and separation.1–4 The combination of the properties of POMs with their varying coordination modes, together with a number of their ability to form organic–inorganic hybrids with transition metal complex or lanthanide complex moieties, provides an impetus for the synthesis of these multifunctional materials.5–9

The Keggin structure {XM12O40} that is built from the association of a central XO4 tetrahedron surrounded by four M3O13 groups, where X is the heteroatom (most commonly Si or P) and M is a transition metal ion in octahedral coordination with a high oxidation state, is the most archetypal POM structure. Five theoretically possible geometrical isomers of the Keggin structure, designated by the prefixes α, β, γ, δ, and ε, which result from the serial 60° rotations around the 3-fold axes of the M3O13 units, have been reported by Baker and Figgis.10 Interestingly, the ε-Keggin POMs have always been observed, encountered with four capping centers.

In the past decade, there have been only a few reports about hybrid polyoxomolybdates based on a mixed-valence ε-Keggin unit. {ε-PMoV8MoVI4O40−x(OH)xM4} is a versatile building unit, with M being either a zinc ion or a lanthanum ion located at the vertices of a mildly distorted tetrahedron. The charge of the Keggin unit relies on the number of protonated oxo bridging ligands, which varies from 0 to 5. Depending on their geometric orientation and the number of coordination sites, POMs can be functionalized by organic linkers easily either by directly connecting to the oxygen atoms of the POM or via rare earth or transition metal ions grafted at the surface of the POM.11–13 Various organic linkers such as bipyridine,14–16 benzenedicarboxylic acid,17 imidazole,18 trimesic acid19 and para-azobipyridine20 have been successfully reacted with the ε-Keggin ions leading to a new family of 1D–3D materials. Additionally, these materials can display interesting magnetic, optical and catalytic properties,21–23 although only a few reports have discussed their electrochemical behavior.24,25 As the isolated structures of POMs have better electrochemical properties and catalytic activity, it is therefore vital to design isolated novel ε-Keggin POMs.

We describe herein the synthesis and characterization of two novel polyoxometalates, Na[PMoV8MoVI4O38(OH)2Zn4][pyim]2·1.5H2O ε(pyim)2 and [PMoV8MoVI4O37(OH)3Zn4]2[pyim]6·4H2O ε2(pyim)6, containing monomeric, chain-like and dimeric isolated ε-Zn Keggin building blocks connected by pyim ligands. It is noted that ε2(pyim)6 was obtained with an isolated structure built of a dimerized ε-Zn Keggin when the pH value was lowered slightly. One of the biggest differences between other syntheses and ours is that the ε-Keggin ions originate from the self-assembly of molybdenum powder or molybdate with phosphoric acid or phosphorous acid in the others, while the ε-Keggin ions of our hybrid polyoxometalates come from the configuration transformation from α-Keggin. Still, it is striking that there is no other example of an isolated cluster of a dimerized ε-Keggin POM. Besides the structural data, the fluorescence properties and electrochemical properties of the two materials have therefore been investigated. More interestingly, ε2(pyim)6 shows high catalytic activity for the esterification of phosphoric acid with equimolar lauryl alcohol to monoalkyl phosphate ester (MAP).

Experimental section

Materials and physical methods

2-(2-Pyridyl)-imidazole was prepared according to the literature,26 while other chemical reagents were commercially available and used without further purification. C, H and N elemental analyses were performed on a Perkin-Elmer 2400 CHN elemental analyzer. IR spectra of all compounds were recorded with a Nicolet Impact 410 FTIR spectrometer using pressed KBr pellets in the 4000–450 cm−1 region. TG measurements were carried out on a Diamond thermogravimetric analyzer in flowing N2 atmosphere from 25 to 800 °C with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. The solid state fluorescence properties of ε(pyim)2 and ε2(pyim)6 were investigated using an F-4600 FL Spectrophotometer. Powder XRD patterns were obtained on a Bruker D8X diffractometer equipped with monochromatized Cu-Kα (λ = 0.15418 nm) radiation at room temperature, and the data were collected in the range of 5° ≤ 2θ ≤ 50°. A CHI 440 electrochemical quartz crystal microbalance connected to a Digital-586 personal computer was used for the electrochemical experiments. A conventional three-electrode cell was used at room temperature. A saturated calomel electrode (SCE) was used as the reference electrode and a platinum wire as the counter electrode. The compounds ε(pyim)2 and ε2(pyim)6 bulk-modified carbon paste electrodes (ε(pyim)2-CPE and ε2(pyim)6-CPE) were used as the working electrodes.

Synthesis of Na[PMoV8MoVI4O38(OH)2Zn4][pyim]2·1.5H2O (ε(pyim)2)

A mixture of Zn(OAC)2·2H2O (0.1103 g, 0.50 mmol) and H3PMo12O40·xH2O (0.4452 g, 0.21 mmol) were dissolved in 8 mL of distilled water. The mixture was stirred for 2 h at room temperature and the pH of the mixture was adjusted to 3.5 with 6 M NaOH. Then a solution of pyim (0.0366 g, 0.25 mmol) in 4 mL of alcohol was added, with stirring for 0.5 h. The suspension was put into a 20 mL Teflon-lined stainless-steel autoclave and kept under autogenous pressure at 170 °C for 4.5 days. After slowly cooling to room temperature, black block crystals were filtered and washed with distilled water (28.81% yield based on Mo). Elemental analysis (%) calcd (found) for ε(pyim)2: C 7.92 (7.88), H 0.79 (0.72), N 3.46 (3.43). IR (solid KBr pellet, cm−1): 3448 (s), 1611 (m), 1470 (w), 1378 (m), 1105 (m), 938 (s), 818 (m), 777 (s), 691 (w), 546 (w).

Synthesis of [PMoV8MoVI4O37(OH)3Zn4]2[pyim]6·4H2O (ε2(pyim)6)

The synthesis procedure for ε2(pyim)6 is similar to that for ε(pyim)2, except for pH = 3.0. After slowly cooling to room temperature, black block crystals were filtered and washed with distilled water (58.82% yield based on Mo). Elemental analysis (%) calcd (found) for ε(pyim)2: C 11.27 (11.11), H 1.10 (1.02), N 4.93 (4.95). IR (solid KBr pellet, cm−1): 3450 (s), 1618 (s), 1570 (w), 1469 (s), 1382 (m), 1294 (w), 1106 (w), 937 (s), 822 (m), 775 (s), 681 (w), 540 (w).

X-Ray crystallography

The single-crystal XRD of ε(pyim)2 and ε2(pyim)6 were obtained from a Bruker Apex II CCD with Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at 296 K using ω–2θ scan method. The crystal structures were solved by direct method and refined by the full-matrix least-squares methods on F2 using the SHELX-97 program package. All of the non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. The hydrogen atoms of organic ligands were refined in calculated positions, assigned isotropic thermal parameters, and allowed to ride on their parent atoms, while the H atoms for O–H and water were not located. Results and discussion

Synthesis

Hydrothermal synthesis has recently been proved to be a powerful method in the synthesis of POM-based organic–inorganic hybrid compounds. Many factors can affect the nucleation and crystal growth of the final products during a specific hydrothermal synthesis, such as the type of initial reactants, starting concentrations of reactants, time, pH values, solvents and temperature. In our case, the pH of the solution plays an important role for the formation of two compounds based on the ε-Zn Keggin units with different structures. In our parallel experiments, we only got powder impurities without crystals using a pH above 4 or below 3. The dimerized 0D ε-Zn Keggin ε2(pyim)6 was obtained when the pH value was lowered slightly from 3.5 to 3.0. The ligands imidazole and triazole were used instead of the ligand pyim in our parallel experiments. We only got some precipitations, so also pyim may have a structure-directing role in the crystallization process of the two compounds. It is also worth mentioning that the biggest difference in the synthesis compared with other methods is that the ε-Keggin ions of two compounds come from the configuration transformation from α-Keggin structural compounds, while these originate from the self-assembly of molybdenum powder or molybdate with phosphoric acid or phosphorous acid in other cases.

PXRD analyses

PXRD measurements for ε(pyim)2 and ε2(pyim)6 were determined at room temperature (Fig. S1 and S2†), the diffraction peak positions of the experimental XRD patterns of ε(pyim)2 and ε2(pyim)6 are in accordance with those of simulated XRD patterns, indicating the phase purity of the two compounds.

IR spectra

The infrared spectra of ε(pyim)2 and ε2(pyim)6 are similar (Fig. S3 and S4†). Taking ε2(pyim)6 as the example, several regions can be differentiated: typical absorption characteristic peaks for 2-(2-pyridyl)-imidazole are found around 1150–1611 cm−1. The signature of Mo–O and P–O vibrations of the inorganic skeleton of the two ε-POM are identified at around 938 and 1105 cm−1, respectively, while the Mo–O–Mo vibrations are encountered below 938 cm−1. In addition, O–H vibrations are found at around 3448 cm−1.

Crystal structure of 1

The two hybrid materials are based on the well-known {ε-PMoV8MoVI4O40Zn4} POM unit, a reduced ε-Keggin isomer capped by four Zn(ii) ions in tetrahedral coordination on its surface. In the structures of the ε-Zn derivatives,27 the ε-PMo12 Keggin isomer, containing 8 Mo(v) and 4 Mo(vi) ions, is an eight-electron reduced POM, as confirmed by the examination of the Mo(v)–Mo(v) bonds (ca. 2.6 Å) and Mo(vi)–Mo(vi) bonds (ca. 3.2 Å), as well as bond valence sum calculations on the Mo centers (Table S1 in ESI†). Indeed, the valence bond calculations show clearly that Mo3, Mo5, Mo8 and Mo10 are Mo(v) ions, also in agreement with their intermetallic Mo–Mo bonds.

Two different coordination modes are encountered in ε(pyim)2. Thus, Zn1 and Zn2 are both tetra-coordinated in a tetrahedron coordination geometry, being bound to three oxygen atoms of one ε-Keggin core and one oxygen atom of another ε-Keggin core. Zn3 and Zn4 are both bound to three oxygen atoms of one ε-Keggin core and two nitrogen atoms of a pyim ligand, and are thus penta-coordinated in a square-pyramidal coordination geometry (Fig. 1).

In ε(pyim)2, the zigzag chain of the POM building blocks can be seen as the condensation of {ε-PMoV8MoVI4O40Zn4} POMs via Zn–O bonds (1.944(6)–2.275(9) Å) with two adjacent POMs. Such condensation has also been evidenced in the chains built in the previously reported [ε(trim)]∞ 19 and molybdogermanate ε-Keggin POMs {GeMoV8MoVI4O36(OH)4Ni4}.28 Due to pyim being used, the conformation of the [PMoV8MoVI4O38(OH)2Zn4pyim2]n chain in ε(pyim)2 is very different. Then, adjacent 1D zigzag chains are linked up to yield a 2D net structure via Na–O bonds with an average bond length of 2.491(5) Å, and each sodium atom connects to the four adjacent POMs (Fig. 2). Free water molecules partake in strong H-bonding interactions with the polyanion framework and fill the cavities of the 3D supramolecular architecture.

Crystal structure of 2

Slight variations in the conditions of the experimental synthesis had a strong influence on the structures of the compounds, affording another phase, ε2(pyim)6. Examination of the Mo⋯Mo distances and valence bond calculations (Table S1 in the ESI†) have confirmed the presence of eight Mo(v) ions, four Mo(vi) ions and three bridging oxygen atoms that are protonated. Differently to ε(pyim)2, the inorganic building unit in ε2(pyim)6 is a dimerized form of the ε-Zn POM, resulting from the condensation of two ε-Zn POM units through the formation of two Zn–O bonds between a capping Zn(ii) ion on a ε-Zn POM unit and the bridging oxygen atom of the adjacent ε-Zn POM (Fig. 3).

The dimer has already been described in ε2(pazo)4,11ε2(im)4,9 and ε2(trim)2,8 which are 1D, 2D and 3D, respectively. In ε2(pazo)4, the two ZnII ions on the dimeric ε-Zn POMs are bound to one O atom of a free HPO32− ion, being from the H3PO3 precursor, which allows the connection of the dimers into a 1D chain. While in ε2(im)4, the two ZnII ions on the dimeric ε-Zn POMs are coordinated to bridging imidazolate ligands that ensure the connection of the dimeric ε-Zn POMs to four adjacent dimers, thus leading to the formation of ε2(im)4 into a 2D layered structure. The 3D framework of ε2(trim)2 is generated by the connection of the dimeric ε-Zn POMs and the bifunctionality of the organic ligand. Since the pyim was used, we have fortunately synthesized the first isolated structure in the family of the dimerized form of ε-Zn POMs. It follows that this dimeric ε-Zn POM possesses six accessible capping Zn(ii) ions and four of them are chelated by a pyim ligand. The connection of the dimeric ε-Zn POMs via H-bonding interactions generates a 3D supramolecular framework that stacks up in a windmill style along the a axis (Fig. 4).

Although our experimental structures of ε(pyim)2 and ε2(pyim)6 are not Z-POMOF type structures, the synthesis of ε(pyim)2 shows that the pyim ligands can also connect ε-Zn POMs and the successful synthesis of ε2(pyim)6 verified that the dimerized form of ε-Zn POMs can also be isolated structures. Our experimental structures suggest that a Z-POMOF structure with pyim ligands could be obtained through varying the synthetic conditions, particularly the pH and temperature, in our future work.

Fluorescence spectrum

The luminescence studies of ε(pyim)2 and ε2(pyim)6 and the ligand pyim were explored in the solid state at room temperature. The emission bands are centered at about 370 nm for imidazole, which may be attributed to the ligand-centered π*–π electronic transitions. Comparably, ε(pyim)2 and ε2(pyim)6 exhibit a similar emission band at ca. 398 nm and 400 nm, respectively, upon excitation at 597 and 600 nm (Fig. S5 and S6†). Clearly, the emission spectra of ε(pyim)2 and ε2(pyim)6 are both red-shifted compared with the free pyim ligands, which may be attributed to the charge-transition between the ligands and metal ion centers. In comparison with free organic ligands, the emission intensity increases as the rigidity of the ligands is increased by reducing the loss of energy through thermal vibrations due to the hydrogen bonding interactions between the organic ligands and the guest water molecules.

Voltammetric behavior

The electrochemical behaviour of ε(pyim)2 and ε2(pyim)6 was investigated in a 1 M H2SO4 aqueous solution at different scan rates. The bulk-modified CPE, which is easy to prepare and handle, becomes a good optimal choice to study the electrochemical properties as ε(pyim)2 and ε2(pyim)6 are insoluble in water and common organic solvents. The CV plots for ε(pyim)2-CPE and ε2(pyim)6-CPE in a 1 M H2SO4 aqueous solution at different scan rates are presented in Fig. 5 and 6.

Three reversible redox peaks (I–I′, II–II′, III–III′) appear in the potential range of −200 to 800 mV for both ε(pyim)2 and ε2(pyim)6. The mean peak potentials (E1/2 = (Epa + Epc)/2) for ε(pyim)2 are at 366 mV, 181 mV and −43 mV, respectively. For ε2(pyim)6 the mean peak potentials are at 357 mV (I–I′), 200 mV (II–II′) and −42 mV (III–III′). These could be ascribed to the redox progress of ZnII/0 and MoVI/V. In addition, the pairs of reversible redox peaks I–I′, II–II′ and III–III′ can be ascribed to three consecutive two-electron processes.29,30 The peak potentials change gradually along with the scan rates from 80 to 400 mV s−1 for ε(pyim)2 and from 20 to 200 mV s−1 for ε2(pyim)6. The anodic peak potentials transfer to the positive direction and the corresponding cathodic peak potentials shift toward the negative direction with increasing scan rates.

Thermogravimetric analyses

The thermal behavior of ε(pyim)2 and ε2(pyim)6 was investigated on crystalline samples under a N2 atmosphere from 25 to 800 °C. The TG curve (Fig. S7†) of ε(pyim)2 shows a slight weight loss of 1.13% before 180 °C, corresponding to the release of 1.5 lattice water molecules (calc. 1.11%). The second weight loss of 13.13% from 140 to 440 °C is assigned to the removal of all pyim ligands and the dehydration of two hydroxyl groups (calc. 13.37%). The TG curve (Fig. S8†) of ε2(pyim)6 is similar to that of ε(pyim)2, also exhibiting two weight loss steps. The first weight loss of 1.45% between 25 to 150 °C corresponds to the release of 4 lattice water molecules (calc. 1.41%). The second weight loss of 19.17% between 195 to 700 °C is assigned to the removal of all pyim ligands and the dehydration of six hydroxyl groups (calc. 13.37%).

Catalytic activity experiment

The esterification of phosphoric acid with equimolar lauryl alcohol (Scheme 1) was performed in a 100 mL three-necked round-bottomed flask fitted with a Dean-Stark trap (toluene was heated to reflux to remove water) and a magnetic stirrer. A solution of ε2(pyim)6 (0.095 g), phosphoric acid (0.067 mol, 85% aqueous solution), and lauryl alcohol (0.067 mol) in toluene (15 mL) was added into the flask and the mixture was heated at 110 °C for 25 h under stirring, while maintaining gentle reflux. After quenching the reaction, the product mixture was diluted with water (5 mL) and isopropanol (5 mL), transferred into a separating funnel and allowed to settle for one hour. The supernatant oil layer was then separated directly from the mixture and evaporated on a rotary evaporator at 90 °C until dry. The yield and selectivity of mono-dodecyl phosphate (MAP) in the product mixture were determined by potentiometric titration based on the amount of phosphoric acid in the starting reactants with the three different dissociation constants of H3PO4.31,32

Our experimental results show that a high yield of 88.5% and selectivity of 93.8% for mono-dodecyl phosphate was achieved with toluene as the dehydrant when the direct esterification of phosphoric acid with lauryl alcohol was carried out at 110 °C for 24 h under the optimum catalyst dosage of 0.5% (mass fraction). The above catalytic result is comparable with other inorganic–organic compounds based on polyanions.32,33

Conclusions

The 1D structure of ε(pyim)2 is built up of {PMoV8MoVI4O38(OH)2Zn4} units modified by pyim organic ligands. To the best of our knowledge, ε2(pyim)6 is the first isolated structural compound of dimeric ε-Zn POMs, with good electrochemical properties, and high catalytic activity in esterification, constructed of eight-reduced ε-Keggin cores. The different structures of ε(pyim)2 and ε2(pyim)6 demonstrate that slight changes of the pH in the synthesis conditions can lead to a large difference in the structures of the compounds you obtain. The luminescent and electrochemical properties indicate that ε(pyim)2 and ε2(pyim)6 have a wide range of potential applications in, e.g., photo or electrocatalysis. In addition, obtaining ε(pyim)2 and ε2(pyim)6 based on the pyim ligand is an encouraging step towards the synthesis of a new family of POMOFs linked by pyim ligands.
