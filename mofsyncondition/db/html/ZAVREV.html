Introduction

Lanthanide metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) are attracting increasing attention not only due to their desirable structural features1,2 but also due to their exceptional optical3 and magnetic properties4 arising from 4f electrons. Until now, a large number of lanthanide MOFs with various structures and interesting properties have been obtained, but design and control over lanthanide-based frameworks appear to be a formidable task primarily due to their high and variable coordination numbers.5–7 As is known, rare earth ions have a high affinity for hard donor atoms and ligand containing oxygen or hybrid oxygen–nitrogen atoms, especially multicarboxylate ligands, which are usually employed in the architectures for construction of lanthanide coordination polymers with unique structures and useful properties. The majority of reported lanthanide frameworks are formed by using rigid aromatic multicarboxylic acids such as benzenedicarboxylate,8 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylate9 and 4,4′-biphenyldicarboxylate,10 however, far less effort has been expended on the semirigid V-shaped carboxylate ligands.11

The semirigid V-shaped multicarboxylic acids, actually two benzene rings connected by a non-metallic atom (C, O, S, and N atoms), are excellent building blocks for the construction of frameworks due to quite a few advantages.11 First, the variation and richness of coordination modes bound to metal ions are possible and provide diverse structural motifs. Second, the flexible nature will cause the carboxyl groups to connect metal ions in different directions and favor the formation of helical and interpenetrating structure.

The tricarboxylic acid, 5-(4-carboxy-2-nitrophenoxy)-isophthalic acid (H3L), is a V-shaped semirigid ligand which can provide divergent sites for polymer formation together with a nitryl group intended to occupy the cavities of the expected network. Meanwhile, this ligand with an oxygen atom as the flexible linker is of great interest because such a ligand can bend and rotate freely when it coordinates to metal centers. Herein, we describe the synthesis and structural characterization of thirteen novel metal–organic frameworks using a series of lanthanides with the tricarboxylate ligand. Photoluminescence spectra were collected for the Eu(iii) complex. Furthermore, the magnetic susceptibility of Gd(iii) complexes has been investigated.

Experimental

Materials and methods

The Ln(NO3)3·6H2O (Ln = La, Ce, Pr, Nd, Sm, Eu, Gd, Tb, Dy, Ho, Er and Tm) compounds were prepared by dissolving the corresponding rare earth oxides in an aqueous solution of HNO3 (6.0 M) while adding a bit of H2O2 for Tb4O7 and were then evaporated at 100 °C. The other reagents were purchased from commercial sources and used as received. IR spectra were obtained from KBr pellets on a Perkin-Elmer 580B IR spectrometer in the 400–4000 cm−1 region (SI). Elemental analyses (C, H, N) were performed with a VarioEL analyzer. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was performed on a Perkin-Elmer TG-7 analyzer heated from 40 to 700 °C under nitrogen. Powder X-ray diffraction data were collected on a Bruker D8-ADVANCE diffractometer equipped with Cu-Kα at a scan speed of 5° min−1. The solid-state luminescence spectra were recorded on a Hitachi F-4500 fluorescence spectrophotometer. Temperature-dependent magnetic measurements were carried out on a Quantum Design SQUID MPMS-7 magnetometer with an applied field of 1000 Oe. The diamagnetic corrections for the compounds were estimated using Pascal's constants, and magnetic data were corrected for diamagnetic contributions of the sample holder.12

Synthesis

Compounds 1–12 were obtained in a typical procedure, a mixture of Ln(NO3)3·6H2O (0.10 mmol) (Ln = La, Ce, Pr, Nd, Sm, Eu, Gd, Tb, Dy, Ho, Er and Tm), H3L (0.10 mmol), DMF (2 mL), CH3CH2OH (0.5 mL), and H2O (0.5 mL) was placed in a Teflon reactor (20 mL) and heated at 110 °C for three days. After the mixture was gradually cooled to room temperature at a rate of 10 °C h−1, single crystals of compounds suitable for X-ray study were obtained.

X-Ray crystallography

The X-ray intensity data for the thirteen compounds were collected on a Bruker SMART APEX CCD diffractometer with graphite-monochromatized Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). The crystal structure was solved by means of direct methods and refined employing full-matrix least squares on F2 (SHELXTL-97).13 The hydrogen atoms attached to carbons were generated geometrically. Anisotropic thermal parameters were used to refine all non-hydrogen atoms except for the atoms (C3, C13 and O4 for 7′; O1w for 1–3 and 10–12). For compounds 1–12, the abnormal thermal factors for atoms of disordered DMF molecules were restrained using commands of EADP. The disordered C atoms of disordered DMF molecules in compounds 1–12 (C16, C16′, C17, C17′, C18, C18′, C18′′ for 3 and 5; C16, C16′, C17, C17′, C18, C18′ for 1, 4 and 7; C17, C17′ for 6 and 8–12) were refined using carbon atoms split over two or three sites, with a total occupancy of 1 and identical anisotropic displacement parameters. The refinements converged with crystallographic agreement factors are summarized in Table 1.

Result and discussion

Structural description of 7

X-Ray structural analyses reveal that compounds 1–8 are isostructural and crystallize in the monoclinic space group C2/c. As a representative example, the crystal structure of 7 is described in detail. In the asymmetric unit of [GdL(DMF)2]·0.25H2O, there are one nine-coordinated gadolinium ion, one tricarboxylate ligand (L), and two DMF molecules. As shown in Fig. 1, the La(iii) ion is nine-coordinated within an O9 donor set, where two oxygen atoms (O10 and O11) originate from coordinated DMF molecules, and the other oxygen atoms are from carboxyl groups. The coordination environment of each Gd(iii) ion is a distorted tricapped trigonal prism. The bases of the prism are formed by the atoms O2, O2A, O7C and O3D, O4D, O10. The remaining vertexes are occupied by O1, O8B and O11 (the average Gd–O bond length is 2.452 Å, which falls in the range of Ln–O bond lengths typical of Ln(iii) complexes).14 Two crystallographically equivalent Gd(iii) ions are connected through four independent carboxylate bridges to generate a paddle-wheel building block with a Gd⋯Gd distance of 4.118 Å. In the binuclear paddle-wheel building blocks, there are two types of bridges: (i) bridging oxygen atoms of carboxyl groups, and (ii) bridging carboxyl groups in syn–syn conformation. This binuclear unit is a successful building block, through which many interesting structures have been constructed in the past decade. Similar binuclear motifs have been recognized in the coordination chemistry of lanthanides, both in coordination polymer compounds and in discrete polynuclear complex molecules.15

The complicated 3D framework structure of 7 is constructed from the dinuclear species. Along the a axis, the adjacent binuclear building blocks are linked through the isophthalic carboxylates; while along the c axis, the binuclear motifs are joined by the tricarboxylate ligands, giving rise to a 3D dense framework with channels filled by coordinated DMF molecules (Fig. 1b and 1c). As often observed in closely packed network structures, π–π contacts exist between the benzene rings with the nitryl group (with a centroid-to-centroid distance of 3.82 Å). In order to better identify the nature of the intricate framework, suitable connectors can be defined by using a topological approach. As illustrated in Fig. 2, the binuclear motif can be considered as a six-connecting node since it is connected with six neighboring identical motifs through six L ligands, whereas the L ligand can be regarded as the three-connecting nodes because each L ligand links three binuclear motifs. The resulting (3,6)-connected net can be rationalized as having a flu-3,6-C2/c topology with the Schläfli symbol of (42.6)2(44.62.87.102). To the best of our knowledge, this (3,6)-connected net has rarely been observed in MOFs, and only three compounds were reported using a tricarboxylate ligand.16

Compounds 9–12 almost have the same structure as 7 with a tiny difference in dinuclear building blocks, which is the result of lanthanide contraction. For compounds 9–12, there are also two types of bridges in the binuclear paddle-wheel building blocks, bridging carboxyl groups in syn–syn, and syn–anti conformation, which is different from the bridging modes of carboxyl groups in 7. This is because the distance between bridging oxygen atoms of the carboxyl group and the Ln(iii) ion (Ln = Dy, Ho, Er, and Tm) is too long to form a covalent bond as the atomic number increases. The Ln(iii) ion in compound 9–12 is eight-coordinated with a square anti-prism geometry. On the other hand, for all compounds, the corresponding average Ln–O bond distances become shorter with the increasing atomic number of lanthanide, which is also a direct consequence of lanthanide contraction (Fig. 3).17

Structural description of 7′

Compound 7′ crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/c with one Gd(iii), one L3−, and three coordinated aqua molecules in the asymmetric unit. As shown in Fig. 4a, each Gd(iii) ion lies in a distorted square anti-prism and is coordinated to eight oxygen atoms from four carboxylate groups of the ligands and three aqua molecules. Interestingly, there are strong hydrogen bonds (Table S1†) between the carboxylate group and coordinated aqua molecule [O12–H12C⋯O7 (x − 1, y, z − 1), O11–H11B⋯O7 (−x + 1, −y + 1, −z + 2)], which may greatly attribute to the distortion of Gd(iii) coordination geometry. The carboxylic Gd–O, and Gd–O (H2O) bond distances are in the range of 2.287(9) to 2.553(10), and 2.403(9) to 2.475(9) Å, which are comparable to those reported for other Gd–O donor compounds.14 There are three coordination modes for the carboxylic ligand in 7′, which are different from those in 7 (Fig. 5): (a) the two carboxylate groups of the ligand are coordinated to the Gd(iii) ion in the chelating and mono-coordination modes; (b) the carboxylate group adjacent to the nitryl group of the ligand connects two Gd(iii) ions via a bridging bidentate mode in syn–anti conformation.

As depicted in Fig. 4b, in the bc plane the Gd(iii) ions bridged by isophthalic carboxylate groups yield 16-membered rings (i), which are linked by the ligand to form the rectangular rings (ii) with a large aspect ratio. The propagation of these rings affords a 2D layer, then the carboxylate group adjacent to the nitryl group of the ligand connects two Gd(iii) ions of the neighboring 16-membered rings along the a axis with the separation of 5.439 Å to generate a 3D open framework (Fig. 4c).

A better insight into the nature of this intricate framework can be achieved by the application of the topological approach. In the framework, each Gd(iii) ion is connected with four ligands through a chelating bidentate carboxylate group, a mono-dentate and two bi-monodentate carboxylate groups. It is reasonable to regard it as an inorganic four-connected node. Since each ligand coordinates with four Gd(iii) ions through carboxylate groups, it is clearer to describe the structure when the ligand is regarded as an organic four-connected node. The resulting net can be rationalized as having zeolite BCT topology with the Schläfli symbol of (4·65) (Fig. 6). However, we noticed that among the currently known examples of zeolite BCT topology the majority are based on transitional metals, and those based on rare earth metals have still remained unreported until now, which is attributed to the higher coordination number and more flexible coordination geometry of rare earth metals.18

Compounds 7 and 7′ exhibiting quite a different structure were synthesized from the same starting materials under solvo- and hydrothermal conditions, respectively. Therefore, we can draw the conclusion that the reaction conditions play an important role in the final structure of the coordination polymers.

Thermal analysis

The isomorphous frameworks lead to a similar thermal decomposition process, so we took compounds 7 and 7′ as representative examples for thermogravimetric analyses to study the stability of the two types of complexes. Their thermal behavior was investigated under a nitrogen atmosphere with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 (Fig. S1†). The TG curve of 7 showed a weight loss of 0.69% from 40 to 180 °C corresponding to the loss of an uncoordinated water molecule (calculated 0.62%). Then, the weight loss of 16.90% between 180 and 320 °C corresponds to the loss of one-and-a-half DMF molecules (calculated 16.79%). The remaining weight of 27.01% corresponds to the formation of Gd2O3 (calculated 27.79%). For compound 7′, the weight loss of 9.10% corresponding to the release of three water molecules was observed from 80 to 350 °C (calculated 9.72%). Then, the backbone started to collapse above 400 °C.

The experimental powder XRD patterns for compounds 7 and 7′ are in good agreement with the corresponding simulated ones (Fig. S2 and S3†).

IR spectra

The compounds display the similar IR spectra (Fig. S4†). For compound 7, the characteristic bands of carboxylate groups are shown in the range 1561–1686 cm−1 for asymmetric stretching and 1346–1460 cm−1 for symmetric stretching. The band at 3088, 785 and 730 cm−1 are attributed to the aromatic skeleton vibration of the benzene ring. The broad band observed at 3438 cm−1 corresponds to the O–H stretching vibrations of water molecules. Absorption at 2944 cm−1 is due to the asymmetric stretching vibration of the methyl group of the coordinated DMF molecules, which is absent for compound 7′.

Photoluminescent properties

The solid-state excitation and emission spectrum of compound 6 at room temperature is shown in Fig. 7. The compound exhibits typical emission of Eu(iii) ions upon excitation at 400 nm. The emission bands in 6 arise from 5D0 → 7FJ (J = 0–4) transition of Eu(iii) ions. The 5D0 → 7F3 transitions are too weak to be observed. The relative intensity of the 5D0 → 7F2 transition and the 5D0 → 7F1 transition is widely used as a measure of the coordination state and the site symmetry of the europium ion, while the intensity of the 5D0 → 7F1 transition (magnetic dipole transition) is insensitive to the coordination environment, the 5D0 → 7F2 transition is electric-dipole allowed, and its intensity increases as the site symmetry is lowered.19 In the spectrum of compound 6, the intensity ratio 5D0 → 7F2/5D0 → 7F1 is much higher than a typical value for a centrosymmetric Eu(iii) center, which signifies that the Eu(iii) ion is not located at the inversion center and the symmetry of the Eu(iii) ion site is low. This is in agreement with the single-crystal structure of 6. The luminescence lifetime value for the 5D0 level of the Eu(iii) complex was determined to be 0.21 ms from the luminescence decay profile at room temperature by fitting with a mono-exponential curve, indicative of the presence of one distinct emitting species (Fig. S5†).

Magnetic properties

The temperature-dependent magnetic susceptibility measurements of compounds 7 and 7′ have been performed on the polycrystalline samples in the temperature range 2–300 K at an external field of 0.1 T. The magnetic susceptibility of 7versus temperature is shown in Fig. 8, the χMT value is 16.06 cm3 mol−1 K at 300 K, which is close to the spin-only value of 15.75 cm3 mol−1 K calculated for two isolated Gd(iii) ions (S = 7/2, g = 2). Upon cooling, this value remains practically constant until 30 K and decreases further to reach a value of 15.05 cm3 mol−1 K at 2 K, indicating the presence of intramolecular ferromagnetic interactions between the Gd(iii) ions mediated by the carboxylate bridges. The magnetic susceptibility obeys the Curie–Weiss law with the Weiss constant, θ = −3.36 K, and the Curie constant, C = 16.18 cm3 mol−1 K per Gd2 unit.

The crystal structure of the compound has shown that the Gd(iii) ions are bridged by carboxylate groups to form binuclear units and then the ligands connect the dimer at a long distance. So, the magnetic interactions are negligible except for the coupling between the Gd(iii) ions within the dimer. To quantitatively evaluate the magnetic interactions in 7, the data were fitted to the expression of susceptibility for binuclear systems based on the Hamiltonian H = −2JS1S2 (S1 = S2 = 7/2).20 The best fit was obtained with a values of J = −0.020 cm−1, and g = 2.02. This result indicates that weak anti-ferromagnetic coupling interactions exist between the adjacent Gd(iii) sites.

For complex 7′, χMT is almost a constant from 8.00 cm3 mol−1 K at 300 K to 7.86 cm3 mol−1 K at 10 K, which is close to the spin-only (g = 2) value of 7.875 cm3 mol−1 K for a single Gd(iii) ion (Fig. 9). Upon further cooling, the decrease is abrupt but very small, with χMT reaching a value of 7.64 cm3 mol−1 K at 2.5 K. This feature indicates the presence of very weak anti-ferromagnetic coupling between the Gd(iii) ions. The magnetic susceptibility obeys the Curie–Weiss law with the Weiss constant, θ = −0.23 K, and the Curie constant, C = 8.03 cm3 mol−1 K.

Conclusions

In summary, thirteen novel rare earth organic frameworks exhibiting two different structural types have been synthesized under hydro- and solvothermal conditions respectively, based on the semirigid tricarboxylic acid (H3L). Compounds 1–12 are constructed from paddle-wheel building blocks forming (3,6)-connected networks with flu-3,6-C2/c topology. The teeny difference in the structure of these compounds is the result of lanthanide contraction. Compound 7′ features a 3D open framework with BCT topology, by constructing inorganic and organic four-connected building units. The structural difference implies that the reaction conditions have great influence on the structure of the compounds. Compound 6 shows Eu(iii) characteristic emission, which could be anticipated as potential fluorescent materials. Magnetic susceptibility data show that compounds 7 and 7′ have weak anti-ferromagnetic behavior. Further efforts will be focused on synthesizing novel lanthanides compounds with amazing structural motifs and properties.
