Introduction

In recent years, considerable effort has been concentrated on the design and synthesis of novel metal–organic framework (MOF) materials, due to their fascinating structural diversity and interesting properties as well as their wide potential application, such as in gas adsorption and separation, magnetism, heterogeneous catalysis, and photoluminescence.1,2 An important task is to synthesize MOFs with targeted structures and expected properties. However, investigation of the factors influencing the structures of MOFs is still a challenge because there are various factors that can affect the structure and properties of MOFs,3 including the bridging ligands, metal centers, reaction conditions, and the ratio of metal to ligand.4,5 Among these factors, the bridging ligand is one of the most important factors because its size, flexibility/rigidity, coordination site/donor atom, and the potential steric effect it poses may lead to the formation of MOFs with various structures and properties.6,7

In previous studies, tripodal ligands with an aromatic center and rigid multicarboxylate ligands have been demonstrated to be the most frequently utilized organic ligands for the preparation of MOFs due to their strong and versatile coordination behavior.8,9 In the present work, mixed ligands of 1,3,5-tris(1-imidazolyl)benzene (tib) and 2,2′-dinitro-4,4′-biphenyl dicarboxylic acid (H2dnbpdc) were chosen for the following consideration: (i) they are rigid building blocks containing imidazole and carboxylate groups, which tend to form robust MOFs; (ii) compared with 4,4′-biphenyl dicarboxylate (bpdc2−), the nitro groups of dnbpdc2− will not be involved in coordination with metal centers; albeit they may influence the structures, such as preventing interpenetration and creating porosity of the frameworks as well as affecting the properties of the resulting MOFs due to their electronic and geometric effects.10–12 In addition, Mn(ii), Zn(ii), and Cd(ii) were selected in the present study due to their strong coordination ability with N- and O-donor ligands. Furthermore, their definite and variable coordination number and geometry as well as their different ionic radii may lead to the formation of MOFs with diverse structures. Fortunately, four new MOFs [M3(tib)(dnbpdc)3(H2O)2] [M = Mn (1), Cd (2)], [Zn3(tib)2(dnbpdc)2.5(Hdnbpdc)]·4DEF (3), [Cd(tib)(dnbpdc)(H2O)]·DMF·H2O (4) (DEF = N,N-diethylformamide, DMF = N,N-dimethylformamide) were successfully synthesized and characterized by elemental analyses, single crystal and powder X-ray diffraction, and thermogravimetric analyses. It is noteworthy that 1 displayed ferromagnetic and antiferromagnetic couplings (J13 = 1.36 cm−1, J23 = −1.72 cm−1) within the trinuclear Mn(ii) subunit, as this is the first example of a Mn-carboxylate-tib-MOF that exhibits both ferromagnetic and antiferromagnetic interactions. We also performed Knoevenagel condensation reactions catalyzed by activated 3 to demonstrate its selective heterogeneous catalytic property. Furthermore, the adsorption property of 3 was also studied.

Experimental

Materials and methods

All commercially available chemicals and solvents were of reagent grade and were used as received without further purification. Ligands tib and H2dnbpdc were prepared according to procedures reported previously.13 Elemental analyses for C, H, and N were performed on an Elementar Vario MICRO Elemental analyzer at the analysis center of Nanjing University. FT-IR spectra were recorded in the range of 400–4000 cm−1 on a Bruker Vector 22 FT-IR spectrophotometer using KBr pellets. Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were carried out on a Mettler-Toledo (TGA/DSC1) thermal analyzer under nitrogen at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) analyses were performed on a Bruker D8 Advance X-ray diffractometer with Cu Kα (λ = 1.5418 Å) radiation. Sorption experiments were carried out on a Belsorp-max volumetric gas sorption instrument. Magnetic measurements were made on a Quantum Design instrument with a SQUID detector in the temperature range of 2–300 K. Magnetization measurements were recorded in the ±7 T range of an external magnetic field.

Preparation of [Mn3(tib)(dnbpdc)3(H2O)2] (1)

A mixture of tib (27.6 mg, 0.1 mmol), H2dnbpdc (33.0 mg, 0.1 mmol), and Mn(Ac)2·4H2O (49.0 mg, 0.2 mmol) in H2O (10 mL) was sealed in a 18 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel container and heated at 160 °C for 72 h, and then cooled to room temperature. Light yellow block crystals of 1 were obtained in a 65% yield (based on H2dnbpdc). Anal. Calcd for 1 (C57H34N12O26Mn3): C, 46.64; H, 2.33; N, 11.45%. Found: C, 46.45; H, 2.09; N, 11.26%. IR (KBr pellet, cm−1): 3429 (m), 1585 (s), 1530 (s), 1395 (s), 1345 (s), 1245 (m), 1068 (m), 914 (m), 838 (m), 785 (m), 674 (w), 432 (w).

Preparation of [Cd3(tib)(dnbpdc)3(H2O)2] (2)

Complex 2 was synthesized by the same procedure used for the preparation of 1, except that Cd(Ac)2·2H2O (53.4 mg, 0.2 mmol) was used instead of Mn(Ac)2·4H2O. After cooling to room temperature, yellow block crystals of 2 were obtained in an 80% yield (based on H2dnbpdc). Anal. Calcd for 2 (C57H34N12O26Cd3): C, 41.74; H, 2.09; N, 10.25%. Found: C, 41.65; H, 2.30; N, 10.15%. IR (KBr pellet, cm−1): 3424 (m), 1620 (s), 1582 (s), 1527 (s), 1401 (s), 1345 (s), 1246 (m), 1068 (m), 1010 (m), 912 (m), 837 (m), 784 (m), 672 (w), 416 (w).

Preparation of [Zn3(tib)2(dnbpdc)2.5(Hdnbpdc)]·4DEF (3)

A mixture of tib (13.8 mg, 0.05 mmol), H2dnbpdc (16.5 mg, 0.05 mmol), and ZnSO4·7H2O (28.8 mg, 0.1 mmol) in DEF/H2O (8 mL, v/v, 1 : 1) was sealed in a 18 mL glass vial and heated at 90 °C for 72 h, and then slowly cooled to room temperature. Light yellow platelet-shaped crystals of 3 were obtained in a 70% yield (based on H2dnbpdc). Anal. Calcd for 3 (C109H90N23O32Zn3): C, 53.87; H, 3.73; N, 13.26%. Found: C, 53.65; H, 3.96; N, 13.13%. IR (KBr pellet, cm−1): 3201 (s), 1717 (s), 1693 (s), 1629 (m), 1400 (s), 1386 (s), 1311 (m), 1274 (m), 1196 (s), 1113 (m), 856 (m), 717 (m), 668 (m), 418 (w).

Preparation of [Cd(tib)(dnbpdc)(H2O)]·DMF·H2O (4)

Complex 4 was synthesized by the same procedure used for the preparation of 3, except that Cd(Ac)2·2H2O (26.7 mg, 0.1 mmol) in DMF/H2O (8 mL, v/v, 1 : 1) was used instead of ZnSO4·7H2O in DEF/H2O. After cooling to room temperature, colorless platelet-shaped crystals of 4 were obtained in an 85% yield (based on H2dnbpdc). Anal. Calcd for 4 (C32H29N9O11Cd): C, 46.42; H, 3.53; N, 15.22%. Found: C, 46.26; H, 3.69; N, 15.07%. IR (KBr pellet, cm−1): 3396 (m), 1664 (s), 1614 (s), 1527 (s), 1400 (s), 1345 (s), 1253 (m), 1073 (m), 1013 (m), 928 (m), 828 (m), 788 (m), 654 (w), 420 (w).

Sample activation

Solvent-exchanged sample was prepared by immersing the as-synthesized sample 3 in methanol for 3 days to remove the nonvolatile solvates, with the solvent decanted every 8 h and fresh methanol added. The activated sample 3′ was obtained by heating the solvent-exchanged sample at 423 K under a dynamic high vacuum for 10 h.

Knoevenagel condensation reaction catalyzed by 3′

A solution of benzaldehyde (1.0 mmol) and malononitrile (1.0 mmol) (or ethyl cyanoacetate or tert-butyl cyanoacetate) in benzene (10 mL) was stirred for 5 min. Then, 3′ (0.04 mmol, 4 mol%) was added. The suspension was stirred at 90 °C for 12 h. The mixture was filtered after the reaction, and the solvent was removed by a rotary evaporator. The resulting crude product was purified by column chromatography using silica gel and n-hexane/ethyl acetate (10/1) as the eluent.

X-ray crystallography

Crystallographic data of 1–4 were collected on a Bruker Smart Apex II CCD area-detector diffractometer with graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) using the ω-scan technique. The diffraction data were integrated using the SAINT program,14 which was also used for the intensity corrections for the Lorentz and polarization effects. A semi-empirical absorption correction was applied using the SADABS program.15 The structures were solved by direct methods using SHELXS-2014 and all the non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically on F2 by the full-matrix least-squares technique using the cc.16 All the hydrogen atoms, except for those of the water molecules, were generated geometrically and refined isotropically using the riding model. The hydrogen atoms of the coordinated water molecules were found from the Fourier map directly, while those of the free water molecules were not found. Because the free solvent molecules in 3 are highly disordered and impossible to refine using conventional discrete-atom models, the SQUEEZE subroutine of the PLATON software suite17 was applied to remove the scattering from the highly disordered solvent molecules. The formula of 3 was calculated based on volume/count_electron analysis, TGA, and elemental analysis. The reported refinements are of the guest-free structures obtained by the SQUEEZE subroutine, and the results are attached to the CIF file. The atoms C77, C78, C79, O27, O28, and N19 in 3 are disordered into two positions each with a site occupancy of 0.5. The details of the crystal parameters, data collection, and refinements for 1–4 are listed in Table 1, while selected bond lengths and angles are given in Table S1 (ESI†) and the hydrogen bonding data for 4 are provided in Table S2 (ESI†).

Results and discussion

Crystal structure description

As shown in Table 1, X-ray crystallographic analysis revealed that 1 and 2 are isomorphous with the same monoclinic P21/n space group, while 3 crystallizes in the monoclinic crystal system with the space group P21/c, and 4 crystallizes in the triclinic P space group. Therefore, the structures of 1, 3, and 4 are described here in detail.

Crystal structure of [Mn3(tib)(dnbpdc)3(H2O)2] (1)

As shown in Fig. 1a, Mn1, Mn2, and Mn3 are all six-coordinated with a distorted octahedral coordination geometry, in which Mn1 is coordinated by six carboxylate oxygen atoms (O1, O2, O10, O11C, O12C, and O19D) from four different dnbpdc2− ligands; while both Mn2 and Mn3 are coordinated by four carboxylate oxygen atoms (O4A, O9, O18, and O20E for Mn2; O3A, O10, O12C, and O17 for Mn3) from four different dnbpdc2− ligands, one imidazole nitrogen atom (N7 for Mn2; N9B for Mn3) from tib ligand, and one coordinated water molecule (O1W for Mn2; O2W for Mn3). It can be seen that Mn1, Mn2, and Mn3 are connected together through carboxylate groups to form a trinuclear cluster, which can be treated as a secondary building unit (SBU) (Fig. 1b). It is notable that the dnbpdc2− ligands in 1 present three different coordination modes, as illustrated in Scheme 1a–c: one is the (κ1–κ2–κ1)–(κ2)–μ4-dnbpdc tetradentate coordination mode to connect four Mn(ii) atoms (Scheme 1a), another one acts as a μ3 bridging ligand to connect three Mn(ii) atoms, with its carboxylate groups adopting the (κ1–κ1)–(κ1)–μ3-dnbpdc coordination mode (Scheme 1b), and the third one is a tridentate linkage ligand to connect three Mn(ii) atoms, with its carboxylate groups adopting the (κ1–κ1)–(κ2)–μ3-dnbpdc coordination mode (Scheme 1c). In addition, one of the three imidazole groups of the tib ligand does not take part in the coordination, and thus each tib ligand acts as a bidentate linker to connect two different Mn(ii). Accordingly, the three dnbpdc2− ligands with μ3- and μ4-bridging modes link the SBUs to form a three-dimensional (3D) framework (Fig. 1c), while the bidentate tib ligands fill in the 3D framework of 1 (Fig. 1d). A better insight into the nature of this intricate architecture can be achieved by application of the topological approach. As described above, the SBUs can be viewed as nodes and the ligands as spacers (Fig. S1, ESI†), thereby 1 exhibits an unusual 8-connected uninodal 3D framework with a point symbol of {33·415·59·6} topology (Fig. 1d).

Crystal structure of [Zn3(tib)2(dnbpdc)2.5(Hdnbpdc)]·4DEF (3)

There are three crystallographically independent Zn(ii) atoms in the asymmetric unit of 3 (Fig. 2a). Zn1 is five-coordinated with a trigonal bipyramidal coordination geometry by three carboxylate oxygen atoms (O22, O23, and O24C) from two different dnbpdc2− ligands and two imidazole nitrogen atoms (N1, N7) from two different tib ligands, while Zn2 and Zn3 are both six-coordinated with a distorted octahedral coordination geometry. Zn2 is coordinated by four carboxylate oxygen atoms (O5, O6, O7A, and O8A) from two different dnbpdc2− ligands and two imidazole nitrogen atoms (N3D, N11) from two different tib ligands, while Zn3 is also coordinated by four carboxylate oxygen atoms (O13, O14, O20, and O21) from two different dnbpdc2− ligands and two imidazole nitrogen atoms (N5, N9) from two different tib ligands. Each tib ligand links three Zn(ii) atoms to form an infinite one-dimensional (1D) chain in 3 (Fig. 2b). It is notable that dnbpdc2− presents three different coordination modes, as illustrated in Scheme 1d–f: one is the (κ1)–(κ2)–μ2-dnbpdc bidentate coordination mode to connect two Zn(ii) atoms (Scheme 1d), another one acts as a bidentate bridging ligand to connect two Zn(ii) atoms using its carboxylate groups (Scheme 1e), and the third one acts as a monodentate linkage ligand to connect one Zn(ii) atom with its carboxylate group (Scheme 1f). Tridentate tib and three kinds of dnbpdc2− ligands are linked together by Zn(ii) atoms to form the 3D framework of 3 (Fig. 2c), with a 1D channel along the b axis (Fig. 2c). The high-solvent accessible volume is 3849 Å3 out of the 11101.8 Å3 unit cell volume (34.7% of the total crystal volume), as calculated by PLATON. To better understand the 3D structure of 3, a topological analysis was carried out. As shown in Fig. S2, ESI,† Zn1, Zn2, Zn3, tib, and dnbpdc2− ligands can be considered as four-, four-, three-, three-, and two-connectors, respectively. According to the simplification principle,18 the resulting structure of 3 is a (3,3,4,4)-connected 4-nodal 3D framework with a point symbol of {4·6·8}2{4·62}{4·85}{62·83·10} (Fig. 2d).

Crystal structure of [Cd(tib)(dnbpdc)(H2O)]·DMF·H2O (4)

As shown in Fig. 3a, each Cd(ii) is six-coordinated by three imidazole nitrogen atoms, two carboxylate oxygen atoms, and one coordinated water molecule. Each tib ligand links three Cd(ii) atoms using its three imidazole groups to form an infinite 1D chain (Fig. 3b). It is noteworthy that dnbpdc2− acts as terminal ligand alternately coordinating at the up and down positions of the 1D chain (Fig. 3b) in the monodentate linkage mode (Scheme 1f). Furthermore, there are C–H⋯O hydrogen bonding interactions between the neighboring 1D chains to generate a 3D supramolecular framework of 4 (Fig. 3c, Table S2, ESI†).

Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA)

Complexes 1–4 are air stable, and their thermal stability was investigated in the temperature range of 30–800 °C by TG measurements (Fig. S3, ESI†). The TG curve of 1 displays a weight loss of 2.2% between 60–240 °C, indicating the exclusion of coordinated water molecules (calc. 2.5 wt%). The framework is thermally stable to 350 °C. The TG curve of 2 is similar to that of 1 due to their same framework structure. The TG curve of 3 shows a weight loss of 18.0% from 30 to 320 °C, corresponding to the loss of free DEF molecules (calc. 17.5 wt%), and the residue is stable up to about 400 °C. The TG results of the samples after the solvent exchange and vacuum activation of 3 (Fig. S4, ESI†) confirm the removal of solvent molecules in the framework of 3. For 4, a weight loss of 13.5% was observed in the temperature range of 90–305 °C, which corresponds to the loss of water and DMF molecules (calc. 13.2 wt%), and a further weight loss was observed at about 360 °C, owing to the collapse of the framework of 4.

Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) analysis

The pure phase of the synthesized compounds 1–4 was confirmed by powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) measurements, and the results are shown in Fig. S5 (ESI†). Each PXRD pattern of the as-synthesized samples was consistent with the simulated one, indicating the phase purity of the bulk samples. Furthermore, Fig. S5c (ESI†) shows the PXRD patterns of 3 under different conditions, indicating that the crystalline structure of 3 is even preserved after the removal of solvent molecules and catalytic reaction (vide post).

Magnetic property of 1

There are trinuclear Mn(ii) SBUs in 1 and thus magnetic interactions between the Mn(ii) centers bridged by the carboxylate oxygen atoms and carboxylate groups can be expected. Therefore, magnetic measurements of 1 were carried out from 300 to 2 K with an applied magnetic field of 2000 Oe, and the temperature dependence of χMT and χM is shown in Fig. 4a and b, respectively. The magnetic interactions between the adjacent SBUs can be ignored since the center-to-center distances between two SBUs are long; for example, 9.33, 9.56, and 11.68 Å separated by tib and 13.92 Å separated by dnbpdc2− in 1. Thus, only the interactions within the SBU are considered. The value of χMT at 300 K is 13.16 cm3 mol−1 K, which is well in agreement with the expected value for the three isolated Mn(ii) ions with S = 5/2 and g = 2.0 (4.375 cm3 mol−1 K per Mn(ii) ion), indicating all the Mn(ii) ions are in the high-spin state. The χMT is almost invariable down to 50 K, and then decreases smoothly upon cooling to reach a quasi plateau in the temperature range of 50–40 K (χMT = 11.30 cm3 mol−1 K), and then gradually decreases with further cooling to reach 4.04 cm3 mol−1 K at 2.0 K (Fig. 4a). These features are interpreted as follows: the spin–orbit coupling effect of the high-spin Mn(ii) ion (depopulation of the higher energy Kramers doublets) and the zero field splitting of the two Mn(ii) ions account for the decrease of χMT in the high-temperature range, whereas the quasi plateau is due to the competition between antiferromagnetic coupling and an intramolecular ferromagnetic interaction between the Mn(ii) ions.19 In 1, there are three magnetic exchange pathways: (1) through the bridging oxygen atoms, where the Mn1–Mn3 separation is 3.51 Å; (2) through the bridging carboxylate groups, where the Mn2–Mn3 separation is 3.87 Å; (3) through the bridging oxygen atom and carboxylate group, where the Mn1–Mn2 separation is 5.79 Å (Fig. 1b). The best fit of the magnetic susceptibility data was obtained from the use of PHI software,20 where the best least-squares fitting of the theoretical equations to the experimentally magnetic data of 1 led to values of J13 = 1.36 cm−1, J23 = −1.72 cm−1, g1 = g2 = g3 = 2.02, zJ′ = −0.05 cm−1, and R = 4.2 × 10−3 [agreement factor, defined as ∑[(χMT)obsd − (χMT)calcd]2/∑(χMT)obsd2]. The best fit is shown in Fig. 4 as a solid line. The positive value for J13 confirms the ferromagnetic coupling between adjacent Mn1 and Mn3 ions through the bridging oxygen atoms with a small Mn–O–Mn angle of 98.45°, which generally features ferromagnetic coupling.21 Whereas the negative value for J23 confirmed the consistently antiferromagnetic coupling between adjacent Mn2 and Mn3 ions through the bridging carboxylate groups.22 As for the adjacent Mn1 and Mn3 ions, only very weak coupling in the order of 10−4 was observed and thus, this can be ignored.

Catalytic property of 3

From the crystal structural analysis, it was found that the porous structure of 3 provides a guest-accessible functional organic site (FOS), namely through the CO groups on the surface of the channels (Fig. S6, ESI†), which can act as electron donors.23 This prompted us to study its base catalytic property, and thus a Knoevenagel condensation reaction catalyzed by the activated sample 3 (3′) was performed. The results of the reactions of benzaldehyde with each of the cyano compounds of malononitrile, ethyl cyanoacetate, and tert-butyl cyanoacetate catalyzed by 3′ are shown in Fig. 5. It can be seen that the malononitrile was a good substrate, producing 84.5% conversion of the adduct (Fig. S7, ESI†), but the other substrates reacted negligibly, with yields of 40.2% and 8.1%, respectively (Fig. 5). The results can be explained by the different sizes of the substrates: malononitrile = 4.5 × 6.9 Å2, ethyl cyanoacetate = 4.5 × 10.3 Å2, and tert-butyl cyanoacetate = 5.8 × 10.3 Å2, since the pore size of 3′ is 4.8 × 7.1 Å2. Furthermore, the interaction of malononitrile with 3′ can be seen from the IR spectra (Fig. S8, ESI†).24 The results suggest that such guest-selective reaction occurs in the channels of 3′. In addition, 3′ promoted the reaction with a good yield compared with the tib, H2dnbpdc, and Zn(NO3)2 (Fig. S9, ESI†), which means that the active catalytic species is the CO groups on the surface of the channels, but not the imidazole, nitro, or Zn2+.25 Furthermore, 1, 2, and 4 did not show such catalytic activity due to their non (1 and 2) or small (4) channels. Therefore, 3′ containing effective FOS was demonstrated to exhibit selective catalytic activity.26

The heterogeneity and recyclability of 3′ in the Knoevenagel condensation of benzaldehyde and malononitrile were examined. After the removal of 3′ from the reaction system, the reaction did not proceed further, demonstrating the catalytic activity of 3′. To test the reuse of the catalyst, 3′ was isolated by filtration and then rinsed and soaked in benzene. This procedure was repeated three times every day prior to the next catalytic run to remove substrates or products that might block the pores.27 The second and third runs gave decreased conversion of 78% and 65%, respectively (Fig. S10, ESI†). The decreased catalytic activity implies the lower stability of the framework toward the reaction, which was also confirmed by the PXRD data (Fig. S5c, ESI†).

Sorption property of 3

The porosity of activated 3 was further studied by adsorption measurements of N2 at 77 K, CO2 at 273 K, and MeOH and EtOH at 298 K and the results are shown in Fig. 6 and 7. The N2 sorption exhibited a type I isotherm, which is a characteristic of microporous materials. On the basis of the N2 adsorption isotherm, the BET surface area of 3′ was estimated to be 504 cm2 g−1 and the Langmuir surface area was 683 cm2 g−1 (Fig. S11, ESI†). The pore volume obtained from the sorption isotherm was 0.25 cm3 g−1, which is close to the value expected from the crystallographic data (0.28 cm3 g−1). The pore size distributions derived from the N2 isotherms by the Horvath–Kawazoe (HK) method suggested that the average pore size for 3′ was 5.4 Å in diameter (Fig. S12, ESI†). The CO2 adsorption capacity was 63.03 cm3 g−1 (123.91 mg g−1) at 273 K and 0.98 atm. The uptake of MeOH was 130.41 cm3 g−1 (186.33 mg g−1) at 0.99 atm, corresponding to 13.44 MeOH molecules per formula unit of 3′. The desorption profile of MeOH did not retrace the adsorption one with a large hysteresis loop, which may be ascribed to the strong adsorbate–adsorbent interactions.28 In contrast, no hysteresis loop was observed in the EtOH adsorption isotherms (Fig. 7). The final uptake value of EtOH was 61 cm3 g−1 (124.34 mg g−1) at 0.99 atm, corresponding to 6.24 EtOH molecules per formula unit of 3′.

Conclusions

In summary, in this work we successfully synthesized four new metal–organic frameworks [M3(tib)(dnbpdc)3(H2O)2] [M = Mn (1), Cd (2)], [Zn3(tib)2(dnbpdc)2.5(Hdnbpdc)]·4DEF (3), and [Cd(tib)(dnbpdc)(H2O)]·DMF·H2O (4) by using mixed organic ligands. They displayed diverse structures and topologies. Furthermore, it is interesting that 1 displayed ferromagnetic and antiferromagnetic interactions within the trinuclear Mn(ii) subunit. 3 demonstrated gas, methanol, and ethanol adsorption and heterogeneous catalytic activity on the Knoevenagel condensation reaction. The results of this work may help researchers in the rational design and synthesis of multifunctional MOFs.
