Introduction

There is growing interest in the crystal engineering of polymeric compounds such as coordination polymers (CPs) or metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) with 1D, 2D, and 3D structures not only for their potential applications in several fields but also for their fascinating architectures and topologies.1 The properties of these compounds are mainly dependent on the nature of the metal centres and the organic linkers1 that determines the type and strength of interactions between them in the solid state. One-dimensional (1D) coordination polymers2 are the simplest topological type of these CPs. The chemistry of 1D compounds has attracted much attention because of their potential applications in nanotechnology.3 Especially, the bifunctional materials that combine magnetism and electrical conductivity are particularly interesting and several systems with these properties have been studied.4 A singular type of 1D compounds are the so-called MMX polymers.

These polymers can be constructed using dimetallic units with a paddlewheel structure of the type [M2(μ-L-L)4] (M = transition metal; L-L = mononegative three-atom bridging ligand) and halide ions. By linking the dimetallic units through axial halide ligands one-dimensional chains are obtained (Fig. 1). These complexes are usually semiconductors but some of them exhibit metallic conduction. The first observation of metallic conductivity in MMX compounds was made by Bellitto and colleagues5 for the complex [Pt2(MeCS2)4I], and to date, the family of compounds [M2(RCS2)4I] (M = Ni, Pt; R = alkyl chain group) remain the most studied one from the electrical point of view.6 Interestingly, many other transition metals also form dinuclear paddlewheel compounds with different metal–metal bond orders (from 1/2 to 4) and usually present a strong electron delocalization along the M–M bond.7 In these complexes the most common oxidation states for the M2n+ unit correspond to n = 4, 5 and 6 although some examples of n = 3 and 7 have also been described.7

The ruthenium complexes [Ru2X(μ-L-L)4] (L-L = monoanionic bidentate ligand) constitute a family of MMX-chain compounds which have been intensively studied due to their rich variety of electronic structures and magnetic characteristics. These formally mixed valence Ru25+ compounds (in fact they are averaged compounds) show a special stability in this oxidation state. Although the halotetracarboxylatodiruthenium complexes have been intensively studied7,8 similar amidato complexes have been less studied.9–12 Recently, Ren and colleagues10 have reported a useful procedure (that requires the use of a micro-Soxhlet extraction apparatus) for the preparation of soluble chloridotetraamidatodiruthenium complexes, and more recently, we have described other experimental methods using solvothermal or microwave activation.9a The solvothermal activation is very useful to obtain single crystals of these usually very insoluble products.

Conductivity measurements in one-dimensional MMX paddlewheel complexes, except in nickel and platinum dithiocarboxylato derivatives, are actually scarce in the literature13 and, in particular, conductivity measurements in single crystals of amidatodiruthenium compounds are unknown. The temperature dependence of the electric resistivity of an alternating chain of paddlewheel [Ru2(μ-O2CEt)4]+ units and [Pt(mnt)2]− (mnt2− = maleonitriledithiolate) fragments has been studied.14

Here we report the synthesis and characterization of a new series of amidato complexes formulated as [Ru2X(μ-NHOCC6H4-R)4]n [X = Br, R = o-Me (1), m-Me (2), p-Me (3); X = I, R = o-Me (4), m-Me (5), p-Me (6)]. This large family has been prepared using solvothermal or microwave activation procedures. The properties of these compounds can be easily tuned by changing the bridging halide ligand and the preparation of single crystals enables us to discuss their properties in detail on the basis of their structures. The solvothermal synthesis has allowed the isolation of crystalline solids in all cases and the crystal structure determination has been carried out for all of them. The magnetic properties have also been studied for the six compounds. The influence of the halide ligand on the electrical properties of complexes 1, 4, and on the recently published9a [Ru2Cl(μ-NHOCC6H4-o-Me)4]n (7) compound is also described.

Results and discussions

A. Synthetic aspects

As is known, the synthesis of amidato complexes of ruthenium using conventional activation methods requires melted ligand,9c,11,12 and 2-methoxyethanol9b or toluene10 as solvents. However, we have shown that the solvothermal or microwave activation is very useful in the synthesis of this type of complex and is especially useful to obtain single crystals.9a Thus, the bromide complexes [Ru2Br(μ-NHOCC6H4-R)4]n [R = o-Me (1), m-Me (2), p-Me (3)] have been prepared by reaction of [Ru2Br(μ-O2CMe)4] and the corresponding toluamide in the presence of triethylamine and potassium bromide. The preparation of the iododerivatives [Ru2I(μ-NHOCC6H4-R)4]n [R = o-Me (4), m-Me (5), p-Me (6)] have been carried out in a similar way using [Ru2I(μ-O2CMe)4] and potassium iodide. In these reactions we have only used ethanol or methanol as solvents following the principles of Green Chemistry. In all the reactions performed under microwave activation, the compounds were obtained as single-phase microcrystalline powders. As we have previously observed, solvothermal synthesis allows easier preparation of higher quality single crystals and all the structures of these six compounds have been determined. Compounds 1–6 are the first bromo- and iodo-derivatives of tetraamidato complexes of ruthenium.

B. Crystal structures

The crystal structure of complexes 1–6 have been solved by single crystal X-ray methods. All complexes adopt a paddlewheel arrangement with the two ruthenium atoms connected by four bridging amidate ligands. The Ru–Ru distances are: 2.298(1) Å (in 1), 2.297(3) and 2.290(2) Å (in 2), 2.3000(10) Å (in 3), 2.3001(11), 2.3106(12), 2.3097(12) and 2.3079(10) Å (in 4), 2.301(2) and 2.302(3) Å (in 5), and 2.290(3) Å (in 6). The Ru–Ru distances in 1–6 are similar to the ones found in the previously reported neutral9a,10,11b,c [Ru2Cl(μ-NHOCR)4]n and cationic complexes9b,c,15 [Ru2(μ-NHOCR)4]+. Representative bond lengths and angles for compounds 1–6 are presented in Table 1. As expected, the Ru–Ru distance shows a very small dependence on the nature of the amidate ligand and no significant differences are also observed with the change of halide ligand.

Given the asymmetry of the amidate bridging ligands in the polymeric [Ru2X(μ-NHOCR)4] compounds, there are four possible linkage isomers depending on the relative coordination of the ligands, with the following possible coordination environments of the ruthenium atoms: RuN4–RuO4 (4,0 disposition), RuN3O–RuO3N (3,1 disposition), cis-RuN2O2 (cis-2,2 disposition) and trans-RuN2O2 (trans-2,2 disposition). Hitherto all structures of tetraamidatodiruthenium complexes described in the literature and five of the six compounds prepared in this work show a cis-RuN2O2 environment. The only exception is the chloride compound 7 that shows the first trans-RuN2O2 disposition of the amidate ligands.9a The analogous bromide complex 1, which contains the o-methylbenzamidate ligand, probably shows a trans-RuN2O2 disposition (Fig. 2) although the disorder in the N and O positions does not allow to confirm this assumption. In contrast, the closely related iodide compound 4 containing the same amidate ligand shows clearly the more habitual cis-RuN2O2 environment (Fig. 3), also observed in complexes 2, 3, 5 and 6 (Fig. S1–S4 in the ESI†). The bromide complex 1 shows a linear arrangement of the paddlewheel units in the resulting 1D coordination polymer. In the chloride compound 7, which displays also a linear disposition of the chains, this arrangement was attributed to the interaction between the hydrogen atom attached to the nitrogen coordinated to the metal and the methyl substituent of the ring located in the ortho-position.9a This interaction prompts the ring to adopt a perpendicular orientation with respect to the plane defined by the two metal atoms and the amidate group, favouring the linear disposition of the paddlewheel units. In complex 1 the aromatic ring of the amidate groups adopts the same perpendicular disposition. This arrangement avoids the steric hindrance between the diruthenium units increased by the presence of the methyl substituent in the ortho-position leading to the formation of linear chains with a Ru–Br–Ru angle of 180.0° (Fig. 2). In contrast, in complexes 2, 3 and 5 which have the methyl substituent in meta- and para-positions the phenyl rings are parallel to the Ru–Ru–N–C–O plane favoring the formation of zigzag chains with Ru–X–Ru angles varying from 110.16(2) to 115.39(4)° (Table 1 and Fig. S1–S4 in the ESI†). In these compounds the Ru–X–Ru angle decreases as the size of the X− ligand increases. Table 2 presents the Ru–X–Ru angles of a series of known chloro, bromo and iodo complexes of the type [Ru2X(μ-O2CR)4] and [Ru2X(μ-NHOCR)4] with the same carboxylate or amidate ligand. These values show that a greater size of the axial halide ligand determines a larger distance between the diruthenium units allowing a more acute Ru–X–Ru angle. In the iodide derivative 4 in spite of the presence of an ortho-methyl substituent the distance between the diruthenium units allows the formation of zigzag chains in contrast with the analogous chloride and bromide derivatives.

C. Electrical conductivity measurements

The electrical conductivity measurements in the series [Ru2X(μ-NHOCC6H4-o-Me)4]n [X = Cl (7), Br (1) and I (4)] show that the three samples present very high room temperature resistance values (above our detection limit of 5 × 1011 Ω) and, accordingly, we have measured them in the temperature range 300–400 K. These measurements show that the three compounds present semiconducting behaviours with conductivity values at 400 K in the range 0.3–3.0 × 10−8 S cm−1 for the Cl derivative (7), 7–18 × 10−8 S cm−1 for the Br derivative (1) and 27–68 × 10−8 S cm−1 for the I derivative (4) with average values of 1.4 × 10−8, 12.8 × 10−8 and 46.8 × 10−8 S cm−1, respectively (Fig. 4). These values are in agreement with the expected order (Cl < Br < I) from orbital overlap considerations and also from previous observations in related halogen-bridged series,13a showing that the conductivity values increase as the orbital overlap increases since it favours the electron delocalization.

The activation energies (obtained from the Arrhenius plots in the temperature range where the sample is conducting enough to be measured with accuracy) also increase when passing from Cl to Br and I, showing average values of ca. 1.4, 1.6 and 1.8 eV for the Cl, Br and I derivatives, respectively (inset in Fig. 4). This order parallels the one observed in the conductivity values and for the Cl and Br derivatives (where the Ru–X–Ru bond angle is 180°) this can be explained by the fact that the shorter the inter-dimer distance (as in the Cl derivative), the wider the bandwidth and, therefore, the narrower the band gap. The problem appears with the I derivative, where the activation energy should be smaller since the inter-dimer distance is much shorter (4.724 Å, compared to 5.151 Å in the Cl and 5.325 Å in the Br derivatives). This result has to be attributed to the smaller Ru–X–Ru bond angle observed in the I derivative (110.67° and 110.16° compared to 180° in the Cl and Br derivatives) and confirms, as expected, that the Ru–X–Ru angle has to play an important role in determining the activation energy.

In summary, the electrical conductivity increases as the size of the halogen orbital increases (Cl < Br < I) whereas the band gap increases (Cl < Br < I) as the interdimer distance increases, except in the I derivative where the smaller Ru–X–Ru bond angle must also play an important role in reducing the bandwidth (and, therefore, increasing the band gap).

D. Spectroscopic properties

The IR spectra of compounds 1–6 show very intense antisymmetric and symmetric stretching CON bands (1540–1400 cm−1) due to the bridging equatorial amidate ligands. A low intense NH stretching vibration appears in all cases, in the 3400–3300 cm−1 region. The pattern of all spectra is very similar, indicating that the basic unit of two ruthenium atoms supported by four bridging amidate ligands is maintained.

The diffuse reflectance electronic spectra of all the complexes were acquired (Table S1 and Fig. S5 and S6 in the ESI†). The absorption band observed in the range 289–342 nm could be assigned to a LMCT σ(axial ligand) → σ*(Ru2) transition, in agreement with those described21 for the 307 nm band observed in the diiodo anion [Ru2I2(μ-O2CPr)4]−. The absorption band observed in the range 365–401 nm could be assigned to a ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT) π(O/N) → π*(Ru2) transition according to the TDDFT-PCM calculations described by Cukiernik and coworkers.22 The band observed between 471 and 492 nm is mainly due to a π(RuO/N,Ru2) → π*(Ru2) transition9a,17,23 although it could also have some σ → σ* character.21 The observed shoulder at 555 and 550 nm for complexes 1 and 4, respectively, could be assigned to a σ(Ru-axial ligand) → π*(Ru2) transition.7,17,21 A close band at 681 nm assigned to the mixed transition: π → π* (52%), σ → σ* (33%), and δ → δ* (11%) has been predicted, but the low extinction coefficient of this band, which has not been observed previously,22 makes more probable the former assignation. Additionally, all the complexes show a band in the NIR region, in the range 921–1007 nm, which can be assigned to the δ(Ru2) → δ*(Ru2) transition.9a,17,21,23 Finally, all compounds show a band in the range 267–278 nm whose assignment is difficult since it can be composed of several overlapping transitions, according to Cukiernik and coworkers.22

E. Magnetic properties

The magnetic moments at room temperature of compounds 1–6 range from 3.84 to 4.38 μB. These values are consistent with the presence of three unpaired electrons per dimer unit and support an electronic configuration of σ2π4δ2(π*δ*)3 as proposed by Norman et al.24 for tetracarboxylatodiruthenium compounds. These magnetic moments are in accordance with those observed in amidato9–12 and carboxylato7,8 complexes containing the Ru25+ unit. The representation of the magnetic molar susceptibility versus temperature continuously increases with decreasing temperature. In all cases the magnetic moment exhibits a decrease, mainly at very low temperatures. This behaviour has been observed in other zigzag polymeric Ru25+ compounds and has been ascribed to a large zero-field splitting (ZFS) and a weak degree of antiferromagnetic coupling between the dimetallic units. The model of Cukiernik et al.25 has been successfully used to fit the magnetic data of similar diruthenium complexes.9a,b,17 This model takes into account the existence of zero field splitting (D), a weak antiferromagnetic coupling (zJ), a temperature independent paramagnetism (TIP) and a paramagnetic impurity (P) (eqn (1)). The zero field splitting effect on the susceptibility can be quantified by considering the Hamiltonian HD = S·D·S, as described by O'Connor.26 The perturbation of a weak antiferromagnetic coupling over the zero field splitting system can be considered by using the molecular field approximation.26 Thus, for an S = 3/2 spin system the magnetic susceptibility can be expressed as: χ′ = χ ′M/[1 − (2zJ/Ng2β2)χ ′M] where χ ′M = χM + TIP and χM = (χ|| + 2χ⊥)/3 χ|| = (Ng2β2/kT) (1 + 9e−2D/kT)/4(1 + e−2D/kT) χ⊥ = (Ng2β2/kT) [4 + (3kT/D)(1 − e−2D/kT)]/4(1 + e−2D/kT)

Finally, the consideration of the paramagnetic impurity (P) leads to the expression χ ′mol = [(1 − P)χ′] + [P Ngmo2β2/4kT] (1)

Very good agreement between the experimental and calculated curves of the magnetic susceptibility and the magnetic moment has been observed for compounds 2–6. Fig. 5 shows the experimental and calculated curves for complex 2 using this model (similar fits are obtained for complex 3–6, Fig. S7–S10 in the ESI†). The calculated magnetic parameters (g, D, zJ, TIP and P) obtained in these fits are collected in Table 3 together with σ2 value, which indicates the quality of the fits. These complexes show large zero-field splitting, with D values ranging from 41.10 to 60.10 cm−1, similar to those found for analogous compounds.7,8,9a,b,17,25,27,28 The calculated zJ values vary from 0.00 to −3.92 cm−1. These low values are in accordance8,9a,17,25,27 with the presence of zigzag chains, where the antiferromagnetic interaction is propagated through the bridging halide atoms. It has been proposed25 that the interdimer exchange in chlorotetracarboxylatodiruthenium complexes decreases with decreasing interdimer angle and becomes zero for an angle of 125°. In the halotetraamidatodiruthenium compounds 1–7 together with the other known examples of [Ru2X(μ-L-L)4] chain compounds (X = halide, μ-L-L = caboxylate or amidate) (Table 4) we can establish an almost linear correlation between the magnetic coupling (zJ) and the two main parameters expected to determine the magnetic coupling: the Ru–X–Ru bond angle and the Ru–X bond distance. Thus, both parameters separately show a slight linear correlation (the strength of the magnetic coupling increases as the Ru–X–Ru angle increases, Fig. S14, and as the Ru–X length decreases, Fig. S16†). An even better correlation can be established if we consider the ratio between the Ru–X length and the Ru–X–Ru angle (Fig. 6). This correlation is linear and shows that the strength of the magnetic coupling increases as the Ru–X/Ru–X–Ru ratio decreases (short length and large angle). As expected, the D parameter is not correlated with any of these structural parameters or with their ratio (Fig. S13, S15 and S17†). Note that compound 20 shows a worse correlation because its D parameter is quite low and, therefore, zJ may be overestimated.

Compound 1 deserves special consideration since it is a linear 1D polymer and does not show a maximum in the plot of the magnetic susceptibility vs. temperature (Fig. 7), as has been previously observed in all the linear halotetracarboxylato derivatives29 and in the amidate compound 7, which also shows a linear disposition of the dimetallic units.9a Compound 1 is the first example of a linear Ru2X(μ-L-L)4 (X = halide, μ-L-L = caboxylate or amidate) showing a continuous increase of the molar susceptibility with decreasing temperature. Previous studies9a,29 indicate that linear compounds have a stronger antiferromagnetic coupling than the compounds with zigzag chains, giving rise to a maximum in the magnetic susceptibility curve. A model considering zero field splitting and a large antiferromagnetic coupling has been described29b to fit the magnetic data of these linear compounds.

Due to the absence of maximum in the magnetic susceptibility plot towards temperature in complex 1 the antiferromagnetic interactions must be very weak and therefore the magnetic data have been fitted using the model of Cukiernik.25 Two types of paramagnetic impurities have been considered: (a) a mononuclear complex of Ru(iii), as is usual in similar fits, and (b) one linear polymorph of complex 1 with fixed g, D, zJ and TIP values taken from a similar compound. In the case (a), very good agreement between experimental and calculated magnetic data has been obtained. Table 3 shows the magnetic parameters obtained in this fit. The paramagnetic impurity value (0.09%) calculated from this option is similar to that observed in other zigzag chlorotetraamidato derivatives.9a

In the case (b), the g, D, zJ and TIP values of the paramagnetic impurity were taken from those calculated for compound 7. In this case (Fig. S11 in the ESI†) the quality of the fit (σ2 = 4.33 × 10−4) is similar to the one obtained in case (a) but a higher value of paramagnetic impurity (P = 31.91%) was obtained. Since the crystal structure of complex 1 shows a linear arrangement this fit suggests the presence of a mixture of polymorphs in the crystalline synthesized compound. Nevertheless, powder X ray diffraction studies carried on the whole compound indicate the presence of only one crystalline phase, whose diffractogram is completely coincident with the calculated one from the crystal structure of the linear compound (Fig. S12 in the ESI†). The fit with this model but considering a linear chain as the main product and a zigzag chain as the paramagnetic impurity leads to unrealistic parameters.

In view of these results, we can conclude that compound 1 is the first example of linear Ru2X(μ-L-L)4 (X = halide, μ-L-L = caboxylate or amidate) showing very weak antiferromagnetic interactions between dimetallic units (zJ = −4.13 cm−1).

Conclusions

Solvothermal and microwave synthesis has allowed to isolate the first bromo and iodo tetraamidatodiruthenium compounds of the type [Ru2X(μ-NHOCC6H4-R)4]n. The reaction conditions in the solvothermal synthesis have permitted to isolate single crystal of these extremely insoluble compounds and the crystal structures of all of them have been solved. Complex 1 shows a Ru–Br–Ru angle of 180° whereas complexes 2–6 display zigzag chains. When the size of the halide ligand increases, the higher separation of the dimer units favors the formation of zigzag chains decreasing the Ru–X–Ru angle. Complex 1 does not show a maximum in the magnetic susceptibility curve towards temperature in contrast with the previous compounds with a linear chain arrangement. Conductivity measurements in the series [Ru2X(μ-NHOCC6H4-o-Me)4]n, X = Cl (7), Br (1) and I (4) show that the three compounds are semiconductors with conductivity values at 400 K that increase in the order Cl < Br < I in agreement with the expected order from orbital overlap considerations. The activation energies of the three compounds follow a similar order (Cl < Br < I) also in agreement with the larger inter-dimer distance as X increases, resulting in narrower bands and, therefore, in larger band gaps.

Experimental section

General considerations

All reactants and solvents were used as received. The precursors [Ru2Cl(μ-O2CMe)4], [Ru2Br(μ-O2CMe)4], and [Ru2I(μ-O2CMe)4] were synthesized according to published general procedures.33 Compound 7 was prepared by a previously reported procedure.9a Microwave reactions were carried out in an ETHOS ONE microwave oven using TFM Teflon closed vessels equipped with a temperature sensor and pressure control. Solvothermal syntheses were carried out in a Memmert Universal Oven UFE 400 using Teflon-lined stainless steel autoclaves. FT-IR spectra were recorded in a Perkin Elmer Spectrum 100 with a universal ATR accessory with a spectral range of 4000–650 cm−1. Electronic spectra of the complexes in the solid state were acquired on a Cary 5G spectrophotometer equipped with a Praying Mantis accessory for diffuse reflectance measurements. The reflectance data were converted by the instrument software to the F(R∞) values according to the Kubelka–Munk theory. Elemental analyses were done by the Microanalytical Service of the Universidad Complutense de Madrid. Fast Atom Bombardment (FAB) mass spectra were recorded by the Research Interdepartmental Service (SIdI) of Universidad Autónoma de Madrid using a VG AutoSpec mass spectrometer and m-nitrobenzyl alcohol (m-NBA) as the matrix. Nominal molecular masses and the isotopic distribution of all peaks were calculated using the MASAS34 computer program, using a polynomial expansion based on natural abundances of the isotopes. The variable-temperature magnetic susceptibility data were measured on a Quantum Design MPMSXL SQUID (Superconducting Quantum Interference Device) susceptometer at 0.5 T over a temperature range of 2–300 K. All data were corrected for the diamagnetic contribution of the samples and for the signal of the sample holder. The molar diamagnetic corrections for the complexes were calculated on the basis of Pascal's constants.

X-Ray data collection and structure refinement

Data collection for all compounds was carried out at room temperature on a Bruker Smart CCD diffractometer using graphite-monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) operating at 50 kV and 20 mA for 2, 3 and 5, 50 kV and 25 mA for 1 and 4 and 50 kV and 35 mA for 6. In all cases, data were collected over a hemisphere of the reciprocal space by combination of three exposure sets. Each exposure was of 20 s for all compounds, covered 0.3 in ω. The cell parameters were determined and refined by a least-squares fit of all reflections. The first 100 frames were recollected at the end of the data collection to monitor crystal decay, and no appreciable decay was observed.

A summary of the fundamental crystal and refinement data is given in Tables S2 and S3.†

The structures were solved by direct methods and refined by full-matrix least-square procedures on F2 (SHELXL-97).35 All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. All hydrogen atoms were included in their calculated positions and refined riding on the respective carbon atoms. Mercury CSD 2.036 was used for molecular graphics.

In compound 1 the nitrogen and oxygen atoms are equally disordered over two sites by symmetry. In compound 2, all of the rings present a little disorder and have been refined using rigid bodies. In compound 6, the phenyl ring C18–C23 has been refined as a rigid body. In compounds 2 and 5, some of the C-atoms were refined using geometric restraints and variable common C–C distances.

All hydrogen atoms of compound 1–6 were included in their calculated positions and refined riding on the respective carbon atoms.

Further crystallographic details for the structure reported here may be obtained from the Cambridge Crystallographic Data Center on quoting the depository numbers CCDC 952770–952775.

Electrical conductivity measurements

The DC conductivity measurements were carried out with the four or two contacts methods (depending on the size of the single crystals) on four (for 1 and 4) and five (for 7) different single crystals, in the temperature range 300–400 K. The results were identical in the cooling and warming scans. The contacts were made with Pt wires (25 μm diameter) using graphite paste. The samples were measured in a Quantum Design PPMS-9 equipment connected to an external voltage source (Keithley model 2400 source-meter) and an amperometer (Keithley model 6514 electrometer). The samples were measured by applying a constant voltage of 10 or 20 V and measuring the intensity. All the conductivity quoted values have been measured in the voltage range where the crystals are Ohmic conductors. The cooling and warming rates were 0.5 K min−1 in all cases.

Synthesis of [Ru2Br(μ-NHOCC6H4-o-Me)4]n (1)

The synthesis of this compound was carried out following two different methods:

Method (a): Microwave assisted synthesis. Bromidotetrakis(acetato)diruthenium(ii,iii) (0.13 g, 0.25 mmol), o-toluamide (0.20 g, 1.5 mmol), potassium bromide (0.24 g, 2 mmol), triethylamine (0.25 mL) and absolute ethanol (8 mL) were added into an 85 mL TFM Teflon vessel with a magnetic stirrer bar. The vessel was sealed with a lid equipped with temperature and pressure sensors, and placed in the microwave oven. The reaction mixture was then treated by a three steps program consisting of (i) 15 min heating ramp up to 120 °C, (ii) 16 h isotherm at 120 °C, and (iii) 20 min cooling ramp down to room temperature. A brown coloured suspension was obtained. A microcrystalline solid was isolated by filtration and washed twice with 10 mL of cold ethanol. Yield: 10%. When the reaction was carried out without stirring and with a cooling ramp of 24 h, a mixture of the powder and crystals of 1 was obtained.

Iodotetrakis(acetato)diruthenium(ii,iii) (0.14 g, 0.25 mmol), o-toluamide (0.20 g, 1.5 mmol), potassium iodide (0.34 g, 2 mmol), triethylamine (0.25 mL) and absolute ethanol (8 mL) were added into a 85 mL TFM Teflon vessel with a magnetic stirrer bar. The vessel was sealed with a lid equipped with temperature and pressure sensors, and placed in the microwave oven. The reaction mixture was then treated by a three steps program consisting of (i) 15 min heating ramp up to 120 °C, (ii) 16 h isotherm at 120 °C, and (iii) 20 min cooling ramp down to room temperature. A brown coloured suspension was obtained. A solid is obtained by filtration and washed twice with 10 mL of cold ethanol. Yield: 20%.
