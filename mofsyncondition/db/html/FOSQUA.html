Introduction

Crystal engineering, a vigorous technique receiving widespread interest from chemists and material scientists, has becoming a flourishing research area in respect to the design of coordination polymers (CPs) by means of effective control over the self-assembly processes.1–3 With the development of crystal engineering, rational design of coordination polymers on a fundamental level is now possible by means of the elaborate choice of metals and organic ligands,4 although prediction of the precise solid-state structure remains a long-term challenge owing to some unpredictable elements.5 Factors such as reaction conditions (pressure, temperature, pH, or light), solvents, template molecules, and auxiliary ligands have been proven to have a great influence on framework structures.6 Furthermore, previous research on the possible correlation between structure and function,7, 8 which is greatly advanced through the concept of secondary building units (SBUs),1–4,7,8 made it promising and attractive to realize desired functional materials by tuning the crystal structures. As a result, considerable current research effort has been intensively focused on the deliberate use of functional ligands or metals which will generate practical materials with potential applications in a number of chosen fields.9

Azodyes, an important class of elements in pigment industries, have recently been put forward in coordination chemistry as a burgeoning research target in view of the fact that the physical properties of pigments rest not solely with their molecular structures but also significantly with their intermolecular interactions,10–12 and the structural importance of these interactions is of considerable interest in the area of crystal engineering. In this regard, investigations probing into the packing patterns of their coordination compounds and exploration of the structural motifs of azo colorant derivatives are of great significance. Recently, some metal complex dyes have found applications in high-technology realm12 and the structures of those complexes were identified by analytical and spectroscopic methods.13 It should be noted that most commercially available pigments are designed to be highly insoluble and, as a consequence, are inappropriate to form high quality single crystals suitable for X-ray diffraction.11 Therefore, there is a dearth of crystallographic information about their detailed coordination behavior at present. However, sulfonated azo dyes, which represent a subclass of colorants modified with RSO3−groups to become more soluble and less toxic,11 are promising candidates to isolate crystalline solids. Furthermore, RSO3−groups are long known as excellent candidates to construct supramolecular compounds thanks to their noticeable abilities in forming coordinative and hydrogen bonds.14 Hence sulfonated azo dyes might be feasible choices for connecting crystallographic coordination chemistry and colorant chemicals.

As part of our long-term ongoing research on the coordination chemistry of organosulfonate ligands,15 we have extended our interest to sulfonated monoazo dyes and attempt to reveal possible structural motifs which may be of relevance to the colorant industry. In this paper, systematic investigation of the coordination chemistry of a sulfonated monoazo dye, 4-aminoazobenzene-4′-sulfonic monosodium salt (denoted as 4,4′-abs for 4-aminoazobenzene-4′-sulfonic anion) is documented. As a consequence, it has been shown that 4,4′-abs is a good choice for understanding the role of intermolecular interactions because of the existence of extensive hydrogen bonds and π⋯π interactions within the solid structures. Moreover, structure–property relationships of the as-synthesized derivatives were also studied.

Results and discussion

Crystals of parent ligandL (1), precursor NaL·HL (2), and d-block transition metal compounds 3a–3e, 4 and a lead compound 3f were prepared from typical diffusion processes at room temperature. Reactions with directly mixed aqueous, methanolic or water–methanol (1 : 1) solutions under the same conditions (pH, temperatures and ratios of reactants) did not yield the final products but clear solutions after months. Hydrothermal reactions of lanthanide nitrates, 4,4′-abs and auxiliary ligand phen lead to the formation of compounds 5a–5c. Successful isolation of the final products 5a–5c does not depend mainly on a metal-to-ligandL ratio but the amount of auxiliary ligand phen and the pH. All reactions were undertaken in stoichiometric ratios considering charge balances of negative organosulfonate groups and metal cations. The compounds were structurally characterized by single-crystal X-ray diffractions.

Crystal structures of parent ligand L (1) and precursor NaL·HL (2)

The crystal structure of the 4,4′-abs ligand has not been determined crystallographically, although the coordination chemistry of 4,4′-abs ligands and s-block metals has been previously investigated.11 In this work, crystals of the parent ligandL were obtained from its acidified monosodium salt precursors. Single-crystal X-ray analysis indicated it contained two mono-protonated 4,4′-Habs moieties and four associated water molecules per formula unit. The protonated site lies on the amino group rather than the azo chromogen. The two 4,4′-Habs moieties arrange in a head-to-head fashion interacting with two water molecules and their tail amino groups link to two other water molecules through hydrogen bonding interactions (Fig. 1(a)). Typical hydrogen bond lengths are in the range of 1.800–2.135 Å (Table S1, ESI†). Pairs of head-to-head {4,4′-Habs}2 units pack alternately in the ac plane via both hydrogen bonding and π⋯π interactions (distance of adjacent parallel aromatic rings is 2.855 Å). The layers are linked along the b axis by interlamellar hydrogen bonds to form a 3-D supramolecular network (Fig. 1(b)).

Structural analysis of a single crystal of the NaL·HL precursor presented a ribbon-like structure, with the formula Na(4,4′-abs)(4,4′-Habs) (2). The central Na atom displays an octahedral coordination environment defined by one N-donor, five O-donors from three individual 4,4′-abs ligands and two aqua ligands (Fig. 2) with Na–N length of 2.6460(2) Å and Na–O bonds ranging from 2.3428(2) to 2.5552(2) Å. It is interesting that there are two types of 4,4′-abs ligands playing ‘bridging’ and ‘grafting’ roles (Fig. 3(a) and (c)), respectively. The ‘grafting’ 4,4′-abs ligands are protonated in need of charge balance. A pair of ‘bridging’ 4,4′-abs ligands double connects two pairs of sodium atoms to form a ribbon (Fig. 3(b)) with each sodium center decorating an additional ‘grafting’ 4,4′-abs ligand (Fig. 3(a), ‘grafting’ 4,4′-abs are represented by different shades). It is noted that the ‘bridging’ 4,4′-abs ligands in 2 adopt apparently ‘bowed’ configuration, which can also be observed in the previously reported compound Na(H2O)2(4,4′-abs).11a

The packing pattern of 2 shows a 3-D quasi pillared-layer network constructed from hydrogen bonded supramolecular nets pillared by spatially cross-linking 4,4′-abs backbones (Fig. 3(d)). The hydrogen bonded nets can be viewed as 1-D ribbons connecting through extensive hydrogen bonds on the sodium sulfonate sections. The hydrogen bonds fall into four groups, namely Ow⋯H⋯Ow (2.838 and 2.893 Å), Osulfonate⋯H⋯Ow (2.722–2.964 Å), Namido⋯H⋯Ow (2.804 Å) and Namido⋯H⋯Osulfonate (2.812–3.024 Å).

Crystal structures of d-block metal derivatives: primary evidence for ligand control of structures?

Reactions exploiting d-block transition metals (M = Mn 3a, Co 3b, Cu 3c, Zn 3d, and Cd 3e) and 4,4′-abs ligands lead to five coordination compounds, which are isostructural double-strand chain structures. They have a general formula of M(H2O)2(4,4′-abs)2. Single-crystal X-ray diffraction showed that the crystallographically unique metal center of each isostructural compound is coordinated by two N atoms and two O atoms from four individual 4,4′-abs ligands, and two additional aqua ligands (Fig. 4(a)). The metal center resides in a standard octahedral environment with bond lengths comparable to relevant analogs. It should be noted that compound 3c gives axial bonds of 2.3380(2) Å for the octahedral Cu centers, which are clearly shorter than most Cu–O bonds elongated due to Jahn–Teller effects.

Adjacent metal centers are double bridged by pairs of 4,4′-abs ligands, which adopt bis-monodente linking fashions, to generate double-strand chains (Fig. 4(b)). The two sulfonate groups bonded to a metal center lie in a pseudo trans relationship, meaning that adjacent bridging ligands adopt a ‘head-to-tail’ arrangement. The chains are interconnected through extensive hydrogen bonding to give an overall 3-D supramolecular network (Fig. 4(c)). Similar to 2, it possesses a 3-D quasi pillared-layer packing pattern built from hydrogen bonded supramolecular nets pillared by 4,4′-abs backbones. Typical hydrogen bonds are Ow⋯H⋯Ow (2.409–2.767 Å) and Osulfonate⋯H⋯Ow (2.734–2.767 Å) taking the example of 3d. H-bonds for other compounds are listed in Table S1 (ESI†).

It is not unexpected that compounds 3a, 3b and 3e possess isostructural chain structures with octahedral metal centers because Mn, Co, and Cd usually adopt similar octahedral coordination environments. However, Cu ions display versatile square-planar, square-pyramidal [4 + 1] and octahedral [4 + 2] fashions, and Zn ions are known to prefer tetrahedral rather than octahedral geometries (although the later is also common). Compounds 3c and 3d were not originally expected to form isostructural analogs of 3a, 3b and 3e. It is therefore believed that a ligand domination of octahedral metal centers exists in the reaction system of 4,4′-abs. Further evidence comes from another p-block metal Pb. Lead ions display versatile coordination numbers and coordination fashions, varying from highly symmetrical to the completely asymmetric geometries, ascribed to lone pair electrons.16 However, the successful isolation of an isostructural Pb-containing compound (3f) confirms that the bis-monodented bridging coordination fashion of 4,4′-abs ligands dominates the coordination geometry of the metal centers. Later attempts on substitution of Ag ions, who is less likely to form an octahedral center, were shown to break the geometric domination of 4,4′-abs ligands in transition-metal compounds by inducing a different linking manner.

In the structure of compound 4, the crystallographically unique Ag(1) center connects to three sulfonate O atoms and one N atom from four independent 4,4′-abs ligands, adopting a tetrahedral coordination mode (Fig. 5(a)). Each Ag atom links to three sulfonate groups and a sulfonate group joins three Ag atoms through its three O-donors to form a three-connected silver sulfonate net containing four- (Ag2S2) and eight-membered (Ag4S4) rings (Fig. 5(b)). Such a 4.82silver sulfonate net is observed for the first time in silver sulfonate coordination compounds. Arrays of 4,4′-abs backbones link adjacent silver sulfonate nets to give rise to an extended pillared-layer framework (Fig. 5(c)). Notably, each 4,4′-abs ligand combines four Ag atoms by three O-donors and one N-donor. By virtue of the particular way a 4,4′-abs ligand connects to metal centers, it can be simplified to a tetrahedral linker and the Ag centers also act as tetrahedral nodes. In this way, the whole framework can be regarded as a uninodal 4.65 net.

The isostructural compounds 3a–3f exhibit small but regular variation of their azochromophores as demonstrated by UV-visspectra (Fig. 6) because the NN bonds, although similar, have more or less single bond character after bonding to metal centers which leads to different spectroscopic shifts. The broad absorbance peak at 391 nm (for L, NN bond length of 1.226 Å) is ascribed to the absorption of the azo group and the peak at 259 nm is assigned to the absorption of aromatic rings of the ligand. Hypsochromic shifts in UV-visspectra for 3f-Pb (NN bond length of 1.24 Å), 3a-Mn (NN bond length of 1.258 Å), 3b-Co (NN bond length of 1.261 Å), 3c-Cu (NN bond length of 1.255 Å) are observed at 389, 387, 386 and 380 nm, respectively. However, bathochromic shifts occur for compounds 3d-Zn (NN bond length of 1.251 Å) and 3e-Cd (NN bond length of 1.192 Å) at 395 and 393 nm, respectively. In general, UV characteristics of azo groups of compounds 3a–3f display hypsochromic shifts with the same trend as the bond length changes, although maximal hypsochromic (compound 3c-Cu) and bathochromic (compound 3d-Zn) shifts seem not to obey this trend. Moreover, UV characteristics of aromatic rings present general hypsochromic shifts for all compounds 3a–3f.

Luminescent properties of compounds 3d-Zn, 3e-Cd and L were detected. Under excitation of 350 nm, the emission band is observed at 407 nm (for 3d) and double emission bands (for 3e) occur at 390 and 411 nm, respectively. In comparison with the results of similar coordination compounds, the emission bands of both 3d and 3e can be assigned to the ligand-to-metal charge-transfer (LMCT) bands.17 A typical emission band for L is observed at 587 nm upon excitation of 415 nm, which is in accordance with the nature of the visible orange color.

Crystal structures of f-block metal derivatives

Auxiliary phen ligands were used to meet the geometric requirement of large lanthanide ions in reactions of 4f metal ions and 4,4′-abs ligands. Reactions of lanthanide nitrates (La(NO3)3·6H2O, Pr(NO3)3·6H2O, Nd(NO3)3·6H2O and Sm(NO3)3·6H2O), 4,4′-abs, and auxiliary ligand phen resulted in polycrystalline samples, which were characterized by elemental analyses and XRPDspectra to identify their identical compositions (Fig. S1 in ESI†).18 Attempts towards the isolation of single crystals failed. However, reactions using Ln(NO3)3·6H2O (herein, Ln = Gd 5a, Tb 5b,19 and Ho 5c) as starting materials lead to single crystals with a set of new structures.

Crystallographic evidence resulting from single-crystal X-ray diffraction of compounds 5a and 5c indicated that the structural motifs of 4f metals are mononuclear complexes aided by phen ligands, which is further confirmed by XRPD (Fig. S2 in ESI†) and elemental analyses of the bulk (microcrystalline) material of 5b.

The crystallographically unique Ln center has an eight-coordinate coordination environment and is coordinated by two chelating phen ligands, three ‘head-on’ 4,4′-abs ligands, and a waterligand (Fig. 7(a)). Two of the three 4,4′-abs ligands in a molecular unit are significantly twisted. There are intramolecular π⋯π interactions between parallel aromatic rings of phen ligands and inner aromatic rings of 4,4′-abs ligands. Distances between the parallel aromatic rings are typically in the range of 3.513–3.561 Å. Each unit links to four neighboring ones via three sulfonate groups and the waterligandvia extensive hydrogen bonding to generate a hydrogen-bonded ribbon subunit (Fig. 7(b)).

Typical hydrogen bonds are 2.688 Å (Ow⋯H⋯Ow) and 2.731–2.841 Å (Osulfonate⋯H⋯Ow). It is worth noting that the uncoordinated amino groups are not involved in the formation of hydrogen bonds. The hydrogen-bonded subunits further pack into a 3-D supramolecular framework via complex intermolecular π⋯π interactions between parallel outer aromatic rings of 4,4′-abs ligands, with the closest distance being 3.622 Å (Fig. 7(c)).

Assembly of composite multilayer films

As the colorants are absorbed through surface adhesion, it is vital to mimic the structure-related behavior of dye complexes on a solid surface. Our early research demonstrated that amino containing components were rational candidates in assembling multilayer films through hydrogen bonding interactions20 based on the LBL (layer-by-layer) technique. Moreover, components with well-defined size, shape and structure offer helpful information in understanding structure–property relations. Considering the above mentioned points, 5c-Ho, which contains triple free amino groups as potential hydrogen bonding donors in its crystal structure, was chosen to be used as a model compound in the preparation of multilayer films with an O-rich polyoxometalate species [BW12O40]5− (denoted as BW12 hereafter) as hydrogen bonding acceptors. The possible structure–property relation was subsequently tentatively explored. The results indicate that the chromophoric properties of the dye complexes are greatly influenced by the intermolecular interactions.

A composite multilayer film was prepared viaLBL self-assembly method. The schematic assembly process is shown in Scheme 1. In order to adsorb the anionic BW12 component, the substrate was pre-coated with a positively-charged PEI (PEI = poly(ethylenimine)) layer. The PEI-coated substrate was then alternately immersed in the anionic polyoxometalate BW12 and 5c-Hodye complex solutions in a cycle. Between each adsorption step, the film was washed with water and allowed to dry in a nitrogen stream. UV-visspectroscopy was used to monitor the LBL assembly. Fig. 8 shows the UV-vis spectra of the composite multilayer film PEI-(BW12/5c-Ho)n. The absorbance increases with the number of double layers (a cycle) of (BW12/5c-Ho), indicating that BW12 and 5c-Ho components are successfully incorporated into the film. The absorbance at 228 nm is assigned to the phen ligands and the absorbance peak at 275 nm, showing a bathochromic shift of 10 nm compared with the band at 265 nm in aqueous solution of 5c-Ho, is ascribed to the overlapped absorbance of BW12 anions, phen ligands and the aromatic rings of 4,4′-abs ligands. Interestingly, the characteristic absorbance of the azo chromogen, expected at 395 nm, is covered showing a significant hypochromic effect. A shoulder peak around 300 nm becomes clear when adsorption cycles add to 4 (double layers), which is tentatively assigned to the hypsochromic shifts of azo groups due to extensive hydrogen bonding between components BW12 and 5c-Ho. The film shows a continuous and consistent adsorption to the solid surface demonstrated by adsorption cycles up to 10 (n = 11 in this case), which further proves that intermolecular hydrogen bonding is an effective driving force for the growth of the multilayer film. In IR spectra carried out on CaF2 slides of PEI-BW12, PEI-(BW12/5c-Ho), and PEI-(BW12/5c-Ho)6, the peaks at 3496 cm−1 (for PEI-BW12) and 3473 cm−1 (PEI-(BW12/5c-Ho), and PEI-(BW12/5c-Ho)6) show shifts of 26 cm−1 when 5c-Ho was assembled into the film, indicating that the NH2groups in 5c-Ho are involved in hydrogen bonding.20 This further confirms H-bonds are the main driving force in assembling the film.

The half-way film (n = 6) morphology was characterized by AFM images (Fig. 9). The surface of the films is uniform and smooth. The root-mean-square (rms) roughness was determined to be 0.712 nm calculated over an area of 1.0410 μm2 and the mean particle size was 11.966 nm. Corresponding phase images exhibit a nanosized patch domain structure (Fig. 9(b)). In order to evaluate the effect of the BW12 agent on the surface morphology of the composite film, the AFM images of single component 5c-Ho samples were recorded on mica slides (Fig. S3 in ESI†). Thanks to the large percentage of organic species in 5c-Ho, the sample shows obvious molecular aggregates with particle sizes around 250 nm, as well as a soft character. By comparison, 5c-Ho particles in the PEI-(BW12/5c-Ho)n film possess smaller sizes possibly due to the ‘tailoring’ effect of the large inorganic BW12clusters by involving intermolecular hydrogen bonds. This proves that intermolecular interactions may influence the structure of the dye complexes and consequently their physical properties.

In order to further demonstrate that BW12 plays significant roles in the assembly of the PEI-(BW12/5c-Ho)n film a PEI-(PEG/5c-Ho)n (PEG = poly(ethylene glycol)) multilayer film was also prepared and the surface properties were investigated. PEG was selected in consideration of its rich surface oxygen atoms as possible N⋯H⋯O hydrogen bonding acceptors. The UV-visspectra of the PEI-(BW12/5c-Ho)n film exhibit consistent absorbance peaks with the aqueous 5c-Ho solution. The characteristic peaks of azochromophores were clearly detected at 400 nm and the peaks at 265 and 228 nm are assigned to the absorbance of phen and 4,4′-abs ligands (Fig. S4 in ESI†). The low intensity of the peaks indicates that a small amount of 5c-Ho component was assembled into the multilayer film (Fig. S4 in ESI†). However, no obvious absorbance increase was observed when the number of the double layers (a cycle) of (PEG/5c-Ho) exceeds three. The possible reason for the observation of an inconsistent adsorption on the PEI-(PEG/5c-Ho)n films might be the prominent organic species of both components, which results in massive molecular aggregation preventing the formation of effective hydrogen bonding interaction for film assembly. It is therefore believed that the composite component plays a key role in the assembly of the film.

The surface chemistry of the films indicates that the physical behavior of the dye complexes might be influenced by the intermolecular interactions between dye complexes and the additives as well as the redox properties of the composite components. Future studies focusing on understanding the effects of additives, such as anti-blocking and anti-oxygenation agents, are indispensable for the commercial development of dye complexes.

Discussion

A graphical representation of six possible coordination modes of 4,4′-abs ligand is presented in Scheme 2 according to its four potential binding sites with coordinating and bridging types.

Previous studies on the s-block metal derivatives revealed three different modes, namely ‘head-on’ bidentate (type II), ‘head-on’ tridentate (type III) and ‘head-tail’ tridentate (type V) bridging coordination modes. In the structures of p- and d-block metal derivatives, 4,4′-abs ligands exhibit three types of new coordination modes, namely ‘head-on’ (type I, in 5a and 5c), ‘head–tail’ tridentate (type IV, in 3a–3e), and ‘head–tail’ tetradentate (type VI, 4). Type I and VI represent the minimum and maximum coordination modes, respectively. Furthermore, compound 2, the sodium precursor, displays the first example containing a mixture of two different coordination modes (type I and V), which illustrates the difficulty in controlling structures of s-block metal derivatives because of the various coordination modes of s-block metals. However, unlike s-block metal derivatives, whose structures are dominated by the ionic nature of their bonding and the tendency to bind to oxygen donors, the structures of d- and f-block metal derivatives are more likely dominated by the coordination preferences of the ligands. As a result, compounds 3a–3e all possess octahedral metal centers resulting in isostructural double-strand chain structures. This is further confirmed by the successful isolation of three isostructural f-block metal derivatives, 5a–5c, possessing mononuclear structures with eight-coordinate metal centers.

On the other hand, the nature of the metal ions can also affect the preferred coordination geometries of the ligands and this is relevant to the understanding of the active coordinating sites in different reaction systems. The 4,4′-abs ligands adopt nearly planar conformations when they bond to univalent metals, although 4,4′-abs ligands in all these compounds are somewhat twisted on their N-N groups. However, the 4,4′-abs ligands in the coordination compounds of divalent and trivalent metals possess significantly twisted 4,4′-abs ligands, as shown in Scheme 3. Dihedral angles of the twisted 4,4′-abs ligands were found to be 36–40° (in 3, Scheme 3(a)), and 20 and 49° (in 5, Scheme 3(b) and (c)) for the two twisted 4,4′-abs ligands out of three. Furthermore, the structural dimensions of the transition metal derivatives are largely determined by ratios of ligands to metals, which are dependant on to the valences of the metals. Thus, the steric effects of the ligand configurations are more pronounced when high valence metals are used to form low-dimensional structures with considerable intermolecular voids and, of course, more complex intermolecular interactions.

Conclusions

A sulfonated monoazo dye, 4-aminoazobenzene-4′-sulfonic monosodium salt, has proven a suitable candidate for the isolation of d- and f-block metal complexes. Structural studies on isostructural compounds of d- and f-block metal derivatives shows a prominent ligand dominance of the structures. The successful preparation of dye complexes also helps to illustrate the detailed intermolecular interactions contained in these materials. This study has indicated that both inner and outer supramolecular interactions can have a great influence on the physical behavior of dye complexes on a solid surface.

Experimental

General procedures

All chemicals were commercially purchased and used without purification. K5BW12O40 (BW12) was prepared by published method.21 Elemental analyses (C, H, N and S) were carried out with an Elementar Vario EL III analyzer. Mn, Co, Cu, Zn, Pb, Cd, Ag, Gd, Tb and Ho were determined by a Jobin Yvon Ultima2 ICP atomic emission Spectrometer. Infrared (IR) spectra were recorded with PerkinElmer Spectrum One as KBr pellets in the range 400–4000 cm−1. X-Ray powder diffraction (XRPD) was performed with a Rigaku DMAX 2500 diffractometer. UV-visspectra was recorded with Lambda35 and Lambda900 (Perkin Elmer, USA) spectrometers (a quartz slide was used for recording the UV-vis spectra of the films). Luminescence spectra for the solid samples were recorded with a PE LS55 fluorescence spectrophotometer. Single-crystal X-ray diffraction was carried out by Rigaku Saturn70 diffractometer. AFM images were taken on a single-crystal silicon slide using a Nanoscope IIIa (Digital Instruments Inc., Veeco, USA) operating in the tapping mode with silicon tips.

Syntheses of [Ln(H2O)(phen)2(4,4′-abs)3]·3H2O (Ln = Gd 5a, Tb 5b and Ho 5c)

X-Ray data collection and structure solutions and refinements

Data for compounds 1–5 were collected on a Rigaku Saturn70 diffractometer equipped with graphite-monochromated Mo-Kα radiation with a radiation wavelength of 0.71073 Å by using the ω-scan technique. All hydrogen atoms in compounds 1, 2, 3a–3d and 4 were located in difference Fourier maps. The organic hydrogen atoms in 3e, 3f, 5a and 5c were positioned geometrically and the hydrogen atoms bonded to water oxygen atoms were not located. All absorption corrections were performed using the CrystalClear program.22 Structures were solved by direct methods and refined on F2 by full-matrix least squares using the SHELXL-97 program package.23 All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. The crystallographic data for compounds 1–5 are listed in Table 1.

Preparation of PEI-(BW12/5c-Ho)n multilayer film

Sample films used for UV-vis measurements were prepared on quartz slides and films used for AFM measurements were prepared on single-crystal silicon slides. A quartz (or single-crystal silicon) slide was cleaned with a ‘piranha solution’ at 80 °C for 40 min, and thoroughly rinsed with distilled water. Further purification was carried out by immersion in a H2O–H2O2–NH3 (5 : 1 : 1, v/v/v) bath for 30 min at 70 °C. The clean quartz (or single-crystal silicon) slide was immersed in aqueous solution of PEI for 20 min, washing with water and drying with nitrogen stream. The pre-coated substrate was then cyclically dipped into BW12 and 5c-Ho solution (20 min for each). Between the adsorption processes, the quartz (or single-crystal silicon) slide was washed with water and dried with a nitrogen stream. Cyclic repetition of the adsorption steps leads to the formation of the multilayer film.

Preparation of PEI-(PEG/5c-Ho)n multilayer film

The PEI-(PEG/5c-Ho)n film was prepared using a similar procedure to the preparation of the PEI-(BW12/5c-Ho)n film. PEG was used in the cyclic repetition of the adsorption steps instead of BW12.
