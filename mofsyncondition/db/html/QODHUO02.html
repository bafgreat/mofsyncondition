Introduction

Most of the materials exhibit positive thermal expansion (PTE), namely, the temperature increase leading to expansion. However, some solid-state materials display negative thermal expansion (NTE),1 when heated will result in material shrinkage. Although the NTE phenomenon is rare in most cases, the NTE materials have potential applications in electronic devices, optical mirrors, thermomechanical actuators or sensors, laminates, tooth fillings, and so on.2 The mechanisms relevant to NTE are classified into three categories by Takenaka: flexible network, atomic radius contraction and magnetovolume effect.3 In fact, NTE was discovered in a variety of inorganic oxides such as ZrW2O8,4 zeolites and cyanide-bridged coordination polymers,5 as well as rare organic crystalline materials.6 Among the reported NTE materials, most of them exhibit uniaxial NTE behaviors, while area NTE materials are rarely found.7

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) are new types of crystalline inorganic–organic hybrid materials and are intensely studied more recently due to their intriguing structural diversities, fascinating network topologies as well as wide applications in gas storage and separation, heterogeneous catalysis and drug delivery.8 In fact, much attention has been paid to the NTE effect of MOFs. Such NTE behavior originates from local twisting vibrations around the metal centers accompanied with long-range translational and vibrational thermal motion of the organic linkers.9 Meanwhile, the rationalization of geometric mechanisms (network topologies) of MOFs with NTE behavior has attracted much attention.10 For instance, Ag(2-methylimidazolate) and HMOF-1 with non-interpenetrated cds-type frameworks10a,b display the highly anisotropic thermal responses, which arise from their ‘wine-rack’-like motif that couples a contraction in one direction and an expansion in the perpendicular direction. In 1993, Baughman and Galvão predicted that the ths-type topology with the hinge deformation mode could exhibit an exceptional negative thermal expansion.11 This hinge deformation mode is similar to a folding wine rack. Despite the fact that the ths-type crystal structure had already been reported,12 there is no example of the thermal response of a hinged framework with ths-type topology.

In addition, the thermal expansion behavior could also be modulated by the guest motifs in some host–guest inclusion supramolecules.5b,10c,13 The introduction of guest molecules or ions into the nanopores of the host frameworks may produce a crowded host–guest compound, and the guests also damp the vibrational motion of the host framework. Moreover, weak noncovalent interactions can play an important role in NTE materials.14 For example, Ag⋯Ag interactions are responsible for the “colossal” anisotropic thermal expansion in compound Ag3[Co(CN)6] that owns the ‘wine-rack’ motif.14a By flexing like a wine-rack, the Ag⋯Ag interactions in this compound drive the unusually large PTE effect along two crystal axes while an equally strong NTE effect is driven along the third axis upon heating. Thus, the combination of multiple supramolecular interactions (e.g. argentophilic and π–π interactions) may also create a special effect.

Herein, we present the host–guest coordination architectures, Ag4(tpt)4{δ-[Mo8O26]}·1.5H2O (A), and the corresponding dehydrated phase Ag4(tpt)4{δ-[Mo8O26]} (B) (tpt = 2,4,6-tris(4-pyridyl)-1,3,5-triazine). Compound A contains a four-fold interpenetrated three-dimensional (3D) nanoporous coordination framework that captures δ-[Mo8O26]4− isomers as counter anions and uncoordinated water molecules as guests (Fig. 1a). It is interesting that both of them possess unusual thermal properties; A possesses anisotropic coefficients of thermal expansion with a linear expansion in the b direction but nonlinear decreases in the a and c directions; however, B exhibits near-linear NTE in the a and c directions and positive linear thermal expansion in the b direction over a broad temperature range. These unusually different thermal properties are ascribed to the unique nature of the host framework and the hydrogen bonds between guest H2O molecules and δ-[Mo8O26]4− anions.

Results and discussion

Structural characterization

Pale-yellow block crystals of A were harvested from the hydrothermal reaction using a mixture of Ag(CH3COO), (NH4)6Mo7O24·4H2O and tpt at 180 °C for 6 days. The single-crystal diffraction analysis of A reveals that it crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/c, and the asymmetric unit contains two silver ions, two tpt ligands, one and a half water molecules and one half of δ-[Mo8O26]4− species (Fig. S10a†). All crystallographically independent Ag(i) ions adopt triangle coordination geometry by three N atoms from three individual tpt ligands. In turn, each tpt ligand binds to three Ag(i) ions via three outer pyridyl donors, leaving the triazine core unbound (Fig. S10b†). The interconnection between Ag(i) ions and tpt ligands results in an infinite 3D nanoporous coordination framework. From the topological point of view, both Ag(i) ions and tpt ligands act as 3-connecting nodes, so the complicated framework of A can be further simplified as a four-fold interpenetrated 3D 103-ths net (Fig. S10c†). For a single ths-like cationic host framework, there are two handedness helical nanotubes spiralling along the a and b axes, and their pitches are equal to the unit cell a and b parameters, respectively (Fig. 1b and 1c). It can be seen that one-dimensional lozenge-shaped channels run along the c-axis, which is parallel to the hinge bonds of the hinge deformation mode (Fig. 1d and 1e). With regard to the four fold interpenetrating ths-like networks (I, II, III, IV), there are two types of arrangements of each adjacent array of the single ths-like net. In the structure, I and II, III and IV are in head-to-head conformation with unsupported Ag⋯Ag argentophilic interactions and face-to-face π–π interactions, while II and III, I and IV are in head-to-tail conformation just as a result of weak face-to-face π–π interactions (Fig. 2a–2c). The structure of A was characterized by single-crystal X-ray diffraction at 100 K and in the sets I and II, III and IV, the tpt ligand runs in a parallel mode and the aromatic ring is stacked with the centroid–centroid distances between two triazine rings and the twisted angle being 3.731 Å and 29.67°, respectively, indicating π–π interactions; however, in the sets II and III, I and IV, the tpt ligand runs in an antiparallel way with the centroid–centroid distances between triazine rings and pyridyl rings and the twisted angle being 3.515 Å and 26.62°, respectively (Fig. S11†). From the b axis view, it is found that these four interpenetrating ths-like nets are sets of weak face-to-face π–π interactions that form helical chains oriented parallel to the b direction (Fig. S12†). As a result, the combination of argentophilic and π–π interactions leads to the formation of slipped stacked structures running along the b axis. Significantly, the four-fold interpenetrated nanosized channels are still filled with δ-[Mo8O26]4− isomers with approximate C2h symmetry, and water molecules as guests.

Remarkably, the host framework of A connected by fairly rigid tpt linkers is an example of the ths-type structures, and it is suitable for a study on the thermal properties of the ths-type topology. Hence, A was directly exposed to a constant N2 stream at ambient pressure from 100 K to 500 K at 30 K intervals. However, the structural parameters revealed that A completely lost guest water molecules and converted to the dehydrated phase B while the host framework remained intact at 370 K. The same test case is applied in the dehydrated phase B. In the whole investigated temperature range, both crystals maintained the same monoclinic space group C2/c, no phase transitions were observed, and this result is also supported by differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) (Fig. S9†).

Thermal expansion behavior

By close inspection of the thermal expansion behavior of A, it is noted that A undergoes shrinking along the a and c axes and a simultaneous elongation along the b axis over the whole temperature range of 100–340 K (Fig. 3a and 3b). Calculation of thermal expansion for the principal axes is performed with PASCal software and the relationship between the principal axes and the monoclinic crystallographic axes is considered.7b It is seen that the average principal coefficients of thermal expansion for A are α = −18.07 MK−1, 74.82 MK−1 and −5.12 MK−1 along axes approximately parallel to the a axis, exactly parallel to the b axis and approximately parallel to the c axis, respectively (Table S5†). Therefore there are two NTE behaviors in the three principal thermal expansions and the NTE effect is perpendicular to the b axis. In other words, A not only shows strong PTE parallel to the b axis, but also shows large and rare area NTE effects in the ac plane. With regard to the magnitude of the coefficient along axes approximately parallel to the a axis, about twice larger than that of the well-known NTE material ZrW2O8,15 it is about an order of magnitude larger than the usual (positive) thermal expansion of molecular materials as well as metals and ceramics. In contrast to the thermal expansion behavior of A, B exhibits the linear thermal expansion behavior in all crystal axes in the 100–340 K temperature range with an about equal NTE coefficient of −18.61 MK−1 along the axis approximately parallel to the a axis and larger variation of the NTE coefficient of −8.04 MK−1 along the axis approximately parallel to the c axis as well as a decrescent PTE coefficient of 65.45 MK−1 along the axis parallel to the b axis (Fig. 3c and 3d, Table S6†). This behavior clearly indicates that the anomalous expansion behaviors of B are correlated with the guest water molecules lost in the framework voids. Furthermore, B can have a wide temperature window of 100–500 K, corresponding to the principal coefficients of thermal expansion αBa = −22.32 MK−1, αBc = −7.47 MK−1, αBb = 77.24 MK−1, respectively, and the area coefficient of thermal expansion αB = αBa + αBc = −29.79 MK−1, which is among fairly large area coefficient of thermal expansion of reported MOF materials (Table S7†).7b Goodwin et al. more recently reported a 2D layered material Ag(tcm) (tcm− = tricyanomethanide) with the most extreme area NTE value of −102(3) MK−1 over the temperature range of 100–250 K;16 however, the four independent interpenetrating 3D networks B exhibit quite a strong area NTE effect in a wide temperature range of 100–500 K in contrast to Ag(tcm). In order to further investigate the reversibility of the hinged movement, when the test temperature of A is up to 500 K (A has converted to the dehydrated phase B), then it is back to 160 K. As a result, the value of the unit cell parameters shows a tendency to decrease in the b axis and increase in the a and c axes (Fig. S3†).

The abnormal thermal expansion behaviors for A and B are attributed to the hinge deformation mode (Fig. 2d and 2e). It is well known that the hinged motion of a framework always couples an expansion in one direction while a contraction in the perpendicular direction. Therefore, the thermal responses of A and B are anisotropic with PTE along the b direction oriented perpendicular to the hinge bonds coupled to NTE in the a and c directions. It is noteworthy that the slipped stacked structures run along the b direction observed in the host framework of A and B, meanwhile the thermal variation of the observed Ag⋯Ag distances and centroid–centroid distances between triazine rings and pyridyl rings of the head-to-tail conformation is approximately linear over the entire temperature range (Fig. 3a and 3c), while the centroid–centroid distances between two triazine rings of the head-to-head conformation exhibiting the same trend with Ag⋯Ag distances (Fig. 2c). The thermal motion of argentophilic and π–π interactions that is able to act as the microscopic driving forces result in the hinging of the host framework, even though all the bond lengths in the structure increase accompanied with the elevated temperature. Consequently, the b axis is affected by the temperature change to the same extent as the Ag⋯Ag distances and the centroid–centroid distances between triazine rings and pyridyl rings of the head-to-tail conformation (Fig. 3a and 3c). Furthermore, there are strong hydrogen bond interactions (O13⋯O14) between the guest H2O molecules and the δ-[Mo8O26]4− anions from the terminal oxygen atoms, which are the guest motifs located in the well-defined channels of the host framework A (Fig. S13†). The change of the c lattice parameter is similar to that of a considerable variation of the hydrogen bond distances over the temperature range of 100–340 K (Fig. 3b). The combination of thermogravimetric analysis and mass spectrometry reveals the guest H2O loss at around 345 K (Fig. S6†). The guest H2O loss is quite close to the abrupt changing region of the thermal expansion behavior of the a axis. Thus, the in situ desorption of guest H2O molecules from the host framework results in increasing of the a lattice parameter; simultaneously, the slight change in the value of the c lattice parameter might be originated from the destruction of hydrogen bonds (Fig. 3b). Therefore, the dehydrated phase B shows the larger values of NTE in the a and c axes and a smaller value of PTE in the b axis in contrast to A. It is clearly visible that the crowded hinged coordination networks A and B result from the presence of the guest δ-[Mo8O26]4− anions, which are kept in the cationic host framework by the Coulomb force, strongly vibrating with the temperature rise. Therefore the negative volume thermal expansions of A and B are unlikely.11 In fact, both of the PTE and NTE are compensative to each other, resulting in the observed positive volume thermal expansion behavior of A and B (Fig. S2†).

Thermochromic behavior

A shows thermochromic behavior when the temperature rises with the color of A gradually shifting from pale yellow through crimson to brown but not reversible (Fig. 4a). The thermochromic behavior of A from pale yellow to crimson takes place at 370 K upon the loss of guest water molecules. This phenomenon suggests that the dehydration of A could be responsible for the thermochromic transformation. And this result can be observed in the diffuse reflectance UV-vis spectrum, in which two absorption peaks at 430 and 565 nm are in accordance with the change of color of A (Fig. 4b). In addition, the temperature dependence of the Ag⋯Ag and π–π interactions is also reflected in the emission spectra of the compounds in solids. And at room temperature a red shift of the emission with the difference of heat-treating temperature can also be found in the luminescence spectra (Fig. 4c). The tpt ligand exhibits luminescence emission at 454 nm under 380 nm excitation, while the A before heating (A-pale yellow) have a red shift luminescence with an emission maximum peak at 472 nm upon excitation at 400 nm. Furthermore, the A after 370 K heating (B-crimson) shows the red shift (Δ = 8 nm) luminescence at 480 nm (λex = 400 nm) with respect to A-pale yellow, and upon excitation at 370 nm, the A after 500 K heating (B-brown) displays a slight red-shift compared with B-crimson, from 480 nm to 485 nm. As was lately reported, an extraordinary temperature dependence of the Ag⋯Ag distances in some N-heterocyclic carbene silver(i) complex salts are also observed in the luminescence spectra.17 This phenomenon is akin to the luminescence thermochromism of Cu4I4 coordination polymers, which is attributed to temperature dependent variation of the Cu⋯Cu distances.18 Meanwhile, the heat-induced luminescence change can also arise from π–π interactions.19

Conclusions

In summary, we report here the direct structural evidence for the existence of negative thermal expansion in a flexible framework with the hinge-like ths-type topology, Ag4(tpt)4{δ-[Mo8O26]}·1.5H2O (A) and its dehydrated phase Ag4(tpt)4{δ-[Mo8O26]} (B). Compound A is a typical example of self-sorting supramolecular systems including cationic host networks, guest polyoxometalates and crystalline water molecules through argentophilic interactions, π–π interactions and hydrogen bonds. And the supramolecular interactions can lead to different thermal expansion behaviors of crystalline solid A and dehydrated phase B. Meanwhile, A and B both exhibit much stronger area NTE effects. In addition, supramolecular interactions can also play a key role in thermochromic behavior of compound A. Furthermore, the NTE materials may also show negative linear compressibility (NLC, linear expansion under hydrostatic pressure).20 The unusual NTE behaviors of A and B may be used as a guide for the future design and exploitation of other NTE materials in the field of MOFs.
