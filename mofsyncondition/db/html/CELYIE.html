Porous coordination polymers (PCPs), or Metal–Organic Frameworks (MOFs), represent a new class of functional molecular materials1 capable of improving functions like storage,2 separation,3 sensing4 and catalysis5 of zeolites. Most of their applications are related to pore sizes, shapes, flexibility, and structure/environment. Particularly interesting are those PCPs in which the solid-state properties of the framework, like magnetism,6 luminescence,7 and conductivity,8 change upon sorption/desorption of guest molecules. This new generation of PCPs act in a sensory way by expressing the host–guest interactions. Among them the Fe(ii) spin crossover (SCO) porous coordination polymers (SCO-PCPs) are worth noting.

SCO complexes have attracted much interest in recent years principally due to their potential for practical applications.9–12 A plethora of novel thermal- and light-induced spin transition phenomena are observed due to the extreme sensitivity of the SCO-PCP behaviour to changes, including humidity or solvent inclusion in the crystal lattice, allowing the fine-tuning of the SCO properties.13

Among a range of such materials are a series of SCO-PCPs of the general type [Fe(L)2(NCS)2] (L = bis-unidentate ligand)14 which exhibit complex guest-dependent SCO behaviour, and a family of highly cooperative Hofmann-type materials [FeII(L)MII(CN)4]·Guest (L = pyrazine, bispyridyl, 4,4′-azopyridine, bis(4-pyridyl)acetylene, 1,2-di(4-pyridyl)ethylene and a thiourea analogue; MII = Ni, Pd, and Pt)15–21 and associated analogues.13 These 3D microporous metallogranic frameworks exhibit abrupt transitions with pronounced thermal hysteresis especially in the case of the thiourea analogue where the hysteresis cycle is 60 K wide. Such materials show a memory effect, a property widely sought for practical applications. However, two-step and even multistep spin transitions extend the scope for the application of SCO systems in areas such as data storage,22 photo-responsive devices, molecular sensors and all photonic molecular computing.23 Multistep SCO materials24 can be divided into two general categories, the first in which the stepwise behaviour results from inherent structural inequivalence of the different SCO centers and the second where this inequivalence occurred only in the intermediate phases.

Hysteretic tristability, a three-step switching occurring both upon cooling and upon warming with thermal hysteresis, is a completely new phenomenon in the area of multistep SCO materials and until now there is only one example reported for the case of 3D Hofmann-type PCPs. The 3D system [Fe(dpsme) Pt(CN)4]·2/3dpsme·xEtOH·yH2O (where dpsme = 4,4′-di(pyridylthio)methane; EtOH = ethanol) exhibits a three-step spin transition where each one of the four lattice spin states adopts different long-range orderings of high spin and low spin sites yielding inequivalent iron(ii) centres for the intermediate states.25a There are also reports of 3D Hofmann-type polymers with more rigid pillared ligands and host–guest molecules exhibiting multistep SCO behaviour in their hydrated or/and dehydrated forms. In general, solvent molecules may induce dramatic changes in the spin transition behaviour via subtle electronic effects and/or structural modifications but still the role of the solvent molecule in terms of host–guest interactions and more particularly in the cases where the invited water molecule is strongly disordered remains unclear.25b–d

In an effort to isolate and characterize new {Fe(C4H4N2)[M(CN)4]}·Guest SCO-PCP clathrate systems, we report the synthesis, isolation and structural characterization of two compounds with the molecular formula {Fe(C4H4N2)[M(CN)4]}·(C4H4N2)·1.2H2O where M = Pt(ii) (1), Pd(ii) (2), as well as preliminary magnetic susceptibility studies. Our prime interest is to investigate how the host–guest interactions influence the cooperative nature of the SCO system. It should be noted here that an isomorphous 3D Hofmann-type polymer of the form {Fe(C4H4N2)[M(CN)4]}·(C4H4N2) (the dehydrated form of compound 1) has been reported earlier with a different synthetic route and completely different magnetic behaviour.26 The crystal structure of PCP compounds 1 and 2 is isomorphous and exhibits a Hofmann-type three-dimensional clathrate structure forming a 3D apollonium network. The interesting feature of compounds 1 and 2 is the fact that both guest interactive sites A and B are occupied, in contrast to all the structures given in ref. 26, where only A or only B sites are occupied (Fig. S1, ESI‡). The Fe(ii) centres in 1 and 2 lie on a crystallographic site with a 2/m point symmetry and have a slightly axially distorted octahedral geometry, with two bridging pyrazine molecules trans-oriented and four CN-nitrogen atoms to complete the coordination sphere (Fig. S2, ESI‡). As in the case of {Fe(C4H4N2)[Pt(CN)4]}·CS2 and {Fe(C4H4N2)[Pt(CN)4]}·C4H4N2,26 the pyrazine ligands are ordered. Selected bond lengths and angles are given in the ESI.‡ The equatorial and axial Fe–N bond lengths are 2.149 and 2.245 Å for Pt and 2.130 and 2.246 Å correspondingly for Pd which are characteristic of the high spin states of the iron centres. The Fe–pyrazine units form a 1D chain in the a crystallographic direction (Fig. S3, ESI‡), and the Pt-cyanate units join these chains within the b–c or (100) crystallographic plane. It is known26 that this arrangement forms a cage structure (Fig. 1) where the pyrazine molecules occupy the sites between the pyrazine bridges (sites A, along the c-axis, Fig. S1, ESI‡) and the oxygen atoms of water molecules occupy partially sites close to site B (Fig. S1, ESI‡). More specifically the water molecules occupy the corresponding sites as the C atoms of the CS2 molecule in the structure of {Fe(C4H4N2)[Pt(CN)4]}·CS2.26

The magnetic properties of complex 1 are presented in Fig. 2 in the form of a thermal dependence of the χMT product before and after heating to 430 K at a cooling/heating rate of 2 K min−1. Considering the hydrated compound, χMT is equal to 3.8 cm3 K mol−1, at 300 K which corresponds to a quintet spin state. This value is higher than usual for paramagnetic FeII compounds in the HS state, possibly due to a contribution of the orbital angular moment. The spin conversion for 1 is complete and takes place in three different steps. Upon cooling, χMT undergoes a gradual decrease to 3.5 cm3 K mol−1 at T 1c = 264 K with a non-drastic thermal hysteresis loop while two more drastic and reversible steps appear at T 2c = 198 K (ca. 3.0 cm3 K mol−1) and T 3c = 126 K (ca. 1.25 cm3 K mol−1) with narrow hysteresis loops ca. 13 K (second step) and ca. 28 K (third step), respectively, defining sharp spin transitions that end in an inclined plateau. Finally, below 80 K, the compound is essentially in the LS state. In the same figure is shown the dramatic change in the spin crossover characteristics following thermal dehydration treatment.

Heating the sample at 430 K for 1 hour is necessary to remove the water of crystallization as well as the pyrazine guest molecules (Fig. S4, ESI‡).17 This form, [Fe(C4H4N2){Pt(CN)4}], reveals a complete transition centred at room temperature with a wider square-shaped hysteresis loop which is reproducible over several cycles and has been already observed earlier.17 This compound retains its spin states for several months if it is kept in a dry atmosphere at room temperature. In the same figure (lower) are shown the curves of γHS(T) and the molar fraction of high-spin (HS) molecules, as a function of temperature along with the derivative of γHS(T).

The transition temperature, T1/2, where 50% of the complexes was in the HS and 50% in the LS states, γHS = 0.5, was close to the second step at ca. 222 K. According to the derivative of γHS(T), two sharp peaks appear at T 3c = 126 K and T 2c = 198 K denoting rapid spin transitions associated with strong cooperative interactions, while a third broad peak appears at T 1c = 264 K denoting gradual spin transitions associated with no interactions or weakly cooperative interactions.

Quite amazingly the magnetic behaviour of the isomorphous compound26 without water solvent molecules is simply paramagnetic retaining the HS state in the whole temperature range. The spin conversion for compound 2 is complete and takes place in two different steps (Fig. S6, ESI‡). Upon cooling, χMT undergoes two drastic and reversible steps at T 2c = 198 K (ca. 3.0 cm3 K mol−1) and T 3c = 126 K (ca. 1.25 cm3 K mol−1) with narrow hysteresis loops at ca. 13 K and ca. 28 K, respectively, defining sharp spin transitions that end in an inclined plateau, and at 80 K the compound is essentially in the LS state. The analogue (without water/pyrazine molecules taken after heating the sample at 430 K for 1 hour) again shows dramatic changes in the spin crossover characteristics. An analogous two-step behaviour has been observed recently in an isomorphous 3D polymer [Fe(C4H4N2){Pt(CN)4}]·0.9(C4H2O3)·(H2O)27 confirming our results that a multistep thermal hysteresis is possible when sites A and B of the guest-free [Fe(C4H4N2){Pt(CN)4}] network are occupied by guest molecules.

The spin transition curves of compounds 1 and 2 respectively measured at cooling and heating rates of 1, 2, and 5 K min−1 are shown in Fig. 3. For each heating and cooling rate, several cycles of different heating and cooling rates were performed in order to ensure that the observed differences in spin transition behaviour were caused by the different heating and cooling rates and were not due to different cycles within the respective experiment. All data presented here were collected within the third cycle within the respective experiment in each of the three runs with different rates. The sample was kept at room temperature for 3 weeks to recover its original SCO properties before starting these experiments. Concerning compound 1 the spin crossover curve of the 5 K min−1 run was the most abrupt one with ΔT80 (temperature interval for the 80% spin state conversion) being 95 K, 89 K and 79 K for the three rates respectively. Compound 2 differs since for all three different cycles the ΔT80 was the same and equal to 75 K while there was a translation of the curves to higher critical temperatures upon increasing the cooling/heating rate. The observed effects most likely originate from lattice dynamic effects.

Powder samples of compounds 1 and 2 have been rapidly field (or zero field) cooled at 5 K in the SQUID cavity, and then a heating process was followed from 5 to 300 K with a rate of 2 K min−1. Fig. 3 shows that the quenched samples of 1 and 2 reach values of γHS = 0.37 and γHS = 0.41 respectively at ca. 5 K. By heating the samples at a rate of 2 K min−1, γHS increases to values of γHS = 0.78 and γHS = 0.83 at ca. 57 K and then decreases to γHS = 0.64 and γHS = 0.5 at 95 K, respectively. It should be noted that the actual increase of γHS from 5 to 57 K is closely related to the ZFS effects of the Fe(ii) ion and not to the influence of the expanded lattice28 while thermal quenching happens efficiently from 55 K to 95 K. Above 100 K, the lattice begins to relax and thereby shrinks with a concomitant spin transition to the LS phase. From here on, the thermally induced spin transition is expected to be entropy driven. However, the spin transition curve does not match one of the fresh samples of 1 in the 200–300 K region. This is an indication that the lattice relaxes with delay.

Only close to 300 K does the spin transition curve of the trapped sample match well with that of the fresh sample while for sample 2 the lattice relaxation takes place at 224 K.

Conclusions

Preliminary magnetic measurements of the hybrid microporous Hofmann-like spin crossover polymers {Fe(C4H4N2)[M(CN)4]}·(C4H4N2)·1.2H2O where M = Pt(ii) (1), Pd(ii) (2) showed well-resolved hysteretic three-step for 1 and two-step for 2 thermally induced spin transitions. These results confirm previous findings that a multistep thermal hysteresis is possible when sites A and B of the guest-free [Fe(C4H4N2){Pt(CN)4}] network are occupied by guest molecules. Quenching experiments revealed that the lattice relaxation of 1 and 2 takes place with a delay close to 300 K and 224 K, respectively. Differences in spin transition behaviour were observed by changing the heating and cooling rates probably originating from lattice dynamic effects. The nature of the first step in compound 1 will be further investigated (single crystal diffraction at various temperatures). New measurements (DSC, variable temperature pXRD, Mössbauer and LIESST effect studies) are underway to investigate the dynamic character and multistep magnetic behaviour of these lattices.
