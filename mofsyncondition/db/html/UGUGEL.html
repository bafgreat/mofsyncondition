Introduction

Rare earth (RE) metal–organic complexes constructed from multifunctional organic ligands are currently of great interest due to their aesthetically appealing structural topology stemming from the high coordination numbers and diverse coordination geometries of RE metal cations and potential applications in the fields of catalysis,1 molecular magnets2 and chemical sensors.3 Especially, the extremely narrow emission bands, large Stokes shift and relatively long lifetimes arising from electronic transitions within the partially filled 4f shell of the RE metal cations make them potential candidates in various visible and near-infrared (NIR) luminescent materials.4 However, it is well known that direct excitation of the 4f–4f transitions is difficult due to the low optical cross section of RE metal cations arising from the forbidden nature of these transitions, namely Laporte forbidden,5 which restricts their development to some extent. To overcome the defect, sensitizing chromophore or antenna molecules having broad and intense absorption bands in the UV-region are often introduced into the coordination sphere of the RE metal cations to enhance the efficiency of absorption and emission.

In order to achieve this purpose, judicious choice and synthesis of multifunctional ligands from efficient energy transfer to the RE metal cations after excitation has attracted much attention.6 In current reports, bifunctional ligands such as isonicotinate,7a nicotinate,7b sulfobenzenedicarboxylates,7c sulfonate–phosphonate,7d and phosphonabenzoate7e are most widely investigated However, RE metal–organic complexes utilizing trifunctional ligands, i.e. organic species with three different functional groups, are rarely concerned. In this field, a classic ligand, 5-sulfosalicylic acid (H3SSA), possessing three functional groups: –COOH, –SO3H and –OH, has been employed to construct lanthanide metal–organic complexes.8a,b In 2006 and 2008, Q. Meng8a and J.-Q. Xu8b reported some RE metal–organic complexes with the similar chemical formula of [Ln(SSA)(H2O)2]n [Ln = Ce, Pr, Gd, Sm, Nd, Tb, Eu, Yb, Dy]. These complexes presented monotonous structures owing to the strong coordination of the –COOH and the neighbouring –OH and the fact that the soft base of –SO3 is not prone to coordinate to the hard acid of RE metal cations in view of the Hard–Soft–Acid–Base (HSAB) principle. Therefore, to improve the coordination ability of the –SO3H group and enrich the structure types, we designed another trifunctional ligand, 2-hydroxyl-4-carboxylbenzenesulfonic acid (H3L), with the aforementioned three groups, in which the –OH locating at the ortho position of –SO3H can improve the coordination ability of the –SO3H group. In our previous work, the ligand H3L was used to react with different silver(i) salts and generated a new family of silver(i)-sulfonates with diverse architectures.8c It is interesting to note that one of these complexes can sensitize the Tb(iii) ion to exhibit characteristic emissions at room temperature in aqueous solution. Accordingly, we speculate that H3L would be a potential antenna molecule based on the reason that the aryl ring can act as a sensitizer for RE-centered luminescence by energy transfer from a ligand-centered excited state (the so-called antenna effect).9 To date, RE metal–organic complexes based on such trifunctional ligands have not been reported. Moreover, the use of 2,2′-bipy and 1,10-phen might act as a structuring effect because of their π-system and assist in the luminescence behaviour of the RE metal cations through initial absorption and energy transfer. Bearing these conceptions in mind, we aim to modulate the structures and luminescence of RE metal–organic complexes through controlling the number of the removed H atoms from H3L and the addition of auxiliary N-heterocyclic ligands (2,2′-bipy and 1,10-phen). Therefore, we report herein the syntheses, crystal structures and luminescence properties of 38 complexes based on the reaction of H3L and rare earth (RE) metal nitrates together with 2,2′-bipy and 1,10-phen molecules, namely, [La(H2L)2(ox)0.5(H2O)4]n·2nH2O (1-La) (ox = oxalate), [RE2(H2L)2(ox)(H2O)12]·2(H2L)·8H2O (2-RE) (RE = Nd, Sm, Eu, Gd, Tb, Dy), [RE(SO4)(H2O)7]·(H2L)·3H2O (3-RE) (RE = Ho, Er, Tm, Yb, Lu and Y), [RE(L)(H2O)3]n·nH2O (4-RE) (RE = Er, Tm, Yb and Lu), [RE(L)(2,2′-bipy)(H2O)]n (5-RE) (RE = La, Pr, Nd, Sm, Eu, Gd, Tb, Dy, Ho and Y, 2,2′-bipy = 2,2′-bipyridine), [RE(L)(1,10-phen)(H2O)]n (6-RE) (RE = La, Pr, Nd, Sm, Eu, 1,10-phen = 1,10-phenanthroline), and [RE(L′)(1,10-phen)2(H2O)]n (7-RE) (RE = Gd, Tb, Ho, Er, Yb and Lu, H3L′ = 2-hydroxy-3-nitro-4-carboxybenzenesulfonic acid). These 38 complexes present seven structure types of mononuclear, dinuclear, zig-zag chain, helical chain, as well as layer motif. Meanwhile, the in situ generated oxalate and H3L′ also play crucial roles in modulating the topological structures. Luminescence investigation reveals that 1-La, 2-Eu, 2-Tb, 3-Tm, 3-Yb, 3-Lu, 3-Y, 4-Lu, 5-Tb, 5-Y, 6-La and 7-Tb exhibit ultraviolet and visible emissions in the solid state at room temperature, in which complexes 2-Eu, 2-Tb, 5-Tb, 5-Y and 7-Tb show longer luminescence lifetimes from 390.47 to 1211.57 μs. In the meantime, complexes 2-Nd, 3-Yb, 4-Yb, 5-Nd, 6-Nd, and 7-Yb show near-infrared (NIR) emissions. Moreover, the infra-red spectrum (IR), thermogravimetry analysis (TG), and PXRD of the 17 luminescent complexes were also investigated.

Experimental

Materials and methods

All chemicals and solvents were of A. R. grade and used without further purification in the syntheses. The ligand H3L (m.p. 230–232 °C) was synthesized according to the previously reported method.8c Elemental analyses were carried out with a Vario MICRO from Elementar Analysensysteme GmbH, and the infrared spectra (IR) were recorded from KBr pellets in the range of 4000–400 cm−1 on a Bruker Equinox 55 FT-IR spectrometer. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns were measured at 293 K on a Bruker D8 diffractometer (Cu Kα, λ = 1.54059 Å). TG analyses were carried out on a PerkinElmer TG/DTA 6300 thermal analyzer under a flowing N2 atmosphere, with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. Luminescence spectra were measured on a PerkinElmer LS 55 luminescence spectrometer. Luminescence lifetime measurements for some complexes were measured on an Edinburgh FLS 920 luminescence spectrometer equipped with an LED excitation source of 375 nm with a pulse duration full width at half maximum (FWHM) of approximately 64 ps.

Results and discussion

Syntheses

H3L could be partially or completely deprotonated into diverse anions through the adjustment of pH value owing to the different acidities of the three groups. Meanwhile, synthetic methods, such as self-assembly at room temperature and hydrothermal synthesis, play a vital role in determining the quality and structure of outcome. In the present work, the room temperature reaction tends to form 0D and 1D structures while hydrothermal synthesis tends to form 1D and 2D structures (Scheme 1). In a typical process, RE metal nitrates and H3L were mixed in aqueous solution in a 1 : 3 ratio to remove the H atom of the –SO3H group. After stirring for 10 min at room temperature, the mixture was filtered and left in atmosphere for evaporation for several weeks, thus giving rise to the formation of three species, that is, 1-La, 2-RE (RE = Nd, Sm, Eu, Gd, Tb, Dy) and 3-RE (RE = Ho, Er, Tm, Yb, Lu and Y). It is interesting to note that in situ generated oxalate dianions are observed in complexes 1-La and 2-RE that contain light RE metal cations, which may arise from the reductive coupling of ambient CO2.11 In comparison, complexes 3-RE with heavy RE metal cations contain in situ generated sulfate dianions that originate from the oxysulfide in the laboratory.

Subsequently, the reaction of equal amounts of RE metal nitrates and H3L in aqueous solution at 140 °C for 3 days under the existence of NaOH gives rise to the formation of complexes 4-RE (RE = Er, Tm, Yb and Lu), in which the H3L was fully deprotonated to form L3−. However, for the system containing light RE metal cations, no high quality crystals were obtained. Clear inspection indicates that there are still a certain amount of H2O molecules occupying the coordination sites of RE metal cations. Based on such status, with the aim of reducing the coordination of H2O molecules and further modulating the structures and luminescence properties, chelating 2,2′-bipy and 1,10-phen are introduced to the reaction system. Naturally, RE metal nitrates, H3L and 2,2′-bipy/1,10-phen are mixed in aqueous solution in equal amounts and hydrothermally react at 120 or 125 °C for 3 days. Consequently, complexes 5-RE (RE = La, Pr, Nd, Sm, Eu, Gd, Tb, Dy, Ho and Y), 6-RE (RE = La, Pr, Nd, Sm, Eu) and 7-RE (RE = Gd, Tb, Ho, Er, Yb and Lu) are obtained. As expected, the amounts of coordinated H2O molecules are significantly reduced, which lead to different structures and luminescence properties. Complexes 5-RE containing 2,2′-bipy present the same layer structure. Interestingly, complexes 6-RE containing light RE metal cations and 1,10-phen exhibit the similar structure as observed in complexes 5-RE. Complexes 7-RE with heavy RE metal cations and 1,10-phen present a helical chain structure with the assistance of a new ligand H3L′ obtained from the in situ nitration of H3L during the hydrothermal reaction. In a word, the pH values, synthetic methods, as well as the N-heterocyclic ligands play crucial roles toward the structure of RE metal–organic complexes.

Crystal structures of complexes

Single-crystal X-ray analyses indicate that these 38 complexes exhibit seven structure types relying on different experimental conditions and reactants. For clarity, only 7 of them are selected as representative to illustrate their crystal structures as follow.

1-La. As illustrated in Fig. S1 and S2 (ESI†), each La(iii) cation in complex 1-La is nine-coordinated and exists in distorted tri-capped trigonal prism. The La–O bond lengths vary from 2.477(6) to 2.777(6) Å (Table S2, ESI†). The S2-H2L− monoanions act in the μ2-bridging mode to connect two adjacent La(iii) cations into a dinuclear unit with the La⋯La separation being 11.370(1) Å (Fig. 1). Then, the in situ generated oxalate dianions serve as linkers, joining adjacent dinuclear units in the double chelating coordination mode into a zig-zag chain structure as illustrated in Fig. 1. Such a connection mode leads to a short La⋯La separation of 6.431(4) Å, which provides the chance for the formation of C–H⋯π interactions12 (C6–H6A⋯π, 3.744(2) Å, ∠C6–H6A⋯π = 142.6°) between the π electron density of phenyl rings of S2-H2L− monoanions and CH groups of terminal coordinated S1-H2L− monoanions. Moreover, the intricate hydrogen bonds between donors (coordinated and lattice water molecules) and acceptors (H2L− monoanions and oxalate dianions) further extend the zig-zag chains into a 3D hybrid supramolecular network (Fig. S3 and Table S3, ESI†).

2-Tb. This complex has complicated molecular structures as shown in Fig. S1 (ESI†). The two Tb(iii) cations are connected by the in situ generated oxalate dianions in the double chelating coordination mode to generate a dinulear unit, in which the Tb(iii) cation also exists in a nine-coordinated tri-capped trigonal prism (Fig. S2, ESI†). The Tb–O bond lengths falling in the range 2.360(3)–2.502(3) Å (Table S2, ESI†) are obviously shorter than those in 1-La owing to the smaller radius of the Tb(iii) cation. With such a difference, the Tb⋯Tb separation of 6.300(5) Å in the dinuclear unit is also somewhat short. As illustrated in Fig. 2, adjacent dinuclear units interact with each other through the O–H⋯O hydrogen bonds between O5w molecules and oxalate O14 atoms (Table S3, ESI†), thus giving rise to a chain structure along the a-axis. Then, the lattice O7w and O8w molecules serve as a “glue” to extend adjacent chains into a layer motif through O–H⋯O hydrogen bonds with coordinated O3w (Table S3, ESI†). Such an interconnection also gives rise to the formation of R 22(8) and R 66(16) graph sets. Moreover, three pairs of hydrogen bonds among hydroxyl, carboxyl, sulfonate and lattice water molecules connect the aforementioned layers to form a 3D hybrid supramolecular network (Fig. S4 and Table S3, ESI†).

3-Lu. Owing to the small ionic radius among the RE metal cations, the Lu(iii) cation here is eight-coordinated with the coordination geometry being preferably regarded as anti-tetragonal prism (Fig. S1 and S2, ESI†). Meanwhile, this complex also has the smallest Lu–O bond lengths varying from 2.247(4) to 2.391(5) Å (Table S2, ESI†). The in situ generated sulfate dianions play an important role in confirming the inorganic layer structure. As illustrated in Fig. 3, the sulfate dianions form O–H⋯O hydrogen bonds with coordinated O4w, O6w and O7w molecules through the three uncoordinated oxygen atoms (Table S3, ESI†), which extend adjacent mononuclear units into a layer motif incorporating R 44(20) graph sets. Then, the hydrogen bonds (Table S3, ESI†) between sulfate O8 atoms and coordinated O7w molecules further connect adjacent two single layers into a double layer structure (Fig. 3). Furthermore, the free H2L− monoanions act as pillars to join double layers into a 3D hybrid supramolecular network (Fig. S5 and Table S3, ESI†). It is interesting to note that the interconnection of H2L− monoanions with mononuclear units at the points of O3, O6, O3w and O9w generate a helical chain structure along the b-axis (Fig. 3).

4-Lu. As observed in the complex 3-Lu, the Lu(iii) cation here is also eight-coordinated and displays a distorted anti-tetragonal prism (Fig. S1 and S2, ESI†). The Lu–O bond lengths varying from 2.287(4) to 2.379(4) Å are approximate to those in the complex 3-Lu. However, the coordination of L3− trianion results in a different structure in the present complex. As shown in Fig. 4, the deprotonated hydroxyl O4 join two adjacent Lu(iii) cations together with its symmetry related one in the μ2-bridging mode to form a dinuclear unit with the Lu⋯Lu separation being 3.734(3) Å. Then, the chelating carboxyl and monodentate sulfonate groups connect adjacent dinuclear units through the phenyl rings into a (4,4) layer motif which contains alternate right- and left-handed helices. In general, the L3− trianion presents a μ3(κ1O1:κ2O4:κ1O5:κ1O6) coordination mode. Furthermore, hydrogen bonds between sulfonate O2 and O3 and coordinated O2w and O3w molecules extend the adjacent layers into a 3D hybrid supramolecular network (Fig. S6 and Table S3, ESI†).

5-Tb. Different from the complex 2-Tb, the Tb(iii) cation here is eight-coordinated and displays a distorted anti-tetragonal prism (Fig. S1 and S2, ESI†). The Tb–O bond lengths are comparable to those in the complex 2-Tb (Table S2, ESI†). As shown in Fig. 5, the carboxyl and sulfonate groups adopt the same μ2(κ1O:κ1O′) coordination mode and join adjacent Tb(iii) cations into right- and left-handed helical chain structures with the shortest Tb⋯Tb distance being 5.279(3) Å. Then, the phenyl rings extend adjacent helical chains into a layer motif. Therefore, the L3− trianion here presents different a μ4(κ1O1:κ1O2:κ1O4:κ1O5:κ1O6) coordination mode from that in the complex 4-Lu. The 3D supramolecular network here is only extended by C–H⋯π interactions12 (C6–H6⋯π, 3.595(5) Å, ∠C6–H6⋯π = 159.1°) owing to the smaller size of 2,2′-bipy than the 1,10-phen molecule (Fig. 5, Fig. S7 and Table S3, ESI†).

6-La. In contrast to the nine-coordinated La(iii) cation in the complex 1-La, the present La(iii) cation shows an eight-coordinated anti-tetragonal prism due to the coordination of the large 1,10-phen (Fig. S1 and S2, ESI†). The La–O bond lengths varying from 2.369(2) to 2.598(2) Å are shorter than those in the complex 1-La (Table S2, ESI†). With the same μ4(κ1O1:κ1O2:κ1O4:κ1O5:κ1O6) coordination mode, L3− trianions connect adjacent La(iii) cations into a layer structure as observed in the complex 5-Tb (Fig. 6). Moreover, the 1,10-phen molecules that locate on both sides of the layer form π⋯π (Cg1⋯Cg2, 3.615(1) Å) and C–H⋯π interactions12 (C12–H12⋯Cg3, 3.632(5) Å, ∠C12–H12⋯Cg3 = 139.7°) with 1,10-phen molecules and L3− trianions in the adjacent layer, thus generating a 3D supramolecular network (Fig. 6, Fig. S8 and Table S3, ESI†).

7-Tb. This complex is obtained by a similar method with the complex 6-La. However, they exhibit completely different structures, even the molecular structure (Fig. S1, ESI†). Different from complex 2-Tb, owing to the coordination of two large 1,10-phen molecules, the Tb(iii) cation is eight-coordinated and displays a distorted anti-tetragonal prism (Fig. S2, ESI†). With such a reason, the Tb–O bond lengths falling in the range 2.232(6)–2.366(6) Å are also shorter than those in the complex 2-Tb (Table S2, ESI†). Compared with the L3− trianion in the complex 6-La, the in situ generated L′3− trianions adopt a simple μ2(κ1O1:κ1O4:κ1O7) coordination mode and connect adjacent Tb(iii) cations into right- and left-handed helical chain structures (Fig. 7). Meanwhile, the homohelical chains interact with each other through the hydrogen bonds between coordinated O1w molecules and sulfonate O2 atoms, thus generating a layer structure in the ab plane (Fig. 7). Moreover, large amounts of π⋯π interactions between two types of 1,10-phen molecules are observed with the centroid-to-centroid distances being 3.606(1) (Cg2⋯Cg2), 3.847(1) (Cg1⋯Cg1 and Cg3⋯Cg3) and 3.700(1) Å (Cg3⋯Cg4), which extend adjacent hydrogen bonding layers into a 3D supramolecular network (Fig. 7, Fig. S9 and Table S3, ESI†).

Structure evolution and in situ reaction

As is known to all, RE(iii) cations have large affinity to the H2O molecule according to the Hard–Soft–Acid–Base (HSAB) principle. Meanwhile, the soft base of –SO3 is not prone to coordinate to the hard acid of RE metal cations. Therefore, one can see that improving the coordination ability of the –SO3H group would significantly influence the topological structures of target complexes. Previous work13 has demonstrated that the hydroxyl group at the ortho-position of the –SO3 group can not only improve the coordination ability, but also enrich its coordination modes. As shown in Fig. 8, three types of anions induced by the pH value and in situ nitration are obtained from the dehydrogenation of H3L in these complexes, i.e. H2L− monoanion, and L3− and L′3− trianions. The different coordination modes of three anions result in diverse structures. The H2L− monoanion in the complex 3-Lu only serves as a counter-anion to maintain charge balance. Accordingly, this complex only exhibits a mononuclear unit with the coordination of H2O molecules and the sulfate dianion. Except as a counter-anion, the H2L− monoanion in the complex 2-Tb coordinates to the Tb(iii) cation in the monodentate mode, reducing the coordination number of H2O molecules. Then, the in situ generated oxalate dianion connects adjacent Tb(iii) cations into a dinuclear unit. In the complex 1-La, the H2L− monoanion shows monodentate and μ2-bridging modes to further prevent the coordination of H2O molecules and join two adjacent La(iii) cations into dinuclear units which are joined by in situ generated oxalate dianions into zigzag chains. With the increase of pH value, all the three active hydrogen atoms of H3L are removed and then the L3− trianions are formed in complexes 4-Lu, 5-Tb and 6-La, which act in μ3(κ1O1:κ2O4:κ1O5:κ1O6) and μ4(κ1O1:κ1O2:κ1O4:κ1O5:κ1O6) coordination modes (Fig. 8) to join adjacent RE(iii) cations into two types of layer motifs involving alternate right- and left-handed helical chains. It should be noted that in situ generated L′3− trianions in the complex 7-Tb only link adjacent Tb(iii) cations in the μ2(κ1O1:κ1O4:κ1O7) coordination mode (Fig. 8) to form a helical chain structure. This is mainly ascribed to the substitution of the nitryl group that increases the steric hindrance of hydroxyl and carboxyl groups, thus simplifying their coordination modes. In general, at low pH value, only the sulfonate H atom is removed to form the H2L− monoanion that acts as a counter-anion or ligand in simple coordination modes. Hence, complexes with low-dimensional structure are obtained. By contrast, H3L can lose all the three active hydrogen atoms at high pH value to form L3− and L′3− trianions. Meanwhile, they exhibit intricate coordination modes to join adjacent RE(iii) cations into chain and layer motifs. Moreover, the introduction of N-heterocyclic ligands can not only adjust the alkalinity of the reaction system, but also effectively reduce the number of coordinated H2O molecules, thus improving the coordination modes of anions.

An in situ reaction is usually found in the course of some experiment processes, especially for the hydrothermal reaction, which can not only generate new contents, but also lead to the formation of novel ligands that could not be easily obtained from conventional synthetic methods, thus improving crystal growth and forming materials not accessible by other means.14 In the present work, three types of in situ reactions are observed during the formation of some complexes. The first one is the in situ formation of oxalate dianion in complexes 1-La and 2-RE. As is known to all, the formation of oxalate dianions is the frequently reported in situ phenomenon in coordination polymers.15 To date, several pathways have been posited, most of which can be described as the reductive coupling of ambient CO2,11 the cleavage followed by the coupling of carboxylate groups16 as well as the decomposition or oxidation of organic species.17 Furthermore, instances of the oxidative coupling of methanol and ethanol to yield oxalate dianions have also been reported.18 In general, the middle two cases occur under hydrothermal conditions with high temperature and pressure. Therefore, in view of the experimental conditions in the current work, the first case would be the most proper explanation. Such a proposed mechanism has been previously conducted in the Yb(iii) porphyrinate dimer by T. C. W. Mak and co-workers in 2000.19 The second one is the in situ formation of the sulfate dianion in complexes 3-RE, which may originate from the oxysulfide in the laboratory. In a word, light RE metal cations tend to induce the formation of oxalate while heavy RE metal cations tend to induce the formation of sulfate. Hence, the difference between the two types of in situ reactions may be mainly ascribed to lanthanide contraction. In order to verify the aforementioned speculation, further analyses on the solution and solid by-products through GC-MS and PXRD characterization studies were performed. Both of the two types of characterization studies reveal that no arene by-products were detected in these two phases, which demonstrated that H3L did not decompose in these reaction systems. The third one refers to the in situ nitration of H3L under hydrothermal conditions. Actually, nitration is quite a common reaction in organic synthesis.20 In most instances, the nitration substitution is performed with a mixture of nitric and sulfuric acids. In 2006, Zhu and co-workers reported the first nitration reaction achieved only by lead nitrate rather than by mixed HNO3/H2SO4 under hydrothermal conditions, which shows that the coordination can remarkably activate the nitration.21 Meanwhile, the authors indicate that the 1,10-phen molecule also seems to be important for the nitration owing to the fact that the similar reaction system with the 2,2′-bipy molecule did not form a nitration product. In this sense, the in situ nitration of H3L in complexes 7-RE (RE = Gd, Tb, Ho, Er, Yb and Lu) also complies with such a proposed procedure (Scheme 2). As a result of the formation of these in situ ligands, interesting structures are obtained.

IR spectroscopy

The peaks observed in the range of 1701–1690 cm−1 for complexes 1-La, 2-RE, and 3-RE can be attributed to the anti-symmetric stretching vibrations of carboxyl CO groups, while the symmetric stretching vibrations of carboxyl CO groups in these complexes locate at 1417–1413 cm−1. Both the vibrations demonstrate that the H atoms of the carboxyl groups in these complexes are not removed. By contrast, the anti-symmetric and symmetric stretching vibrations of carboxyl COO− groups for complexes 4-RE, 5-RE, 6-RE and 7-RE are observed at the range of 1668–1648 and 1407–1401 cm−1, which indicate that the H atoms of the carboxyl groups in these complexes are removed. The observations of these peaks and their different positions indicate that the carboxyl groups in these complexes are deprotonated and coordinate to the RE(iii) cations in diverse coordination modes.22 The peaks observed at low wavenumbers of 1649–1641 and 1377–1369 cm−1 in complexes 1-La and 2-RE can be ascribed to the anti-symmetric and symmetric stretching vibrations of oxalate COO− groups. For sulfate anions in the complexes 3-RE, their characteristic vibrations appear in the range of 1108–1101 cm−1. The peaks in the range 1603–1436 cm−1 can be assigned to the skeletal vibrations of the 1,10-phen, 2,2′-bipy molecules and the phenyl groups of H2L−, L3− and L′3− anions. The peaks observed in the range 1367–1365 cm−1 can be assigned to the vibrations of the nitryl group of L′3− anions. The characteristic vibrations of νas(SO3−) in these 17 complexes are in the range of 1243–1141 and 1091–1081 cm−1, whereas the νs(SO3−) absorptions are in the range of 1029–1014 cm−1.8c,13,23 In addition, the stretching bands during 3430–3187 cm−1 in the IR spectra can be attributed to the existence of the hydroxyl groups of H2L− monoanions, water molecules, as well as the formation of extensive hydrogen bonds in the 17 selected complexes.

Luminescence property

In view of the line-like and high color-pure emissions, the RE metal–organic complexes may possess excellent luminescence properties. Therefore, the luminescence properties of these 38 complexes and H3L in the solid state at room temperature were investigated, in which the emission spectrum for H3L (λex = 280 nm, λem = 353 nm) is identical to our previous work.8c The failure to observe luminescence emissions for 4-Tm, 5-Eu, 6-Eu, Ho(iii), and Er(iii)-based complexes in this work could be probably attributed to the mismatch of the energy levels between the triplet state of the organic ligands and the excited state of these RE(iii) cations to produce an effective energy transfer, such that their emission intensities are too weak to be detected.24 The metal-centered (MC) electronic levels are well above the ligand-centered electronic levels of organic ligands, and typically, the Gd(iii) cations are known to be located at 31 000 cm−1. Therefore, ligand-to-metal energy transfer and the consequent MC luminescence cannot be observed for Gd(iii) complexes.25 The test results show that 17 of these 38 complexes exhibit different ultraviolet, visible and near-infrared (NIR) emissions. Their excitation and emission maxima are listed in Table 2. In view of their different topological structures and emission peaks, their luminescence properties are discussed in the following four parts.

The first part includes complexes 1-La, 5-Y and 6-La, in which the two La(iii) complexes exhibit blue emission while the Y complex exhibits green emission (Fig. 9a), which are all assigned to the ligand-centered (LC) π*–π transitions.4d Compared with the emission of free H3L, bathochromic shifts of 89, 110 and 148 nm are observed for the three complexes. Such bathochromic shifts are probably attributable to the different coordination environments around RE(iii) cations.26 The enhancement of emission intensity compared to the free ligand is probably attributed to the RE metal–ligand coordination which effectively increases the rigidity of the ligand and reduces the nonradioactive decay of the intraligand excited state.27 Meanwhile, the C–H⋯π interactions between N-heterocyclic molecules and L3− trianions in the complexes 5-Y and 6-La also increase the chance of charge transfer, which then enhance the emission intensity.

The second part includes complexes 2-Eu, 2-Tb, 5-Tb and 7-Tb. For the complex 2-Eu, the characteristic transition of the Eu(iii) cation is observed upon excitation (Fig. 9b), which can be attributed to 5D0 → 7F0 (580 nm), 5D0 → 7F1 (593 nm), 5D0 → 7F2 (620 nm), 5D0 → 7F3 (688 nm) and 5D0 → 7F4 (695 nm). The most intense transitions of 5D0 → 7F1 and 5D0 → 7F2 imply red light emission of the complex 2-Eu and the appearance of the symmetry forbidden emission 5D0 → 7F0 at 580 nm indicates that the Eu(iii) cation possesses a non-centrosymmetric environment.28 With a similar excitation, the complex 2-Tb displays four characteristic bands of 5D4 → 7F6 (491 nm), 5D4 → 7F5 (546 nm), 5D4 → 7F4 (585 nm) and 5D4 → 7F3 (622 nm) of the Tb(iii) cation. Meanwhile, the ligand-centered transition (π*–π) is stronger at 367 nm, indicating the ineffective sensitization from the H3L ligand to the Tb(iii) cation.4d The enhancement and bathochromic shift of this emission band (Fig. 9c) compared to that of H3L may result from the coordination of the ligand to the metal cations.29 Complexes 5-Tb and 7-Tb also present four typical emission bands of Tb(iii) cations (Table 2), which could still be ascribed to 5D4 → 7FJ (J = 3–6) transitions. It is noteworthy that the ligand-centered emission is totally quenched in these two complexes, indicating that the triplet state of L3− and L′3− matches well with the excited state of the Tb(iii) cation and the energy transfer from the ligand to the Tb(iii) cation is efficient.4d Comparing the structures of complexes 2-Tb, 5-Tb and 7-Tb, we can see that partially coordinated water molecules of 2-Tb were removed from the coordination sphere of the Tb(iii) cation and replaced by nitrogen atoms from 2,2′-bipy and 1,10-phen molecules in complexes 5-Tb and 7-Tb. Thus, a decrease of the number of coordination O–H oscillators with high energy induced an increase in the emission intensity. Also, the presence of the strongly chelating 1,10-phen and 2,2′-bipy ligands together with the C–H⋯π and π⋯π interactions (Fig. 5–7) results in an increase of emission for Tb(iii) cations in these two complexes, described as the antenna effect.

The third part includes complexes 3-Tm, 3-Yb, 3-Lu, 3-Y and 4-Lu. A shown in Table 2, these five complexes exhibit purple emission with the maximum varying from 354 to 370 nm. Owing to the resemblance of these emission spectra in comparison with that of H3L (Fig. 9d), their luminescence emissions can probably be assigned to the ligand-centered (LC) π*–π transitions.4d The various degrees of hypsochromic shifts from 1 to 17 nm for these emission peaks are probably attributable to the differences of RE(iii) cations and the coordination environment around them.26 The reason for the enhancement of the emission intensity towards to H3L is identical to the first part. It should be indicated that the emissions for these RE(iii)-based complexes are rarely reported, especially for Y(iii) and Lu(iii)-based complexes.30An insight into the excited-state information of luminescence for these complexes is performed by measuring their luminescence lifetimes. In the light of their emission intensity, only the luminescence lifetimes of complexes 2-Eu, 2-Tb, 5-Tb, 5-Y, and 7-Tb were measured and the corresponding data are shown in Table 2. The decays for these five complexes in the solid state at room temperature are found to fit single-exponential (Fig. S10, ESI†). The lifetimes in these complexes distribute in the range of 390.47–1211.57 μs. Such long lifetimes indicate that efficient energy transfer occurs from the organic ligands to the RE(iii) cations.

Moreover, luminescence tests indicate that complexes 2-Nd, 3-Yb, 4-Yb, 5-Nd, 6-Nd and 7-Yb in the fourth part show NIR emissions upon excitation at 466 nm. As shown in Fig. 9e, complexes 2-Nd, 5-Nd and 6-Nd display a strong emission band at 1055 nm (4F3/2 → 4I11/2), an emission band with much lower intensity at 890 nm (4F3/2 → 4I9/2), and a very weak band at 1325 nm (4F3/2 → 4I13/2) in the NIR region, which can be ascribed to the characteristic emission bands of the Nd(iii) cation. Similarly, complexes 3-Yb, 4-Yb and 7-Yb also display the characteristic emission bands of the Yb(iii) cation at 995 nm for 3-Yb, 1000 nm for 4-Yb, as well as 975 and 1010 nm for 7-Yb (assigned to the 2F5/2 → 2F7/2 transition) under excitation at 466 nm (Fig. 9f). Compared with complexes 3-Yb and 4-Yb, partial coordinated water molecules around the Yb(iii) cation are replaced by 1,10-phen molecules in the complex 7-Yb, which then lowers the symmetry of the coordination sphere of the Yb(iii) cation. Consequently, Stark splitting of the energy level of the Yb(iii) cation is observed, giving rise to the two emission peaks in the spectrum of the complex 7-Yb. The intensity changes in these two series of complexes are also ascribed to the reason that a decrease of the number of coordination O–H oscillators with high energy induced an increase in the emission intensity. Meanwhile, the presence of the strongly chelating 1,10-phen and 2,2′-bipy ligands together with the C–H⋯π and π⋯π interactions (Fig. 5–7) results in an increase of emission. The observation of emissions for these complexes fully demonstrated that H3L is an excellent antenna molecule to sensitize the emission of RE metal cations in a wide spectral range.

Conclusions

In summary, 38 complexes have been successfully obtained from the reaction of 2-hydroxyl-4-carboxylbenzenesulfonic acid (H3L) and RE metal nitrates together with two N-heterocyclic ligands. H3L evolved into different forms of anions, i.e. H2L− and L3−, which connect adjacent RE(iii) cations in various coordination modes to form mono- and dinuclear units, zigzag and helical chains, as well as diverse layer motifs. Meanwhile, it is interesting to note that the in situ generated oxalate dianions in 1-La and 2-RE and the in situ generated L′3− in 7-RE also play important roles in modulating their structures. Moreover, 17 of the 38 complexes present solid state luminescence with purple, blue, green and red emissions at room temperature, in which complexes 2-Eu, 2-Tb, 5-Tb, 5-Y and 7-Tb show longer luminescence lifetimes from 390.47 to 1211.57 μs. Complexes 2-Nd, 3-Yb, 4-Yb, 5-Nd, 6-Nd and 7-Yb show NIR emissions. The present study provides a strategy that the structures and luminescence properties of RE metal–organic complexes with multifunctional arenesulfonate could be preferably adjusted through the modifications of experimental conditions.
