1 Introduction

Synthesising multifunctional magnets is one of the major trends in molecular magnetism because the functions of the final material are fully determined by the proper choice of the building blocks.1 In this field, 2D bimetallic oxalate-based materials of general formula A[M1M2III(ox)3] (with M1 = Li+, Na+ or Mn2+, Ni2+, Fe2+,…; M2III = Cr, Co, Fe, Ru; ox = (C2O4)2− and An+ a counter-cation whose charge ensures the electroneutrality of the compound) are widely studied because a large variety of cations, namely tetraalkylammonium NR4+ (R = n-propyl, n-butyl, n-pentyl),2,3 [(C6H5)3PNP(C6H5)3]+,4 ferrocenic ammonium [C5H5FeC5H4CH2NR3]+,5 phosphonium or arsenium cations,4,6a stilbazolium,7BEDT-TTF,8,9b ferricinium and cobalticinium9a can be inserted into the interlayer space. Some of these cations introduce a new physical function in the material such as non-linear optics, (supra) conduction.1,8,9b Another crucial property, chirality, arises from the D3 symmetry of the tris(oxalato)metalate moieties. We are currently developing the enantioselective synthesis of optically active bimetallic oxalate-based magnets10 in order to exploit this property and investigate a recently discovered cross-effect: magneto-chiral dichroism (MChD).11

The understanding of the asymmetric formation of these networks is a key point for the rational design of new optically active molecular magnetic materials. X-Ray diffraction both on single crystals and powders appears as a central technique to answer the questions about the influence of the asymmetric starting materials enantiomeric purity, of the counter-cations and of other experimental parameters on the enantioselectivity of the syntheses. The problem remains difficult because 2D oxalate-based networks are composed of 2D honeycomb anionic layers [M1M2III(ox)3]n− with counter cations An+ in between the layers. Within one anionic layer, the adjacent metal centres have alternatively the (Δ) and (Λ) configurations. This feature weakens the overall chirality of the structure,12 making it difficult to determine unambiguously whether a compound is chiral or not.

Though much of the available structural data on these 2D oxalate-based compounds are based on powder X-ray diffraction, some single-crystal X-ray diffraction structural studies were reported.2,3,13 Because of rapid racemisation rates compared to crystallisation ones and/or unknown resolution of the starting building blocks, these structures were obtained starting from the racemic anionic block [M(ox)3]3− (M = Cr, Fe). When the template cation is [N(n-C4H9)4]+, the crystal structure of [N(n-C4H9)4][MnCr(ox)3] has the achiral space group R3c.2 On the contrary, for [N(n-C5H11)4][MnFe(ox)3] the reaction leading to single crystals from the racemic tris(oxalato)ferrate(iii) in presence of Mn2+ and [N(n-C5H11)4]+ occurs with a spontaneous resolution of [N(n-C5H11)4][(Δ)-Mn(Λ)-Fe(ox)3] and [N(n-C5H11)4][(Λ)-Mn(Δ)-Fe(ox)3].3b The found space group is enantiomorphous (C2221). The co-ordinated metal centres are chiral as well as the [N(n-C5H11)4]+cation due to a homochiral conformation of the alkyl chains around the nitrogen atom. Because the counter-cation seems to be able to direct the synthesis towards chiral structures, we decided to exploit this chiral self-assembling effect to obtain optically active material starting from resolved ammonium cations. Resolved planar chiral ferrocenylammoniums lead to optically active materials starting from (rac)-[CrIII(ox)3]3− in the presence of Mn2+.14 Unfortunately, hydrolysis of the cation occurs during the crystallisation process and does not allow one to clearly correlate the configuration of the starting ammonium with the configuration of the metal centres observed in the final single-crystal.15 More robust chiral ammonium cations are thus needed to obtain optically active single crystals. We recently reported the synthesis of 2D oxalate-bridged bimetallic anionic networks [MnIICrIII(ox)3]− templated by asymmetric ammonium cations (NR1R2R3R4)+.16 For example, [N(C2H5)(n-C3H7)(n-C4H9)(n-C5H11)][MnCr(ox)3] exhibits a transition to a ferromagnetically ordered state at T = 7 K, close to the values of Tc of other 2D [MnCr] networks.6 The structural characterisation of these compounds did not answer to the question of a possible spontaneous resolution as observed for [N(n-C5H9)4][MnFe(ox)3].3b

We report herein the synthesis of [N(n-C4H9)4][MIIMIII(ox)3] (MII = Mn, MIII = Cr (1), MII = Fe, MIII = Cr (2)) and [N(C2H5)(n-C3H7)(n-C4H9)(n-C5H11)][MIIMIII(ox)3] (MII = Mn, MIII = Cr (3), MII = Fe, MIII = Cr (4), MII = Mn, MIII = Fe (5)) compounds. Chromium containing compounds were prepared starting from both racemic and resolved (Δ)- or (Λ)-isomers of [Cr(ox)3]3−. The single crystal X-ray structure of [MnCr] 3 is presented and discussed. Through a systematic study of the powder X-ray diffraction patterns, we will try and bring answers about the parameters that govern the spontaneous resolution of 2D oxalate-based networks. The magnetic properties of the synthesised compounds are presented.

2 Experimental

The IR spectra were recorded on a Bio-Rad IRFT spectrometer as KBr pellets in the 4000–250 cm−1 region. Elemental analyses were performed at the SIARE-UPMC (Paris) and at the Institute of Problems of Chemical Physics (Chernogolovka). Circular dichroism (CD) curves were recorded using a Jasco model J-810 spectropolarimeter as KBr pellets (1.2 mg of product in 120 mg of KBr). To afford comparison the spectra were normalised and corrected for baseline deviation. Optical rotations were measured on an EPO1 (0.5 cm cell) or Ameria AA5 (10 cm cell) polarimeter. NMR spectra were recorded on a Bruker AC 300 spectrometer.

2.2. XRD and magnetic properties

The powder diffraction patterns were collected at room temperature on a Philips PW-1050 goniometer using the Bragg–Brentano configuration and Mn-filtered Fe-Kα radiation and on a ADP-2-01 diffractometer, using V-filtered Cr-Kα radiation. The X-ray powder patterns are first analyzed by the EVA program,18 in order to verify the disappearance of the starting compounds, the crystallinity of the material and to calculate d-spacings. The cell parameters are refined using the CELREF program.19 The profile powder diffraction patterns are then refined using the WINPLOTR suite of programs.20

Diffraction data were collected on a Nonius KappaCCD diffractometer at 173 K. Unit cell parameter determination, data collection strategy and integration were carried out with the Nonius EVAL-14 suite of programs.21 The data were corrected for absorption by a multi-scan method.22 The structure was solved by direct methods with SHELXS-86,23 refined by full least-squares on F2 and completed with SHELXL-97.24 Graphics were carried out with DIAMOND.25 All non-H atoms were refined with anisotropic displacement parameters except for the atoms of the alkylammonium cations (severely disordered) which were considered isotropic and consequently H atoms were excluded from the calculations.

Magnetic measurements were performed using a Quantum Design MPMS-5S SQUID magnetometer in the DC mode at external magnetic fields up to 5 T and temperatures ranging between 5 and 300 K.

3 Results and discussion

3.1. XRD analysis (structural properties)

3.1.1. The N(n-C4H9)4 containing compounds

To determine the effect of the divalent metal ions on the resolution process, we discuss the structure of compounds [MnCr] 1 and [FeCr] 2 containing the symmetrical [N(n-C4H9)4]+cation. The X-ray diffraction patterns for 1, (Δ)-1, (Λ)-1 and 2 are available as ESI.† The diffraction patterns for compounds (Δ)-1 and (Λ)-1 obtained from (Δ)- or (Λ)-[CrIII(ox)3]3− respectively are identical and significantly different to the one of 1 obtained from rac-[CrIII(ox)3]3−. The thinness of the peaks indicates an improved crystallinity compared to those previously mentioned in the literature.10a These patterns can thus be refined. The latter is refined using the R3c achiral space group. On the contrary, this achiral space group does not allow the refinement of the experimental pattern of (Δ)-1 and (Λ)-1. Two chiral space groups, P3 or P63, can be proposed. The choice between the two is not easy: in both cases, the discrepancy between calculated and experimental diffraction patterns is significant, especially for the (004) reflection intensity (d = 4.464 Å). This is not surprising taking into consideration the well-known problem of the disorder of alkyl chains of organic cations as well as twinning effects.26 Considering the recently determined single crystal structure of [N(n-C3H7)3H][MnCr(ox)3],15 the X-ray diffraction pattern of (Δ)-1 and (Λ)-1 is finally refined using the P63 chiral space group with a hexagonal unit cell with a = 9.409(1) Å and c = 17.838(3) Å (Table 1).

For [MnCr] 1, the calculated powder diffraction pattern derived from the atomic co-ordinates of a purely achiral phase leaves some extra peaks unidentified. In view of structural data for (Δ)-1 and (Λ)-1, we fit the experimental profiles as a two-phase pattern corresponding to the sum of an achiral R3c and a chiral P63 phase. Nevertheless, as first recognised in achiral N(n-C3H7)4[MnFe(ox)3] and [N(n-C4H9)4][MnFe(ox)3],27 the deviation from the experimental X-ray powder pattern shall rather be attributed to stacking faults of the honeycomb layers in the polycrystalline samples, e.g. to P63 sequences within an R3c overall structure.

The P63 unit cell and half of the R3c unit cell are shown in Fig. 1. While for the R3c packing there are only MII–MIII nearest neighbour interactions, for the P63 sequence there are exclusively MII–MII nearest neighbour pairs leading to a distinct packing of the anionic layers for the two space groups. Moreover, in the achiral R3c space group, the unit cell contains 6 honeycomb layers with alternate chirality, i.e. (Δ)-Mn and (Λ)-Cr in one layer and (Λ)-Mn and (Δ)-Cr in a neighbouring one (Fig. 1a). They are indeed transformed into one another by a gliding plane. In the three-layered P63 unit cell, each metal ion keeps its configuration from one layer to the other (Fig. 1b) because the anionic layers are transformed into one another other by a 63-screw axis.

Powder diffraction patterns of [FeCr] 2, (Δ)-2 and (Λ)-2 (see ESI†) strongly contrast with those of 1, (Δ)-1 and (Λ)-1. Firstly, the diffraction profiles (hk0) and (00l) are sharp, while the (h0l) and (hkl) reflections are much broader. These observations indicate that the atoms within the layers are strictly long-range ordered while the layer stacking is long-range ordered as well but definitely presents some disorder. Secondly, all three samples have similar diffraction patterns, which may be assigned to a chiral P63 space group. Some weak, broad and unresolved lines related to the R3c sequences can also be distinguished in the diffraction patterns of [FeCr] 2. As for [MnCr] 1, this feature is attributed to stacking faults.27

The most striking result is that the optical purity of the starting anionic block does not play any role in the organisation of the anionic layers in these structures. This means that the substitution of MnII by FeII causes a first order spontaneous resolution during the crystallisation process of the material. Nevertheless, because it is composed of equal quantities of chiral crystallites of opposite configuration, compound 2 does not exhibit any optical activity. Such a sample is called a conglomerate.28 In contrast, (Δ)-2 and (Λ)-2, prepared from (Δ)- or (Λ)-[Cr(ox)3]3− have the same P63 chiral space group AND may show a Cotton effect (vide infra) because only one type of crystallite is present in the powder.

3.1.2. The N(C2H5)(n-C3H7)(n-C4H9)(n-C5H11) containing compounds

For compound [MnCr] 3, the X-ray diffraction pattern (Fig. 2a) is assigned to the R3c achiral space group as reported for single crystal data of 2D networks with [N(n-C4H9)4]+ and [N(n-C3H7)4]+cations.6b Weak extra reflections are once again attributed to stacking faults with P63 sequences. Lattice parameters refined for the trigonal unit cell are a = b = 9.3944(8) Å; c = 51.615(6) Å (Table 1).

Like (Δ)-1 and (Λ)-1, (Δ)-3 and (Λ)-3 were refined in the hexagonal P63 space group with unit cell parameters a = b = 9.3856(25) and c = 17.2009 Å(19) (Fig. 2b). No distinct peaks belonging to an achiral R3c phase have been found despite the non-perfect enantiomeric purity of the resolved (Δ)- and (Λ)-K3[Cr(ox)3] isomers.29 The existence of a three-fold axis indicates that the alkyl chains of the ammonium are not distinguished by powder X-ray diffraction, likely because they are orientationally disordered. The interlayer separation, i.e. the closest Mn–Cr and Mn–Mn distances in racemic R3c and chiral P63 structures respectively are practically the same. This value, d = 8.6 Å, is intermediate between those for 2D networks with symmetric [N(n-C4H9)4]+ (d = 8.95 Å) and [N(n-C3H7)4]+ (d = 8.16 Å) cations.6b

The gliding plane along the c-axis is one of the symmetry operations associated with the R3c space group. This operation transforms one [N(C2H5)(n-C3H7)(n-C4H9)(n-C5H11)]+ situated in one interlayer above a “(Δ)-Cr(ox)3” moiety into its enantiomer in the next interlayer space. This second ammonium is situated above a “(Λ)-Cr(ox)3” moiety. The analysis of the powder diffraction patterns does not allow an unambiguous determination of the absolute configurations of the two chiral centers within these pairs.

The structure analysis of a single crystal of [MnCr] 3 confirms the X-ray powder results. The space group (R3c) and unit cell parameters were found to be the same as those derived from the powder X-ray diffraction analysis (Table 1). A view of the crystal structure along the c axis is shown in Fig. 3.

As expected the nitrogen atoms are located above the chromium(iii) ions. The ammonium cation is disordered around a three-fold axis. Well-defined interlayer separation and the position of nitrogen strongly suggest that the axial group is the n-butyl one. The glide plane symmetry operation automatically generates the opposite configuration of the asymmetric cation when going from one layer to another.

In order to get a better insight into the influence of the divalent cation on a possible first order spontaneous resolution process when a chiral ammonium is present, on one hand we have replaced Mn(ii) by Fe(ii) (compound [FeCr] 4) and, on the other hand, we have replaced the Cr(iii) by Fe(iii) (compound [MnFe] 5). The diffraction patterns of these two compounds are shown in Fig. 4 and 5.

As is clearly seen in Fig. 4, both compounds exhibit a good crystallinity. The diffraction pattern of [FeCr] 4 was refined in the P63 chiral space group (Fig. 4a). Weak reflections, belonging to the R3c phase, are again generated by the presence of stacking faults. The diffraction pattern of [MnFe] 5 was refined in the R3c space group with P63 sequences corresponding to stacking faults (Fig. 5). Hence the situation for the chiral ammonium is totally similar to what was observed for tetrabutylammonium. This confirms the ability of iron(ii) to induce a spontaneous resolution of 2D oxalate-based networks.

3.2. Cotton effects

Compounds (Δ)-1, (Λ)-1,10a (Δ)-2, (Λ)-2, (Δ)-3 and (Λ)-3 (Fig. 6a) and (Δ)-4 and (Λ)-4 (Fig. 6b) show Cotton effects. The two forms of a given compound lead to symmetrical spectra with respect to the wavelength axis. For compounds [MnCr] 1 and [MnCr] 3, the spectra exhibit an ‘extremum’ at 550 nm. For compounds [FeCr] 2 and [FeCr] 4, this ‘extremum’ is located at 570 and 560 nm respectively. The first observation assesses the enantioselectivity of the synthetic process. The observed signals correspond to the d–d transition of Cr(iii). The sign of the Cotton effect is positive for the “(Λ)-Cr(ox)3” and negative for the “(Δ)-Cr(ox)3” moieties in the anionic network.10a The difference in the ‘extremum’ energy shall be attributed to the influence of the divalent metal ion bridged to Cr(iii). In turn, the linkage of the divalent ion to the oxalate ligand slightly modifies the ligand field imposed by the oxalates on the chromium(iii) and hence the electronic transitions centred on Cr(iii). No influence of the ammonium cations on the Cr(iii) transitions is detected. Moreover, in the case of [MnCr] 3 and [FeCr] 4, no additional signal arising from the chiral ammonium cation can be seen. Hopefully it does not mean that the two enantiomers are present in the same proportion. This absence of dichroic signal is indeed correlated to the absence of electronic transitions in this spectral region in such aliphatic ammonium cations.

3.3. Magnetic properties

SQUID magnetization measurements were performed on (Δ)-1, (Λ)-1, (Δ)-2, (Λ)-2, (Δ)-3, (Λ)-3, 4, (Δ)-4, (Λ)-4 and 5.

The molar susceptibility χM has been measured in 0.1 T magnetic field between 2 and 300 K. For all Cr(iii) containing compounds, the χMT product increases monotonically when the temperature is decreased and then diverges at the Curie temperature TC (Fig. 7). In contrast, for 5, the χMT product decreases monotonically when the temperature is decreased and then diverges at the Néel temperature TN. For all compounds, the χM−1versusT curves are linear between 100 and 300 K and can be fitted using a Curie–Weiss law χM−1 = (T − Θ)/C where Θ and C are the Weiss and Curie constants respectively (Table 2). All the compounds exhibit long-range magnetic order at low temperature (Table 2).

The behaviour of the two enantiomers of 1, 2, 3 and 4 in the paramagnetic state confirms the ferromagnetic nature of the exchange interaction between the metal ions for chromium(iii) containing compounds30 while the measurement for 5 are in line with the antiferromagnetic exchange coupling previously observed in iron(iii) containing 2D oxalate-based networks.4 The Weiss constants for the optically active forms of 1, 2, 3 compare well with those previously published.16,30 The differences between the two enantiomers and between the optically active and racemic compounds are within the experimental errors. The Curie–Weiss temperatures for 4, (Δ)-4 and (Λ)-4 are comparable with those observed in 2, (Δ)-2 and (Λ)-2. This also holds for the comparison between 5 and [N(C4H9)4][MnFe(ox)3].4 All the chromium(iii) containing compounds exhibit a ferromagnetic order at low temperature while, for 5, the appearance of a non-zero weak magnetisation is the sign of a canted antiferromagnetic order.

A comparison of the magnetic properties of the compounds obtained using either [N(C4H9)4]+ or [N(C2H5)(n-C3H7)(n-C4H9)(n-C5H11)]+ as template cation but the same couple of magnetic ions, e.g.1 and 3, 2 and 4 and 5 and [N(C4H9)4][MnFe(ox)3] confirms that the magnetic properties of these compounds merely depend on the choice of the template cation. This is a good indication that these compounds behave nearly as ideal 2D magnets. As demonstrated by Obha et al.31 using heterometallic dimers as chemical models and the theoretical approached developed by Kahn,32 both the nature of the exchange coupling and its magnitude is mainly determined by the choice of the two metal ions linked by the oxalate ligand. The fact that the optical purity of the compounds hardly affects their magnetic properties is in line with the 2D nature of the magnetic ordering. As demonstrated by the structural study, optical purity has indeed a noticeable influence on the packing of the honeycomb layers but it does not lead to great changes within these layers and hence, as a direct consequence of Kahn's model, this parameter has a weak influence on the magnetic properties of the compounds.

Conclusions

Pursuing our efforts in the design of optically active magnetic molecular materials, we have synthesised 2D oxalate-based bimetallic anionic networks [MIIMIII(ox)3]− with symmetric [N (n-C4H9)4]+ and asymmetric centred chiral [N(C2H5)(n-C3H7)(n-C4H9)(n-C5H11)]+ ammonium as template cations. Several noticeable features came out of the systematic structural analysis that we have undertaken by powder and single-crystal X-ray diffraction. For MIII = CrIII, it appears that varying MII from Mn2+ to Fe2+ led, when starting from rac-[CrIII(ox)3]3−, to a first-order spontaneous resolution: crystallites belonging to the highly symmetrical P63 hexagonal chiral space group are obtained in equal amounts forming a conglomerate without any optical activity. We have also shown that, whatever the ammonium cation employed, all the 2D networks synthesised in their optically active forms starting from resolved tris(oxalato)chromate(iii) crystallise in the P63 space group. This last result opens the possibility of synthesising these optically active materials by chiral self-assembly starting from resolved and (configurationaly) stable ammonium salts.
