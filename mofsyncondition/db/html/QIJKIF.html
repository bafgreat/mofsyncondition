Introduction

Nowadays, widespread attentions have been paid to hybrid organic–inorganic materials, especially hybrid materials that combine both M–O clusters and organic ligands, not only because of their intriguing varieties of structures, but also owing to their potential applications in the fields of magnetism, catalysis, photochemistry and porous materials.1–5 In the study of hybrid organic–inorganic compounds, organic components, which are introduced as ligands to the secondary metal sites, can act as structure-directing agents to regulate inorganic frameworks, fill the space and balance the charge.6–8 In this area, a development is the modification of inorganic polyoxometalates (POMs) with various organic ligands and transition metal complexes. POMs have been used as one of the major inorganic building blocks to construct multifunctional organic–inorganic hybrid materials due to their properties of diversity of structure, M–O clusters and chemical stability such as oxidative stability, thermal stability and hydrolytic stability.9–13 One of the contemporary focuses in POM chemistry is vanadates, so the rational design and preparation of new hybrid organic–inorganic vanadate compounds with potential applications are significant.14–17 Since the valence state of vanadium varies from +3 to +5, the corresponding coordination geometry of vanadium are octahedra, square pyramid, and tetrahedra. V–O clusters exhibit a range of different dimensional structures due to various polyhedral fragments built of VOn units (n = 4, 5, 6).18 The structure of {VxOy}n− ranges from monomeric [VO4]3− units to polymeric ([V2O7]4−, [V4O12]4−, [V6O18]6−, [V10O28]6−), from [VO3]nn− chains to [VxOy]n− networks.1 Up to now, some hybrid tetravanadates have been reported, such as [Ag(bpy)]4V4O12·2H2O, [Ag(dpa)]4V4O12·4H2O,3 [{Cu(bipy)(en)}{Cu(bipy)(H2O)}{VO3}4]n,19 [Zn2(bbi)2(V4O12)],20 Co(HAep)2(VO3)4, Ni(HAep)2(VO3)4, Cu(HAep)2(VO3)4,21 [Ag(bbi)][{Ag(bbi)}4{Ag3(V4O12)2}]·2H2O.22 With the introduction of transition metal–ligand units, the skeletons and properties of vanadates can be altered.23,24 Thus, this synthetic method can yield new routes to many hybrid vanadate materials. The choice of transition metal and organic ligand, especially the organic ligand, is crucial to the structure of the resulting vanadates. Considering the donor atoms and flexibility of the ligands, organic ligands can chelate a metal center or bridge two metal cations. In order to obtain open frameworks, two possible synthetic routes can be used. Firstly, chelating ligands are used, such as 2,2′-bipyridine, 1,10-phenanthroline,25,26 while the other route comprises the use of bridging ligands, such as 4,4′-bipyridine, 1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)ethane.27,28 The above two routes were chosen to synthesize new vanadates in our work.

Here, we have designed and synthesized two new inorganic–organic hybrid vanadates, [Zn(pyim)]2V4O12 (1) and [Cu(bim)2]2V4O12(H2O)·CH3CH2OH (2), employing different organic ligand coordinated different metal sites as structure-directing agent. 2-(2-Pyridyl)imidazole is used as chelating ligand in the formation of 1, while bis(1-imidazolyl)methane acts as bridging ligand in 2. Structural analysis indicates that layered 1 and 2 are constructed by the building units sine wave-like {V4O12}n4n− chains and {V4O12}4− cycles, respectively.

Experimental section

Materials and physical methods

2-(2-Pyridyl)imidazole (pyim)29 and bis(1-imidazolyl)methane (bim)30 were prepared according to literature methods and characterized by IR spectra, while other chemicals were of reagent grade obtained from commercial sources and used without further purification. C, H, N elemental analyses were performed on a Perkin-Elmer 2400 CHN elemental analyzer. The infrared radiation spectra were recorded as KBr pellets on a Nicolet iS10 FTIR spectrometer. TG analyses were carried out in nitrogen atmosphere from 25 to 800 °C at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 on a Diamond TG/DTA instrument (Perkin-Elmer) thermal analyzer. Solid state fluorescence properties of 1 were carried out using an F-4600 FL Spectrophotometer. Magnetic susceptibility measurements of 2 were performed on a Quantum Design MPMS SQUID magnetometer using single crystal samples. Powder XRD patterns were obtained on Bruker D8X diffractometer equipped with monochromatized Cu-Kα (λ = 0.15418 nm) radiation at room temperature, and the data were collected in the range of 5° ≤ 2θ ≤ 50°.

Synthesis of [Zn(pyim)]2V4O12 (1)

A mixture of NH4VO3 (0.0739 g, 0.63 mmol), Zn(CH3CO2)2·2H2O (0.0988 g, 0.45 mmol), 2-(2-pyridyl)imidazole (0.0302 g, 0.21 mmol) was added into 5 ml H2O and stirred for half an hour. Then the pH was adjusted to about 6.0 with 25% ammonia water, and the solution was stirred for one more hour. At last, the resulting mixture was transferred into a 25 ml Teflon-lined stainless-steel autoclave, heated to 160 °C for 4 days. After cooling to room temperature, the product was washed with water and dried in air for 1 day to give the light-yellow rhombic crystals (0.0592 g, yield 46% based on V). Elemental analysis found (%): C, 24.08; H, 1.59; N, 10.67 (Calcd (%): C, 23.53; H, 1.73; N, 10.29).

Synthesis of [Cu(bim)2]2V4O12(H2O)·CH3CH2OH (2)

A mixture of NH4VO3 (0.3002 g, 2.57 mmol), Cu(CH3CO2)2·H2O (0.1005 g, 0.50 mmol), bis(1-imidazolyl)methane (0.0499 g, 0.34 mmol) was added into a mixture solvent of 2 ml CH3CH2OH and 5 ml H2O and stirred for half an hour. Then the pH was adjusted to about 10.0 with 2.0 mol L−1 NaOH, and was kept on adjusting to about 5.0 with 50% H3PO4. After the solution was stirred for one more hour, the resulting mixture was transferred into a 25 ml Teflon-lined stainless-steel autoclave, heated to 170 °C for 5 days. After cooling to room temperature, the product was washed with water and dried in air for 1 day to give the blue irregular crystals (0.2151 g, yield 28% based on V). Elemental analysis found (%): C, 30.37; H, 3.38; N, 18.91 (Calcd (%): C, 30.09; H, 3.53; N, 18.71).

X-Ray crystallography

The single-crystal XRD data of 1 and 2 were obtained from a Bruker Apex II CCD with Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at 296 K using ω–2θ scan method. The crystal structures were solved by direct method and refined by the full-matrix least-squares methods on F2 using the SHELX-97 program package.31 All of the non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. The hydrogen atoms of organic ligands were refined in calculated positions, assigned isotropic thermal parameters, and allowed to ride on their parent atoms. While the H atoms for disordered CH3CH2OH are not located. The occupied factors for C15, C16 and O17 are 0.5. Further details of the crystal data and structure refinement are listed in Table 1. The selected bond distances and angles for 1 and 2 are listed in Tables S1a and S1b.†

Results and discussion

Synthesis

In the synthesizing process of 1 and 2, many factors, such as pH, transition metal and solvent, can influence the growth and structures of the crystals. For 1, the pH value is one crucial factor in the reaction. Parallel experiments showed that we can only obtain compound 1 when pH = 6.0. The choice of transition metal is another key factor. We only obtained powder when chose other M(CH3CO2)2 (M = Co, Cu, Mn, Ni, Cd). For 2, the pH value is also one important factor. Parallel experiments indicated that compound 2 can be obtained when pH = 4.0, 5.0, 6.0. While the yield is the highest when pH = 5.0. The choice of solvent is another decisive factor. At first, we only used water, methanol or ethanol as single solvent, but we did not get any crystal. So afterwards we took advantage of water and methanol, water and ethanol as mixture solvent. Then we could only obtain compound 2 by using use water and ethanol as mixture solvent. The reactant molar ratio was studied as well. We determined the reproducibility of the two compounds by repeated experiments. According to our experiments, we drew the conclusion that the two compounds have good reproducibility. The most suitable mole ratio of NH4VO3, Zn(CH3CO2)2·2H2O, and 2-(2-pyridyl)imidazole for compound 1 is 12 : 9 : 4, while the ratio of NH4VO3, Cu(CH3CO2)2·H2O, and bis(1-imidazolyl)methane for 2 should be 50 : 10 : 7.

PXRD analyses

PXRD measurements for 1 and 2 were determined at room temperature. As shown in Fig. S1a and S1b,† the diffraction peak positions of the experimental XRD patterns of 1 and 2 are in agreement with that of simulated XRD patterns, which indicates the phase purity of compounds 1 and 2.

IR spectra

The IR spectrum of compound 1 is shown in Fig. S2a.† The band at 953 cm−1 is due to ν(VO), and the bands at 630–810 cm−1 are attributed to ν(O–V–O) and ν(V–Ob). Typically sharp peaks for 2-(2-pyridyl)imidazole are in the range of 1280–1620 cm−1. In addition, the band at 3440 cm−1 corresponds to ν(O–H).

The IR spectrum of 2 is shown in Fig. S2b.† The band at 921 cm−1 is due to ν(VO), and the bands at 658–754 cm−1 are attributed to ν(O–V–O) and ν(V–Ob). The peak region of 1240–1620 cm−1 is the typical vibration mode of bis(1-imidazolyl)methane. Besides, the bands at 2930–3430 cm−1 are due to ν(O–H) and ν(N–H).

Crystal structure of 1

The single-crystal X-ray crystallographic analysis reveals that compound 1 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P. As shown in Fig. 1, the asymmetric unit of 1 is composed of two crystallographically independent ZnII cations, two pyim ligands and four VO4 polyhedra. The layered framework of [Zn(pyim)]2V4O12 consists of uneven heterometallic oxide {Zn2V4O12}n layers with pyim ligands projecting into the interlamellar area, and the pyim subunits interdigitate in the adjacent layers, as shown in Fig. 2.

In the structure of 1, all the vanadium(v) atoms are in tetrahedral coordination environments, each vanadium center is coordinated by four O atoms (Fig. 1). The bond lengths of V–O range from 1.598(3) to 1.828(3) Å and the O–V–O angles range from 106.42(10)° to 112.07(15)° (Table S1a†), which are consistent with previously reported vanadates.19–21 As shown in Fig. 3, there are two types of sine wave-like {V4O12}n4n− chains. Chain A is made up of V(1)O4 and V(3)O4 tetrahedra, while chain B is comprised of V(2)O4 and V(4)O4 tetrahedra. Each VO4 tetrahedron is linked to adjacent two VO4 tetrahedra by sharing the corners. This concatenation gives rise to {V4O12}n4n− chains in the [100] direction. These chains constitute the fundamental motif of layers and arrange in an ABABAB sequence along the [001] direction, while neighboring chains A and B do not overlap with each other (Fig. 3). To the best of our knowledge, although the V–O chains have been found in reported vanadates, the sine wave-like V–O chain has not been observed before. The torsion angles of O–V–O–V of 1 are listed in Table 2, compared with that of previously reported torsion angles,19 the V–O chains in 1 are more tortile.

ZnII cations are in trigonal bipyramid coordination environments (Fig. 1). Each ZnII center is connected with three O atoms from two {V4O12}n4n− chains and two N atoms from one pyim ligand. Trigonal bipyramids link adjacent V–O chains A and B to generate an interesting V–O–Zn layer, as shown in Fig. 4(a). There are three distinct cyclic submotifs in the V–O–Zn network: six-membered {Zn2V4O6} ring, four-membered {Zn2V2O4} ring and five-membered {ZnV4O5} ring, as shown in Fig. 4(b)–4(d).

The organic bidentate ligand pyim is chelating the ZnII center via the nitrogen atom of pyridine and the nitrogen atom of imidazole, perpendicular to the V–O–Zn layer. Owing to the existence of pyim, the connection between layer and layer is cut off. Besides, due to the face-to-face arrangement of the pyim, π–π stacking interactions of ligands can help to stabilize the structure. At the same time, these layers are linked to each other via N(3)–H(3B)⋯O(3), N(6)–H(6)A⋯O(4), and C(8)–H(8)A⋯O(9) hydrogen bonding interactions (Table S2a†) to make a supermolecular structure.

Compared with recently reported 2-D vanadates based on V–O chains such as [{Cu(bipy)(en)}{Cu(bipy)(H2O)}{VO3}4]n,19 [Zn2(bbi)2(V4O12)]20 and M(C6H16N3)2(VO3)4 (M = Co, Ni, Cu),21 the V–O chain in compound 1 is sine wave-like which is more tortile than reported before.

Crystal structure of 2

The single-crystal X-ray crystallographic analysis shows that compound 2 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P2(1)/n. As shown in Fig. 5, the asymmetric unit of 2 consists of two crystallographically different CuII cations, two bim ligands, two VO4 polyhedra, half CH3CH2OH and one water molecule. [Cu(bim)2]2V4O12(H2O)·CH3CH2OH also shows a two-dimensional layered structure (Fig. 6), in which {Cu(V4O12)}2− chains are connected by {Cu(bim)4}2+ subunits.

Resembling compound 1, the vanadium(v) sites of 2 also exhibit distorted tetrahedral geometry (Fig. 5), with the V–O bond distances and O–V–O angles (Table S1b†) in normal range, which is consistent with previously reported {V4O12}4− cycles.3,22,32 As shown in Fig. 7(a), each {V4O12}4− cycle is constructed from four corner-shared VO4 tetrahedra. There are two different CuII cations in 2, and both the CuII cations are in distorted octahedral coordination environments. Cu(1) is coordinated by two O atoms from two {V4O12}4− cycles and four N atoms from four bim ligands (Fig. 7(b)), while Cu(2) center is coordinated by two O atoms of water molecules and four N atoms from four bim ligands (Fig. 7(c)).

The {V4O12}4− cycles share two oxygen atoms with the {Cu(1)N4O2} octahedra, to form {Cu(1)V4O12}2− inorganic chains along the b-axis, which are further linked by {Cu(2)(bim)4}2+ subunits to generate a two-dimensional layer, as shown in Fig. 8. Neighboring layers are connected by hydrogen bonding interactions to make a supermolecular structure, which is due to the interaction between {V4O12}4− unit with water molecule and bim ligand (Table S2b†).

Interestingly, two bim ligands link both Cu(1) and Cu(2) to generate a concave surface {Cu2(bim)2}4+, which acts as host confining a disordered guest ethanol molecule through hydrogen bonding interactions, as shown in Fig. 9.

Compared with recently reported 2-D vanadates based on V–O cycles such as [Ag(bpy)]4V4O12·2H2O, [Ag(dpa)]4V4O12·4H2O,3 [Ag(bbi)][{Ag(bbi)}4{Ag3(V4O12)2}]·2H2O,22 (NH4)4[(V4O12)·(Me10Q[5]@0.5H2O)2]·∼13H2O, Li4(H2O)5[(V4O12)·(Me10Q[5]@H2O)2]·∼20H2O, Na4(H2O)2[(V4O12)·(Q[5])2]·∼15H2O,32 organic ligands are connected with transition metal centers in compounds 2 which may lead to changes of magnetic property. Besides, bim is not used to prepare layered vanadates.

Fluorescence spectrum

The fluorescence spectrum of compound 1 was recorded in solid state at room temperature. As shown in Fig. 10, very strong maximum excitation and emission wavelengths of 1 are observed at 469 nm and 585 nm. Contrastively, the free pyim ligand displays very weak emission at 370 nm in the UV region.33 The maximum emission of 1 has a red shift, which probably is assigned to π–π* transition fluorescence. The enhanced fluorescence efficiency of 1 is attributed to the coordination of the pyim ligand to the Zn2+ ions that effectively increases the rigidity of pyim ligand and reduces the loss of energy by thermal vibrations. The energy difference in Zn ions caused by the coordination environment makes the red shift of the emission from UV light to the green color region.

Magnetic property

In order to investigate the preliminary magnetic properties of 2, the variable-temperature magnetic susceptibility measurements were applied in the range of 2–300 K. The plot of χm, χmT versus T of 2 are shown in Fig. 11. The μeff value of 2 at 300 K is 2.62 μB which is close to that of two non-interacting Cu2+ cations (2.63 μB) with S = 1/2 and assuming g = 2.15, and it gradually decreases to a minimum value of 1.81 μB at 2 K, revealing antiferromagnetic behavior in 2. The magnetic susceptibility data are in accordance with the Curie–Weiss law between 150 and 300 K with constants of C = 1.46 emu · mol · K−1 and θ = −214.28 K. Compared with reported Cu2+ dimers,34 the antiferromagnetic interactions between Cu2+ ions in 2 is much stronger.

Thermogravimetric analyses

Thermal analyses of compounds 1 and 2 were performed in the range of 25–800 °C. As shown in Fig. S3a,† the total weight loss of compound 1 from 25 to 800 °C is 35.87% (Calcd 35.54%), which is due to the removal of pyim.

The weight loss of compound 2 (Fig. S3b†) is 6.42% (Calcd 6.52%) from 25 to 150 °C corresponding to the removal of the H2O and CH3COOH. Meanwhile, the weight loss of 48.38% in the range of 150–800 °C can be ascribed to the removal of bim (Calcd 49.49%).

Conclusions

In summary, two layered hybrid organic–inorganic compounds based on M–V–O framework and different organic ligands were prepared by hydrothermal and solvothermal methods respectively. In the structure of 1 and 2, transition metal, vanadium and oxygen atoms constitute the fundamental framework, and organic ligands chelate a metal center or bridge two metal ions. In 1, Zn, V and O atoms form the fundamental networks and pyim ligands chelate Zn center. The sine wave-like V–O chain in 1 is really rare, which is more twisted than similar V–O chain reported before. In 2, the Cu–V–O atoms make up the fundamental chains, and these chains are connected by bridging bim ligands. The successful synthesis of compounds 1 and 2 provide a method to prepare compounds based on polyoxovanadates. The introduction of different transition metal–ligand units can induce distinct structures and brings diverse properties. The introduction of pyim ligands shows a good fluorescence property in 1. In 2, by variable-temperature magnetic susceptibility analysis, the introduction of transition metal copper provides strong antiferromagnetic interactions between Cu2+ ions.
