Introduction

Fe3+ and Cr2O72− ions are two important ions in daily life. The Fe3+ ion is an essential biological element and plays an important role in many biochemical and physiological processes, such as DNA replication, cellular life cycles, hemoglobin formation, oxygen uptake, electron transfer, and so on,1 while the Cr2O72− ion is an important oxidant in the laboratory and industry.2 However, an excess of Fe3+ ions is harmful to the human body and possibly results in some diseases.3 Also, the continuous usage of Cr2O72− ions may cause cancer and teratogenicity.4 Therefore, the excessive usage of Fe3+ and Cr2O72− ions would give rise to serious environmental pollution. On the other hand, nitrobenzene (NB) belongs to the family of nitroaromatic compounds which are widely used in the explosives, dye and pharmaceutical industries and have become serious pollution sources of groundwater, soil, and other security applications due to their explosivity and toxicity.5 Thus, NB also causes environmental pollution. The selective detection of Fe3+/Cr2O72− ions and NB molecules is urgently required and is of great significance for human health and environmental protection. Therefore, it has become particularly important and necessary to develop a low-cost, high-efficiency and fast-response fluorescence sensing method for the trace detection of Fe3+/Cr2O72− ions and NB molecules.

Porous coordination polymers (PCPs), also known as metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), are a class of highly crystalline organic–inorganic hybrid porous materials constructed by metal ions/clusters and organic ligands.6 MOFs have become promising candidates for applications in many fields including gas storage/separation, catalysis, drug delivery, sensing/detection, etc.7 Moreover, MOFs are also excellent materials for the luminescence detection of metal ions and organic pollutants. Currently, luminescent MOFs for sensing Cr2O72− anions are relatively limited,2,8 in comparison with the ones for sensing Fe3+ cations.1b–g,9 However, luminescent MOFs are reported mostly for sensing Fe3+/Cr2O72− ions in nonaqueous solvents, and thus, to use MOFs as probes to determine Fe3+/Cr2O72− ions in aqueous solutions attracts increasing attention.4b,8b,c,9b,c,e,f Relatively speaking, to date, luminescent MOFs for sensing NB molecules have been extensively investigated.10

Usually, the luminescence of MOFs originates from the organic ligands containing aromatic or conjugated π moieties, metal ions/clusters, or guests encapsulated in the cavities of MOFs. Consequently, the rational design of organic ligands is very important, because the ligand is an extremely useful building block which will directly influence the structures and luminescence properties of the obtained MOFs.11 The 3-position substituted imidazo[1,2-a]pyridine (IP) derivatives are a class of potential heterocyclic ligands with nitrogen as the donor atom. However, coordination compounds based on a 3-position substituted IP ligand have seldom been reported.12,13 Inspired by the potential of imidazo[1,2-a]pyridine derivatives to biological activities as well as recent interest in their coordination behaviors, we have synthesized a new 3-position substituted IP ligand by which one novel porous coordination polymer (PCP), [Zn(L)2]·2DMF (named as USTC-5, USTC = University of Science and Technology of China), was solvothermally synthesized and structurally characterized. This new PCP material reveals selective sensing of Fe3+, Cr2O72−, CrO42−, MnO4− and NB molecules with high quenching constant values and low detection limits. The luminescence quenching mechanisms are also discussed. Moreover, the gas adsorption properties of this MOF are presented. Interestingly, this PCP displays reversible luminescence color conversion induced by protonation–deprotonation processes, making it a stimuli-responsive PCP material.

Experimental

Materials and methods

All the chemicals were AR grade reagents obtained from commercial sources and used without further purification. Microanalytical data (C, H, N) were collected using a Vario ELIII elemental analyzer. FT-IR spectra were recorded using a Bruker EQUINOX55 FT-IR spectrophotometer. 1H NMR spectra were collected on a Bruker Avance 400 MHz NMR spectrometer using DMSO-d6 as the solvent. 13C solid-state magic angle spinning (MAS) NMR experiments have been performed using a Bruker Avance 400 MHz spectrometer using the 4 mm zirconia rotors as sample holders spinning. UV/vis absorption spectra were recorded using a UV-3100 spectrophotometer in aqueous or DMF solutions at 298 K. Photoluminescence (PL) spectra were recorded at room temperature on a Fluorolog-3-TAU fluorescence spectro-photometer. The solid-state quantum yield (absolute value) was measured by the integrating sphere method on a Fluorolog-3-TAU fluorescence spectrophotometer equipped with a BaSO4-coated integrating sphere with the wavelength range of 355–665 nm. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns were collected on a Philips X’pert PRO SUPER diffractometer operating with nickel-filtered Cu-Kα radiation (λ = 1.540598 Å) at 40 kV and 200 mA. Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were performed under nitrogen with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 with a Shimadzu TGA-50H TG analyzer. Gas adsorption/desorption isotherms were recorded using an Omnisorp 100 CX instrument. The specific surface area value was calculated using the BET model. Both N2 and CO2 were of 99.999% purity.

Synthesis of the HL ligand

Substrates 1 and 2 were synthesized according to previous procedures, respectively,12b,14 A flask was loaded with substrate 1 (2.62 g, 8 mmol), substrate 2 (2.97 g, 8.8 mmol) and ammonium acetate (30.83 g, 400 mmol). Then acetic acid (80 mL) was added. The resulting mixture was stirred at 120 °C for 24 h. After being cooled to room temperature, the resulting solid was filtered and washed with H2O and ethanol, and then recrystallized by DMF/H2O. Finally, the yellow 4-(4-(2-chloroimidazo[1,2-a]pyridin-3-yl)-[2,3′-bipyridin]-6-yl)benzoic acid (Scheme 1, HL) was obtained (1.40 g, 41.2% yield). 1H NMR (DMSO-d6, 400 MHz, Fig. S1, ESI†): δ 13.05 (s, 1H), 9.47 (s, 1H), 8.73 (d, J = 6.5 Hz, 2H), 8.66 (d, J = 7.8 Hz, 1H), 8.42 (d, J = 8.0 Hz, 2H), 8.35 (d, J = 9.6 Hz, 2H), 8.12 (d, J = 8.0 Hz, 2H), 7.72 (d, J = 9.0 Hz, 1H), 7.64–7.58 (m, 1H), 7.51 (t, J = 7.9 Hz, 1H), 7.12 (t, J = 6.7 Hz, 1H). IR (KBr, cm−1): 1702s, 1662m, 1603vs, 1547m, 1496m, 1420ws, 1398w, 1345m, 1329m, 1310s, 1280m, 1257s, 1195s, 777m, 754m, 696m.

Solvothermal synthesis of [Zn(L)2]·2DMF (USTC-5)

A mixture of Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (44.0 mg, 0.15 mmol), HL ligand (43.0 mg, 0.10 mmol), and DMF (10.0 mL) was placed into a 25 mL Teflon-lined stainless autoclave. The autoclave was sealed and heated at 100 °C for 24 h under autogenous pressure and static conditions. The resulting crystals were collected by filtration and washed with H2O and DMF, and then dried in air. Yellow single crystals of USTC-5, suitable for X-ray single-crystal diffraction analysis, were obtained. TGA indicates the existence of adsorptive waters on USTC-5 (Fig. 1). The weight loss of 5.0% from room temperature to 70 °C corresponds to the loss of adsorptive three H2O molecules (calc.: 4.8%). TGA and elemental analysis further provided evidence for the un-coordinating DMF in the channels of this PCP. These DMF molecules were further identified using a combination of TGA (Fig. 1) and elemental analysis. Anal. calc.: C, 58.01; H, 4.30; N, 12.53%. Found: C, 56.07; H, 4.10; N, 11.62%. The weight loss of 13.0% between 70 °C and 202 °C corresponds to the loss of two DMF molecules (calc.: 13.1%). Thus, the formula of USTC-5 is [Zn(L)2]·2DMF (Yield: 33.0 mg, 0.031 mmol, 62.0% based on HL). IR (KBr, cm−1): 1659w, 1602vs, 1551s, 1499w, 1388s, 1310s, 1254m, 1020m, 789m, 706m.

Luminescence sensing experiments

First, the as-synthesized USTC-5 material was dried to remove the guest molecules by evacuation under vacuum at 190 °C for 12 h. TGA shows that guest DMF molecules were removed completely for activated USTC-5 (Fig. 1). The PXRD pattern of activated USTC-5 confirms the stability of the framework in the absence of guest molecules. (Fig. S2, ESI†), Then, the ground sample of activated USTC-5 (5 mg) was dispersed in an aqueous solution (5 mL) of M(NO3)x (Mx+ = K+, Na+, Ca2+, Mg2+, Al3+, Zn2+, Cd2+, Pb2+, Mn2+, Co2+, Ni2+, Cu2+, Ag+, or Fe3+, 0.01 mol L−1) or KyA (Ay− = Cl−, Br−, I−, ClO3−, BrO3−, NO3−, CO32−, HCO3−, SO42−, H2PO4−, SCN−, OAc−, C2O42−, Cr2O72−, CrO42− or MnO4−, 0.01 mol L−1) at room temperature. After the ground sample of activated USTC-5 was dispersed in different concentrations of Fe3+ or Cr2O72− aqueous solutions for 10 min, the loading amounts of Fe3+ or Cr2O72− in USTC-5 were then analyzed by ICP, and the results are provided in Table S1 (ESI†). Luminescence data of the suspension were collected after 3 min.

For the sensing of organic solvents, the ground sample of activated USTC-5 (5 mg) was dispersed in various solvents (5 mL) of DMF, DMA (N,N-dimethyl acetamide), DMSO, NMP (N-methylpyrrolidone), MeOH, EtOH, acetone, 1,4-dioxane, THF, CH3CN, CH2Cl2, CHCl3, toluene, or NB at room temperature. Luminescence data of suspension were collected after 3 min.

Typical procedure for fluorescence titration studies

The ground sample of activated USTC-5 (5 mg) was dispersed in solution (5 mL) and subsequently transferred to a quartz cell of 1 cm width. Quenching tests were carried out by changing the concentration of Fe3+/Cr2O72− (CrO42− or MnO4−) aqueous solutions, or NB's DMF solutions.

Recyclable luminescence experiments

The reproducibility of USTC-5 towards sensing Fe3+, Cr2O72−, CrO42−, MnO4− and NB molecules was studied. After the first quenching experiment, the USTC-5 powder was recovered by centrifugation and washed with water (Fe3+, Cr2O72−, CrO42− or MnO4−) or DMF (NB). The recovered solid was collected and subsequently used in the successive sensing experiments.

Single-crystal X-ray crystallography

The X-ray diffraction measurement was performed at 291(2) K on a Gemini S Ultra CCD diffractometer (Oxford diffraction Ltd) using graphite monochromated Cu-Kα (λ = 1.54184 Å). The structure was solved by direct methods (SHELXL) and completed by the difference Fourier method (SHELXL). Refinement was performed against F2 by weighted full-matrix least-squares (SHELXL), and empirical absorption correction (SCALE3 ABSPACK) was applied. All non-hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic displacement parameters. The hydrogen atoms were generated geometrically and refined by the riding mode. The weighted R factor (Rw) and all goodness of S are based on F2, and the conventional R factor (R) is based on F. The SQUEEZE option of PLATON15 was used to calculate the solvent disordered area and remove their contribution to the overall intensity data. The final chemical formula of USTC-5 was obtained from crystal data combined with the results of elemental and thermogravimetric analyses. Selected bond lengths and bond angles are given in Table S2 (ESI†).

Results and discussion

Syntheses and crystal structure

As shown in Scheme 1, the HL ligand was synthesized by the Kröhnke pyridine synthesis method14a from substrates 1 and 2; then, USTC-5 was solvothermally synthesized. The complete deprotonation of the carboxylic group of the HL ligand to give L− in USTC-5 was confirmed by IR spectral data (no 1702 cm−1 of carboxylic group characteristic band of HL ligand, Fig. S3, ESI†). The USTC-5 sample was identified by elemental analysis and TGA (see the Experimental section for details). The phase purity was demonstrated by the experimental and calculated powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns from the single crystal data of USTC-5 (Fig. S2, ESI†).

The single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis indicates that USTC-5 crystallizes in the monoclinic crystal system and P21/c space group.‡ The asymmetric unit consists of two L− ligands and one Zn(ii) ion. As shown in Fig. 2, the coordination geometry of the central Zn1(ii) ion is a distorted octahedron, in which the Zn(ii) ion is coordinated to four carboxylate oxygen atoms (O1a, O2a, O3a and O4a) in the chelating fashion from two L− ligands, and two pyridyl nitrogen atoms (N1 and N5) from other two L− ligands. The Zn1–O and Zn1–N bond distances vary from 2.0776(17) to 2.354(2) Å, and 2.0702(17) to 2.0806(18) Å, respectively (Table S2, ESI†). The Zn–O/N bond lengths are all consistent with the corresponding bond lengths reported in the literature.16 Two L− ligands bridge two Zn(ii) centres to form a rectangle grid (Fig. 3). Each rectangle grid connects to adjacent ones through the metal nodes to form a spiro-like one-dimensional (1D) chain along the a-axis (Fig. 3). Moreover, two 2-chloroimidazo[1,2-a]pyridine rings are located at two apices of each rectangle grid (Fig. 3).

As shown in Fig. 4, 1D chains are further organized by intermolecular close contacts (C3⋯N8 of 3.304 Å) to build a two-dimensional (2D) layer lying in the ac plane, which is further assembled into a porous three-dimensional (3D) supra-molecular framework (Fig. 5) via intermolecular close contacts (C31⋯O4 3.165 Å, C28⋯O2 3.444 Å and C45⋯N6 3.410 Å). There exist the quasi-hexagonal channels along the b-axis with a pore size of ∼10.6 Å, showing a larger guest-free porous framework (Fig. 5). The channels are filled by disordered DMF solvent molecules in the as-prepared crystals of USTC-5. The solvent molecules are established to be two DMF molecules per [Zn(L)2] unit by elemental and TG analyses. The solvent accessible volume is 28.5% (1509.5 Å3) per unit cell (5295.5 Å3) estimated by using PLATON.17 It is noteworthy that this porous supramolecular framework (USTC-5) is thermally stable up to 350 °C which can be clarified from TGA and PXRD experiments. The as-synthesized USTC-5 samples were dried to remove the solvent (DMF and/or H2O) molecules by evacuation under vacuum at 190 °C for 12 h to afford the activated USTC-5. TGA shows that the solvent molecules were removed completely for activated USTC-5 (Fig. 1). The PXRD pattern of activated USTC-5 confirms the stability of supramolecular frameworks in the absence of guest solvent molecules (Fig. S2, ESI†).

Gas adsorption behaviors

In order to study the nature of the pores and gas adsorption properties of USTC-5, the gas adsorption isotherms are measured for N2 and CO2, respectively. The N2 adsorption isotherm of the activated USTC-5 sample is measured at 77 K (Fig. S4, ESI†). Surprisingly, the activated USTC-5 only has a very low amount of N2 gas adsorption with a total pore volume of 0.0067 cm3 g−1 and BET surface area of 2.49 m2 g−1. As a result, the activated USTC-5 only possesses a very low N2 gas uptake value of ∼4.3 cm3 g−1 (0.54 wt%) at 77 K and P/P0 = 1.0 (Fig. S4, ESI†). As shown in Fig. S4 (ESI†), a broad N2 gas adsorption/desorption hysteresis implies the flexibility18 of supramolecular frameworks. The CO2 and N2 gas adsorption isotherms of activated USTC-5 were measured at 273 K (Fig. 6). At 273 K and P/P0 = 1.0, the activated USTC-5 exhibits a CO2 adsorption capacity of ∼34.26 cm3 g−1 (6.85 wt%), which is obviously greater than the N2 gas adsorption capacity of ∼1.41 cm3 g−1 (0.18 wt%). CO2 gas is known to have better adsorptivity than N2 gas, due to the higher quadrupole moment. The channels of the activated USTC-5 are uniformly decorated with Lewis basic pyridinyl sites, which can have strong interactions with incoming CO2 adsorbate molecules.19 The CO2/N2 selectivity (S) is calculated by the Henry's constants (KH) of the gases, according to: S(CO2/N2) = KH(CO2)/KH(N2),20 thus, the CO2/N2 selectivity (S) of USTC-5 at 273 K is 35.94 (KH(CO2) = 55.525/KH(N2) = 1.545, Fig. S5 (ESI†)). Therefore, USTC-5 is a better CO2 adsorption, and/or a promising CO2/N2 selective PCP material.14b

Photoluminescence (PL) properties of HL and USTC-5

Solid-state excitation and emission spectra of the HL ligand and USTC-5 are presented in Fig. 7. The free HL displays a main emission peak at 509 nm following two shoulders at 447 and 408 nm (λex = 365 nm), which are probably attributable to the π–π* or n–π* electron transitions.21USTC-5 reveals a blue-shifted PL spectrum with an emission peak at 424 nm (λex = 360 nm) which stems from the ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT).22 The luminescence quantum yield (Φ) of the HL ligand and USTC-5 is 6.9% (at 509 nm) and 2.2% (at 424 nm), respectively.

Sensing of cations

The intense blue luminescence of USTC-5 (Fig. 7) indicates that it is a desirable candidate for sensing common metal cations. The ground samples of activated USTC-5 were dispersed in the aqueous solutions of M(NO3)x (0.01 mol L−1, Mx+ = K+, Na+, Ca2+, Mg2+, Al3+, Zn2+, Cd2+, Pb2+, Mn2+, Co2+, Ni2+, Cu2+, Ag+ and Fe3+, respectively). Interestingly, the luminescence intensity of USTC-5 reveals a forceful quenching effect via the addition of Fe3+ ions (Fig. 8a, inset). Conversely, other metal ions have no obvious effects on the luminescence intensity of USTC-5 (Fig. 8a and Fig. S6, ESI†), indicating that USTC-5 can be used as a promising luminescent probe for sensing Fe3+ ions in aqueous solution. To investigate the influence of the Fe3+ concentration on luminescence intensity of USTC-5, luminescence spectra were collected under different Fe3+ ion concentrations. As shown in Fig. 8b, on increasing the Fe3+ ion concentration from 1 × 10−5 to 5 × 10−3 mol L−1, the luminescence intensity of USTC-5 suspensions gradually decreases. In particular, luminescence was almost completely quenched, when dispersed in 3 × 10−3 mol L−1 aqueous solution of Fe3+ ions. In order to further explore the linear correlation of Fe3+ concentration on the luminescence intensity of USTC-5 suspensions, a series of aqueous solutions of Fe3+ ions in the concentration range of 1 × 10−5 to 1 × 10−3 mol L−1 were prepared. As shown in Fig. S7 (ESI†), there is a good linear correlation between the quenching level (I0/I) and the concentration of Fe3+ ions. Quantitatively, the quenching constant (Ksv), calculated from the Stern–Volmer equation (I0/I = Ksv[M] + 1), can be used to explain the quenching efficiency. Here, I0 and I are the luminescence intensity of the USTC-5 suspended in the aqueous solution in the absence and presence of Fe3+ ions, respectively; [M] is the molar concentration of Fe3+ ions. As indicated in the Stern–Volmer plot of USTC-5, in the low concentration region (1 × 10−5 to 1 × 10−4 mol L−1, Fig. S8, ESI†), the Ksv is 9.85 × 103 L mol−1, which is consistent with those of previously reported MOF-based sensors (Table S3, ESI†). The detection limit for Fe3+ ions was further calculated by 3σ/k (σ: standard error of the fitting line; k: slope (Fig. S8, inset, ESI†))4b to be 1.91 μmol L−1, which is relatively high, compared to the reported example.4b Moreover, the luminescence intensity of USTC-5 is easily recovered by washing with water several times, and the recovered USTC-5 sample can be again used for five cycles, and the quenching efficiencies remain high (above 92.8%) (Fig. S9, ESI†).

To study the possible luminescence sensing mechanism for Fe3+ ions, the PXRD patterns of the USTC-5 sample after sensing Fe3+ ions and the recycled sample after five cycles are almost the same as that of the as-synthesized USTC-5 powder (Fig. S10, ESI†). The results verified that the supramolecular framework of USTC-5 still remains intact after sensing Fe3+ ions and recyclability experiments, implying that the luminescence quenching is not caused by collapse of the framework. It is also very difficult for the neutral USTC-5 to capture Fe3+ ions through the cationic exchange process. According to the UV-vis absorption spectra (Fig. S11, ESI†), only the Fe3+ ion aqueous solution displays a strong absorption band from 230 to 400 nm, while other metal ions have no significant absorption band in this wavelength range. We speculate that electron-deficient Fe3+ ions (due to their unsaturated outer layer electronic state) should tend to interact with uncoordinated pyridine-type N atoms of the ligand (Fig. 3), which tend to give electrons, leading to the luminescence quenching of USTC-5.23 Moreover, the quenching mechanism could be ascribed to the electron transfer from the electron-rich PCP to the electron-deficient Fe3+ ions.24 On the other hand, the UV-vis absorption peak of Fe3+ ion aqueous solution exhibits little overlap with the excitation spectrum of USTC-5 (Fig. S11, ESI†), therefore, there is no sufficient evidence for competitive adsorption between Fe3+ and USTC-5.25

In fact, the selective detection of specific metal ions among mixed metal ions is highly required. Therefore, the selective sensing and anti-interference detection of USTC-5 were investigated by competitive experiments. An Fe3+ aqueous solution (1 mL, 0.001 mol L−1) was separately added to aqueous solutions containing USTC-5 with various metal ions (3 mL, 0.01 mol L−1), and the changes in the emission intensities were recorded. As shown in Fig. S12 (ESI†), it is obvious that the quenching efficiency of Fe3+ ions mixed with other metal (interfering) ions was effective, demonstrating that the Fe3+ ions can be highly selectively sensed.

Sensing of anions

The same procedure was used to investigate the sensing properties for anions by using KyA (Ay− = Cl−, Br−, I−, ClO3−, BrO3−, NO3−, CO32−, HCO3−, SO42−, H2PO4−, SCN−, OAc−, C2O42−, Cr2O72−, CrO42− or MnO4−) aqueous solutions (0.01 mol L−1). As shown in Fig. 9, the quenching effect of Cr2O72−, CrO42− and MnO4− ions is very obvious, meanwhile other anions only reveal slight effects on the luminescence intensities (Fig. 9 and Fig. S13, ESI†). As indicated in Fig. S14–S16 (ESI†), the luminescence intensity is gradually decreased with the increase of the Cr2O72−, CrO42− or MnO4− ion concentrations from 1 × 10−5 to 1 × 10−3 mol L−1, in which luminescence was almost completely quenched in 8 × 10−4 mol L−1 aqueous solution of Cr2O72− or MnO4− ions. Moreover, there is also a good linear correlation between the quenching level (I0/I) and the concentration of Cr2O72−, CrO42− or MnO4− ions (Fig. S17–S19, ESI†). Furthermore, the calculated quenching constant Ksv for Cr2O72−, CrO42− and MnO4− is found to be 1.25 × 104, 1.34 × 104 and 1.92 × 104 L mol−1, respectively, as verified by linear fitting of the Stern–Volmer plot in a low concentration range (Fig. S20–S22, ESI†). The detection limit of USTC-5 for Cr2O72−, CrO42− and MnO4− ions is 1.45, 11.40 and 23.40 μmol L−1, respectively. Notably, there are rare MOFs which possess luminescence quenching for Cr2O72−, CrO42−and MnO4− ions (Table S3, ESI†). The PXRD patterns of USTC-5 after five cycles for sensing Cr2O72−−, CrO42− and MnO4− ions are still in good agreement with the as-synthesized one (Fig. S10, ESI†), indicating that the framework of USTC-5 remains unchanged. According to the UV-vis absorption spectra (Fig. S23, ESI†), only the overlaps between Cr2O72−, CrO42−and MnO4− ion aqueous solutions and the excitation spectrum of USTC-5 were observed, indicating that the competitive adsorption of excitation wavelength energy between USTC-5 and Cr2O72−, CrO42−or MnO4− ions is responsible for the luminescence quenching.2,25 It should be indicated that the interaction between USTC-5 and Cr2O72− (also including CrO42− or MnO4−) is weak, which can be supported by very low loading amounts of Cr2O72− in USTC-5, compared to that of Fe3+ ions (Table S1, ESI†). The relatively high loading amounts of Fe3+ ions (10 times higher than that of Cr2O72−, Table S1, ESI†) might be ascribed to the interaction between electron-rich PCPs and electron-deficient Fe3+ ions, but, USTC-5 and Cr2O72− (CrO42− or MnO4−) ions do not show such interactions. In addition, the recyclability experiments (five cycles) for Cr2O72−, CrO42− and MnO4− ions display high quenching efficiencies (above 98.0%) (Fig. S24–S26, ESI†).

Moreover, selective sensing and anti-interference detection of USTC-5 for Cr2O72−, CrO42− and MnO4− ions were also performed by competitive experiments. The Cr2O72−, CrO42− or MnO4− aqueous solution (1 mL, 0.001 mol L−1) was separately added to aqueous solutions containing USTC-5 with various anion ions (3 mL, 0.01 mol L−1), and the changes in the emission intensities were recorded. As shown in Fig. S27 (ESI†), it is obvious that the quenching efficiency of Cr2O72−, CrO42− and MnO4− ions mixed with other interfering anion ions was effective, verifying that the Cr2O72−, CrO42− and MnO4− ions can be highly and selectively sensed.

Sensing of organic solvents

The interesting luminescence sensing features of USTC-5 stimulate us to further explore its sensing properties for organic solvents, such as, DMF, DMA, DMSO, NMP, MeOH, EtOH, acetone, 1,4-dioxane, THF, CH3CN, CH2Cl2, CHCl3, toluene and NB. As shown in Fig. 10a and Fig. S28 (ESI†), the luminescence intensity obviously depends on the type of organic solvent, in which the NB molecule reveals a pronounced quenching effect. Such sensitive quenching behavior is of great interest for sensing trace amounts of NB molecules.10e,26 To better understand the quenching effect of NB, the ground sample of activated USTC-5 was dispersed in DMF solutions with gradually increasing NB contents to adjust the emission response. As expected, as the NB concentration increases from 2.6 × 10−4 to 2.2 × 10−2 mol L−1, the USTC-5 sample exhibits obvious quenching in ∼2 × 10−2 mol L−1 DMF solution of NB molecules (Fig. 10b). There is also a good linear correlation between the quenching level (I0/I) and the concentration of NB (Fig. S29, ESI†). As shown in Fig. S30 (ESI†), the Stern–Volmer plot shows a good linear correlation. The Ksv value for NB in USTC-5 is 7.7 × 102 L mol−1 which is consistent with those of previously reported MOF-based sensors (Table S3, ESI†), while the detection limit for NB is 12.0 μmol L−1. As for the quenching mechanism of USTC-5 towards NB, we speculate that it is possibly ascribed to the competitive adsorption of excitation wavelength energy between USTC-5 and NB molecules. First, the luminescence quenching does not come from the collapse of the framework owing to the structural integrity after sensing NB, as confirmed by PXRD patterns (Fig. S10, ESI†). Second, the UV-vis absorption spectra display an overlap between the absorption band of NB and the excitation wavelength of USTC-5 (Fig. S31, ESI†). Furthermore, as an electron-withdrawing group, the nitro group can lead to conjugation to stabilize the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital of the aromatic compounds, resulting in the electrons shifting from the PCP framework to the electron-deficient nitrobenzene, and thus, leading to the luminescence quenching.24 The recyclable performance of USTC-5 in NB was also investigated, which reveals that the quenching efficiency is maintained above 99.0% for five cycles (Fig. S32, ESI†).

Switchable luminescence via acid–base vapor stimuli

USTC-5 reveals another unique and interesting function, i.e. the protonation–deprotonation by acid–base vapor can induce reversible luminescence switches. When the ground samples of activated USTC-5 (50 mg) were exposed to HCl vapor in a closed container within 4 min, blue luminescence of USTC-5 (Fig. 11, inset (upper)) was transferred to green light (Fig. 11, inset (middle)). On the other hand, after subsequent exposure of HCl vapor-disposed USTC-5 to NH3 vapor in a closed container within 10 min, green light can be back to blue luminescence (Fig. 11, inset (bottom)). The conversion between different luminescence colors is reversible in nature, that is, USTC-5 turns into the corresponding HCl vapor-disposed sample by using HCl vapor, and then can be again back to USTC-5 by using NH3 vapor (Fig. 11). As a result, the acid–base vapor can switch luminescence colors of USTC-5. From Fig. 11, it was found out that the protonation of USTC-5 by HCl vapor led to red-shifted emission (from 424 to 501 nm), and thus the blue luminescence was transferred to green light (Fig. 11, inset (middle)). However, NH3 vapor can recover its blue luminescence (Fig. 11, inset (bottom)).

Such HCl vapor-induced luminescence color transformation should be ascribed to the protonation effect of pyridine-type nitrogen atoms in the USTC-5 framework, which can be demonstrated by the IR spectrum of HCl vapor-disposed USTC-5. As shown in Fig. S33b (ESI†), one can find out that the protonation of USTC-5 by HCl vapor led to the appearance of a new peak at 1697 cm−1 assigned to the νCN stretching vibration of the protonated 2-chloroimidazo[1,2-a]pyridine or pyridine rings (CN+–H); however, this IR peak would disappear after further treatment with NH3 vapor (Fig. S33c, ESI†). Moreover, the 13C solid-state NMR spectrum of HCl vapor-disposed USTC-5 exhibits similar carbon signals to that of USTC-5 itself (Fig. S34, ESI†), implying the molecular structural integrity of the ligand after being exposed to HCl vapor. Therefore, the luminescence switching performance does actually result from the protonation–deprotonation switching of the 2-chloro-imidazo[1,2-a]pyridine or pyridine rings in USTC-5. Thus, when a microcrystalline powder of USTC-5 was exposed to HCl vapor, a protonation process for 2-chloro-imidazo[1,2-a]pyridine rings takes place (Fig. S35, ESI†), and the crystals lose crystallinity to become amorphous27 (Fig. S36b, ESI†), and the luminescence color changes from blue to green (Fig. 11, inset). The amorphous form is possibly attributed to the collapse of the supramolecular framework of USTC-5, because the protonation of 2-chloroimidazo[1,2-a]pyridine rings would affect the intermolecular close contact between C3 and N8 (Fig. 4), resulting in the collapse of the 2D supramolecular layer. Surprisingly, this change is reversible: when HCl vapor-disposed USTC-5 was subsequently exposed to NH3 vapor, the amorphous powder recovers the crystallinity of USTC-5 due to the deprotonation by NH3 vapor (Fig. S36c, ESI†), and the luminescence color again changes to blue from green (Fig. 11, inset). Such switchable behaviors in response to external HCl and NH3 vapor stimuli might be of great importance for practical application in optical recording, sensing, or molecular devices and switches.

Conclusions

In conclusion, on the basis of a new 3-position substituted IP ligand and zinc ion, a novel PCP (USTC-5) has been solvothermally synthesized and characterized. This PCP possesses larger quasi-hexagonal channels with better CO2/N2 selective adsorption. This blue-emitting luminescent PCP material serves as a multi-responsive luminescent sensor, capable of sensing Fe3+, Cr2O72−, CrO42−, MnO4− and NB. More interestingly, USTC-5 also exhibits reversible luminescence color switching in response to acid–base vapor stimuli, which originates from the conversion between the amorphous form and the crystal mediated by protonation–deprotonation processes. This work demonstrates that luminescent PCPs can be rationally designed to reveal excellent luminescence sensing properties for the detection of different analytes, and reversible stimulus-responsiveness.
