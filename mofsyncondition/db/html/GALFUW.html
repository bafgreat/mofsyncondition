Introduction

Design and construction of metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) with novel topologies and porous features has been motivated by their diversified functions with potential application such as electrochemistry, photophysics, catalysis, adsorption and separation.1,2 Consequently, many examples of MOFs with interesting compositions and structures have been synthesized through taking various factors into account, such as the metal/ligand nature, solvent, templates, and counterions.3

It is well known that carboxylates are universally employed ligands in MOFs chemistry, which may adopt diverse binding modes such as syn–syn, syn–anti, anti–anti.4 Very interesting and promising results have been obtained by using phenyldicarboxylates ligands as efficient linkers. Subsequently, it is confirmed that the existence of a noncoordinating electron-donating or electron-withdrawing groups on the aromatic backbone of some phenyldicarboxylates ligands may have a significant effect on the assembly of the coordination networks in our previous studies.5

Now, we are interested in extending the facile synthetic strategy from phenyldicarboxylates to heterocyclic dicarboxylates. To the best of our knowledge, 2,3-pyridinedicarboxylic acid (H2PYDC) is among the common heterocyclic carboxylic acids, and a number of transition metal–H2PYDC complexes with low-dimensional structures such as discrete,6 1D7 or 2D motifs7b,7c,8 have been documented so far, however, only a small amount of the literature has concerned metal complexes of H2PYDC with 3D structures.9 The reasons which prevent extension into high-dimensional MOFs are described as follows:7b,9b (1) It prefers to chelate metal centers through one nitrogen atom of the pyridine ring and an oxygen atom of the α-carboxyl group to form five-membered rings, and the β-carboxyl group remains uncoordinated.6a,6b (2) Decarboxylation of the α-carboxyl group was observed for the H2PYDC under hydrothermal conditions.10 In order to overcome these problems, we chose the versatile H2EPDA ligand, as a derivative of H2PYDC, to assemble MOFs. In comparison with H2PYDC, H2EPDA (5-ethyl-pyridine-2,3-dicarboxylic acid) remains largely unexplored hitherto in the field of coordination polymers, and this ligand can probably provide the potential to enrich the unpredictable and interesting structural and functional diversities of network structures due to the existence of a noncoordinating electron-donating group (-CH2CH3) on the pyridine backbone. Furthermore, the rodlike and symmetrically bridging ligands, such as BPP (1,3-di(4-pyridyl)propane), BPE (1,2-di(4-pyridyl)ethylene) and BPA (1,2-bi(4-pyridyl)ethane)) with different carbon backbones between the two 4-pyridyl rings, can modify the structures and properties of the resulting materials by cooperative coordination together with carboxylate group to meet the requirement of coordination geometries of metal ions in the assembly process.11 Thus, molecule size and the flexibility of dipyridyl-type ligands are also vital in determining the structure of the coordination architectures in addition to the influences imposed by the possible coordination environments of the transition metal. Additionally, the d10 metals are often used to build novel coordination architectures because some of its polymeric compounds exhibit photoluminescence properties.12

With these considerations in mind, we report herein the binding of H2EPDA and dipyridyl-type ligands to Zn(ii) centers forming three new metal–organic frameworks with interesting structural topologies, porous features and physicochemical properties. In addition, thermal degeneration of all three complexes are also given. The influence of dipyridyl linkers and solvent on the final structures of the assemblies is discussed (see Scheme 1).

Experimental

Materials and physical measurements

All chemicals were used as obtained without further purification. Elemental analyses for C, H and N were performed on a Vario EL III elemental analyzer. Infrared spectra were recorded as KBr pellets using an Avatar(tm) 360 E. S. P. IR spectrophotometer over a range 4000–400 cm−1. The luminescence spectra were performed on an Aminco Bowman Series 2 Luminescence spectrometer at room temperature. The thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were carried out on a STA449C integration thermal analyzer. The crystal data collection were recorded with a Bruker SMART APEX II CCD diffractometer with a scanning rate of 4° min−1.

Syntheses of complexes

Synthesis of {[Zn2(EPDA)2(BPP)2]·(H2O)}n (1)

The mixtures of H2EPDA (0.1 mmol, 19.5 mg), BPP (0.2 mmol, 39.7 mg), Zn(Ac)2·2H2O (0.1 mmol, 21.9 mg), ethanol (4.0 ml), and H2O (4.0 ml) were sealed in a 23 ml Teflon-lined autoclave, heated to 120 °C for 4 days, then cooled at 5 °C h−1 to room temperature. Colorless block crystals were obtained. Elemental analysis calcd (%) for C44H43N6O9Zn2: C 57.03, H 4.68, N 9.07; Found: C 56.27, H 4.82, N 9.02. Selected IR (KBr, cm−1): 3325(w), 1669(s), 1575(m), 1454(w), 1437(m), 1366(s), 1339(s), 704(s), 660(s).

Synthesis of [Zn3(EPDA)2(HEPDA)2(BPE)]n (2)

A mixture of H2EPDA (0.2 mmol, 39.1 mg), BPE(0.1 mmol, 18.2 mg), Zn(Ac)2·2H2O (0.1 mmol, 21.9 mg), methanol (4.0 ml), and H2O (4.0 ml) was placed in a 23 ml Teflon-lined autoclave, heated to 120 °C for 4 days, and then cooled at 5 °C h−1 to room temperature. Colorless block crystals were obtained. Elemental analysis (%): calcd for C48H40N6O16Zn3: C 50.26, H 3.51, N 7.33; Found: C 50.78, H 3.49, N 7.29. IR (KBr, cm−1): 1605(s), 1561(s), 1506(w), 1455(m), 1387(s), 1360(s), 776(m), 687(w).

Synthesis of [Zn3(EPDA)2(HEPDA)2(BPA)]n (3)

A mixture of H2EPDA (0.2 mmol, 39.1 mg), BPA (0.1 mmol, 18.6 mg), Zn(Ac)2·2H2O (0.1 mmol, 21.9 mg), KOH (0.1mmol), ethanol (4.0 ml) and H2O (4.0 ml) was placed in a 23 ml Teflon liner stainless steel reactor. The vessel was heated to 120 °C for 4 days, and then cooled at 5 °C h−1 to room temperature. Colorless block crystals were obtained. Elemental analysis calcd (%) for C48H42N6O16Zn3: C 50.18, H 3.68, N 7.31; Found: C 50.22, H 3.62, N 7.26. Selected IR (KBr, cm−1): 1607(s), 1559(s), 1456(m), 1370(s), 1318(w), 777(m), 689(w).

X-Ray crystallography

Crystals of the three title complexes with suitable size were mounted on a Bruker Smart APEX II CCD diffractometer equipped with graphite-monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.071073 nm) by using the φ/ω scan technique at room temperature. Absorption corrections were based on symmetry equivalent reflections using the SADABS program.13 The structures were solved by direct methods with SHELXS-97 and followed by successive Fourier and difference Fourier syntheses and refined by full-matrix least-squares on F2.14 All non-hydrogen atoms in three complexes were refined with anisotropic thermal parameters. The hydrogen atoms were placed in calculated positions and refined isotropically with a riding model except for those bound to water molecules and hydroxyl groups, which were initially located in a difference Fourier map and included in the final refinement by use of geometrical restraints with the O–H distances being fixed at 0.85 Å for the hydroxyl groups and 0.92 Å for water molecules, and Uiso(H) equivalent to 1.5 times of Ueq(O). The EPDA2− dianion in complex 3 and the HEPDA− monoanion in 2 are disordered into two positions and some soft constraints were also applied. Results and discussion

Synthesis

The solvothermal method proved to be an efficient synthetic strategy to produce new materials, which can give access to some metastable compounds and promote crystal growth.15 The crystalline forms of the title compounds with good yields are dominated by the specific organic solvent (ethanol or methanol), which demonstrate that three compounds seem sensitive to solvent. The synthetic route for three compounds is summarized in Scheme 1. Furthermore, attempts to vary other synthetic parameters such as pH value, molar ratio of the reactants and the identity of the base are also unsuccessful, only producing some precipitates or microcrystalline products that are unsuitable for single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis owing to rapid polymerization. Various coordination modes of H2EPDA ligand observed in compounds 1–3 are shown in Scheme 2, and the further structural details are described below.

Description of crystal structures

{[Zn2(EPDA)2(BPP)2]·(H2O)}n (1)

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction reveal that compound 1 is a rare 2D → 3D interpenetrating example on the base of a new layer motif. The asymmetry unit contains two EPDA2− dianions, two BPP ligands and one free water molecule, as shown in Fig. 1a. The Zn1(half occupied) and Zn3 (half occupied) atoms show similar six-coordinated environments with two oxygen atoms and two nitrogen atoms from two symmetry-related EPDA2− dianions occupying the equatorial positions, and two nitrogen atoms from two symmetry-related BPP molecules occupying the axial positions. The Zn2 atom is tetrahedrally coordinated by two nitrogen atoms from another two symmetry-related BPP ligands and two oxygen atoms from the β-carboxyl groups of two individual EPDA2− dianions. All the Zn–N bond lengths are in the range of 2.0204(19)–2.270(2) Å and the Zn–O bond separations are between 1.9310(17)–2.0854(16) Å. The α-carboxylate group of EPDA2− dianion and pyridyl-ring are almost parallel as the dihedral angle, while the β-carboxylate group and pyridyl-ring are approximately vertical.

Two EPDA2−ligands adopt a μ-(κ3N,Oα:Oβ) coordination mode (Scheme 2a) to connect two adjacent Zn atoms resulting in one zigzag chain with the Zn1⋯Zn2 distance of 7.6365(6) Å and the Zn2⋯Zn3 distance of 7.8355(6) Å. The Zn1 and Zn3 atoms are linked together by one BPP molecule to generate another zigzag chain propagating along the established direction. Two zigzag chains interweave by sharing Zn1 and Zn3 atoms to form one double-stranded chain (Fig. S1a†). The adjacent double-stranded chains are further cross-linked by the N atom coordinations of the other BPP molecule with the tetrahedrally coordinated Zn2 atoms to produce an undulated thick-layer with a thickness of about 12.0051 Å (Fig. 1b). Notably, the arc-shaped BPP ligands protrude from both sides of the layers, thus each undulated layer is catenated with the two nearest neighbors in parallel fashion. Namely, a rare example of parallel polycatenation is observed, where the loops in one layer are simultaneously penetrated by the BPP rods from two adjacent layered networks (upper and lower) (Fig. 1c and d).

Topologically, all the Zn(ii) centers can be regarded as four-connected network nodes and the EPDA2− and BPP can be considered as two kinds of linkers, which results in a binodal 4-connected network with the Schläfli symbol of (3.64.8)(32.6.72.8), representing a new topological prototype of layered network (Fig. 1d). The vertex symbols for Zn1/Zn3 and Zn2 nodes are (32.6.72.8) and (3.64.8), respectively. The H-bonding interactions play an important role in forming by self-assembly and enhancing the stability of the resultant 3D framework. The crystal water molecules reside in the hole of the layers (Fig. S1b†), forming two functional hydrogen bonds with each other and the carboxylate oxygen atoms of EPDA2− dianions: (a) hydrogen bonds between free water molecules from one layer and the uncoordinated β-carboxylate O atoms from the other layer (O9–H2W⋯O3D with the bond distance of 2.934(3) Å and bond angle of 171.3°) link the adjacent layers to form the three-dimensional framework; (b) hydrogen bonds between uncoordinated water oxygen atoms and the coordinated α-carboxylate O atoms from the same layer (O9–H1W⋯O1C with the bond distance of 2.900(3) Å and bond angle of 172.3°) engender in the 2D undular layer region.

The previously reported compound {[Zn(PYDC) (BPP)]·2.5H2O}n9b shows a 2D → 3D parallel interpenetrating framework with (4,4) nets, which possesses the void space accounts for 14.5% of the crystal volume, quite different from the structural motif observed here. This structural difference may be ascribed to the synergetic electron-donating and steric effects of the –CH2CH3group in H2EPDA.

[Zn3(EPDA)2(HEPDA)2(BPE)]n (2)

Compound 2 crystallizes in the space group P and the asymmetry unit contains one and half Zn(ii) centers, one EPDA2− dianion, one HEPDA− monoanion and half BPE molecule (Fig. 2a). The Zn1 atom has a slightly distorted octahedral environment [ZnO4N2] defined by two nitrogen atoms (N1, N1A) and two oxygen atoms (O1, O1A) from two symmetry-related EPDA2− dianions in the equatorial plane with its trans axial positions occupied by two oxygen atoms (O7B, O7C) from β-carboxyl groups belonging to two symmetry-related HEPDA− monoanions. The Zn2 atom (half occupied) resides in a distorted square-pyramidal geometry [ZnO4N] with the basal plane defined by N2 and O5 atoms from one HEPDA− monoanion, O2 from one EPDA2− dianion, and N3 from one BPE molecule. The apical site is occupied by one oxygen atom O4D from another EPDA2− dianion. All the Zn–N and Zn–O bond lengths are in the range of 2.0785(17)–2.1417(17) Å and 1.9921(15)–2.2306(17) Å, respectively. The coordination mode adopted by the EPDA2−ligand can be classified as μ3-(κ4N,Oα:Oa′:Oβ) (Scheme 2b). The ethyl group (C15, C16) of HEPDA− monoanion is disordered over two crystallographic positions. Both carboxylate groups and the pyridyl-ring of HEPDA− monoanion are almost parallel as the dihedral angle, whereas the α-carboxylate group of EPDA2− dianion is almost parallel to the pyridine ring plane and β-carboxylate group is nearly perpendicular to the pyridine ring plane.

Two symmetry-related Zn2 atoms are connected to the central Zn1 atom through two EPDA2− dianions in a μ-(κ3N,Oα:Oa′) bridging mode forming a linear Zn3 building block. Each Zn3 motif interacts with two neighbors viaEPDA2− dilation in the μ-(κ1Oβ) coordination mode propagating a chainlike unit along the b axis (Fig. S2a†). The Zn1⋯Zn2 separation within the trinuclear block is equal to 4.5250(4) Å. Individual chains stacked together with a -AAAA- sequence are further cohered by HEPDA− monoanions in the the μ-(κ3N,Oα:Oβ) coordination mode (Scheme 2c), establishing a Zn carboxylate layer parallel to the ab plane with quadrangle apertures (Fig. 2b). Additionally, the adjacent Zn carboxylate layers are pillared by trans-BPE spacer by connecting Zn2 atoms to construct a porous three-dimensional framework (Fig. 2c) with the pore size of 2.37 × 6.57 Å2 (the short C⋯C distance not including the van der Waals radii, as shown in Fig. S2b†), illustrating that BPE tethers extend the coordination polymer connectivity into a higher dimensionality.

The opening of each channel is irregular hexagon in shape and delimited by the alternative arrangement of four EPDA2− dianions, two BPE units, six Zn centers forming thicker pore wall (Fig. S2b†). Ethyl groups of EPDA2− dianions which are regular point slightly into the channels with a dihedral angle of 74.61° between the pyridine rings of EPDA2−ligand and the corresponding wall plane of the channel, reducing the effective diameter of the channels (Fig. 2c). The effective free volume of 2 is calculated by PLATON16 analysis as 4.2% of the crystal volume and located at both sides of regular ethyl groups, as indicated in Fig. 2c.

From a topological perspective, the [Zn3] fragments can be regarded as the network nodes. They are directly connected to four neighboring [Zn3] fragments through the EPDA2− anions and HEPDA− anions in two directions producing a (4,4) sheet. The ‘[Zn3] fragments’ of two adjacent layers are further interlinked by BPE ligands making the fragments as six-connecting nodes. Thus, this structure can be regarded as α-Po topology (pcu net) with the Schläfli symbol of (412.63) (Fig. 2d).

[Zn3(EPDA)2(HEPDA)2(BPA)]n (3)

Compound 3 also crystallizes in the space group P iso-topological to 2 with the major differences, resulting from the replacement of the BPE ligand by the more flexible BPA ligand, described as follows:

(1) The ethyl groups of EPDA2− dianions which point slightly into the channels are disordered over two crystallographic positions in 3 (Fig. 3a), while ethyl groups of EPDA2− dianions pointing into the voids are regular in 2.

(2) The HEPDA− monoanion bridges are inclined in 3 rather than almost perpendicular to chain unit in 2 (Fig. 3b), and thus decreasing the distance between chain motifs, as reflected in the unit cell dimension along the a-axis (9.87 for 2vs. 9.09 Å for 3), though the unit cell dimensions along the b- and c-axis are almost the same.

(3) The distance between Zn carboxylate layers shortens slightly also, which probably results from the conformational flexibility of the BPA molecule: geometry structure of the BPA ligand is not coplanar, while that of the BPE ligand are nearly coplanar, thus the BPA ligand has a molecule length of 9.2972(25) Å in 3 slightly shorter than 9.3939(29) Å for BPE in 2 though -CH2CH2- group has a larger span than -CHCH- group.

(4) The framework structure of 3 has a higher degree of condensation than that of 2; thus, the solvent-accessible volume in both two compounds is estimated to be 4.2% for 2 and 1.6% for 3 respectively, which is attributed to the disorders of ethyl groups sitting on the hexagonal channels (Fig. 3c) and conformational flexibility of pillared BPA molecule. The free volumes are located at both sides of disordered ethyl groups, as indicated in Fig. 3c.

Here, we can give a brief summary about the effect of ancillary ligands on the specific MOFs based on this well-defined synthesis system. Solvothermal reactions of Zn(ii) and 5-ethyl-pyridine-2,3-dicarboxylic acid produce three novel MOFs regulated by three types of dipyridyl-type ancillary ligands (BPP, BPE and BPA) with different flexibility resulting from the nature of connecting groups between two pyridine rings (-CH2CH2CH2-, -CHCH-, and -CH2CH2-). When flexible BPP ligand is introduced into the systems, the Zn double-stranded chain motifs are linked by the BPP molecule to form the corrugated flexible layers, which intertwine in such a way that the BPP rods in one layer thread through the loops of neighbouring parallel layer resulting in a 2D → 3D interpenetrating structure containing a new layer motif. This may be attributed to the fact that three methylene groups in BPP ancillary ligand can rotate freely, leading to this molecule with the larger degree of freedom. For two isomorphic compounds 2 and 3, the BPE (or BPA) molecule and (H)EPDA ligands link Zn3 fragments into two extended architectures with 6-connected pcu topology. The structural disparity for two complexes are mainly attributed to the different nature of the spacers (-CH2CH2- and -CHCH-) in the ancillary ligands except resulting from the disordered ethyl group of EPDA2− dianion, as discussed above. Additionally, as the increase in the rigidity of dipyridyl-type ligands, the assembled structures show a tendency to form the dense phase (d1 = 1.487 for 1 < d3 = 1.602 for 3 < d2 = 1.605 g cm−3 for 2).

Thermal stabilities of 1–3

Thermal degenerations of three materials have been investigated by thermogravimetric (TG) experiments, which is an important parameter for metal–organic framework materials as shown in Fig. 4. The results show that complexes 1–3 have a high thermal stability in air. The framework of compound 1 holds the inertia up to ca. 200 °C. Subsequently, a sharp weight loss is observed in a wider temperature range 200–550 °C, followed by a slow weight loss until 900 °C, which is attributed to the removal of the solvent waters and decomposition of organic ligands.17 Its mass remnant of 17.98% corresponded to ZnO (calcd.17.47%). The TG curves for complexes 2 and 3 show that a slight weight loss from room temperature until about 230 °C and 280 °C, respectively, which may be correspond to the removal of the disordered solvent waters in the channels, and then they began to decompose with a two-step weight loss until 900 °C. The observed final mass remnant of 19.26% for 2 likely represented deposition of ZnO is agreed with the calculated value of 21.17%, meanwhile the final residue of 19.21% for 3 is close to the calculated 21.14% based on ZnO.

Photoluminescent properties

The luminescent properties of complexes with d10 metal centers are of great interest for their potential applications as photoactive materials.18 The photoluminescence properties of 1–3 and powdered free ligands were investigated in the solid state at room temperature, as illustrated in Fig. 5. It is shown that complex 1 exhibits very weak emission, and complexes 2 and 3 exhibit similar fluorescence emission with bimodal bands at ca. 359/373 nm (λex = 330 nm) and 353/371 nm (λex = 300 nm), respectively. In order to understand the nature of such emission bands, the luminescent properties of free ligands were also measured. Noting that there is no obvious emission observed for free H2EPDA ligand for excitation wavelengths between 250 and 480 nm, and free BPP molecule display extremely weak emission, whereas free BPE and BPA molecules show similar emissions at ca. 368 nm and 365 nm upon excitation at 300 nm, respectively (Fig. 5).

Very weak emission for 1 is in accordance with non-emitting properties for H2EPDA and BPP molecules. The integral spectra of complexes 2 and 3 have similar characteristics to that of free BPE and BPA molecules, respectively. Apparently, the emissions of both two complexes should be assigned as the π–π* and/or n–π* transition19 related to the ancillary ligands therein. The cleavages of the spectra probably resulted from the slight rearrangement of energy levels affected by the enhanced rigidity on the ligand moieties upon metal coordination. In addition, similar emission shapes for complexes 2 and 3 are probably attributed to their closely related crystal structures, identical local ligand environments, and almost the same emission characteristic for BPE and BPA molecules.

Conclusions

In summary, three novel Zn(ii) metal–organic frameworks have been successfully assembled by the mild solvothermal route on the basis of a well-defined synthetic system of Zn/H2EPDA/dipyridyl-molecule, providing a case study to exemplify the effect of ancillary ligands on the structures and properties of the resulting coordination polymers. Complex 1 contains undulated layers with a new topology, which intertwine each other to produce a rare 2D → 3D interpenetrating structure. The isomorphic structures for complexes 2 and 3 consist of Zn carboxylate layers pillared further by BPE or BPA molecules to generate three-dimensional frameworks with 6-connected pcu topology. It is shown that the nature of dipyridyl-type ancillary ligands with different flexibility and heterocyclic carboxylate derivatives with electron-donating groups on the heterocycles are crucial for the assembly of diversified coordination networks. Moreover, photoluminescence behaviors for all complexes are closely associated with the different nature and the local environments around metal ions for organic ligands. Our preliminary results reveal that the heterocyclic carboxylate derivatives are efficient linkers for the construction of novel MOFs. Further work in this area is in progress.
