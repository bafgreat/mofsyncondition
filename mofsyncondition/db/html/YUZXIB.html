Introduction

Helical and homochiral structures are ubiquitous in nature and are integral to various biological functions.1 Natural biopolymers such as DNA and proteins have acquired a definite helical sense (e.g., right handed R-helix) associated with the homochirality of their components (e.g., d-sugars and l-amino acids).2 Chemists have made great efforts to introduce helicity into artificial systems.3 Of particular interest is the design and preparation of homochiral helical coordination polymers, because of their potential applications in the fields of asymmetric catalysis, separation, chiral synthesis, and non-linear optics.4–7 Although both racemic and achiral ligands can be employed to generate intrinsically chiral helices, the overall chirality of the product is difficult to predict or control. When racemic or achiral ligands are employed to generate helices, bulk racemates typically result with only a few exceptions where spontaneous resolution upon crystallisation occurs.8,9 On the other hand, the use of enantiopure ligands will necessarily lead to the stereoselective synthesis of helices with the same handedness.

Metallosalen-based architectures [salen = N,N′-ethylenebis (salicylideneaminato)] have diverse potential applications in catalysis and separation.10–12 We have recently shown that semi-flexible di-4-pyridyl-functionalized metallosalen complexes could be used to construct chiral metallamacrocycles and helical coordination polymers for guest recognition and separation.11,12b

Here, we report the self-assembly of homochiral helical polymers with tunable pitches from di-3-pyridyl-functionalized metallosalen complexes with the general formula of [ML] [M = Zn, Co, Ni, Mn(O2CH) 1–4; H2L = (R,R)-(−)-N,N′-bis(3-tert-butyl-5-(3-pyridyl)salicylidene)-1,2-diaminocyclohexane] were readily obtained in good yields by heating a mixture of corresponding metal salts and H2L (1 : 1 molar ratio) in DMF, alcohol and water at 80 °C for one day. The products are all stable in air and insoluble in water and common organic solvents and were formulated on the basis of microanalysis, IR, and thermogravimetric analysis (TGA). The phase purity of the bulk samples of 1–4 were established by comparison of their observed and simulated powder X-ray diffraction patterns (PXRD).

Experimental

General

All of the chemicals are commercially available, and were used without further purification. The ligand (R,R)-(−)-N,N′-bis(3-tert-butyl-5-(3-pyridyl)salicylidene)-1,2-diaminocyclohexane) was prepared according to published procedures.12a Elemental analyses were performed with an EA1110 CHNS-0 CE elemental analyzer. The IR (KBr pellet) spectrum was recorded (400–4000 cm−1 region) on a Nicolet Magna 750 FT-IR spectrometer. The CD spectra were recorded on a J-800spectropolarimeter (Jasco, Japan). Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were carried out in an N2 atmosphere with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 on a STA449C integration thermal analyzer. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) data were collected on a DMAX2500 diffractometer using CuKα radiation. The calculated PXRD patterns were produced using the SHELXTL-XPOW program and single crystal reflection data. All fluorescence measurements were carried out on a LS 50B luminescence spectrometer (Perkin-Elmer, Inc., USA). All UV-vis absorption spectrum were recorded on a Lambda 20 UV-vis spectrometer (Perkin-Elmer, Inc., USA).

Preparation of ZnL, 1

A mixture of Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (14.9 mg, 0.05 mmol), H2L (29.5 mg, 0.05 mmol), DMF (1 mL), MeOH (5 mL) and water (0.5 mL) was sealed in a 20 mL vial with a screw cap and heated at 80 °C for one day. The mixture was then cooled to room temperature and light-yellow block-like crystals were collected, washed with ether and dried in air. Yield: 25.4 mg, 78% based on Zn. Results and discussion

Structural description

A single-crystal X-ray diffraction study revealed that complexes 1–4 all crystallize in the monoclinic space groupP21, and adopt a 1D structure (Fig. 1). In 1–3, the asymmetric units each contains two [ML] units and the M2+ ions each adopts a square pyramidal geometry with the equatorial plane occupied by the central N2O2 donors of one L ligand and the apical position by one pyridine of another L ligand. The M–O and M–N bond lengths are in range of 1.949(3)–1.966(3) and 2.085(4)–2.094(3) Å for 1, 1.871(6)–1.899(6) and 1.857(9)–1.896(7) Å for 2 and 1.954(5)–1.968(5) and 2.079(6)–2.100(6) Å for 3, respectively. The metal centers are above ca. 0.44, 0.15 and 0.46 Å the planes formed by N2O2 coordination sites toward the axial pyridyl group for 1–3, respectively. In 4, the Mn3+ centers adopt a distorted octahedral geometry with the equatorial plane occupied by the central N2O2 donors of one L ligand, one apical position by one pyridine of another L ligand and the other apical position by one monodentate formate. The Mn–N and Mn–O bond lengths range from 1.962(10) to 1.997(10) Å and from 1.852(8) to 1.896(8) Å, respectively. The metal centers are above ca. 0.14 Å the planes formed by N2O2 coordination sites toward the axial pyridyl group.

Each ML units in 1–4 thus use one terminal pyridyl group to coordinate another ML units building an infinite chain running along the b-axis for 1, 2 and 4, and the [101] axis for 3, while remains another pyridine uncoordinated.

Both 1 and 2 adopt a left-handed helical structure, 4 adopts a right-handed helical structure that is probably caused by the monodentate formate, and 3 adopts a zigzag chain structure. All helices are generated around the crystallographic 21 axis with pitch lengths of 10.430(1) Å for 1, 12.656(2) Å for 2, 18.798(6) Å for 3 and 12.191(5) Å for 4 (Fig. 2). Although the linkages of ML in 1–4 are the same, the metallosalen ligands undergo significant twists by coordinating to different metal ions. The average lengths of adjacent M⋯M centers increase from 10.152 Å in 1, 10.596 Å in 2 to 10.798 Å in 3. The average dihedral angles of the coordinated pyridyl rings and the adjacent phenyl rings increase from 12.96 Å in 1, 39.39 Å in 2 to 41.45 Å in 3. The average dihedral angles of the coordinated pyridyl rings and the N2O2 planes also increase from 78.80° in 1, 88.74° in 2 to 88.94° in 3, respectively. Moreover, the average turning angles (defined by three adjacent metal centers) of the zigzag chains are widened from 61.830° in 1, 73.336° in 2 to 121.004° in 3. As a result, 1 has the shortest helical pitch and 3 has the longest one. Whereas, in 4, the average length of adjacent Mn⋯Mn centers is 11.434 Å, the average dihedral angle of the coordinated pyridyl rings and the N2O2 planes is just 68.31°, the average dihedral angles of the coordinated pyridyl rings and the adjacent phenyl rings is 27.88° and the average turning angle of MnL chains is 64.431°. As a result, the helical pitch of 4 lies in the middle of 1 and 3. Overall, the helix pitches are in the order 3 > 2 > 4 > 1. In all four complexes, strong inter-chain π⋯π interactions between extended π conjugated ML units (plane-to-plane separation ca. 3.29–3.53 Å) direct the packing of the 1D helices in parallel into a 2D framework in the bc plane.

Chirality properties

The electronic spectra of H2L show four major π–π* transitions at 225, 256, 294 and 336 nm. Upon the formation of compound 3, there is a new band around 578 nm for 3, which is probably due to d-d transitions of Ni2+ in a pyramidal geometry. The solid-state circular dichroism (CD) spectrum measurements in KBr pellets confirmed the optical activity and enantiomeric nature of 3. It exhibits a negative π–π* transitions at 263 nm, a positive π–π* transitions at 343 nm, negative charge-transfer at 427 nm and a positive d–d transitions at about 595 nm.13 The solid-state CD spectra of 1, 2 and 4 are similar to that of 3 (Fig. 3), indicating that compounds 1–4 all have the same enantiomeric nature.

Thermal properties

Thermal gravimetric analysis was carried out to examine the stability of the framework, and showed that no guest molecules exist in the frameworks of the four complexes. The TGA curves of 1–4 in a nitrogen atmosphere reveal clean weight loss steps occurring at 440, 360, 390 and 340 °C, respectively, which correspond to decomposition of the CP’s framework, which ended after 800, 730, 660 and 645 °C (Fig. 4).

Luminescent properties

Of the four complexes, only 1 exhibits intense photoluminescence in the solid state with the emission maximum at 474 nm upon excitation at 265 nm and is shown in Fig. 5. The emission peak is essentially the same as the solid-state fluorescence signal of the free ligand H2L at 462 nm. We thus believed that the luminescence signals of ZnL originate from ligand-centered n–π* or π–π* process.

Conclusions

In the present work, we have synthesized four helical coordination polymers based on the semi-flexible pyridyl-functionalized metallosalen complexes. The ability to control the helical pitches by using different metal ions is a key to future synthesis of functional helical structures, which hold great promise in chiral technology and nanotechnology, and may also contribute to the understanding of helical organized systems in biology.14
