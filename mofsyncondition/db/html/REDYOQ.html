A novel double-stranded staircase Cu(I)-iodide coordination polymer based on bis(4-pyridyl-carboxylate) ligand with flexible propyl spacer: Syntheses, crystal structure, luminescence properties and thermal stability

Author links open overlay panelMichaelBoltec
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.inoche.2012.07.036Get rights and content

Abstract

A novel supramolecular Cu(I) complex {Cu2I2L}n (L: propane-1,3-diyl bis-(4-pyridine-carboxylate)) (1) has been prepared by self-assembly reaction of CuI and the flexible ligand (L) using an acetonitrile and acetonitrile/water (4:1) and mixture respectively, in presence of an excess of KI. X-ray diffraction analyses revealed that (1) is one-dimensional coordination polymer with double-stranded staircase motif. The structure was also characterized by i.r. spectroscopy, and thermogravimetry. In the solid state at room temperature, the compound exhibits orange phosphorescence with emission maxima at 590 nm.

Graphical abstract

A new photoluminescent complex of copper (I) Iodide polymer, [Cu2I2(L)]n (L = propane-1,3-diyl bis-(4-pyridine-carboxylate)), has been synthesized by diffusion method and structurally characterized by X-ray diffraction. The structure of complex features a 1-D one-dimensional coordination polymer with double-stranded staircase motif.

Recently, there has been considerable interest in the luminescent properties of coordination polymers, particularly in view of the potential applications as sensors and light emitting diodes. Copper (I) iodides are fascinating building blocks in the self-assembly process. Reactions involving copper (I) iodides and multi-dentate ligands are capable of adopting a variety of coordination modes ranging from low-dimensional, discrete complexes to 3D polymeric networks for example square dimers, cubane tetramers, and also chain structures like “zig-zag” and “stair step” polymers [1b]. Moreover, the structural motifs often depend on the synthetic conditions employed, and thus copper (I) iodide clusters display structural versatilities with resulting luminescent properties closely correlated with their structural characteristics, such as Cu(I)⋯Cu(I) interactions as representative example [1], [2].

The d10 metal complexes with nitrogen-containing ligand have been synthesized and their luminescent behavior has been studied [5], mainly the photoluminescent properties of monovalent copper complexes. It has been reported that luminescence emission of Cu(I) complexes and polymers is structurally dependent and varies from blue to red light [3]. Generally, the electronic transitions responsible for photoluminescence properties of Cu(I)-halide polymeric complexes with N-donors ligands can be assigned to metal centered (MC), ligand centered (LC), and metal to ligand charge transfer (MLCT) and the presence of a halide in complexes also enables halide to ligand charge transfer (XLCT) [3].

This paper forms part of our continuing study on the synthesis, structural characterization and photophysical properties of metal-organic frameworks based on d10 ions and flexible ligands [4a-b]. In this report, we have focused our investigation on CuI coordination polymers with propane-1,3-bis-(4-pyridyl carboxylate) used as flexible ligand (L = pyCO2-(CH2)3-O2Cpy, py = 4-pyridyl), which has been used in other self-assembly reaction with cobalt salt [4c], and structurally characterized as free ligand by us [4b]. During our investigation, we have found a new 1D coordination polymer {Cu2I2L}n containing infinite staircases, composed of Cu2I2 subunits.

The coordination polymer (1) was obtained under diffusion reaction between CuI and propane-1,3-diyl bis-(4-pyridine-carboxylate), L (Scheme 1) in acetonitrile and acetonitrile/water (4:1) mixture respectively, in presence of an excess of KI [11]. Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that compound crystallizes in C2/c space group. The asymmetric unit shows one Cu(I) atom, one iodide atom and one-half ligand L [12]. In the core {Cu2I2L}n structural units, the Cu atom is coordinated by three iodide atoms symmetry-related and one N-atom from the ligand forming a distorted tetrahedral environment (Fig. 1) with CuI bond distances and ICuI bond angles ranging from 2.6464 (5) to 2.6569 (5) Å, and 107.573 (17)–109.90 (8) ° respectively and comparable to other related compounds [6], [8]a–d]. These tetrahedra are connected by a common iodide atom into CuI chains forming infinite one-dimensional double stranded staircase (Fig. 3). The distance Cu1–Cu1a (a: − x + 1, − y + 1, − z + 1) is 2.6799 (8) Å which is shorter than the sum of van der Waals radii (2.8 Å), indicating a weak metal–metal interaction. The distance Cu1–Cu1b (b: − x + 1, y, − z + 1/2) is 2.8149 (9) Å, which is slightly longer than the sum of the van der Waals radii of two coppers [1a]. The CuN, distance of 2.062(3) Å is typical for similar copper (I) complexes [6d] and comparable with a closely related compound [6c] as representative examples.

The ligand L = pyCO2-(CH2)3-O2Cpy (py = 4-pyridyl) was first reported by Song et al., [4c]. In the polymeric cobalt structure reported [4c], L adopts a GG conformation while in our structure the conformation is GG′ (U-form), [6b] avoiding a larger dimensionality of the polymer with the neighboring inorganic chain as has been observed in previous report of Cu(I) polymers with bidentate ligands [6a]. This conformation produces supramolecular π–π stacking interactions between pyridyl rings with distance centroid–centroid at 3.657 (7) Å, (Fig. 2) and not observed in [4c]. An entirely similar pattern of angles and distances is observed for L in both compounds.

The photoluminescent properties of (1) upon the radiation of UV light in the solid state at room temperature have also been investigated. The excellent photoluminescence of Cu(I) complexes encourages us to investigate the solid-state photophysical property of (1) at room temperature. In the solid state, (1) shows bright orange luminescence when excited at 365 nm with 4 W hand-held UV lamp (Fig. 4) and it displays a strong orange emission at 570 nm upon excitation at 264 nm. The free ligand (L), exhibits blue luminescence emission band with maximum at 439 nm upon excitation at 370 nm in the solid state, which is attributed to intraligand n–π* and π–π* charge transfer. The lifetime of the emission at room temperature can be characterized by single exponential curves with τ = 0.60 ms which is longer than those in related compounds [7a-d], indicating phosphorescence emission from triplet excited state [3]. The emission energy of (1) is much lower than that of the free ligand, which eliminates an intraligand (IL) excited state. The metal-to-ligand charge transfer (MLCT) and halide-to-ligand charge transfer (XLCT) excited states are important in the photophysical properties of the copper (I) complexes constructed from easily reducible ligand [1a]. Therefore, for copper (I) iodide cluster compounds, the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) is presumably composed largely of iodide p and copper d orbitals respectively. The low unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) is presumably composed largely of π* ligand orbital. Thus, excited-state assignments must account for mayor components of iodide to ligand charge transfer and metal to ligand charge transfer, (X + M)LCT ([Ipz → Lπ⁎] and [3d → Lπ⁎]) [9a–c]. These assignments are in agree with theoretical calculations make for copper (I) iodide complexes with aromatic heterocyclic ligand [7e].

The absorption spectra is very broad (300–550 nm), while the emission band is narrow (< 100 nm), indicating that the phosphorescence color is relatively pure. It should be mentioned that luminescent materials with broad absorption and sharp emission band are very important in flat panel display devices and field emission displays (Fig. 5). Therefore, (1) with the absorption band covering the whole UV-vis-NIR spectral region and bandgap of ~ 2.2 eV [10] may be a good potential semiconductor material for low-voltage solar cells due to the efficiency absorption of the majority of the sunlight [8].

The thermal stability of (1), was studied using thermal gravimetric analysis (TGA) from 30 to 800 °C under flowing Nitrogen with a heating rate of 5 °C/min (Fig. 6). The thermogravimetric analyses revealed that (1) is thermally stable up to 280 °C. Furthermore the TGA curve shows that there is no chemical decomposition up to 800 °C approximately. The decomposition thermal process is produced on three stages. The first-step weight loss occurs from 80 to 280 °C and corresponds to the loss of two adsorbed water molecules (calcd. 5.22%; found 5.12%). The IR spectrum of (1) shows a broad vibration band at ~ 3000 cm− 1 assigned to stretching OH of the water molecules. The second-step weight loss occurs from 281 to 300 °C which is attributable to the complete removal of the ligand molecule (calcd. 41.1%; found 40.7%). The third-step weight loss occurs from 300 to 700 °C which is attributable to the decomposition of copper halides inorganic portion of the compound (calcd. 53.7%; found 54.1%).
