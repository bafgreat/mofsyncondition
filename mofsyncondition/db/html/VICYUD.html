Introduction

Porous metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have received considerable attention because of their diverse chemical compositions and architectures as well as their potential applications in gas adsorption and ion recognition.1 To achieve these applications, porous MOFs are required to possess large pore size, high surface area and high framework stability. A common strategy for enhancing these properties is to use linear extension of the rigid organic linkers to construct robust networks.2 However, introducing longer linkers to form porous MOFs causes a very strong tendency to result in interpenetration, which diminishes the porosity and reduces the space available. As a result, exploring highly symmetrical multifunctional organic ligands and suitable metal salts in a design strategy to construct coordination complexes with special topologies are of great interest, due to the predictability of the resulting networks.3 This approach has been widely used in the design of extended MOFs, exemplified by linking the tetrahedral or square-planar building units for forming the 4-connected networks.4 Secondary building units (SBUs) that can predict the topologies of structures are widely used to design and construct the extended frameworks that are attracting much interest in the field of materials science, chemistry and crystallography. A tetracarboxyl tection, 3,3′,5,5′-azobenzene-tetracarboxylic acid (H4abtc) has attracted much attention as a polytopic bridge in recent years, several microporous MOFs with 4-connected topologies from different coordination geometric metal ions have been reported.5

We have been interested in the design and synthesis of coordination polymers showing unusual topology structure.6 In this paper, we report the synthesis, crystal structure and adsorption properties of three novel coordination polymers.

Experimental section

All chemicals for syntheses are analytical grade and used as purchased without further purification. Elemental analysis for carbon, hydrogen and nitrogen were carried out on a Vario EL III elemental analyzer. Infrared spectra were recorded on a Nicolet A370FT-IR spectrometer using KBr pellets in the 400–4000 cm−1 region. TGA experiments were performed on a Shimadzu DT-20B thermogravimetric analyzer from 20 to 800 °C at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 in air. The fluorescence spectra were measured on a Shimadzu RF-5301 spectrophotometer. UV-visspectra were recorded on a pgeneral TU-1900 spectrometer. Gas adsorption measurement was performed in the ASAP (Accelerated Surface Area and Porosimetry) 2020 System. ICP was performed on Thermo Fisher iCAP 6300. The morphology of the obtained Cu(ii)-exchanged product of complex 2 was observed by a field-emission microscope (JEOL-JSM-6700F 25KV). Electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) spectra were recorded by a JES-FA 200 spectrometer with DICE ENDOR accessory, EN801 resonator, and an ENI A-500 rf power amplifier. Rf powers ranged from 240 to 400 W across the 100 MHz scanned range, microwave power was 2 mW. 1H NMR spectrum was measured on Bruker AV 500 MHz spectrometer.

Synthesis of 3,3′,5,5′-azobenzenetetracarboxylic acid

Reductive coupling of nitro compound for the preparation of symmetric azo-benzene compound was used to synthesize the title ligand3,3′,5,5′-azobenzenetetracarboxylic acid.73,3′,5,5′-Azobenzenetetracarboxylic acid (H4abtc) can easily be oxidized to form mono- and di-oxygenated compounds H4aobtc and/or H4ao2btc as shown in Scheme 1.8

Synthesis of {[Zn2(η6-ao2btc)(η2-2,2′-bpy)2(H2O)2]·2H2O}1n (1)

A mixture of ZnCl2 (13.6 mg, 0.1 mmol), H4abtc (18.0 mg, 0.05 mmol) and 2,2′-bpy (7.8 mg, 0.05 mmol) were dissolved in solution of DMF (3 ml), CH3CN (3 ml) and H2O (2 ml). Precipitation formed after stirring the mixture for several minutes. 1 M HCl was added dropwise until all precipitation disappeared. Then the clear solution was placed in a 15 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel and heated at 85 °C for 12 hours. Upon cooling to room temperature at a rate of 5 °C per 0.5 h, yellow block crystals were obtained with a yield of 33.2% (based on H4abtc). FT-IR (KBr, cm−1): 3390(s), 3107(s), 1627(s), 1599(s), 1564(s), 1474(s), 1440(s), 1349(m), 1246(m), 1097(m), 922(m), 768(s), 731(m), 675(w). Anal. calcd (%) for C18H15N3O7Zn: C, 47.97; H, 3.35; N, 9.32. Found: C, 48.92; H, 3.30; N, 9.59.

Synthesis of {[Zn2(η8-aobtc)(η2-phen)(H2O)]·DMF}3n (2)

A mixture of Zn(NO3)·6H2O (44.6 mg, 0.15 mmol), H4abtc (18.0 mg, 0.05 mmol) and 1,10-phen (19.8 mg, 0.1 mmol) were dissolved in solution of DMF (3 ml), CH3CN (3 ml) and H2O (2 ml). Similar the synthesis of 1, precipitation formed after stirring the mixture. The precipitation was dissolved by adding several drops of 1 M HCl. Then the clear solution was placed in a 15 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel and heated at 85 °C for 12 hours. Upon cooling to room temperature, at a rate of 5 °C per 0.5 h, yellow block crystals were obtained with yield of 51.2% (based on H4abtc). FT-IR (KBr, cm−1): 3412(s), 2930(w), 1660(vs), 1624(s), 1572(m), 1432(s), 1369(s), 1103(m), 850(m), 781(s), 725(s). Anal. calcd (%) for C31H23N5O11Zn2: C, 48.21; H, 3.00; N, 9.07. Found: C, 48.50; H, 3.25; N, 9.80.

Synthesis of {(Hap)2·[Zn3·(η9-aobtc)2]·2H2O}3n (3)

Complex 3 was synthesized from ZnSO4·7H2O (28.7 mg, 0.1 mmol), H4abtc (18.0 mg, 0.05 mmol) and 4-aminopyridine (9.4 mg, 0.1 mmol) in DMF (3 ml), CH3CN (3 ml) and H2O (2 ml). The experimental procedures were the same as synthesis of 2. Pale yellow block crystals were obtained with yield of 53.0% (based on H4abtc). FT-IR (KBr, cm−1): 3439(s), 3354(m), 3228(s), 3098(m), 2985(w), 1649(s), 1621(vs), 1574(m), 1531(w), 1441(w), 1379(s), 1346(w), 1202(m), 1107(w), 995(w), 802(w), 778(m), 728(s). Anal. calcd (%) for C42H30N8O20Zn3: C, 43.38; H, 2.61; N, 9.64. Found: C, 43.69; H, 2.85; N, 9.55.

Cation exchange of complex 2

The crystals of as-synthesized complex 2 (each 30 mg) were immersed into aqueous solutions of 10 mg ml−1 (3 ml) Cu(NO3)2, Co(NO3)2 or Ni(NO3)2 for five days. The samples of metal exchanged complex with visual color change were obtained. The post-exchange samples were analyzed by PXRD and IR spectra. The metal ions were analyzed by ICP, the operating parameters of CP-OES are as follows: power 1150 W, plasma gas flow rate is 15 L min−1, auxiliary gas flow is 1.5 L min−1, nebulizer pressure is 0.2 MPa, pump speed is 50 rpm and cleaning time is 30 seconds.

Iodine adsorption and desorption of complex 2

The I2adsorption studies followed the procedure reported by Zeng et al.9a 30 mg of single crystals of 2 were immersed in 4.0 ml 0.005 mol L−1 I2 cyclohexane solution in a sealed vial at room temperature. 120 hours later, the crystal was filtered and washed with cyclohexane. The dark product fades fast at room temperature in summer due to the volatility of I2. I2 content in the sample was analyzed by UV-visspectra. All the I2 encapsulated samples were immersed in 1.0 mL ethanol, then 50 μL of the ethanol solution was transferred to a UV cuvette and diluted to 3 ml with ethanol. The spectra were measured at different times to monitor the desorption process.

Methylene blue substitution of complex 3

Methylene blue (0.25 g) was dissolved in 3 ml water and filtered to remove impurities. To the solution was added 25 mg of complex 3. The mixture was left at room temperature for a week with occasional shaking. After filtration from the dye solution, the solid complex was washed with water. The dried sample was analyzed with PXRD and NMR. The NMR samples were prepared as following: complex 3 or its encapsulation samples (∼5 mg) were suspended in D2O, solid NaOH (∼20 mg) was added to the suspension, after a few minutes the samples will dissolved in D2O completely.

X-ray crystallography

Well-shaped single crystals of complex 1–3 were selected for X-ray diffraction study. Data were collected on a Bruker SMART CCD diffractometer with graphite monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71703 Å) by using the phi and omega scan method at 296(2) K. The structures were solved by direct method and refined by the full-matrix least-squares on F2 using the SHELXTL-97 program.10 Anisotropic thermal parameters were assigned to all non-hydrogen atoms and hydrogen atoms were located and included at their calculated position. Complex 2 contains large voids, which are occupied by disordered solvent molecules (DMF). The quality of the crystal was poor so the R1 and wR2 values observed are higher than that of complex 1. To resolve these issues, the contribution of solvent electron density was removed by the SQUEEZE routine in PLATON.11 The crystallographic data and structure refinement results of complex 1–3 are listed in Table 1, the selected bond lengths and angles and H-bonds are listed in Tables S1 and S2 in the ESI.†

Results and discussion

Description of the crystal structure

{[Zn2(η6-ao2btc)(η2-2,2′-bpy)2(H2O)2]·2H2O}1n (1)

X-ray crystallography reveals that complex 1 crystallized in the P2(1)/n space group. There is only one crystallographically independent Zn ion in complex 1, as shown in Fig. 1a. All the carboxylate groups are deprotonated, which agrees well with the absence of ∼1700 cm−1 absorption in IR spectrum. Zn is coordinated by three carboxylate oxygen atoms (O2 and O3, O4) from two ao2btc4−ligands, one water molecule (O1W), and two N atoms (N1 and N2) from 2,2′-bpy to form a distorted octahedron environment. Zn1–O2 has the shortest distance at 2.035(6) Å, while Zn1–O4#1 has the longest distance at 2.317(11) Å. The Zn–O(water) distance is 2.117(7) (Zn1–O1W) Å (Table S1, ESI†). The smallest bond angle is O3 #1–Zn1–O4#1 at 56.2(4)° and the largest bond angle is O1W–Zn1–N2 at 168.3(3)°. The other angles are close to ∼90° or 120°. We have proposed a parameter σ to quantify how distorted an octahedral geometry can be, where σ = [αmin + αmax − 180]/90, αmin and αmax are the minimum and maximum angles in an octahedron.6a The σ around Zn1 is 0.50, which indicate high distortion. The azo NN bond of the ligand was di-oxygenated as indicated in Fig. 1a. The NN bonds of the ligand resides on inversion center, thus the two benzene rings are coplanar. However, the dihedral angle between the benzene ring and the azoxy plane is 30.614(699)°. Both carboxylates coordinate to the Zn(ii) ion. The two carboxylates coordinate to Zn(ii) in monodentate and bidentate fashion. Two ligands linked two metal atoms alternately to generate an infinite 1D hinged neutral chain containing 24-membered M2L2macrocycles as shown in Fig. 1b. The centre to edge C8 distance between adjacent bipy in c-direction is 3.474(1) Å, a typical π–π interaction distance. There are not only π–π interaction between bipy, but also the benzene ring in ao2btc4−, where the distance between the centroid of a benzene ring and adjacent benzene C13 is 3.432(1) Å (shown in Fig. 1c and 1d). It is the π–π interactions between the bipy in the c-axis and benzene ring in the ab plane that help the complex pack into a 3D structure.

{[Zn2(η8-aobtc)(η2-phen)(H2O)]·DMF}3n (2)

Complex 2 crystallizes in the monoclinic form with P21/c space group. The crystal structure of complex 2 is shown in Fig. 2a. There were two Zn(ii) ions, one aobtc4− anion, one phen and one lattice water molecule in the asymmetric unit. Zn1 is six-coordinated with three carboxylate oxygen atoms from three different aobtc4−ligands, two phen nitrogen atoms, and one water oxygen to form an octahedral coordination environment with N2O4 donor set. The basal plane was constructed by the coordination of N1, N2, O6, and O7 donor. Zn2 is six-coordinate and assumed disordered octahedral geometry which was coordinated by two chelating carboxyl group of aobtc4−ligand, and two monodentate carboxyl groups of aobtc4−. The σ values around Zn1 and Zn2 are 0.83 and 0.35, respectively. There is only 35% octahedron in the Zn2 coordination environment.6a

Zn1 and Zn2 are bridged by three carboxylate groups as shown in Fig. 2a. Two of the carboxylates bridge Zn1 and Zn2 via a 1,3-bridge, one carboxylate bridges Zn1 and Zn2 via O1. The Zn1⋯Zn2 distance is 3.355(1) Å. The Zn2(CO2)4(H2O)N2 unit coordinates to four carboxylates from four ligands and thus can be considered as a 4-connected SBU (Fig. 2a, inset), while each aobtc4− unit coordinates to four Zn2(CO2)4(H2O)N2 units, which can also act as a 4-connected rectangular SBU as shown in Fig. 2b. It also worthy to mention that the two benzene rings in aobtc4− have a dihedral angle of 14.3657(7)°, which is nearly coplanar as shown in Fig. 2a.

Extension of the coordination geometry around each SBU will therefore give a 3D porous framework with PbS-type topology (Fig. 2b). From the viewpoint of network topology, complex 2 can be simplified as a 4,4-connected 4-connected node network, the Schlafli symbol for this uniform network is {42.84}. There are cavity channels with a pore diameter of approximately 9 Å viewed along the b axis (Fig. 2c). These channels are interconnected through the helical chains (Fig. 2b, blue and green). PLATON calculation indicates the void volume of 2 with and without water molecules and DMF being 16.5% and 41.4%, respectively. The crystalline DMF partially fills the cavity as shown in Fig. 2c.

{(Hap)2·[Zn3·(η9-aobtc)2]·2H2O}3n (3)

The X-ray crystallographic analysis provides unambiguous evidence for the porous structure of 3. Complex 3 was crystallized in the triclinic space group P. Fig. 3a shows the structure of 3 in the atom numbering scheme. There are one and a half Zn(ii), one deprotonated aobtc4−, and one Hap+cation in the asymmetric unit. Zn1 was located in the symmetry center (Fig. 3a). Both Zn(ii) ions coordinated to six carboxylate oxygen atoms in octahedral environments. The azo group in the ligand in 3 is mono-oxygenated, which is similar to that in 2. The Zn1–O3 distances of 2.252(4) Å is obviously longer than all other Zn1–O distances of 2.010(4)–2.043(4) Å, coordination geometry around Zn1 is in a six-coordinated ideal octahedron. Zn2 is in a six-coordinated distorted octahedral geometry and coordinated by six oxygen atoms from four independent aobtc4− ions. These two oxygen atoms are from two independent chelate carboxylates (Fig. 3a). The shortest bond is Zn2–O2 at 1.972(4) Å.

Zn1 and Zn2 are bridged by three carboxylate groups from three independent aobtc4−ligands. Similar to that in complex 2, there are two 1,3-bridging and one 1,1-bridging carboxylates between Zn1 and Zn2 in 3. However, the Zn1 and Zn2 distances of 3.2755(1) Å is obviously shorter than 3.355(1) Å in complex 2. All carboxylate oxygen atoms in aobtc4− coordinated to Zn(ii) ions. There are one 1,3-chelate, one 1,1,3-chelate and two 1,3-bridging carboxylate groups. The aobtc4− links to four trinuclear Zn(ii) moieties (Fig. 3a). The trinuclear Zn(ii) coordinates to eight carboxylate groups from eight aobtc4−. The dihedral angle of the two benzene rings in aobtc4− is 76.784(1)°, which is almost perpendicular to each other, much larger than being coplanar in 1 and 14.3657(7)° in 2. In the counterion Hap+, the proton binds to the pyridinenitrogen rather than the aminenitrogen atom. The N3–H3A distance is 0.860(7) Å, which is similar to the N–H distances in the amine group. Proton H3A is further stabilized by H-bond between H3A and O4 (Fig. 3a). The basicity of pyridinenitrogen is stronger than the aminenitrogen.

Fig. 3b is the view of 3 from c-axis. The counter cation is presented as thin lines. If aobtc4− is simplified into a 4-connected-linker, the crystal structure can be represented as in Fig. 3c. Distinct colors (green, blue and purple) represent three individual aobtc4−ligands. One aobtc4− links four trinuclear Zn(ii) in tetrahedral geometry (Fig. 3c) due to the perpendicularity between the two benzene rings in aobtc4−. This ligand links four metal ion in an essentially coplanar manner in 1 and 2.

There are rectangular channels with a pore size of approximately 7 × 15 Å2 (Fig. 3b). The N2adsorption isotherm at 295 K indicates the median pore width is 12.405 Å (Table S3 and Fig. S1 in the ESI†). The void space is partially filled with one Hap+cation and two water molecules. PLATON calculation indicates the void volume of complex 3 without 4-aminopyridine and water molecules being 32.7%. Topological analysis indicates that complex 3 can be represented as a 4,8 two-nodal network with Schlafli notation of {412.612.84}{46}2.

Structural comparison and discussion

All three complexes are synthesized under similar conditions with a Zn(ii) to H4abtc ratio of 2–3 : 1. In the presence of bipy or phen, ternary complex 1 or 2 were obtained. However, in presence of 4-aminopyridine, the complex is a binary complex. 4-Aminopyridine was protonated at the pyridinenitrogen atom and was a counter cation. In all the complexes, the H4abtc were fully deprotonated. Important structure parameters are listed in Table 2. The ligand provides all its carboxylates to coordinate with Zn(ii). Coordination number varies from 6–9. The azo adopts trans configuration in 1 and 2. The two benzene rings are almost coplanar in 1 and 2, while in 3, the two benzene rings are almost perpendicular. All the carboxylates are essentially coplanar with the attached benzene ring. So far, only a few examples of azobiphenal tetracarboxylates have been reported.5g,i H4abtc ligand, which exists in partially or completely deprotonated forms, can adopt diverse binding modes and geometrical configurations to provide unique multidimensional structures (Scheme 2). The coordination numbers can vary from 10 to 2 according to synthetic conditions. Complex 1, 2 and 3 are in η6μ4, η8μ7, η9μ7 coordination mode, respectively. All these modes in 1, 2 and 3 are unprecedented.

It should be noted that azo linkages in all the three crystals were oxidized. Ligand in 1 was oxidized to form a di-oxygenated azo group, while the ligands in complex 2 and 3 were oxidized into mono-oxygenated azo groups. This phenomenon indicates that the azo group is unstable in air in the presence of Zn(ii).5f,8 Both di-oxygenated ao2btc4− and mono-oxygenated aobtc4− have been reported previously.5f,8 The two benzene rings and NO atoms in the di-oxygenated ligand are coplanar 5f (Table 2), while the mono-oxygenated ligands are not coplanar (Table 2), which agrees well with literature data.8 Both coplanar and non-coplanar configurations exist in non-oxygenated ligands.5

Completely deprotonated 3,3′,5,5′-azobenzenetetracarboxylic acid (abtc4−) without a second building block tends to form a 3D coordination polymer according to ref. 5a–c, complex 3 is a good example. With chelating 1,10-phenanthroline, complex 2 is also a 3D coordination polymer. However, with a chelating 2,2′-bpy, only a 1D coordination polymer formed (complex 1). In 1, each Zn(ii) chelates with a bpy, which means the Zn(ii) can only coordinate with two carboxylates. Meanwhile in 2, the dinuclear Zn(ii) chelates only one phen. The Zn1 atom chelates to phen and also coordinates to three carboxylate oxygen atoms from three ligands, and Zn2 coordinates with 4 aobtc4−ligands. In complex 1, the metal ion is a 2-connector, while in 2, the dinuclear Zn(ii) is a 4-connected linker. Without a secondary building block, the trinuclear Zn(ii) note in 3 is a 8-connected linker.

Cation exchange of complex 2

Insertion and/or exchange of cation can be used to transform complexes from one crystalline form to another. This could be achieved by immersing crystals into an aqueous solution of a certain metal salt and generate the replacement of cations.12 The crystal of as-synthesized complex 2 was immersed into an aqueous solution of 10 mg ml−1 Cu(NO3)2, Co(NO3)2 or Ni(NO3)2 for five days and a visual color change was observed. Fig. S2 (ESI†) shows the visual color change during metal ion exchange in aqueous solution at room temperature. Compared to complex 2 (bright yellow), the colors of the resulting crystalline solids changed to dark green (Cu2+ exchanged), brown (Co2+ exchanged), and pale yellow (Ni2+ exchanged), respectively (Fig. 4). It is worthy to mention that all the metal ion exchanged products are very tiny nice crystals under a biological microscope although they are difficult to characterize with single crystal XRD. ICP metal analysis for the metal exchanged products are: Cu 9.033, Zn 1.621; Ni 6.565, Zn 2.701; Co 4.847, Zn 3.582 ppm for the three metal ion exchanged products. The Zn2 in complex 2 becomes Zn0.3Cu1.7, Zn0.6Ni1.4 and Zn0.85Co1.15 respectively. Thermodynamically, according the Irving–William series, all Zn(ii) in complex 2 can be replaced by Cu(ii), Ni(ii) and Co(ii). Under the same experimental conditions (concentration, temperature, exchange time, and post-processing, etc.), the incomplete metal ion exchange is due to kinetic reasons not a thermodynamic reason. The difference in exchange amount in the same reaction condition is a measurement of exchange-rate. The exchange rate is Cu2+ > Ni2+ > Co2+. Stability (Irving–William series) difference is the drive force in metal ion exchange process.

IR spectra of complex 2 before and after metal ion exchange are shown in Fig. S3 (ESI†). There is essentially no obvious change in the IR spectrum in the 4000–1800 cm−1 region. However, the strong peaks at 1626 and 1575 cm−1 in 2 shifted to lower wavenumbers after metal cation exchange. The 1626 cm−1 of the carboxylate group in complex 2 shifted to 1620, 1616 and 1614 cm−1 after being exchanged with Cu2+, Co2+ and Ni2+cations, respectively. The CO asymmetric stretching vibration at 1575 cm−1 also shifted to lower wavenumber (1560, 1562, 1554 cm−1) after exchanged by cations. The 1364 cm−1 C–O vibration of the carboxylic acid was slightly shifted 4–6 cm−1 lower. PXRD patterns are shown in Fig. S4 (ESI†). In Fig. S4, ESI,†XRD was used to check the purity of complex 2, all the peaks displayed in the measured pattern for complex 2 closely match those peaks in the simulated pattern generated from single-crystal diffraction data, indicating a single phase of complex 2 was formed. The simulated and experimental PXRD patterns agree well. Cu(ii) exchanged sample has no signal due to Cu Kα radiation used in the diffraction. The strongest diffraction peaks at 7.9, 10.4, 12.0 and 14.6° almost disappeared after metal ion exchange. New peaks at 13.3 and 16.6° appeared after metal ion exchange. These indicate that the crystallinity was destroyed after metal exchange. The Cu(ii) exchanged sample was analyzed by SEM shown in Fig. S5 (ESI†). The SEM photograph indicated that although the crystal partially retained its shape, cracks were generated on the surface and internally in the crystal. After Cu2+, Co2+, Ni2+cation exchange, only the Cu2+ exchanged sample has an EPR signal at g = 2.132, which is consistent with EPR signal of Cu ions (Fig. S6, ESI†).

Iodine adsorption and desorption of complex 2

Complex 2 is a porous coordination polymer with a 9 Å diameter void channel, which is enough to encapsulate I2. Dark purple solutions of I2 fade slowly to very light purple (Fig. S7, ESI†), while the crystals of 2 get darker. The single crystals retained their original well-defined external forms, but the crystals diffracted poorly on the X-ray beam and the resolution of the structure was poor. In comparison, the uptake of I2 in complex 2 is remarkably lower than that of lactate MOF in ref. 9a.

Conversely, the delivery of I2 from 2⊃nI2 was performed in ethanol at room temperature and monitored by UV-visspectroscopy. Fig. 5 shows the spectra change during the desorption process in ethanol. Within the first 60 minutes, the release of I2 in ethanol increases linearly with time, indicating that I2 release is governed by the host–guest interaction.9b After that I2 release moves toward the equilibrium state which is a free diffusion process. The release kinetics of iodine resembled those of zeolite and activated carbon.9a The temporal evolution of the UV spectrum for iodine in ethanol solution, which shows λmax at 224 nm, and the intensity of absorption band at 224 nm is proportional to concentration of I2. As seen from the inset curve of Fig. 5, it took about two hours to reach equilibrium. The absorbance at 224 nm is 0.85. Based on the standard iodine curve of UV-visspectrum reported by Zeng et al.,9a the encapsulated sample has a formula of 2⊃0.1I2. This insignificant I2 encapsulation is possibly due to the volatilization of encapsulated I2. The I2 encapsulated in the cavity of crystal surface are immobile, which prevent inner cavity encapsulation.

Methylene blue substitution of complex 3

The 4-aminopyridine cation in complex 3 is mono-protonated. This cation can be exchanged by methylene blue. Immersing 3 in methylene blue aqueous solution for one week, the color of the crystals of 3 changed from yellow to army green (Fig. 6, insert). PXRD patterns indicate the crystallinity is maintained before and after encapsulation of methylene blue as shown in Fig. S8 (ESI†). Complex 3 and its methylene blue exchanged sample were carefully analyzed by NMR before and after methylene blue substitution (Fig. 6). These two samples were suspended in D2O solution then solid NaOH added to give clear solutions. Two new signals between 2.5 and 3.0 appeared after methylene blue replacement. Based on integration of methylene blue protons, ∼0.1 methylene blue was encapsulated for 3. Only Hap+ in crystal surface cavity was exchanged by methylene blue cation. This phenomenon is identical to I2 encapsulation by 2.

IR spectroscopy

The IR spectra of complex 1–3, shown in Fig. S9 (ESI†), offer some valuable information about the metal–ligand bonding and the coordination modes of the carboxylates. There are two strong absorptions at 1564 and 1349 cm−1 of complex 1, 1564 cm−1 is attributed to νas(COO−) of the monodentate carboxylate, and 1349 cm−1 may correspond to the νas(COO−) of the bridging bidentate carboxylate groups. The strong bands at 1246 and 1097 cm−1 in complex 1 are assigned to C–O stretching vibrations.13 The band at 733 cm−1 in complex 1 is the characteristic absorption of 2,2′-bpy. In complex 2 the absorbance at 3442 cm−1 is attributed to vibration of OH−. There is no absorption in 1690–1730 cm−1 region which indicates complete deprotonation of the carboxylic groups. The strong absorption at 1660 and 1624 cm−1 in complex 2 is assigned to νas(CO) stretching vibration of the carboxylic group, and the significant shifts to lower wavenumbers show the coordination of the carboxylate oxygen atoms to metal ions compared with the literature.6f The strong absorbance bands at 1572 and 1432 cm−1 are also attributed to νas(COO−) and bridging bidentate carboxylate groups. The characteristic absorption of 1,10-phen is observed at 850 and 725 cm−1 in complex 2 which is somewhat like the reported data.6i In complex 3 the absorbance at 3439 cm−1 is attributed to vibration of OH− and the band at 3228 cm−1 is attributed to vibration of N–H of 4-ap. The absorbance at 3098 cm−1 is the expected value for the ν(CH) stretching vibration of the aromatic group. The strong absorption at 1647 and 1619 cm−1 assigned to νas(CO) stretching vibration of the carboxylic group (COO−) indicates that the carboxyl groups are completely deprotonation like in complex 2.

Thermogravimetric analysis

The TG–DSC analyses were carried out to examine the thermal stabilities of the synthesized complexes 1–3. Samples were heated in air from room temperature to 800 °C. The TG–DSC curves are provided in Fig. S10–S12 (ESI†). The TG–DSC curve of complex 1 showed water loss in the temperature range 50–100 °C. The weight loss of 1.82% from room temperature to 90 °C corresponds to the release of one cocrystallized water molecule (calc. 2.00%). From 92 to 152 °C, two coordinated water molecules were lost (weight loss of 4.07%, calc. 4.00%). A weight loss of 6.89% was observed from 150 to 230 °C, which corresponded to the loss of two NO molecules (calc. 6.67%), which means the framework of the ligand began to decompose. With bis-oxo group in the azo moiety, the framework of 1 is so unstable that it decomposed at ∼150 °C with simultaneously released NO. The azo oxidation of ligand decreased the thermal stability of the complex significantly. The remaining weight of 16.82% (calc. 18.06%) corresponds to the percentage of ZnO above 500 °C. For complex 2, a weight loss of 2.53% (calc. 2.33%) was observed in the temperature range of 30–71 °C, which corresponded to the loss of one uncoordinated water molecule, and then a weight loss of 9.39% indicated the loss of one DMF molecular (calc. 9.46%). The framework starts to decompose on approaching 300 °C. In contrast to complex 2, complex 3 is more stable as it has higher decomposition temperature. The complex 3 exhibited a total weight lost 10.41% from room temperature to 380 °C, which agreed with the departure of one crystalline water molecule and one ap molecule (calc. 11.79%). The decomposition temperature persisted up to about 405 °C and then the mother frameworks began to collapse.

Fluorescent spectra

Metal–organic polymer complexes with a d10 configuration (such as Zn2+ and Cd2+) have been found to exhibit photoluminescent properties.14 We examined the emission spectra of Zn(ii) complex 1, 2, 3 and free H4abtc ligand. The excitation and emission spectra of complexes 1, 2 and 3 and free ligand were measured in the solid state at room temperature (Fig. S13, ESI†) and the data are summarized in Table 3. All complexes as well as the ligand exhibit an emission peak at 387 nm when excited at 360 nm. There are emission peaks at 450 nm and 468 nm in complexes 1 and 2, respectively, which are consistent with free H4abtc ligand. Complex 3 shows fluorescent emission maximum at 387 nm, 430 nm and 469 nm. The luminescent behavior of complex 3 is similar to the free ligand except that the 430 nm emission in the ligand shifted to 450 nm in 3. The emission and excitation of complex 3 are somewhat different from those of 1 and 2. This difference is possibly due to the non-coplanar properties of the ligand in 3, while in 1, 2 and free ligand, the ligand is almost coplanar. Ligand oxygenation has no discernible influence on the fluorescence compared with ligand coplanarity. The main emissions of complex 1–3 and free ligand are in the 400–480 nm region, which is the blue region, suggesting that they may potentially be useful for blue fluorescent materials.15 We attributed the emissions to the intraligand transition of ligand 3,3′,5,5′-azobenzenetetracarboxylate.

Conclusion

Three novel coordination polymers {[Zn2(η6-ao2btc) (η2-2,2′-bpy)2(H2O)2]·2H2O}1n (1), {[Zn2(η8-aobtc)(η2-phen)(H2O)]·DMF}3n (2), and {(Hap)2[Zn3(η9-aobtc)2]·2H2O}3n (3) have been prepared under hydrothermal conditions. Complex 1 is a 1D chain polymer, while 2 and 3 are porous 3D metal–organic frameworks with cavity size of 9 Å diameter and 7 × 15 Å rectangular cavity, respectively. Ligand3,3′,5,5′-azobenzenetetracarboxylic acid (H4abtc) is fully deprotonated in all three complexes and links four mono-, di- and tri-nuclear Zn(ii) centers in 1, 2 and 3, respectively. Mononuclear ZnO6 in 1, dinuclear Zn2(CO2)4(H2O)N2 in 2 and Zn3(CO2)8 in 3 are 2, 4 and 8 connected nodes. Zn(ii) in complex 2 can be replaced by Cu(ii), Ni(ii) and Co(ii) with simultaneous loss of crystallinity. The metal ion exchange rate decreases in the order Co(ii) < Ni(ii) < Cu(ii). 2 can encapsulate I2 in cyclohexane solution to form 2⊃0.1I2. The Hap+ in 3 can be replaced by methylene blue in aqueous solution to form 3⊃0.1methylene blue.
