Introduction

Transition metal complexes with 1D chains or quasi-1D chain geometries have received significant attention for several decades1 due to their potential for tunable electronic conductivity2,3 and/or magnetic properties.4,5 Synthetic design of coordination polymers using transition metal complexes as building blocks and organic molecules as spacers has also been of interest due to their applications in the fields of catalysis,6 conductivity,7 and magnetism.8–10 The first bimetallic metal building block to be so-described is [Cu2(OAc)4]·2H2O, which was structurally described in 1952 and later often referred to as a “paddlewheel” or “lantern” structure.11 Magnetic measurements on this and related Cu lantern complexes showed them to exhibit intramolecular antiferromagnetic coupling between the Cu ions, with the strength of such coupling dependent in part on the nature of the bridging ligands.12 Since that time, hundreds of lantern complexes with four O,O-carboxylato ligands have been prepared with homo- and heterobimetallic combinations,13–17 including neutral complexes of two divalent metals such as Cr(ii),18 Zn(ii),19 Ru(ii),20 Mo(ii),21 Pt(ii),22 and Rh(ii).23 The M–M distance in such lantern complexes depends on the metals utilized, which also determines the resulting bond order between the metal atoms. For example, a {Ru(ii)2} compound has an σ2π4δ2δ*2π*2 electronic configuration and two unpaired electrons leading to a 2.26 Å RuRu bond.24 A quadruply bonded Cr2 lantern, with a σ2π4δ2 configuration, has a Cr–Cr distance of 2.362 Å.25

Lantern complexes with different chalcogen donors, including S,S-dithiocarboxylate26–28 and S,O-thiocarboxylate, have also been synthesized and reported, although they are far less numerous. To date there are 71 structurally-characterized29 homobimetallic dithiocarboxylate lantern complexes that have been prepared with a variety of metals including Pt, [Pt2(S2CR)4] (R = CH3, pentyl, cyclohexyl),30 Ni in the semiconductor [Ni2(S2CCH3)4]I,31 Mo in [Mo2(S2CC6H5)4],32 and Pd in [Pd2(S2CCH3)4].33 To date only three metals have been used in homobimetallic lantern complexes with monothiocarboxylates: Ni, Mo, and Rh. In each of [Ni2(tba)4(EtOH)],34 [Ni2(tba)4(NCCH3)],35 [Ni2(SAc)4(EtOH)],36 and [Ni2(SAc)4(NCCH3)],37 there are distinct {NiO4} and {NiS4} coordination environments. With Rh, [Rh2(SOCtBu)4(PPh3)2],38 [Rh2(SAc)4(HSAc)2],39 [Rh2(tba)4(py)2],40 and [Rh2(SOCtBu)4(py)2]40 each metal atom has {RhO2S2} coordination and the Rh(ii) centers are directly bonded forming diamagnetic species. Similarly, the Mo(ii) ions in [Mo2(tba)4(OPPh3)2]41 both have {MoS2O2} coordination from the carboxylates. The presence of both hard and soft donor sites make thiocarboxylates interesting ligands with the ability to site differentiate metal binding.

In recent years, our group has investigated extensively compounds of these ligands and prepared a large number of heterobimetallic thiocarboxylate lantern complexes, some with unusual magnetic properties. The first series reported used thiobenzoate with terminal H2O ligands in the form [PtM(tba)4(OH2)] (M = Fe, Co, Ni; tba = thiobenzoate) and two possessed antiferromagnetic coupling (M = Co, J = −10 cm−1; Ni, J = −60 cm−1) through an unbridged metallophilic Pt⋯Pt contact between lantern units.42 This series was expanded to include complexes featuring a thioacetate backbone and an N-donor terminal ligand, [PtM(SAc)4(L)] (M = Co, Ni, Zn; L = H2O, 3-NO2py) which featured similar magnetic coupling in the Co and Ni derivatives and demonstrated some generality in the phenomenon.43 Complexes with stronger axial donors such as L = DMSO, DMF, pyridine, pyNH2, did not demonstrate metallophilic interactions or magnetic coupling.44 Instead, three distinct lantern dimerization motifs were observed indicating that the donor strength of the axial ligand L tunes the Pt electronic environment such that only weaker donors like H2O favor a metallophilic contact and antiferromagnetic coupling.44 To understand further the electronic environment at Pt, a computational and 195Pt NMR study was made of the diamagnetic compounds in this family, including the known M = Zn complexes, as well as new lantern compounds with M = Mg and Ca.45 This investigation revealed that the Pt–Zn bonding interaction can be described as a metal-only Lewis acceptor (Pt)–donor (M) interaction, whereas the Mg and Ca compounds have little internal Pt⋯M interaction and have largely ionic bonding. Moving beyond the dimeric lantern constructs, we have begun investigating bidentate axial ligands that could make chains. The series {[Na(crown)x][PtM(SAc)4(NCS)]} was prepared with M = Mn, Co, Ni, Zn, but did not show any Pt⋯SNCS interaction, apparently due to electrostatic repulsion between the lantern complexes.46 Using Cr(iii), a quasi-1D infinite zig-zag chain of [PtCr(tba)4(NCS)] units was prepared, which displays a ferromagnetic interaction, J/kB = 1.21 cm−1, between Cr centers that are 7.772(1) Å apart.46

The two nitrogen donor-containing ligands pyrazine (pyz) and 1,4-diazabicyclo[2.2.2]octane (DABCO) are ideal for forming linear, quasi-1D chains by bridging metal centers. In part due to the ease by which these compounds can be used in assembling extended structures, much research has been performed on the molecular orbital basis for magnetic coupling in compounds bridged by these species.47 Both pyrazine and DABCO, shown in Scheme 1, possess a feature which has been successful in coupling in organic systems: two lone pairs separated by three σ bonds. While pyrazine also possesses a π system for electronic coupling, it was predicted that exchange coupling would be more effective through its σ system.47 In both DABCO and pyrazine, the nitrogen lone pairs are capable of interacting through empty σ* orbitals in the center of each ligand, facilitating a magnetic interaction through the ligands.48

Magnetic properties of several Cu 1D-chain complexes of the type [Cu2(tren)2(bridge)]n(ClO4)4n have been reported48 in which the bridge is either pyz or DABCO. Only the pyrazine complex showed signs of an antiferromagnetic exchange interaction in magnetic susceptibility data measured down to 4.2 K. The first example of magnetic exchange across pyrazine was reported by Villa and Hatfield in 1971.49 A 1D-chain composed of [Cu(NO3)2(pyz)] displayed an antiferromagnetic exchange with J = −6 cm−1. Subsequently pyrazine has been used to bridge lantern complexes with metal cores of {Cu2},50 {Cr2},51 {Rh2},52–54 and {Mo2}55 into infinite quasi-1D chains, as well as bridging {Ru2}56 and {Os2}57 lanterns into discrete tetrametallic species. The quasi-1D chain [Cu2(OAc)4(pyz)]∞ displays an intraunit magnetic exchange of J = −325 cm−1, and an interunit exchange of J′ = −0.1 cm−1.50 Full magnetic characterization has not been performed on the other quasi-1D chains, though it was noted that the {Mo2} example shows no significant interaction between the lantern units due to the weak coordination at the Mo axial position. Similarly, the magnetic exchange has not been quantified for the singly-bridged tetrametallic compounds [(chp)4M2](μ2-pyz)[M2(chp)4]2+ (M = Ru, Os; Hchp = 6-chloro-2-hydroxypyridine), but it was noted that the M = Ru analogue showed essentially no exchange across pyrazine56 whereas the M = Os compound showed significant coupling. No coupling constant could be determined due to the complex nature of the ground state with large zero-field splitting and orbital contributions.57 The earliest example of DABCO as an axial ligand for lantern complexes was reported in 1983 and resulted in the quasi-1D infinite chain compound [Cu2(OAc)4(DABCO)]∞, though no magnetic characterization was reported.58 Since that time DABCO has been used to bridge lantern complexes into a variety of geometries. Additional 1D chains have been prepared from lanterns with {Mo2},55 {Rh2},59 {Cd2},60 and {Zn2}60 cores, as well as additional Cu examples.60–63 Of these only [Mo2(OAc)4(DABCO)]∞ 55 and [Cu2(ACA)4(DABCO)]∞ 61 (HACA = anthracene-9-carboxylic acid) reported magnetic characterization, with neither showing any magnetic exchange.

More recently DABCO has been used as a bridging ligand in MOFs based on paddlewheel units, bridging Co,70 Ni,71 Cu,72 and Zn70,72 centers. For none of these MOFs was any magnetic characterization reported. Finally, there is a single example of a lantern complex [Cu2(3-iodobenzoate)4(DABCO)2] with terminal DABCO ligands.73

Herein we describe our investigations of [Ni2(SOCR)4] lantern complexes bridged with DABCO and pyrazine. Like the heterobimetallic [PtM(SOCR)4] complexes, these lanterns contain spin on one metal atom, the {NiO4} center. Quasi 1D chains containing Ni have been reported in the past. We adopt here a polyhedral perspective to give an overview of these compounds.7 Ni centers of various coordination polyhedra are corner- and edge-linked into quasi-1D coordination polymers.7 Several basic and representative connectivity patterns in quasi-1D bridged polymers containing Ni are given in Scheme 2. Long organic ligands, however, are not as good communicators of magnetic information as are shorter linkers such as halides, cyanide, azide, pseudohalides, pyrazine, and oxalate. For example, the chain [Ni(L)(μ1,1-N3)2 (L = 1,2-diaminocyclohexane) shows a strong ferromagnetic interaction arising from the μ1,1-azido bridging Ni(ii) atoms.74 The magnetic behavior in the dinuclear Ni(ii)-1D-chain complex, [Ni2(L)2(μ1,1-N3)2(pyz)]∞ (HL = 2-[(3-methylamino-propylimino)-methyl]-phenol), is dominated by ferromagnetic behavior attributed to interactions within the dinuclear units through the μ1,1-azido bridges of the chelating ligand.75 Herein we report the synthesis and characterization of four quasi-1D homobimetallic Ni2-lantern chains containing N,N′-donor bridging ligands, [Ni2(SOCR)4(L)]∞ (R = Ph, L = DABCO (1); R = Ph, L = pyz (2); R = CH3, L = DABCO (3); R = CH3, L = pyz (4)), as well as a dimeric lantern complex containing quinuclidine as a terminal ligand, [Ni2(tba)4(quin)] (5). We also report an alternate synthetic route and spectroscopic data for the previously reported mononuclear [Ni(tba)2(py)2] (6).

Magnetic susceptibility measurements

Evans’ method79,80 solution magnetic susceptibility measurements were performed for 5 and 6. 1H NMR spectra were collected on a Varian 500 MHz spectrometer. Solutions were prepared with CDCl3 doped with hexamethyldisiloxane, and the shift in 1H resonances was measured for both the residual protio solvent and the CH3 protons of hexamethyldisiloxane.

Solid-state magnetic susceptibility data for compounds 1–4 were collected using a Quantum Design MPMS XL SQUID magnetometer. Powdered microcrystalline samples were loaded into polyethylene bags and inserted into a straw before transportation to the magnetometer. The presence of ferromagnetic impurities was probed by a variable field analysis (0 to 10 kOe) of the magnetisation at 100 K; lack of curvature in these M vs. H plots (Fig. S15–S20†) indicate the absence of significant ferromagnetic impurities. Magnetic susceptibility data were collected at temperatures ranging from 2 to 300 K. Data were corrected for the diamagnetic contributions of the sample holder and bag by subtracting empty containers; corrections for the sample were calculated from Pascal's constants.81

X-ray crystallography

Crystals of 2–5 were mounted on a Cryoloop with Paratone-N oil, and data were collected at 100 K with a Bruker APEX II CCD diffractometer using Mo Kα radiation. Data were corrected for absorption with SADABS, and structures were solved by direct methods. All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically by full matrix least-squares on F2. For 2–5 hydrogen atoms were placed in calculated positions with appropriate riding parameters. All software is contained in various libraries (SHELXTL, SMART, and SAINT) maintained by Bruker AXS, Madison, WI.82

In the structure of 2, reflections (−3 5 0) and (2 0 0) were affected by the beam stop and were omitted from the refinement. The structure exhibits disorder induced by a 180° rotation of the thiocarboxylate ligands, inducing a ca. 0.7 Å shift of the two Ni ions of the paddlewheel and the bridging pyrazine ligand. The occupancy ratio of the two possible geometries refined to 0.914(2) to 0.086(2). The ADPs of Ni and pyrazine atoms were each constrained to be identical between major and minor moieties. An enhanced rigid bond restraint with an effective standard deviation of 0.002 Å2 was used for the Ni and disordered thiocarboxylate atoms.

For 3, reflections (−3 5 0), (−8 4 1), (1 7 1), (−1 7 1), (−1 7 3), (1 5 0), (−9 1 3), (−8 2 1) and (10 4 3) were affected by the beam stop and were omitted from the refinement. Twinning by pseudo-merohedry by a 180° rotation around the [1 0 2] direct lattice direction was handled using the twin matrix {−1 0 0, 0 −1 0, 1 0 1} resulting in a BASF value of 0.061(3). Both thioacetate anions were refined using a two-part disorder model with a common occupancy ratio. The four disordered moieties were restrained to have similar geometries (SAME restraint), and Uij components of ADPs were restrained to be similar for atoms closer to each other than 1.7 Å (SIMU restraint). The thioacetate anions in the minor moiety were restrained to be planar (FLAT restraint). The shared occupancy ratio refined to 0.962(5) to 0.038(5). The DABCO ligand was also disordered in a 50 : 50 manner as a result of its location across a crystallographic two fold axis. Uij components of ADPs of all DABCO carbon and nitrogen atoms were restrained to be similar for atoms closer to each other than 1.7 Å.

In 4, reflection (−14 4 5) was affected by the beam stop and was omitted from the refinement. Twinning by pseudo-merohedry by a 180° rotation around the [1 0 2] direct lattice direction was handled using the twin matrix {−1 0 0, 0 −1 0, 1 0 1} resulting in a BASF value of 0.413(3). Both thioacetate anions were refined using a two-part disorder model with a common occupancy ratio. The four disordered moieties were restrained to have similar geometries (SAME restraint), and Uij components of ADPs were restrained to be similar for atoms closer to each other than 1.7 Å (SIMU restraint). The shared occupancy ratio refined to 0.815(7) to 0.185(5). The ADPs of C5 and C6 were constrained to be identical (EADP).

For 5, reflection (0 0 1) was affected by the beam stop and was omitted from the refinement. The thiocarboxylate units of all four thiobenzoate ligands are disordered by a rotation of about 30° around their C–C axes. The thiocarboxylate units rotate in concert and the occupancy ratio is shared among the four thiobenzoate ligands. For one ligand the disorder extends out to the phenyl unit. All C–S and C–O distances were each restrained to be similar in length (SADI commands) and minor thiocarboxylate units were restrained to be planar (FLAT command). ADPs of oxygen and carbon atoms were constrained to be identical for the major and minor moieties. For the completely disordered thiobenzoate ligand the geometries of the major and minor moiety were restrained to be similar. A solvate site is occupied by a disordered and partially occupied methylene chloride molecule. Two symmetry equivalent moieties are disordered around an inversion center. The two C–Cl distances were restrained to be similar (SADI command). The disorder extends to the immediately neighboring phenyl ring, with the major moiety phenyl position having the same occupancy as the total of the methylene chloride solvate occupancy. The geometries of the two phenyl units were restrained to be similar (SAME command). Uij components of ADPs of all disordered atoms were restrained to be similar for atoms closer to each other than 1.7 Å (SIMU restraint). Given these conditions the occupancy ratio for the thiocarboxylate units refined to 0.9182(11) to 0.0818(11), and the occupancy for the methylene chloride molecule (both disordered moieties combined) to 0.582(3).

Computational studies

The ORCA83 electronic structure program was used for geometry optimizations and density functional theory (DFT) calculations of zero-field splitting (ZFS) parameters, g-tensors, and magnetic exchange coupling constants. All ORCA calculations were performed at the PBE084,85 level of theory with relativistically recontracted TZVP86 basis sets and the scalar zeroth-order relativistic approximation (ZORA).87 Natural bond orbital (NBO) analysis of electronic delocalizations was performed at the PBE0/cc-pVTZ level using the NBO 5.9 module88,89 as implemented in Firefly 8.1.0,90 which is partially based on the GAMESS (US)91 source code. DFT results were verified with n-electron valence state perturbation theory (NEVPT2)92–94 calculations. Further details of the computational methods are presented in the ESI.†

Results and discussion

Synthesis and structures

The precursors [Ni2(SAc)4(EtOH)]76 and [Ni2(tba)4(EtOH)]37 were synthesized according to their published procedures, and combined with N-donor ligands as summarized in Scheme 3. The dark brown single crystals of four 1D chain Ni compounds with the common lantern core [Ni2(SOCR)4(L)]∞ (R = Ph, L = DABCO (1); R = Ph, L = pyz (2); R = CH3, L = DABCO (3); R = CH3, L = pyz (4)) were prepared by layering the [Ni2(SOCR)4(EtOH)] solutions with equimolar N-donor ligand solutions and storing at ∼9° C. Compound 5, [Ni2(tba)4(quin)] was prepared by dissolving the freshly prepared precursor in acetone and adding an acetone solution of quinuclidine (quin) resulting in a color change from brown to yellow-brown. Preparation of [Ni2(SOCR)4(py)]2 compounds was unsuccessful. Upon addition of two pyridines per lantern to the precursor solution, a distinct color change from brown to green was observed, indicating the loss of the lantern structure and formation of the mononuclear [Ni(tba)2(py)2], 6. This compound has been previously reported,95,96 and crystallographically characterized in cis95 and trans97 isomers.

Single crystal X-ray diffraction data collection parameters for 2–5 are given in Table S1† and selected bond distances and angles are given in Table 1. Structure determinations of 2–4 reveal that the Ni2-lantern units remain intact, and that each dinuclear unit is linked by the N,N′-donor bridging ligands DABCO or pyrazine to form an infinite quasi-1D chain that propagates along the [010] direction. The chains all crystallize in the C2/c space group, with the chains parallel to the b axis. An ORTEP of the asymmetric unit of 2 and adjacent molecules are shown in Fig. 1 and a view along the quasi-1D axis is shown in Fig. 2. Analogous plots are presented in the ESI† for 3 and 4 (Fig. S1–S4†). The two Ni atoms in the [Ni2(SOCR)4] units are bridged by four thiocarboxylate ligands such that one Ni atom is surrounded by four sulfur atoms and the other by four oxygen atoms in a similar square-planar arrangement. The coordination spheres of the nickel atoms are completed by a Ni–Ni interaction between the two metal atoms and a nitrogen donor atom from the pyrazine or DABCO ligand to yield a distorted octahedral environment for each nickel atom.

Because of the size difference between S and O, the {Ni2SOC} atoms in the bridging chelate ring are not all coplanar. The {NiO4} and {NiS4} planes are twisted about the Ni–Ni axis by 18–23° from an ideal eclipsed configuration which is slightly smaller than the twist found in the precursor structures (22–24° and 21–24° for [Ni2(tba)4(EtOH)] and [Ni2(SAc)4(EtOH)], respectively). This difference leads to Ni–Ni distances (2.5316(18), 2.595(2) and 2.540(4) Å in 2, 3 and 4, respectively) that are slightly longer than those in the precursor structures (2.5012(12) and 2.506–2.511(2) Å for [Ni2(tba)4(EtOH)] and [Ni2(SAc)4(EtOH)], respectively). These distances are close to the range of Ni–Ni distances of 2.5336(8)–2.5502(11) Å in the Cambridge Structural Database (CSD),29 except that of 3 is slightly longer than that found in the charge-neutral, mixed-valent, 1D iodine-bridged binuclear chain compounds, [Ni II/III2(RCS2)4I]∞ (R = Et, n-Pr and n-Bu),68 (average 2.535 Å) but they are all significantly shorter than the sum of the van der Waals radii of the two Ni atoms (3.26 Å). The Ni–O distances ranging from 1.953(9)–2.099(19) Å in 2–4 are similar to those of their precursors as well as previously reported heterobimetallic {PtNi} lantern complexes.42–44 The Ni–S distances of 2.205(9)–2.264(5) Å found in these complexes are consistent with several other similar complexes such as [Ni2(S2CCH3)4] with a range between 2.197 and 2.211 Å.31 There is only one known compound in which dimeric Ni2 units [Ni2L2(N3)2] (HL = (2-[(3-methylamino-propylimino)-methyl]-phenol)) are bridged in a 1D chain by pyrazine,75 but no 1D chain compound with dimeric Ni2 units joined by DABCO bridges has been reported. The Ni–N(pyz) bond distances of 2.057(7)–2.262(8) Å in 2 and 4 are longer than an average Ni–N(pyz) distance of 2.11(5) Å for 47 examples in the CSD.29,98,99 However, the Ni–N(DABCO) distance of 2.164(11)–2.432(11) Å in 3 is comparable to the average Ni–N(DABCO) distance of 2.21(9) Å for 18 examples from the CSD. The Ni–Ni–N bond angles in 2–4 are rigorously 180°.

The structure of 5, shown in Fig. 3, features a dimeric assembly of two lantern complexes related by a crystallographic inversion center through a {Ni2S2} rhombus. Each dinuclear unit is capped on one side, at Ni(2), by a quinuclidine ligand. The other nickel atom, Ni(1), is not coordinated to N but instead interacts with a sulfur atom of the other lantern unit. The Ni–Ni distance within each lantern is 2.5747(4) Å, which is about 0.07 Å longer than in the precursor. The {NiS4} and {NiO4} planes are twisted about the Ni–Ni axis by 21.74–23.86° away from an eclipsed configuration, slightly greater than the angles in 2–4. The Ni–N(quin) bond length is 2.1238(16) Å, which is slightly longer than the value of 2.068(3) Å reported for (quin)Ni(C2H4)2.100 The Ni–Ni–N bond angle is nearly linear, 175.53(5)°. Two S atoms from two thiobenzoate ligands mutually bridge the inversion-center-related Ni–lantern complexes, via two symmetry equivalent Ni⋯S interactions. The Ni–S distances are 2.8330(1) Å, which are shorter than the sum of their van der Waals radii (3.26 Å). The coordinated {Ni2S2} atoms form a planar core with a Ni⋯Ni separation of 3.8828(1) Å and have a Ni–Ni–Ni angle between the lantern units of 134.58°.

A search of the CSD for dinickel lantern complexes with carboxylate ligands having any chalcogen donor produced 46 structures that had O,O (34), O,S (4), or S,S (8) donors. A summarizing table is given in Table S4.† All these complexes have terminal Lewis basic donors on the Ni atoms that are {NiO4} coordinated and do not have small molecules bound to the {NiS4} centers. These {NiS4} coordination environments sometimes have solid-state interactions with S atoms from an adjacent lantern, as described above and elsewhere.46,101 A few of the [Ni2(S2CR)4] complexes31 have been oxidized with I2 to form chains of mixed-valent Ni(ii)Ni(iii) complexes bridged by iodide. Based on our experience with thiocarboxylate lantern complexes,42–46 metals with {MO4} coordination either bind Lewis basic small molecules in the axial positions, or form insoluble species probably by intermolecular coordination to adjacent carboxylates.

The insoluble species can only be broken up by strong donor solvents such as DMSO or DMF.44 These observations are also supported by the [Ni2(E2CR)4] structures summarized in the ESI.† The most conspicuous difference observed between the new structures reported herein and those in the literature, is that the [Ni2(O2CR)4] complexes have longer intramolecular Ni⋯Ni distances (2.68(7) Å) than the [Ni2(OSCR)4], 2.503(7) Å or [Ni2(S2CR)4] 2.54(1) Å. A plot of the Ni⋯Ni distances versus the Ni–Ni–L angles is given in Fig. 4. The shortening seems to be due to the torsion induced by the larger S atoms in the chelate rings.

Electronic spectroscopy

Diffuse reflectance UV-vis-NIR spectra were collected for 1–4 in the solid state due to low solubility of the compounds and a Kubelka Munk analysis was performed. The four quasi-1D compounds all exhibit similar absorbance patterns, as shown in Fig. 5. Two LMCT absorbances are observed in the UV range, around 290 and 380 nm. One d–d transition is seen in the visible range between 492–502 nm, and there is a weak absorption in the NIR region, perhaps due to an intermetallic d–d transition42 with a broader range of energies: 1150 nm (1), 1291 nm (2), 1251 nm (3) and 1193 nm (4) (Fig. 5 and S5†).

Electronic absorption spectra for 5 and 6 were recorded in acetone (Fig. S6–S7†). Compound 5 displays a weak, broad d–d transition in the visible range around 710 nm. Although it has been previously structurally characterized,94–96 other spectroscopic data have not been published for 6 and therefore we include them here. A broad peak assigned to a d–d transition is centered at 624 nm, and an absorption in the NIR region around 1096 nm is also seen, but not yet assigned.

Magnetic measurements

To provide some background on the variable-temperature magnetic susceptibility data of [Ni2(E2CR)(L)] (where E = S or O) complexes, a brief literature survey is warranted. Few of the structurally characterized dinickel complexes have been studied magnetically. Of the ones studied, most linkers containing two O donor atoms lead to paramagnetic Ni(ii) centers with strong antiferromagnetic intramolecular interactions, yielding S = 0 ground states. A [Ni2(O2CR)4(4,4′bipy)] unit used in the synthesis of a metal organic framework exhibits strong intramolecular antiferromagnetic interactions between S = 1 Ni centers (J = −190.03 cm−1).102 Meanwhile, the isolated anthracene carboxylate [Ni2(O2CC14H9)4(py)2] (J = −537 cm−1)103 and the analogous pivalate lanterns [Ni2(O2CC4H9)4(2,3-lutidine)2] and [Ni2(O2CC4H9)4(py)2] also show strong intramolecular antiferromagnetic interactions.104 Interestingly, a NEt3-capped dinuclear complex in the latter report remains S = 1 for the temperatures probed, suggesting that the coupling between Ni centers can also be modulated by the capping ligand. The intramolecular interactions in the S,O-linked species [Ni2(tba)4(EtOH)] also lead to a triplet ground state.37 Dimerization in the solid state via a square of mutual {Ni2S2} contacts, as in 5, generates weak intermolecular antiferromagnetic interactions (J = −3.76 cm−1). In contrast, structurally characterized [Ni2(S2CR)4] complexes are singlets across all temperatures measured.68

Given the opportunity for tuning magnetic properties in S,O-bound Ni2 lanterns, we also investigated the properties of both the molecular compounds 5 and 6. Fits of the variable temperature magnetic susceptibility data, performed in the solid state for mononuclear 6 (Fig. S10†), give magnetic parameters typical for S = 1 Ni(ii) complexes, including g = 2.21 and axial anisotropy |D| = 3.18 cm−1. Solution measurements via Evans’ method79,80 give a room temperature χMT value of 0.99 cm3 K mol−1 (μeff = 2.81), which is close to the expected spin-only magnetic moment for a high spin Ni(ii) center (χMT = 1.00 cm3 K mol−1, μeff = 2.83) and is consistent with previously synthesized Ni(ii) octahedral complexes.105

Meanwhile, the dinuclear complex 5 displays a solution room temperature χMT value of 1.10 cm3 K mol−1 (μeff = 3.11), which is consistent with an isolated S = 1 ground state. This triplet system could be described as two Ni centers with differing spin states (S = 1 and 0), or alternatively as two strongly antiferromagnetically coupled S = 1 centers, with two unpaired electrons residing in orthogonal orbitals. A further complication arises from the fact that the dinuclear complexes in 5 interact in the solid state through Ni2S2 linkages, and antiferromagnetic magnetic interactions might be expected if this structure were maintained in solution. Although it is unlikely that the “dimer” revealed in the crystal structure remains intact in solution, it cannot be discounted from the characterizations discussed thus far.

An attempt to remove structural ambiguity by measuring the solid state magnetic properties of 5 reveals different temperature dependent behavior, and highlights the delicate balance of magnetic interactions in the system. Powdered samples of 5 exhibit a room temperature χMT value of 2.10 cm3 K mol−1 per formula unit, suggesting the presence of two S = 1 Ni(ii) centers in each lantern (Fig. 6), in contrast to [Ni(tba)4(EtOH)] which contains an S = 0 {NiS4} center and an S = 1 {NiO4} center. A monotonic decrease in χMT becomes more pronounced below 75 K, eventually reaching 0.09 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K. The temperature dependent behavior is consistent with weak antiferromagnetic coupling between the Ni centers (J = −2.18 cm−1 obtained by fitting susceptibility data “as a monomer” using PHI).106

The behavior of 5 differs from that observed for [Ni2(tba)4(EtOH)], which is formulated as dimers of S = 1 lanterns that couple antiferromagnetically through Ni2S2 linkages (J = −3.76 cm−1).37 The main differences in the structures are the change in capping ligand (N vs. O coordination) and the Ni⋯Ni distances (2.5747(4) Å vs. 2.500(1) Å). The slightly longer internickel distance in 5 may reflect a trans influence due to slightly stronger donation from the quinuclidine ligand. Further weakening of the {NiS4} site due to interlantern Ni2S2 interactions may generate a second S = 1 Ni(ii) species. The comparison of 5 and [Ni2(tba)4(EtOH)] shows the significance of the axial capping ligand on modulating intralantern magnetic interactions.

The solid state magnetic properties of 1–4 were also investigated to evaluate any changes in coupling upon incorporation of the lanterns into a chain motif. Overall, the temperature- and field-dependent magnetization properties of the four chain compounds are very similar. The magnetization saturation experiments performed at 2 K (Fig. S11–S14†) show magnetization values that approach 2 μB, suggestive of S = 1 ground states for these systems.

Meanwhile, the temperature dependencies of the χMT products of the four chain compounds are also similar (Fig. 6 and S8–S9†). Data and best fit lines for 1 essentially overlay those for 2, therefore the former are presented in Fig. S8.† The room temperature values range from 1.28 cm3 K mol−1 to 1.40 cm3 K mol−1, which are consistent with lantern S = 1 ground states. The χMT values remain greater than 1 cm3 K mol−1 until low temperature (>10 K). The small but linear increase in χMT values as temperature is increased above 10 K is consistent with temperature independent paramagnetism (TIP). The lack of curvature in χMT values at higher temperatures suggests that the S = 1 state is relatively well isolated. The sharper downturns at lower temperatures, however, can be indicative of magnetic anisotropy or antiferromagnetic interactions between lantern units. Comparison of the low-temperature χMT behaviors for 1–4 to that observed for the mononuclear 6 (Fig. S10†) suggests that anisotropy alone is not responsible for the sharper downturn.

The overall temperature dependencies of the susceptibility data do not allow us to separate a Ni–Ni bonded triplet from a mixed spin-state species in which one {NiS4} site is diamagnetic and the other {NiO4} site has two unpaired spins. The Ni⋯Ni distance for 5 is bracketed by the distances observed in 2–4 (Table 1), so that quantity cannot be used to explain the different behaviors between the chains and the dimer of lanterns. The {NiO4}–N distances are ∼0.1–0.3 Å shorter than the {NiS4}–N distances for each of 2–4, consistent with the {NiO4} center being more Lewis acidic than the {NiS4} one.

In order to further characterize the magnetic properties of 1–4, the magnetic susceptibility data were fit using both the Van Vleck equation and the Fisher chain model (Tables S2 and S3†).107 Details of the fitting procedures and results are collected in the ESI.† Fits of the data to both models give comparable values of g and TIP. The mean field interactions obtained from PHI106 (zJ) in 1–4 are weakly antiferromagnetic, approximately −0.1 cm−1; inclusion of anisotropy parameters does not significantly improve the fits nor change the magnitude of intermolecular interactions. Meanwhile, assuming only one-dimensional intrachain coupling, the Fisher chain model gives comparable coupling parameters (−0.13 > J > −0.17 cm−1). These analyses indicate that interactions along the chain are weak. For comparison, a related Cr–Pt lantern bridged by isothiocyanate shows an exchange coupling parameter that is ∼10 times larger than those measured here.46 There seems to be minimal coupling between Ni2 lanterns, even though pyz and DABCO were originally hypothesized to allow for magnetic communication through their respective σ systems.

Notably, neither model successfully fits the discontinuities in χMT values observed at ∼10 K. Examination of χMvs. T plots for compounds 1–4 show no evidence of long range magnetic ordering. Thus, considering that the paramagnetism of the Ni ions in the lantern is very sensitive to small structural changes, we suspect that a redistribution of spin states occurs concomitant with interlantern exchange coupling.

Computational studies

Compounds 1–4 were modeled as monomeric lanterns axially capped at each metal center with the applicable ligand [L{Ni2(SOCR)4}L] (1A–4A). Assignment of the triplet state as the ground state as indicated by the magnetic measurements is supported by computational analyses. Geometrically, the triplet states of 1A–4A demonstrate better agreement with experimental values of Ni–E (where E = Ni, N, O, S) length and S–Ni–Ni–O torsion angle than optimized structures of singlet, quintet, and several broken symmetry variations (Tables S6, S34–S57†). Analysis of the relative energies further supported this conclusion, as the triplet state was found to be most energetically favorable of the six examined options (Table S5†). Calculated values for the isotropic g-value are reasonably close to those obtained experimentally (Table S26†) and within the range expected for Ni(ii) centers. These results were verified with NEVPT2 calculations.

There are two possible distributions of spin across the triplet state, as shown in Scheme 4. There can be delocalization across both centers and two S = 1/2 ions (left), or localization on a single center (right). Examination of spin density confirms that the sulfur ligated NiII centers are low-spin, S = 0, while the oxygen ligated NiII centers are high-spin, S = 1. The more localized arrangement is energetically favored by at least 6 kcal mol−1 for 1A–4A as calculated at the NEVPT2(4,4) level (Scheme 4, Table S33†).

Mulliken, Löwdin, and natural spin population analyses of the optimized triplet state also show that the unpaired spins are primarily localized on the {NiO4} center. Population values for the triplet states of 1A–4A are greater than 1.60 on the {NiO4} in all schemes, in contrast to less than 0.13 on the {NiS4} centers (Tables S7–S16†). The majority of the spin on the high-spin NiII is found in the 3dz2 and 3dx2−y2 orbitals in all cases, as predicted by simple ligand-field theory, and the difference between Ni centers is clearest in the case of 4A. The natural spin population in the 3dz2 orbital of {NiO4} is 0.767, while that on {NiS4} is 0.080. Total natural spin populations on the {NiS4} center are comparable to those on the pyrazine N coordinated to the {NiO4}. The majority of the spin on the former is primarily located in the 3dz2 orbital. Together, these data indicate that Ni in {NiS4} is behaving as a σ-donor ligand to the {NiO4} center (Fig. S41–S44†). Visualizations of the spin density for the singlet, triplet, quintet, and BS(2,2) structures of 1A–4A are presented in the ESI.†

A second model was used to estimate the magnetic exchange coupling between the lanterns for 1–4. In this model, three axial N-donor ligands (DABCO or pyrazine) capped the two lantern units with one of these ligands bridging the two lanterns [L{Ni2(SOCR)4}L{Ni2(SOCR)4}L] (1B–4B). Geometries were optimized with quintet wavefunctions, and then single point BS(2,2) calculations were performed on the equilibrium structures. 1B–4B all showed very weak exchange coupling between the lanterns (|J| < 0.3 K, Table S17†), consistent with the experimental measurements.

Compound 5 was modeled as a “monomer”, such that the {NiS4} metal center was not capped with an axial ligand, but an N-donor was bound to the {NiO4} end, [{Ni2(tba)4}(L)] (5A). As shown in Tables S5, S6, and S58–S63,† the relative energies of the different spin states and the geometries of 5A all point to the lantern in 5 being described as a triplet with an electronic structure similar to what was described for 1A–4A (Tables S11, S16, S33; Fig. S37–40 and S45†). This interpretation is also consistent with previously published magnetism studies on [Ni2(SOCR)4] complexes.37 The solid-state magnetic measurements, however, suggest that 5 can also be described with a BS(2,2) wavefunction in which each of the nickel centers is high spin and the ground state wavefunction is an open-shell singlet.

The lack of an axial capping ligand is expected to have a significant effect on the spin state of the {NiS4} center based on simple molecular orbital theory. In the absence of a capping ligand, a common square-planar environment obtains, and it is expected that the Ni 3dz2 orbital will be stabilized, and thus the low-spin state of the metal center is observed. In the presence of an additional donor, namely a fifth S atom from an adjacent lantern, a more octahedral environment with a relatively destabilized 3dz2 orbital is present. Work is currently underway to quantify and elucidate the effect that an axial capping ligand has on the spin state of the {NiS4} center.

Conclusions

The reaction of [Ni2(SOCR)4(EtOH)] with N,N′-donor bridges affords the new quasi-1D chain complexes of the common unit [Ni2(SOCR)4(L)]∞ (R = Ph, L = DABCO (1); R = Ph, L = pyz (2); R = CH3, L = DABCO (3); R = CH3, L = pyz (4)). The reaction of [Ni2(tba)4(EtOH)] with quinuclidine yields the dinuclear complex [Ni2(tba)4(quin)]2 (5) and with pyridine yields the mononuclear [Ni(tba)2(py)2] (6). Solid state magnetic characterizations of chains 1–4 reveal that an S = 1 ground state (per Ni2 lantern) is appropriate, and DFT calculations support a high-spin S = 1 {NiO4} center and a low-spin S = 0 {NiS4} center. Fits to the magnetic susceptibility data suggest that communication through pyrazine and DABCO σ systems is weak, but small changes to the bridging ligand may produce a large effect on the coupling. In the solid state, the dimeric 5 shows a significant effect of the capping ligand on the strength of intraunit coupling. Exploratory efforts to map the tunability of magnetic coupling in these and related systems are underway.
