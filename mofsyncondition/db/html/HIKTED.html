Introduction

In recent times, coordination polymers (CPs) have emerged as a novel class of porous materials due to their fascinating architectures1 and noteworthy applications in sorption,2 separation,3 catalysis,4 sensing,5 conduction,6 and ion exchange and transport.7 Such interest is largely due to their high surface area, porosity, and tunable architectures that often regulate their applications.1,8 For designing new CPs, many factors play crucial roles in controlling both the dimensionality and topology of the resultant material;9 however, the framework structure of a CP primarily depends on the coordination preferences of a metal and the type of ligand as a linker.10 In this context, the use of carboxylate based ligands has been quite successful in the construction of assorted CPs.11 Such a noteworthy feature is not only due to the ability of a carboxylate group to coordinate a variety of metal ions, but also due to its capability to support assorted coordination geometries.11 Another notable feature that carboxylate-based ligands impart in the resultant CPs is their enhanced thermal stability that has greatly assisted in heterogeneous catalysis.12

In pursuit of robust heterogeneous catalysts, the structural and thermal stability of CPs and the non-interpenetrable nature of the resultant network play important roles.13 These points warrant the development of next-generation stable and robust yet catalytically active CPs.14 In this context, CPs based on metalloligands have emerged as suitable alternatives to metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) based on organic linkers.15 Our group has developed a variety of metalloligand-based CPs functioning as noteworthy heterogeneous catalysts for various organic transformations.15,16 Such transformations vary from Lewis-acid catalyzed reactions16a,b to oxidation reactions15b to challenging coupling reactions.16c

Oxidation reactions of olefins to epoxides and alcohols to aldehydes are fundamental reactions to produce numerous fine chemicals.17 While many homogenous catalysts including metal salts have been developed,18 their efficiency, stability and recyclability are the major challenges.19 Therefore, significant efforts have been devoted to develop heterogeneous catalysts to overcome such drawbacks.20 In this context, CPs containing catalytically active transition metals provide better options owing to their high surface area, porosity, stable architectures, and particularly recyclability.21 Recently, a few copper-based CPs have been utilized as heterogeneous catalysts for olefin-epoxidation and benzyl alcohol oxidation reactions, thus adequately justifying the role of Cu(ii) ions in promoting oxidation reactions.22 In this work, we present the synthesis and characterization of two Cu(ii)-based CPs, 1-Cu and 2-Cu, and their role as heterogeneous catalysts for the oxidation of a variety of olefins and assorted benzyl alcohols.

Experimental section

Materials and reagents

All commercially available chemicals and reagents were used without further purification unless otherwise stated. Standard methods were used to purify organic solvents. Metalloligands Et4N[Co(L4COOH)2] (1) and Et4N[Co(L3COOH)2] (2) were synthesized according to our earlier report.23

Syntheses

[{(1)Cu2.5(μ4–H2O)0.625(H2O)2.5}·(H2O)8]n (1-Cu)

Metalloligand 1 (100 mg, 0.100 mmol) was dissolved in 10 ml MeOH and a 5 ml MeOH solution of Cu(OAc)2·H2O (50 mg, 0.25 mmol) was added to it under magnetic stirring. The mixing of two solutions caused an immediate precipitation; however, the reaction mixture was further stirred for 2 h to ensure complete precipitation. The precipitate was filtered and dissolved in 2 ml water and layered with tert-butanol. A pale green coloured crystalline material resulted after a period of 6–7 d, which was filtered and dried under vacuum. Yield: 208 mg (83%). C42H64.25CoCu2.5N6O23.12 (1223.13): calcd C, 41.24; H, 3.71; N, 6.78; found C, 41.59; H, 4.04; N, 6.79. FTIR spectrum (Zn–Se ATR, selected peaks): (ν/cm−1): 3401 (OH), 1573, 1565 (CO).

[(2)Cu2(μ2-OH)(H2O)6·{Cu(H2O)6}·2H2O]n (2-Cu)

This compound was synthesized by layering an aqueous solution (3 ml) of Cu(OAc)2·H2O (50 mg, 0.25 mmol) over a solution of metalloligand 2 (100 mg, 0.100 mmol) dissolved in DMSO (5 ml) with an intermediate layer of tert-butanol. A green coloured crystalline product resulted at the interface of two solutions within 4–5 d that was filtered, washed with diethyl ether and dried under vacuum. Yield: 215 mg (88%). C42H71CoCu3N6O27 (1321.45): calcd C, 38.18; H, 3.89; N, 6.36; found C, 38.24; H, 3.75; N, 6.39. FTIR spectrum (Zn–Se ATR, selected peaks): (ν/cm−1): 3387 (OH), 1593, 1556 (CO).

Physical measurements

Elemental analysis data were obtained with an Elementar Analysen Systeme GmbH Vario EL-III instrument. FTIR spectra were recorded with a PerkinElmer Spectrum-Two spectrometer having Zn–Se ATR. NMR spectral measurements were carried out with a Jeol 400 MHz spectrometer. PerkinElmer Clarus 580 and Shimadzu QP 2010 instruments were respectively used for gas chromatography (GC) and GC-MS studies having an RTX-5SIL-MS column. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) and differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) were performed with DTG 60 Shimadzu and TA-DSC Q200 instruments, respectively, under a nitrogen atmosphere. X-ray powder diffraction studies were performed either with an X'Pert Pro from PANalytical or a Bruker AXS D8 Discover instrument (Cu-Kα radiation, λ = 1.54184 Å). The samples were ground and subjected to the range of θ = 5–50° with a slow scan rate at room temperature. The experimental X-ray powder diffraction patterns both for 1-Cu and 2-Cu were fitted to Le Bail refinement using TOPAS3 software with lattice parameters generated from the single crystal X-ray diffraction data.24

Crystallography

Single crystal X-ray diffraction data collection for 1-Cu was done on an Oxford Xcalibur CCD diffractometer. The crystals used were coated with paratone oil. The data collection, cell refinement and reduction were done by CrysAlisPro, ver. 1.171.33.49b.25 The structure was solved by direct methods using SIR-200426 and refined by using the SHELXL program27a in Olex227b in the space group I4/m in a tetragonal lattice system. All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. All hydrogen atoms were located from the Fourier map. However, in the solid-state structure, most of the lattice water molecules were highly disordered, so those oxygen atoms were modelled and refined with partial occupancy. The refinement also showed large solvent accessible voids of total potential solvent area volume of 2140 Å3 with the total volume per unit cell of 6261 Å3. For 1-Cu, about 34% (2140 Å3) of the unit cell amounts to solvent accessible voids which can host the remaining water molecules. The solvent accessible voids were located at two positions 0(x) 0(y) 0.5(z) of 1070 Å3 and 0.5(x) 0.5(y) 0(z) of 1070 Å3, respectively. As there was a large amount of diffused electron density (all less than 1.0 e Å3, however) the model was SQUEEZED to remove any diffused solvent by using the programme PLATON SQUEEZE.28 The latter improved R = 0.0688 for 2954 (observed) and R = 0.0756 for 29 481 reflections with 196 parameters and no restraints. The fact that the residual electron density was less than 1 eÅ3 signifies that this large amount of diffused electron density may be due to a large number of water molecules present only in the voids. The final model being submitted here is the SQUEEZED one. The present structure solution is the best model with the available data for poorly diffracting crystals of 1-Cu. Details of the crystallographic data collection and structure solution parameters are provided in Table S1 (ESI†). The network topology of 1-Cu was calculated by ToposPro 5.1.0.7 using the cluster simplification method.29

General procedure for the catalytic reaction

In a typical epoxidation reaction, an olefin was treated with isobutyraldehyde in 1 : 3 ratio under an O2 atmosphere in the presence of 2 mol% of a catalyst (1-Cu or 2-Cu). Thin layer chromatography (TLC) and/or gas chromatography (GC) techniques were used to ascertain the progress of the reaction. After completion, the reaction was quenched by the addition of 2 ml of ethyl acetate which resulted in complete separation of the catalyst which was filtered off and dried under vacuum. The ethyl acetate layer was washed with water (×2 times) and dried over anhyd. Na2SO4. The solvent was evaporated to isolate the organic product(s). Using flash column chromatography on silica gel with 5% EtOAc/hexanes as the eluent, the crude organic product(s) were purified and analyzed by the GC/GC-MS techniques in all cases and 1H NMR spectroscopy in a few selected cases.

Results and discussion

Synthesis and characterization of 1-Cu and 2-Cu

Green-coloured crystalline 1-Cu and 2-Cu were respectively synthesized by the reaction of metalloligands 1 and 2 with Cu(OAc)2. Both CPs show νO–H stretches at 3380–3400 cm−1 due to the presence of coordinated and lattice water molecules (Fig. S1 and S2, ESI†).15c,d,30 Amidic νco and arylcarboxylate νcoo stretches were noted as strong bands between 1557–1573 cm−1.15c,d,30 For both CPs, TGA confirmed the presence of ligated and lattice water molecules by displaying weight loss between 30 and 160 °C (Fig. S3 and S4, ESI†). For 1-Cu, the observed weight change of 18.04% (calcd 18.51%) corresponds to three coordinated and eight lattice water molecules. A similar thermal change was noted for 2-Cu for the loss of one bridging hydroxide and six coordinated and eight lattice water molecules (Obs. 20.41%; calcd 20.37%). DSC studies further strengthen the TGA observations by displaying exothermic features for the loss of ligated and lattice water molecules. Diffuse-reflectance absorption spectra for both CPs display broad features at ca. 450 and ca. 715 nm (Fig. S5 and S6, ESI†). The latter spectral band is assigned to the d–d transition based on the Cu(ii) ion.31 The broadness of the peak at ca. 715 nm is also due to the presence of the d–d transition of Co(iii) ions of a metalloligand.23,32

X-ray powder diffraction (XRPD) patterns confirmed the crystalline homogeneity and single crystalline phase of the bulk products (Fig. S7A and S8A, ESI†). In both cases, experimental XRPD patterns were fitted to Le Bail refinement24 with lattice parameters generated from the single crystal X-ray diffraction data and such an exercise provided good match (Fig. S7B and S8B, ESI†). Crystals of 2-Cu diffracted poorly and therefore its crystal structure could not be obtained. However, its XRPD pattern closely matched with that of our earlier Ni-based CP using the identical metalloligand (Fig. S8, ESI†).16c Such a comparison allowed us to tentatively suggest its composition as follows: [(2)Cu2(μ2-OH)(H2O)6·{Cu(H2O)6}], where [Cu(H2O)6]2+ balances the overall 2− charge on the polymer.16c

Crystal structure of 1-Cu

1-Cu was structurally characterized and the crystal structure is shown in Fig. 1. 1-Cu crystallized in a tetragonal cell with I4/m space group. The asymmetric unit consisted of 1/4 of a metalloligand, 1/2 Cu(ii) ion, 1/4 of a bridging water molecule, one coordinated water molecule, and two lattice water molecules (Fig. 1a). Every metalloligand provides one negative charge whereas all four deprotonated carboxylate groups contribute four negative charges, therefore 2.5 Cu(ii) ions balance the 5− charge with the following network composition: [{(1)Cu2.5(μ4–H2O)0.625(H2O)2.5}]n. This formula was established by a combination of electron diffraction density, TGA, and elemental analyses.33

In a metalloligand, a Co3+ ion is coordinated by four Namide groups in a distorted basal plane whereas two Npyridine atoms occupy the axial positions maintaining a compressed octahedral geometry (Fig. 1b).16,34 Such a structural feature has been noted in our earlier metalloligands and metalloligand-based CPs.16,34 The Co–Namide bond distance (1.944) was noted to be longer than the Co–Npyridine distance (1.833), as observed earlier.16,34

A metalloligand offers four arylcarboxylate fragments that coordinate to a group of four Cu(ii) ions in different directions, thus generating a 3D network (Fig. 1b–d). Such four Cu(ii) ions are bridged by a μ4-aqua group. Any Cu(ii) ion displays a unique eight-coordinated geometry where six sites come from Ocarboxylate from different metalloligands, one from a μ4-bridging water molecule and one from a terminal water molecule (Fig. 1c). Therefore, the resulting secondary building units (SBUs) have the following composition: [Cu4(μ4-H2O)(RCOO)8]. Such SBUs functioned as 8-connected nodes that were further connected to metalloligands, acting as four-connected nodes.15b,16c A combination of SBUs and metalloligands generates a 3D network with a flu topology and point symbol {412·612·84}{46}2 (Fig. 1f).351-Cu exhibited pores of 11.59 × 16.78 Å2 dimensions throughout the 3D network (Fig. 1e). Such pores were connected to form channels occupying lattice water molecules. We believe that such a structural feature should benefit substrate accessibility during the heterogeneous catalysis.

Exchange studies

The crystal structure of 1-Cu exhibited the presence of coordinated water molecules. In order to evaluate their possible displacement and/or exchange by a potential substrate or a reagent, we carried out a few exchange experiments.36 For the solvent exchange experiment, 1-Cu was heated up to 80 °C under vacuum for 6 h in order to remove both coordinated and lattice water molecules. The desolvated 1-Cu was allowed to equilibrate in a sealed environment containing D2O vapours. This exercise resulted in exchange of both coordinated and lattice H2O molecules by D2O which was evident from the observation of νO–D stretches at 2500 cm−1 (Fig. S9, ESI†). A similar experiment involving MeOH vapours resulted in the exchange of water molecules with MeOH. For such a sample, the νc–o (potentially ligated MeOH) stretch was noticed at 1020 cm−1 in the FTIR spectrum (Fig. S10, ESI†).

Iodine uptake study

To evaluate the possible inclusion abilities of the present CPs, vapour uptake of molecular iodine was investigated using 1-Cu as a representative case. For such an experiment, 20 mg sample of desolvated 1-Cu was allowed to equilibrate in a sealed tube containing molecular iodine. The original green colour of 1-Cu was changed to dark brown suggesting the inclusion of molecular iodine within the pores of 1-Cu (Fig. S11, ESI†). The net weight gain was 1.9 mg corresponding to 9.5% weight change. The inclusion of molecular iodine was found to be reversible and application of either vacuum at ambient temperature or suspending 1-Cu in any solvent was found to release I2 in a time-dependent manner. Such a fact suggests the stable nature of CPs in accommodating and releasing molecular iodine from their porous structure.

The presence of secondary Cu(ii) ions in two CPs suggested their possible redox involvement. For such a purpose, both 1-Cu and 2-Cu were suspended in a MeOH solution of KI. Notably, both 1-Cu and 2-Cu were able to oxidize the iodide ion to molecular iodine which further reacted with the free iodide ion to form the triiodide ion. The formation of the triiodide ion was confirmed by the observation of signature peaks at ca. 450, 360 and 290 nm in the UV-visible spectrum recorded in MeOH (Fig. S12, ESI†). The generation of the triiodide ion infers that the secondary Cu(ii) ions in the CPs were reduced to the Cu(i) state. Therefore, the EPR spectrum of 1-Cu was recorded before and after the addition of KI to gain insight into the mechanism. The solid-state powder EPR spectrum of 1-Cu displayed tetragonal features with g⊥ = 2.08 and g∥ = 2.34 (Fig. S13, ESI†).31 Such spectral features justify the solid-state crystal structure. Interestingly, these spectral features disappeared after the reaction with the iodide ion, thus supporting the reduction of the Cu(ii) state to the Cu(i) state. As a reference, Co3+-based metalloligands do not react with the iodide ion. Importantly, the in situ generated Cu(i) state is re-oxidized to the Cu(ii) form in open air, most probably due to the molecular oxygen. The facile redox conversion between the Cu2+–Cu+ states suggests the potential role of the present CPs in redox-based catalytic reactions. Therefore, epoxidation of olefins and oxidation of benzyl alcohols were investigated.

Catalytic applications of 1-Cu and 2-Cu

The crystal structure of 1-Cu exhibited the presence of pores and channels whereas exchange and I2 uptake studies not only inferred the possible replacement of the metal-bound water molecules but also inclusion abilities. Such features together with facile Cu2+–Cu+ conversion created potential catalytic opportunities.16,22

Epoxidation reactions

Oxidation of olefins is an industrially and commercially important reaction to obtain various fine chemicals.17 In this context, epoxidation is a significant reaction in organic chemistry as an epoxide can lead to a diverse range of ring-opened products.37 Because of its low cost and environmentally friendly nature, epoxidation of olefins using molecular oxygen as an oxidant and an aldehyde as a co-reactant is an attractive option.38 Both 1-Cu and 2-Cu were tested for the epoxidation reactions. Initially, several control experiments were carried out to optimize the reaction conditions to obtain the desirable product in high yield while minimizing the formation of undesirable by-products (Table 1). Several oxidizing agents such as H2O2, TBHP (in water), TBHP (in decane), PhIO, N-methyl-morpholine-N-oxide, meta-chloroperbenzoic acid, NaOCl and O2/isobutyraldehyde were screened with styrene as the model substrate and 1-Cu as a representative catalyst in order to find out the best oxidising agent (entries 1–7). Notably, only molecular O2 with isobutyraldehyde resulted in high epoxidation with a small amount of benzaldehyde as the by-product. Various solvents were screened to evaluate their possible effect on the epoxidation reaction (entries 8–12). Importantly, with most of the solvents, the amount of benzaldehyde as the by-product was quite high. More importantly, solvent-free reaction conditions provided the best results with a minimum amount of benzaldehyde (entry 7). We also attempted to understand the role of a few additives in the epoxidation reaction.23,39 The use of NaOAc, pyridine, t-BuOH and morpholine as the additives did not improve the epoxidation of styrene (entries 13–16). A variation in catalyst loading suggests that increasing the amount of 1-Cu increased the epoxidation yield while the best results were obtained with 2 mol% catalyst load (entries 17–20; Fig. S14, ESI†). Epoxidation reactions were also attempted at different temperatures. Quantitative conversion (epoxide: 90%; benzaldehyde: 10%) was observed at 50 °C whereas both lower and higher temperatures resulted in either a lower product yield or higher yield of benzaldehyde (entries 21–25). The use of a few metal salts (CuCl, CuCl2, and Cu(OAc)2) as the catalysts did not result in any epoxidation of styrene (entries 26–28). Such a fact clearly establishes the critical role played by the copper-based CPs in promoting the epoxidation reactions.

These control and optimization experiments allowed the epoxidation reaction of assorted substrates to be explored and the results are presented in Tables 2–4. In all cases, an olefin was allowed to react with isobutyraldehyde in a 1 : 3 ratio in the dioxygen environment with only 2 mol% of a catalyst (1-Cu and 2-Cu) at 50 °C under solvent-free conditions. Under these conditions, a smooth reaction took place producing a high yield of the respective epoxide along with a small amount of the corresponding aldehyde (Table 2). The presence of an electron-donating and/or electron-withdrawing substituent at the para-position of styrene did not result in a significant difference in the epoxidation and both CPs were equally effective; producing 79–90% of styrene-epoxide and 10–21% of the corresponding aldehyde (entries 1–6). Interestingly, when sterically hindered substrates were used, the product yield decreased due to the limited accessibility of a substrate through the pores and channels of the CPs (entries 7–9). In fact, an interesting trend was noticed between the substrate size and epoxidation yield: 1-vinylnaphthalene (7.00 × 8.81 Å2; 62%), 2-vinylnapthalene (7.00 × 10.37 Å2; 67%) and 9-vinylanthracene (8.42 × 10.34 Å2; 53%).40 Such a trend supports the effect of substrate size on the epoxidation reaction.15d Although epoxidation of both cis-stilbene and trans-stilbene was comparatively poor (entries 10 and 11), benzaldehyde was produced only in a trace amount.15d

Both CPs, 1-Cu and 2-Cu, were further used for the epoxidation of assorted cyclic olefins (Table 3).15d In all cases, good to excellent yields of the desirable epoxide (E) were noted whereas the amount of keto product (K-1) was limited (entries 1–5). In the case of cyclohexene, the respective dione (K-2) was also obtained as the by-product in addition to the keto product (K-1) (entry 2). Importantly, 1-methylcyclohexene showed high epoxide formation among the series, possibly due to the +I effect of the methyl group.41

Both CPs, 1-Cu and 2-Cu, were also effective in promoting the epoxidation of long-chain terminal alkenes (entries 1–4, Table 4). Both CPs were successfully able to oxidize 1-hexene (74–78%), 1-heptene (62–67%), 1-nonene (67–73%), and 1-decene (68–73%) effortlessly. Notably, oxidative product conversion (epoxide + ketone) of various terminal alkenes was nearly quantitative in most cases.15d

Benzyl alcohol oxidation reactions

After successful epoxidation reactions, the catalytic activities of both 1-Cu and 2-Cu were further extended to the oxidation of various benzyl alcohols.42 At first, control experiments were conducted taking benzyl alcohol as a model substrate along with 1-Cu as a representative catalyst to optimize the reaction conditions (Table 5). These experiments allowed us to select tert-butyl hydroperoxide (TBHP) as the best oxidant (entries 1–3). It was also noted that the substrate oxidation depended upon the amount of TBHP being used in the catalytic reaction (Fig S15, ESI†). Investigation of catalyst loading illustrates the best results with 2 mol% load whereas a decreased amount of catalyst hampered the oxidation (entries 4–7). For the oxidation of benzyl alcohol, solvent-free reaction conditions showed the best results when compared with those in different solvents (H2O, MeOH, CH3CN, EtOAc, EtOH and DMF; entries 8–13). Additional experiments suggested the requirement of a mild elevated temperature of 50 °C for the best oxidation (entries 14–17), whereas the use of Cu(OAc)2, CuCl and CuCl2 as the catalysts produced negligible benzaldehyde (entries 18–20). Such a fact necessitates the role of a well-defined catalyst in promoting the oxidation reaction.

To expand the scope of catalysis, oxidation of various substituted benzyl alcohols and related substrates was performed using both CPs (Table 6). These alcohols were oxidized to their corresponding aldehydes conveniently within 6 h. Both CPs were equally effective and resulted in high conversion. For example, benzyl alcohol and assorted para-substituted benzyl alcohols produced the corresponding aldehydes in nearly similar yields (entries 1–6). However, oxidation was slightly better for smaller substrates when compared to the bulkier substrates (entries 7–9). Notably, a similar observation was noted for the epoxidation reactions of bulkier substrates. Collectively, these results point towards the substrate accessibility as a limiting factor.43

Leaching experiment

It is important to substantiate that the secondary Cu(ii) ions are not leached out from the present CPs during the course of the catalytic reaction; therefore, a hot-filtration leaching test was executed.15d,44 For the oxidation of benzyl alcohol using TBHP, 1-Cu was selected as a representative catalyst to perform this test (Fig. 2).42 During the course of catalytic reaction (black dots), 1-Cu was filtered off after 3 h and the reaction was further allowed to proceed (red dots). As can be seen from Fig. 2, removal of 1-Cu resulted in nearly termination of oxidation as was monitored till 10 h. Importantly, re-addition of 1-Cu to the same reaction mixture at 5 h immediately caused significant oxidation of benzyl alcohol to benzaldehyde (green triangles). This experiment unambiguously infers that the catalytically active species are not leached out from the present CPs and they act as true heterogeneous catalysts.

Further support came from the inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS) measurements of hot filtrates from the catalytic reactions.22a–c The copper species leached into the solution was noted to be only 1.1 and 1.7% during the epoxidation and benzyl alcohol oxidation reactions, respectively, using 1-Cu as the representative catalyst. On the other hand, negligible leaching of copper (0.2%) was noted when 1-Cu was suspended in DMF for 48 h. These experiments indicate the stable nature of the present CPs during the heterogeneous oxidation reactions.

Recyclability experiment

Taking advantage of the heterogeneous nature of catalysis, both CPs can be easily recovered from the reaction mixture by simple filtration. Such a fact provided the opportunity to check their recyclability. Taking 1-Cu as a representative catalyst, recyclability was checked for five consecutive cycles with no apparent loss of catalytic activity for the oxidation of benzyl alcohol using TBHP (Fig. 3). In addition, convenient separation of CPs from the reaction mixture allowed characterization of them and comparison of them with pristine samples. The FTIR spectrum (Fig. S16, ESI†) and powder XRD pattern (Fig. S17, ESI†) of the recovered 1-Cu were found to closely match those of the pristine sample. Such a fact confirms that both the structural integrity and crystallinity of a CP are retained during the catalytic reaction.

Mechanistic insight

Because of the fact that the use of both Cu(i) and Cu(ii) salts only resulted in inadequate oxidation of styrene to styrene oxide and benzyl alcohol to benzaldehyde, the catalytic role of the present CPs is clearly visible. A few additional experiments were performed to investigate the possible steps involved in the oxidation of benzyl alcohol with TBHP in the presence of 1-Cu as a representative catalyst. To test the stability, 1-Cu was refluxed in benzyl alcohol and in TBHP for 3 h. In both cases, the FTIR spectrum of the recovered sample did not reveal any noticeable difference from that of pristine 1-Cu. Such an observation suggests that 1-Cu was un-reactive towards both these reagents separately and only acted as a catalyst for a reaction between them. It may further be noted that no product formation was observed in the absence of TBHP, thus ruling out auto-oxidation during the course of the catalytic reaction. These experiments point towards the direct role of Cu-based CPs in promoting the oxidation reactions.

Conclusions

In this work, we have shown the synthesis and characterization of two Cu(ii)-based coordination polymers which were synthesized by using two unique cobalt-based metalloligands decorated with appended arylcarboxylate groups. CP 1-Cu was structurally characterised and it showed a porous 3D architecture due to the coordination of secondary Cu(ii) ions to the appended carboxylate groups from metalloligands. Both CPs, 1-Cu and 2-Cu, acted as heterogeneous catalysts for the epoxidation of various olefins using O2 and employing isobutyraldehyde as the co-reductor and for the peroxide-mediated oxidation of assorted benzyl alcohols under solvent-free conditions. Reusability and leaching experiments supported the stable and true heterogeneous nature of catalysis, thus enhancing the significance of the present copper-based coordination polymers in oxidation catalysis.
