With the vigorous development of science, technology, and industrialization, lanthanides1–3 are widely used in optoelectronic materials,4 superconducting materials,5 energy materials,6 and permanent magnetic materials.7 However, it is very difficult to achieve industrialized production of single lanthanides because of their chemical and physical similarities.8–10 At present, lanthanide separation is commonly carried out via fractional step method,11 ion exchange,12 solvent extraction,13 molten salt electrolysis,14 hierarchical crystallization, and stratified precipitation.15 However, the fractional step and ion exchange methods are unsuitable for mass production due to long cycles and high consumption of resources. Solvent extraction and molten salt electrolysis cannot be a preference for industrial manufacture due to their low selectivity and severe pollution. The early crystallization separation, such as hierarchical crystallization and stratified precipitation, often results in the loss of resources due to the moderate difference in the solubility of oxalic acid salt and carbonate. Thus, there is an urgent need for an approach with simple operation and high selectivity and performance for lanthanide separation.

Recently Bu et al.16 introduced the concept of selective crystallization of Ln3+ ions into MOFs, where a mixed d-camphoric and formic ligands were used to selectively crystallize specific lanthanide ions into MOFs; this led to two categories in the MOF structure, strongly dependent on the size of Ln3+ ions. Inspired by this, we successfully obtained 13 Ln-MOFs, which could be crystallographically divided into four types of ligands, by selecting olsalazine as an organic ligand, creating a more accurate lanthanide separation via size-selective crystallization of Ln-MOFs. To clearly distinguish them, these 13 Ln-MOFs were classified into four types: I (Ln = La, Ce, and Pr), II (Ln = Nd, Sm, and Eu), III (Ln = Gd, Tb, Dy, and Ho), and IV (Ln = Er, Tm, and Lu) (Fig. 1).

The crystals were synthesized by lanthanide nitrate and olsalazine sodium (Na2H2OLZ) with a ratio of 1 : 1.5 in a DMF/H2O mixed solvent at 115 °C for three days (see the ESI† for details of the synthesis).

The crystals of type I were determined by single X-ray diffraction, crystallizing in the monoclinic C2/c space group with the general formula of Ln2(OLZ) (H2OLZ)(DMF)(H2O)2·2H2O (OLZ4− = olsalazine). Type II crystallized in the monoclinic P2/c space group with the general formula of Ln(OLZ)0.5(H2OLZ)0.5(DMF)(μ2-DMF)0.5. Type III belongs to the monoclinic P21/c space group with the general formula of Ln(OLZ)0.5(H2OLZ)0.5(DMF)(H2O)·H2O, and type IV crystallized in the C2/c monoclinic space group with the general formula of Ln2(OLZ)(H2OLZ)(H2O)4. The phase purity of these bulk samples was confirmed by powder X-ray diffraction (Fig. S1–S4, ESI†), and their thermal stability was evaluated by thermogravimetric analysis (Fig. S1–S4, ESI†). As shown in Fig. 1, the asymmetrical unit of I and III contained the same component including one Ln(iii) center, one OLZ4− anion, one partly protonated H2OLZ2− ligand, one coordinated DMF, and one coordinated water molecule. However, the coordination geometry of Ln ion was different, with I showing a nine-coordinated tricapped triangular prism and III having an eight-coordinated bicapped triangular prism. Different from I and III, the asymmetrical unit of II contained one Ln(iii) center, one OLZ4− anion, one partly protonated H2OLZ2− ligand, one terminally coordinated DMF molecule, and one bridging coordinated DMF molecule. The asymmetrical unit of IV contained one Ln(iii) center, one OLZ4− anion, one partly protonated H2OLZ2− ligand, and two terminally coordinated water molecules. Both II and IV afforded eight-coordinated bicapped triangular prisms. The organic ligands in type II–IV adopted two types of coordination modes: one showing complete deprotonation with μ4:η1:η2:η2:η1:η2:η1 mode and one showing complete protonation on the hydroxyl group with μ4:η0:η1:η1:η0:η1:η1 mode (Fig. S5, ESI†). For type I, except for the μ4:η0:η1:η1:η0:η1:η1 mode of the H2OLZ2− ligand, the OLZ4− ligands displayed the μ6:η2:η2:η2:η2:η2:η1 mode. In light of this, adjacent Ln(iii) ions were connected together by OLZ4− to generate 2D layers, which were further pillared by H2OLZ2− ligands to form a 3D framework (Fig. 2). We could find that these 3D networks showed various appearances dependent on the size of Ln(iii) ions. To clearly present the data, we selected the smallest quadrangular matrix to provide a representation. Type I afforded a 14.2 Å × 13.3 Å matrix with ca. 45.7° intersection angle, and the corresponding values were 15.5 Å × 14.6 Å/81.5° for II, 15.0 Å × 13.8 Å/39.6° for III, and 14.9 Å × 13.9 Å/51° for IV.

In light of the abovementioned size-selective crystallization of Ln-MOFs, we speculated that mixing different Ln3+ ions together and certain Ln3+ ions would be preferred for nucleation, crystallization, and precipitation. Thus, we conducted a series of experiments to explore whether Ln3+ could achieve the separation process. First, we carried out a preliminary separation between two lanthanide ions such as La and Nd ions (a mixture with one from type I and one from type II), and a 1 : 1 ratio of La/Nd was used to grow MOF by a similar solvothermal method (see details in the ESI†). The results showed that the smaller Ln3+ ion was preferred for crystallization with the Nd phase, belonging to type II, as it gave only one product, which was confirmed by single crystal X-ray diffraction, XRD, and energy dispersive spectrometry (EDS). Similarly, further experiments on Nd + Dy and Dy + Tm mixtures were carried out, also giving one product for the smaller Ln3+ ion (Table 1, Fig. S6–S8, ESI†). The abovementioned results indicated that in this system, the smaller Ln3+ more easily formed a more stable MOF, whereas the larger ion was even unable to form nuclei in the solution phase in the beginning itself.

Furthermore, to prove that size-selective crystallization of Ln-MOFs could be applied to a more complicated system, we carried out a complicated separation among three lanthanide ions, such as a La–Nd–Dy, mixture and four lanthanide ions, such as a La–Nd–Dy–Tm, mixture. The single crystal X-ray diffraction, XRD, and EDS still supported the generation of the product for the smaller Ln3+ ion (Table 1, Fig. S9–S11, ESI†).

In conclusion, through solvothermal synthesis, we determined that olsalazine ligands were capable of providing a fine discrimination towards Ln3+ ions due to their slight difference in ionic radii, consequently leading to the generation of four types of Ln-MOFs. The results showed that this size-selective crystallization of Ln-MOFs was able to provide a 100% separation for smaller Ln3+ ions, suggesting an extremely effective method for lanthanide separation.
