Introduction

Metal nanoclusters (NC) can easily serve as nanosized building blocks with other specific functional motifs for assembly into periodic structures in crystalline materials, which could find applications in various fields including chemical sensing, catalysis, and gas separation.1–29 Roy and co-workers17 used electron-donating Co6E8 (E = Se or Te) and an electron-accepting Fe8O4 cluster as building blocks to create diverse, solid-state materials with hierarchical structures driven by charge transfer between clusters and subsequent intercluster electrostatic attraction. Zheng and co-workers19 used Cd4-TC4A (H4TC4A = p-tert-butylthiacalix[4]arene) as second building units (SBUs) for assembly, and obtained a Cd24 cluster with enhanced photocatalytic activity. Coordination assembly of coinage metal clusters into rigid framework materials using bi/multi-dentate linkers is promising for generating functional materials for luminescent sensing and photocatalysis, in which the structure of the linker considerably affects the properties of the metal cluster-assembled materials.30–40

Cucurbit[n]urils (CB[n]) are a classic type of supramolecular synthon, which consists of a constricted hydrophobic cavity and two identical, polar carbonyl fringe portals that can bind meticulously tailored guests to form stable host–guest complexation.41–54 When the linear linkers for assembling metal clusters are combined with CB[n], the new structure and novel physical or chemical properties are generated.

Here the bidentate CB[6]-N,N′-hexamethylene-bis(pyrazinyl hexafluorophosphate) linkers55,56 [abbreviated as L·PF6 (BPHF@CB[6])] were explored in the assembly of silver(i) clusters into crystalline framework structures, and we obtained three 1D silver cluster-assembled materials (SCMs), that are: [Ag12(SC7H7)6(CH3SO3)8(L·PF6)]n (SCM 1), [Ag12(SC7H7)6(CF3COO)8(DMF)3(CH3OH)1(L·PF6)]n (SCM 2), and [Ag12(iPrS)6(CH3SO3)8(L·PF6)]n (SCM 3). Their crystal structures and optical properties were studied systematically.

Experimental

Materials and measurements

The [PhCH2SAg]n was prepared by the reaction of equal molar equivalents of AgNO3 and PhCH2SH in acetonitrile (CH3CN). The [iPrSAg]n, CB[6], N,N′-hexamethylene-bis(pyrazinyl bromide) (BPHB) and BPHF@CB[6] (L·PF6) were prepared according to methods taken from the literature.49,55,56 Other reagents and solvents were obtained from commercial sources and used without further purification. The powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns of the samples were measured on a PANalytical X'Pert Pro X-ray diffractometer (Cu Kα) at ambient conditions. The UV/Vis diffuse-reflectance spectra were obtained on a Hitachi U-2000 spectrophotometer. The 1H-NMR spectroscopy was performed on a Bruker 400 MHz FT-NMR spectrometer operating at 400 MHz with (CD3)2SO as the solvent. Fourier transform infrared (FT-IR) spectra were obtained using a Bruker ALPHA II spectrometer. The luminescence spectra were obtained on a Horiba Scientific Fluorolog-3 spectrofluorometer. The emission spectra of NC a and SCM 1–3 in the solid state were measured under the same conditions: slit width, 4.5 nm; light source, 360 nm excitation; test temperature, 298 K. Variable-temperature steady-state emission spectra of solid state specimens were measured using an East Changing Technology TC 202 temperature controller after each sample was evacuated for 1 h using a Value VRD-16 vacuum pump. Luminescence decay and time-resolved emission spectroscopy (TRES) curves were obtained using a Horiba Scientific Fluorolog-3 spectrofluorometer equipped with a 370 nm or a 403 nm laser, and measuring was conducted in time-correlated single photon counting mode (TCSPC) with a resolution time of 200 ps. Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were measured on a TA Instruments SDT 2960 thermal analyser, from room temperature to 800 °C at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 under a nitrogen atmosphere. The electrospray ionisation mass spectrometry (ESI-MS) spectrum of the ligand was measured on an AB Sciex X500R Q-TOF spectrometer in positive mode.

X-ray crystallography

Single crystals with appropriate dimensions were analysed on a Rigaku XtaLAB Pro diffractometer with Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5418 Å). Data collection and reduction were handled using the Rigaku CrysAlis Pro software,57 and multi-scan absorption corrections were applied to the data. All the structures were solved using direct methods (SHELXS)58 and refined by full-matrix least squares on F2 using Olex2,59 which took advantage of the SHELXL-2015 module.60 All the atoms were refined anisotropically. The hydrogen atoms were placed at the calculated positions, and refined using idealized geometries and assigned fixed isotropic displacement parameters. The structure refinement was handled with different strategies on the basis of the electron density distribution and the complexity of the disorder. The detailed information of the crystal data, data collection and refinement results for all the compounds are summarized in Tables S1 and S2 (ESI).†

Preparation of Ag12(SC7H7)6(CH3SO3)6(C5H5N)6 (NC a) and SCM 1–3

NC a

[PhCH2SAg]n (5 mg, 0.022 mmol) and CH3SO3Ag (4 mg, 0.020 mmol) were dissolved in 3 mL of a mixed solution (CH3CN : toluene = 1 : 1) under stirring and a clear solution was obtained. Next, 10 μL of pyridine was added to the solution and stirred at room temperature for 5 min. The resultant colourless solution was allowed to evaporate slowly in darkness at room temperature for 2 d and colourless hexagonal crystals were produced. The crystals were filtered off, washed with ether and dried at room temperature. The yield was 35% based on Ag. The elemental analysis calculation (%) for C78H90Ag12N6O18S12 (3268.97, NC a after vacuum treatment for 1 h) was C 30.43, H 2.95, N 2.73; the measured results were C 30.55, H 2.92, N 2.67.

[Ag12(SC7H7)6(CH3SO3)8(L·PF6)]n (SCM 1)

[PhCH2SAg]n (5 mg, 0.022 mmol) and CH3SO3Ag (4 mg, 0.020 mmol) were dissolved in 3 mL of a mixed solution (methanol : toluene = 1 : 1) under stirring and a clear solution was obtained. Next, the solution was carefully layered on 1 mL of DMF solution of L·PF6 ligand (8 mg) in a test tube. The test tube was sealed and left in darkness at room temperature for 2 d to produce colourless block crystals at the two-phase interface. The crystals were filtered off, washed with methanol and dried at room temperature. The yield was 20% based on Ag. The elemental analysis calculation (%) for C100H122N28S14Ag12O36 (4035.53, SCM 1 after vacuum treatment for 1 h) was C 29.76, H 3.05, N 9.72; the measured results were C 29.41, H 3.02, N 9.33.

[Ag12(SC7H7)6(CF3COO)8(DMF)3(CH3OH)1(L·PF6)]n (SCM 2)

[PhCH2SAg]n (5 mg, 0.022 mmol) and CF3COOAg (5 mg, 0.023 mmol) were dissolved in 3 mL of a mixed solution (methanol : toluene = 1 : 1) under stirring and a clear solution was obtained. Next, 1 mL of DMF solution of L·PF6 ligand (8 mg) was added to the solution and stirred at room temperature for 5 min. The resultant colourless solution was allowed to evaporate slowly in darkness at room temperature for 2 d to produce light-yellow coloured block crystals. The crystals were filtered off, washed with methanol and dried at room temperature. The yield was 25% based on Ag. The elemental analysis calculation (%) for C108H98N28F24S6Ag12O28 (4178.87, SCM 2 after vacuum treatment for 1 h) was C 31.04, H 2.36, N 9.38; the measured results were C 30.85, H 2.28, N 9.05.

[Ag12(iPrS)6(CH3SO3)8(L·PF6)]n (SCM 3)

[iPrSAg]n (9 mg, 0.05 mmol) and CH3SO3Ag (10 mg, 0.05 mmol) were dissolved in 2 mL of DMF solution under stirring and a clear solution was obtained. Next, 1 mL of DMF solution of L·PF6 ligand (8 mg) was added to the solution and stirred at room temperature for 5 min. The resultant colourless solution was allowed to evaporate slowly in darkness, at room temperature, for 1 d to produce pale yellow coloured block crystals. The crystals were filtered off, washed with ethanol and dried at room temperature. The yield was 32% based on Ag. The elemental analysis calculation (%) for C76H122N28S14Ag12O36 (3747.28, SCM 3 after vacuum treatment for 1 h) was C 24.36, H 3.28, N 10.47; the measured results were C 24.13, H 3.29, N 10.29.

Results and discussion

Synthesis and characterisation of pseudorotaxane

The synthesis method of pseudorotaxane BPHF@CB[6] (L·PF6), which had good solubility in DMF, was as follows. Firstly, the BPHBs were threaded through CB[6] in a water system and then an anion exchange with hexafluorophosphate, as a counter ion, was performed as shown in Fig. 1. The chemical composition of the isolated ligand was obtained using ESI-MS in positive mode. As shown in Fig. S1 (ESI),† the most intense peaks at m/z ∼620.5 Da and 1386.0 Da in the experimental isotope pattern were consistent with the species of [L·PF6-2PF6−]2+ (calculated: 620.7) and [L·PF6-PF6−]+ (calculated: 1385.9), respectively. As shown in the 1H-NMR spectrum (Fig. S2, ESI†), the signals of H1, H2 and H3 belonging to the methylene groups in L·PF6 were up-shifted by δ of 0.97, 0.99 and 0.22 ppm when compared to BPHB, which intimated that the alkyl chain of the BPHF was centred inside the CB[6] cavity due to the proton-shielding effect of the hollow cavity of CB[6]. Meanwhile, the H4, and H5 of pyrazine were down-shifted by δ of 0.12, 0.01 ppm, respectively, which implied that the pyrazine groups at both ends were exposed outside the cavity when compared to BPHB.

X-ray crystal structures and characterisation

In order to better explain the effect of pseudorotaxane-based linkers on the structure and performance of the crystal, isolated clusters NC a coordinated by pyridine were synthesized. Single-crystal X-ray diffraction (SCXRD) analysis showed that NC a crystallized in the monoclinic space group P21/n sharing similar molecular structures as found in previous reports16,30,36 with an empty Ag12S66+ cuboctahedron co-stabilized by six CH3SO3− anions and six pyridine ligands (Fig. S3, ESI†). The Ag12S6 core in NC a showed a disconnected D3d Ag3–Ag6–Ag3 three-layer arrangement (Fig. S4a, ESI†) and the six middle layer Ag(i) atoms each bear a terminal monodentate pyridine ligand to support the periphery of the Ag12S6 cluster.

Slow reaction between a clear solution containg [PhCH2SAg]n oligomers and CH3SO3Ag and L·PF6 ligands solution easily produced orange-red coloured fluorescent SCM 1 crystals in a moderate yield at room temperature. The SCXRD analysis revealed that the SCM 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P2/c. The core of SCM 1 was an empty cuboctahedron shape and consisted of twelve Ag atoms linked through AgI⋯AgI argentophilic interactions (the Ag⋯Ag distances were in the range of 2.883–3.373 Å). The cuboctahedron core displayed D3d symmetry and contained eight triangular faces: two triangles with Ag⋯Ag distances lying in the range of 2.883–2.953 Å on the top and bottom layers, and six lateral triangles with two short sides shared by a trapezoid capped by μ4-SCH2Ph (Fig. S4b, ESI†). The auxiliary CH3SO3− anionic ligands were bound to Ag atoms in the μ1-O and μ2-O,O′ modes. The Ag12S6 cores of SCM 1 were both connected by L·PF6 linkers through a terminal Ag–N bond in two asymmetric silver sites of the six middle layer Ag(i) atoms, resulting in a 1D zigzag structure (Fig. 2a).

The architecture of SCM 2 is shown in Fig. 2b, which crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/n and contained Ag12S6 clusters, which were bridged by pyrazine groups of the string molecule from L·PF6. The Ag12 core structure was a similar cubic octahedron through the AgI⋯AgI argentophilic interactions (Fig. S4c, ESI†). Two μ1-CF3COO− and six μ2-CF3COO− auxiliary ligands were attached to the Ag atoms. The nanoclusters were further extended into linear 1D chains by L·PF6 linkers in a symmetrical coordination of two silver atoms at the core waist. The SCM 3 also showed a linear 1D chain structure (Fig. 2c), in which the asymmetric unit contained an Ag12 core with two bidentate L·PF6 linkers, six μ4-SC3H8 bridging ligands, and eight CH3SO3− auxiliary ligands (Fig. S4d, ESI†).

The phase purity and chemical formulas of SCM 1–3 and NC a were further verified using PXRD (Fig. S5–S8, ESI†). The thermal stability of SCM 1 and SCM 3 were superior to that of NC a, but the thermal stability of SCM 2 was similar to that of NC a, which was confirmed by TGA and the results are presented in Fig. S9 (ESI).†

The infrared spectra of all the compounds were collected and are shown in Fig. S10 (ESI)† and the corresponding characteristic peaks of SCM 1–3 were assigned by comparison with L·PF6 and BPHB. A characteristic pyridine ring stretching band (∼1446 cm−1) was observed in the infrared (IR) spectrum of NC a. The IR spectra of SCM 1–3 show the expected pyrazine ring stretching ribbons at 1460 cm−1. The typical vibrations corresponding to the carbonyl groups of CB[6] are found in the regions of 1730 cm−1 (SCM 1), 1743 cm−1 (SCM 2) and 1730 cm−1 (SCM 3), respectively.

Optical properties

The UV/vis diffuse-reflectance spectroscopy was used to study the optical absorption in the solid state. As indicated in Fig. 3a, discrete clusters NC a consisting of an Ag12S6 core showed a broad absorption region between 220 nm and 370 nm. Upon connection of the discrete clusters with conjugated L·PF6 linkers, the SCM 1–3 exhibited a broader absorption region between 220 nm and 400 nm (Fig. 3a), which could be attributed to the charge transfer between the clusters and the conjugated L·PF6 linkers.31

The NC a displayed a faint yellow emission at 575 nm under ambient conditions (Fig. 3b). The decay measurement at 298 K was fitted to a single-exponential function, giving a lifetime value of τ = 112 ns, implying that the emission was a fluorescence excited state which was probably related to charge transfer from S 3p to Ag 5s perturbed by AgI⋯AgI interactions (Fig. S11, ESI†).31 The temperature-dependent luminescence spectra in Fig. S12 (ESI)† showed that as the temperature decreased from 298 to 83 K, the emission peaks showed a mild red shift from 575 nm to 580 nm, accompanied by the appearance of new peaks at 465 nm and 704 nm. The lifetime (Fig. S13, ESI†) of the three emission bands were calculated to be 166 μs (λem = 580 nm), 399 μs (λem = 465 nm), and 56.4 μs (λem = 704 nm) at 83 K, respectively, which indicated that the phosphorescence with triplet excited states replaced the luminescence at low temperatures.27,31

In contrast, the luminescent spectra (Fig. 3b) showed that the emission peaks of a series of 1D chains assembled from the Ag12 cluster with rigid pseudorotaxane precursors showed a red-shifted phenomenon. It was intriguing to observe that the fluorescence intensity of SCM 1 (QY = 4.88%) was dramatically increased compared to that NC a (QY = 0.22%) and that they displayed an intense red-orange emission at 630 nm and 656 nm, respectively, with a wide excitation range of 290–380 nm (Fig. 3b and Fig. S14, ESI†). The lifetime values of SCM 1 were found to be 2.00 μs (λem = 630 nm) and 2.10 μs (λem = 656 nm) (Fig. S15, ESI†). The non-radiative transition rate knr showed an extreme decrease from 8.33 × 106 s−1 to 4.76 × 105 s−1, and the radiative transition kr increased from 1.83 × 104 s−1 to 2.44 × 104 s−1, respectively (Table S3, ESI†). Therefore, the supramolecular host–guest assemblies formed between the BPHF linked to the silver core and the CB[6] improved the structural rigidity. Subsequently, the intramolecular vibration and rotation of BPHF were greatly suppressed, which promoted radiative transition and reduced the nonradiative relaxation of the excited states.51–53 As a result, the luminescence performance was predominantly increased. As the temperature decreased, the lower-energy (LE) emission intensity gradually increased together with an obvious red shift of the main emission peak (from 656 nm to 672 nm), whereas a higher-energy (HE) band centred at 630 nm gradually blue shifted to 585 nm (Fig. 4a). The LE emission (τ83 K = 91.3 μs) and HE emission (τ83 K = 92.9 μs) also exhibited a microsecond lifetime which is shown in Fig. S16 (ESI),† which indicated characteristics of phosphorescence, which possibly originated from the metal cluster centre and the cluster-to-ligand charge transition (CLCT) modified by the interactions between the metal core and the surface ligands.30,31,35

When the protecting ligands were changed to iPrS− ligands, there were tremendous changes in the coordination site of the L·PF6 linkers, and these resulted in the linear configuration of SCM 3. Its room temperature emission spectrum showed a main emission peak at 572 nm and a shoulder peak at 655 nm under excitation in the range of 290–380 nm (QY = 4.04%, Fig. 3b, Fig. S17, S18 and Table S3, ESI†). The lifetime values of SCM 3 were found to be 67.6 ns (λem = 572 nm) and 55.4 ns (λem = 655 nm) at 298 K (Fig. S19, ESI†). When the temperature decreased to 83 K from 298 K, the emission intensity of SCM 3 increased gradually, accompanied by an obvious red shift of the main emission peak from 572 nm to 583 nm as shown in Fig. 4c. Meanwhile, the lifetime values were found to sharply increase to 67.8 μs and 83.7 μs at 83 K corresponding to emission peaks of 583 nm and 655 nm, respectively (Fig. S20, ESI†).

The SCM 2 crystal emitted a broad and constant peak at 578 nm under optimal excitation at 350 nm and the emission peak showed a slight blue shift from 290 nm to 380 nm excitation (Fig. 3b and Fig. S21, ESI†). This shows the lowest QY of 0.18%, which was probably because of the use of the electron withdrawing CF3COO− group, and the non-radiative transition rate knr extremely increase to 1.08 × 107 s−1 (Table S3, ESI†). When performing a variable-temperature emission test from 298 K to 83 K, the fluorescence intensity of SCM 2 increased and the emission peaks gradually red shifted from 578 nm to 610 nm (Fig. 4b). An increased lifetime of 118 μs of SCM 2 at 83 K was observed and this value was much longer than 92.9 ns at 298 K (Fig. S22, S23, ESI†). The SCM 2 and SCM 3 are tentative of their phosphorescent character at 83 K in the solid state that came from a ligand-to-cluster charge transition (LMCT) excited state from the mixing of a metal-centred state.36,40 The detailed photophysical properties of NC a and SCM 1–3 (emission wavelength, lifetime at 298 K and 83 K, QY and transition rate) are summarized in Tables S3 and S4, ESI.†

Conclusions

In this work, pseudorotaxane-based linkers have been utilized in the construction of three thiolated silver cluster-based materials. SCM 1 displayed enhanced luminescence behaviour with a 22-fold enhancement in the QY compared with that of the isolated cluster NC a without linkers. SCM 2 and SCM 3 with different auxiliary carboxylate ligands show different optical properties from SCM 1. This study demonstrated that the ligand and structure have important roles in the photoluminescence when extending the network of silver-chalcogenide clusters.
