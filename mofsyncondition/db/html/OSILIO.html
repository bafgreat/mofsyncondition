1. Introduction

Over the past few decades, research on solid-state materials (organic, inorganic or coordination polymers) with adjustable luminescence performance has attracted considerable attention due to their potential applications in optical switches, sensing and data storage, etc.1 Compared with simplex organic or inorganic luminescent materials, coordination polymers (CPs) not only retain the unique properties of inorganic nodes and organic molecules but also achieve new properties through the synergy between them.2 CPs built by rationally linking inorganic nodes with functional organic molecules have attracted attention among researchers in developing adjustable luminescent materials.3 For example, Zang et al. reported a new Tb-MOF with photochromism, photomodulated fluorescence and luminescent pH sensing.4 Bu et al. report a new “smart” luminescent material responsive to both temperature and pressure changes, and the material shows superior luminescence-tuning performance.5

It is known that there have been numerous approaches for switching the luminescence performance, such as acid/base reaction, light and heat controls, and mechanical force.6 Light control is more attractive due to its more convenient, easy-to-operate and environmentally friendly characteristics.7 The single form of light control luminescence has been much researched.8 As we learn more, luminescence emission in one compound could be controlled not only by tuning the exciting wavelength but also by increasing photoirradiation time.9 For example, Ma et al. reported a newly designed organic molecule, which realizes both emission colour change and on/off switching, and introduceed the application of information encryption.10 However, there are rare reports on this type of multiform light-controlled luminescent materials in CPs.11 In order to achieve this type of luminescent CPs, it is very important to select suitable organic ligands and metal ion. Viologen derivatives have been reported for their good luminescence and the photoswitching of luminescence properties.12 It is an ideal functional motif for light-controlled luminescent CPs.13 In addition, transition metals such as zinc and cadmium have low cost, abundant resources and are prone to metal-to-ligand charge transfer (MLCT) interaction with ligands.14 The use of zinc or cadmium to construct such luminescent CPs has greater practical significance in all aspects of social life.

We successfully synthesized two isostructural compounds, namely [Me(ipbp)Cl]·H2O (ipbp = 1-(3,5-dicarboxyphenyl)-4,4′-bipyridinium, Me = Cd (1), Me = Zn (2)). They exhibited the photoluminescence phenomenon. Upon continuous irradiation with a xenon lamp, the emission intensity of 1 and 2 significantly reduced. Until irradiation for 30 min, the maximum quenching of the emission intensity of 1 and 2 is 90.8% and 95.7%, respectively, which is high contrast. Moreover, the luminous colour of both compounds changed from blue to green and even to yellow as the excitation wavelength increased. The study will contribute to develop multiform light-controlled luminescent CPs.

2. Experimental

2.1 Materials and methods

All chemicals, obtained from commercial sources, were of reagent grade quality and were used without further purification except for the H2ipbpCl (1-(3,5-dicarboxyphenyl)-4,4′-bipyridinium chloride) ligand, which was prepared according to the literature. Solid-state UV-vis spectra were recorded by PerkinElmer Lambda 950 at room temperature. The photoluminescence spectra were recorded on an Edinburgh FL920 phosphorimeter using a 450 W xenon lamp as the excitation source. The ESR signal at the X band was recorded using polycrystalline samples on a Bruker A300 spectrometer. A vario EL III CHN elemental analyser determined C, H, and N for the elemental analyses.

2.2 Syntheses

H2ipbpCl

The H2ipbpCl ligand was synthesized according to a previously reported literature method.15 Hydrobromic acid (48%) had been changed to hydrochloric acid (38%) in the synthesis of ligands for obtaining H2ipbpCl (Fig. S7†).

Compound 1

[Cd(ipbp)Cl]·H2O. CdCl2 (0.018 g, 0.10 mmol) and H2ipbpCl (0.032 g, 0.10 mmol) were added into a mixed solvent of DMF (4 mL) and distilled H2O (4 mL), and then sealed in a 20 mL glass bottle after stirring for 20 min. The glass bottle was heated at 100 °C for 1 day and then cooled down to room temperature. Light yellow thin piece crystals of 1 were collected by filtration with a 70 wt% yield based on H2ipbpCl. Elem. anal. calcd for [Cd(ipbp)Cl]·H2O: C, 44.16%; H, 2.70%; N, 5.79%. Found: C, 43.54%; H, 3.06%; N, 5.63%.

Compound 2

[Zn(ipbp)Cl]·H2O. ZnCl2 (0.014 g, 0.10 mmol) and H2ipbpCl (0.032 g, 0.10 mmol) were added into a mixed solvent of DMF (4 mL) and distilled H2O (4 mL), and then sealed in a 20 mL glass bottle after stirring for 20 min. The glass bottle was heated at 100 °C for 1 day and then cooled down to room temperature. Light yellow thin piece crystals of 2 were collected by filtration with a 75 wt% yield based on H2ipbpCl. Elem. anal. calcd for [Zn(ipbp)Cl]·H2O: C, 49.34%; H, 2.99%; N, 6.39%. Found: C, 47.65%; H, 3.18%; N, 6.19%.

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction data of 1 and 2 performed by a Rigaku PILATUS CCD diffractometer equipped with graphite-monochromate Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at 293 K. The structures were solved by direct methods, and the subsequent successive difference Fourier syntheses yielded other non-hydrogen atoms. All atoms except hydrogen atoms were performed through the anisotropic refinement. The final structures were refined using a full-matrix least-squares refinement on F2 using the SHELXL-97 program package.16 Pertinent crystal data and structure refinements are summarized in Table S1.† The bond lengths and angles are listed in Tables S2 and S3.†

3. Results and discussion

3.1 Crystal structure

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction indicated that the two compounds are isostructural with a 2D layer structure. Compound 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/c. There are one Cd2+, one ipbp−, one Cl− and one free water molecule in the asymmetric unit. The Cd(ii) ion is six-coordinated by four carboxylic oxygen atoms from two different ipbp ligands, one nitrogen atom from another ipbp ligand and one chloride ion in distorted tetrahedron geometries (Fig. 1a). The Cd–O bond lengths fall in the 2.334(4)–2.444(4) Å range, the bond length of Cd–Cl is 2.4533(14) Å, and the bond length of Cd–N is 2.306(4) Å (Table S2†). The dihedral angle between the centre pyridine and the isophthalic group is 47.25°; the dihedral angle between the centre pyridine and the edge pyridine is 36.70°, while the dihedral angle between the isophthalic moieties and the edge pyridine is 13.16°. The six-coordinated Cd centres are linked together by ipbp ligands to construct a 2D layer (Fig. 1b and c). Also, the 2D layer is a Z-shaped non-planar layer, stacked in the direction of [001], with a bending angle of 115.07° (Fig. 1d). The π–π interactions are between the centre pyridine and the isophthalic group in the ipbp ligand, and the distance is 3.4857(71) or 3.4510(68) Å (Fig. S1a†). Compound 2 is not discussed here because it is isomorphic to 1, and the detailed structural information is shown in Fig. S1b and S2, Tables S1 and S3.† This structure is similar to that of [Cd (ipbp) Br]·1.75H2O.17

3.2 Photochromism

These two isostructural compounds exhibit detectable colour changes under xenon lamp (ca. 200 mW cm−2) irradiation. Under the xenon lamp irradiation, light yellow samples of 1 (named 1A) and 2 (named 2A) can quickly turn into green (named as 1B and 2B, respectively) with an increase in the irradiation time. 1B is relatively stable in air and will experience a slow fading process in darkness and is difficult to return to its original colour within a month. The colour fading of 2B can be achieved by leaving it in air in dark for 7 days. The colouring and fading of 2 are reversible processes, indicating that 2 has photochromic properties.

UV-vis spectra and PXRD spectra were recorded to investigate the mechanisms of the photochromic behaviours of the two compounds. The UV-vis spectra of 1A have no obvious absorption peak, but two characteristic bands centred at 420 nm and 660 nm appear after light irradiation, and it is difficult to recover the original colour after 1 month in darkness at ambient atmosphere (Fig. 2a). For 2, there is no obvious absorption peak in the UV-vis spectra before irradiation, and two characteristic broad bands around 410 nm and 650 nm appear after irradiation, which almost disappeared after decolouration (Fig. 2b). The electron spin-resonance spectra (ESR) studies indicate that 1A has no ESR signal, while 1B has a radical signal at g = 2.0025 (Fig. 2c). Similarly, 2A has no ESR signal and 2B has a radical signal at g = 2.0029 (Fig. 2d). The PXRD patterns of the two compounds before and after irradiation are identical, ruling out the possible structural changes and decompositions (Fig. S3†). These spectral characteristics indicated that the free radical produced by photoirradiation caused the colour change of 1 and 2.18

3.3 Luminescence

The luminescent material with tuning colour emission and on/off switching to the application of information encryption in the solid state is a new research topic.19

The solid-state luminescence properties of 1 and 2 were explored at room temperature. Interestingly, 1 and 2 display excitation-wavelength-dependent photoluminescence emission in a wide wavelength range (from ∼400 nm to ∼520 nm for 1 and ∼390 nm to ∼530 nm for 2) in solid state. As shown in Fig. 3a and S4a,†1 exhibited maximum emission at ∼400 nm when excited at a short wavelength of 260–330 nm, and emitted blue luminescence with the CIE colour coordinates of (0.19, 0.14) to (0.19, 0.16). Then, varying excitation wavelengths to 360 nm, the emission intensity at 400 nm is reduced and a new emission peak is generated at ∼520 nm, and 1 emitted green luminescence with the CIE colour coordinates of (0.30, 0.46). Upon changing the excitation wavelength to 460 nm, yellow luminescence emission can be observed, and the CIE chromaticity coordinates were (0.38, 0.58). When 2 is excited at wavelengths ranging from 260 to 460 nm, numerous emission colours from blue to yellow can also be observed (Fig. 3b and S4b†). As a result, the overall emissions of 1 and 2 gradually evolve from ultraviolet to visible by changing excitation wavelengths. Based on its maximum emission, the lifetimes of 1 under excitation at 330 nm and 360 nm are 1.51 ns and 2.61 ns, respectively (Fig. S8a†). Also, the lifetimes of 2 under excitation at 335 nm and 460 nm are 1.13 ns and 3.53 ns, respectively (Fig. S8b†).

The luminescence spectra of the samples of 1 and 2 were recorded at different intervals of the xenon lamp (ca. 200 mW cm−2) irradiation. Upon continuous photoirradiation with the xenon lamp, the emission intensity of 1 and 2 decreased dramatically. After photoirradiation for 30 min, the emission intensity of 1 at 400 nm and 520 nm decreased by 90.8% and 87.3%, respectively (Fig. 4a and b). For 2, after 30 min of irradiation, the emission intensity at 390 nm decreased by 95.7% and the emission intensity at 535 nm decreased by 88.1% (Fig. 4c and d). The reason for the luminescence quenching of two compounds may be that the luminescence emission band of the sample and the UV-vis wide absorption band have spectral overlay (Fig. S6†). Compared with most known transition metal-based photochromic compounds, they have a short saturation time and high luminescence contrast.21 They may be induced by the stronger π–π interactions between the ipbp− ligands, leading to better electron transfer capabilities.22 It is worth noting that the fluorescence emission peak of 1 shows a blue-shift of about 10 nm after illumination. In addition, as the irradiation time increases, the CIE coordinate at λex = 330 nm goes from (0.18, 0.14) to (0.20, 0.22), and λex = 365 nm goes from (0.30, 0.44) to (0.26, 0.42) (Fig. S4c and S4d†). The blueshift of the emission peak may be due to the incomplete spectral overlay of the photoluminescence emission band and the UV-vis absorption band (Fig. S6a†).21

We have evaluated the density of states (DOS) and band structure to perform theoretical calculations on the structures of 1 and 2 to understand the photoluminescence mechanism. The calculation uses the CASTEP program based on the density functional theory to expand the wave function by a plane wave. The full and partial DOS of 1 and 2 are shown in Fig. S9.† For 1, the valence bands (VBs) between energy −4.0 eV and the Fermi level (0.0 eV) is mainly contributed by a mixture of p–π orbitals of ipbp− ligand as well as partial p orbitals of Cl−. The conduction bands (CBs) between energy 1.3 and 4.0 eV is almost dominated by the p–π* anti-bonding orbitals of the ipbp− ligand. By comparing the location and profile of the fluorescence emission spectra of 1, 2 and the Hipbpy ligand, it can be concluded that the emissions of 1 and 2 mainly come from the Hipbpy ligand (Fig. S5†). At the excitation wavelength of 330 nm, the emissions of 1 and 2 mainly centralized at about 400 nm. However, there are only small emissions at 520 nm, which is different from the emission of ligand (Fig. S5a†). This phenomenon may be caused by the synergistic effects between the ligands and the metal ion, which has been reported in other transition-metal CPs.9,20 Based on these, it can be considered that the emission peak at ∼400 nm is mainly originating from the ipbp−-centered transition and the metal-to-ligand charge transfer (MLCT). The emission peak at ∼520 nm of 1 should be due to the ipbp− intergroup transition.23 For 2, it has the same result as 1. Combining the comparison of the fluorescence emission spectra of 2 and Hipbpy and the DOS calculation of 2, it indicated that the emission at ~390 nm can be reasonably attributed to the ipbp−-centered transition and the MLCT (Fig. S5a†). Also, the emission of 2 at ∼530 nm is similar to the luminescence of the Hipbpy ligand should be due to the ipbp− intergroup transition.23

4. Conclusions

In conclusion, we report two isostructural compounds [Me(ipbp)Cl] H2O (ipbp =1-(3,5-dicarboxyphenyl)-4,4′-bipyridinium, Me = Cd (1), Me = Zn (2)). The X-Ray single-crystal diffraction analysis shows that the two isomorphic compounds have a Z-shaped non-planar 2D structure. These two compounds exhibit photochromic behaviour under xenon irradiation, which is caused by the formation of free radicals generated by electron transfer. Furthermore, with the increase in the excitation wavelength, the overall emissions of 1 and 2 gradually change from ultraviolet light to visible light in the solid state. Besides, 1 and 2 show time-dependent photoluminescence emission properties. They exhibit a shorter saturation time and a high luminescence contrast than most known transition metal-based photochromic compounds. Therefore, they not only show excitation-wavelength-dependent photoluminescence emission but also exhibit the photoirradiation-time-dependent photoluminescence emission in the solid state. This study may provide a new strategy for the development of multiform light-controlled luminescent CPs.
