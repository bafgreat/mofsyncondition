“Self-assembly is the autonomous organisation of components into patterns or structures without human intervention”.1 Understanding the mechanisms behind such processes remains a significant challenge in chemistry, although careful choice of boundary reaction conditions can impart a degree of control over the aggregation of structural components in complex reaction systems. In the area of polyoxometalate (POM) chemistry, the concept of self-assembly is relevant at the molecular level and beyond, with POMs often displaying complicated, high nuclearity structures, sometimes including the incorporation of additional structural components such as organic ligands.2 Indeed one of the most remarkable examples of molecular self-assembly is the crystallisation of a Mo368POM with nearly 2000 non-hydrogen atoms and a diameter comparable to haemoglobin (6 nm).3

Beyond the molecular level, self-assembly processes in POM chemistry have afforded compounds with infinite 1D chain, 2D sheet and 3D network structures, where POM building blocks are linked through covalent bonds and/or supramolecular interactions to organic or metal–organic units.4–7POM-containing 3D framework materials are of particular interest, with recent reports of examples that display properties such as selective sorption, catalytic activity and redox single crystal to single crystal transformations.5 Perhaps the most systematically investigated POM-based framework materials to date are those containing a {Ni6PW9} core, which has been functionalised with carboxylate ligands to generate structural building blocks that can combine in a predictable manner.6POMs as structural components of extended materials offer advantages in terms of size and coordination flexibility over more conventional coordination units. While attempts to design POM-based frameworks rely heavily on knowledge gained from the synthesis of “classical” coordination polymers and metal–organic frameworks,8 serendipity and self-assembly often afford the most interesting examples.7

Useful for the formation of POM-based frameworks is a metastable precursor that is capable of reorganisation to accommodate the additional structural building blocks available in solution. In recent work,9–11 we have demonstrated the versatility of the POM [As2W19O67(H2O)]14− as a precursor that can undergo isomerisation and/or dissociation–association processes at the {WO(H2O)} “hinge” of the cluster.12 Upon dissociation of the precursor in the presence of carboxylate-containing organic ligands such as acetate, glycine and picolinate, a portion of the monomeric tungstate species that is released can be scavenged. Of particular interest is a picolinate-chelated tungstate unit, {WO2(pic)}, which can have a profound effect on the generation of new POM architectures. We have recently reported the site specific luminescence properties of Tb3+ ions with picolinate chromophores in the Tb-containing member, K4Li4H4[Tb8(pic)6(H2O)22(AsW8O30)4{WO2(pic)}6]·58H2O (2), of a family of discrete 0D octanuclear lanthanoid-organo-POM complexes.10 Further exploration of the reaction system that gave rise to 2 has resulted in the isolation of the new POM-based 3D framework, K5Na[K2{Dy(H2O)3}2{As2W19O68}{WO2(pic)}2]·27H2O (1).

Compound 1 was prepared‡ by a slight variation of the synthetic method that affords 2.10 Solid Dy(NO3)3, 2-picolinic acid and [As2W19O67(H2O)]14− were mixed in the stoichiometric ratio 2 : 4 : 1 at pH 3.4, resulting in single crystals of 1 after two days in a sealed vessel. The reaction mixture must not be allowed to evaporate to dryness, as this affords an intractable oil. Replacement of Dy with Gd or Tb appears to afford analogous compounds, although crystals amenable to X-ray structure determination have not been obtained. Compounds 1 and 2 are both obtained in reasonable yield (47 and 18%, respectively) and the synthesis of 1 differs from that of 2 in only two particulars: the concentration of the reactants has been doubled and lithium ions have been omitted. However the structure of the two compounds are very different, with the 3D framework 1 containing {B-α-AsW9O33} building blocks instead of the {B-β-AsW8O30}units evident in the discrete molecular species 2; in addition the picolinate ligands in 1 do not coordinate to the lanthanoid centres, as is observed in 2. It is notable that while 1 is formed within a few days, 2 crystallises over a period of weeks and all efforts to speed up its synthesis actually result in a mixture of compounds that appears to contain 1. It is likely that 1 is a kinetic product and 2 is a thermodynamic product of the same reaction, with the presence of solubilising lithium ions and the lower ionic strength working together to keep the products in solution for long enough to allow the formation of 2. The small differences in the synthesis of two such structurally diverse compounds exemplifies the complex equilibria that exist in POM solution chemistry and the important roles that ionic strength and cation identity play in determining the species that crystallises from solution.

Single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis§ of 1 reveal that the structure can be considered as a 3D framework comprised of constituent {{Dy(H2O)3}2{As2W19O68}{WO2(pic)}2} POM units and {K4O4} distorted cubane units (Fig. 1 and 2). A number of unique structural features are evident in both building blocks. The two {WO2(pic)} moieties in each of the {{Dy2(H2O)3}2{As2W19O68}{WO2(pic)}2} POM building blocks of 1 are key to the resulting 3D structure. The tungsten centre of each {WO2(pic)} fragment is coordinated to the pyridylnitrogen and carboxylate oxygen donor atom of a picolinate ligand, forming a 5-membered chelate ring. These {WO2(pic)} fragments were observed previously in 2 and are generated in situ following partial scavenging of free tungstate that is released on dissociation of the [As2W19O67(H2O)]14− starting material into the constituent {B-α-AsW9O33} fragments. The remaining unscavenged tungstate recombines with the {B-α-AsW9O33} anions to form the more condensed {As2W19O68} unit observed in 1. A similar structural rearrangement has been observed previously.11 This more open arrangement is then capable of accommodating additional metal centres due to the dramatic change in the geometry of the lacunary site. Bound into the lacunary site of each {As2W19O68} POM subunit are two Dy3+ ions, which do not contribute to formation of the 3D framework, together with the two crucial {WO2(pic)} fragments located between the Dy centres. The two {WO2(pic)} units incorporated into the POM provides the route by which the POM building block can act as a 2-connecting unit to link the other building block of the 3D structure, the {K4O4} distorted cubanes. Structurally, the potassium centres in the {K4O4} units are each chelated by the carboxylate functional group of a picolinate ligand, with one of the oxygen atoms from each ligand occupying the corners of the cubane and the other linking the cubane to the POM through a K–O–W connection to the W centres from the {WO2(pic)} fragments. The K–O(–K/W) bonds are in the range 2.82–2.91 Å. Each {K4O4} unit is connected to four independent POM units through the {WO2(pic)} tungsten centres, generating the tetrahedral arrangement of POMs around the central cubane template. The presence of analogous {K4O4} distorted cubanes in extended framework materials is rare with only a few examples in the literature, none of which also contain POMs.13 A number of alkoxo, aryloxo and siloxo-bridged {K4O4} distorted cubanes are known.14 Given their directional nature, the M–O bonds in these alkali metal cubane complexes are generally regarded as having a significant covalent component.14 The K–O bonds that make up the distorted cubanes in 1 are clearly directional and therefore also likely to be significantly covalent.

Within the 3D structure of 1, each {K4O4} distorted cubane is a 4-connecting node, with the {{Dy(H2O)3}2{As2W19O68}{WO2(pic)}2} POM units acting as 2-connecting linkers. This results in a diamondoid network topology with recognisable adamantane cages (Fig. 2) and interconnecting channels. The shortest route around the six membered rings that make up the adamantane units is ≈96 Å, with ring dimensions of 33.9, 23.7 and 16.2 Å, measured from the centre of the {K4O4} distorted cubanes. The bulky nature of the POM linkers gives rise to their protrusion into the channels, resulting in their internal surface being ridged rather than smooth.

Although the diamondoid network topology is by no means unusual in framework materials, the nature of the constituent nodes and linkers in compound 1 render it a novel example. The formation of 1 was a result of a number of simultaneous or sequential processes involving reorganisation of the reactants to yield the structural building blocks and their assembly to afford the final connectivity observed in the framework. These processes include the formation of {K4O4} distorted cubanes, the partial disassembly of the [As2W19O67(H2O)]14−POM precursor and its reassembly into a new POM unit, and the formation of the important {WO2(pic)} linking fragments. Efforts to functionalise compound 1 are presently underway and will be reported in due course.
