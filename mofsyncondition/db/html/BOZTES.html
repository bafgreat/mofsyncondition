Introduction

Functional complexes have been studied extensively due to their diverse structural and potential applications in numerous areas.1 The infinite number of organic ligands and metal centers can be used to construct complexes with virtually limitless structures and properties.2,3 Metal–organic coordination polymers (CPs) or metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) are a new kind of functional complex materials that have potential applications in gas storage,4,5 separation,6,7 catalysis,8,9 magnetism,10,11 luminescence12 and chemical sensing.13,14 Infinite 1D, 2D and 3D coordination structures can be achieved by the appropriate choice of metal nodes, bridging ligands and synthetic conditions.15 In fact, a large number of MOFs have been synthesized using lanthanide (Ln), transition and main group metal ions as well as various carboxylates as ligands.16,17 Because of the unique 4f electronic configuration and physicochemical properties of Ln ions, coordination polymers containing Ln ions have been of particular interest.11,18,19 There is great interest in the study of the luminescence properties of Ln-MOFs due to their many applications, such as in luminescent materials, displays, medical imaging and luminescent sensors for chemical species in biological and environmental systems.12,18,20 Recently, several investigators have begun exploring the potential of MOFs as luminescent sensors for sensing cations, anions, small molecules, pH levels and temperature.21–24 The emission of Ln-MOFs derived from ligand-to-metal energy transfer could be tuned by metal or ligand coordination interactions.25 Polycarboxylic acids have become popular and widely used ligands for these frameworks because they contain multiple oxygen atoms available for coordination; those oxygen atoms also have strong binding affinities and coordinate with metal ions in a variety of bridging capacities which leads to structural diversity and pore structures.26,27 Metal ions are infinitely linked to form extended coordination polymers. Rigid polycarboxylic acid ligands have good absorption in the UV-vis region and can sensitize Ln3+ luminescence. Some luminescent Ln-complexes have been prepared combining rigid polycarboxylic acids as bridging ligands and 1,10-phenanthroline (phen) as an auxiliary ligand, which achieved strong ligand-to-metal energy transfer and intense Ln3+-centered emission.28

Our interest is in the design and synthesis of Ln-MOF complexes and their applications for luminescence sensing. This study focuses on MOFs with semi-rigid bridging ligands because they undergo a variety of coordination methods, link to metal ions and form interesting structures while simultaneously offering antenna effects, which transfers the absorbed energy to the Ln ions through intersystem crossing for MOF luminescence.29 These linker ligands contain aromatic moieties, which can be used for light absorption due to π electrons and subsequent energy transfer to the metal ions, which gives effective Ln luminescence. In addition, they exhibit a certain degree of flexibility, which can result in framework diversity and often determines the final architecture due to their versatile coordination conformations. For instance, Eu3+ with 2,3′-oxybis(benzoic acid) (2,3′-H2oba) and phen gave the 3D [Eu2(2,3′-oba)3(phen)2]n containing [Eu1O7N2] and [Eu2O6N2] units with the 2,3′-oba ligand.30 Ln3+ ions with 3,4′-oxybis(benzoic acid) (3,4′-oba), ox and phen gave the isostructural 3D [Ln(3,4′-oba) (phen)2(ox)0.5]n (Ln = Sm, Eu, Gd, Tb, Dy). Within the 3D structure, the [LnO6N2] units are bridged by ox and 3,4′-oba mixed ligands.31 A 2D luminescent framework [Eu(O-cpia)(phen)(H2O)]n was successfully constructed through assembly of 5-(2-carboxyphenoxy)-1,3-benzenedicarboxylic acid (O-cpiaH3) and phen with the Eu3+ ion.32 These complexes show interesting structures, good stability and luminescence properties.

Herein, new Ln-based metal organic frameworks, Ln-MOFs, [Ln(cphtH)phen(ox)0.5]n·mH2O (Ln = Sm 1, Eu 2, Gd 3, Tb 4 and Dy 5), were prepared by a ternary mixed-ligand synthesis strategy. The semi-rigid tricarboxylic acid ligand, 4-(4-carboxyphenoxy)-isophthalic acid (cphtH3), is isomeric with O-cpiaH3. CphtH3 contains two aromatic groups and three carboxylate groups, all of which have strong interactions with the metal center by way of six coordinating O atoms and an antenna effect for luminescent Ln-MOFs. So, cphtH3 is one promising candidate for the synthesis of metal coordination polymers with novel structures and properties. However, coordination polymers containing the cphtH3 ligand have received little attention in the literature.33 Ox is a small molecule bridging ligand with strong coordination ability.34 Phen is a rigid ligand and coordinates with metals to form a stable chelating ring. As the ancillary organic ligand, phen can improve the energy transfer efficiency from the linkers to the Ln3+ ions and can also crowd out the coordination water molecules.35 The structures, luminescence properties, and luminescence sensing properties of the synthesized complexes 1–5 are examined. These complexes feature 3D structures and are different from the complexes containing semi-rigid bridging ligands mentioned above.29–32 The luminescence properties of the solid Ln-MOFs are studied. In particular, the Eu-MOF exhibits intense luminescence in the visible region, which is attractive as a red emitting material. More importantly, this Eu-MOF is also intensely luminescent in some solvents including water over a wide pH range (4–9) upon excitation with near-visible light (358 nm); this indicates its potential application for luminescence sensing of analytes in solution. So, a chemical sensor based on the luminescence of Eu-MOF for sensing quercetin and metal ions is discussed. Quercetin exists in fruits, vegetables, beverages and plants; it is used as a food antioxidant and can also be used as an edible yellow pigment. Quercetin has medicinal effects which include the potential for anticancer activity.36 There are several reports on the detection of quercetin.36–39 Under 358 nm UV-vis excitation, sensing is achieved by luminescence quenching of Eu-MOF. In addition, the detection of Fe3+ ions has been reported by some groups;22,30–32,40 however, we realized the detection of Fe3+ ions in several solvents based on the luminescence of Eu-MOF. Our experimental results confirm that the mixed ligand synthetic approach can meet the need of a high coordination number for Ln ions; the complexes exhibited high dimensional structures, high stabilities and strong luminescence.

Experimental

Materials and methods

All experimental materials were purchased from commercial sources. C, H, and N elemental analyses were run on an Elementar Vario EL elemental analyzer. Powder X-ray diffraction was carried out on a PANalytical X'Pert PRO MPD diffractometer with Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). Infrared (IR) spectra were recorded on a Tensor 37 spectrophotometer (Bruker) using KBr pellets in the range 4000–400 cm−1. Thermogravimetric analyses were performed on an HCT-2 thermal analyzer under an air atmosphere at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. Fluorescence spectra were recorded on an FL7000 fluorescence spectrophotometer (Japan, Hitachi Company). UV-vis spectra were recorded on a 2550 UV-vis spectrophotometer (Shimadzu, Japan). Nitrogen sorption isotherms were measured at 77 K using a Micromeritics ASAP 2020 HD88 analyser.

Results and discussion

Description of the crystal structure

The five Ln-MOFs, [Ln(cphtH)(phen)(ox)0.5]n·mH2O (Ln = Sm 1, Eu 2, Gd 3, Tb 4 and Dy 5; m = 0 for 1, 3, 4, and 5; m = 2 for 2), were isostructural and crystallized in the same space group P21/c (Table 1). As far as we know, these are the first examples of Ln3+–cphtH complexes. The crystal structures can be described as a nine-coordinate polyhedron of the type [LnO7N2] with bridging cphtH–ox mixed linkers to give an open framework. Taking complex 2 as an example, the structure is described here. The asymmetric unit of 2 includes one crystallographically independent Eu3+ ion, one cphtH as a pentadentate ligand, half an ox molecule as a tetradentate ligand, one bidentate-chelate phen ligand and free water molecules. The Eu3+ ion is nine-coordinate and bound directly to five oxygen atoms (O1A, O4B, O5B, O6 and O7) from three cphtH ligands, two oxygen atoms (O8 and O9) from the ox ligand and two nitrogen atoms (N1 and N2) from the phen molecule, leading to a [EuO7N2] tricapped trigonal prismatic polyhedron (Fig. 1a). Coordination bond lengths range from 2.398(3)–2.497(3) Å for the Eu–O(cphtH) bond, 2.371(3)–2.443(3) Å for the Eu–O(ox) bond and 2.590(4)–2.619(4) Å for the Eu–N(phen) bond. The O–Eu–O bond angle ranges from 52.66(10)° to 146.94(11)° and the N–Eu–N bond angle is 63.15(12)°. Each cphtH as a 3-c ligand connects three Eu3+ ions in the μ1:η1η1/μ1:η1η1/μ1:η1η0 coordination mode (Fig. 1b), with the Eu3+ ions at distances of 9.893, 12.085 and 13.962 Å. Ox as a 2-c ligand bridges two Eu3+ ions in the μ2:η1η1/μ2:η1η1 coordination mode at a distance of 6.181 Å. The cphtH and ox mixed ligands coordinate to the Eu3+ which leads to an intricate 3D framework (Fig. 1c). The structure can also be described in which two Eu ions are connected by an ox ligand to form a [Eu–ox–Eu] dimer unit as a secondary building unit (SBU), which is alternately connected by cphtH to form a 3D structure. The phen molecule serves as the terminal ligand and occupies the coordination sites of the Eu3+ ions lying in the skeleton. In the anhydrous complexes, the Ln coordination positions are occupied by ox, phen and additional neutral organic ligands which prevent water molecules from coordinating to Eu3+. Topological analysis reveals that the 3D skeleton can be viewed as a 6-c uninodal net with a topology of point symbol {33·43·58·6}. The 3D network consists of two types of helices, left- and right-handed helical chains having a repeating unit consisting of one [Eu1–ox–Eu1] unit, one Eu and two cphtH ligands with a pitch of 10.465 Å along the a axis (Fig. 1d). The space-filling model of 2 shows channels down the b axis (Fig. 1c). PLATON calculations suggest that the free volume after the removal of free water molecules is 9.4% per unit volume. Complexes 1–5 are isomorphic and have similar structures. However, the average bond length of the Ln–O and Ln–N bonds decreases in the order of the complexes from 1 to 5, which is caused by the lanthanide contraction effect. The average bond lengths of 1–5 for Ln–O decreased continuously from 2.452 (for 1), 2.445 (for 2), 2.435 (for 3), and 2.423 (for 4) to 2.409 Å (for 5). Similarly, the Ln–N distance also decreased from 2.611 Å (for 1) to 2.569 Å (for 5). These are consistent with the order of decreasing ionic radius from Sm3+ to Dy3+ ions.

A structural comparison of the Eu-MOF with other reported complexes containing semi-rigid bridging ligands reveals that the crystal structure of the Eu-MOF differs from the ones synthesized containing semi-rigid bridging ligands in our laboratory.30–32 Eu3+ with the 2, 3′-H2oba and phen ligands gave [Eu2(2,3′-oba)3(phen)2]n containing [Eu1O7N2] and [Eu2O6N2] polyhedra. Ln3+ ions with 3,4′-oba, ox and phen also gave 3D MOFs, [Ln(3,4′-oba) (phen)2(ox)0.5]n (Ln = Sm, Eu, Gd, Tb, Dy). Within the 3D structure, the [LnO6N2] units are bridged by ox and 3,4′-oba mixed ligands. With O-cpia and phen as ligands, the 2D [Eu(O-cpia)(phen)]n was obtained with EuO6N2 in distorted square antiprismatic units. CphtH3 is a semi-rigid tricarboxylic acid with three carboxylic groups. It has multiple coordination sites and coordination modes; these allow for the construction of complexes with novel structures. Using cphtH3, ox and phen as mixed ligands in combination with Ln metal ions, 3D coordination networks, Ln-MOFs (Ln = Sm 1, Eu 2, Gd 3, Tb 4 and Dy 5), were constructed with [LnO7N2] tricapped trigonal prismatic polyhedra. CphtH3 is isomeric with O-cpiaH3. When compared with complexes containing the O-cpiaH3 ligand, Ln-MOFs 1–5 based on the cphtH3 ligand show a high coordination number and high dimensional structure. Using a combination of cphtH3, ox and phen mixed ligands, new Ln-MOFs without coordination solvent molecules were constructed. They have high stability, an intense Ln-centered emission and make chemical sense.

PXRD patterns and BET surface area measurements

Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) was used to analyze the purity of the as-synthesized Ln-MOF crystals. As shown in Fig. S1,† the experimental diffraction peaks agree with the simulated peaks which verifies the purity of the Ln-MOF samples. To examine the BET surface area of the Eu-MOF, N2 sorption isotherms were measured at 77 K using a Micromeritics ASAP 2020 HD88 analyzer. The Eu-MOF sample was activated at 90 °C under vacuum for gas sorption studies. The N2 adsorption–desorption isotherms are shown in Fig. S2† and a Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface area of 3.3020 m2 g−1 (Langmuir surface area: 3.6940 m2 g−1) is obtained.

Thermal stability

The thermal stability of Eu-MOF was measured with thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) from 28–800 °C (Fig. S3†). TGA indicates that complexes 1–5 show similar thermal behaviour. For Eu-MOF, a small weight loss of 6.0% occurs between 58 and 212 °C which corresponds to the loss of water molecules (calcd. 5.1%). The further weight loss between 360 and 550 °C is due to the loss of organic ligands within the Eu-MOF framework, indicating that the complex can be stable up to 360 °C. The remaining residue may be Eu2O3, and the total weight loss is 65.8% (calcd. 64.8%).

Chemical stability in solvents and aqueous solutions of varying pH

The Eu-MOF is insoluble in water and in common organic solvents including ethanol, acetone, acetonitrile, and ethyl acetate. The Eu-MOF samples were soaked in several solutions for 3 days; these samples were collected and measured by PXRD. As shown in Fig. S4,† the sample PXRD patterns in these solvents are consistent with the Eu-MOF crystal structure showing that the Eu-MOF skeleton structure is still maintained in solution. In particular, for the sample immersed in aqueous solutions at various pH levels (pH = 3, 4, 9 and 10) for 3 days, it is found by PXRD to remain stable in aqueous solutions of pH = 4 and 9. In aqueous solutions of pH = 3 and 10, the PXRD patterns are slightly different as compared to the others. These results indicate that the Eu-MOF exhibits high chemical stability in some organic solvents and in both acidic and basic aqueous solutions (pH = 4 and 9).

Luminescence properties

The emission spectra of the ligands were recorded (Fig. S5†), which are attributed to the ligand π*–π transition. The emission maxima were obtained at 354 nm for cphtH3 (λex = 321 nm) and 432 nm for phen (λex = 350 nm). The Gd-complex (3) exhibited a broad blue emission band from 350–450 nm with a maximum at 380 nm (λex = 340 nm) which arose from ligand-centered (LC) emission (Fig. S5†). The Sm- and Dy-based complexes (1 and 5) presented dual-emission spectra, which are composed of a broad ligand-centered (LC) emission peak originating from the π*–π transition together with narrow metal-centered (MC) emission peaks, under maximum excitation at 356 nm and 350 nm, respectively (Fig. 2a and b). The emission spectrum of 1 consists of main bands at 563, 599 and 645 nm, assigned to 4G5/2 → 6HJ (J = 5/2, 7/2, and 9/2) transitions of the Sm3+ ion. Also, the main bands at 482 and 574 nm, assigned to 4F9/2 → 6HJ (J = 15/2 and 13/2) transitions of the Dy3+ ion in 5 are observed. The intense broad emission bands centered at 394 nm for 1 and at 407 nm for 5 originate from the ligands, indicating incomplete ligand to Sm3+/Dy3+ energy transfer. Interestingly, upon excitation at 356 nm, the luminescence characteristics of 1 become close to white-light emission with CIE coordinates of (0.362, 0.281) originating from a single component Sm-MOF by combining the LC and MC emission (Fig. S6†). Single component white emission from MOFs was observed in a small number of MOF coordination compounds.31,43,44

It is interesting to note that when irradiated at 365 nm, complexes 2 and 4 show red and green light, respectively. The excitation spectra of 2 and 4 were recorded and monitored at 546 and 620 nm, respectively; these show a broad ligand band and weak Ln 4f–4f absorption peaks. Furthermore, the peak intensity of the ligand absorption is higher than that of the 4f–4f absorption; this implies that the organic ligands are capable of sensitizing the Eu3+/Tb3+ ions, leading to the strong emission of the Eu- and Tb-MOFs. The ligand absorption band, with a maximum at approximately 358 nm for 2 and 360 nm for 4, may be ascribed to the ligands (the π–π* transition), which show a good antenna effect. For 4, narrow excitation bands correspond to the transitions from the 7F6 → 5D3 (378 nm) and 5D4 (488 nm) energy levels of Tb3+. Upon maximum excitation at 360 nm, 4 exhibits an emission spectrum composed of four main peaks at 490, 546, 585, and 622 nm, assigned to the 5D4 → 7FJ (J = 6–3) transitions of the Tb3+ ion (Fig. 3a). The more intense peak at 546 nm corresponds to the 5D4 → 7F5 transition, emitting light with the CIE coordinates (0.259, 0.613) which is in the green zone (Fig. S6†). For 2, half the width of the excitation spectrum ranges from 245 to 370 nm with an excitation maximum of 358 nm. The characteristic Eu3+ intra-4f6 excitation transitions are seen in the excitation spectrum, from the 7F0 ground state to 5L7 (375 nm), 5G6 (380–385 nm), 5L6 (395 nm), 5D3 (418 nm), 5D2 (464 nm), and 5D1 (536 nm) (Fig. 3b). The f–f transition bands are weaker than the ligand band, confirming the existence of energy transfer from the ligands to the Eu3+ ion. Under maximum excitation at 358 nm, the emission spectrum consists of two main bands at 594 and 620 nm, which are assigned to the 5D0 → 7F1 and 5D0 → 7F2 transitions of Eu3+, respectively (Fig. 3c). The emission is dominated by the 5D0 → 7F2 transition. So, the complex shows bright red luminescence with the CIE coordinates (0.658, 0.334) (Fig. S6†). The emission spectra of 2 were also realized by f–f absorption excitations at 375, 380, 386, 400, 465 and 537 nm. The emission intensity of the f–f absorption peaks is less than that at the ligand excitation maximum of 358 nm (Fig. S7†). So, 358 nm is the most suitable excitation wavelength for the emission of 2, indicating a more efficient ligand-to-Eu energy transfer process. In fact, the excitation wavelength of 358 nm for 2 is close to near-visible irradiation (350 nm < λex < 420 nm). The Eu-MOF shows great chemical stability; even after two years, the same emission spectrum of the Eu-MOF material is obtained as compared with the original spectrum. This stability is required for solid-state lighting materials.

Complexes 1, 2, 4, and 5 have excitation wavelength maxima at 356, 358, 360 and 350 nm, respectively, which are near-visible radiation (350 nm < λex < 420 nm). Long excitation wavelengths for luminescent materials are important for both theoretical and practical applications, which can reduce damage and reduce energy. Different Ln3+ ions show different luminescence characteristics. The Sm- and Dy-complexes (1 and 5) have dual-emission bands comprising LC and MC emission while complexes 2 and 4 show characteristic MC emission, red for Eu and green for Tb. The Gd-complex (3) shows LC blue emission. The good luminescence properties of the complexes indicate that the use of mixed ligands can obtain highly luminescent complexes. In particular, the Eu-MOF exhibits intense characteristic luminescence in the visible region, which is attractive as a red emitting material. It can be a promising new type of sensing material.

Luminescence properties of the Eu-MOF in solvents

The solid state Eu-MOF shows strong red fluorescence upon excitation at 358 nm and was examined in varying solvents for potential fluorescence applications such as luminescence sensing. The Eu-MOF sample was dispersed in common solvents; these solvents included H2O, CH3OH, EtOH, CH3CN, DMF, THF, isopropanol, ethyl acetate, CH2Cl2 and acetone. The emission spectra of Eu-MOF in these solvents excited at 358 nm are shown in Fig. 4. All solvents except DMF show characteristic Eu3+ emission at 593 and 616 nm for the 5D0 → 7FJ (J = 1, 2) transitions. The emission intensity of the 5D0 → 7F2 transition at 616 nm features a distinct solvent dependence and the luminescence color changes from red and pink to blue depending on the solvent (Fig. S8†). There are three phenomena involved—first, the Eu3+ emission almost disappeared because the 5D0 → 7F2 emission is quenched and the broad ligand emission band appears, such as in isopropanol. Thus, the red luminescence color of Eu-MOF disappeared and it fell within the blue region. Second, the Eu3+ emission is weakened followed by the appearance of a broad ligand emission band, emitting light in the pink region (in acetone). Third, the emission intensities of Eu-MOF immersed in ethanol, acetone, acetonitrile and ethyl acetate are significantly stronger than the others; they show intense 5D0 → 7F2 emission which falls in the red region.

Luminescence properties of Eu-MOF in aqueous solutions with various pH

The acidity and alkalinity of the water environment system is not stable. Examining the effect of pH on the Eu-MOF luminescence properties for sensing application is important. Therefore, the emission spectra of the Eu-MOF complex in aqueous solutions of various pH were also investigated. The Eu-MOF samples (2 mg) were immersed in solutions (3 mL) with different pH levels (2–12) and then ultrasonically agitated (30 min) to form emulsions. Fig. 5 shows the emission intensity changes (at 616 nm, 5D0 → 7F2 transition) versus pH; the Eu-MOF shows clear pH-dependent emission. Notably, the Eu-MOF shows intense luminescence between the pH levels 4 and 9; the emission intensity is decreased gradually below pH = 4 and above pH = 10. When the pH is 2 or 12, the luminescence is almost completely quenched. These results show that the Eu-MOF exhibits intense luminescence activity even in acidic or basic water solutions over a wide pH range (4–9); only in highly acidic or basic solutions, the Eu-MOF exhibits low luminescence. The Eu-MOF has good luminescence properties in a variety of solvents including ethanol, acetone, acetonitrile, ethyl acetate and water with varying pH levels (4–9); they show that the complex can be used as a chemical sensor for luminescence sensing in common solvents containing water between pH = 4 and 9.

Luminescence sensing of quercetin

The unique luminescence of Eu-MOF in the solid state and in solutions is also high under UV-vis light; the luminescence color of the Eu-MOF both in the solid state and solution is bright red. Thus, the complex is suitable for use as a luminescent sensor and is studied further for sensing applications. The luminescence sensing of quercetin using the Eu-MOF is studied due to the benefits of quercetin in plants.37 However, there are a few reports in the literature,37–39 which led us to test the luminescence sensing of quercetin in a water system. To explore the ability of Eu-MOF to detect amounts of quercetin, a quercetin solution was added dropwise to a Eu-MOF aqueous suspension and the intensity of the 5D0 → 7F2 transition was monitored by luminescence titration. As shown in Fig. 6a, the luminescence intensity gradually decreases with added quercetin. When 300 μL of the quercetin solution (1 mM) is dispersed into the suspension, the luminescence quenching efficiency (QE) is 77%. An emission intensity I0/I − 1 vs. quercetin concentration ([M]) plot (I0 and I are the luminescence intensities before and after the addition of the analyte; [M] is the concentration of the analyte) was obtained at the emission maximum of 620 nm, as shown in Fig. 6b. The plot displays the linear relationship between I0/I − 1 and quercetin concentration. According to the Stern–Volmer equation, (I0/I) = 1 + Ksv[M] (Ksv is the quenching constant; the Ksv value represents sensitivity towards the analyte), a Ksv of 4.12 × 104 M−1 is obtained (correlation coefficient, 0.993), indicating high quenching of the Eu-MOF luminescence. A detection limit of 34 μM is calculated using the equation DL = 3σ/Ksv (σ denotes the standard deviation). There are spectrophotometric,45 chromatographic37 and electrochemical46 reports on the detection of quercetin. Using Eu-MOF as a luminescent sensor, we have realized the detection of quercetin in a water system by luminescence spectroscopy. Furthermore, quercetin quenched the red emission of Eu-MOF to colorless which is readily confirmed by the naked eye under UV light (365 nm) (Fig. 6a).

In a practical sense, it is more important for a sensor to have high selectivity towards a target object. Considering the applications of quercetin in pharmacology, selectivity experiments towards cations, anions and small molecules (Ca2+, Na+, K+, HCO3−, creatinine, glucose, urea, glutamic acid) were performed by monitoring the Eu-MOF luminescence. Extra quercetin was added to the Eu-MOF aqueous solution including other interfering agents; the results of these quenching experiments are shown in Fig. 7a which shows that the quenching effect of quercetin on the luminescence of the Eu-MOF is not influenced by interfering agents, confirming that Eu-MOF has high selectivity towards sensing quercetin. At the same time, other flavone analogues and polyphenols, such as catechin, daidzein, catechol, resorcin and hydroquinone (structures are shown in Fig. S9†), are compared with quercetin in luminescence sensing (Fig. 7b). Quercetin in the aqueous system containing interfering agents is easily recognized by the distinct luminescence quenching response. This observation reveals that Eu-MOF shows high selectivity towards quercetin detection in an aqueous environment.

Sensing mechanism for quercetin

From Results and discussion above, the Stern–Volmer equation of quercetin quenching on luminescent Eu-MOF is linear. After the Eu-MOF sample was immersed in a quercetin solution, the PXRD curve remains the same as that of the pure sample, which indicates that quercetin does not destroy the complex structure (Fig. S10†). Luminescence quenching is not caused by structural change. It is well known that the Eu3+ luminescence is generated via an energy-transfer mechanism. The photo-induced electron transfer (PET) mechanism is discussed for luminescence quenching of the Eu-MOF. If the electrons excited by light of the fluorophore transfer from the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) of the donor into that of a quenching agent, the result is fluorescence quenching.47–49 The energy levels of the highest occupied molecular orbitals (HOMOs) and LUMOs of the ligand and analytes were calculated by density functional theory (DFT) (Fig. 8a). DFT calculations at the B3LYP/6-31G level, however, show that the LUMO energy level (−1.9981 eV) of the ligand is lower than that of quercetin (−1.6514 eV), which eliminates the PET mechanism for luminescent quenching of Eu-MOF by quercetin.

The luminescence quenching mechanism is examined further by investigating the UV-vis absorption spectra of quercetin solutions. A comparison of the UV-vis absorption spectrum with the excitation and emission spectra of Eu-MOF (Fig. 8b) shows that the UV-vis absorption of quercetin does not overlap with the emission of Eu-MOF. However, the absorption spectrum does show some overlap with the excitation spectrum of Eu-MOF. This creates a competition for the absorption of light between quercetin and the MOF; the absorption of quercetin might hinder the energy absorption of Eu-MOF, which decreases populated emissive state of Eu-MOF, leading to luminescence quenching.47,49–51 However, the maximum absorption wavelengths of the other analytes are around 300 nm, which are far from the excitation energy of the Eu-MOF. Obviously, the absorption wavelengths are also smaller than that of quercetin (Fig. S11†). Therefore, they have a small effect on the luminescence of the Eu-MOF, compared to quercetin.

Luminescence sensing of inorganic ions

The detection of metal ions has been reported previously in some literature studies.22,30–32 The sensing properties of the new synthesized Eu-MOF towards the detection of metal ions were tested. A 3 mg sample of Eu-MOF was immersed in aqueous solutions containing M(NO3)n (2 mL of a 10 mM solution; M = K+, Mg2+, Cr3+, Ba2+, Pb2+, Cd2+, Ni2+, Co2+, Fe3+, Zn2+ and Cu2+) to form Eu-MOF@Mn+ suspensions; their luminescence was examined at 358 nm. The Eu-MOF@Mn+ is expected to show changes to luminescence intensity (5D0 → 7F2) with the addition of the various cations. In aqueous solution (Fig. 9a and b), the Mg2+ ion slightly increases the emission intensity. In contrast, K+, Cr3+, Ba2+, Cd2+, Ni2+, Co2+, Fe3+, Zn2+ and Cu2+ ions all decrease the emission intensity and the quenching effect of Fe3+ is the most significant. The sensitivity to luminescence quenching is also analyzed by titration of Eu-MOF@Fe3+ in solution and calculated using the linear Stern–Volmer plot (I0/I) = 1 + Ksv[M]. As shown in Fig. 9d, the Ksv value is 4.2 × 103 M−1. The detection limit (3σ/Ksv) is 0.335 mM, which is a clear indication of the sensing sensitivity towards Fe3+.

The selectivity to Fe-ion detection in water was tested (Fig. S12†). In an aqueous solution containing other metal ions, Fe3+ still shows luminescence quenching for the Eu-MOF; the presence of other ions does not interfere with the recognition of Fe3+. By examining previous literature reports and the difference in the UV-vis absorption spectra of metal ions (Fig. S13†), the UV-vis absorption of Fe3+ is found to overlap with that of Eu-MOF which indicates that absorption by the complex is weakened due to absorption by Fe3+, leading to luminescence quenching.30,31 The PXRD pattern indicates that incorporation of Fe3+ does not destroy the complex structure (Fig. S10†).

Luminescence sensing of metal ions with Eu-MOF was also tested in other solutions using ethanol, acetone, acetonitrile and ethyl acetate as the solvents. Thus, the emission spectra of Eu-MOF@Mn+ in these solutions were examined at 358 nm. Fig. S14–S17† show the emission intensity changes (at 616 nm, 5D0 → 7F2 transition) versus Mn+ ions. These results indicate that Fe3+ can quench the luminescence of Eu-MOF in the solvents. This ability may be related to the special electronic configuration (d5) of the Fe3+ ion. Luminescence titration (I0/I − 1 vs. [M]) shows a linear relationship with Ksv values of 2.98 × 103 M−1 in acetone, 2.17 × 104 M−1 in acetonitrile, 1.79 × 104 M−1 in ethanol, and 1.36 × 104 M−1 in ethyl acetate. The detection limits are calculated to be 0.473 mM in acetone, 0.065 mM in acetonitrile, 0.078 mM in ethanol, and 0.103 mM in ethyl acetate. The results indicate that Eu-MOF can be used as a chemical sensor for luminescence sensing of Fe3+ ions in some solutions and the detection limit is compared (Table S2†).

Conclusion

Five Ln (Sm, Eu, Gd, Tb, and Dy)-MOF complexes have been synthesized, and in the presence of dual-bridging mixed ligands cphtH/ox and an ancillary phen ligand, their crystal structures are 3D featuring micropores. The frameworks display chemical stability in aqueous solutions including both acidic and basic solutions (pH = 4–9) as well as several organic solvents. The luminescence properties of the solid Ln-MOFs were studied. The maximum excitation wavelengths (350–360 nm) of these complexes are in the near visible region. The Sm- and Dy-complexes (1 and 5) are dual-emissive materials exhibiting LC and MC emission while complexes 2 and 4 show characteristic MC emission. Notably, the luminescence of Eu-MOF (2) is maintained in some organic and aqueous solvent systems, and even tolerated acidic or basic solutions (pH = 4–9). The new Eu-MOF as a red luminescent sensor based on Eu3+ emission with a long excitation wavelength (358 nm) is obtained. Luminescence sensing of quercetin in water and Fe3+ ions in several solvents is realized with high selectivity and a low detection limit. These studies suggest that highly stable 3D lanthanide-coordination frameworks with good luminescence and luminescence sensing properties can be constructed by a ternary mixed-ligand. The Eu-MOF has promising potential in luminescence sensing of several types of analytes including medicinal molecules.
