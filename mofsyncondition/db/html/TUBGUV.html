As an extremely vital industrial chemical, propylene (C3H6) is widely utilized in the production of multiple downstream products, such as polypropylene, acrylonitrile, and propylene epoxide.1 In the petroleum industry, the purification of propylene from a propyne/propylene (C3H4/C3H6) mixture has been recognized as one of the most challenging separation processes, owing to their very similar sizes, boiling point, and relative volatility.2 Although the extractive distillation and catalytic hydrogenation have been applied in the realm of C3H4 and C3H6 separation, their high energy-consuming and environment unfriendly shortcomings force the revolution of separation technologies.3 As an effective and energy-efficient purification method, adsorptive separation utilizing customized porous materials has thus attracted intensive attention from both academic community and industry fields.4

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), emerging as a new class of porous materials, have been attracting intense interest for various applications, particularly adsorption-based ones in recent years, due to their diversified tunability with regard to pore size, shape, and internal physicochemical environments.5 Among some of the pioneering studies, MOFs with a flexible-robust structure, pillared inorganic anions, or limited nanospace have been recognized as highly selective C3H4 adsorbents, which are mostly associated with a thermodynamic-based mechanism.6 In particular, it has been reported that UTSA-200 with ultrasmall channels (3.4 Å) and slight structural flexibility exhibits by far a record C3H4/C3H6 selectivity.7

Although some progress has been made, the application of MOFs for gas separation still faces challenges, particularly, the contradiction between adsorption capacity and separation selectivity and optimized diffusion dynamics. The ideal materials would be expected to combine favorable adsorption sites, tailored pore shape/size, and suitable free volume, and thus achieve an adequate selectivity and relatively high working capacity. Aiming at this, thus far some strategies have been developed, such as the introduction of multifunctional binding sites into MOFs or the use of a pore space partition approach to modify MOFs with high porosity.8 These strategic targets are usually achieved via the rational design of the ligand functionality and coordination unsaturated metal centre at the atomic level.9 Both methods are quite efficient in realizing MOFs for good C2H2/C2H4, CO2/C2H2, C3H8/CH4, C2H6/CH4, or CO2/CH4 separations; nevertheless, the exploration of the C3H4/C3H6 system is rarely performed.8a,9b

Cu(ii) paddlewheel secondary building units (SBUs) have been widely utilized to construct MOFs with high surface area and rich open metal sites (OMSs) after the removal of terminal coordinated molecules.10 Besides, due to the weak acidity of the C3H4 molecule, we conceived that it would be favorable to introduce abundant N-rich Lewis basic sites (LBSs) to enhance the adsorptive interaction. On the basis of these considerations, we designed and synthesized a ligand acid, 5,5′-(1,3-phenylenebis (azanediyl)) diisophthalic acid (H4PBDA), which possesses an isophthalate section and amino group. This ligand could assemble with a paddlewheel Cu(ii) SBU to fabricate the framework with metal–organic polyhedra (MOPs),11 OMSs, and LBSs simultaneously. As expected, a MOF, [Cu12(PBDA)4(DABCO)3(COO)8(H2O)6]·xS (S = solvent molecules; DABCO = 1,4-diazabicyclo[2.2.2]-octane) (BUT-310, BUT = Beijing University of Technology) with high accessible free volume, a novel pore system, and multiple functional sites was synthesized on this ligand. Powder X-ray diffraction confirms the phase purity of the synthesized material (Fig. S3, ESI†). Interestingly, BUT-310 exhibits combined features of high adsorption capacities for C3 light hydrocarbon and excellent separation performance for the C3H4 and C3H6 mixture, which are confirmed by single-component adsorption, column breakthrough, and molecular simulations.

Single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis revealed that BUT-310 crystallizes in the cubic system with the Fmm space group. As displayed in Fig. 1a, the PBDA4− ligand contains two terminal isophthalate entities, which are assembled with Cu(ii) paddlewheel motifs to generate a typical cuboctahedral MOP cage.12 Furthermore, another symmetric cage is built from twelve Cu(ii) paddlewheels, six DABCO ligands, and four PBDAs4−, outlining in a tetrahedron. The two types of polyhedral packing arrangement result in the final multiple pore system, including three-dimensional (3D) siphonate channels (approximate diameter of 9 Å) along the a axis. Obviously, the DABCO axial ligand in the structure plays an important role in balancing the flexibility of the PBDA4− ligand and stabilizing the whole framework. It is particularly worth noting that the PBDA4− ligands in the framework are disordered (Fig. 1b), originating from the crystallographically imposed symmetry.13 Structural refinement of BUT-310 indicates that the occupancy of PBDA4− is close to 1/3 (Table S2, ESI†). This disorder causes the “defect” of the observed structure in Fig. 1e, which creates additional tortuous channels for guest molecule diffusion. As shown in Fig. 1c and d, after removing coordinated solvents on the copper centers, the open metal sites and Lewis basic N sites from the ligand PBDA4− decorate the pore surface of this MOF, which would be functional for the gas adsorption and separation discussed below. From a topological point of view, the classical cuboctahedron MOP can be considered as a 12-connected vertex, which links another twelve crystallographically independent MOPs through linear rods to build a 3D network with a fcu topology (Fig. S5, ESI†). On another perspective, the Cu(ii) paddlewheel SBU with a square geometry and interconnected disordered PBDA4− ligand can be regarded as 4- and 6-connected nodes, respectively, making up a (4,6)-connected network with a point symbol of {43·612}2{46}3 as calculated with TOPOS software (Fig. S6, ESI†).14 The total accessible volume of this MOF after eliminating the guest molecules is 57.2% estimated by PLATON. BUT-310 shows good thermal stability, being stable up to 290 °C (Fig. S11, ESI†). The reversible adsorption isotherm obtained by N2 adsorption measurement at 77 K exhibits a stepwise adsorption behavior (P/P0 = 0.16), which implies the cage-type structure of BUT-310.15 These results also confirm that the framework possesses permanent porosity and microporous features, with the calculated BET surface area of 1811 m2 g−1 (Fig. 2a).

The high densities of both OMSs and LBSs and rich porous diffusion channels in its framework inspire us to explore the capacities of BUT-310 for the adsorption of small gas molecules, including energy gas (H2), greenhouse gases (CH4/CO2), and C2/C3 hydrocarbons. Single-component adsorption isotherms of guest-free BUT-310 for the above gases were measured at 298 K, 273 K, and 77 K, respectively (Fig. S17, ESI†). As shown in Fig. 2b, BUT-310 shows higher uptake capacities for C3 species than C2, and the trend of adsorption amount is C3H4 > C3H6 ≈ C3H8 ≫ C2H2 ≈ C2H4 ≈ C2H6. Notably, the C3H4 uptake capacity (243 cm3 g−1) of BUT-310 at 298 K and 101.3 kPa is higher than that in most of reported MOFs with OMSs and large porosities, such as Fe-MOF-74 (175 cm3 g−1), Co-MOF-74 (170 cm3 g−1), Cr-BTC (230 cm3 g−1) and Cr-BTT (170 cm3 g−1).7 Obviously, compared with C3H4, BUT-310 possesses a lower adsorption capacity for C3H6 under each similar pressure, giving a possibility for the separation of the two gases.

The isosteric heats of adsorption (Qst) values for C3H4 and C3H6 at low coverage were calculated to be 32.3 and 30.6 kJ mol−1, according to the virial equation (Fig. S17, ESI†). Moreover, the evaluated ideal adsorbed solution theory (IAST) selectivity of C3H4 over C3H6 (50 : 50 volume ratio) in BUT-310 at 1 bar based on the single-component sorption data was 2.73 (Fig. S15, ESI†). Interestingly, we note that the Qst value of C3H4 of BUT-310 is notably lower than that of several reported materials with strong adsorption sites, like ELM-12 (60.6 kJ mol−1),6a ZU-62 (≈73.0 kJ mol−1),16 and UTSA-200 (≈56.0 kJ mol−1).7 This result suggests that BUT-310 might be a promising material for the removal of C3H4 from the C3H4/C3H6 mixture with lower regeneration energy consumption. Moreover, a similar Qst value for C3H4 and C3H6 and low IAST selectivity manifest that the kinetic effect might be another assisted separation driving force for the C3H4/C3H6 mixture, if it works. Therefore, kinetic adsorption behavior was subsequently checked, which is related to the diffusion rate of the adsorbates within the adsorbent pores. As presented in Fig. S18,† BUT-310 shows a slightly faster uptake of C3H4 than C3H6 at 298 and 308 K, accompanied by the whole stage of adsorption before reaching equilibrium. The longer equilibrium time for C3H6 thus not only demonstrates the tortuous diffusion path but also endows BUT-310 with the purification potential of C3H6 based on the synergistic effect (thermodynamic/kinetic-based mechanism).17

To further probe the C3H4/C3H6 separation performance of BUT-310 in practical devices, column breakthrough experiments on a mixed gas of C3H4/C3H6 (50 : 50 volume ratio) were carried out at 298 K and 1.0 bar (Fig. S23, ESI†). As the mixed gas flowed into the column packed with activated BUT-310 powder, purified C3H6 was detected in the outlet gas flow, while C3H4 was still trapped in the column (Fig. 2c). The observed final retention time of C3H4 was 22.2 min g−1, and the related C3H6 processing capacity was up to 44.4 cm3 g−1, which is higher than that of the benchmark material ELM-12 (10.6 cm3 g−1).6a In addition, the column can be conveniently regenerated at room temperature after reaching saturation (Fig. 2d). As displayed in Fig. S25,† nearly no obvious decay was observed in the separation performance during 5 cycles, confirming good reproductivity to some extent.

In order to gain a deep insight into the separation processes of this MOF toward C3H4/C3H6, grand canonical Monte Carlo (GCMC) simulations were utilized to understand the nature of adsorption sites and molecular packing states within the framework.18 As illustrated in Fig. S21,† both C3H4 and C3H6 molecules are primarily accumulated in the cage-like structure region, which may provide strong overlapping potentials, and the bonding sites mostly locate around the paddlewheel moieties with open metal sites and alkaline nitrogen atoms (R2NH groups) (Fig. 3a/b/d/e). Besides, the DFT calculations on the fragment-based model also revealed that both C3H4 and C3H6 molecules are more preferentially adsorbed at Cu(ii) sites with the C⋯Cu distances of 2.64 and 2.67 Å, respectively, which might be due to the stronger interactions between the electron-rich π bonding orbitals in C3H4 and C3H6 and the vacant orbitals of the open Cu(ii) sites (Fig. 3c/f).19,20 Furthermore, the molecular dynamics simulations on BUT-310 were performed to investigate the diffusion behavior of C3. As shown in Fig. S22,† the self-diffusion coefficients of C3H4/C3H6 were calculated to be 0.79 × 10−9 m2 s−1 and 0.72 × 10−9 m2 s−1, which confirm that C3H4 has a slightly larger diffusion rate than C3H6.

These results verify that the introduction of OMSs and LBSs would significantly enhance the interaction between the framework and gas molecules. Concretely, OMSs serve the function based on the typical cuboctahedral MOP cage and LBSs exhibit efficacy with another symmetric cage, which guarantees the wide and uniform distribution of binding sites within each channel (as illustrated in Fig. S8, ESI†). It should be noted that the original size of the larger cage structure (15 Å) is much larger than that of the gas adsorbates. Therefore, it's difficult to separate light hydrocarbon mixtures with the same carbon numbers. Fortunately, the disordered arrangement of the PBDA4− ligands in the structure could reduce the actual “window” size and provide extra tortuous diffusion paths for C3H4 and C3H6 molecules. Correspondingly, it also enlarges the interception effect of the Lewis basic sites (LBSs) on gas adsorbates and contributes to different gas dynamic diffusion/packing efficiencies within the pore system.

In summary, we have successfully constructed a microporous MOF, BUT-310 with high densities of both OMSs and LIBs, based on paddlewheel Cu(ii) and a newly designed ligand. This MOF shows a relatively high adsorption capacity toward C3 hydrocarbons and excellent separation performance toward the C3H4/C3H6 mixture. The efficient adsorptive separation of C3H4 over C3H6 in BUT-310 can be attributed to an optimized and synergistic effect: stronger electrostatic/acid–base interactions related to OMSs and LIBs and a faster kinetic diffusion rate within the framework. This work not only creates a novel MOF adsorbent for the potentially purifying application of C3H6 from the C3H4/C3H6 system, but also suggests a new strategy to achieve the balance between adsorption capacity and separation selectivity in porous materials.
