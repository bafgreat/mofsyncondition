Introduction

In recent years, organic dyes have been widely used in industries such as textiles, printing, paper, and so on.1 A large volume of industrial wastes that contain various types of dyes are discharged, which becomes a serious environmental problem as it causes a series of negative effects on humans and animals.2 To remove organic dyes from industrial wastes, existing methods, such as chemical degradation, biodegradation, and physical adsorption, are usually used but they also have high costs and low efficiencies.3–10 More importantly, the poor selectivity of the known adsorbents limits their application in the dye separation and purification process. Therefore, it is necessary to exploit high-performance adsorbents with excellent selectivity and low-cost routes for the extraction of purely targeted dyes.

As is already known, metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) with unprecedented porosity are being extensively developed in the storage and separation fields (including gas molecules and organic molecules).11–18 For use in these applications, MOFs have many characteristic advantages such as adjustable pore structure and large specific surface area (SA), that are essential traits for a recyclable adsorbent. Recent work has shown that the pore window size of the MOFs is an important factor for charged organic dye absorption and separation.19–27 In particular, MOFs with a pore size larger than 2 nm are desirable. In terms of the charge effect of the main framework, MOFs can be classified into two categories, ionic and neutral MOFs.28–35 When compared with neutral frameworks, the ionic frameworks are a type of charged device with stronger electrostatic potential, which implies outstanding properties for the adsorption of organic dyes via ion-exchange.36–42 However, existing research has shown that the structural prediction and controllability of MOF-based materials still face huge challenges, which are derived from the complicated influencing factors, two primary categories of which are the nature of the organic linkers and the reaction conditions, although plenty of MOFs have been constructed from the assembly of metal centers and organic linkers.43

It is well-established that using a tripod ligand is more inclined to form high-dimensional interpenetrated frameworks which possess improved stability and enhanced size- and shape-selective effects towards the guest molecules, thus giving rise to a positive effect on the selective recognition of organic dyes.44–46 In the research reported here, a tripod imidazole carboxylic acid ligand (H3ITTC = 4,4′,4′′-(1H-imidazole-2,4,5-triyl) tribenzoic acid) was chosen and successfully used to synthesize two MOFs, namely [Zn3(ITTC)3] (Me2NH2)3·3DMF·H2O (1) and [Cd2(ITTC)3](Me2NH2)5·2DMF (2). The crystal structures and components of the resulting MOFs were characterized in detail. Both MOFs are anionic frameworks with a 2-fold interpenetrating 3D network. As expected, the anionic framework can be used to selectively adsorb and separate the cationic organic dye, methylene blue (MB) relying on the size-matching effect and the electrostatic interactions.

Experimental

Materials and physical measurements

All the commercially available chemicals were used without further purification. Elemental analysis (C, H, and N) was performed using a PerkinElmer 2400 CHN elemental analyzer. A Bruker Equinox 55 FT-IR spectrometer was used to record the Fourier-transform infrared spectroscopy (FT-IR) spectra in the range of 400–4000 cm−1 using KBr pellets. The phase purity of the bulk or polycrystalline samples was examined using powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) measurements which were made on a Rigaku RU20 diffractometer at 60 kV, 300 mA, and Ga Kα radiation (λ = 1.34139 Å), with a scanning speed of 5° min−1 and a step size of 0.02° in 2θ. The thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) data for compounds 1 and 2 were collected using a NETZSCH STA 449 F3 instrument under a N2 atmosphere at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 from 30 to 900 °C, and an empty Al2O3 crucible was used as the reference. Gas adsorption–desorption isotherms were obtained using a Micromeritics ASAP 2460 surface area and pore analyzer. The UV-vis spectroscopic studies were collected on a Shimadzu WFH-203B spectrophotometer.

Synthesis of 1, [Zn3(ITTC)3](Me2NH2)3·3DMF·H2O (1)

A mixture of H3ITTC (0.03 mmol, 0.0090 g) and Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (0.01 mmol, 0.0041 g) were dissolved in 7 mL of DMF in a 15 mL vial and then heated in a Teflon-lined stainless-steel vessel at 140 °C for 3 d, and then cooled, at a rate of 3 °C h−1, to room temperature. Canary yellow colored, block shaped crystals were collected by filtration and then dried in air. The yield of compound 1 is 60.3% (based on Zn2+). Anal. (%) calcd for C87H86Zn3N12O22: C, 56.55%; H, 4.69%; N, 9.10%. Found: C, 56.06%; H, 4.52%; N, 8.75%. Synthesis of 2, [Cd2(ITTC)3](Me2NH2)5·2DMF (2)

A mixture of H3ITTC (0.06 mmol, 0.0180 g) and Cd(NO3)2·4H2O (0.02 mmol, 0.0062 g) were dissolved in 6 mL of DMF in a 15 mL vial and then heated in a Teflon-lined stainless-steel vessel at 140 °C for 3 d, and then cooled at a rate of 3 °C h−1 to room temperature. Canary yellow colored strip shaped crystals were collected by filtration and dried in air. The yield of compound 2 is 68.5% (based on Cd2+). Anal. Experiments on organic dyes sorption and separation

In order to explore adsorption kinetics, 5 mg of freshly prepared crystals of 1 or 2 were immersed in 8 ml of a dye solution in DMF (10 mg L−1). After a certain amount of time, 3 mL of dye solution was taken out for UV-vis absorption measurement and was then poured back after the test. For the separation of a mixed dye solution, 5 mg of freshly prepared crystals of 1 or 2 were immersed in 8 mL of a dye solution in DMF (the concentration of both of dyes was 10 mg L−1). After a certain amount of time, 3 mL of the dye solution was taken out for UV-vis absorption measurement and was then poured back after the test.

Experiments of dye release

Freshly prepared crystals of 1 or 2 (5 mg) were soaked in 8 mL of a DMF solution of MB (10 mg L−1) for a specific amount of time, then filtered and washed with DMF to obtain MB@1 or MB@2. Dye-releasing experiments were then performed by immersing MB@1 or MB@2 (5 mg) in pure DMF (8 mL) and a NaCl-saturated DMF solution (8 mL). The UV-vis absorption spectra were measured to monitor the dye-release process.

Experiments on the reusability for MB adsorption

The crystals after the dye release experiment were then soaked in an excess of NaCl-saturated DMF solution overnight to exchange as much of the MB in the MB@1 or MB@2 as possible. The crystals were further filtered out, and the previous adsorption–desorption cycle was repeated. The crystals were washed at least three times with DMF during all the filtrations.

Crystallographic data collection and refinement

A suitable single crystal of the complex was mounted on glass fibers for the X-ray measurements. Then the reflection data were collected at a low temperature on a Bruker SMART APEX CCD-based diffractometer using graphite mono-chromated Ga Kα radiation (λ = 1.34139 Å). An empirical absorption correction method was applied using the SADABS program, and the data processing was accomplished with the SAINT processing program. The structure was solved using direct methods and refined with full-matrix least-squares on F2 using the SHELXH program.47,48 All the non-hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic displacement parameters. Results and discussion

Crystal structure descriptions

The X-ray single crystal diffraction analysis revealed that 1 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P. The entire structure of 1 is a 3D coordination architecture comprising two identical 3D networks interpenetrating into each other. Compound 1 is crystallographically centrosymmetric and its asymmetric unit consists of three independent Zn2+ ions coordinated in different ways, three ITTC3− ligands, three [(CH3)2NH2]+, three DMF molecules and one H2O molecule. Three types of Zn2+ ions were present in the asymmetric unit of 1 and supplied with five-surrounding carboxyl oxygen atoms for Zn1 and Zn2, and four-surrounding carboxyl oxygen atoms for Zn3. As shown in Fig. 1, Zn1 is penta-coordinated with a distorted trigonal bipyramidal coordination geometry completed by five carboxyl O atoms (O1A, O2A, O3, O17 and O17A) from three different ITTC3− ligands, whereas Zn2 is bound to five carboxylate O atoms (O4, O7, O8, O18 and O18A) from three distinct ITTC3−, also displaying a distorted trigonal bipyramidal coordination geometry. In comparison, Zn3 adopted a distorted tetrahedron coordination geometry surrounded by four carboxyl O atoms (O12A, O12B, O13 and O15A) from three distinct ITTC3− ligands. Interestingly, the ITTC3− ligands of 1 were indicative of three different coordination modes (Fig. S1, ESI†), in which the three carboxyl groups adopted: (i) a bridging symmetry, bidentate and monodentate, and (ii) a chelating bidentate, bridging bidentate and monodentate. Two crystallographically independent Zn1 and Zn2 atoms were linked by three carboxylate groups with a μ2-η1:η1 coordination mode to form a dinuclear [Zn2(COO)3]+ secondary building unit (SBU) with a Zn⋯Zn distance of 3.38 Å. In the structure of 1, three carboxyl groups in each conjugated tripod ITTC3− ligand connected with two [Zn2(COO)3]+ SBU and one Zn3 ion to generate a 2D coordination network (Fig. 2a). Then, the ITTC3− ligand linked the adjacent layer to produce a 3D framework structure with large channels along the a axis (Fig. 2c). The pore size was about 31.7 Å × 15.6 Å. This was an anionic framework and the extra charges were ensured by the cationic [(CH3)2NH2]+ molecules decomposed in situ from DMF. Moreover, because the high porosity offered the opportunity to penetrate into the other framework, two 3D frameworks interpenetrated each other (Fig. 2b), giving rise to a 2-fold interpenetrated structure by noncovalent interactions including π⋯π stacking, C–H⋯π and hydrogen bonds.49 The real pore size in the 2-fold interpenetrated framework is 30.0 Å × 14.4 Å. The 2-fold interpenetrated 3D network can be simplified as a (3,3,4,5)-connected new type network (Schläfli symbol {4·6^2}2{4^2·6^6·8^2}{6^3}{6^5·8}) (Fig. 2d).

Compound 2 crystallized in the monoclinic space group C2/m. The unit cell of 2 contained one crystallographically independent Cd2+ atom, one and a half ITTC3− ligands, two and a half [(CH3)2NH2]+ and one DMF molecule. According to the charge balance consideration, the anionic framework was balanced by [(CH3)2NH2]+, from the decomposition of the DMF solvent during the solvothermal reaction. Each Cd2+ ion was coordinated with seven carboxyl O atoms (O2, O3A, O4A, O5B, O6B, O7, O8) from four distinct ITTC3− ligands, showing a distorted pentagonal bipyramid geometry (Fig. 3). As shown in Fig. S2 (ESI†), the ITTC3− ligands exhibited two coordination modes of (κ1-κ1)(κ1-κ1)-μ2 and (κ1-κ0)(κ1-κ1)(κ1-κ1)-μ3, respectively. The distorted tripod ITTC3− ligand and three Cd2+ atoms were interconnected to give a 3D framework with 1D hexagonal channels along the c axis (Fig. 4). The pore diameter was 34.1 Å. The channels were decorated by uncoordinated carboxyl O atoms. Similar to that in 1, a two-fold framework interpenetration also occurred in 2 (Fig. 4b), and the real pore diameter in the 2-fold interpenetrated framework is 31.9 Å. From the topological viewpoint, the 3D structure of 2 can be simplified as a 2-fold interpenetrating (3,4)-connected fcu topology structure with the Schläfli symbol of {6^3}{6^5·8} (Fig. 4c).

PXRD, stability and porosity

PXRD analyses were employed to check the phase purity of 1 and 2. The results showed that the diffraction patterns of the fresh samples were in good agreement with the simulated ones from the single crystal structure data, thus indicating their pure phases (Fig. S3, ESI†). Next, the fresh samples of 1 and 2 were immersed in DMF for 24 h. The PXRD analyses showed that the frameworks of 1 and 2 were relatively intact after immersion in DMF, which indicated their excellent stability in DMF (Fig. S4, ESI†). The TGA curves showed that the frameworks of 1 and 2 could be stable up to 395 °C and 400 °C, respectively (Fig. S5, ESI†). For 1, the first weight loss of 20.25% occurred at 25–390 °C which was attributed to the removal of [(CH3)2NH2]+, H2O and DMF (cal. 20.34%). Thereafter, the TGA curve remained unaltered up to 395 °C, and then subsequently the framework began to collapse. For 2, a sharp drop of weight (20.04%) at 25–290 °C was attributed to the loss of DMF and [(CH3)2NH2]+ (cal. 20.08%), and then a plateau was observed from 290 to 400 °C before the framework decomposition.

In order to assess their permanent porosity, N2 adsorption measurements were conducted on samples 1 and 2 which were pre-activated by solvent exchange with methanol and subsequent activation at 80 °C for 8 h (under vacuum). The chosen activation conditions were those which were able to liberate the solvent molecules from the metal ions. For 1 and 2, the subsequent N2 adsorption measurements taken at 77 K indicated a modest BET SA of only 17.05 m2 g−1 and 4.66 m2 g−1, respectively (Fig. S7, ESI†). A literature search showed, that large-pore MOFs, particularly those with mesopores, continued to face problems with pore collapse upon activation.50 Many existing large-pore networks lost significant amounts of accessible SA during solvent removal/exchange.51–54 The interactions between the solvent and the MOF walls can help stabilize the framework. However, the removal of solvent molecules puts stress on the heavy framework struts, distorting the MOF structure, and eventually resulting in pore collapse.55 Therefore, for 1 and 2, there was a negligible accessible SA due to a total framework collapse.

Organic dyes absorption and separation

Because the host–guest electrostatic interaction in anionic frameworks would facilitate the capture of a cationic guest, the potential applications of 1 and 2 in ionic dye capture were investigated. Considering the size effect, three widely used organic dyes were chosen for the studies, including a cationic dye (MB), a neutral dye (Sudan I (SDI)), and an anionic dye (methyl orange (MO)). These dyes had a similar size and molecular weight but different charges (Table S2, ESI†).

Next, the absorption behaviors of 1 are first discussed in detail, and then these behaviors are compared with those of 2. First, the freshly synthesized crystals (5 mg) of 1 were immersed in DMF solutions of the different dyes (the dye solutions contained MB (10 mg L−1), MO (10 mg L−1) and SDI (10 mg L−1)). After soaking for different periods of time, the absorption capacities of dyes were determined by measuring the absorption intensities of the corresponding dyes. As expected, 1 could selectively absorb cationic dyes. As shown in Fig. 5, the cationic dyes are quickly absorbed by 1, leading to a color change throughout the crystals. In contrast, the neutral dye, SDI, could only be absorbed temporarily, which may be due to the lack of host–guest electrostatic interactions. For the anionic dye. MO, only negligible absorption was found in 1. This was consistent with the deduction that the anionic framework does not absorb anionic dye. Indeed, the color of the MB aqueous solution noticeably changed from deep blue to colorless, and this change, which was also monitored with UV-vis spectroscopy, showed that the dramatic intensity drop of the maximum absorption band of MB occurred at 664 nm, and the color had nearly disappeared after 4 h, which indicated that the MB dye molecules were effectively adsorbed by 1 (Fig. 5a). Nevertheless, under the same experimental conditions, little absorbance change for MO at 465 nm was observed after 4 h, which implied there was the poor adsorption of 1 towards the MO dye molecules, and this displayed the potentially selective adsorption for the cationic MB rather than the anionic MO (Fig. 5b). The neutral dye, SDI, showed a similar absorption behavior with 1 as MO, and little absorbance change for MO was observed after 4 h, at 477 nm (Fig. 5c). Moreover, because the adsorption rate was a relatively crucial parameter to determine the value of an efficient adsorbent, the adsorption kinetics of 1 for MB dye were determined and fitted by a pseudo-second-order kinetic model. The adsorption efficiency and quantity (Q) were calculated using the following equations: efficiency (%) = (C0 − Ct) × 100/C0 and Q = (C0 − Ct) × V/m (C0 = the initial concentration of the dye solution, Ct = the concentration of 1 that adsorbs the dye at t min, and m = adsorbent mass). From the UV-vis spectra, it was calculated that in the first 4 h, 1 adsorbed 96% MB at a loading of 14.88 mg g−1 (Fig. 5d).

Furthermore, the maximal adsorption capacity of dyes on 1 was also determined. As-synthesized compound 1 was dispersed in freshly formulated aqueous solutions with different concentrations of MB. The concentrations of MB after adsorption were obtained by UV-vis spectroscopy after 4 h. As shown in Fig. S8 (ESI†), 1 exhibited a type I adsorption isotherm and the maximal adsorption capacity Q0 derived from the Langmuir equation was ∼22.51 mg g−1.

Furthermore, 1 was used to separate the cationic MB dye from the dye mixtures. A further experiment showed that 1 can selectively adsorb MB, but there is no effective adsorption with the anionic dye MO and the neutral dye SDI (Fig. 6). For the mixtures of MB/MO and MB/SDI DMF solution with equivalent concentrations of 10 mg L−1, introduction of 5 mg of crystals 1 leads to prominent MB adsorption but very limited adsorption of MO and SDI. The cationic dye MB could be adsorbed from the dye mixtures, whereas the anionic MO and neutral SDI dye could not be absorbed and remained in the supernatants. This was also confirmed by the color change during dye capture. As explained previously, this phenomenon of selective adsorption of the cationic dye (MB) was mainly due to the anionic framework properties of 1; that is, only the cationic dye can be rapidly adsorbed into the channels of the MOFs by ion exchange with [(CH3)2NH2]+. Such a mechanism was also confirmed by the results of the dye release experiments. The dye molecules in MB@1 can be released in a DMF solution of NaCl but were hardly released at all in pure DMF (Fig. S9, ESI†). This means that the cationic MB in the MB@1 cannot be exchanged with the neutral DMF molecules but can be exchanged with the Na+ ions from NaCl. The role of NaCl in dye release was to provide Na+ ions to exchange with the cationic MB in MB@1. Considering charge conservation, it was confirmed that the cationic MB was indeed introduced into the 1via ion exchange with [(CH3)2NH2]+ in the original MOFs.56,57 Comparing the cationic MB molecular size with the pore sizes of 1, the MB molecules can be adsorbed into the pores of the 1. The PXRD analyses showed that the frameworks of 1 after the dye release experiments was stable (Fig. S4, ESI†). The reusability experiments of 1 for MB adsorption showed that 1 still exhibited a high adsorption capacity for MB after five cycles (Fig. S11, ESI†). The PXRD patterns indicated that the frameworks of 1 were basically unchanged after five adsorption/desorption cycles for MB (Fig. S4, ESI†). Therefore, the results demonstrated that 1 could be used to selectively absorb cationic dyes from dye mixtures and as a potential absorbent to remove cationic dye from effluents.

Also, a portion of the as-synthesized compound 2 (5 mg) was dispersed in a freshly formulated solution of 8 mL of DMF containing 10 mg L−1 of cationic MB at an ambient temperature. Compared with the adsorption of MB on 1, this showed a rapid change in color, and the MB solution gradually changed from blue to colorless at a much faster rate during this experiment (Fig. 7a). According to the UV-vis absorption spectra, the maximum peak at 664 nm had nearly disappeared after 3 h. For the MO and SDI adsorption, the results we obtained were similar to those for compound 1, because 2 also had a negatively charged framework. Under the same experimental conditions but with MO and SDI solutions instead of MB solution, only a slight decrease in the intensity of the signal was observed in the UV-vis absorption spectra at the maximum absorption peak of MO after 3 h, which indicated that compound 2 showed little or no adsorption of the MO and SDI molecules (Fig. 7b and c). As material 2 showed a better adsorption ability, it was further studied for use in the adsorption of mixtures of MB/MO and MB/SDI. According to the UV-vis spectra, it could be seen that in the first 3 h, 2 adsorbed 99% MB at a loading of 15.36 mg g−1 (Fig. 7d). As shown in Fig. S8 (ESI†), 2 also exhibited a type I adsorption isotherm and the maximal adsorption capacity on 2 was larger than that on 1, which was ∼26.24 mg g−1. Also, 2 exhibited good selectivity for MB absorption as described for 1 (Fig. 8). Dye release experiments showed that the dye molecules in MB@2 can also be released in a DMF solution of NaCl (Fig. S10, ESI†). Meanwhile, the reusability experiments of 2 for MB adsorption revealed that 2 still possessed a high adsorption capacity for MB after five cycles (Fig. S11, ESI†). The PXRD analyses showed that the frameworks of 2 were still relatively intact after five adsorption/desorption cycles for MB (Fig. S4, ESI†).

With regard to the dye adsorption performances reported in related research, the prominent dye selectivity and capacity of 1 and 2 further highlight the significance of the charge effect as well as the size-matching effect factors for performance optimization.58–61 The adsorption experiments clearly show that 2, which has the larger pore dimension and pore volume, has an excellent adsorption capacity relative to 1. In addition, the channels in 2 were decorated by uncoordinated carbonyl O atoms, which were also responsible for the superior adsorption capacity of MB.

Conclusions

In summary, two novel anionic MOFs with a 2-fold interpenetrating 3D framework were constructed from a bifunctional H3ITTC ligand. Compound 1 features an unprecedented 2-fold interpenetrated 3D network topology that contains 1D rectangular channels, whereas 2 displays a 2-fold interpenetrated 3,4-connected fcu topology structure with 1D regular hexagonal channels. Based on charge and size-matching effect, both compounds exhibit selective adsorption behavior for the cationic organic dye, MB. In particular, compound 2, in which the channels contain uncoordinated carbonyl O atoms, shows superior absorption and separation of the MB dye. Therefore, both mesoporous MOFs could be considered as promising materials to use to clean the environment by the simple physical adsorption of organic dyes. This work provides details for the construction of functional MOFs, which could probably be used in the field of organic dye absorption and separation.
