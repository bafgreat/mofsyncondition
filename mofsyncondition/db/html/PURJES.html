Microporous metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have attracted considerable attention in recent years due to their high thermal stability and porosity.1–3 Several research groups—including ours—have explored MOFs and related porous materials for selective gas separation, sensing, catalysis, and explosive detection.4–19 Separation of various gas mixtures has significant industrial application; for example carbon dioxide capture and separation has attracted much attention due to concerns over greenhouse gas emissions.20,21

Porous MOFs can be synthesised using multidentate ligands to obtain rigid frameworks; however, the final structural topology is highly influenced by several factors including metal–ligand ratio, solvent of crystallisation and metal oxidation states.1 However, predicting the final topology using flexible ligands is even more difficult because the existence of several supramolecular isomers, with little or no difference in energy, makes it difficult to predict and control the final outcome of crystallisation. Therefore the design and synthesis of such supramolecular isomers is still a challenging aspect of crystal engineering.22,23 Herein we report three porous metal–organic supramolecular isomers—PtS, diamondoid and Lonsdaleite networks—generated from a single building block (tetrakis[4-(carboxyphenyl)oxamethyl]methane, 1) with differences in solid-state packing, the amount of gas uptake, and selectivity toward other gasses. The three networks are shown as 2, 3 and 4 in Fig. 1, respectively.

Flexible tetrahedral organic linker 1 was obtained in two steps using a published procedure24 and was subsequently used as a building block for the generation of interpenetrated MOFs. Single crystals of 2 were successfully obtained by heating a dimethylformamide (DMF) solution containing 1 : 1 mixture of 1 and Zn(NO3)2·6H2O at (110 °C) for 24 h.†‡ Similarly, two additional supramolecular isomers were obtained by adding pyrazine and dabco during the crystallisation process to produce 3 and 4.‡ Notably, these isomers did not show the involvement of either additive in the zinc coordination modes in 3 and 4. All of the isomers were successfully synthesised and reproduced in bulk. Although the crystals remained stable for days to weeks, several attempts to collect single-crystal X-ray diffraction data on 2 using either Mo or Cu sources failed. Therefore, crystallographic measurements on 2 were performed using synchrotron radiation.‡ The asymmetric unit in 2 shows tetrahedral ligand 1 connected to two zinc atoms in a paddle-wheel fashion (Fig. 1). Structural analysis of 2 indicates that ordered and distorted paddle-wheel binuclear Zn2 units are pillared by water and DMF molecules to complete the three-dimensional (3-D) structure. The topology of 2 can be best described as a PtS net where three identical nets are interpenetrated to form a triply interpenetrating framework (Fig. 1 and ESI† ). The pores in 2 are occupied by both non-coordinated and coordinated DMF molecules. Similarly, secondary building units in 3 are generated using coordination modes shown in Fig. 1, and in which each tetrahedral ligand is connected to Zn2 clusters that, in turn, are connected to a DMF molecule to complete the coordination geometry around each zinc centre. Close scrutiny of 3 shows a two-fold interpenetrated hexagonal adamantanoid network filled with guest DMF and water molecules (Fig. 1 and see ESI† ), whereas the 3-D network in 4 shows a rare two-fold interpenetrated Lonsdaleite network structure (Fig. 1 and see ESI† ).25–27 Unlike in 2 and 3, four tetrahedral building blocks in 4 are connected to one zinc atom to result a 3D porous network filled with DMF and water molecules in the cavity.

Thermal analysis of 2–4 shows 15–30% of weight loss between room temperature and 200 °C, corresponding to the loss of DMF and water molecules from the host cavity (see ESI† ). To determine network stability following solvent removal, variable-temperature powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) measurements under vacuum were collected on all three materials. Variable-temperature PXRD on complex 3 shows considerable change in the powder pattern at high temperature (see ESI† ). This could be caused by framework contraction upon solvent removal, but no major changes were observed in PXRD patterns for complexes 2 and 4 (see ESI† ).

In order to elucidate the absorption and desorption kinetics of various gases, complexes 2–4 were activated separately overnight at 200 °C under vacuum and placed in a HPVA-100 volumetric analyzer for gas sorption experiments at room temperature. Approximately 100 mg of complex 2 was placed in a sample chamber and the volume adsorbed per gram of sample was plotted against the CO2 pressure (see ESI† ). At high pressure, the calculated weight percentage of CO2 was 6%, which is lower than for many reported MOFs. We propose that this may be attributable to the reduced pore size due to the triple interpenetration and simultaneous framework contraction upon solvent removal. However, it is important to note that despite the framework contraction and interpenetration, the non-porous form still sorbs 6 wt% of CO2, indicating a large accessible void space in the solid state.28,29

Similar gas sorption measurements were conducted on activated samples of 3 and 4 under identical conditions. At high pressure, samples 3 and 4 sorb 24% and 14 wt% of CO2, respectively (Fig. 2). It is noteworthy that the majority of MOFs and related systems studied to date have focused on achieving high mass loadings, while very few have dealt with gas selectivity.30 In this regard, 2–4 were explored further to identify the selectivity towards nitrogen, methane and hydrogen. Experiments with all pure gases on 2–4 at low pressures (1 bar) revealed that all appear to sorb very little or have no uptake of nitrogen, hydrogen and methane (Fig. 3). However, complexes 3 and 4 sorbed 7 wt% and 4 wt% of methane at high pressure (25 bar, Fig. 2). These results suggest that uptake of methane, particularly for complex 3, exceeds that of Zeolite 13X, but that CO2 uptake is lower than that reported for MOF-177 and activated carbon (MAXSORB).31,32 Selective sorption of CO2 over N2 in 2–4 might be attributable to the strong dipole–quadrupole interactions between the highly polarizing open-metal sites facing the cavity that can discriminate the gases, thereby presenting the potential for industrial application.28,31

As pointed out earlier, variable-temperature PXRD experiments on complex 3 shows considerable change in the powder patterns due to the framework contraction upon solvent removal. To test the breathing behaviour as noted in similar systems,4 high-pressure PXRD experiments were conducted on complex 3. Notably, the original phase cannot be generated either by simply returning to the original temperature, or exposing the material to high pressures of CO2 (see ESI† ).

In conclusion, we have successfully obtained three supramolecular isomers from one building block. These materials show markedly different gas sorption properties in the solid state and the potential to isolate and characterise more supramolecular isomers by simply varying the choice of pillar employed during crystallisation. Further studies are under way in order to understand the effect of solvent, temperature and additives on all three supramolecular isomers, and on related systems for selective separation of gases, including hydrocarbons.
