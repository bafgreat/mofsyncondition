Abstract

A series of coordination compounds [Cd2(trad)7Br2][Cd(trad)Br3]2 (1), [Cd3(trad)6{N(CN)2}4(H2O)2](N(CN)2)2 (2), [{Cd2(trad)5}{Cd(N(CN)2)6}]·3CH3OH (3), [{Cd3(trad)6(SeCN)2}{Cd(SeCN)4}2] (4), [Cd2(trad)3(NCS)4] (5), [Cd3(tr2ad)3(μ-NCS)3](NCS)3 (6), [Cd3(tr2ad)6](NO3)6·22H2O (7), [Cu3(tr2ad)4(SO4)(H2O)3](SO4)2·34H2O (8) and [Cu2(OH)(tr2ad)2](NO3)3·4H2O (9) (trad = 4-(adamantan-1-yl)-1,2,4-triazole; tr2ad = 1,3-bis(1,2,4-triazol-4-yl)-adamantane) revealed the potential of 1,2,4-triazolyl functionalized adamantanes for design of metal–organic polymers incorporating polynuclear coordination units as multiconnected nodes. Structures 1–5 are based upon characteristic di- and trinuclear clusters involving triple triazole bridges [M(μ2-trad)3M] (M = Cd), while doubling of the ligand functionality (tr2ad) allows integration of the clusters into 3D polymeric frameworks 6–9 (M = Cd, Cu).

Graphical abstract

A series of Cd and Cu(II) complexes involving mono- and bifunctional 1,2,4-triazol-4-yl substituted adamantane ligands reveal formation of bi- and trinuclear coordination units and their integration into the structures of coordination polymers by geometrically rigid adamantane-1,3-diyl spacers.

1. Introduction

The metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) incorporating secondary-building units (SBUs) as nodes have received a significant attention due to their exciting potential properties for applications [1]. The role of organic ligands as bridges between polymetallic centers is crucial, since the layout of the ligand encodes the MOF structure and properties through such factors as size of the tecton, its geometrical rigidity, ability for bridging of many metal ions, etc. In this context, 1,2,4-triazole derivatives possess many attractive attributes for the construction of the SBU containing polymers [2]. The 4-alkyl(aryl) substituted 1,2,4-triazoles commonly support short N1,N2-bridges suitable for binding two closely separated metal centers [3], thus providing formation of pinwheel-like clusters with the expected low nuclearity and rigid geometry: [M2(μ2-tr)3], [M3(μ2-tr)6] (M = Cd, Co, Cu, Ni) [2], [3], which solely may be viewed as the potentially multiconnected nodes for the extended coordination frameworks. In such a way, the ligands combining several triazole functions (cf. 4,4′-bitriazole [4], [5], bitriazolyl ethylene [6], 1,4-phenylene [7], etc.) allow construction of the polynuclear coordination core and its integration into a polymer. Recently, we have demonstrated the utility of such approach for the related Cu(II)/4,4′-bitriazole/X− (X = Cl, Br, OH) systems, in which the halogenide and hydroxide anions behave as co-ligands in the linear [Cu3(μ2-Cl)2]4+ [8] and triangular Cu3(μ3-OH)5+ [4] SBUs whereas the bitriazole molecules link them into eight-connected networks.

As especially attractive and promising spacer for the developing of multivalent triazole building blocks we consider rigid adamantane skeleton, which offers access to a series of di-, tri- and tetrasubstituted tectons by functionalization at four available bridgehead positions [9] (Scheme 1). Unlike geometrically flexible aliphatic analogs, the adamantane scaffold provides constrained and proper binding directions, while aliphatic nature of the spacer is important for prevention of multiple CH/π and π/π stacking interactions, which commonly dominate structure of coordination polymers based upon lengthy aromatic ligands [10] and led to closely packed patterns instead of the extended framework architectures. Thus the adamantane-supported platform was successfully utilized for the construction of Cu(II) and Zn-carboxylates [11], Cu(II) phosphonates [12] and Ag(I)-sulfonate [13] coordination polymers. However, the use of adamantane-based polynucleating nitrogen ligands is very limited [14], in particular due to synthetic difficulties of such functionalization.

In this paper we introduce a series of Cd(II) and Cu(II) complexes based upon the adamantane-supported core bearing mono- and bitriazolyl functionalities. The prototypal monofunctional trad is a suitable model system for preparation of simple di- or trinuclear complexes, while doubling of the number of triazole donor functions gives the opportunity for incorporation of the clusters into MOFs. On the other hand the integration of the clusters in the polymeric structure could be influenced by the use of nucleophilic anions, e.g. halogenides and pseudohalogenides, which offer wide possibilities for supporting additional connection within the cluster as well as for binding the clusters together, thus increasing the connectivity in the polymer.

2. Results and discussion

Examination of the model monofunctional adamantane trad revealed formation of multiple triazole-bridged di- and trinuclear clusters. Even the presence of nucleophilic anions, such as Br−, NCS−, NCSe− and N(CN)2-, does not mitigate against the organization of the polynuclear core that is a salient and well-predictable feature of the system [3]. In most cases, the coordination of the triazole ligand leads to a complete exclusion of the nucleophilic anion from the inner coordination sphere (Scheme 2).

This situation is best illustrated by prototypal structure of the bromide [Cd2(trad)7Br2][Cd(trad)Br3]2 (1), which consists of dinuclear cationic clusters together with simpler anionic species, placed loosely in the lattice (Fig. 1, Table 1). Such co-existence could be an evidence of easy structural transformation between mono- and binuclear trz-complexes. The structure of the [Cd2(trad)7Br2]2+ unit is built around three bridging η2-trz groups (“triple triazole bridge”) connecting two Cd1 ions. The octahedral environment of the latter is completed by two monodentate triazoles and one Br− ion. The [Cd(trad)Br3]− anion displays a distorted tetrahedral coordination around Cd2, which is completed by a monodentate trad and three bromide ions (Cd–N 2.260(5); Cd–Br 2.5338(10)–2.5802(8) Å). This structurally simple complex, however, is unprecedented for the triazole ligands. The combination of discrete mono- and polynuclear 1,2,4-triazole species in the lattice is also uncommon and it was previously observed in a couple cases, such as Fe(NCS)2 complex with 4-tolyltriazole [15].

Comparable polynuclear units could be found also in the structure of dicyanamide compounds, which demonstrate two distinct modes of organization. In [Cd3(trad)6{N(CN)2}4(H2O)2](N(CN)2)2 (2), two triple-bridged fragments, seen in the above structure, share Cd1 ion and thus generate even more complicated trinuclear core. This central Cd1 ion is situated on a center of inversion and has an octahedral coordination environment consisting of six triazole-N donors. The environment of the two side Cd2 ions is completed by water molecules and two monodentate N(CN)2- ligands (Fig. 1, Table 1), which are unusual for cadmium dicyanamide complexes with non-chelating co-ligands [16]. The additional dicyanamide anions are eliminated from the metal environment forming a pair of hydrogen bonds –CN⋯HO with the coordinated water molecules (N⋯O 2.786(4) and 2.951(4) Å). This is contrary to a series of related compounds with 4-heteroaryl substituted 1,2,4-triazoles, in which the competition between the triazole and dicyanamide ligands led to monodentate coordination of triazole [17]. The two-dimensional structure of [{Cd2(trad)5}{Cd(N(CN)2)6}]·3CH3OH (3) (Fig. 2) is constituted by the basic dinuclear [Cd2(μ-trad)3]4+ units, which coordinate two additional monodentate trad ligands and also some of the outer nitrile nitrogen atoms of four octahedral [Cd(N(CN)2)6]4− ions. These dicyanamide bridges provide connection of the anions to four closest dicadmium clusters, while the other two trans-situated N(CN)2- anions are terminal. Thus, both of the alternating structural elements, [Cd2(trad)5]4+ and Cd(N(CN)2)6]4−, display the same connectivity of four, and they could be regarded as square-planar net nodes of a planar (4, 4) net. The “pseudohalometallate” [Cd(N(CN)2)6]4− itself is quite unusual and related structural fragments was found previously only in polymeric [Cd(N(CN)2)3]-n anion [18] and in mixed-pseudohalogenide complex [Cd(N(CN)2)4 (NCS)2]4− [19].

The structure of selenocyanate [{Cd3(trad)6(SeCN)2}{Cd(SeCN)4}2] (4) reveals formation of trinuclear cationic clusters and complex cadmium/pseudohalide anions as two alternating parts of the coordination network. In this sense the structure bears a close resemblance to both dicyanamides 2, 3 and combines their main features. Similarly to 2, the trinuclear cationic cluster [Cd3(η2-trz)6(SeCN)2]4+ is supported by two triple triazole bridges, the central Cd1 ion is situated on an inversion center and has octahedral N6 coordination, while the outer Cd2 ions coordinate three trad ligands and terminal SeCN-groups (Cd–Se 2.815(3) Å) (Fig. 3, Table 2). The tetrahedral Cd(SeCN)42- anions act as bitopic connectors between Cd2 centers separated by 10.22 Å. This affords the extended square grid network utilizing the trinuclear clusters as net nodes and the Cd(SeCN)42- ions as links.

The substitution of selenocyanate for thiocyanate has a pronounced significance for the entire structure, while preserving main subtle features of cadmium/triazole coordination. Thus the one-dimensional structure of [Cd2(trad)3(NCS)2(μ-NCS)2] (5) also includes the characteristic triple-bridged binuclear unit and may be represented as alternation of triple triazole (Cd–N 2.34–2.37 Å) and double NCS-N,N-bridges along a chain of Cd ions separated by 3.68 and 4.05 Å (Fig. 4). Additional NCS− ions are terminal and they complete the octahedral environment of metal ions as well as contribute to the commonly encountered fragment [Cd2(μ-NCS)2(NCS)2] [20], [21]. This could be compared with actually the same supramolecular motif in cadmium thiocyanate complex with 4-t-butyl-1,2,4-triazole [21] and illustrates the possibility for extension of a polynuclear triazole array by employing short anionic bridges.

It is worth noting, however, that neither of the compounds 1–5 demonstrates interplay and synergy of triazole and anionic bridges: in all the cases the basic [Cd(μ-tr)3Cd] or [Cd(μ-tr)3Cd(μ-tr)3Cd] units remain intact and they appear clearly distinguishable motif of the structure. This observation is important for rational design of complicated metal–organic frameworks, which may be anticipated for the related bi- and polyfunctional tectons as linkers between the polynuclear metal/triazole clusters. The prospects for synthesis of such compounds are revealed by structures 6–9 based upon bifunctional adamantane tr2ad.

New coordination motif in the structure of [Cd3(tr2ad)3(μ-NCS)3](NCS)3 (6) may be regarded as the only and very illustrative exception from the general scheme since it adopts the infinite cadmium/triazole linkage dominated by heteroligand triple bridges (Fig. 5, Table 3), unlike prototypal complex with a simpler 4,4′-bitriazole ligand [22]. Thus the framework consists of infinite [Cd(μ-tr)2(μ-NCS)] chains running along b direction in the crystal. This motif is new, while combination of the double triazole and NCS− bridges is known for discrete di- and trinuclear species and either NCS-N,N or -S,S functions of the ambifunctional anion are possible [23]. Within the chain the adjacent metal ions are linked by a combined bridge of two triazoles and a thiocyanate, while the adamantane-1,3-diyl spacers connect this array into a 2D net (Fig. 5). Angular configuration of the organic tectons led to tubular structure and the resulting channels along b axis provide space for the placement of non-coordinated disordered NCS− anions. Similarly to 5, most of the coordinated thiocyanates in the structure are found as N,N-bridges, though one of two unique coordination chains is based upon alternation of N,N- and equally disordered N,N-/S,S-NCS bridges. In the latter case the bridged cadmium ions are slightly more distal (3.822 vs. 3.759 Å).

A remarkably different situation is realized in the structures of [Cd3(tr2ad)6](NO3)6·22H2O (7) and [Cu3(tr2ad)4(SO4)(H2O)3](SO4)2·34H2O (8), which closely correspond to polynuclear motifs in 1–4 and suggest the utility of the characteristic metal/triazole oligomers as secondary-building blocks for the framework structure. In the nitrate 7, the basic unit of the MOF is represented by a centrosymmetric trinuclear [Cd3(μ3-tr2ad)6]6+ cluster inherited from the above trad model type with two triple triazole bridges and two sets of monodentate triazole-N donors coordinated to the side Cd2 ions (Fig. 6). Thus all present organic ligands are tridentate and uniform in their function, connecting pairs of neighboring tricadmium nodes in 3D network. Weak nucleophilicity of the nitrate counter anions facilitates the formation of an arrangement containing 12 triazolyl groups around the Cd3 core. Such behavior potentially could result in organization of a network with extremely high connectivity (up to 12). However, the angular configuration of the organic tectons enforces the double connection of the nodes by pairs of the bitriazoles that reduces the entire connectivity of the network down to six, similarly to cadmium complex with 2,6-(1,2,4-triazol-4-yl)pyridine [24], which is the only known precedent for the maximum possible 12-triazole environment of the trinuclear complex. The “twelve-arm” [Cd3(μ3-tr2ad)6]6+ clusters in the bc plane are connected with each of four neighbors by pair of bitriazole ligands generating a planar square grid subtopology, while two other pairs of tr2ad linkers provide connection in the third direction thus setting up a primitive cubic net.

The employing of trinuclear cluster approach as a basic concept for preparation of the triazole-based MOF could be extended to the copper(II) complexes. Indeed, the structure of the sulfate complex 8 contains the desired Cu3 SBU motif, which possesses an asymmetric arrangement. The cluster consists of three sorts of copper(II) atoms in different coordination environments (Fig. 7, Table 4). The central Cu(2) atom adopts a typical distorted octahedral coordination CuN6(trz) [Cu–N 1.992(4)–2.053(4) Å (equatorial) and 2.250(4), 2.296(4) Å (axial)], however for the peripheral copper atoms the corresponding distortion is more significant. In the equatorial plane, the coordination around both Cu1 and Cu3 ions is constituted by three triazole nitrogen atoms and a water molecule, while the axial positions are occupied by triazole nitrogens and oxygen atoms of water (Cu3) or very distal sulfate group (Cu1–O7 2.80 Å). In total, the Cu3 core accommodates six bidentate and two monodentate triazole groups. They correspond to two sorts of the organic bridges: μ3- and μ4-tr2ad, which are present in a ratio of 1:1. The eightfold triazole environment of Cu3 core was observed for the nitrate complex with 4-(2-pyridyl)triazole [25], however the two related systems exhibit even higher coordination [26]. The μ4-ligands bridge the neighboring trinuclear clusters at distances of 12.02–13.75 Å in the bc plane, while further connection occurs in the orthogonal direction by pairs of tridentate tr2ad molecules, exactly in the same fashion like the one seen in structure 7. Correspondingly, the entire topology remains intact and could be regarded as a 3D primitive cubic net.

Further evolution of the structural type can be seen in the nitrate [Cu2(μ-OH)(tr2ad)2](NO3)3·4H2O (9) (Table 4), which includes the bitriazole bridged trinuclear units as a subtopology of 3D framework. Following this scheme, each unit accommodates eight triazole groups and is linked with four closest neighbors by means of a pair of tr2ad tectons, similarly to structures 7 and 8. Resulting 2D four-connected nets (internodal distance is 12.35 Å) are parallel to the bc plane and are linked in the third direction by hydroxo bridges and additional Cu1 ions (Fig. 8). The combination of triazole and μ2-OH bridges between the copper(II) ions has only one structural precedent [27]. The characteristic trinuclear Cu/triazole clusters appear to be incorporated into an infinite coordination bonded chain along a axis. The latter is formed by alternation of η2-triazole triple bridges (Cu2⋯Cu3 3.8202(7) Å) and combined triazole/hydroxo bridges (Cu1⋯Cu2 3.3957(6) Å) uniting copper ions along a chain. Side Cu2 ion of the Cu3 core as well as Cu1 ion of the hydroxo fragment are accessible for additional interactions and additionally coordinate a very distal water molecule and nitrate anions (Cu–O 2.702(10), 2.768(12) Å).

In these structures, the non-coordinated anions (NO3- or SO42-) are housed inside the large crystal cavities providing their strong hydrophilic nature. As a result, the frameworks enclathrate a large amount of solvate water molecules (up to 34 per a single trinuclear cluster in 8), which are involved into an extensive hydrogen bonding. Among a number of weak interactions in the structures, the most notable is the very unusual stacking between the non-coordinated sulfate anion and triazole ring in 8 (Fig. 7). This may be attributed to anion/π interactions, similar to those recently documented for a set of most electron deficient heterocycles, such as 1,2,4,5-tetrazines and 1,3,5-triazines [28]. Thus the oxygen atom O8 is situated almost exactly above the ring centroid, with very short interatomic contacts O⋯C/N at 2.86–3.03 Å and the O⋯π separation amounting a record value of 2.736(6) Å [29].

3. Experimental

3.1. Preparation of the ligands

All chemicals were of reagent grade and used as received without further purification. 1,3-Bis(acetylamino)adamantane was readily available as a product of a Ritter reaction between 1,3-dibromoadamantane and acetonitrile [30]. The triazole ligands were prepared by an acid-catalyzed condensation of the corresponding amines and N,N-dimethylformamide azine [31] in yields of 52% and 64%, respectively.

3.1.1. 1,3-Diaminoadamantane

A solution of 1,3-bis(acetylamino)adamantane (8.87 g, 35.4 mmol) in a mixture of 80 mL conc. HCl and 60 mL water was refluxed for 23 h. 3.3. Crystallography

Crystallographic measurements were made using Stoe Image Plate Diffraction System (1–5, numerical absorption correction using X-RED and X-SHAPE) and Siemens SMART CCD area-detector diffractometer (6–9, empirical absorption corrections using SADABS) (graphite monochromated Mo Kα radiation, λ = 0.71073 Å). The structures were solved by direct methods and refined in anisotropic approximation using shelxs-97 and shelxl-97 [32] (Table 5). In structures 1–5, some of the adamantyl groups show typical “rotational” disorder. In most cases the disordered parts were refined anisotropically without restraints, while for 2 the disorder was modeled with a set of soft restraints for C–C bond lengths and thermal parameters. In 5, terminal NCSe− groups are disordered and they were refined with fixed geometry. For 8, two of three present non-coordinated SO42- ions were refined with restrained anisotropic thermal values. In this structure some of the non-coordinated water molecules are badly disordered and therefore the electron density corresponding to eight (out of 34 independent) water molecules was modeled using Squeeze routine. It was not possible to locate hydrogen atoms of the solvate water. For 9, thermal displacement parameters for the non-coordinated NO3- ions and water molecules are appreciably higher than those of the rest of the structure and one of the nitrates is disordered over two equally populated overlapping positions. A stable model was obtained applying a set of restraints in geometry and in anisotropic thermal parameters. In the structures 2 and 8, hydrogen atoms of coordinated water molecules were located and then constrained with Uiso = 1.5Ueq (O). In all structures, CH hydrogens were idealized with isotropic U values at 1.2 times of equivalent isotropic U value of the corresponding carbon atom.

4. Conclusions

Our study demonstrates new and potentially fruitful design principles for developing multivalent molecular building blocks for the synthesis of polynuclear and polymeric metal complexes and shows a convenient approach for heteroaryl-functionalization of the adamantane platform. The proposed triazole derivatives offer a range of possibilities for generation of polynuclear metal clusters and their incorporation into metal–organic frameworks. Even wider possibilities may be expected for a family of tri- and tetrafunctional building blocks as well as for polyfunctionalized derivatives of polyadamantane series, such as 1,1′-biadamantane. We are currently elaborating synthesis of polyvalent adamantane tectons and metal–organic framework solids thereof.
