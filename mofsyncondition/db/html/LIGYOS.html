Introduction

In order to meet the development of green energy, proton exchange membrane fuel cells (PEMFCs) have drawn extensive attention.1–5 Furthermore, proton conducting materials play critical roles in achieving good performance for PEMFCs. In contrast to other proton conducting materials, such as polymers,6 carbon-based materials,7 solid acids,8 and perovskite oxides,9,10 metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have tunable structural features and high chemical stability.11,12 To date, there are two prevailing approaches to improve the proton conductivity of MOFs. One is to directly import the proton carriers into the pores to act as counterions (e.g., NH4+, H3O+, Me2NH2+, HSO4−)13–24 and to adapt the application to higher temperatures (e.g., imidazole, 1,2,4-triazole, H2SO4, H3PO4).25–33 And the other is to modify the organic ligands by replacing their functional groups with hydrophilic groups such as –NH2, –COOH, –OH, –SO3H and –PO3H2,34–37 which are for the enhancement of their acidity and hydrophilicity. According to a previous report, the latter method is more significant for improving proton conductivity due to chemical stability as well as structural diversity.11 Various MOFs have already been reported to display higher proton conductivity, such as aryl polysulfonate,34 polyphosphonate,38,39 polycarboxylic40,41 and polyhydroxy MOFs.20

Compared with other aromatic ligands (e.g., aryl polysulfonate, polyphosphonate), aromatic carboxylates are easily acquired and possess a rich variety of coordination modes, which interact with metal centers to form stably multidimensional frameworks.35,42 Moreover, these oxygen atoms may contribute to the formation of hydrogen bond networks that can provide an efficient pathway for proton transfer. Compared to transition metal-based metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), lanthanide–metal organic frameworks (Ln–MOFs) as proton conducting materials have received more and more attention recently owing to their fascinating features. For example, lanthanide ions possess higher coordination numbers and flexible coordination geometries to form appealing structural topologies, which may form more hydrogen bond networks and porous hydrophilic channels contributing to the proton transfer.43–45

To fulfill the application of MOFs in fuel cells, it is very indispensable to add them into membranes. In the last few years, composite membrane-based MOFs have been studied. Composite membrane JUC-200@PVA-10 has been prepared and shows an upper proton conductivity reaching up to 1.25 × 10−3 S cm−1 at 323 K in 100% RH.38 Composite membrane MOF-808@PVDF-55 has been fabricated and exhibits good mechanical properties and durability for practical application. In addition, it displays a medium proton conductivity up to 1.56 × 10−4 S cm−1 at 338 K in 100% RH.46

Herein, using a bipyridine-carboxylate ligand (Scheme S1, ESI†) and lanthanide metal ions (Eu3+, Tb3+), two novel Ln–MOFs have been constructed and structurally characterized. Two complexes have similar three-dimensional (3D) porous frameworks structured by hydrophilic one-dimensional (1D) rod-shaped homometallic Ln1–Ln2–Ln3 secondary building units and two types of irregular 1D channels. AC impedance analysis displays that 1 and 2 have high proton conductivity at moderate temperature and 98% RH, which are comparable to other reported proton conducting Ln–MOF materials under the same conditions. In order to apply the proton-conductive material in fuel cells, it is necessary to make it into a membrane. Poly(vinyl alcohol) (PVA) as a hygroscopic polymer can effectively absorb the water molecules and serve as linkers among the MOF particles to form the proton transfer channels, thus, the proton conductivity of composite membranes would be improved. In this work, PVA was used to combine with 1 and 2 to fabricate polymer composite membranes (denoted as 1@PVA-X and 2@PVA-X, X is the mass percentage of 1 or 2). As we expected, the measurement of proton conductivity on these composite membranes displays that the proton conductivities of both 1@PVA-10 and 2@PVA-10 are higher than those of 1 and 2 under the same conditions.

Experimental section

Physical measurements

The infrared spectra were recorded on a Bruker Tensor 27 Fourier transform infrared spectrometer in the 4000–500 cm−1 region, using KBr pellets. 1H NMR spectra were recorded on a Bruker AVANCE III 400 M in CDCl3 and DMSO-d6. Elemental analyses of C, H and N were carried out on a Model 240 Perkin-Elmer elemental analyzer. The X-ray powder diffraction patterns (PXRD) were recorded on a Shimadzu Labx XRD-6100 diffractometer, operated at 40 kV and 30.0 mA, using a Cu target tube and graphite monochromator. The intensity data were recorded by continuous scan in 2θ/θ mode from 5° to 50° with a step size of 0.02° and a scan speed of 5° min−1. Thermogravimetry analysis (TGA) was performed using an SDT-Q600 at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 in the temperature range of 25–800 °C under a nitrogen atmosphere. The electron micrographs of the composite membrane were obtained using a field-emission scanning electron microscope (SEM, JSM 6380LV). The electro-chemical impedance spectra (EIS) were recorded on a CHI650E electrochemical workstation by applying an alternating current amplitude voltage of 0.01 V over the frequency range from 1 to 106 Hz. The impedance results were analyzed using Zview3.1 software.

Chemicals

All metal salts and organic solvents used in the experiment were of reagent-grade. The molecular weight of PVA is 105 000. The ligand 4,4-(4,4′-bipyridine-2,6-dily) dibenzoic (bpydbH2) was synthesized according to the literature.47

Preparation of 1

A mixture containing bpydbH2 (0.04 g, 0.1 mmol), EuCl3·6H2O (0.0366 g, 0.1 mmol), 10 mL DMF, 2 mL EtOH, and 2 mL H2O was placed in a Teflon-lined autoclave, heated to 160 °C for 84 h under autogenous pressure, and then cooled to room temperature at a rate of 3 °C h−1. The block-shaped yellow crystals were collected and washed three times with acetone, and finally dried in a vacuum oven (yield: 36% based on bpydbH2). Elemental anal. calc. for C85H77N10O22Eu3: C, 49.84; N, 6.84; H, 3.76%. Found: C, 49.80; N, 6.81; H, 3.73%. FTIR (KBr, cm−1): 1674(m), 1591(m), 1545(m), 1403(vs), 1088(w), 1014(w), 868(w), 817(m), 704(w), 649(w).

2 was synthesized using the same procedure with TbCl3·6H2O instead of EuCl3·6H2O (yield: 40%). Anal. calcd. for C85H75N10O21Tb3 (Mr: 2049.34): C 49.77; N, 6.83; H, 3.66%. Found: C, 49.72; N, 6.80; H, 3.62%. IR (KBr, cm−1): 1672(w), 1594(s), 1519(m), 1409(s), 1327(m), 1089(w), 862(w), 808(m), 702(w).

Synthesis of composite membranes

The conventional solution casting method was performed to prepare the composite membranes containing different contents of 1 or 2 particles (5%, 10%, or 15% wt). 0.5 g PVA was dissolved in 8 mL boiling water under stirring. The 25–75 mg micro-sized 1 or 2 powders was added into the clear PVA solution, and the mixture was stirred vigorously for 3 h at room temperature. After degasification, the above homogenous solution was cast onto a clear glass plate and dried at room temperature for 48 h. A series of composite membranes are designated as 1@PVA-X or 2@PVA-X representing 1 or 2 as the filler, in which X (5%, 10%, or 15%) is the weight percentage ratio of the 1 or 2 micro-sized powder to PVA. The film thicknesses were about 0.1 mm.

X-ray crystallographic study

Diffraction intensity data for 1 and 2 were collected on a Rigaku Saturn CCD diffractometer at 120 K using graphite-monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) with the ω-scan technique. Lorentz polarization and absorption corrections were also applied. The structures were solved by direct methods and refined by full matrix least-squares on F2 employing SHELXS-97 and SHELXL-97 programs.48,49 The nonhydrogen atoms were located in successive difference Fourier syntheses and refined with anisotropic thermal parameters on F2. Hydrogen atoms were generated theoretically at the specific atoms and refined isotropically with fixed thermal factors. The crystal data and details of structural determination refinement are presented in Table S1.† Selected bond lengths and angles of 1 and 2 are listed in Table S2 (ESI†).

Proton conductivity measurement

The alternating current (AC) impedance spectroscopy measurements were performed over a frequency range between 1 MHz to 1 Hz with an alternating current amplitude voltage of 0.01V. Slice samples of 1 and 2 of 6 mm in diameter and 0.3–0.5 mm in thickness were prepared by pressing the powder material (about 150 mg) at 6 MPa. Impedance spectra of 1 and 2 were obtained at different humidities and temperatures. The impedance spectra of composite membranes were acquired at different humidities. The humidity was controlled by different saturated aqueous salt solutions, which were salt solutions of K2SO4 (∼98% RH), KNO3 (∼93% RH), ZnSO4 (∼90% RH), KCl (∼86% RH), and (NH4)2SO4 (∼81% RH). The AC impedance spectroscopy measurements were performed after the measuring cell with the samples placed at 81%–98% RH about three days. The sample pellets were covered with carbon paint to improve the contact with electrodes. The proton conductivities of the samples were calculated from the impedance values by applying the following equation σ = d/SR,32 in which σ is the conductivity (S cm−1), d is the thickness (cm) of the slice sample, S denotes the electrode area (cm2) and R stands for the impedance (Ω).

Results and discussion

Crystal structure

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analyses indicate that 1 and 2 are isomorphous and crystallize in the monoclinic space group P21/n (Table S1†). It is worth noting that there are solvent molecules including DMF and water in 1 and 2.

The structural analyses reveal that 1 and 2 are similar, thus herein only the crystal structure of 1 is represented. In the asymmetric unit, there are three Eu3+ ions, three bpydb2− ligands, two μ3-OH groups, one formate ion and one coordinated DMF molecule (Fig. 1a). It is worth mentioning that the formate ion can be generated by hydrolysis of DMF under solvothermal conditions.50,51 Both Eu1 and Eu2 are eight-coordinated in a double capped triangular prism coordination geometry (Fig. S1†). Eu3 is nine-coordinated in a monocapped square antiprism with O4 at the capped position coordination geometry (Fig. S1†). The bpydb2− ligand bridges four Eu3+ ions through its one η2-O atom and three unidentate O atoms (μ4-η2:η1:η1:η1) (Fig. S2†). Notably, Eu1, Eu2 and Eu3 are connected by O13 to form a trinuclear {Eu3} cluster. Besides, two Eu1 are linked with Eu3 by O14 to form another trinuclear {Eu3} cluster. Moreover, two kinds of trinuclear {Eu3} clusters alternate connection to form a hexanuclear {Eu6} cluster by carboxylate oxygen atoms and μ3-O atoms. The two adjacent {Eu6} clusters are linked by two O15 atoms from a formate ion to form 1D rod-shaped secondary building units (SUBs) running along the a axis (Fig. 1b). The adjoining 1D rod-shaped SUBs are connected by the bpydb2− ligand to form a 3D porous framework (Fig. 1c), in which there are two types of irregular 1D channels along the a direction (Fig. S3†). The total solvent-accessible volume of 1 was calculated to be 23.7% using the PLATON program.52 From a topological perspective, if each Eu hexamer is regarded as a 12-connected node, the structure of 1 can be simplified as a 12-connected fcu topology (Fig. 1d) with a Schläfli symbol of 324·436·56, which is still uncommon.53

Furthermore, there are two interesting features in 1 and 2. The first feature is that μ3-OH can provide extra protons, which can move as solvated H+ along the 1D rod-shaped hydrophilic chain under high humidity. The second feature is the existence of hydrogen-bonding interactions in the cavity. For 1, there are four types of hydrogen-bonding interactions: O–H⋯O interactions between μ3-OH and an oxygen atom of the formate ion, O–H⋯O interactions between μ3-OH and lattice water molecules, O–H⋯O interactions between carboxyl oxygen atoms and lattice water molecules, and O–H⋯O interactions between carboxyl oxygen atoms and μ3-OH (Fig. 1e). For 2, there are three types of hydrogen-bonding interactions: O–H⋯O interactions between a hydrogen atom of the benzene ring and carboxyl oxygen atoms, O–H⋯O interactions between μ3-OH and carboxyl oxygen atoms, and O–H⋯O interactions between μ3-OH and an oxygen atom of the formate ion (Fig. S4e†). In addition, a continuous hydrogen bonding chain exists in 2 among the carboxyl oxygen atoms, hydrogen atom of the benzene ring, carboxyl oxygen atoms and μ3-OH. The existence of extra protons as proton carriers and hydrogen-bonding chains as proton-conducting pathways suggests that 1 and 2 are likely potential candidates for proton conducting materials. The hydrogen bond lengths and angles of 1 and 2 are listed in Table S3.† The crystal structures of 2 are shown in Fig. S4.†

TGA and PXRD patterns

The TGA results indicate that there is an obvious weight loss of about 14.9% (the calculated value is 16.1%) for 1 and 13.7% (the calculated value is 15.1%) for 2 from 25 °C to 350 °C, which is attributed to the loss of free water and DMF solvent molecules as well as coordinated DMF molecules. After a platform, another weight loss is also observed starting from about 560 °C, which may be the collapse of the coordination framework (Fig. S5†).

To corroborate the phase purity and stability of the samples at high humidity, PXRD has been carried out. The analysis results reveal that the PXRD patterns of 1 and 2 are in good agreement with the results simulated from the single crystal data at room temperature, respectively, indicating the phase purity of the powder samples. Under 98% RH, the PXRD patterns of the samples are consistent with the results simulated, revealing that the powder samples are stable at high humidity (Fig. 2).

Morphology of composite membranes

The morphology of the 1@PVA-X and 2@PVA-X composite membranes was observed by SEM (Fig. 3 and S6†). The crystals were relatively evenly distributed in the membranes. With increasing the contents of 1 or 2, more close packing of the crystals was clearly observed and the composite membranes gradually became less smooth. When the mass fraction of 1 or 2 increased up to 15%, the membrane became extremely unsmooth and crevices appeared on the membrane surfaces (Fig. S7†). This phenomenon was quite normal. Increasing the amount of the inorganic phase in the mixed matrix membrane would lead to brittle and less flexible composite membranes.

Proton conductivity properties of 1 and 2

MOFs with hydrophilic groups are often used as proton-conductive materials at high humidity.36,54 Considering the structure features including extra protons and hydrogen-bonding chains, these compounds can be potentially proton-conductive materials. In general, the proton conductivity properties of MOFs are affected by two main factors: relative humidity (RH) and temperature.55,56 The proton conductivities of 1 and 2 were investigated by the AC impedance method using compacted slices of the powdered sample.

For 1, the Nyquist plots of the slice samples were obtained at room temperature under different relative humidities (RH). At room temperature under 98% RH, the Nyquist plot shows a semicircle in the high-frequency region and an inclined tail in the low-frequency region, which is an intrinsic feature of a proton conductor, and displays two distinct charge transport regimes (Fig. 4a).14,34 The semicircle corresponds to the bulk resistance, which is due to the gain interior contribution, and the inclined tail corresponds to the stacking of protons at the electrodes.57 With the relative humidity increasing, the size of semicircles obviously decrease in the Nyquist plots, indicating that the proton conductivity of 1 is highly humidity-dependent and increases with increasing humidity (Fig. S8†).

In Fig. 4b, the proton conductivity of 1 is 1.1 × 10−5 S cm−1 under 98% RH, which is approximately two orders of magnitude higher than the value obtained under 81% RH at room temperature. The conductivity at 298 K under 98% RH for 1 is comparable to those of other Ln–MOFs, such as the Gd-compound with two different types of hydrophilic channels formed by adsorbed water molecules with coordinated water molecules as well as with carboxylic oxygen (2.37 × 10−6 S cm−1 at 298 K under 95% RH) and the Dy-compound (1.84 × 10−6 S cm−1 at 298 K under 95% RH),58 and is slightly lower than that of the Gd-compound (6.3 × 10−5 S cm−1 at 298 K under 97% RH).59 In order to investigate the effect of temperature on proton conductivity, the Nyquist plots were obtained at different temperatures under 98% RH. From 298 K to 325 K, with increasing temperature, the sizes of semicircles also obviously decrease, revealing the temperature dependence of the conductivity of 1 as well as an obvious increase in the conductivity with increasing temperature (Fig. 4c). The values of proton conductivity reaches up to 1.7 × 10−4 S cm−1 at 325 K, which is about one order of magnitude higher than the value obtained at 298 K. To obtain more insights into the proton-transport information, the value of proton-transport activation energy Ea is calculated using the formula (eqn (1)) derived from the Arrhenius equation, in which kB is the Boltzmann constant (J K−1), Ea is the proton-transport activation energy (eV), σ represents the proton conductivity (S cm−1), T denotes the test temperature (K), and A is the pre-exponential factor. (1)

The value of activation energy Ea is 0.63 eV from the linear fitting of the Arrhenius plot of ln(σT) against 1000/T (Fig. 4d). The value exceeds the range for the Grotthuss mechanism (Ea < 0.4 eV),60 in other words, the vehicle mechanism (Ea > 0.4 eV) could operate for the proton transport of 1.61 The hydrophilic sites, including the coordination formate ion and μ3-OH, present in the porous channel could absorb water molecules, which can move as solvated H+. In a word, the solvated water uptake gives rise to vehicle-type proton transport.34 Specifically, a comparison of other similar reported Ln–MOFs is listed in Table S4.†

For 2, the Nyquist plots have also been measured at room temperature under different relative humidities (RH). The size of semicircles obviously tapers with increasing relative humidity (Fig. 5a and S9†). The σ values are 2.1 × 10−8 S cm−1 (81%), 2.5 × 10−8 S cm−1 (86%), 4.3 × 10−8 S cm−1 (90%), 5.8 × 10−7 S cm−1 (93%), and 1.1 × 10−6 S cm−1 (98%) at 298 K (Fig. 5b). Interestingly, comparing the proton conductivity of 1 with 2 under the same conditions, the σ values of 1 are approximately one order of magnitude higher than those of 2. The reason may be the presence of more lattice water molecules in 1. Otherwise, to explore the effect of temperature on proton conductivity, the Nyquist plots have been obtained from 298 K to 334 K under 98% RH. The size of semicircles is also distinctly decreased along with increasing temperatures (Fig. 5c). The value of proton conductivity attains 1.1 × 10−4 S cm−1 at 334 K, which is two orders of magnitude higher than that obtained at 298 K. The activation energy (Ea) of 2 is estimated from the impedance spectra obtained at 98% RH between 298 K and 322 K to be 0.48 eV (Fig. 5d). This suggests that the proton transport process of 2 also follows the vehicle mechanism. Notably, the activation energy (Ea) of 2 is lower than that of 1. The reason may be the existence of the successive hydrogen bonding chains in 2, which tend to obey the Grotthuss mechanism.60 In other words, the two mechanisms may exist in 2 simultaneously. However, the vehicle mechanism may play a certain promotion function.

Proton conduction of composite membranes

We also investigated the proton conduction properties of 1@PVA and 2@PVA composite membranes by measuring their AC impedance spectra under different relative humidities. The proton conductivities of the composite membranes are higher than those of 1 or 2 under the same conditions (Fig. 6). This can be interpreted that the PVA polymer can provide a continuous proton transfer pathway for 1 and 2. The proton conductivities of composite membranes obviously increase with the 1 or 2 added from 5% to 10% under 98% RH (Fig. 6b and d). It can be observed that the proton conductivity of 1@PVA-10 is 6.5 × 10−5 S cm−1, which is approximately 2.63 times higher than that of 1@PVA-5 under the same conditions (Fig. 6a). For 2@PVA, varying the loading amounts of 2 from 5% (2@PVA-5) to 10% (2@PVA-10) leads to an increase of proton conductivity from 1.93 × 10−5 S cm−1 to 8.0 × 10−5 S cm−1 (Fig. 6d). The increase of composite membrane conductivities is probably caused by the intrinsic proton of 1 or 2 particles in this content range (5–10%). The proton conductivity of 1@PVA-15 is 1.07 × 10−5 S cm−1 (Fig. 6b), which is lower than that of 1@PVA-10 under the same conditions. This is mainly because as the filling quantity of 1 increases, the reunion occurs easily, which will block the proton transfer in composite membranes.62 The same phenomenon also appears on 2@PVA-15. At room temperature, with increasing relative humidity, the proton conductivity of composite membranes increases continuously (Fig. 6a and c). The Nyquist plots of 1@PVA-10 and 2@PVA-10 are shown in Fig. S10.†

Otherwise, in order to explore the proton conduction mechanism of 1@PVA-10 and 2@PVA-10, the Nyquist plots have been obtained from 292 K to 338 K under 98% RH. For 1@PVA-10, the size of semicircles distinctly tapers with increasing temperatures (Fig. 7a). The value of proton conductivity attains 2.1 × 10−4 S cm−1 at 338 K. The Ea of 1@PVA-10 is 0.21 eV between 292 K and 338 K at 98% RH. (Fig. 7b). This suggests that the proton transport process of 1@PVA-10 follows the Grotthuss mechanism. For 2@PVA-10, the size of semicircles also obviously decreases with increasing temperature (Fig. 7c). The value of proton conductivity reaches up to 2.9 × 10−4 S cm−1 at 338 K. The Ea of 2@PVA-10 is 0.29 eV (Fig. 7d), indicating that the proton transport process of 2@PVA-10 also follows the Grotthuss mechanism.

To compare the proton conductivity of other MOF composite membranes, some MOF composite membranes are selected in Table S5.† The proton conductivities of 1@PVA-10 and 2@PVA-10 are higher than that of MOF-508@PVDF-55, which can be explained that PVDF can't provide a proton transport channel in the composite membrane. However, the values of 1@PVA-10 and 2@PVA-10 are lower than that of JUC-200@PVA-10, which may be due to the existing efficient hydrogen bond chains in JUC-200. In addition, the values of 1@PVA-10 and 2@PVA-10 are much less than that of S-UiO-66@GO@SPEEK-10, which is attributed to the existence of GO and inherent Brønsted acid sites in the S-UiO-66 structure. In a word, the proton conduction of the composite membrane is closely related to the selected MOFs and polymer membrane.

Proton conduction mechanism

As mentioned above, the activation energies (Ea) of 1 and 2 are calculated to be 0.63 eV and 0.48 eV, which indicates their conduction behavior corresponding to the vehicle mechanism. The high proton conductivities of 1 and 2 need a high humidity environment, which is common for many other proton conducting MOFs. Furthermore, the μ3-OH existing in 1 and 2 can move as solvated H+ under high humidity, which can improve the proton transfer.

In the composite membranes 1@PVA-10 and 2@PVA-10, the proton transfer inside the crystals obeys the vehicle mechanism as described above. Outside the crystals, the two mechanisms may coexist. For the Grotthuss mechanism, three possible hydrogen-bonding networks are the abundant water molecules in the membrane, the alcoholic hydroxyl groups in PVA, and the μ3-OH groups in the crystal surface. In other words, protons dissociating from the crystal carrier jump into the hydrogen-bonding network and are delivered to the next adjacent R–OH carrier sites.62 For the vehicle process, the free water molecules can accept the protons from μ3-OH groups acting as vehicles and move along a certain direction.

Conclusions

In conclusion, we have successfully synthesized two 12-connected 3D porous lanthanide–organic frameworks, using bipyridine-carboxylate ligand bpydbH2 and lanthanide metal ions (Eu3+, Tb3+). Due to the existence of extra protons included in μ3-OH and hydrogen-bonding chains, 1 and 2 are likely potential candidates for proton conducting materials under high humidity. AC impedance analysis shows that the proton conductivity of 1 and 2 reaches up to 1.7 × 10−4 S cm−1 at 325 K and 1.1 × 10−4 S cm−1 at 334 K, respectively, under 98% RH, which are comparable to other reported proton conducting Ln–MOF materials under the same conditions. The activation energy (Ea) acquired from the Arrhenius plot is 0.64 eV for 1 and 0.48 eV for 2, indicating that the proton conduction behaviors obey the vehicle mechanism. To meet the practical application of the materials in PEMFCs, 1@PVA-X and 2@PVA-X composite membranes were prepared. The proton conductivities of 1@PVA-10 and 2@PVA-10 are 2.1 × 10−4 S cm−1 and 2.9 × 10−4 S cm−1 at 338 K and 98% RH. Furthermore, the conduction mechanisms of 1 and 2 as well as their composite membranes 1@PVA-10 and 2@PVA-10 have also been discussed. As far as we know, it is the first report on the fabrication of Ln–MOF-based composite membranes for proton conduction. For further progress of Ln–MOFs in PEMFCs, this work may play a certain pathfinder role.
