The 2-D double layer structures of indium–benzenemulticarboxylate coordination polymers with green fluorescence

Author links open overlay panel, , , , ,
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ica.2008.02.009Get rights and content

Abstract

Two coordination polymers, [In(btc)(2,2′-bpy)(H2O)]n · nH2O (1) and [In2(btec)(2,2′-bpy)2Cl2]n (2) (H3btc = 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylic acid, H4btec = 1,2,4,5-benzenetetracarboxylic acid, 2,2′-bpy = 2,2′-bipyridine), have been prepared under hydrothermal conditions. Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analyses reveal that compounds 1 and 2 consist of two-dimensional double layer structures with (3, 3) grids and (4, 2) grids, respectively. The grids along two different directions in 1 are of the opposite chirality, while the interactions of double-layer sheets in 2 create a three-dimensional supramolecular network with one-dimensional tunnels. The additional green luminescences for both 1 and 2 in the solid state at low temperature imply the red shift of emission energy in the compounds, which may derive from LMCT. Their X-ray powder diffractions and thermogravimetric analyses are also discussed.

1. Introduction

Low-dimensional coordination polymers, like one-dimensional chain-like and two-dimensional layer-like structures, represent one of the most flourishing areas due to their interesting structural features which induce electroconductive, nonlinear optic and magnetic properties different from those of three-dimensional coordination polymers [1]. So far a number of low-dimensional coordination polymers assembled from transition metal ions and organic components have been prepared and applied as functional materials [2], [3], [4]. The use of mixed organic ligands, such as benzenemulticarboxylate (bmc) and N-donor chelate ligands, has been considered as an effective method toward the construction of low-dimensional polymers. The bmc ligands are characteristic of multiple bridging ability and versatile coordination mode. However, the chelate ligand is capable of passivating metal sites by reducing the available metal ion binding sites. Thus, the introduction of N-donor chelate ligands into the metal sites may inhibit polymer growth in other directions to favor the growth of low-dimensional framework. In this respect, enormous compounds based on the {M2+/bmc/L} (M2+ = divalent transition metal ions, L = N-donor chelate ligands) frameworks have been reported [5], [6], [7]. On the contrary, the structures based on {In3+/bmc/L} system are rare [8]. It was postulated that the incorporation of trivalent metal ions might induce new structural evolution due to the increased valence charge of metal centers. Based on this idea, we have synthesized a series of indium(III)–organic compounds, such as 2-D material {[In(Hbtc)2(4,4′-bpy)](4,4′-Hbpy)(H2O)0.5}n, 3-D chiral porous compound [In2(μ-OH)2(btc)2]n · 2nHpy, 2-D double layer network [In2(btc)2(H2O)2]n · 2nH2O and 3-D network [In2(H2btec)2(OH)2]n · 2nH2O [9]. Herein, we report two compounds: [In(btc)(2,2′-bpy)(H2O)]n · nH2O (1) and [In2(btec)(2,2′-bpy)2Cl2]n (2) (H3btc = 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylic acid, H4btec = 1,2,4,5-benzenetetracarboxylic acid, 2,2′-bpy = 2,2′-bipyridine), which possess unprecedented two-dimensional double-layer sheets constructed from (3, 3) grids and (4, 2) grids, respectively.

2. Experimental

2.1. Materials and measurements

All reagents were used as obtained from commercial sources without further purification. IR (KBr pellets) spectra were recorded in the 400–4000 cm−1 range using a Perkin–Elmer Spectrum One FTIR spectrometer. Elemental analyses were carried out on Elementar Vario EL III microanalyzer. Fluorescent data were collected on an Edinburgh FL-FS920 TCSPC system, and TG (thermal gravimetric) analyses were performed with a heating rate of 15 °C min−1 using a NETZSCH STA 449C simultaneous TG-DSC instrument. Powder X-ray diffraction data were taken on a Rigaku DMAX2500 diffractometer.

2.2. Synthesis

2.2.1. [In(btc)(2,2′-bpy)(H2O)]n · nH2O (1)

InCl3 · 4H2O (0.5 mmol), H3btc (0.5 mmol), 2,2′-bpy (2 mmol) and H2O (6 ml) were sealed in 30-ml Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel and heated at 165 °C for 85 h, and then the reactant was cooled at a rate of 6 °C h−1 to give large light yellow tetragonally shaped crystals. The yield is 80% based on InCl3. The crystals were collected and washed with N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF) and distilled water. Anal. Calc. for C19H15N2O8In: C, 44.39; N, 5.45; H, 2.94. Found: C, 44.49; N, 5.49; H, 2.81%. IR: (cm−1) 3560(w), 3114(w), 1646(s), 1611(s), 1554(s), 1577(s), 1554(s), 1477(m), 1446(s), 1358(s), 1327(s), 1310(s), 1249(m), 1165(m), 1025(m), 924(w), 760(s).

2.2.2. [In2(btec)(2,2′-bpy)2Cl2]n (2)

InCl3 · 4H2O (0.5 mmol), pyromellitic dianhydride (0.25 mmol), 2,2′-bpy (1 mmol) and H2O (6 ml) were sealed in 30-ml Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel and heated at 165 °C for 85 h, and then the reactant was cooled at a rate of 6 °C h−1 to give light yellow tetragonally shaped crystals. The yield is 75% based on InCl3. The crystals were collected and washed with DMF and distilled water. Anal. Calc. for C15H9N2O4ClIn: C, 41.75; N, 6.49; H, 2.10. Found: C, 41.92; N, 6.53; H, 2.31%. IR: (cm−1) 3435(w), 3113(w), 1579(s), 1504(m), 1478(m), 1448(s), 1388(s), 1321(m), 1252(w), 1159(w), 1029(m), 926(w), 888(m), 773(s).

2.3. Crystallographic studies

The X-ray single-crystal data of 1 and 2 were collected on a Rigaku mercury CCD diffractometer with graphite-monochromated Mo Kα (λ = 0.71073 Å) radiation by using ω–2θ scan method at room temperature. The structures were solved with direct methods and refined on F2 with full-matrix least-squares methods using shelxs-97 and shelxl-97 programs, respectively [10]. All non-hydrogen atoms in 1 and 2 were refined anisotropically. All hydrogen atoms were added in the riding model and refined isotropically. 3. Results and discussion

3.1. Synthesis

The hydrolysis reaction of pyromellitic dianhydride under hydrothermal conditions gives H4btec, which further aggregates with indium ions to produce compound 2. The cases of using pyromellitic dianhydride to synthesize M-btec compounds by one-pot method have also been found in the literature [11]. In this reaction system 2,2′-bpy acts not only as a bridging ligand but also a base reagent to deprotonate the carboxylic acid. Therefore, the treatment of adding only stoichiometric amount of 2,2′-bpy will not lead to the formation of 1 or 2. Thus, to ensure the purity of 1 and 2, excessive 2,2′-bpy is needed.

3.2. Structural description

3.2.1. [In(btc)(2,2′-bpy)(H2O)]n · nH2O (1)

Based on the single X-ray diffraction analysis, each indium ion is surrounded by three oxygen atoms from three btc groups, two nitrogen atoms from one 2,2′-bpy molecule and one terminal water molecule to form a slightly distorted octahedral motif (Fig. S1), where the In–O(N) bond lengths vary at a small range. Two nitrogen atoms occupy two cis-apical positions. Bond valence sum [12] confirms that all indium ions are in the +3 oxidation state. The bond valence of O7 is 0.429, indicating it is a water oxygen atom actually. Each carboxylate of the btc group binds to one indium(III) center via monodentate mode. In such a way three indium ions and three btc groups are connected to generate a severely distorted (3, 3) grid, as shown in Fig. 1a. The distances between each two of the three indium(III) centers within the grid are almost the same. All the three btc benzene rings are not parallel to the plane defined by these three indium centers. The grid only bears C1 molecular symmetry and therefore it has chirality. The grids are extended through (1 1 0) plane to produce a double-layer sheet (Fig. 1b and Fig. 2). The grids reproduced along [1 0 0] direction and the ones along [0 1 0] are of the opposite chirality. The sheet is made up of two parallel indium-ion layers, which are separated by 6.86 Å. The chelating 2,2′-bpy molecules are attached to both sides of the sheet. The terminal water molecules slightly stretch out of the sheet, interacting with the carboxylate oxygen atoms in the same sheet to form intra-sheet hydrogen bonds and with the carboxylate oxygen atoms in the adjacent sheet to form inter-sheet hydrogen bonds. The free water molecules are located inside the sheet and form weaker hydrogen bonds with the sheet (Fig. 1b). The sheets are stacked by way of ABAB with the distance between the sheets being 2.49 Å. Such a short distance forces 2,2′-bpy molecules from one sheet to protrude into another neighboring sheet and consequently arouse π–π contact with 2,2′-bpy molecules. And the centroid-to-centroid distances of btc benzene rings from different sheets are 3.90 Å and 3.75 Å, respectively. Thus a 3-D supramolecular network (Fig. 3) is constructed via the weak non-covalent interactions of abovementioned π–π stacking and hydrogen bonding.

3.2.2. [In2(btec)(2,2′-bpy)2 Cl2]n (2)

Each indium ion is coordinated by four oxygen atoms from two btec groups, two nitrogen atoms from one 2,2′-bpy molecule and one terminal chlorine atom to form a distorted pentagonal bipyramid (Fig. S1), since the In–O(N)(Cl) bond lengths vary at a large range. One chlorine atom and one nitrogen atom occupy the axial positions of the bipyramid. Bond valence sum [12] also confirms that all indium ions are in the +3 oxidation state. Each of carboxylate arms of the btec group binds to one indium ion via bidentate chelating mode. Arranged by this way four indium centers and four btec groups give rise to a (4, 2) grid (Fig. 4), where four indium centers are coplanar. The grid possesses the Ci molecular symmetry with the inverse center situated at a special position (0, 0.5, 0) which is also the center of the grid. The opposite two btec benzene rings with centroid-to-centroid distances being 16.520(9) and 9.819(6) Å, respectively, are parallel with each other and locate at both sides of the plane defined by the four indium centers. The grids are extended through (1 1 0) plane to generate a double-layers sheet (Fig. 5), which comprise two parallel indium-ion layers separated by 3.70 Å. In–Cl vectors lying down in the sheet point inside the grids. The chelating 2,2′-bpy molecules are attached to both sides of the sheet. The sheets, separated by 6.14 Å, are stacked by way of ABAB, and two adjacent sheets are related through the a-glide plane parallel to (1 1 0) direction. The 2,2′-bpy molecules from the adjacent sheets are interdigitated, resulting in π–π interactions with the centroid-to-centroid distances being 3.715 Å. The interaction of sheets creates a three-dimensional supramolecular network (Fig. 6 and Fig. S2) with one-dimensional tunnels surrounded by the sheets and 2,2′-bpy molecules.

3.3. XRD, thermal analyses and fluorescent properties

The homogeneities of compounds 1 and 2 are confirmed by X-ray powder diffraction analyses (XRD, see Fig. 7). Their peaks are in good agreement with those calculated from X-ray single-crystal diffraction data.

Thermogravimetric diagram (Fig. 8) of 1 shows that the first weight loss of 5.8% at 227 °C corresponds to the loss of 1.7 water molecules (calculated: 6.0%) per formula. There is a temperature region between 227 and 400 °C where the framework of 1 remains intact and does not show obvious weight loss. The second weight loss, occurring from 400 to 500 °C, is the stage that its framework loses organic component. The residue weights 42% and remains its weight until 670 °C. As the third stage, continuing rising the temperature results in the further weight loss. The framework of 2 remains intact up to at least 400 °C, exhibiting exceptionally good thermal stability. In the temperature range from 405 to 476 °C, compound 2 dramatically loses weight and begins decomposing due to the removal of organic moiety. And its residue weights 45.3%. Similarly, continuing rising the temperature will lead to the further weight loss.

The emission spectra of compounds 1 and 2 in the solid state were measured at room and low temperatures. Their emissions at room temperature occurring at 366 nm for 1and 376 nm for 2 with excitation at λex = 340 nm are attributed to the metal-perturbed intraligand emissions of btc or btec, since the similar emissions with λmax at around 370 nm are also observed for the free ligands of benzenemulticarboxylic acids [13]. It is interesting that with decreasing temperature, both compounds give rise to a red shift in the emission energy and produce the additional green luminescence at low temperature (10 K): emission banks peaking at 358 and 511 nm (λex = 272 nm) for 1, and at 342 and 482 nm (λex = 280 nm) for 2 (shown in Fig. 9). These red shifts at low temperature might be attributable to the ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT). It is worth of note that the photophysical properties in indiumIII–organic coordination polymers, especially at low temperature, are rarely reported in the literature.

4. Conclusion

Compounds 1 and 2 represent two novel frameworks, which possess two-dimensional double-layer sheets with (3, 3) grids and (4, 2) grids, respectively. Their successful isolations provide the examples of {In3+/bmc/L} assembly for the design of low-dimensional coordination polymers. The emission spectra of 1 and 2 at very low temperature show the additional green luminescence with red shifts of emission energy, which may derive from LMCT.
