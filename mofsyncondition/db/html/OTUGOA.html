Syntheses, structures and magnetic properties of three 1-D chain metal–nitroxide coordination polymers bridged by e,e-trans-1,4-cyclohexanedicarboxylate

Abstract

Three new one-dimensional (1-D) chain metal–nitroxide complexes of the formula [M(NIT4Py)2(e,e-trans-1,4-chdc)(H2O)2]n (M = Co(II) 1, Ni(II) 2 and Zn(II) 3; NIT4Py = 2-(4′-pyridyl)-4,4,5,5-tetramethylimidazoline-1-oxyl-3-oxide and 1,4-chdc = 1,4-cyclohexanedicarboxylate dianion) have been synthesized and characterized structurally as well as magnetically. The X-ray crystal structure analyses of complexes 1, 2 and 3 reveal that they are isostructural. Three complexes all crystallize in neutral 1-D chains where metal–nitroxide units [M(NIT4Py)2(H2O)2] are linked by the linear 1,4-cyclohexanedicarboxylate dianion. The 1,4-chdc completes the segregation and only possesses the e,e-trans-conformation, although there are both cis- and trans-isomers in the raw material. The magnetic measurements show that complexes 1 and 2 both exhibit weak antiferromagnetic interactions between the metal ions and the nitroxides.

Graphical abstract

Three one-dimensional metal–nitroxide polymers have been synthesized. The magnetic measurements show the weak antiferromagnetic interactions between the metal ions and the nitroxides.

1. Introduction

In recent years, pyridyl-substituted nitroxide radicals have attracted extensive concern in the design and synthesis of molecular based magnetic materials. These stable and easy coordinating radicals can act not only as good building blocks for metal ions but also spin carriers themselves [1], [2]. A very promising strategy in this field is to combine nitroxide-type radicals with transition metal ions [3], [4]. By employment of this strategy, a large number of metal–radical complexes with various structures have been synthesized and magnetically characterized [5], [6], [7], [8], [9].

In order to design multi-dimensional materials in the metal–radical approach, applicable bridges such as pseudohalide, dicyanide and dicyanoargentate(I) have been used. A large number of new coordination polymers, such as one-dimensional (1-D) [10], [11], [12], [13], [14], two-dimensional (2-D) [15] and three-dimensional (3-D) [16], have been obtained. Meantime, the rigid phenyldicarboxylic acids, that are isophthalic and terephthalic acid, have also been widely investigated for designing coordination polymers of metal–nitroxide complexes [17], [18], [19], [20], [21]. Replacing the benzene ring of the terephthalate by a cyclohexane, it was thought to potentially provide some advantages associated with the additional flexibility of cyclohexane to the framework. The flexible 1,4-cyclohexanedicarboxylic acid (H2chdc) possesses a chair-type structure with three preponderant geometric isomers with regard to two carboxyl groups, as shown in Scheme 1 [22]. Thus, 1,4-cyclohexanedicarboxylate can connect metal ions in different directions, which may be a good candidate for the formation of polymeric structure systems [23], [24]. However, to the best of our knowledge, the report concerning 1,4-cyclohexanedicarboxylic acid in multi-dimensional metal–nitroxide systems has not been found so far. With the purpose of obtaining materials with unusual structures derived from nitronyl nitroxide radicals and 1,4-cyclohexanedicarboxylate ligand, we present here three new metal–nitroxide coordination polymers [M(NIT4Py)2(e,e-trans-1,4-chdc)(H2O)2]n (M = Co(II) 1, Ni(II) 2 and Zn(II) 3) bridged by e,e-trans-1,4-cyclohexanedicarboxylate ligand.

2. Experimental

2.1. General

All chemicals were reagent grade and used without purification. The radical ligand NIT4Py, 2-(4′-pyridyl)-4,4,5,5-tetramethylimidazoline-1-oxyl-3-oxide, was prepared according to the literature method [25]. Elemental analyses for carbon, hydrogen and nitrogen were carried out on a Model 240 Perkin-Elmer elemental analyzer. The Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR) measurements were performed with a FTIR-650 spectrometer in the 4000–400 cm−1 regions, using KBr pellets. Variable temperature magnetic susceptibilities were carried out with a Quantum Design MPMS-XL-7 magnetometer in the temperature range 2.0–300 K at a magnetic field of 2000 G. The molar magnetic susceptibility was corrected from the sample holder and diamagnetic contributions of all constituent atoms by using Pascal’s constants.

2.2. Synthesis

2.2.1. [Co(NIT4Py)2(e,e-trans-1,4-chdc)(H2O)2]n (1)

The aqueous solution (5 ml) of the neutralized mixture of cis- and trans-1,4-cyclohexanedicarboxylic acid (0.017 g, 0.1 mmol) with NaOH (0.008 g, 0.2 mmol) was added dropwise to the aqueous solution (10 ml) obtained by mixing Co(NO3)·6H2O (0.029 g, 0.1 mmol) and NIT4Py (0.047 g, 0.2 mmol). The dark blue solution was stirred at room temperature for 3 h and filtered. Green block crystals for X-ray structure analysis were grown at room temperature by the slow evaporation for 3 weeks. Yield: 0.056 g (76%). Anal. Calc. for C32H46CoN6O10: C, 52.34; H, 6.27; N, 11.45. Found: C, 52.16; H, 6.30; N, 11.56%. Important IR data (KBr, cm−1): 1538 (s), 1406 (s), 1377 (m).

2.2.2. [Ni(NIT4Py)2(e,e-trans-1,4-chdc)(H2O)2]n (2)

The reaction was carried out in the same way as for complex 1 except that NiCl2·6H2O (0.024 g, 0.1 mmol) was used instead of Co(NO3)·6H2O. Blue block crystals were grown at room temperature by the slow evaporation for 3 weeks. Yield: 0.051 g (70%). Anal. Calc. for C32H46NiN6O10: C, 52.35; H, 6.27; N, 11.45. Found: C, 52.27; H, 6.31; N, 11.53%. Important IR data (KBr, cm−1): 1550 (s), 1403 (s), 1376 (m).

2.2.3. [Zn(NIT4Py)2(e,e-trans-1,4-chdc)(H2O)2]n (3)

The reaction was carried out in the same way as for complex 1 except that Zn(NO3)·6H2O (0.030 g, 0.1 mmol) was used instead of Co(NO3)·6H2O. Purple block crystals were grown at room temperature by the slow evaporation for 3 weeks. Yield: 0.053 g (72%). Anal. Calc. for C32H46ZnN6O10: C, 51.88; H, 6.22; N, 11.35. Found: C, 51.64; H, 6.14; N, 11.45%. Important IR data (KBr, cm−1): 1558 (s), 1406 (s), 1378 (m).

2.3. X-ray crystallography

The block single-crystals of complexes 1 (0.38 × 0.32 × 0.22 mm), 2 (0.15 × 0.13 × 0.12 mm) and 3 (0.28 × 0.22 × 0.21 mm) were selected and mounted on glass fibers. The X-ray diffraction measurements of complexes 1 and 3 were made on a Bruker CCD area detector diffractometer equipped with a graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at 296(2) K, while that of complex 2 was collected on a Bruker APEX-II CCD diffractometer employing graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). The empirical adsorption corrections by sadabs were carried out. The structures were solved by direct methods using shelxs-97 program [26] and refined with shelxl-97 [27] by full-matrix least-squares techniques on F2. All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically, while the hydrogen atoms were located geometrically and refined isotropically using a riding model. 3. Results and discussion

3.1. Syntheses and the conformation of 1,4-cyclohexanedicarboxylate

Given that the most abundant source of 1,4-cyclohexanedicarboxylic acid is a mixture of cis and trans geometric isomers, most of the syntheses use this mixture as a starting material and, consequently, this introduces the question of segregation and mixing of these isomers in the resulting compounds. An important part of the work with this acid is to understand the way, where the molecular bricks are organized by the cyclohexane with the divergent carboxyl groups oriented in the different isomers. The results reported in the literature on metal complexes contain both segregation [24], [28] and mixing [23], although the former appears to be dominant. The geometry of the cis-1,4-chdc adopts the energetically most favorable equatorial–axial conformation of the boat and consequently, has an L shape. The carboxyl groups of the trans-1,4-chdc adopt the equatorial positions of the boat and the directional property is linear, which is thermodynamically more stable.

In our work, a mixture of the geometric cis- and trans-isomers of 1,4-cyclohexanedicarboxylic acid has also been used as a starting material, and we find complete segregation in the presence of nitroxide radical ligands according to the crystal structures. In three complexes, the 1,4-cyclohexanedicarboxylic acid only acts as a dianion and the cyclohexane adopts the boat geometry, possessing the linear e,e-trans-conformation. Such selective coordination has rarely been observed in the metal–nitroxide coordination polymers and may provide possibilities for molecular recognition and separation after further exploration.

3.2. Crystal structures of 1–3

The X-ray crystal structure analyses of 1, 2 and 3 revealed that the three complexes are isostructural (see Table 1). Therefore, we will restrict our description to the cobalt compound (1) and only mention pertinent points for the nickel and zinc compounds where appropriate.

Complex [Co(NIT4Py)2(e,e-trans-1,4-chdc)(H2O)2]n 1 consists of neutral 1-D coordination chains and a perspective view of the chain structure is depicted in Fig. 1. In this complex, the Co(II) ion is six-coordinated and has a distorted octahedral geometry. Two oxygen atoms from two e,e-trans-1,4-chdc ligands and two oxygen atoms from two water molecules form the equatorial plane of the Co(II) ion in a trans arrangement. The Co–O distances vary from 2.0583(17) to 2.1547(17) Å and the bond angles of O(8A)–Co(1)–O(5) and O(9)–Co(1)–O(10) are 177.01(7) and 178.74(7)°, respectively. Two NIT4Py radical ligands coordinate to the Co(II) ion through two pyridyl nitrogen atoms (N(1) and N(4)) with bond lengths and angle of Co(1)–N(1) = 2.1689(19) Å, Co(1)–N(4) = 2.1608(19) Å and N(4)–Co(1)–N(1) = 179.12(8)°, which occupy two axial positions. In the radical ligands of three complexes, the N–O bond lengths are in the range of 1.270–1.281 Å, which are corresponding to the distances in nitroxide ranges from 1.25 to 1.32 Å [3], [4], [17], [18]. The dihedral angles formed by the pydidyl rings and nitroxide moieties are 32.453 and 32.716° for complex 1, 32.787 and 34.162° for complex 2 and 32.881 and 32.273° for complex 3, respectively. Meantime, each 1,4-cyclohexanedicarboxylate dianion binds two metal ions in a bis-monodentate mode, and possesses only one kind of e,e-trans-conformation (Scheme 1), which results in a 1-D chain structure. Along the 1-D chain, the distances between the successive metal ions are 11.714, 11.641 and 11.742 Å for 1–3, respectively, corresponding to the cell length (b). The shortest interchain M–M separations are 5.50 Å for 1, 5.46 Å for 2 and 5.52 Å for 3, respectively.

In the complex 1, there exist intra- and inter-molecular hydrogen bonds between the uncoordinated carboxyl oxygen atoms and coordinated water molecules (O(9)–O(5) (1 − x, 1 − y, −z), O(9)–O(7) (x, −1 + y, z), O(10)–O(6) (2 − x, 1 − y, −z), O(10)–O(6) (x, y, z)), as shown in Table 5. The 1-D chains are linked by hydrogen bond interactions leading to a 2-D grid-shaped layer structure (see Fig. 2). Furthermore, the shortest interchain contacts in space between the uncoordinated nitroxide groups are 3.553 Å for 1, 3.455 Å for 2 and 3.553 Å for 3, which may stabilize the packing of the structure. View of the nitroxides packing of complex 1 is illustrated in Fig. 3.

3.3. Magnetic properties

The variable temperature magnetic susceptibilities of complexes 1 and 2 were measured in the temperature range 2–300 K at 2000 G. The molar magnetic susceptibility per Co(NIT4Py)2(e,e-trans-1,4-chdc)(H2O)2 unit for complex 1 or per Ni(NIT4Py)2(e,e-trans-1,4-chdc)(H2O)2 unit for complex 2, χM, was corrected from the sample holder and diamagnetic contributions of all constituent atoms by using Pasal’s constants. The plots of χMT and χM−1 versus T of two complexes are shown in Fig. 4, Fig. 5, respectively.

3.3.1. [Co(NIT4Py)2(e,e-trans-1,4-chdc)(H2O)2]n (1)

At room temperature for complex 1, the χMT value is 3.73 cm3 K mol−1, which is obviously larger than the spin-only value 2.63 cm3 K mol−1 expected for uncorrelated one S = 3/2 spin and two S = 1/2 spins. This is indicative of an unquenched orbital contribution from the high-spin Co(II) ion (4T1g). As the temperature is lowered, the χMT value decreases smoothly down to around 35 K and then sharply decreases to 0.39 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K. From the plots of χM−1 against T shown in Fig. 4, the complex follows the Curie–Weiss law in the range of 5–300 K. The Curie constant and the Weiss temperature (θ) are 3.93 cm3 K mol−1 and −14.05 K, respectively. The magnetic behaviour indicates antiferromagnetic interaction in complex 1. From 300 to 120 K, the χMT values almost being a constant, indicate the weak intramolecular magnetic coupling. For the present magnetic system, there are three kinds of magnetic interactions, namely: (i) Co(II) interacting with the coordinated radicals through the pyridine ring, (ii) Co(II)–Co(II) interaction through the 1,4-cyclohexanedicarboxylate bridge and (iii) the nitroxide–nitroxide interaction through space between the neighboring N–O groups (3.553 Å). To quantificationally evaluate the exchange coupling constants, it should be built a theoretical model which contains an unquenched orbital contribution from the high-spin Co(II) ion (4T1g) and above three kinds of magnetic interactions. However, it would be very difficult to incorporate all of these into a model to fit the magnetic data. Compared with the first magnetic interaction, the last two kinds of exchange couplings should be very weak to be neglected because of the large distances [3], [24]. In order to simplify the magnetic system, we neglected the unquenched orbital contribution, and just considered the first kind of exchange interaction, while the last two kinds of exchange couplings were deal with the mean-field approximation. So the system of complex 1 was treated as a R–Co(II)–R trinuclear model. The resulting magnetic susceptibility expression is given by Eq. 3.3.2. [Ni(NIT4Py)2(e,e-trans-1,4-chdc)(H2O)2]n (2)

For complex 2, at room temperature, the χMT is 1.93 cm3 K mol−1, which is a little higher than the value (1.75 cm3 K mol−1) expected for uncoupled one Ni(II) ion (S = 1) and two NIT4Py (S = 1/2). This value smoothly decreases down to around 30 K and then sharply decreases to 0.14 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K. From the plots of χM−1 against T shown in Fig. 5, the complex follows the Curie–Weiss law in the range of 10–300 K. The Curie constant and the Weiss temperature (θ) are 2.01 cm3 K mol−1 and −10.17 K, respectively. The magnetic behaviour indicates that there is antiferromagnetic interaction for complex 2. From the structural features of complex 2, the following magnetic interactions can exist in the system, (i) Ni(II) interacting with the coordinated radicals through the pyridine ring, (ii) Ni(II)–Ni(II) interaction through the 1,4-cyclohexanedicarboxylate bridge and (iii) the nitroxide–nitroxide interaction through space between the neighboring N–O groups (3.455 Å). To quantificationally evaluate the exchange coupling constants, the last two kinds of exchange couplings are also very weak to be neglected, and we just considered the first kind of exchange coupling just like complex 1. The fitting results confirm that there exist a weak antiferromagnetic interaction between the Ni(II) ion and NIT4Py radical, which can compare with the results for other Ni(II)–nitroxide complexes [31], [32].

The antiferromagnetic interactions between metal ions and NIT4Py have been detailedly explained by a spin polarization mechanism of the radical π-electrons on NIT4Py following the orthogonality of 3d magnetic orbitals on metal ions and the 2pπ orbital on the pyridine rings [30], [32]. Owing to spin polarization of the positive spin on radical center, the sign alternation of spin density leads to significant negative spin density at N atom of pyridine. Furthermore, as the crystal structure shows in Fig. 1, NIT4py radicals coordinate to metal ions at the axial positions, conducing the orthogonality between the 2pπ orbital at N atom of pyridine and the magnetic orbital dx2-y2 and dz2 of metal ions, which leads to negative spin density at metal ion the same as that on N atom of pyridine. Thus, the antiferromagnetic interactions will occur due to the opposite spin signs between metal ions and NIT4Py.

4. Conclusion

The reaction of metal salts, nitroxide radicals and the mixtures of geometric isomers of 1,4-cyclohexanedicarboxylate results in total segregation in the complexes, with 1,4-chdc only possessing the e,e-trans-conformation. Three 1-D chain metal–nitroxide coordination polymers [M(NIT4Py)2(e,e-trans-1,4-chdc)(H2O)2]n (M = Co(II), Ni(II) and Zn(II)), all crystallize in neutral 1-D chains where metal–nitroxide units [M(NIT4Py)2(H2O)2] are linked by linear 1,4-cyclohexanedicarboxylate dianion. The magnetic properties of complexes 1 and 2 were investigated and discussed, showing the antiferromagnetic interaction between the metal ions and NIT4Py radicals. These results are of significance for understanding the magneto-structural correlations.
