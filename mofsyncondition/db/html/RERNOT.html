First examples of rare earth benzoate chain complexes doped with radicals as paramagnetic carriers: Synthesis, structure, and magnetic properties

Abstract

Four new lanthanide complexes {[Ln(pnba)3(H2O)2]·(NITpPy)·(MeOH)}n (Ln = Gd (1), Tb (2), Yb (3), and {[Yb(pnba)3(H2O)]·(NITmPy)}n (4) (Hpnba = p-nitrobenzoic acid; NITpPy = 2-(4-pyridyl)-4,4,5,5-tetramethyl-4,5-dihydro-1H-imidazolyl-1-oxyl-3-oxide; NITmPy = 2-(3-pyridyl)-4,4,5,5-tetra-methyl-4,5-dihydro-1H-imidazolyl-1-oxyl-3-oxide) were synthesized and characterized. Single crystal X-ray analyses reveal that complexes 1, 2, and 3 are isomorphous, and the center LnIII ions are eight-coordinated. Alternate (μ2-η2:η1)2 and (μ2-η1:η1)2 type bridging modes by carboxylate ligands result in the formation of chain skeletons with two different Ln⋯Ln distances. However, in complex 4, Yb ions are seven-coordinated and bridged by a sole (μ2-η1:η1)3 mode pnba. In the four complexes, radicals that are paramagnetic spin carriers are doped in as guest molecules and are located between the columns composed of LnIII ions through weak intermolecular interactions. In addition, short contact between ORadical atoms with LnIII ions was observed. Careful inspection of the structures reveals that guest molecules have a significant function in determining the structure. Magnetic measurements were performed for complexes 1, 2, and 4. The magnetic studies show that very weak antiferromagnetic couplings exist between GdIII–GdIII in complex 1.

1. Introduction

Over the past two decades, considerable attention has been focused on the design and synthesis of new molecular-based magnetic materials combining lanthanide ions with organic radicals as magnetic centers [1], [2], [3], [4], [5]. Lanthanide ions have a large number of unpaired f-electrons, which produce a wide range of electron energy levels and a large intrinsic magnetic anisotropy [6]. However, populating the f excited states is routinely achieved through coordinated ligands because f–f transitions are electric-dipole forbidden. Furthermore, carboxylate ligands are highly complementary towards rare earth metals [7], [8]. Rare earth carboxylate complexes with a polymeric network and chain structures have been known for many years. A large number of rare earth benzoate complexes have been obtained with several different carboxylates coordination modes (Scheme 1): monodentate (η1), bidentate (η2), bridging bidentate (μ2-η1:η1), and bridging tridentate (μ2-η1:η2), which can be simultaneous or separate [9], [10]. The diversity of carboxylates and their coordination modes, combined with the unpredictability of lanthanide coordination geometries, has led to the formation of thousands of new compounds with aesthetic and unique properties. On the other hand, nitronyl nitroxide radicals (NITR), which can function as spin carriers and magnetic couplers, have also attracted much attention [11], [12], [13], [14], [15] and are widely used as ligands in the metal–radical approach to construct molecule magnetic complexes (Scheme 2) [16]. Nowadays, NITR can be easily modified chemically by changing the substituent R. As a result, many functionalized NITR have been synthesized and studied [17], [18], [19], [20]. Several investigations have explained the crucial role of the substituent of NITR in modulating the dynamics of magnetization [21], [22].

Thus far, lanthanide complexes with benzoate and their derivatives, which have chain structure, have been extensively studied [9], [10], [23], [24], [25], [26], [27], [28]. However, doped radicals as guest molecules in the lanthanide chain complexes have never been reported. In the present study, we use pnba to react with LnIII ions, plus with NITR, and obtained four new complexes formulated as {[Ln(pnba)3(H2O)2]·(NITpPy)·(MeOH)}n (Ln = GdIII 1, TbIII 2, and YbIII 3), and {[Yb(pnba)3(H2O)]·(NITmPy)}n (4). Herein, we report the syntheses, structures, and magnetic properties of these complexes.

2. Experimental

2.1. Preparation of complexes 1–4

All chemicals and solvents used for the syntheses were reagent grade without further purification. The complex was prepared starting from potassium p-nitrobenzoate, and the radical ligands (NITpPy and NITmPy) were prepared based on the methods described previously [29], [30]. The four complexes were obtained by mixing Ln(NO3)·xH2O (Ln = Gd, Tb and Yb), potassium p-nitrobenzoate, and radicals (NITpPy or NITmPy) at a 1:3:1 M ratio in a mixed solution of methanol and CH2Cl2 which lead to the formation of blue crystals.

2.1.1. {[Gd(pnba)3(H2O)2]·(NITpPy)·(MeOH)}n (1)

A methanol solution (10 mL) of Gd(NO3)3·6H2O (0.023 g, 0.05 mmol) and potassium p-nitrobenzoate (0.031 g, 0.15 mmol) was stirred for 0.5 h. A solution of NITpPy (0.012 g, 0.05 mmol) in CH2Cl2 (10 mL) was then added in the former solution in a dropwise manner. The mixture was stirred for 0.5 h at ambient temperature and then filtered. The clear blue filtrate was kept in the dark and was slowly evaporated at room temperature. A few weeks later, dark-blue crystals suitable for X-ray analysis were obtained. Yield: 0.053 g, 80%. 2.2. Physical measurements

The contents of carbon, hydrogen, and nitrogen were determined with a Vario-EL III element analyzer. Fourier transform infrared spectra were recorded using KBr disks on an FTS-40 infrared spectrometer in the 4000–400 cm−1 region. The phase purity of the samples was investigated by powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) measurements carried out on a Bruker D8 Advance diffractometer equipped with Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5406 Å) at a scan speed of 1°/min. Simulations of the PXRD patterns were obtained using single-crystal data and processed by the mercury v1.4 program, which is available free of charge through the internet at http://www.iucr.org. Variable-temperature susceptibility measurements of crystalline samples were performed on a Quantum Design MPMS-XL SQUID magnetometer. Diamagnetic corrections were performed from Pascal’s constants, and an experimental correction for a sample holder was also applied.

2.3. X-ray crystallographic measurements

The selected single crystal with suitable dimensions was mounted on a glass fiber and used for X-ray diffraction analyses. Crystallographic data were collected at 293 K on a Bruker Smart AXS CCD diffractometer with graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). Empirical absorption corrections were carried out using the sadabs program [31]. The structures were solved by direct methods and refined on F2 by the full-matrix least-squares technique by using the shelxl-97 software [32]. All non-hydrogen atoms were located using difference Fourier syntheses and were finally refined with anisotropic displacement parameters. All the hydrogen atoms of the organic ligands were placed by geometrical considerations and isotropically refined with fixed U values by using a riding model. The details of the crystallographic data collection and refinement parameters are summarized in Table 1. The main bond lengths and angles are presented in Table 2.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Description of the crystal structures

3.1.1. {[Ln(pnba)3(H2O)2](NITpPy)(MeOH)2}n (Ln = Gd 1, Tb 2 and Yb 3)

Single crystal X-ray analyses reveal that complexes 1, 2, and 3 are isomorphous. Therefore, the crystal structures of complex 1 will be discussed in detail as a representative example. The partially labeled polymeric structure of complex 1 is shown in Fig. 1 in the orientation that best displays the coordination environments around the GdIII ions. Non-coordinated guest molecules (methanol and NITpPy), which are present in the structure, are not shown. In complex 1, each Gd atom is coordinated with eight oxygen atoms, which correspond to five pnba ligands and two water molecules. The Gd–O distances are in the range of 2.282(2)–2.803(2) Å with the shortest value corresponding to the bond between Gd1 and O5 of a η1 ligand, and the longest to O1 from a μ2-η2:η1 ligand, which is similar to the structures previously reported. Alternate (μ2-η2:η1)2 and (μ2-η1:η1)2 type bridging modes by carboxylate moieties linked the neighboring metal ions to a chain with Gd⋯Gd distances of 4.296 (Gd1⋯Gd1a) and 5.281 Å (Gd1⋯Gd1b). The shorter distance is consistent with the presence of the μ2-η2:η1 carboxylate moieties, which linked the GdIII ions, and the longer distance with the μ2-η1:η1 modes between the neighbor pairs of GdIII ions [33], [34].

Furthermore, NITpPy ligands and methanol solvent molecules are present in the structure located between the columns composed of LnIII ions, which is best shown in the packing structure of Fig. 2. In the NITpPy ligand, the imidazole ring fragment is nearly planar and makes a dihedral angle of 14.34° with the pyridyl ring. NITpPy ligands are almost parallel to each other, and π⋯π interactions are observed through pyridyl rings between the adjacent NITpPy ligands with a Cg⋯Cg (centroid–centroid) distance of 3.81 Å. In the molecule, weak π⋯π stacking of μ2-η2:η1 mode pnba with μ2-η1:η1 mode edge phenyl rings (Cg⋯Cg distance of 3.81 Å) and the μ2-η1:η1 with η1 edge phenyl rings (Cg⋯Cg distance of 3.89 Å) were also observed. However, the rings are not parallel because the planes that they span intersect with an angle of 6.34° and 12.1°, respectively. Fig. 3 shows the rich hydrogen bonds in complex 1 (dotted lines), where two coordinated water molecules are involved in the intermolecular hydrogen bonds as the donor. One water molecule (O13) interacts with an uncoordinated methanol oxygen atom and a μ2-η1:η1 carboxylate oxygen with bond lengths of 2.589(4) (O13⋯O17) and 2.837(3) Å (O13⋯O9b). Another water molecule (O14) interacts with an uncoordinated oxygen atom of the NITpPy group and a η1 carboxylate oxygen with bond lengths of 2.769(4) (O14⋯O15c) and 2.706(3) Å (O14⋯O6a). In addition, the uncoordinated methanol molecule interacts with a pyridyl nitrogen atom of NITpPy with a bond length of 2.686(5) Å (O17⋯N4).

The short distances between the ORadical atoms of the N–O groups with the adjacent metal atoms are 4.52 (Gd1⋯O15c) and 5.04 Å (Gd1a⋯O15c) for complex 1, as shown in Fig. S1. The corresponding values are 4.53 (Tb1⋯O15c) and 5.05 Å (Tb1a⋯O15c) for complex 2, and 4.53 (Yb⋯O15c) and 5.05 Å (Yb1a⋯O15c) for complex 3. The short contacts indicate the possible presence of weak magnetic interactions between the metal ions and radicals [35], [36], [37].

The eight-coordination geometries are square antiprism (SAPR), dodecahedral (DD), bicapped trigonal prism (TPRS), and so on [38]. Complexes 1–3 were examined using the semi-quantitative method of polytopal analysis [39], [40]. In complex 1, the δ1 and δ2 values, which show the planarity of the squares, are 25.0° and 23.2°, respectively. The value of δ1 is close to the angle (29.5°) of the ideal DD polyhedron, whereas δ2 is fairly close to the angle (21.8°) of the ideal TPRS polyhedron. The δ3, δ4, φ1, and φ2 values are 38.5°, 32.2°, 9.2°, and 18.1°, respectively. Compared with the angles of SAPR and TPRS polyhedrons, all values are closer to the angles (48.2°, 48.2°, 14.1°, and 14.1°) of the ideal TPRS polyhedron. Therefore, the geometry around the Tb atom can be described as irregular DD or irregular TPRS polyhedron, and is closer to the irregular TPRS polyhedron. In complexes 2 and 3, the corresponding δ and φ values are similar to those of complex 1; this result reveals that the geometry around Tb and Yb atoms is similar to the Gd atom.

Many factors such as the lanthanide contraction effect, the steric crowding around the metal ions, the coordination modes of carboxylate ligands, and the coligands have significantly affected the structural features of lanthanide carboxylate complexes [41]. Compared with the previously observed linear polymeric lanthanide complexes with benzoate and their derivatives as ligands, benzoate ligands in most cases are bound in η2, μ2-η1:η1, and μ2-η2:η1, and the mode of η1 is rare [9], [10], [23], [24], [25], [26], [27], [28], [29]. Determining if the steric crowding around LnIII ions gives rise to the monodentate ligand, or if the Owater⋯Opnba interaction stabilizes the monodentate ligand allowing the tridentate bridge to form is difficult. However, a careful inspection of the structure reveals that a high degree of solvation and the presence of a large number of free molecules within the polymeric complexes, which result in the formation of rich hydrogen bonds and π⋯π interactions, must have a significant effect in determining the structure [10].

3.1.2. {[Yb(pnb)3(H2O)] (NITmPy)}n (4)

The partially labeled polymeric structure of complex 4 is shown in Fig. 4. The Yb atom is coordinated with seven oxygen atoms, which correspond to six pnba ligands and one water molecule. The coordination environment around the Yb cation is best described as a pentagonal bipyramid [42]. The Yb–Opnba distances range from 2.191(6) to 2.384(6) Å and the average value is 2.284 Å, whereas the Yb–Owater distance closely corresponds to (2.258 Å). The average Opnba–C–Opnba angle is 125.15°, which is similar to analogous structures [42]. The Yb cations are bridged in the (μ2-η1:η1)3 modes of pnba ligands, which results in the formation of linear polymer chains with a Yb⋯Yb distance of 4.679 Å. The linear polymer chain is similar to the previously observed complex [Yb(anth)3(H2O)]n [43]. Non-coordinated NITmPy ligands are present in the structure, but methanol molecules are not involved. The packing structure of complex 4 is shown in Fig. 5. Dotted lines show intermolecular hydrogen bonds, where the coordinated water molecule interacts with a pyridine nitrogen atom of one uncoordinated NITmPy and an oxygen atom of another uncoordinated NITmPy with bond lengths of 2.75(1) (O13⋯N4c) and 2.803(11) Å (O13⋯O15). In the NITmPy ligand, the dihedral angle between the imidazole ring fragment and the pyridyl ring is 25.74°, which is larger than the corresponding angle in complex 1. Moreover, the π⋯π interactions of NITmPy molecules through edge pyridyl rings (Cg⋯Cg distance of 3.73 Å) and the π⋯π interactions of pnba ligands (containing N1 and N3) through edge phenyl rings (Cg⋯Cg distance of 3.88 Å, dihedral angle of 6.47°) are observed. Similar to the described structure of complex 1, a short contact between the Oradical with the adjacent metal atoms is observed. The corresponding Yb⋯O values are 4.59 (Yb1⋯O15) and 6.03 Å (Yb1⋯O15a), as shown in Fig. S2.

The four complexes were synthesized in the same experimental method. The results show that the structure of complex 4 is significantly different from the other three complexes. For complex 4, the coordination number of YbIII ions decreases to seven, and the manner by which pnba ligands is bound to the metals is by a sole (μ2-η1:η1) mode. In addition, a lower degree of solvation and weaker intermolecular interactions are also observed. All these differences are caused by changing the guest molecule NITpPy (complexes 1–3) into NITmPy (complex 4). This result indicates that the guest molecules themselves also have a very important function in the formation of the supramolecular framework.

3.2. IR spectra

The IR spectra of the four complexes (Fig. S3) show the characteristic absorptions of the carboxylate stretching vibrations at approximately 1560 cm−1 for asymmetric stretching (νas) and 1420 cm−1 for symmetric stretching (νs) [44]. The absence of strong absorption around 1250 cm−1 indicates that all carboxyl groups (–COOH) of pnba are deprotonated and coordinated to the metal [45]. The stretching vibration of the N–O of the radical appears at 1345 cm−1, which indicates that the oxygen atoms of the conjugate group (ONCNO) of the radicals do not coordinate to lanthanide ions. A strong broad band centered around 3390 cm−1 is assigned to ν(H–O) of the water and methanol molecules with intermolecular hydrogen bonds. These results are in agreement with the crystal structures.

3.3. Magnetic property

Variable-temperature magnetic susceptibility measurements were performed on powdered crystalline samples of complexes 1, 2, and 4 in a 1000 Oe field in the 2–300 K range. The purity of the samples was confirmed by a comparison of experimental and simulated powder X-ray diffraction (Fig. S4).

3.3.1. Magnetic properties for complex 1

The obtained data for complex 1 is shown as χM−1 (inset), χM, and χMT versus T plots in Fig. S5. Based on the data of χM−1 vs. T, the magnetic behavior of the complex follow the Curie–Weiss law χM = C/(T–θ) in the entire range of temperature with a Curie constant C value of 8.22 cm3 K mol−1 and a Weiss temperature θ of –0.368 K. The perfect linear relationship in the plot reveals the characteristic of a system with weak antiferromagnetic interactions. The χMT value is equal to 16.43 cm3 mol−1 K at 300 K, which is in accordance with the expected value of 16.51 cm3 mol−1 K for an uncoupled system for two GdIII ions (8S7/2, S = 7/2, L = 0) and two organic radicals (S = 1/2). The χMT value decreases with decreasing temperature, and the value slowly decreases from approximately 300–25 K. However, a dramatic drop in the value below 25 K to 15.41 cm3 mol−1 K at 2 K is observed. This feature suggests the presence of weak antiferromagnetic interactions in the molecule.

The strength of the magnetic interactions can be quantified in complex 1 because of the absence of spin–orbit coupling for GdIII ion and the magnetic properties of one-dimensional magnetic material, which are mainly determined by the nature of the magnetic centers. From the X-ray analyses results of the complexes, the short distances of ORadical–GdIII are observed. The topology of the compound can be simplified as a cyclic four-spin system. The theoretical expression of the spin Hamiltonian can be deduced as H = − J1(SGd1SGd2) − J2(SGd1SRad1 + SGd2SRad2) − J3(SGd1SRad2 + SGd2SRad1). However, the Kambe vector-coupling method cannot be employed to analyze the variable-temperature susceptibility data. Magnetic exchange interactions between the radicals and GdIII ions are very weak or negligible because the radicals are not directly coordinated. Therefore, the interaction between the GdIII ions through pnba ligands along the chain is mainly taken into consideration. We considered the system as a dinuclear GdIII complex with two uncoordinated radicals (Fig. 6). Thus, the magnetic data were analyzed by a theoretical expression of the spin Hamiltonian H = −J1SGd1SGd2. The uncoordinated radicals are introduced by the mean-field, zj’. The best fit to the experimental data gives g = 2.00, J = −0.0105 cm−1, zj’ = −0.0049 cm−1, and R = 1.5 × 10−3 (R value is defined as Σ[(χM)obs − (χM)calcd]2/Σ(χM)obs2). The fitting results confirm that weak antiferromagnetic coupling exists in the dinuclear units, which corresponds to the reported binuclear complex [Gd(oet)3(DMF)(H2O)]2 [27] and three-dimensional complex [HNMe2][Gd2(m-BDC)3(phen)2]n [46].

3.3.2. Magnetic properties for complexes 2 and 4

Fig. S6 shows the temperature dependence of χMT for complexes 2 and 4. At room temperature, the value of χMT is 23.05 cm3 mol−1 K for complex 2, which in accordance with the expected value of 24.38 cm3 mol−1 K for an uncoupled system for two TbIII ions (7F6, g = 3/2) and two organic radicals (S = 1/2). The corresponding value of χMT is 3.25 cm3 mol−1 K for complex 4, which is higher than the expected value of 2.94 cm3 mol−1 K for an uncoupled system for one YbIII ion (2F7/2, g = 8/7) and one organic radical (S = 1/2). Upon cooling of complex 2, the χMT slowly decreases until 50 K, then rapidly decreases to reach a minimum of 14.08 cm3 mol−1 K at 2 K. However, for complex 4, χMT decreases continuously and evenly to reach a minimum of 1.39 cm3 mol−1 K at 2 K. Usually, the spin–orbital coupling leads the 4fn configuration of LnIII ions (except for GdIII) to split into 2S+1LJ states, and the latter further splits into Stark components under crystal-field perturbation [47]. Thus, using the model exactly like how it was used in complex 1 to estimate the magnetic coupling in complexes 2 and 4 is difficult. The decrease in the χMT product can be theoretically caused by the thermal depopulation of the Stark components derived from the splitting of the free-ion ground state, the antiferromagnetic couplings between the magnetic centers, or the magnetic anisotropic of the metal ions. However, quantifying each contribution separately is difficult [46], [48].

4. Conclusions

By employing an alternative synthetic route, we synthesized and crystallized four lanthanide complexes that can be doped with paramagnetic spin carriers (radicals) into the main lanthanoid carboxylate chain structure. In complexes 1, 2, and 3, the LnIII ions are eight-coordinated, and the carboxylate moieties display η1, μ2-η1:η1, and μ2-η2:η1 coordination modes. In complex 4, the solo μ2-η1:η1 type bridging modes of the carboxylate ligands provide a sevenfold coordination around the YbIII ion. The results reveal that guest molecules within the polymeric complexes have a significant effect in determining the structure. Within the complexes, a short contact between the ORadical atoms of the N–O groups with the adjacent metal atoms is observed. The magnetic studies show that very weak antiferromagnetic couplings exist between GdIII–GdIII in complex 1. Further study is needed to clarify the magnetic exchange interactions.

Up to now, various kinds of radicals have been synthesized and large numbers of lanthanide carboxylate complexes that possess similar chain structure have been reported. Therefore, by changing the host metal chains and the guest radical molecules, numerous new complexes can be synthesized. This insight will be crucial in helping us understand the configuration of lanthanide complexes, the coordination modes of carboxylate moieties, and the magnetic exchange interaction of the complexes. Much work has to be done in further studies.
