Four coordination networks constructed by MII/titb units (titb = 1,3,5-tris(imidazol-1-ylmethyl)-2,4,6-trimethylbenzene) pillared by “rods”

Author links open overlay panel
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.poly.2014.11.006Get rights and content

Abstract

Four new coordination networks, namely, {[Cd3(titb)2(SO4)2(NO3)]·(NO3)·(H2O)}n (1), {[Co3(titb)2(L1)2.5Cl]·(H2O)4}n (2), {[Co3(titb)2(L2)3(H2O)]·(H2O)4}n (3) and {[Co2(titb)2(HL3)(H2O)3]Cl}n (4) (titb = 1,3,5-tris(imidazol-1-ylmethyl)-2,4,6-trimethylbenzene, H2L1 = octanedioic acid, H2L2 = 4,4′-(2,2′-oxybis(ethane-2,1-diyl)bis(oxy))dibenzoic acid and H4L3 = benzene-1,2,4,5-tetracarboxylic acid) have been successfully synthesized hydrothermally and structurally characterized by elemental analysis, IR spectra and X-ray diffraction analyses. All compounds contain MII/titb units, including 0D molecular cage for 1, 1D ladder-like chain for 3 and 2D sheets for 2 and 4, and all the counteranions (sulfate or carboxylate anions) act as a connector to pillar the MII/titb units to generate the 1D to 3D frameworks. Compound 1 exhibits a 1D chain based on discrete Cd3(titb)2 molecular cage connected by sulfates. Compound 2 is described in terms of 2D Co3(titb)2 sheet further linked by finite hexanuclear (Co6L15) chain to form a complicated 3D framework with pentanodal (3,4)-connected topology. Compound 3 is a 2D double-layer formed by the 1D Co3(titb)2 ladder-like chain connected by both pairs of (Co2L2Co3L2)n left-, right-handed helical chains and 1D (Co1L2)n linear chain. Compound 4 is tetranodal (3,5)-connected array based on Co2(titb)2 double-layered sheet pillared by HL4 ligands. Furthermore, the thermal and photoluminescent properties have also been studied.

Graphical abstract

This paper reports four new 1D to 3D coordination networks based on MII/titb units (a discrete molecular cage, a ladder-like chain and two 2D sheets) pillared by “rod”.

1. Introduction

Great interest has been focused on the rapidly expanding field of the construction of high dimensional coordination polymers (CPs) not only due to their aesthetic appeal and interesting topologies but also due to their potential applications in magnetism, electric conductivity, molecular adsorption, heterogeneous catalysis, nonlinear optics and fluorescent materials [1], [2]. Studies in this field have been focused on the design and preparation, as well as the structure–property relationships. Significant progress have been achieved [3], however, it is still a great challenge to rationally prepare and predict the exact structures and composition of target products built by coordination bonds and/or hydrogen bonds in crystal engineering.

It is well-known that product topology can often be controlled and modified by selecting the coordination geometry preferred by the metal ion and the chemical structure of the organic ligand chosen. Usually, high dimensional MOFs can be generated from low dimensional building blocks by the linkage of organic ligands through metal-to-ligand bonds. Accordingly, our strategy is to select two functionally different ligands. One ligand forms metal–ligand low dimensional building blocks (e.g. discrete cage, chains or sheets), whereas the other acts as the rod through metal-to-ligand bonds. Up to now, a large number of beautiful CPs of ingenious design based on flexible imidazole-containing ligands have been constructed by our group and others [4]. Especially, a flexible tripodal ligand, viz. 1,3,5-tris(imidazol-1-ylmethyl)-2,4,6-trimethylbenzene (titb) has attracted much attention due to their various structures as well as their potential properties [5], [6], [7], [8], [9], [10]. The flexible nature of spacer allows it to bend and rotate when it coordinates to metal centers and it usually has two conformations, namely, the cis, cis, cis-conformation and the cis, trans, trans-conformation. Upon careful inspection of the reported cases, we found that it favors to form low dimensional networks such as discrete cage [5], 1D tube [6] and 2D sheet [7], combined with metal ions. On the other hand, the use of carboxylate ligands in conjunction with a secondary co-ligand is an effective approach toward the design and synthesis of novel coordination networks. So far, a few of CPs based on the mixed titb and carboxylate ligands have been crystallographically characterized by our and other teams [8], [9]. As extending our previous work, our synthetical strategy is to select titb to construct metal–ligand low dimensional building blocks and the long bidentate carboxylic acids to act as connecters to obtain the high dimensional coordination networks. Accordingly, octanedioic acid (H2L1) and 4,4′-(2,2′-oxybis(ethane-2,1-diyl)bis(oxy))dibenzoic acid (H2L2) were chosen. Moreover, benzene-1,2,4,5-tetracarboxylic acid (H4L4) was also selected in order to investigate whether polydentate organic carboxylate can act as a pillar as well when it adopts an appropriate coordination mode. Fortunately, four new frameworks, namely, {[Cd3(titb)2(SO4)2(NO3)]·(NO3)·(H2O)}n (1), {[Co3(titb)2(L1)2.5Cl]·(H2O)4}n (2), {[Co3(titb)2(L2)3(H2O)]·(H2O)4}n (3) and {[Co2(titb)2(HL3)(H2O)3]Cl}n (4) were successfully obtained. All compounds were characterized by elemental analysis, IR spectra and X-ray crystallography. The crystal structures as well as topological analysis of these compounds were represented and discussed in detail. In addition, their thermal properties and photoluminescent properties were also studied.

2. Experimental

2.1. Materials and general procedures

Solvents and starting materials for synthesis were purchased commercially and used as received. Elemental analysis for C, H and N was performed on a Perkin–Elmer 240 analyzer. The IR spectrum was recorded as KBr pellets on a Nicolet Magna-FT-IR 560 spectrometer in the 4000–400 cm−1 region. The photoluminescence measurements were carried out on crystalline samples at room temperature, and the spectra were collected with a Hitachi F-2500FL spectrophotometer. The thermogravimetric analyses were investigated on a standard TG analyzer under a nitrogen flow at a heating rate of 5 °C/min for all measurements.

2.2. Preparation

2.2.1. Synthesis of 1

A mixture of CdSO4·8/3H2O (0.26 g, 1.0 mmol), titb (0.36 g, 1.0 mmol), a small amount of HNO3 and water (8 ml) was placed in a 25-ml Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel. The mixture was heated at 150 °C for 3 days, and then the reaction system was cooled to room temperature. Colorless crystals were obtained in yield (based on Cd): 42%. Elemental Anal. Calc. for 1: C, 36.23; H, 3.62; N, 14.08. Found: C, 36.28; H, 3.69; N, 14.01%. Selected IR (KBr): ν (cm−1) = 3422 (s), 3101 (m), 2957 (m), 2880 (m), 1596 (s), 1560 (s), 1522 (s), 1425 (s), 1239 (s), 1105 (s), 1086 (s), 724 (s), 684 (s), 655 (m).

2.2.2. Synthesis of 2

A mixture of CoCl2·6H2O (0.24 g, 1.0 mmol), H2L1 (0.17 g, 1.0 mmol), titb (0.36 g, 1.0 mmol) and water (8 ml) was placed in a 25-ml Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel. The pH value was then adjusted to 5.0 with 1 M NaOH solution and the mixture was heated at 150 °C for 3 days, and then the reaction system was cooled to room temperature. Purple crystals were obtained in yield (based on Co): 49%. Elemental Anal. Calc. for 2: C, 51.87; H, 6.04; N, 11.71. Found: C, 51.98; H, 6.10; N, 11.69%. Selected IR (KBr): ν (cm−1) = 3430 (s), 3118 (m), 2924 (m), 1596 (s),1518(m), 1400 (s), 1277 (s), 1224 (s), 1090 (s), 1019 (m), 949 (m), 851 (m), 767 (m), 658 (s).

2.2.3. Synthesis of 3

The purple crystal of 3 was obtained by adopting the same synthetic procedure as 2 only selecting H2L2 (0.34 g, 1.0 mmol) instead of H2L1. Yield: 32% (based on Co). Elemental Anal. Calc. for 3: C, 57.06; H, 5.29; N, 8.32. Found: C, 57.11; H, 5.21; N, 8.30%. Selected IR (KBr): ν (cm−1) = 3448(s), 3132 (m), 1604 (s), 1541 (s), 1508 (s), 1522 (s), 1384 (s), 1249 (s), 1107(s), 1105 (s), 855 (m), 786 (s), 655 (s).

2.2.4. Synthesis of 4

The purple crystal of 4 was obtained by adopting the same synthetic procedure as 2 only selecting H4L3 (0.25 g, 1.0 mmol) instead of H2L1. Yield: 48% (based on Co). Elemental Anal. 2.3. Crystallographic data collection and refinement

X-ray crystallographic measurements for 1–4. Accurate unit cell parameters were determined by a least-squares fit of 2θ values, and intensity data were measured on a rigaku r-axis rapid IP area detector with Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073) at room temperature. The intensities were corrected for Lorentz and polarization effects as well as for empirical absorption based on multi-scan technique. All structures were solved by direct methods and refined by full matrix least-squares fitting on F2 by shelx-97 [10]. All non-hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic thermal parameters. 3. Results and discussion

3.1. Descriptions of the structures

3.1.1. {[Cd3(titb)2(SO4)2(NO3)]·(NO3)·(H2O)}n (1)

Single-crystal X-ray structural analysis reveals that compound 1 crystallizes in the triclinic group P1¯. There are three independent Cd(II) atoms (Cd1,Cd2 and Cd3) in the asymmetric unit, as shown in Fig. 1a. The Cd1 center adopts a five-coordinated mode (CdO3N2) by coordinating to two O atoms from two different SO42− anions, one O atoms of one NO3− anion and two N atoms from two different titb ligands. The uncoordinated O10 atom of NO3− anion has weak interactions with Cd1 atom (Cd1⋯O10 = 2.685 Å). The Cd2 center also adopts a five-coordinated mode (CdO3N2) by bonding to three O atoms from two different SO42− anions and two N atoms from two different titb ligands. The O10 atom also has weak interactions with Cd2 atom (Cd2⋯O10 = 2.663 Å). The Cd3 center is also five-coordinated mode (CdO3N2) by bonding to three O atoms from three different sulfate anions and two N atoms from two different titb ligands. The Cd-O/N distances range from 2.151(2) to 2.549(1) Å, which are similar to those found in other related Cd complexes [11].

Two types of titb ligands both adopts cis, cis, cis-conformation and coordinate three Cd atoms to afford a discrete Cd3(titb)2 molecular cage (Fig. S1). Two types of SO42− anions adopt two coordination modes, namely, μ4-SO42− and μ3-SO42− anions. Each μ4-SO42− anion coordinates four Cd atoms with nonbonding Cd1⋯Cd2 separation of 4.852 Å, Cd2⋯Cd3 separation of 5.008 Å and Cd3⋯Cd3 separation of 3.840 Å, respectively. Each μ3-SO42− anion coordinates three Cd atoms with nonbonding Cd1⋯Cd2 separation of 6.655 Å and Cd1⋯Cd3 separation of 6.426 Å. Accordingly, the Cd atoms are bonded by such two types of SO42− anions to afford a 1D ladder-like chain, as shown in Fig. 1b. There are two types of NO3− anions; one coordinates the Cd atoms with μ2-NO3− mode and the other just acts as a counteranion. Therefore, compound 1 is a 1D chain based on discrete Cd3(titb)2 molecular cage, as shown in Fig. 1c.

However, three absolutely different structure was obtained, when CdSO4·8/3H2O react with titb and HCl in H2O, MeOH or EtOH [11]. Compound [Cd5Cl2(μ-Cl)4(μ3-Cl)2(η,η,η-μ3-SO4)2(H2O)2(Htitb)2] (A) is a 1D chain in which each centrosymmetric double butterfly-shaped [Cd5Cl2(μ-Cl)4(μ3-Cl)2(H2O)2] unit interlinks with its equivalent ones via two couples of η,η,η-μ3-SO4 anions and two pairs of the Htitb ligands. Compound [Cd4Cl2(μ-Cl)2(η,η,η-μ3-SO4)2(MeOH)2(titb)2] (B) is a 1D zigzag chain, consisting of one centrosymmetric [Cd5Cl2(μ-Cl)2(η,η,η-μ3-SO4)2(MeOH)4(titb)2] unit that interconnects with equivalent ones via sharing Cd3 and its symmetry-related ones. Compound [Cd4Cl2(μ-Cl)2(η,η,η-μ3-SO4)2(titb)2] (C) is a 0D molecular cage, consisting of a [Cd4Cl2(μ-Cl)2(η,η,η-μ3-SO4)2] unit linked by a pair of titb ligands. Moreover, compound 1 also differs from other related compound [Cd3(titb)2(SO4)4(H2O)2(CH3CH2OH)2][Cd(H2O)6]·2H2O, synthesized by layering method and exhibiting a 2D stepwise structure [7c]. Compound 1 differs from all compounds mentioned above mainly due to two aspects as follows: (1) the configuration of titb ligand plays a key role on the final structure: the suitable cis, cis, cis-titb ligand can afford Cd3(titb)2 molecular cage, while the titb ligand with cis, trans, trans-conformation often favors to form 1D or 2D structures; (2) the SO42− anion has various coordination modes: it can be a connecter, if coordinating to more than one metal ion; it just acts as a counteranion, if coordinating to one metal ion or even not participating in the coordination.

3.1.2. {[Co3(titb)2(L1)2.5Cl]·(H2O)4}n (2)

Single-crystal X-ray structural analysis reveals that compound 2 crystallizes in the monoclinic group P21/c. There are three independent Co(II) atoms (Co1,Co2 and Co3) in the asymmetric unit, as shown in Fig. 2a. Both Co1 and Co2 are four-coordinate, composed of two carboxylic O atoms from two L1 anions and two N atoms from two titb ligands with slight differences between bond lengths and bond angles. The Co3 atom also adopts a tetrahedral geometry, coordinated by one carboxylic O atom from one L1 anion, two N atoms from two titb ligands and one chloride anion. The Co–O/N/Cl distances range from 1.912(5) to 2.249(2) Å, which are similar to those found in other related Co complexes [12].

There are also two types of titb ligands, both exhibiting cis, cis, cis-conformation, which connect the Co atoms to generate an irregular 2D Co3(titb)2 sheet (Fig. 2b), rather than discrete Cd3(titb)2 molecular cage in 1. Two types of L1 anions showing linear and cis-modes, connect Co atoms in the arrangement (Co3-(cis)L1-Co2-(linear)L1-Co1-(linear)L1-Co1-(linear)L1-Co2-(cis)L1-Co3) to form two kinds of finite hexanuclear Co-O chains along two different directions (Fig. 2c), resulting from the chloride anion bonded to the terminal Co3 atom. Further, the 2D Co3(titb)2 sheets are connected by such finite hexanuclear Co–N chains to form a 3D framework with some void of space occupied by free waters (Fig. 2d). As determined using the topos software [13], [14], the overall topology was characterized as a pentanodal (3,4)-connected topology with (6·7·8)(6·7·11)(72·8) (62·8·103)(62·72·8·10) by simplification the Co1 and Co2 atoms to 4-connected and Co3 atoms, titb1 and titb2 ligands to 3-connected nodes, respectively.

3.1.3. {[Co3(titb)2(L2)3(H2O)]·(H2O)4}n (3)

Compared to polymer 2, the longer flexible bidentate carboxylic acid, 4,4′-(2,2′-oxybis(ethane-2,1-diyl)bis(oxy))dibenzoic acid (H2L2) was selected instead and the resulting structure is a 2D framework instead 3D in 2. As shown in Fig. 3a, there are three independent Co(II) atoms (Co1, Co2 and Co3) in the asymmetric unit of 3. Each Co1 atom is four-coordinate by two carboxylic O atoms from two L2 anions and two N atoms from two titb ligands to give the CoO2N2 distorted tetrahedral geometry. Each Co2 atom lies in distorted CoO4N2 octahedral coordination environment, defined by four carboxylic O atoms from two L2 anions and two N atoms from two titb ligands. Each Co3 atom also adopts distorted CoO4N2 octahedral geometry with three carboxylic O atoms from two L2 anions, two N atoms from two titb ligands and one water molecule. The Co–O/N bond lengths range from 1.983(4) to 2.342(6) Å. Different from those in 1 and 2, two types of titb ligands exhibit cis, trans, trans-conformation and connect Co atoms to form a 1D Co3(titb)2 ladder-like chain (Fig. 3b). There are two kinds of L2 ligands both of which are completely deprotonated. One bridges Co1 atoms to afford a 1D linear chain running along the c axis direction, while the other links Co2 and Co3 atoms alternately to an afford a pairs of left- and right-handed helical chains along the same direction (Fig. 3c). The overall 2D double-layer is formed by the intersection, at the shared Co nodes, of the 1D titb ladder-like chains and the two types of 1D L2 chains. The structure can also be described in terms of 1D ladder-like chains of composition Co3(titb)26+ that are further pillared by L2 ligands via being coordinated to the Co atoms to form a 2D double-layered framework (Fig. 3d). The overall topology is a pentanodal (3,4)-connected network with (62·84)(64·82)2(62)(6·82) for Co1, Co2, Co3, titb1 and titb2, respectively.

3.1.4. {[Co2(titb)2(HL3)(H2O)3]Cl}n (4)

As compared, a tetradentate carboxylic acid, benzene-1,2,4,5-tetracarboxylic acid (H4L3) was selected to take place of the long dicarboxylates, which also acted as the rod in compound 4. Single-crystal X-ray structural analysis reveals that only 1,2-carboxylate groups of each L3 anion coordinate to Co atoms, 3-carboxylate group just acts as counteranion and 4-carboxylic group is not deprotonated. As illustrated in Fig. 4a, two kinds of Co(II) centers are present in the asymmetric unit, both exhibiting octahedral coordination environment. The coordination sphere around each Co1 atom is composed of three carboxylic O atoms from two L3 anions and three N atoms from three titb ligands, while each Co2 atom is bonded to three N atoms from three titb ligands and three water molecules. The Co–O/N bond lengths are in the ranges of 1.983(4)–2.342(6) Å. Two types of titb ligands adopt cis, cis, cis-conformation and link Co atoms to form a 2D Co2(titb)2 double-layered sheet (Fig. 4b). Further, the 2D Co2(titb)2 double-layers are pillared by HL3 ligands to form a 3D framework with some void of space occupied by free waters and chloride anions (Fig. 4c). If Co1 atom is considered as 5-connected node and Co2, titb1 and titb2 as 3-connected nodes, the overall structure can be represented by tetranodal (3,5)-connected underlying net with point symbol (66·83·10)(4·6·8)2(63) for Co1, Co2, titb1 and titb2, respectively.

3.2. Thermogravimetric properties

Thermogravimetric analyses of complexes 1–4 were performed to investigate its properties change with heating. As shown in Fig. S1, compound 1 first lost weight corresponding to one free water molecules (obsd: 1.56%, calcd: 1.29%) from 85 to 110 °C. After ca. 320 °C, the ligands in 1 started to decompose and the weight of the final residue (28.24%) was assumed to be CdO (calcd: 27.67%). Compound 2 gradually released the free water molecules in the range of 75–110 °C (obsd: 4.90%, calcd: 5.02%). The removal of organic ligands occurred in the range of 316–640 °C. The remaining weight corresponds to the formation of CoO (obsd: 15.89%, calcd: 15.66%). Compound 3 lost the free and coordinated water molecules at 50–130 °C (obsd: 4.62%, calcd: 4.45%) and began to collapse from about 320 °C. At about 610 °C, the remaining weight corresponds to the formation of CoO (obsd: 12.01%, calcd: 11.13%). Compound 4 lost the coordinated water molecules at 110–190 °C (obsd: 4.65%, calcd: 4.58%) and began to decompose at about 360 °C. However, it kept decomposing until 800 °C.

3.3. Photoluminescent property

Photoluminescence experiments for Cd-containing compound 1, as a typical d10 transition-metal configuration which exhibits photoluminescent property [15], were performed at room temperature in the solid state. The emission bands for the free flexible titb ligand is demonstrated at 467 nm (λex = 398 nm) [16]. As shown in Fig. S2, excitation of the microcrystalline samples of 1 at 340 nm produces two emission peaks at 458 and 513 nm, respectively, which can probably be assigned to the intraligand charge transfer of flexible titb ligand.

4. Conclusion

In conclusion, four new compounds based on different MII/titb units, including a discrete Cd3(cis, cis, cis-titb)2 molecular cage for 1, a 2D irregular Co3(cis, cis, cis-titb)2 sheet for 2, a 1D ladder-like Co3(cis, trans, trans-titb)2 chain for 3 and a 2D Co2(cis, cis, cis-titb)2 double-layered sheet for 4, pillared by different “rod”, have been successfully obtained according our synthesis strategy. The steric geometry of flexible titb ligand has a significant influence on the resulting structure, and, in particular, the generation and nature of MII/titb unit. The flexible nature of titb allows the ligands to bend and rotate when it coordinated to metal atoms, resulting in numerous possible conformations, though mainly cis, cis, cis- and cis, cis, trans-conformation. Furthermore, the distance between the three coordinating nitrogen atoms and the dihedral angles between the two adjacent imidazolyl groups in the ligand are quite variable, even if the titb ligands both assume cis, cis, cis- or cis, trans, trans-conformations (Scheme 1). In addition, in compounds 1–3, the simple anions and the long dicarboxylates act as connecters, while in compound 4, the tetradentate carboxylic acid, benzene-1,2,4,5-tetracarboxylic acid was selected as a linker. This indicates that polydentate organic carboxylate can act as a pillar as well when it adopts an appropriate coordination mode.

  • One-dimensional coordination polymers based on metal–nitrogen linkages

    2022, Coordination Chemistry Reviews

    In the past decades, one-dimensional coordination polymers (1D CPs) have been comprehensively studied on account of their unique architectures, fascinating properties, and versatile applications. The structures and properties of 1D CPs are determined by many factors, of which the ligand is the most crucial. In this review, we focus on N-donor ligand-based 1D CPs developed since 2015, including their chemical compositions, synthesis methodologies, topological structures, and structure–property relationships. Moreover, we classify N-donor ligands according to N-donor functional groups, including pyridine, imidazole, pyrazole, triazole, tetrazole, pyrimidine, pyrazine, amino, Schiff base, pyrrole, terpyridine, phenanthroline, dipyrrin, and azide. Finally, the challenges and outlook of N-donor ligand-based 1D CPs are rational discussed.

  • Imidazole-based metal-organic cages: Synthesis, structures, and functions

    2022, Coordination Chemistry Reviews

    The coordination-driven self-assembly provides a favourable methodology for constructing metal–organic architectures with diverse structures and functions. As a classical five-membered heterocyclic ligand, imidazole is widely used in the construction of metal–organic materials with finitely and infinitely structures. In this review, the synthetic methods of imidazole-based metal–organic cages (IMOCs) are introduced and analysed. Subsequently, various type of IMOCs with structure and coordination diversity of imidazole and its polytopic derivatives, including tetrahedron, cube, octahedron, rhombic dodecahedron, tetartoid, bicapped square antiprism, prism, etc., have been rationally categorised by using edge- and face-directed self-assembly strategies. Moreover, the functional applications of these IMOCs, including host–guest chemistry, luminescence, magnetism, catalysis, biological effect, and proton conductivity, are also comprehensively summarized. The relationship between structures and properties of IMOCs are elucidated and concluded, which provides some inspiration for designing new metal–organic cages with advanced functions.
