Dual-functional recyclable luminescent sensors based on 2D lanthanide-based metal-organic frameworks for highly sensitive detection of Fe3+ and 2,4-dinitrophenol

Highlights

•

Four isostructural 2D Ln-MOFs are obtained and exhibit an interesting 2D corrugated layer structure with sql topology.

•

Eu and Tb compounds are the first reported dual-functional luminescent sensors which can sense 2,4-dinitrophenol and Fe3+.

•

Eu and Tb materials are excellent recyclable luminescent sensors for the detection of 2,4-dinitrophenol and Fe3+.

Abstract

A series of lanthanide-based metal-organic frameworks (Ln-MOFs), namely [Ln(L2−)(HL–)(H2O)2]n (Ln = Eu 1, Gd 2, Tb 3 and Dy 4, H2L = 5-(imidazol-1-yl)isophthalic acid) have been successfully assembled under solvothermal conditions. Compounds 1–4 exhibit a 2D corrugated layer structure with {44.62}-sql topology based on [Ln2(CO2)6(H2O)4] units containing uncoordinated carboxylate groups, which further forms a 3D supramolecular structure through hydrogen bond interactions. Compounds 1 and 3 display excellent applications as luminescent sensors for sensing 2,4-dinitrophenol (2,4-DNP) in ethanol system and Fe3+ in aqueous solution with high sensitivity and selectivity based on luminescence quenching effects. Interestingly, 1 and 3 display excellent recyclable behaviors and can be recycled at least 5 times for sensing 2,4-DNP and Fe3+. These two compounds are the first reported dual-functional Ln-MOFs sensors for detecting 2,4-DNP and Fe3+. Therefore, these two materials may be an excellent dual-functional recyclable luminescent sensor for 2,4-dinitrophenol and Fe3+.

1. Introduction

Luminescent lanthanide-based metal-organic frameworks (Ln-MOFs) have attracted enormous attention due to their interesting features, such as large Stokes shifts, high purity of colors and relatively long luminescence lifetimes, which originate from the f–f transitions via an antenna effect [1], [2]. Based on these excellent and unique properties, luminescent Ln-MOFs are considered to be outstanding candidates as luminescent sensors [3], [4]. In the past several years, luminescent Ln-MOFs as potential luminescent sensors have already been studied for the selective detection of small organic molecules and metal ions [5], [6]. Luminescent sensing largely depends on the interaction between the host coordination frameworks and guest molecules [7].

Iron is a very common metal and has widely been utilized in the industrial production. Iron(III) ion also plays an important role in the human body and other biological organisms. Both overloading and deficiency can lead to serious physiological disorders, such as endotoxemia, hepatic cirrhosis and hereditary hemochromatosis [8]. Therefore, sensitive and selective detection of Fe3+ is a very significant object. To the best of our knowledge, luminescent sensors based on Ln-MOFs for detecting Fe3+ are widely studied in organic solvent systems, but investigated rarely in water system [9], [10]. Thus, a sensitive and selective detection method for sensing Fe3+ in water system is urgently required. On the other hand, the detection of nitroaromatic compounds (NACs), such as nitrobenzene (NB), 4-nitrophenol (4-NP) and 2,4-dinitrophenol (2,4-DNP), is also very meaningful since such NACs cause severe environmental damage, health problem and so on [11]. Up to now, some Ln-MOFs have been employed for explosives detection [12], [13], [14], but the luminescent Ln-MOFs which can selectively detect 2,4-DNP is very rare. Although several luminescent Ln-MOFs have been reported for the sensing of NACs or iron(III) ion, there are no reports on the dual-functional sensing properties for the highly selective detection of Fe3+ and 2,4-DNP. Significantly, as one of the important parameter for luminescent sensors, the recyclable performance of luminescent sensors for sensing Fe3+ and 2,4-DNP is very necessary to investigate.

In this paper, a series of Ln-MOFs, [Ln(L2−)(HL–)(H2O)2]n (Ln = Eu 1, Gd 2, Tb 3 and Dy 4, H2L = 5-(imidazol-1-yl)isophthalic acid), were obtained under solvothermal conditions, which exhibit a 2D corrugated layer structure with {44.62}-sql topology based on [Ln2(CO2)6(H2O)4] units containing uncoordinated carboxylate groups. Interestingly, compounds 1 and 3 exhibit dual-functional luminescent sensors for sensing 2,4-DNP in ethanol system and Fe3+ in aqueous solutions with high sensitivity and selectivity based on luminescence quenching effects. Importantly, 1 and 3 show excellent recyclable behaviors and can be reused at least 5 times for sensing 2,4-DNP and Fe3+. Moreover, 1 and 3 are the first reported two luminescent sensors for detecting 2,4-DNP and Fe3+, simultaneously.

2. Experimental section

2.1. Material and methods

All of the chemicals and solvents were used in the synthetic processes as purchased without any further purification. The H2L ligand was bought from Jinan Camolai Trading Company. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns were collected by a Rigaku MiniFlex 600 diffractometer using Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5406 Å). The infrared spectra were recorded from KBr pellets in the range 4000–400 cm−1 with a Bruker Tensor 27 IR spectrometer. Elemental analyses (C, H and N) were performed on a Perkin–Elmer 240 elemental analyzer. Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were recorded in the temperature range of 20–900 °C, with a heating rate of 10 °C/min under flowing nitrogen atmosphere on a Netzsch TG 209 TG-DTA analyzer. The fluorescent spectra were measured on an F-4500 FL Spectrophotometer. The luminescence decay curves were recorded by an Edinburgh Instrument FLS 920P fluorescence spectrometer at room temperature.

2.2. Synthesis of [Ln(L2−)(HL–)(H2O)2]n (Eu 1, Gd 2, Tb 3 and Dy 4)

2.2.1. Synthesis of [Eu(L2−)(HL–)(H2O)2]n (1)

A mixture of Eu2O3 (17.5 mg, 0.05 mmol) and H2L (11.6 mg, 0.05 mmol) was mixed in 10 mL of DMF−H2O (1:9 in v/v). The mixture was put into a Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel (23 mL) and heated at 170 °C for 3 days, followed by cooling to room temperature at a rate of 10 °C h−1. Colorless crystals were collected (yield 53% based on H2L, 17.2 mg). Anal. Calcd for C22H16EuN4O10 (1): C, 40.76, H, 2.49, N 8.64%. Found: C, 40.37, H 2.66, N 8.31%. 2.3. X-ray crystallography

The crystallographic data for complexes 1–4 were collected at 113 K using a Rigaku Saturn CCD diffractometer with graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). The hydrogen atoms of imidazole N and water molecules were found from different Fourier maps and then refined with isotropic temperature factors. Hydrogen atoms attached to carbon atoms were generated geometrically and refined using a riding model. All non-hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic displacement parameters. 3. Results and discussion

3.1. Crystal structure

Single crystal X-ray diffraction reveals that isostructural complexes 1–4 crystallize in the monoclinic system P21/c space group and give a 2D coordination framework. Herein only the structure of complex 3 will be described in detail. The asymmetric unit consists of one Tb3+ ion, two carboxylate ligands (the fully deprotonated carboxylate L2− and the N4-protonated carboxylate HL−) and two coordinated H2O molecules. The Tb3+ ion is eight-coordinated in a [TbO8] distorted square antiprism coordination geometry, in which six oxygen atoms come from four different carboxylate ligands and two oxygen atoms from two H2O molecules (Fig. 1a). The Tb-Ocarboxylate bond lengths vary from 2.350(2) to 2.526(2) Å and Tb-Owater bond lengths are 2.365(2) and 2.367(2) Å, which are in the range of previously published Tb3+ complexes with carboxylic acids and aqua ligands [16]. It should be noted that two ligands in the asymmetric unit are different: one is N4-protonated carboxylate ligand HL− with a μ3-η1:η1:η1:η1 coordination fashion (mode I, Fig. 1b) and the other is completely deprotonated carboxylate ligand L2− with a chelating coordination fashion (mode II, Fig. 1c). The dihedral angles between imidazole ring and benzene ring are 25.090(84)° and 7.317(90)° in mode I and II, respectively. The uncoordinated carboxylate groups of L2− ligands may play a crucial role in the process of sensing metal ions. Moreover, two Tb3+ ions are connected by two bridging carboxylate groups to form a dimer [Tb2(CO2)6(H2O)4] (Fig. S1) and these dimers are further linked by the μ3-bridging ligands to generate a 2D corrugated layer structure (Fig. 2a). Finally, the overall 3D supramolecular structure is formed through Owater–H···Ocarboxylate, Nimidazole–H···Ocarboxylate and Owater–H···Nimidazole hydrogen bonds interactions between the corrugated layers (Fig. S2 and Table S2). From the view point of topology, each Tb dimer can be served as a 4-connected node and μ3-bridging ligand can be considered as a 2-connected linker. Calculating from TOPOS 4.0 software, 3 can be described as a (4,2)-connected network with the point symbol of {44·62} (Fig. 2b) [17].

3.2. PXRD and thermogravimetric analysis of complexes 1–4

The powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) analyses were performed on polycrystalline samples of compounds 1–4 (Fig. S3). The experimental patterns are consistent with the simulated ones from single crystal X-ray data, illustrating that compounds 1–4 are pure samples. The thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) data of 1–4 show a resembling thermal behavior owing to their structural similarity (Fig. S4). Herein, only compound 3 is described in detail. Compound 3 displays a gradual weight loss of approximately 5.49% (calc. 5.78%) in the 65–365 °C range corresponding to the loss of two coordinated water molecules. With further increase of temperature, the decomposition of coordination framework occurred.

3.3. Luminescent properties

The solid-state luminescent properties of compounds 1 and 3 were measured at room temperature. When excited at 395 nm, compound 1 exhibits an intense red luminescence and displays characteristic emission peaks centered at 593, 615, 652, and 699 nm, originating from the transitions 5D0 → 7FJ (J = 1, 2, 3 and 4) of the Eu3+ ion (Fig. S5a). Among these emission peaks, the 5D0 → 7F2 transition is the strongest one [18]. As shown in Fig. S5b, the emission spectrum of compound 3 shows the characteristic emission excited at a wavelength of 390 nm. The emission bands at 491, 545, 587 and 621 nm are assignable to the transitions 5D4 → 7F6, 5D4 → 7F5, 5D4 → 7F4 and 5D4 → 7F3, respectively. The 5D4 → 7F5 transition is the most striking in all emission peaks [19].

3.4. Sensing of small organic molecules

To investigate the potential of compounds 1 and 3 for sensing of small molecules, the finely ground samples 1 or 3 (3 mg) were dispersed in 3 mL different solvents, such as ethanol (EtOH), methanol (MeOH), H2O, propanol (PrOH), acetonitrile (CH3CN), CH2Cl2, N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF), N,N-dimethylacetamide (DMA) and CHCl3 and 0.01 mol L−1 ethanol solution of toluene, benzene, nitrobenzene (NB), 4-nitrophenol (4-NP) and 2,4-dinitrophenol (2,4-DNP), respectively, and then 1- and 3-solvents are treated by ultrasonication to form stable emulsions. Ethanol was chosen as solvent because compounds 1 and 3 exhibit the strongest emission intensity when dispersed in ethanol. The emission of these suspensions is largely dependent on the type of solvents and small organic molecules (Fig. 3).

As shown in Fig. 3, 2,4-DNP exhibits the most significant quenching effect towards 1 and 3. In order to explore the sensing sensitivity towards different small organic molecules (2,4-dinitrophenol (2,4-DNP), 4-nitrophenol (4-NP), nitrobenzene (NB), benzene and toluene), a batch of suspensions of compounds 1 and 3 with gradually increasing analyte concentration in the EtOH system were prepared to record the corresponding luminescent intensity (Fig. 4a and b and Figs. S6–S13).

The luminescence intensities of 1 and 3 gradually decrease with the increase of analyte concentration. To further understand the results, the quenching effect coefficients of compounds 1 and 3 were calculated by using the Stern–Volmer equation (I0/I = 1 + Ksv·[A] [20], where I0 represents the initial intensity of 1 or 3 in the absence of analyte, I is the emission intensity in the presence of analyte with molar concentration [A], and Ksv is the quenching constant (M−1)) (Fig. 4c and d). We found that the values of KSV reach 6.16 × 103 M−1 and 7.75 × 103 M−1 towards 2,4-DNP for 1 and 3, respectively. For other small organic compounds, the values of KSV are listed in Table S3. The calculated detection limits of 2,4-DNP are 16.4 and 16.2 μmol L−1 for 1 and 3, respectively, according to 3δ/k [21]. These results indicate that 1 and 3 possess high sensitivity for 2,4-DNP detection. Thus, 1 and 3 might be utilized as novel functional sensing materials for 2,4-DNP. For complex 1, the order of the fluorescence quenching percentages (= (I0 – I)/I0 × 100%) is 2,4-DNP > 4-NP > NB > benzene > toluene at 0.415 mM; complex 3 has the same order (Fig. 5).

The PXRD patterns of the compounds 1 and 3 after immersion in benzene, toluene, NB, 4-NP and 2,4-DNP solutions are similar to the simulated pattern, demonstrating that the basic framework remains intact in the sensing process (Figs. S14 and S15). The absorption spectra of 2,4-DNP in EtOH were recorded. It was clearly observed that the absorption spectrum of 2,4-DNP shows a remarkable overlap with the excitation spectrum of 1 (or 3) (Fig. S16). Thus, 2,4-DNP will strongly absorb the energy of the excitation light and reduce the effective energy transfer from the ligand to lanthanide ion. The mechanism of the recognition of nitro explosives might be attributed to photoinduced electron transfer [10](d), [22]. Another possible reason for luminescence quenching may be explained as a function of the electron-withdrawing ability. Nitro compounds may capture an electron from the ligand because of the strongly electron-withdrawing ability of the nitro group [23]. The molecular orbitals of L and analytes are determined by density functional theory at the B3LYP/6-31G(d) level [10](d), [23](d), [24]. The ligand L exhibits a high LUMO energy and an excited electron may be easily transferred to the LUMO of an electron-deficient nitro compounds, leading to luminescence quenching. As shown in Fig. S17, the LUMO energy levels of each analytes were lower than the LUMO energy level of L, indicating that these compounds should quench the luminescence to different degrees. Among them, 2,4-DNP exhibited the lowest LUMO energy, consistent with its strongest selective quenching efficiency in the sensing experiments. On the other hand, the quenching behavior might also be ascribed to the interaction, such as π–π interactions and H-bond interactions, between the guest molecules and the coordination framework [25]. In a word, competitive energy absorption, electron transfer and electrostatic interactions may play an intrinsic role in the luminescence quenching process.

3.5. Sensing of metal ions

Considering that complexes 1 and 3 possess uncoordinated carboxylate groups as Lewis basic site that can chelate metal ion, the fluorescent sensing experiment of different metal ions was carried out. To examine the influence of various metal ions on the luminescence of 1 and 3, 3 mg of 1 or 3 was dispersed in 2.7 mL of an aqueous solution, forming a suspension solution by an ultrasound method, and 0.3 mL of an aqueous solution of M(NO3)x (1 × 10−2 M; Mx+ = Na+, K+, Cd2+, Zn2+, Ba2+, Mg2+, Ni2+, Co2+, Cu2+ and Fe3+) was slowly added into the above emulsions to form a suspension of metal−1 or 3 in aqueous (1 × 10−3 M). The luminescence curves of metal−1 and 3 are recorded (Figs. S18 and S19), and only the highest emission peaks at 615 and 545 nm are shown in Fig. 6. It was found that Na+, K+, Cd2+, Zn2+, Ba2+, Mg2+, Ni2+, Co2+ and Cu2+ ions decreased luminescence intensity to different extents and only Fe3+ caused a significant emission quenching effect on luminescence of compounds 1 and 3, implying that 1 and 3 can be regarded as potential candidates for selective sensing of Fe3+ ion. The observed results reveal that 1 and 3 can sense Fe3+ ions efficiently through fluorescence quenching.

Because of the highly sensitivity for Fe3+ ion, the fluorescence intensities of 1 and 3 were recorded to investigate the effects of the different concentration of Fe3+ in aqueous solutions. The luminescence intensities of 1 and 3 gradually decrease with increasing concentration of Fe3+ (Fig. 7a and b). To further investigate the relationship between quenching effect and the Fe3+ concentration, a plot of the luminescence intensity versus Fe3+ concentration was made, which can be well fitted by the Stern−Volmer equation (I0/I = 1 + Ksv·[A]) at low concentrations (Fig. 7c and d). The calculated values of KSV are 1.28 × 104 (complex 1 for Fe3+) and 1.46 × 104 (complex 3 for Fe3+) M−1, respectively. In comparison the values of Ksv with previously reported Ln-MOFs (Table 2), the Ksv values of complexes 1 and 3 are lower than those of {[H2N(Me)2][Eu3(OH)(bpt)3(H2O)3](DMF)2·(H2O)4}n [9a] and {[Tb2(Ccbp)36H2O]·3Cl−·4H2O}n [10b], but higher than those of{[Tb(L1)(DMA)]·(DMA)·(0.5H2O)}n [9c] and {[Eu2(L2)1.5(H2O)2EtOH]·DMF}n [10d]. The Stern−Volmer curves deviate from the linear correlation with the increase of concentration (Fig. 7c and d), illustrating the existence of both static and dynamic quenching processes. The calculated detection limit of Fe3+ ion are 0.17 and 0.20 μmol L−1 for 1 and 3, respectively, according to 3δ/k [21]. These results indicate that compounds 1 and 3 are sensitive to Fe3+ even at low concentration. Moreover, the interference experiment was carried out. The results indicate that the interference from common metal cations can be neglected, further confirming the high selectivity of 1 and 3 for Fe3+ detection (Figs. S20 and S21).

The PXRD patterns of compounds 1-M and 3-M after immersion in aqueous solutions of different metal cations are similar to the simulated one, illustrating that the basic framework remains intact in the process of sensing and the luminescence quenching is not caused by the collapse of the framework (Figs. S22 and S23). As is well-known, the luminescence of Ln-MOFs largely depends on the energy transfer from the ligand to the Ln3+ center. According to the UV-Vis absorption spectra, only the Fe3+ aqueous solution exhibits a strong absorption band, while the solutions of other metal ions have no significant absorption band (Fig. S16). Thus, competitive adsorption of excitation wavelength energy between 1 (or 3) and Fe3+ ions may be responsible for the luminescence quenching [16](a), [26]. In addition, luminescence lifetimes of 3 before and after sensing of Fe3+ are 0.66 and 0.29 μs, respectively (Fig. S24). The decrease of luminescence lifetime further confirms that the dynamic process existed in the luminescence quenching processes and indicates that the energy transfer from the ligand to Tb center has been restrained to a certain degree [10](a), [10](c), [16](a), [27]. Finally, when the Fe3+ ions are added into the aqueous solution of 1 and 3, the uncoordinated carboxylate oxygen atoms acting as the electron donors may donate their electrons to the Fe3+ ions to form a very weak O⋯Fe bond, which could interrupt the energy transfer from the coordination framework to the Ln3+ center, resulting in energy migration and luminescence quenching [28]. All in all, competitive energy absorption, energy transfer and weak coordination interaction may have a synergistic effect on the quenching of fluorescence intensity.

3.6. The recyclable performance of 1 and 3

As one of the important parameter for luminescent sensors, the recyclable performance of luminescent sensors is very necessary to investigate. In order to examine the recyclable performance of 1 and 3, compounds 1 and 3 are immersed in an ethanol solution of 2,4-DNP and aqueous solution of 10−3 M Fe3+ ions respectively and then washed with ethanol or water several times. The luminescence intensities of 1 and 3 exhibit a negligible decrease compared with that in the initial state (Fig. 8 and Fig. S25). Gratifyingly, 1 and 3 can be reused at least 5 times. The results indicate that 1 and 3 possess high stability for repeated detection for 2,4-DNP and Fe3+.

4. Conclusions

In summary, four isostructural 2D Ln-MOFs with uncoordinated carboxylate groups were obtained from a π conjugated carboxylate ligand. The solid state emission spectra of 1 and 3 were investigated at room temperature, where 1 shows the characteristic transitions of the Eu3+ and 3 displays the typical narrow emission bands of Tb3+ ions. Interestingly, 1 and 3 show remarkable applications for the luminescence sensing of 2,4-DNP and Fe3+. The low detection limits and relatively high quenching constants KSV indicate that 1 and 3 possess high sensitivity and selectivity for the sensing of 2,4-DNP and Fe3+. Importantly, 1 and 3 display the excellent recyclable behaviors and can be reused at least 5 times for sensing 2,4-DNP and Fe3+. Therefore, compounds 1 and 3 are the first examples which can be potentially used as recyclable dual-functional luminescent sensors for the detection of 2,4-DNP and Fe3+.

  • Mechanochemical synthesis and theoretical investigations of Fe (II) based MOF containing 4,4′-bipyridine with ordained intercalated p-aminobenzoic acid: Application as fluoroprobe for detection of carbonyl group

    2023, Inorganica Chimica Acta

    A 3D pillared-layer intercalated metal–organic framework synthesized using solvent-free mechanochemical (MC) approach using N-donor rigid organic ligand, 4,4′‐bipyridine (4,4′-bipy) and iron as a metallic center followed by incorporation of chemically labile conjugated organic linker, p-aminobenzoic acid (PABA) into the 3D framework. The as-synthesized Fe-MOF was further characterized by various physiochemical techniques including single crystal X-ray diffraction. The remarkable emissive nature of Fe-MOF investigated in different organic solvents was utilized to develop Fe-MOF based turn-off luminescence sensing with respect to organic compounds having aldehyde and ketone functional moieties. The intercalated PABA in the interstitial voids of the 3D Fe-MOF, found to be superintend as fluoroprobe for detection of carbonyl group as evident from FTIR and DFT calculations. DFT studies revealed that the selective recognizing of aldehydes and ketones substantiated to the development of a stable, non-covalent bonding interactions between carbonyl group and the PABA unit of the MOF.

  • Syntheses, crystal structures, luminescent sensing and photocatalytic properties of two 2D cadmium(II) coordination polymers constructed from mixed ligands

    2022, Inorganica Chimica Acta

    Two ternary Cd(II) coordination polymers (CPs), namely, [Cd(TDC)(L)]n (1) and {[Cd(TCPA)(L)]·H2O}n (2) (H2TDC = 2,5-thiophenedicarboxylic acid, H2TCPA = 2,3,5,6-tetrabromoterephthalic acid, L = 1,4-bis(thiabendazole-1-yl)-2-butene) were hydrothermally synthesized and characterized. Both CPs display the 2D 63 layer, which are finally extended into 3D supramolecular network by weak hydrogen bond interactions. 1 and 2 possess excellent chemical stability in a wide pH range of 2–13. Both Cd(II) CPs can serve as efficient luminescent probes for the sensing of acetylacetone (acac), Fe(III) ions and norfloxacin (NOR) in water. The photodegradation ability of two CPs for methyl violet under UV irradiation was investigated in detail.

Their structural features vary from a 3D pillar-layered 5-fold interpenetrated dia framework (1) to a 2D double-layered non-interpenetrated sql net (2). The different structures between 1 and 2 can be attributed to distinct bridging angles from dicarboxylic ligands. Furthermore, luminescent sensing experiments exhibit that 1 and 2 are efficient dual-responsive sensors for the detection of acetylacetone (acac) and Cr2O72−. Especially, 2 performs very highly sensitive quenching response towards acac in aqueous medium. More interestingly, 2 exhibits significantly enhanced quenching efficiencies than 1 (Ksv of detecting acac: 7.87 × 103 M−1 for 1 and 4.9 × 104 M−1 for 2; Ksv of detecting Cr2O72−: 3.95 × 103 M−1 for 1 and 1.41 × 104 M−1 for 2). The cause for higher quenching efficiencies of 2 has been preliminary discussed. The efficient luminescence quenching mechanisms of 1/2 towards acac/Cr2O72− have been elucidated.

  • Polymeric-coated Fe-doped ceria/gold hybrid nanocomposite as an aptasensor for the catalytic enhanced colorimetric detection of 2,4-dinitrophenol

    2021, Colloids and Surfaces A: Physicochemical and Engineering Aspects

    The Meisenheimer charge transfer reaction has, for many years, been the predominant mode of interaction to detect explosive compounds via the naked eye. However, this method is majorly sensitive to 2,4,6-trinitrotoluene and insensitive to many other explosive compounds. In this work, we report on the development of a novel aptamer-based amphiphilic polymer-coated Fe-doped ceria/gold (Au) hybrid nanocomposite (NC) as a peroxidase mimetic nanozyme for explosive colorimetric detection. Polymeric-Fe-doped ceria NC was synthesized and incorporated in-situ in the hydrothermal synthetic pot of citrate-Au nanoparticles to form a polymer-Fe-doped ceria/Au NC. A streptavidin (strep)-biotinylated DNA aptamer (B-Apt) binding complex was immobilised on the polymer-Fe-doped ceria/Au NC surface and the modified NC was used as a functional nanozyme in a peroxidase mimic assay to catalyse the oxidation of 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine by H2O2. Screening of several explosives showed that 2,4-dinitrophenol (DNP) bonded with higher affinity to the aptamer based on the selective greenish colorimetric catalytic reaction observed. The peroxidase mimetic activities of several nanozymes were tested and our analysis showed that the polymer-Fe-doped ceria/Au NC generated enhanced catalytic activity for DNP detection. Several optimisation reactions including the kinetic reaction mechanism was conducted and a limit of detection of 0.45 µg/mL (2.4 µM) was obtained for DNP recognition. The peroxidase mimetic strep-B-Apt-polymer-Fe-doped ceria/Au NC biosensor was successfully applied for the detection of DNP in tap water and river water with satisfactory recoveries in the range of 97–117%.

  • Highly stable terbium(III)-based metal-organic framework for the detection of m-dinitroaromatics and Fe<sup>3+</sup> in water

    2021, Inorganica Chimica Acta

    A highly luminescent three-dimensional terbium-based metal organic framework [Tb(cpia)(H2O)2]n·nH2O (1) has been employed for the selective detection of m-dinitroaromatics and Fe3+. Stability tests suggest that compound 1 exhibits good stability in hot water and acidic and basic conditions within the pH range of 4–13. The strong green emission of 1 could be efficiently quenched by 1,3-dinitrobenzene, 2,4-dinitrotoluene, 2,6-dinitrotoluene in ethanol. Furthermore, compound 1 also exhibits superior luminescence quenching behavior towards Fe3+ with high sensitivity and selectivity among various metal cations. In addition, the luminescence quenching mechanisms towards different analytes are also discussed in detail. These results demonstrate that compound 1 is a promising luminescent sensor for the simultaneous selective detection of m-dinitroaromatics and Fe3+ in water.

  • Dual luminescent coordination polymers based on flexible aliphatic carboxylate ligands supplemented by rigid bipyridyl ligands for 2,4-dinitrophenol (DNP) and iron(III) ion detection

    2021, Polyhedron

    Based on flexible aliphatic carboxylate ligands supplemented by rigid bipyridyl ligands with isomerized structures, three novel Zn(II) coordination polymers, namely Zn2(dpe)(glutarate)2(H2O)2 (1), Zn2(glutarate)2(tdpe)2(H2O)2 (2) and Zn(H2O)(succinate)(tdpe)2 (3) [dpe = 1,2-di(4-pyridyl)ethylene, tdpe = trans-1-(2-pyridyl)-2-(4-pyridyl)ethylene)], were synthesized by appropriate methods. The crystallographic data of all the complexes present diverse geometries of the Zn(II) centers. There are two independent Zn(II) ions where one is intermediate between square pyramidal and trigonal pyramidal and the other is distorted tetrahedral [in 1] or distorted octahedral [in 2 and 3]. Both carboxylate ligands act as bridging ligands with various coordination modes to build up the main structures, whereas the bipyridyl ligands show only coordination of the 4-pyridyl site with the metal centers, indicating that only dpe acts as a bridging ligand, building up the principal framework in 1. The tdpe ligand presents a monodentate coordination mode in the other two complexes. The structures of all complexes indicate that the coexistence of two ligand types could support higher dimensionalities in the complexes. Complex 1 was further used as a nitroaromatic compound (NAC) and a heavy metal ion sensor. The complex showed highly sensitive and selective sensing of 2,4-dinitrophenol (DNP) and Fe(III) ions, based on its fluorescence quenching effect.
