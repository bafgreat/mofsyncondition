Introduction

The design and synthesis of metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) are continuously considered as one of the most important issues in the field of crystalline solid materials due to their diverse intriguing topologies1 and potential applications in adsorption and separation, solid luminescence, catalysis, sensing, and drug delivery.2 However, how to rationally design and construct MOFs with expected structures and properties is still a great challenge because the final structures are influenced by various factors such as the nature of organic ligands, metal-to-ligand molar ratios, reaction temperature, pH value, counterions, solvent and alkaline reagents in reaction systems.3 Among the above factors, the effect of alkaline reagent has been relatively rarely explored.4 Up to now, a more effective strategy has been to select suitable organic ligands as building blocks to link different metal centers or metal clusters into extended frameworks. It is well known that the rigid polycarboxylate ligands due to their good structural directivity have been widely used as organic linkers or building blocks to construct various expected frameworks with some specific functionalities.5 In comparison with rigid polycarboxylate ligands, flexible polycarboxylate ligands are much more sensitive to the reaction systems, which makes it more difficult to predict and control the final crystalline products. So relatively few MOFs with flexible polycarboxylate ligands have been reported previously.6 However, in recent years, flexible polycarboxylate ligands have become attractive as building elements in the synthesis of multidimensional structures since their conformational freedom coupled with multiple coordination modes could be affected easily by fine-tuning the reaction systems, which may provide more possibilities for the construction of structures with unusual topologies and afford a good opportunity to investigate the correlation of the resultant structures and the reaction systems.7 Recently, we have been focusing our attention on the polycarboxylate ligand of bis(2-carboxyethyl)isocyanurate (H3bci). H3bci as a highly flexible ligand has two freely rotatable propionic acid groups, three carbonyl groups and a N atom in the C3N3 ring, which makes it a good candidate for the construction of coordination polymers with diverse topologies.8 It should be mentioned that H3tci (tri(2-carboxyethyl)-isocyanurate) is a syngeneic ligand of H3bci, which has been widely explored in the coordination polymer system,9 while the H3bci ligand has not been well explored so far, and only a few references have been documented recently.7c,8 In this work, one main goal is to explore the effect of different alkaline reagents in the reaction system on the coordination mode of the H3bci ligand and the resultant structure. Herein, four new complexes, namely, [CdNa(bci)(H2O)3]n (1), [CdK(bci)(H2O)3]n (2), [Cd(Hbci)(H2O)]n (3) and {[(H2en)0.5Cd(bci)]·2H2O}n (4), have been successfully synthesized from the H3bci ligand and Cd(NO3)2 in aqueous solution using NaOH, KOH, triethylamine and ethylenediamine, respectively. 1 and 2 are two different 3D heterometallic frameworks, 3 is a 3D cadmium complex, while 4 is a 2D double-layer structure. Furthermore, four new coordination modes for the H3bci ligand were found in these complexes (Schemes 1 and S1†). Their structural diversities show that the alkaline reagents play a vital role in affecting the coordination mode of the ligand and the subsequent structures during the self-assembly process. Their thermal stabilities and solid photoluminescence properties have been investigated. Unexpectedly, compound 4 shows significant luminescence emission of the guest ethylenediamine molecule, which has not been reported in the coordination polymer system up to now.

Experimental section

Materials and general methods

All reagents and solvents were used as purchased from commercial suppliers without further purification. IR spectra were recorded in the range of 4000–400 cm−1 on a Nicolet Avatar-370 spectrometer. Elemental analyses of C, H, and N were carried out using an Elementar Vario EL III elemental analyzer. Thermogravimetric analyses were performed using an STA409PC instrument. The X-ray powder diffraction patterns were collected by using a Rigaku D/max-2200. Fluorescence spectra for the solid samples were obtained using an RF-5301PC analytical instrument.

Synthesis of [CdNa(bci)(H2O)3]n (1)

An 8 mL H2O solution (or CH3CN/H2O, v : v = 1 : 1, 8 mL) of Cd(NO3)2·4H2O (93 mg, 0.3 mmol), H3bci (55 mg, 0.2 mmol) and NaOH (2 M, 300 μL) was stirred for 10 min in air, then placed in a 25 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel and heated at 100 °C for two days. After cooling to room temperature, colorless rod-like crystals of 1 were obtained and washed with CH3CN and dried in air (yield: ca. 35%). Elemental analysis calcd (%) for 1: C9H14N3O10CdNa: C, 23.52; H, 3.07; N, 9.14. Found: C, 23.46; H, 3.18; N, 9.12.

Synthesis of [CdK(bci)(H2O)3]n (2)

Complex 2 was prepared under conditions similar to those of 1 except KOH (2 M, 300 μL) was used instead of NaOH to form colorless rod-like crystals of 2. The crystals were washed with CH3CN and dried in air (yield: ca. 40%). Elemental analysis calcd (%) for 2: C9H14N3O10CdK: C, 22.72; H, 2.97; N, 8.83. Found: C, 22.61; H, 2.86; N, 8.72.

Synthesis of [Cd(Hbci)(H2O)]n (3)

A mixture of Cd(NO3)2·4H2O (93 mg, 0.3 mmol), H3bci (55 mg, 0.2 mmol) and triethylamine (30 μL) in 8 mL of H2O (or CH3CN/H2O v : v = 1 : 1, 8 mL) was sealed in a 25 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel and heated at 100 °C for two days. After cooling to room temperature, colorless block crystals of 3 were collected by filtration and washed with CH3CN and dried in air (yield: ca. 42%). Elemental analysis calcd (%) for 3: C9H11N3O8Cd: C, 26.92; H, 2.76; N, 10.46. Found: C, 27.04; H, 2.82; N, 10.39.

Synthesis of {[(H2en)0.5Cd(bci)]·2H2O}n(4)

A H2O solution (4 mL) of Cd(NO3)2·4H2O (62 mg, 0.2 mmol), H3bci (27 mg, 0.1 mmol) and ethylenediamine (20 μL) was stirred for 10 min in a 25 mL beaker and then filtered. The filtrate was left for slow evaporation at room temperature. Colorless block crystals of 4 were obtained after one week and washed with CH3CN and dried in air (yield: ca. 32%). Elemental analysis calcd (%) for 4: C10H17N4O9Cd: C, 26.71; H, 3.81; N, 12.46. Found: C, 26.95; H, 3.72; N, 12.28.

X-Ray crystallography

X-ray diffraction data were collected using a Bruker Apex II CCD diffractometer with a graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation source (λ = 0.71073 Å) at 296 K. The structures were solved by direct methods and refined by full-matrix least-squares calculations (F2) by using the SHELXTL software.10 All non-H atoms were refined in the anisotropic approximation against F2 for all reflections. All H atoms were placed at their calculated positions and refined in the isotropic approximation. Result and discussion

The H3bci ligand possesses three carbonyl groups (–CO), a protonated N atom in the C3N3 ring, and two –CH2CH2COOH arms which are highly flexible and can rotate freely around the axes (Scheme S1†). Therefore, it can potentially show diverse coordination modes and conformations during the self-assembly process by tuning the reaction conditions. However, there are no related studies about the correlation of reaction conditions and the H3bci coordination modes, as well as the final structures so far. In this contribution, we will report four new complexes which exhibit structural diversity that is dependent on the alkaline reagent. The structures of complexes 1–4 are discussed in detail in the following sections.

Crystal structure of [CdNa(bci)(H2O)3]n (1)

Compound 1 is a three-dimensional heterometallic framework which crystallizes in the triclinic space group of P. The asymmetric unit consists of one Cd2+ ion, one Na+ ion, one bci3− ligand and three coordinated water molecules. As shown in Fig. 1a, the Cd center is six-coordinated by four carboxyl oxygen atoms (O4A, O5A, O6 and O7) from two different bci3− ligands, a nitrogen atom (N1B) from the third ligand and one μ2-H2O (O1w) to furnish a distorted pentagonal pyramidal geometry. The Na+ center is located in a slightly distorted octahedral coordination environment which is made up of three coordinated water molecules (O1w, O2w and O3w), two carboxyl oxygen atoms (O5D, O7) and one carbonyl oxygen (O3C) from three different bci3− ligands. The Cd–O/N and Na–O bond lengths are in the range of 2.195–2.469 Å and 2.316–2.969 Å, respectively, in good agreement with previous studies except that there is a weak coordination bond of Na–O7 with a distance of 2.969 Å.11 The bci3− ligand adopts a hexadentate coordination mode, of which the two carboxylate groups chelate and bridge one Cd2+ ion and a Na ion in a μ2-η1:η2 mode, respectively, while a carbonyl oxygen atom and a nitrogen atom on the six-membered ring connect the third Cd2+ ion and the third Na+ ion, respectively (Scheme 1a). The Cd2+ center bridges the Na center via one μ2-Ocarboxylate and one μ2-H2O to form a Na–Cd dimer with a Na⋯Cd separation of 3.961 Å, and the adjacent dimers are further linked by a carboxylate group in a μ2-O fashion to form an infinite NaCd–(μ2-O)–NaCd chain with a Cd⋯Na distance of 4.424 Å (Fig. 1b). The chains are connected by bci3− ligands with flexible arms, a carbonyl oxygen atom and a nitrogen atom to give a 3D structure (Fig. 1c). Actually, in the structure, the bci3− ligands can link all Cd ions to give a 2D layer (Fig. 1d and S1†), while the Na+ ions are connected to each other by bci3− ligands resulting in an infinite ladder-like chain (Fig. 1e and S2†), and then the ladder-like chains insert subtly in the parallel 2D layers and interlock each other via μ2-Ocarboxylate and μ2-H2O to form the final 3D structure (Fig. 1f). If the bci3− ligand can be regarded as a 6-connected node, Cd2+ and Na+ ions can be considered as 3-connected nodes, respectively, and the novel 3D heterometallic framework can be described as a 3-nodal 3,3,6-connected T5 type network with the point symbol of {42·64·89}{42·6}{63} (Fig. 1f).

Crystal structure of [CdK(bci)(H2O)3]n (2)

Compound 2 was obtained by using the same procedure as that used for compound 1, except that KOH was used instead of NaOH. Due to the larger radius of the K+ ion, different 3D heterometallic structures of 2 are formed. It crystallizes in the triclinic crystal system with the P space group. The asymmetric unit of 2 consists of one Cd2+ ion, one K+ ion, one bci3− ligand and three coordinated water molecules. As shown in Fig. 2a, the coordination environment of the Cd2+ ion is similar to that of 1. The K+ ion is seven-coordinated, which is different from that of the Na+ center (six-coordinated) in 1. The K+ ion adopts a distorted pentagonal bipyramidal coordination geometry furnished by three water molecules (O1w, O2w and O3w), two carboxyl oxygen atoms (O5D and O7) and two carbonyl oxygen atoms (O2C and O3B) from four different bci3− ligands. The K–O bond lengths are in the range of 2.614–3.123 Å, which are similar to those of the related complexes.12 In 1 and 2, the bci3− ligand shows different coordination modes. In 1, each ligand adopts a μ6-η1:η2:η1:η2:η1:η1 coordination mode to link three Cd2+ ions and three Na+ ions, while in 2, the ligand links three Cd2+ ions and four K+ ions in a μ7-η1:η2:η1:η2:η1:η1:η1 fashion. The difference mainly comes from two carbonyl oxygen atoms involved in the coordination in 2, while in 1, only one carbonyl oxygen is involved in the coordination (Scheme 1b). In 2, the Cd–K dimer is formed via one μ2-Ocarboxylate, one μ2-H2O, a carbonyl oxygen and a N atom bridges with Cd⋯K separation of 3.969 Å, and the adjacent dimers are also further linked by another μ2-Ocarboxylate to form an infinite CdK–(μ2-O)–CdK chain; the K⋯Cd distance of 4.794 Å is obviously longer than the Na⋯Cd distance of 4.424 Å (Fig. 2c). Similar to 1, the neighboring chains are also bridged by bci3− ligands resulting in a 3D framework (Fig. 2b). However, complexes 1 and 2 exhibit completely different architectures, which can be ascribed to the different spatial arrangements of the alkaline earth ions: the Na ions in 1 can be linked by carboxylate groups of the ligand resulting in a ladder-like chain, while the K+ ions in 2 are connected by carbonyl oxygen atoms and carboxylate groups of bci3− to give a 2D double-layer structure (Fig. 2d and S3†). In 2, Cd2+ ions are also linked by bci3− ligands to give a 2D layer with the same structure as 1 (Fig. S4†), and then the 2D Cd-bci structure and 2D K-bci network are interconnected through μ2-Ocarboxylate, μ2-H2O and carbonyl oxygen and a N atom in the ring eventually forming a 3D heterometallic structure (Fig. 2e). Here, the bci3− ligand and Cd2+ and K+ ions can be considered as a 7-connected connector and 3-connected and 4-connected nodes, respectively, and the resultant structure of 2 is a new 3-nodal 3,4,7-connected topology with the point symbol of {4·62}{44·62}{45·612·84} (Fig. 2e).

Crystal structure of [Cd(Hbci)(H2O)]n (3)

Compound 3 was synthesized by using triethylamine as the alkaline reagent. Crystallographic analysis revealed that 3 crystallizes in the orthorhombic system with the Pbca space group; the asymmetric unit consists of one Cd2+ ion, one Hbci2− ligand and one coordinated water molecule. As shown in Fig. 3a, each Cd2+ center is coordinated by five carboxylate atoms (O4, O5, O6A, O7A and O6 B) and one carbonyl oxygen (O1C) from four individual ligands and one coordinated water (O1w) to furnish a slightly distorted pentagonal bipyramidal coordination geometry. The Cd–O bond lengths are in the normal range of 2.224–2.588 Å. Each Hbci2− ligand connects four Cd2+ ions, of which one carboxylate group adopts a μ1-η1:η1 bidentate chelating coordination mode and the other one is in μ2-η1:η2 chelating and bridging modes; in addition, one carbonyl oxygen atom shows a monodentate coordination mode (Scheme 1c). The Cd2+ centers are linked together by chelating and bridging carboxylate groups to form an infinite [Cd(COO)]n chain with a Cd⋯Cd distance of 4.312 Å (Fig. 3b), and then the chains as SBUs are connected by the carbonyl oxygen and carboxylate groups of the ligands to give a novel 3D structure (Fig. 3c and S6†). In 3, each ligand bridges four different Cd2+ ions, and each Cd2+ ion is coordinated by four different ligands; further topological analysis shows that the framework of 3 could be classified as a 4-connected uninodal cag topology with the point symbol of {4·65} (Fig. 3d).

Crystal structure of {[(H2en)0.5Cd(bci)]·2H2O}n (4)

Compound 4 was synthesized by using ethylenediamine as the alkaline reagent. Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that compound 4 is a 2D double-layer structure in the triclinic system with the P space group. The asymmetric unit consists of one Cd2+ ion, one bci3− ligand, two guest water molecules, and half of a protonated ethylenediamine molecule. This half of the ethylenediamine molecule lies about an inversion centre. As shown in Fig. 4a, the Cd2+ ion is six-coordinated and bonded by five oxygen atoms (O1B, O5C, O5D, O6A and O7A) and one nitrogen atom (N1) from five individual bci3− ligands to complete a slightly distorted octahedral geometry. The Cd–O/N bond lengths are in the normal range of 2.318–2.582 Å. Each bci3− ligand connects five Cd2+ ions by one monodentate carbonyl oxygen atom, a monodentate nitrogen atom, a μ2-η0:η2 bidentate bridging carboxylate group and one μ1-η1:η1 monodentate chelating carboxylate group (Scheme 1d). Two Cd2+ ions are connected together by two μ2-η0:η2-bridging carboxylate groups to form a [Cd2(COO)2] dimer with a Cd⋯Cd distance of 3.336 Å. Then, the dimer units as SBUs are united together by six bci3− ligands to form a 2D double-layer structure (Fig. 4b). From the perspective of topology, the [Cd2(COO)2] SBU can be regarded as a 6-connector and each ligand as a 3-connector; complex 4 can be simplified to a 2-nodal (3, 6)-connected 2D kgd net with the point symbol of {43}2{46·66·83} (Fig. 4c). It should be noted that guest water molecules and protonated ethylenediamine insert in the adjacent 2D layers, and then the adjacent 2D layers can be further connected by hydrogen bonds to generate a 3D supramolecular structure (Fig. 4d).

Influence of the alkaline reagent on the coordination modes of the ligand and the structures

From the structural descriptions above, it is clear that compounds 1–4 exhibit diverse structures with different topologies by the similar reaction of the H3bci ligand with the Cd2+ ion in aqueous solution. It can be seen that different alkaline reagents, such as NaOH, KOH, triethylamine and ethylenediamine, used in the reaction system have a vital influence on the construction of these complexes. Compounds 1 and 2 are two different 3D heterometallic frameworks with an infinite heterometallic chain as a SBU, showing a 3-nodal 3,3,6-connected T5 type net and a new 3-nodal 3,4,7-connected topology, respectively. Complex 3 is a cag type framework with a 1D [Cd(COO)]n chain as a SBU, while complex 4 is a 2D double-layer kgd type network with a Cd2 dimer as a SBU. The diverse structures of 1–4 based on different SBUs are present because of a variety of coordination modes and different coordination numbers of the ligand under the influence of the alkaline reagent. In this work, H3bci ligands show four unreported coordination fashions (Schemes 1 and S1†); in complex 1, it exists in a fully deprotonated form, adopts a cis–trans conformation and can connect six metal ions by two μ2-η1:η2 carboxylate groups, one N atom and one carbonyl oxygen, while in compound 2, with the increase in the radius of alkaline earth metal ion, the coordination number of the ligand increases to seven; the conformation and the coordination mode of the ligand are the same as those in 1 except another carbonyl oxygen is also involved in the coordination. In 3, each ligand exists in a partially deprotonated form and shows a cis–cis conformation, which can coordinate to four Cd2+ ions by μ1-η1:η1 and μ2-η1:η2 carboxylate groups and one carbonyl oxygen. In 4, the bci3− ligand shows a cis–trans conformation and a completely deprotonated form; it can connect five Cd2+ ions through one carbonyl oxygen, one N and μ1-η1:η1 and μ2-η0:η2 carboxylate groups. From above, we can see that the flexibility and diverse coordination modes of the H3bci ligand may directly affect the final structure. These results imply that the different alkaline reagent in this system is very important to determine the final architectures of these complexes, which prompted us to continue to explore the correlation of external reaction conditions and the coordination modes of the H3bci ligand, as well as the resultant structure.

Thermal stability and PXRD pattern

To study the thermal stabilities of complexes 1–4, thermal gravimetric analysis was conducted on polycrystalline samples under N2 atmosphere (Fig. 5). Compound 1 shows an initial two-step weight loss of 11.03% from room temperature to 170 °C, corresponding to the loss of three coordinated water molecules (calcd. 11.76%), and then the framework begins to decompose at 320 °C. For complex 2, the weight loss in 110–230 °C is consistent with the removal of three coordinated water molecules (obsd 11.19%, calcd 11.37%), and above 305 °C, a rapid weight loss occurs owing to the decomposition of the organic ligand. The TG curve of 3 shows; the first weight loss of 5.02% at 200–230 °C, which indicates the elimination of a coordinated water molecule (calcd. 4.48%). After that, the framework was stable up to 290 °C and then began to collapse. Complex 4 shows a gradual weight loss of 14.27% from room temperature to 180 °C, suggesting the release of two lattice water molecules and half of a protonated ethylenediamine molecule (calcd 14.91%). Above 260 °C, a rapid weight loss occurs owing to the decomposition of the organic ligand. The X-ray powder diffraction (PXRD) patterns of as-synthesized 1–4 matched the simulated patterns well, indicating that the polycrystalline samples are pure phase, respectively. The slight differences in intensity between the simulated and experimental peaks may be ascribed to the preferred orientation of the crystals. Complex 4 was dried in vacuo at 100 °C and 180 °C for 2 and 10 hours to partially and completely remove guest water and ethylenediamine molecules resulting in 4a and 4b, respectively; the PXRD patterns of 4a and 4b are still in good agreement with the as-synthesized ones, which indicates that complex 4 can maintain a framework after removing the guest molecules (Fig. S7†).

Photoluminescence

Taking into account the excellent luminescence properties of d10 metal complexes,13 the photoluminescence (PL) spectra of 1–4 and H3bci were investigated in the solid state at room temperature. As shown in Fig. 6, H3bci exhibits two emission peaks at 365 and 467 nm upon excitation at 280 nm, which is ascribed to the π*–π or π*–n transition of the intraligands. Under the same experimental conditions, the emission peaks occur at 367 and 468 nm for 1, 368 and 468 nm for 2, and 366 and 468 nm for 3, respectively. The emissions of the three compounds are generally of a ligand-centred (LC) and/or ligand-to-ligand charge transfer (LLCT) nature since their emission peaks are almost coincident with that of the free ligand.14 The observation of a negligible red shift relative to that of the free ligand (λ = 365 nm) may be a result of the coordination of the ligands to the metal centers. Unexpectedly, compound 4 only exhibits a visible broad emission peak near 435 nm (λex = 280 nm), and no obvious emission peaks of the ligand are found, which can be tentatively attributed to the amino group emission of the guest ethylenediamine molecule;15 amino groups can emit fluorescence spectra and has been found in the poly(amido amine) (PAMAM) dendrimer.16 To further confirm the fluorescence emission peak of complex 4 derived from the guest ethylenediamine molecule, the PL spectra of 4a and 4b were investigated, respectively. The emission spectrum of 4a reveal that the broad emission peak becomes weak, and the peaks of the intraligand emission appear at 371 and 467 nm (λex = 280 nm), while 4b shows that the broad peak disappeared, and instead, two typical intraligand emission peaks of 367 and 468 nm (λex = 280 nm) are found. The PL spectra results of 4, 4a and 4b confirm that the ethylenediamine molecule can indeed emit strong luminescence. To our knowledge, there is no work on the fluorescence properties of the coordination polymer containing guest ethylenediamine molecules have been reported up to now. This result tells us that we can introduce different organic amine molecules into the coordination polymer and further study the luminescence effects of different organic amine on the coordination polymer.

Conclusion

In summary, four new coordination polymers have been successfully synthesized from the reactions of flexible H3bci ligand and Cd(NO3)2 under different alkaline reagent of NaOH, KOH, triethylamine and ethylenediamine in aqueous solution, respectively. Complexes 1 and 2 are two different 3D heterometallic frameworks with a T5 type net and a new topology, respectively, while complexes 3 and 4 are two monometallic cadmium complexes displaying 3D and 2D architectures with cag and kgd topologies, respectively. Through investigation on their structural differences, we have found that the alkaline reagent plays a vital role during the self-assembly process, resulting in different conformations and coordination modes of the H3bci ligand, as well as the subsequent structures. So, our present research further enriches the crystal engineering strategy and offers more possibilities of controlling the formation of the desired framework. In addition, complexes 1–4 show intense luminescence emissions at room temperature. Interestingly, the luminescence spectrum of 4 is attributed to the contribution of the guest ethylenediamine molecule, which has not been reported in the coordination polymer system, so it opens a new route to the study of the luminescence of the organic amine molecule in coordination polymers.
