Introduction

Supercapacitors have been considered as potential energy storage devices due to their much higher energy densities, greater power densities and longer cycling ability than those of dielectric capacitors and conventional rechargeable batteries.1,2 There has been an increasing demand for novel supercapacitors with higher performances to be utilized in newly emerging large scale applications, such as grid storage and hybrid electric vehicles.3 Conventional electrode materials could not satisfy the necessary standards because of their improper porous structures, low capacitance, high cost and poor stability.4,5 Therefore, rapid development of novel-innovative supercapacitive electrode materials with high performance is essential.

In the past two decades, metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have been established as a booming class of materials with variable structures and tunable properties, and have drawn great attention due to their wide potential applications.6–8 Recently, the development of MOF-based materials as supercapacitors utilized in the most important energy storage devices has attracted a rapidly growing interest due to their controllable micro-porous structure, tunable pore size and large surface areas.9,10 There are two main strategies for supercapacitive applications of MOFs materials: (1) MOFs are utilized as a template for preparing metal oxides,11,12 metal nanoparticles13 and porous carbon compounds for supercapacitors;14,15 and (2) as-synthesized MOFs materials are directly used as active electrode materials for supercapacitors.16 Compared to the first strategy, the corresponding studies on MOFs directly used as active electrode materials for supercapacitors are still less explored and rarely reported. In practice, after validation by the research result of nearly a decade, the direct use of pristine MOFs as supercapacitor-active materials deserves much more attention, which would not only expand the applied fields of MOF materials but also provide more candidates.

To obtain more higher capacitance electrode materials for supercapacitors from directly used MOFs, an effective route is to design and synthesize novel MOFs with pseudo-capacitive redox metal cations and sufficiently large pores/space for the diffusion and storage of electrolyte solution.15,16 For example, Co-based MOFs exhibited a capacitance of approximately 200 F g−1 in LiOH solution, which must be ascribed to the poor electrical conductivity and steric hindrance to ion insertion.17 However, a layered cobalt-based MOF exhibited a specific capacitance up to 2564 F g−1 at a current density of 1 A g−1 in a 6 M KOH aqueous solution. Such high performance must be attributed to the intrinsic structural characteristics including its layered structure and favorable exposed facets.18 Comparably, the proper porous structures of MOFs for supercapacitors must play an important role in affecting the transport pathway for electrons19 and eliminating steric limitations by the electrolyte ion with its solvation shell.20 Moreover, active metal sites decorated on the surface of pores should also be accompanied by matched electrolyte solution, since the predominant charge storage mechanism of high capacitance is related to the pseudocapacitive reaction.21 Until now, most of the research work has focused on cobalt- and nickel-based MOFs, and less based on copper-based MOFs, since the presented Cu-based MOFs normally exhibit a low capacitive performance.22

With all the above considerations in mind, to synthesize high capacitance electrode materials for supercapacitors from directly used Cu-MOFs, we selected a bi-functional ligand (HL = 4,2′:6′,4′′-terpyridine-4′-carboxylic acid) based on the below considerations: the multi-coordination sites of the ligand are very beneficial for constructing the desired MOFs materials with expected stability,23 which is essential for MOFs used as active materials in supercapacitors. The inclusion of pyridyl rings and halogen ions into the MOFs structure could modulate the properties of electron donor or acceptor and improve the specific capacity and cycle stability, which have been validated by the strategy of decorating graphene structures with heteroatoms.24,25 In accordance with our assumption, two isostructural 3D copper-based MOFs of the formula {[Cu2Cl(OH)(L)2]·(CH3OH)4}n (1) and {[Cu2Br(OH)(L)2]·(CH3OH)4}n (2) with different halogen ions have been synthesized. Their synthesis, crystal structures and electrochemical performance as an electrode material for supercapacitors are reported in this paper. The introduction of halogen ions into the Cu-based MOFs must be responsible for enhancing the whole electrochemical performance. In order to give direct comparisons, another Cu-framework, {[Cu(H2O)(L)2]·(H2O)4}n (3), without halogens has been constructed after optimizing the synthesis conditions. The lack of halogen-bridged clusters and porosity could be responsible for the low supercapacitor ability compared to 1 and 2.

Results and discussion

Synthesis of 1–3

The crystalline samples were synthesized using the solvothermal method. The synthesis of complexes 1 and 2 was carried out using the same solvent systems and different metal salts. A similar procedure was adapted for 3, except for the solvent system. In the IR spectrum, the characteristic absorption peaks of the deprotonated carboxyl group and other functional groups are clearly presented. The strong bands around 1610 and 1383 cm−1 are ascribed to the asymmetric and symmetric stretching vibrations of the deprotonated carboxylate group. No band in the region of 1690–1735 cm−1 indicates a complete deprotonation of the carboxylate groups and is consistent with the result of single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis.

Crystal structures of 1 and 2

When the bi-functional ligand HL reacted with the different salts of copper(i) halogen, two isostructural 3D crystal structures of 1 and 2 were obtained. The detailed description of 1 is presented as an example. Single-crystal X-ray analysis reveals that 1 crystallizes in a monoclinic C2/c space group, and the asymmetric unit consists of one copper atom, one chloride anion, one μ2-bridged hydroxyl ion (O3 atom) and one L ligand (Fig. 1, top). The Cu1 atom is penta-coordinated and adopts the slightly distorted square pyramid coordination geometry. The equatorial sites are occupied by two oxygen atoms from one monodentate carboxylate group and one hydroxyl ion and two pyridyl nitrogen atoms, while the axial positions are occupied by the chloride ion. The Cu–O/N bond lengths fall in the region of 1.943(3) to 2.054(4) Å, in accordance with the typical values of the Cu(ii) state. In addition, the Cu–Cl bond length is 2.539(2) Å. Two central-symmetric Cu1 ions are inter-connected by the μ2-bridged hydroxyl ion and chloride ion to form the di-nuclear secondary building unit, which is further stabilized by two mono-dentate carboxyl groups and four pyridyl groups from six different ligands. Each bi-functional linker bonds to three copper ions from three dinuclear nodes to form the 3D network with 1D channels along the c-axial direction (11.2 × 12.4 Å, Fig. 1 bottom). Considering the bi-functional ligands and dinuclear nodes as three- and six-connecting nodes, the single network should possess a typical binodal 3,6-connected TiSc2 net with the point Schläfli symbol {4·62}2{42·610·83} calculated with TOPOS.26 Furthermore, the large pores are blocked by mutual interpenetration of identical 3D frameworks, generating a novel two-fold interpenetrating architecture, shown in Fig. 2. 1D channels are left in the structure along the c-axial direction, which is filled with guest molecules. The complicated hydrogen bonding interactions and πpy⋯πpy interactions between the two interpenetrating frameworks must be responsible for the final stable crystal structure. Calculated using the PLATON routine, the solvent accessible volume in the dehydrated structure of 1 is about 30.1%.

Substituting the copper(i) salt as copper(i) bromide, the same crystal structures could be obtained, which could be validated by single crystal and power X-ray diffraction measurements (Table 1, Fig. S2 and S3†). The Cu–O/N bond lengths fall in the region of 2.022(3) to 2.060(4) Å, in accordance with the typical values of Cu(ii) state. In addition, the Cu–Br bond length is 2.662(2) Å. The 3D network with 1D channels along the c-axial direction (10.7 × 11.6 Å) has been presented. Calculated using the PLATON routine, the solvent accessible volume in the dehydrated structure of 1 is about 26.1%, which must be ascribed to the larger volume of bromide ions compared to chloride ones.

Crystal structures of 3

By utilizing the CuI as a metal salt, a different 2D layer structure had been synthesized. Single-crystal X-ray analysis reveals that 1 crystallizes in a monoclinic P21/c space group, and the asymmetric unit consists of one copper atom, one coordinated water molecule and two L ligands. Each copper atom is penta-coordinated and adopts the slightly distorted square pyramid coordination geometry. The equatorial sites are occupied by two oxygen atoms from one monodentate carboxylate group and two pyridyl nitrogen atoms, while the axial positions are occupied by the water molecule. The Cu–O/N bond lengths fall in the region of 1.942(3) to 2.055(4) Å, in accordance with the typical values of the Cu(ii) state. Each bi-functional linker bonds to two copper ions to form the 2D layer network (Fig. 3), which stacks over each other. No accessible pores could be observed in the final structure.

Thermal stability analyses

To further estimate the thermal stability of these complexes, their thermal behaviors were investigated by thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) under N2 atmosphere (Fig. S4†). For 1, the first weight loss occurred from 35 °C to 110 °C, indicating the release of the lattice molecules. The weight loss was about 15%. The residual framework starts to decompose beyond 300 °C. For 2, similar thermal behavior to 1 could be observed. The two-interpenetrating framework structures in 1 and 2 must be responsible for the higher thermal stability.

N2 adsorption properties of 1 and 2

The highly porous and stable framework makes 1 and 2 good candidates for gas storage. To check the permanent porosities of 1 and 2, N2 sorption isotherms at 77 K were collected for the desolvated samples obtained by soaking 1 and 2 in MeOH and CH2Cl2 and then vacuum-drying at room temperature and at 80 °C overnight, respectively. As shown in Fig. S5,†1 and 2 display typical type-I adsorption isotherms with a small hysteresis, corresponding to the other ones of micro-porous materials. As expected, sample 1 and 2 adsorb 97 and 80 cm3 g−1 of N2 at 77 K, and the Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) are 302 and 252 m2 g−1, and Langmuir surface areas are 384 and 298 m2 g−1, respectively, indicating the possible space void for adsorbing guest molecules. The existence of proper voids is very important for the supercapacitive properties of two MOFs since the large sizes of the channels are not fit for the improvement of supercapacitive properties. Therefore, the porous structures in these two MOFs are very beneficial for the transport of guest molecules and electrolyte.

Electrochemical performance for supercapacitors

Due to the large specific surface areas and large pore volumes, 1 and 2 might exhibit considerable potential application in high-performance supercapacitors.27 To examine the electrochemical performance in the supercapacitor, crystalline samples of 1 and 2 have been fabricated as supercapacitor electrodes and were intensively investigated by cyclic voltammetry (CV), charge–discharge curves and galvanostatic charge–discharge (GCD) cycling tests in a three-electrode system by using a 6 M KOH electrolyte.

Firstly, to confirm the applicability of 1 and 2 in the supercapacitor, the CV and charge–discharge curves of 1 and 2 electrodes have been studied with a scan rate of 50 mV s−1 and a density current of 0.5 A g−1, respectively. As seen from Fig. S6,† each CV curve exhibits a couple of redox peaks, appearing at around 0.3 V and 0.15 V, indicating that the pseudo capacitive behavior is mainly ascribed to redox reactions caused by the redox reactions of the cations in electrode materials during the test process.28 Comparing between 1 and 2, 1 exhibits the larger CV area. Fig. S7† presents typical GCD curves of 1 and 2 electrodes at a density current of 0.5 A g−1. Similar discharge curves for the two electrodes consisting of two clear voltage stages have been presented: a fast potential drop from 0.4 to 0.2 V, and a slow potential decay from 0.2 to 0 V. The former corresponds to the electric double-layer capacitance from the charge separation on the electrode/electrolyte interface, and the latter represents the pseudocapacitive feature of the electrode. Accordingly, 1 also exhibits a longer discharging time. These results demonstrate 1 possesses a better specific capacitance compared to 2.29

To further characterize the supercapacitive properties of 1 and 2, two electrodes were further investigated by a series of electrochemical measurements. Fig. 4 and S6† illustrates the CV curves with scan rates that varied from 5 to 200 mV s−1 for 1 and 2. When the scan rate was increased from 5 to 200 mVs−1, the separation between the two redox peaks increased slightly due to small over potential of the electrode.30 It was noticed that the redox peaks were still significantly observed even at 200 mV s−1, indicating that this material had a good rate capability. The specific capacitance C (farads per gram) originating from CV curves can be estimated according to the equation C = ∫ IdV/2υΔVm.31 Where I, ΔV, υ and m denote the instant current (A), the potential change (V), the scan rate (mV s−1) and the mass of samples (g). After calculation, the specific capacitance of the 1 and 2 electrodes are 1550 and 934 F g−1 at 5 mV s−1, which obviously decrease to 695 and 387 F g−1 when the scan rate increases to 200 mV s−1. In addition, the slight deformation of CV curves from 100 to 200 mV s−1 might be caused by the restriction of mass transfer or ionic transportation at high scan rates.32

The GCD curves of the 1 and 2 electrodes between 0 and 0.4 V at a density current from 0.5 to 20 A g−1 have been also measured, and are shown in Fig. 5. Afterwards, the specific capacitance C (farads per gram) originating from discharge curves was estimated based on the equation C = It/mΔV.33 Where I, s, m and ΔV represent the constant current (A) applied to the charge–discharge processes, the consumed time (s) for the discharge process, the weight (g) of the active material (1 or 2) on the electrode and the potential range (V). The calculated results indicated that the specific capacitance of 1 electrode is up to 1148 F g−1 at a current density of 0.5 A g−1, which is higher than that of the values of the 2 electrode (688 F g−1). As the current densities increase, the specific capacitances tend to decrease (Fig. 6a). The decline tendency is mainly attributed to the poor availability of the active materials at high current densities.34 When the current densities increase to 20 A g−1, the specific capacitance values are 886 and 495 F g−1 for 1 and 2. Compared to the values at 0.5 A g−1, the small decline ratio indicates the pore channels in the crystal structures of 1 and 2 are beneficial to the exchange and utilization of active materials. The presence of polar sites as oxygen atoms and halogen ions on the surface of channels might be responsible for improving the surface properties and cause pseudocapacitance in 1 and 2. In addition, there is obvious deviation of the discharge curve from a straight line, which demonstrates that the capacitance mainly comes from the Faradic redox reaction of the 1 and 2 species in the materials.35

The cycling performances were evaluated by a charge–discharge cycling test at a current density of 10 A g−1. Fig. 6b shows that the specific capacitance of the 1 and 2 electrodes retain 90 and 82% of the initial capacitance after 2000 continuous cycles. The result indicates that these two Cu-MOFs electrodes have good cycling stability, which is important for practical applications. The distinction between the cycling stability of the 1 and 2 electrodes might be ascribed to the different halogen bridges. Larger Cu–Br bond length might not be beneficial for cycling stability during the charge–discharge cycling test compared with Cu–Cl bond length.

Similar electrochemical measurements have been carried out with sample 3, and the corresponding results are listed in the ESI† (Fig. S11–13). When the current densities increase to 20 A g−1, the specific capacitance value is 122 F g−1 for 3. The lack of solvent accessible channels must be responsible for the low supercapacitive ability, even the uncoordinated N atoms are decorated on the surface of the 2D layer structure.

Combining all the results and analysis, the best electrochemical performance of 1 and 2 could be strongly attributed to the following cooperative characteristics: (1) the proper surface area and porosity could provide the necessary interfaces for the development of the electrostatic charge separation layer and ensures facile OH− intercalation and de-intercalation, as shown in Fig. 7, which is favorable for improving the storage ability of the materials. Although it contains the same terpyridine ligand, compound 3 lacks the effective channel structure, and could not provide enough space for electrolyte storage.36 (2) The decorated hydroxyl ions, nitrogen and halogen atoms on the surface of the channels could have acted as the interaction sites, which should play an important role in improving the surface properties, promoting the electron transport and resulting in a small electron-transfer resistance (Fig. S9 and S10†).37 (3) The adjacent two-fold interpenetrating structures could be from an electron transport channel. As shown in Fig. 7, the rectangular units on the channels left in the two-fold interpenetrating frameworks are easily conductive for electrons. These conductive units are interconnected together along the b and c direction to form the electron transport layer, which could promote electron transport, resulting in a small electron transfer resistance.16 Therefore, such a series of Cu-MOF materials could exhibit a high electrochemical performance in supercapacitors. The comparisons between the other pristine MOFs materials as active electrode materials and Cu-MOFs in this work have been listed in the ESI.† Although a proper porous structure has been observed in the other frameworks, the introduction of halide ions and hydroxyl ions would elevate the efficiency of electro-chemical properties via the comparison between the Cu-LCP and Cu-MOF@C, since the organic linker was a tetrafluoroterephthalate.38

Conclusions

In conclusion, we obtained a series of halogen-bridged di-nuclear Cu based 3D porous MOFs via the solvothermal method, which were further structurally characterized. These Cu-based frameworks exhibit similar two-interpenetrating frameworks, and 1D channels were left in the packing structures. Afterwards, these MOF materials were applied as electrode active materials in supercapacitors. Compound 1 presents a high specific capacitance and good cycling stability in 6 M KOH electrolyte compared to 2. The maximum specific capacitance of the compound 1 electrode could reach 1148 F g−1 at a current density of 0.5 A g−1, along with a high capacitance of 90% retained after 2000 cycles at a current density of 10 A g−1 in 6 M KOH solution. The successful introduction of halogen and hydroxyl ions into the final structures might be responsible for the high performance of the supercapacitor, which makes these Cu MOFs a candidate for electrode active materials for electrochemical energy storage. Our future work will focus on exploring the structural–activity relationship of pristine MOFs materials and their supercapacitor properties.
