Synthesis, spectral characterization and crystal structures of benzyltin complexes with (E)-4-chloro-N′-(2-hydroxy-4-methoxybenzylidene)benzohydrazide

Abstract

The reactions of the Schiff base ligand (E)-4-chloro-N′-(2-hydroxy-4-methoxybenzylidene)benzohydrazide (L) with substituted dibenzyltin dichlorides yielded a series of monobenzyltin complexes with the general formula (RC7H6)Sn(L)(S)Cl, where R = p-CH3, p-Cl, H, p-F, p-Br, o-CH3, o-F and S = H2O, methanol. An exception to this was obtained for the reaction with di(o-chlorobenzyl)tin dichloride, in which the di(o-chlorobenzyl)tin complex, (o-ClC7H6)2Sn(L) was the sole product. An attempt to displace the solvent molecule in the monobenzyltin complexes with 4,4,-bipyridine resulted in a mixture of products, containing a co-crystal of the methanol coordinated monobenzyltin complex and 4,4-bipyridine, (RC7H6)Sn(L)(CH3OH)Cl·4,4-bipyridine, and a six-coordinated dibenzyltin complex, (RC7H6)2Sn(L)Cl·CH3OH. If the donor ligand 4,4′-bipyridine or 4,4′-bipyridine N,N′-dioxide (L′) was added in-situ during the preparation of the benzyltin complexes, either a dibenzyltin complex, (RC7H6)2Sn(L), or a binuclear dibenzyltin complex with the donor ligand (L′), (RC7H6)2SnCl2(L′) [R = p-F], was obtained. Interestingly, both cis- and trans-(RC7H6)2SnCl2(L′) complexes could be formed, as evidenced from their crystal structures. The structural formula of all the products were confirmed by elemental analysis, IR, 1H and 13C NMR spectroscopic studies and by X-ray analysis.

1. Introduction

Organotin complexes have been extensively studied, not only because of their industrial applications, such as biocides, antifouling agents, polymer stabilizers and wood preservers [1], [2], [3], but also owing to their importance in biological applications. Their usage as anti-inflammatory, anti-microbial, antifungal and antioxidants are also well documented [4], [5], [6], [7]. In recent years, investigations have been carried out to test the cytotoxicity and antitumor activity of organotin(IV) complexes with Schiff bases [8], [9]. It has been found that, in general, the biochemical activity of organotin compounds is influenced by the structure of the resulting complexes, and the nature and number of organic groups bound to the tin center [10], [11], [12], [13], [14], [15], [16]. Organotin derivatives exhibit good to high cytotoxicity against various human cancer cell lines and are often more potent than cis-diamminedichloroplatinum (cisplatin) [17], [18], [19], [20]. The large possibility for the variation of the organic moieties and donor ligands linked to the tin center has further strengthened their applications. It has been reported that benzyltin(IV) complexes with Schiff base ligands show high selectivity between the cancer cell lines HCT-8, HL-60 and A549 [21]. As a continuation of the previous work [20], [22], herein we reported the synthesis and characterization of a series of organotin(IV) derivatives derived from the condensation reaction of 4-chlorobenzyhydrazide and 4-methoxybenzaldehyde.

2. Experimental

2.1. Materials

The following commercial chemicals of reagent grade were used as supplied in the synthesis: 4-chlorobenzhydrazide, 2-hydroxy-4-methoxybenzaldehyde, 4-methylbenzyl chloride, 4-chlorobenzyl chloride, benzyl chloride, 2-chlorobenzyl chloride, 4-fluorobenzyl chloride, 4-bromobenzyl bromide, 2-methylbenzyl chloride, 2-fluorobenzyl chloride, 4,4′-bipyridine and 4,4′-bipyridine-N,N′-dioxide. The ligand was prepared as reported [23]. Substituted dibenzyltin dihalides were prepared from the substituted benzyl chloride or bromide according to the literature method [24]. Methanol, which was used as the solvent medium in the preparation of the organotin complexes, was procured commercially and dried over a molecular sieve prior to use.

2.2. Physical measurements

The melting points of the ligand and its organotin complexes were determined on an electrothermal melting point apparatus, and were uncorrected. 1H, 13C and 119Sn NMR spectra were measured in DMSO-d6 at ambient temperature on JEOL Lambda 400 FT-NMR and JOEL JNM GX-270 FT-NMR System spectrometers operating at 400.14 MHz for 1H and 100.63 MHz for 13C NMR. Infrared spectra were recorded in the range 4000–400 cm−1 using a Perkin-Elmer 2000 FTIR spectrophotometer. Elemental analysis was performed in the in-house microanalytical laboratory using a Perkin-Elmer EA2400 CHNS Elemental Analyzer. Single-crystal X-ray data collections were carried out at 100 K using graphite-monochromated Mo Kα (λ = 0.71073 Å) radiation on a Bruker SMART APEXII CCD diffractometer. All structures were solved by direct methods and refined using the full-matrix least-squares procedures on F2 using the shelx-97 program [25]. The data sets were corrected for absorption effects based on multiple scans. Non-hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic displacement parameters. The positions of hydrogen atoms were calculated and included in the structure factor calculations. The molecular graphics were drawn using the xseed plotting program [26].

2.3. Synthesis of the benzyltin complexes

2.3.1. General procedures

Similar procedures were used for the preparation of the benzyltin complexes C1–C8. A representative example for the preparation of the complex C1 is given below.

0.40 g (1.0 mmol) of di(p-methylbenzyl)tin dichloride in 10 ml of methanol was added to a 25 ml of hot methanolic solution of the ligand L (0.30 g, 1.0 mmol). The solution mixture was refluxed with vigorous stirring for 3.5 h and then filtered. The filtrate was evaporated until a precipitate was obtained. The product was filtered, washed with a minimum amount of methanol and dried in air.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Synthetic aspects

The Schiff base ligand (E)-4-chloro-N′-(2-hydroxy-4-methoxybenzylidene)benzohydrazide (L), which enolized in the solution state, was prepared from the condensation reaction of 4-chlorobenzhydrazide with 4-methoxybenzaldehyde (Fig. 3.1). The enolized form of this tridentate ligand L provides three possible binding modes to the metal, as depicted in Fig. 3.2, where the ligand can either act as a monobasic bidentate (L-A), monobasic tridentate (L-B) or dibasic tridentate (L-C) chelator [27]. The organotin complexes were prepared from the reaction of the ligand with the respective diorganotin dihalides, using methanol as the refluxing medium. In this work, the ligand behaves as a dibasic tridentate chelator, replacing the two anionic chloride moieties and coordinating to the tin atom via the enolic oxygen, imino nitrogen and the phenolic oxygen atoms. The resulting substituted dibenzyltin complexes subsequently reacted with HCl, which was a by-product in the reaction, and this resulted in the cleavage of one of the substituted benzyl groups, giving rise to a series of monobenzyltin complexes, C1–C3A and C5A–C8. An exception to this reaction pattern was observed for the di(o-chlorobenzyl)tin complex C4, in which the two o-chlorobenzyl groups are strongly bound to the tin atom, probably due to a significant intramolecular interaction between the chloride substituent and the tin atom which further stabilises the molecular structure. The monobenzyltin complexes were found to be solvent (water or methanol) coordinated in order to achieve a six-coordinated tin geometry. An attempt to displace the solvent molecule with a donor ligand such as pyridine, 4,4′-bipyridine or4,4′-bipyridine N,N′-dioxide was unsuccessful. Interestingly, in the case of the reaction of C5A with 4,4′-bipyridine, the product was found to be a mixture containing a co-crystal of the methanol coordinated monobenzyltin and 4,4-bipyridine (C10) and a six-coordinated dibenzyltin complex (C9). Surprisingly, in the case of complex C9, the ligand behaves as a monobasic tridentate chelator where the carbonyl oxygen of the ligand is bound to the tin center via a coordination bond (Fig. 3.2). The formation of C9 was probably due to a small amount of the intermediate dibenzyltin complex which undergoes an addition reaction with HCl to give the dibenzyltin complex, C9. If the donor ligand was added in situ during the preparation of the benzyltin complexes, the isolated product was either the dibenzyltin complex, such as in the case of C3B, or the dibenzyltin complex with the donor ligand, such as in the case of C11 and C12. The dibenzyltin complex C3B failed to undergo a cleavage reaction, probably due to the removal of the HCl by-product by the basic donor molecules. The formation of C11 and C12 indicates that dibenzyltin dichloride tends to form a more stable polymeric complex with 4,4′-bipyridine N,N′-dioxide than a dibenzyltin complex with the Schiff base. Interestingly, the cis-dibenzyltin (C11) and trans-dibenzyltin (C12) complexes were obtained, as evidenced from their crystal structures. The structural formula of all the products were confirmed by elemental analysis, IR, 1H and 13C NMR spectroscopic studies and by X-ray analysis.

3.2. Spectroscopic characterization

The IR spectra of the ligand L displayed characteristic bands for ν(CO) and ν(CN) at 1655 and 1625 cm−1, respectively. The stretching frequencies of the phenolic –OH and –NH groups appeared as a strong envelope in the range 3200–3500 cm−1 [27]. In the OH stretching frequency region, the ν(–OH) peak disappears in all the monobenzyltin and dibenzyltin complexes, indicating the deprotonation of OH group and the coordination of the phenolic oxygen atom to the tin atom [27]. Similarly, in the carbonyl stretching frequency region, the ν(CO) peak disappears due to the enolization of the carbonyl group to facilitate the formation of the second covalent Sn–O bond. Strong bands in the region 1600–1610 cm−1 are assigned to the ν(CN) stretching. It was also noted that ν(CN) for the benzyltin complexes appears at a lower frequency, supporting the fact that the ligand is also coordinated to the tin atom via the azomethine nitrogen [28]. Attempts were not made to identify the ν(Sn–O), ν(Sn–C) and ν(Sn–N) bands due to the complex pattern in the region below 600 cm−1 in the spectra.

The 1H and 13C NMR spectral data of the free ligand and its organotin complexes are given in Table 3.1, Table 3.2, respectively. The chemical shifts for the aromatic protons and carbons are found within the expected ranges. The 1H NMR spectra of the benzyltin complexes (except for C9) did not show any resonance signals for the O–H and N–H protons of the Schiff base ligand, which are expected to be found in the region between 11 and 12 ppm. In addition, the chemical shift for the azomethine proton in the spectrum of the ligand, which was observed as a sharp singlet at 8.55 ppm, was found to have shifted by about 0.2–0.3 ppm, which further confirmed the successful complexation of the ligand to the tin atom via the enolic oxygen, imino nitrogen and phenolic oxygen atoms. The spectra of the benzyltin complexes were also found to exhibit resonance signals characteristic of benzyl –CH2 protons in the expected intensity ratio. The additional broad strong singlet at ∼3.40 ppm or ∼3.18 ppm is due to the coordinated solvent molecule (water or methanol), particularly in the monobenzyltin complexes. The multiplet which appeared in the range 7.85–8.74 ppm for complexes C10, C11 and C12 is assigned to the aryl protons of the bipyridyl moiety [29]. The 13C NMR spectra of the complexes also show significant downfield shifts of the azomethine carbons resonances (∼164 ppm) compared to that of the free ligand (∼149 ppm).

The heteronuclear coupling constant values are informative in assigning the geometry and coordinating environment of diorganotin complexes. The CSnC angle is calculated using the Lockhart and Manders equation [30], θ(CSnC) = 0.0161|2J(119Sn1H)|2 − 1.32|2J(119Sn1H)| + 133.4. CSnC angle can also be estimated from the 1J(119Sn13C) coupling constant with the Lockhart equation, θ(CSnC) = [|1J(119Sn13C)| + 875]/11.4. In the present study, the calculated CSnC angles from the 2J(119Sn1H) values for the dibenzyltin complexes C3B, C4 and C9 are 132.24, 109.6 and 179.93°, respectively, indicating a penta-coordination at tin for C3B and C4 [22], [25], whereas the CSnC angle for C9 was found to correspond to a six coordinated complex [14]. The magnitude of the CSnC angles are slightly different from those observed in the solid state structures, as determined by X-ray crystallography (147.24° for C3B, 128.19° for C4 and 162.6° for C9). The inconsistency between the calculated CSnC angles with those observed from X-ray crystallography is mainly the result of less bending of the CSnC angles due to the greater rigidity in the solid state.

The 119Sn NMR spectra data of the benzyltin complexes, except for C10, C11 and C12, are listed in Table 3.2. The 119Sn chemical shifts have been used to predict the coordination environment of the tin atom in the complexes. In the case of the dibenzyltin(IV) complexes C3B and C4, the 119Sn NMR resonances occurred in the region −300 to −400 ppm, which are in accord with their penta-coordination [22], [27], [31] in the solid state. On the other hand, the 119Sn NMR spectra of the monobenzyltin complexes revealed a single signal in the region −490 to −500 ppm, indicating that the six-coordinated solid state structures, as determined by X-ray crystallography, are retained in solution [28]

The monobenzyltin complexes C1, C3A, C5A, C5B and C10 are six-coordinated tin structures with distorted octahedral geometries. The octahedral environment around the tin atom comprises a benzyl group, imino nitrogen, phenoxyl oxygen and hydroxyl oxygen atoms from the Schiff base ligand and a chloride anion. The sixth coordination site of the complexes is occupied by the solvent (water or methanol) molecule. As shown in the Fig. 3.3.1, Fig. 3.3.2, Fig. 3.3.5, Fig. 3.3.6, Fig. 3.3.8, the Schiff base ligand behaves as a tridentate dianionic ligand and coordinates to the tin atom, forming five- and six-membered chelate rings with the corresponding bite angles of 67–73° and 78–85°, respectively. These bite angles are comparable with those reported earlier [27], [32]. The five-membered chelating ring in the complexes is practically planar with a root mean square (RMS) deviation from planarity of less than 0.08 Å. The six-membered chelate ring, however, is slightly puckered with a RMS deviation between 0.08 and 0.18 Å. In all the monobenzyltin complexes, the two chelating rings form a dihedral angle of 4.81–11.24°. In the crystal structure of C1, the presence of a methanol molecule in the lattice gives rise to extensive H-bonding in its crystal packing. Similarly, the presence of two waters of crystallization in the crystal structure of C3A also results in an extensive OH⋯O hydrogen bonding interaction in the crystal. In the case of the methanol and water solvated mono(p-fluorobenzyl)tin complexes C5A and C5B, strong intermolecular H bonding between the methanol/water and imino nitrogen atom of an adjacent molecule (Table 3.3.2) results in a dimeric structure for both C5A and C5B (Fig. 3.3.5a).

The crystal structures of the two dibenzyltin complexes C3B and C4 (Fig. 3.3.3, Fig. 3.3.4) revealed that the tin atom is bonded to two benzyl groups, and together with the tridentate Schiff base, forms a five-coordinated diorganotin structure. The overall geometry at the tin atom in both complexes C3B and C4 is a distorted trigonal bipyramidal, as evidenced from the small O1–Sn1–O2 axial angles of 156.88(6) and 154.18(8)°, respectively, similar to those reported earlier [33], [34]. The extent of distortion can be estimated from the τ-value, which is defined as τ = (β − α)/60 where α and β are the two largest donor-metal-donor angles in the five-coordinated environment [35]. The τ value is expected to be zero for a perfect square-pyramidal geometry and unity for a perfect trigonal bipyramidal geometry. The τ-values for complexes C3B and C4 are calculated to be 0.16 and 0.42 respectively, indicating that the coordination of C4 should be intermediate between a trigonal bipyramidal and square pyramidal geometry, while C3B is close to a square pyramidal geometry. A detailed analysis of the bonding geometry of C4 shows that the angles subtended at the equatorial plane, C16–Sn1–C23 and C16–Sn1–N2 [128.2(1) and 129.1(1)°], are relatively larger than that of C23–Sn1–N2 [102.52(10)°], which clearly indicates that there is a significant interaction between the two o-chloro substituents with the tin atom, causing the enlargement of the two angles. Interestingly, the Schiff base ligand in the di(p-fluorobenzyl)tin complex C9 acts as a monobasic tridentate ligand, unlike those in the case of the previous two dibenzyltin complexes C3B and C4. Comparing to similar complexes reported earlier [36] and also with the complexes in this study, the relatively short C7O1 bond [1.249(2) Å] and the longer C7–N1 [1.344(2) Å] bond clearly indicate that the Schiff base has retained its keto configuration upon formation of the dibenzyltin complex. The structure of C9 also differs from the other two penta-coordinated dibenzyltin complexes in that it exhibits a six coordinated tin structure with a distorted octahedral geometry. The geometries are the same as reported by Yin et al. with similar penta- and hexa-coordinated organotin(IV) complexes [37]. The presence of OH⋯O and NH⋯O hydrogen bonding (Table 3.3.2) links the molecules into a polymeric chain parallel to the bc crystallographic plane (Fig. 3.3.7a).

The reaction of complex C5A with 4,4-bipyridine afforded the co-crystal C10 as one product. It was found that the coordinated methanol molecule acts as a hydrogen donor to the bidentate ligand through an O–H⋯O hydrogen bonding interaction [O4–H⋯N3 = 2.675(3) Å, symmetry code i: −x,−y + 1,−z + 1], giving rise to a dinuclear monobenzyltin molecule. The overall structure was further stabilized by a π–π contacts between the benzene ring Cg1 (C1–C6) of the 4-chlorobenzhydrazide fragment and one of the pyridine rings, Cg2ii (C24–C28/N3) with a centroid–centroid distance of 3.892(2) Å (symmetry code ii: −x, 1 − y, 1 − z).

If the reaction of di(p-fluorobenzyl)tin dichloride with the Schiff base ligand L and 4,4-bipyridine N-oxide was carried out in a one-pot reaction mixture, either complex C11 or C12 was formed. The complexes C11 and C12 are isomeric structures, differing only in the cis arrangement of the chloride anions for C11 and trans arrangement of the chloride anions for C12. The overall geometry at the tin atom for both C11 and C12 is a distorted octahedron which is linked by the 4,4′bipyridine N,N′-dioxide ligand into an infinite polymeric chain (Fig. 3.3.9, Fig. 3.3.10).

4. Conclusion

Several monobenzyltin and dibenzyltin complexes containing the Schiff base ligand (E)-4-chloro-N′-(2-hydroxy-4-methoxybenzylidene)benzohydrazide (L) have been synthesized and characterized by FT-IR and NMR (1H,13C and 119Sn) spectroscopic techniques and by X-ray crystallographic diffraction. In all the complexes, the ligand acts as a dibasic tridentate chelator, except for the dibenzyltin complex C9, in which the ligand behaves as a monobasic tridentate ligand. The X-ray crystallographic study revealed that the monobenzyltin complexes are 6-coordinated tin structures with distorted octahedral geometries, whereas the dibenzyltin complexes are 5-coordinated tin structures with distorted trigonal bipyramidal geometries. The di(p-fluorobenzyl)tin dichloride molecules in both the cis and trans-[(p-FC7H6)2SnCl2(L′)] complexes (L′ = 4,4-bipyridine N,N′-dioxide) are linked through a covalently bonded 4,4′-bipyridine N,N′-dioxide spacer group into a polymeric chain.
