The use of H-bonding to increase the dimensionality of coordination networks of Cu(I) and Cu(II)

Author links open overlay panel, ,
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.molstruc.2006.03.106Get rights and content

Abstract

Two two-dimensional coordination networks were prepared by the reaction of a bent bridging ligand, bis(4-pyridyl)amine (bpa), with CuI and CuCl2. Both networks were formed by bpa ligands bridging between Cu2I2 dimers or six-coordinate trans CuCl2 moieties. The networks were isotopological and displayed (4, 4) topology with nodes based on the midpoint of the Cu2I2 dimer or the Cu(II) ion, respectively. Reaction of a monoprotonated bpa ligand with CuSO4 resulted in the formation of a neutral discrete complex. H-bonding networks were formed in all three cases. The two coordination polymers were both extended to give related three-dimensional networks with the resulting CuI H-bonded network displaying twofold interpenetration. The discrete neutral complex formed a two-dimensional H-bonded network and when further H-bonding to H2O solvent molecules was considered, a highly complex three-dimensional network resulted with five different nodes of four different connectivities.

1. Introduction

The development of methods to directly and specifically synthesise topologically interesting architectures in pursuit of new functional materials is one of the more interesting areas of supramolecular chemistry and crystal engineering at present. One method of doing so is to use components which have a high probability of showing an increase in dimensionality of the material due to the formation of further supramolecular interactions. This has been done with the use of multimodal ligands, which have been found to generate various combinations of 1-, 2- and 3-dimensional structures depending on the metal and counter ion employed and the crystallisation conditions [1]. However, an increase in dimensionality has more commonly been achieved with the use of moieties capable of forming supramolecular interactions that individually are weak, but when acting in concert can give rise to surprising topologies. These include H-bonding synthons [2], π–π interactions [3], coordinating cations [4] and anions [5], and d10–d10 interactions [6]. This approach could be considered as a variation on the building-block methodology employed in coordination polymer chemistry but with building blocks generated at the network rather than molecular level.

Of the strategies used for increasing dimensionality, one of the most attractive is the use of hydrogen-bonding motifs. This is due to the relative ease of designing hydrogen-bond donor groups into ligands and the frequency of occurrence of hydrogen-bond receptors in the components of coordination polymers. In addition, of the weak supramolecular interactions used for increasing system dimensionality, hydrogen bonds are among the strongest, and, combined with the directionality of their interaction, make them ideal for generating topologically reliable connections between metal complexes. While a number of systems showing an increase in dimensionality through hydrogen-bonding have been reported [7], the rational design of extended network architectures using such interactions is still an area in its infancy. As a result, the understanding and comparison of these often complex structures is essential. One of the most powerful tools able to be used for this is topological analysis. This is because structures which by observation appear different may, by topological analysis, be shown to be closely related.

The early syntheses of coordination polymers typically used rigid linear bridging ligands such as pyrazine or 4,4′-bipyridine. This was because it was thought that they would allow for the more predictable formation of architectures [8]. In contrast to the structures generated using ligands of this type, polymers formed from simple bent bridging ligands can show significant differences as the framework must adjust to accommodate the bend of the molecule [9]. A ligand of this type is bis(4-pyridyl)amine (bpa) (Scheme 1). In addition to the bend, this ligand has a number of features useful for the formation of supramolecular interactions to potentially extend the dimensionality of the arrays. First, in the solid state it contains a chiral axis and can behave as a two-bladed chiral molecular propeller [10]. Second, the amine of the ligand can act as a hydrogen-bonding motif, with the likely hydrogen-bond acceptors in a supramolecular system being other ligands, anions or solvent molecules. Hydrogen bonds in supramolecular materials can result in either the dimensionality of the system increasing or a modification of the material without changing its dimensionality. Increases in dimensionalities via hydrogen-bonding in supramolecular systems have been seen potentially to lead to interpenetration [7].

The ligand bpa has been reacted with both Cu(I) and Cu(II) salts to form two two-dimensional coordination polymeric nets. As a result of the ability of bpa to form hydrogen bonds both of these complexes were extended to form three-dimensional hydrogen-bonded nets. The topologies of the two-dimensional nets were identical; however, the two-dimensional sheets were found to pack differently. This resulted in related, although not topologically identical, three-dimensional nets being formed. Many workers have used H-bonding arising from protonated heterocycles as tectons to build more elaborate networks [11]. In an attempt to exploit this strategy and to increase the hydrogen-bonding ability of bpa, it was mono-protonated and reacted with Cu(II). The resulting neutral complex was discrete and mononuclear, however hydrogen bonds were formed so that two-dimensional sheets resulted. These sheets were then linked together by further hydrogen bonding with a network of solvent H2O molecules to give a three-dimensional network of complex topology.

2. Experimental

2.1. General

The ligand bpa was prepared according to the literature method [12]. IR spectra were measured with a Perkin-Elmer Spectrum BX FT-IR System, with samples in KBr disks. UV/Vis/NIR spectra were measured with a Cary-500 Scan UV/Vis/NIR spectrophotometer with a 110 mm PTFE coated integrating sphere, on samples diluted with BaSO4. Elemental microanalyses were carried out at the Campbell Microanalytical Laboratory at the University of Otago. Samples for microanalysis were pre-dried in vacuo to remove volatile solvent residues.

3. Complexes

3.1. {[(Cu2I2)(bpa)2]}∞ 1

CuI (56 mg, 0.29 mmol) dissolved in 20 mL of degassed MeCN/H2O (3:1, v/v) was added via cannula to bpa (50 mg, 0.29 mmol) dissolved in 20 mL of degassed MeCN/H2O (3:1, v/v) and stirred 1 h. The yellow solid which precipitated was filtered and dried in vacuo. Yield 71 mg (67%). Colourless X-ray quality crystals were grown from the slow diffusion of an MeCN/H2O (3:1, v/v) solution of bpa layered with an MeCN/H2O (3:1, v/v) solution of CuI. Anal. calcd. for C20H18N6Cu2I2: C, 33.21; H, 2.51; N, 11.62%. Found: C, 33.37; H, 2.21; N, 11.91%.

The blue solid which precipitated was filtered and dried in vacuo. Yield 82 mg (83 % based on Cu). Cobalt-blue X-ray quality crystals of 2·3DMF·112H2O were grown from the slow diffusion of a DMF solution of bpa layered with an aqueous solution of CuCl2·2H2O. Anal. 4. Results and discussion

4.1. Synthesis and structure of [(Cu2I2)(bpa)2]∞ (1)

The 1:1 molar reaction of CuI and bpa produced a yellow solid in reasonable yield which gave microanalytical data consistent with a 1:1 metal-to-ligand ratio. The complex was found to be insoluble in common organic solvents.

X-ray structural analysis showed that this complex formed a two-dimensional network structure, crystallising in the monoclinic space group P21/c. The asymmetric unit of the complex consisted of one Cu(I) cation, one bpa ligand and one I− anion (Fig. 1). Each ligand was bonded to two four-coordinate Cu(I) ions by coordination of the two pyridine N donors. The Cu(I) ions were in turn coordinated to one other adjacent ligand, and to two symmetry-related I− anions. The two I− anions in turn coordinated to another Cu(I) ion, which led to the formation of a Cu2I2 dimer (Fig. 1). This meant that each ligand was linked via two Cu2I2 dimers to six adjacent ligands. The midpoint of the Cu2I2 dimer was on a centre of symmetry. The bridging nature of the Cu2I2 dimers led to the formation of the two-dimensional coordination polymer (Fig. 2).

The planes of the pyridine rings of the bpa ligands were significantly twisted, at an angle of 50.8° with respect to each other. The C–N–C angle across the amine was found to be 126.5(2)°. The two bpa ligands that coordinated to the same Cu(I) ion were of different enantiomers, thus the individual polymer sheets were not chiral. The Npy–Cu and I–Cu bond distances fell in the middle of the range of Npy–Cu and I–Cu bond lengths (1.89–2.52 and 2.48–2.79 Å, respectively) for similar four-coordinate systems as determined by a search of the CSD (version 5.26) [19]. The Cu2I2 dimer was found to be strictly planar, and had a Cu⋯Cu distance of 2.7974(6) Å, which was towards the short end of the range of Cu⋯Cu distances in Cu2I2 dimers (2.46–3.40 Å) [19], [20]. Each NN′Cu2I2N″N‴ polymeric linker was nearly planar through the NN′Cu2N″N‴ atoms (0.0403 Å deviation from planarity), with the Cu2I2 plane perpendicular to this plane (90.4° between planes).

The two-dimensional sheets of the polymer formed in the (1 0 2) plane, and adjacent sheets appeared to be arranged so that the Cu2I2 dimers of one sheet were residing in the centres of 44-membered

metallamacrocyclic rings of the next. The two-dimensional polymer sheets were held together by hydrogen-bonding, and formed two identical, interpenetrating three-dimensional arrays (Fig. 2). The hydrogen bonds were between one of the coordinated I− anions and the amine N–H of the next sheet but one. Due to the offset of the individual two-dimensional polymer sheets the I⋯H–N hydrogen bond passed through the metallamacrocyclic rings, resulting in the interpenetrated, three-dimensional hydrogen-bonded framework. The I⋯H–N distance was 2.87 Å, with corresponding I⋯N separation of 3.64 Å, and I⋯H–N angle of 164° [21]. The aromatic rings of ligands in neighbouring polymer sheets were found to be too distant to take part in π–π interactions, the shortest distance between centroids being 4.01 Å [22]. The polymer sheets were efficiently packed, with no residual solvent-accessible volume available in the structure. This led to an overall packing index for the structure of 0.74 [23].

4.2. Synthesis and structure of {[Cu(bpa)2Cl2]·3DMF·112H2O}∞(2·3DMF·112H2O)

The reaction of CuCl2·2H2O with a threefold excess of bpa produced a blue solid in good yield which gave microanalytical data consistent with a 1:2 metal-to-ligand ratio. Further attempts to prepare this complex using only a 1:2 metal-to-ligand ratio resulted in the production of the 1:1 complex and, despite repeated attempts, X-ray quality crystals of this 1:1 product could not be obtained. Complex 2 was found to be insoluble in common organic solvents. The UV/Vis/NIR spectrum of 2 displayed a single broad d–d feature at 650 nm, suggesting that the coordination geometry around the Cu(II) ions could be distorted octahedral [24]. The complex also showed a number of charge-transfer bands in the UV region, and a band characteristic of bpa in the NIR region.

X-ray structural analysis showed that this complex formed a two-dimensional network structure, crystallising in the orthorhombic space group Pbcn. The asymmetric unit of the complex consisted of half of a Cu(II) cation, one bpa ligand, one Cl− anion and disordered solvent molecules (Fig. 3). None of the solvent molecules could be satisfactorily modelled; therefore they were removed from the atom list for refinement and the PLATON SQUEEZE [23] procedure was applied. The residual electron density in the asymmetric unit was assigned to one and a half molecules of DMF solvent and three quarters of a molecule of H2O solvent.

Each ligand was bonded to two six-coordinate Cu(II) ions, which sat on a twofold rotation axis, by coordination of the two pyridine N donors. The Cu(II) ions were in turn coordinated to three other adjacent ligands, and to two symmetry-related Cl− anions (Fig. 3). This meant that each ligand was linked by Cu coordination to six adjacent ligands. The bridging nature of the Cu(II) ions led to the formation of a two-dimensional coordination polymer. The planes of the pyridine rings of bpa ligands were significantly twisted, at an angle of 48.3° with respect to each other. The C–N–C angle across the amine was found to be 127.5(4)°. Those bpa ligands which were trans to each other were of different enantiomers, thus the individual polymer sheets were not chiral. The Npy–Cu and Cl–Cu bond distances fell in the middle of the range of Npy–Cu and Cl–Cu bond lengths (1.86–2.13 and 2.15–3.01 Å, respectively) for similar six-coordinate systems as determined by a search of the CSD [19]. The distances also indicated that the Cu(II) ions were being affected by a Jahn–Teller distortion.

The two-dimensional corrugated sheets of the polymer formed in the ab plane, and adjacent sheets were held together to form a three-dimensional array via hydrogen-bonding (Fig. 4). The hydrogen bonds were between the coordinated Cl− anions and the amine N–H of the adjacent sheets. The Cl⋯H–N distance was 2.26 Å, with corresponding Cl⋯N separation of 3.10 Å and Cl⋯H–N angle of 165° [21]. The aromatic rings of ligands in neighbouring polymer sheets were found to be too distant to take part in π–π interactions, the shortest distance between centroids being 4.41 Å [22]. Alternating sheets were related to each other in a centrosymmetric fashion.

The polymer sheets packed so that one set of large (7 × 10 Å) rectangular-shaped pores was formed running along the crystallographic c-axis (Fig. 4). Other, much smaller and less regular pores were also seen along other axes. The combined solvent accessible volume of these pores was 52.8% of the unit cell volume, with a packing index for the structure of 0.41. These values were calculated using PLATON [23] from the SQUEEZEd data. It was apparent from earlier attempts at modelling the disordered solvent that it resided in the pores of the framework.

4.3. Synthesis and structure of [Cu(bpaH)2(SO4)2(H2O)2]·4H2O (3·4H2O)

The bpa ligand was monoprotonated by the addition of one equivalent of HCl, after which an excess of SO42− anion was added. On addition of a solution of CuSO4·5H2O a turquoise solid was isolated in low yield. This solid gave microanalytical data consistent with a 1:2 metal-to-ligand ratio. The complex was found to be insoluble in common organic solvents. The IR spectrum showed two features in the SO42− stretching region; a peak of moderate intensity at 1198 cm−1 and a strong and broad peak at 1112 cm−1, consistent with the SO42− anion coordinating to the Cu(II) ion in a monodentate fashion [25]. Also present were peaks indicative of the ligand, at 3422 and 532 cm−1. A broad peak of moderate intensity not present in the spectrum of bpa was also observed at 3059 cm−1, indicative of the pyridinium N+–H [26]. The UV/Vis/NIR spectrum of 3 displayed a single broad d–d feature at 724 nm, suggesting that the coordination geometry around the Cu(II) ions could be distorted octahedral [24]. The complex also showed two charge-transfer bands in the UV region, and a band characteristic of bpa in the NIR region.

X-ray analysis revealed that this structure was a discrete, neutral, mononuclear complex, crystallised in the monoclinic space group C2/c. The asymmetric unit consisted of half of a Cu(II) cation, one monoprotonated bpa ligand (bpaH+), one SO4− anion and three molecules of H2O. One of the H2O molecules and the SO42− anion were coordinated to the Cu(II) centre. The Cu(II) ion, which lay on a centre of symmetry, had a Jahn–Teller distorted octahedral coordination sphere, made up of two pyridine N donors from the two symmetry-related bpaH+ ligands, two O donors from two symmetry-related coordinated H2O molecules, and two O donors from two symmetry-related, monodentate coordinated, SO42− anions (Fig. 5). The binding mode observed for the anion was found to be consistent with the interpretation of the IR spectrum of the complex. All Cu–Npy, Cu–OH2 and Cu–OSO3 bond distances were found to be close to the middle of the range (1.86–2.13, 1.92–2.58 and 2.00–2.67 Å, respectively) for similar six-coordinate Cu–Npy, Cu–OH2 and monodentate Cu–OSO3 systems found in a search of the CSD [19]. The bpaH+ ligand was close to planar, with an angle between the planes of the pyridine rings of 5.4°. The C–N–C angle across the amine was found to be 132.40(18)°. Each Cu(II) centre had one bpa ligand of each enantiomer bound to it as a result of its lying on a crystallographic inversion centre, resulting in an achiral structure. Due to the arrangement of the SO42− anions and H2O molecules about the Cu(II) centres intramolecular hydrogen bonds were formed between them with O–H⋯O distances of 1.96 Å and O⋯O separations of 2.69 Å (Fig. 5).

The individual mononuclear complexes were held together by a number of interactions (Fig. 6). The strongest of these were two sets of hydrogen bonds. The first of these was a symmetry-related pair of hydrogen bonds between the coordinated H2O molecules of one complex and the SO42− anions of the next. The O–H⋯O distance was found to be 1.80 Å, with an O⋯O separation of 2.69 Å. The second set of hydrogen bonds was between the protonated pyridinium of one bpaH+ ligand and one of the O atoms of the SO42− anions. The N–H⋯O distance was found to be 1.80 Å, with a N⋯O separation of 2.67 Å. The first set of hydrogen bonds linked complexes so that they formed one-dimensional metallamacrocyclic polymers propagating along the b-axis with protonated bpaH+ extending out to the sides. The second set of hydrogen bonds formed one-dimensional polymers propagating along the [1 1 0] diagonal axis. The combination of the two sets of hydrogen bonds led to the formation of two-dimensional hydrogen-bonded polymeric sheets in the (0 0 1) plane (Fig. 6). As well as the hydrogen-bonds, π–π interactions between both pyridine rings of bpaH+ ligands were involved in holding the two-dimensional sheets in place. These pyridine rings were in almost exact register with each other, with angles of just 5.08° between them in both cases. The centroid⋯centroid distances were 3.50 and 3.52 Å [22]. In addition to the hydrogen bonds between discrete units of the complex, the non-coordinated H2O molecules were able to form a series of hydrogen bonds as well. The first of these occurred between individual H2O molecules (Fig. 6), with O–H⋯O distances of 1.88 and 2.06 Å and corresponding O⋯O separations of 2.75 and 2.85 Å with O–H⋯O angles of 178° and 150° [21]. This set of hydrogen bonds led to the formation of corrugated one-dimensional hydrogen-bonded chains of water molecules running along the b-axis (Fig. 6). The chains of water molecules were firmly held in place in the structure by the formation of three further sets of hydrogen bonds. Each independent H2O molecule interacted with an O atom of a SO42- anion, with O–H⋯O distances of 1.98 and 2.06 Å, and corresponding O⋯O separations of 2.74 and 2.84 Å with O–H⋯O angles of 168° and 169° [21]. A further interaction also occurred between the amine N–H and a H2O molecule, with an N–H⋯O distance of 2.04 Å and N⋯O separation of 2.92 Å and the N–H⋯O angle of 176° [21]. The interactions between the H2O molecules and the hydrogen-bonded sheets of the complex led to the formation of a three-dimensional hydrogen-bonded net of very complex topology. This net was further reinforced by weak O3S–O⋯H–C interactions between pyridine hydrogen atoms and SO42- anions, with O⋯H distances of 2.33 and 2.34 Å, and corresponding O⋯O separations of 3.16 and 3.24 Å.

The hydrogen-bonded polymeric sheets packed such that, if the non-coordinated water molecules were not considered, two sets of pores were visible. These were small (6 × 4 Å) rectangular-shaped pores running along the b-axis and similarly small (7 × 3 Å) also rectangular pores along the c-axis. The combined solvent-accessible volume of these pores was 10.6% of the unit cell volume; however they were found to be almost completely filled with the H2O solvent molecules, which led to the structure having 1% residual solvent-accessible volume and a packing index of 0.76 [23].

4.4. Topological analyses

The topologies of all three complexes were determined using OLEX, [27] and in all three cases analyses were able to be conducted on both the two-dimensional sheets and the three-dimensional hydrogen-bonded networks. The two-dimensional coordination polymer networks formed by 1 and 2 were found to be isotopological, although their nodes comprised different atoms. Their topological nets were uninodal, with all nodes based on Cu2I2 dimers (1) or Cu(II) ions (2), each of which had links to four other nodes via bpa ligands. All nodes had short topological terms of 44·62, defining the frameworks as (4, 4) nets [28]. Network structures with this topology are common among two-dimensional coordination polymers. Early examples of these structures were formed mostly using simple linear bridging ligands [29], while more recent work has seen the use of more flexible and potentially complex ligands [30].

The topology of the two-dimensional hydrogen-bonded sheets of 3 was found to be similar to the (4, 4) nets of 1 and 2 although it also showed a surprising similarity to the topology of the α-polonium net (Fig. 7). It comprised two different nodes; one based on Cu(II) centres, the other on the O atom of the SO42- anion that was involved in hydrogen-bonding to the pyridinium. These nodes showed different connectivities, the Cu nodes being six-connected, the O nodes three. The O nodes had links to three Cu nodes: one through the coordinated SO42−, one through the O–H⋯O hydrogen bond, and the third through the N–H⋯O hydrogen bond and bpaH+ ligand. This resulted in the O nodes having a short topological term of 43 and the Cu nodes in turn having short topological terms of 46·66·83 (Fig. 7). The 43 vertex symbol for the O node has been recognised to lead to discrete cuboid assemblies [28], some of which have been synthesised as discrete supramolecular entities [31]. The 46·66·83 node appears to be a previously unknown topology. The complete topological net could also be viewed as a two-dimensional section of an α-polonium, 412·63, net, with the three-connected nodes of this network occurring at the points where the “slice” of net was removed (Fig. 7).

The topologies of the three-dimensional hydrogen-bonded networks formed by 1 and 2 were both seen to be related to that of rutile TiO2. For the doubly interpenetrated network formed by 1, the two nets showed identical topologies each comprising of two different nodes: one ligand-based and one based on the Cu2I2 dimer (Fig. 8). The ligand node occurred at the amine N atom and had links to three Cu nodes, one link for each pyridine arm, and another for the N–H⋯I hydrogen bond. This resulted in the ligand nodes having short topological terms of 4·62. The Cu nodes in turn were six-connected with short topological terms of 42·610·83. This particular combination of three- and six-connected nodes is also found in the topological network formed by rutile TiO2 (Fig. 8), and has also been seen in a number of other coordination polymeric systems [7](d), [32], several of which show twofold interpenetration similar to 1. Of these networks, only one is formed in the same manner as 1, where two-dimensional sheets form hydrogen bonds between alternate layers to give interpenetrated nets [7d]. In addition to these structures, the 4·62 node topology has also been found to occur in two other more complex network structures, one three-dimensional [33] and also related to the rutile net, the other two-dimensional [34].

For the non-interpenetrated structure of 2, the topological network was very similar to that of 1 (Fig. 9). It again comprised two different nodes, one ligand-based and one Cu-based, with one node occurring at the amine N atoms, the other at the Cu(II) ions. The ligand nodes were found to have a short topological term of 4·62, resulting from the same interactions as in 1. The Cu nodes in turn were found to have short topological terms of 42·69·84. The resulting network differs slightly from the rutile net formed by 1, but has been seen before in coordination polymers formed from Sm(III) and Gd(III) [32a]. Other than this reported occurrence, the 42·69·84 node topology has not been seen previously.

The topology of 3·4H2O was found to become highly complicated when the hydrogen bonds between the H2O molecules and the discrete units of the complex were also considered as topologically links (Fig. 10). In this case, the topological net comprised five different nodes; six-connected based on Cu, five-connected based on SO42-, three-connected based on the amine N, four-connected based on one H2O molecule and three-connected based on the other. This resulted in short topological terms of 42·52·64. 72·84·11 for the Cu nodes, 4·53·63·7·8·9 for the SO42- nodes, 62·8 for the amine nodes and 53·6·7·8 and 53 for the H2O nodes. Of these five vertex symbols, the only one previously described is 53, which is known to lead to discrete pentagonal dodecahedra [28]. The other four are previously unknown.

5. Conclusion

In the complexes 1–3 the most important additional supramolecular functionality of bpa was found to be its amine group. This permitted the formation of hydrogen bonds to nearby species which increased the dimensionality of the networks being formed.

The two-dimensional Cu(I) and (II) complexes 1 and 2·3DMF·112H2O formed coordination polymers with similar structure and very similar topology despite the different metal-to-ligand ratios and resulting metal coordination environments. This came about because both complexes were both able to accommodate pseudo-square-planar coordination modes for bpa ligands. The individual tetrahedral Cu(I) ions in 1 formed Cu2I2 dimers whose geometries forced the coordinated bpa molecules to bind all in the same plane. Although the Cu(II) ions in 2·3DMF·112H2O had octahedral coordination modes, trans-coordination of the Cl− anions again directed the bpa ligands to coordinate to the Cu(II) in the equatorial plane. In both cases two-dimensional coordination polymers resulted. Despite the apparent similarities between sheets of each complex, adjacent sheets packed together and interacted differently in each case. This suggested that this network building-block approach allowed for a degree of tuning of the final H-bonded network. In 1 the Cu2I2 dimers of one sheet overlaid the centres of the metallamacrocyclic rings of the adjacent sheets, whereas in 2·3DMF·112H2O adjacent sheets were arranged without as much offset between sheets, which resulted in the formation of the pores in the complex. Both coordination grids displayed an overall buckled arrangement due to the bend imposed between Cu centres by the bpa ligand. This allowed for further interactions between two-dimensional sheets as the buckling brought components able to interact into proximity. Both complexes formed hydrogen bonds between the halide anions and the amine N–H but the differences in packing also affected the three-dimensional nets that resulted from these hydrogen bonds. In 1 the packing mode ensured that no amines of one sheet were close enough to form hydrogen bonds with the Cu2I2 dimers of the adjacent sheet, resulting in the interactions only occurring between every second sheet. As a result two identical interpenetrated three-dimensional nets were formed and the complex was non-porous. This contrasted with 2·3DMF·112H2O where, without so much offset between adjacent sheets, hydrogen bonds could form and link all the two-dimensional sheets into one three-dimensional network. The topologies of the individual two-dimensional sheets were determined, as were those of the networks formed by hydrogen-bonding. Considering only the two-dimensional sheets, both 1 and 2 showed the same square grid (4, 4) topologies. When the hydrogen-bonded nets were considered, the topologies were found to be different, although as would be expected from linking two topologically equivalent sheets, there were significant similarities between short topological terms. Both complexes had the topological term 4·62 for their ligand based nodes, but for their Cu based nodes, 1 was found to be 42·610·83, while 2 was 42·69·84. When taken together, the topology of the net in 1 was the topology of the rutile net, while 2 showed that of a rutile-related net that had been seen only once previously [32a].

The structure of 3·4H2O was significantly different, as was to be expected following the protonation of the bpa ligand. In this complex two different hydrogen-bonding motifs served to connect individual mononuclear complexes into two-dimensional sheets. The topology of these hydrogen-bonded sheets was determined to be reminiscent of two-dimensional sections of an α-polonium network. Of the three complexes only 3·4H2O showed incorporated solvent molecules forming topologically significant interactions. The H2O molecules formed corrugated chains which were also able to form hydrogen bonds to the complex molecules. This resulted in the formation of a topologically complicated three-dimensional hydrogen-bonded network.
