Abstract

Six new coordination polymers, namely [Co2(1,2-bdc)2(bibp)2]·H2O (1), [Zn2(1,2-bdc)2(bibp)2]·H2O (2), [Ni2(1,2-bdc)2(bibp)3]·7H2O (3), [Cd(1,2-bdc)(bibp)0.5] (4), [Co(Hbtc)(bibp)] (5), [Cd(Hbtc)(bibp)] (6) (bibp = 1,4-bis(4-(imidazole-1-yl)benzyl)piperazine, 1,2-bdc = 1,2-benzenedicarboxylate, btc = 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylate), have been synthesized under hydrothermal conditions. Their structures were determined by single-crystal X-ray diffraction analyses and further characterized by elemental analyses, IR spectra and TG analyses. Compounds 1–6 display different 2D → 3D interdigitated structures due to their different 2D layers. In 1 and 2, the 2D layers contain single-stranded meso-helical chains. In 3, there are large rhombic cavities in the 2D sheet. In 4, the 2D layer is built from rod-shaped SBUs. In 5 and 6, the 2D layers exhibit highly undulating 44-sql networks. In addition, the magnetic properties for compounds 1, 3 and 5, and the photoluminescence properties for compounds 2, 4 and 6 have also been investigated.

1. Introduction

The interest concerning the rational design and fabrication of entangled coordination polymers (CPs) is greatly expanding because of their undisputed aesthetic topologies and their help in theoretical prediction of the assembling processes, and more importantly, owing to their potential applications as functional solid materials ranging from gas storage to heterogeneous catalysis [1]. So far, a variety of entangled coordination polymers with different structural features, like interpenetration [2], polycatenation [1](a), [3], polythreading [1](a), [4], polyknotting [1](a), [5] etc., have been documented. In comparison with interpenetrating network, interdigitated network has been regarded as a special type of entangled system, since it can be easily disentangled without breaking the links [6]. The main feature of interdigitation is extended arrays in which individual motifs interweave each other in a gearlike (or tongue-and-groove) fashion [7]. Conceptually, rod or string elements are necessary conditions for the formation of interdigitated structures [7], [8].

Admirable synthetic strategies have been developed for this type of entanglement, and a number of elegant examples belonging to this family have been reported. For example, Stuart R. Batten and co-workers have synthesized interdigitating chains which stack together parallelly to generate a 1D + 1D → 2D interdigitated structure [9]. Our group has reported one compound showing a novel (1D + 1D → 3D) interdigitated structure [10]. This compound is obtained from the self-assembly of 1D tubelike structures, in which each 1D tube is interdigitated by dangling arms from four adjacent tubes. Besides, Sun et al. have obtained a (2D + 2D → 3D) interdigitated network based on undulated 2D layers [11]. On the whole, interdigitated network is commonly constructed from lower dimensional motifs, such as (1D + 1D → 2D) [9], [12], (1D + 1D → 3D) [10], [13], (2D + 2D → 2D) [14] and (2D + 2D → 3D) [11], [15]. Clearly, this feature helps to increase the dimensionality of CPs and enhance the stability of the structure. Therefore, the study of interdigitated networks will not only increase the structural diversity for CPs, but also provide some new insights to synthesize stable CPs.

In this article, we focus on assembly reactions of transition metal salts with a long flexible ligand (bibp) and two aromatic polycarboxylates, and the efforts have led to the isolation of six new coordination polymers with 2D → 3D interdigitated structures, namely [Co2(1,2-bdc)2(bibp)2]·H2O (1), [Zn2(1,2-bdc)2(bibp)2]·H2O (2), [Ni2(1,2-bdc)2(bibp)3]·7H2O (3), [Cd(1,2-bdc)(bibp)0.5] (4), [Co(Hbtc)(bibp)] (5), [Cd(Hbtc)(bibp)] (6) (bibp = 1,4-bis(4-(imidazole-1-yl)benzyl)piperazine, 1,2-bdc = 1,2-benzenedicarboxylate, btc = 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylate, Scheme 1). The syntheses, crystal structures and properties of these compounds, along with a structural comparison, will be represented and discussed in this paper.

2. Experimental section

2.1. Materials and methods

The bibp ligand was synthesized according to the reported procedures [16]. Other reagents and solvents were obtained from commercial suppliers and used without further purification.

Elemental analyses (C, H and N) were performed on a Perkin-Elmer 2400 CHN Elemental Analyzer. Co, Zn, Ni and Cd were determined by a tps-7000 Plasma-Spec(I) inductively coupled plasma-atomic emission spectrometer (ICP-AES). IR spectra were recorded in the range 400–4000 cm−1 on a Bio-Rad FTS-185 FT/IR Spectrophotometer using KBr pellets. TG analyses were performed on a NETZSCH STA 449C instrument in flowing N2 with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. PXRD data were recorded on a XD-3 diffractometer using Cu Kα radiation. Excitation and emission spectra were performed on an F-7000 FL fluorescence spectrophotometer equipped with a 150 W xenon lamp as the excitation source. Variable-temperature magnetic susceptibility data were obtained on a SQUID magnetometer (Quantum Design, MPMS-7) in the temperature range of 2–300 K with an applied field of 1.0 kOe. All the magnetic susceptibility data were corrected from diamagnetic contributions estimated from Pascal’s constants.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Description of crystal structures

3.1.1. Structures of [Co2(1,2-bdc)2(bibp)2]·H2O (1) and [Zn2(1,2-bdc)2(bibp)2]·H2O (2)

Compounds 1 and 2 are isostructural and display 2D → 3D interdigitated structures, thus only the structure of 1 will be discussed in detail. As depicted in Fig. 1a, the asymmetric unit of 1 contains two Co(II) ions, two 1,2-bdc ligands, one complete and two half bibp ligands and one free water molecules. Two Co(II) ions, surrounded by two 1,2-bdc ligands and two bibp ligands, respectively, are five-coordinated and exhibit [N2O3] square pyramidal geometries. Each 1,2-bdc anion coordinates to two Co atoms in a (κ1)-(κ2)-μ2 coordination mode (mode I, Scheme S2). Each bibp ligand uses two imidazole rings at either end bridging two Co centres (Scheme S1). Based on these connection modes, the Co(II) ions are connected by 1,2-bdc ligands to form 1D chains and these chains are further extended by bibp ligands to yield a 2D layer with 44-sql net topology (Fig. 1b and c). Interestingly, the 2D layer of 1 contains a single-stranded meso-helix running along the [1,0,0] direction with a pitch of 11.10 Å, which is built from Co atoms, bibp ligands and one kind of 1,2-bdc ligands (Fig. 1d). In addition, the benzene rings of 1,2-bdc ligands out of the plane embed the adjacent layers above and below simultaneously, resulting in a 2D → 3D interdigitated motif (Fig. 1e).

3.1.2. Structure of [Ni2(1,2-bdc)2(bibp)3]·7H2O (3)

When the cobalt acetate in 1 was replaced by the nickel acetate, another different 2D → 3D interdigitated structure of 3 was isolated. As depicted in Fig. 2a, there are two Ni(II) ions, two 1,2-bdc ligands, three bibp ligands and seven lattice water molecules in the asymmetric unit of 3. Both two Ni(II) ions are six-coordinated by three carboxylate oxygen atoms from two 1,2-bdc ligands and three nitrogen atoms from three bibp ligands in a distorted octahedral geometry. The four atoms (O1, O5, N1#1 and N6 for Ni1, or O3#2, O8#2, N13 and N18#3 for Ni2) occupy the equatorial positions, and the other two atoms (O7 and N7 for Ni1, or O2#2 and N12 for Ni2) occupy the axial positions. Each 1,2-bdc anion adopts a (κ1-κ1)-(κ1)-μ2 coordination mode to connect adjacent Ni atoms (mode II, Scheme S2) and each bibp ligand acts as a linear bridging ligand linking two Ni atoms (Scheme S1). In this way, two Ni centres (Ni1 and Ni2) are connected by two carboxylate groups to form a binuclear nickel unit as a secondary building unit (SBU) (Fig. S2). Each SBU is further linked to four nearest neighbors through six bibp ligands to generate a 2D 44-sql net (Fig. 2b). As shown in Fig. 2c, there are large rhombic cavities (with dimensions of 22.01 × 22.45 Å) in the 2D sheet. Notably, the open space within each sheet provides the chance for the interdigitation. The benzene rings out of the plane embed the adjacent layers above and below, resulting in a 2D → 3D interdigitated structure (Fig. 2d).

There are versatile hydrogen bonding interactions between the lattice water and the carboxylate oxygen atoms, the lattice water and piperazine nitrogen atoms (Table S2).

3.1.3. Structure of [Cd(1,2-bdc)(bibp)0.5]·7H2O (4)

Compound 4 shows a different 2D → 3D interdigitated structure, when the nickel acetate in 3 was further replaced by the cadmium acetate. The asymmetric unit of 4 contains one Cd(II) ions, one 1,2-bdc and half a bibp ligand. As shown in Fig. 3a, the crystallographically independent Cd atom adopts a distorted octahedral geometry, being coordinated by five carboxylate oxygen atoms from three 1,2-bdc ligands and one nitrogen atoms from a bibp ligand. The four atoms (O1, O2#1, O3#1 and O4#2) occupy the equatorial positions, and the other two atoms (O3#2 and N1) occupy the axial positions. Each 1,2-bdc ligand bridges neighboring Cd(II) ions in a (κ2-μ2)-(κ1-κ1)-μ3 coordination mode (mode III, Scheme S2). Remarkably, adjacent Cd(II) ions are held together by carboxylate groups of 1,2-bdc to form an infinite Cd(II)-carboxylate chain running along the a axis, which can be taken as a rod-shaped SBU (Fig. 3b). Each bibp ligand acts as a bridging ligand linking two Cd centres (Scheme S1). Therefore adjacent rod-shaped SBUs are further interconnected by bibp ligands to generate a 2D sheet (Fig. 3c). Each sheet can be simplified to a (3,4)-connected 3,4L83 net with the point symbol of (42·6)(42·63·8), in which the 1,2-bdc ligand and Cd atom are regarded as 3- and 4-connected nodes, respectively. As depicted in Fig. 3d, these 2D layers are superimposed with interdigitation involving the nearest neighbors, due to the protrudent benzene rings of 1,2-bdc ligands in the 2D networks.

3.1.4. Structures of [Co(Hbtc)(bibp)] (5) and [Cd(Hbtc)(bibp)] (6)

Besides the dicarboxylate ligands, tricarboxylic acid H3btc was also used in the reaction, and two new 2D → 3D interdigitated structures were obtained. Compounds 5 and 6 are isostructural, so only the structure of 5 will be discussed in detail. There are one Co atom, one Hbtc ligand and one bibp ligand in the asymmetric unit of 5 (Fig. 4a). Each Co atom, surrounded by two Hbtc ligands and two bibp ligands, is four-coordinated and exhibits [N2O2] tetrahedral geometries. The partly deprotonated Hbtc ligand acts as V-shaped ditopic linker which uses only two of the three carboxylate groups to connect two Co(II) ions, while the other one is protonated (mode IV, Scheme S2). Each bibp ligand acts as a linear ligand bridging two Co atoms (Scheme S1). Based on these connection modes, the Co(II) ions are connected by Hbtc ligands and bibp ligands to form a highly undulating 2D 44-sql network (Fig. 4b). The similar highly undulating 2D sql net, assembled from the bibp ligand and a carboxylic ligand, has been reported previously [22]. However, different from the interpenetrating 2D structure in Ref. [22], the 2D layer in 5, with protonated carboxyl group lateral arms projecting from both sides, is simultaneously interdigitated with the adjacent ones, to yield a 2D → 3D interdigitated network (Fig. 4c). In addition, there exist the π…π stacking interactions among adjacent benzene rings of different bibp ligands (centre-to-centre distance: 3.76 Å, Fig. S4). Besides, there are intermolecular O6-H6⋯O3i hydrogen-bonding interactions in the structure (Table S2). These weak interactions further stabilize the structure of 5.

3.2. Comparison of compounds 1–6 and the influencing factors

In a comparison of the structures of 1–4, it was found that the coordination modes of the organic ligands and the coordination geometries of the central metal ions have a significant effect on the formation of the resulting structures. In 1 and 2, the 1,2-bdc anions adopt a (κ1)-(κ2)-μ2 coordination mode linking 5-coordinated Co/Zn(II) cations, and these metal centres only display mononuclear form in the structures. In 3, the 1,2-bdc anions adopt a (κ1-κ1)-(κ1)-μ2 coordination mode linking 6-coordinated Ni(II) cations, and the Ni(II) centres are connected by two carboxylate groups from two 1,2-bdc ligands to form a binuclear nickel unit (Fig. S2). In 4, the 1,2-bdc anions adopt a (κ2-μ2)-(κ1-κ1)-μ3 coordination mode linking 6-coordinated Cd(II) cations, and the Cd(II) centres are held together by carboxylate groups to form a rod-shaped SBU. Obviously, when a carboxylate group of 1,2-bdc adopts (κ1-κ1)-μ2 coordination mode to bind metal centres, it can chelate metal ions and lock their positions into M–O–C–O–M clusters [23], leading to the formation of polynuclear metal clusters (bimetallic SBUs in 3 and rod-shaped SBUs in 4). Hence, although compounds 1–4 are constructed by 1,2-bdc and bibp ligands, the different coordination modes of 1,2-bdc and the different coordination geometries of central metal ions lead to the different 2D layers.

In addition, when H3btc was used instead of 1,2-bdc, compounds 5 and 6 still show 2D → 3D interdigitated structures with 2D layer motifs. The possible reason is that one carboxylate group of Hbtc is protonated and Hbtc acts as a ditopic linker like 1,2-bdc (Scheme S2). In other words, the protonated carboxylate group is uncoordinated and fails to increase the dimensionality of the ultimate structure.

3.3. PXRD patterns and TGA

In order to check the phase purity of compounds 1–6, the X-ray powder diffraction (PXRD) patterns were recorded at room temperature. As shown in Figs. S5–S10, the peak positions of simulated and experimental patterns are in good agreement with each other, demonstrating the phase purity of the products. The differences in intensity may be due to the preferred orientation of the crystalline powder samples.

Thermal gravimetric analyses (TG) were performed on compounds 1–6 to investigate their thermal stabilities (Figs. S11–16). For 1, the weight loss of 1.62% from room temperature to 115 °C is assigned to the release of the lattice water molecules (calcd 1.43%, Fig. S11). Then the sample keeps relatively stable from 116 to 299 °C. The organic components decompose at 300 °C. For 2, the weight loss of 1.54% from room temperature to 155 °C is attributed to the release of the lattice water molecules (calcd 1.41%, Fig. S12). Then the sample keeps relatively stable from 156 to 279 °C. The organic components begin to pyrolyze when the temperature reaches 280 °C. For 3, the weight loss of 7.25% from room temperature to 130 °C is consistent with the removal of the free water molecules (calcd 7.14%, Fig. S13). Then the sample keeps relatively stable from 131 to 230 °C. The departure of the organic components occurs at 231 °C, finally resulting in the formation of NiO as the residue (obsd 8.40%, calcd 8.45%). For 4, TGA shows no weight loss up to 280 °C. The structure decomposes to form CdO as the residue from 281 to 486 °C (obsd 26.47%, calcd 26.99%, Fig. S14). For 5, the sample keeps relatively stable from room temperature to 374 °C, then the decomposition of the organic framework begins above 375 °C (Fig. S15). For 6, the sample keeps relatively stable from room temperature to 371 °C, then the weight loss from 372 to 659 °C is attributed to the collapse of the framework. The remaining residue corresponds to the formation of CdO (obsd 17.56%, calcd 17.86%, Fig. S16).

In addition, for compounds 1, 2 and 5, the total weight losses of 81.16%, 82.05%, 82.04% are less than the calculated value of 88.12%, 87.22%, 88.74% if the final products are assumed to be CoO, ZnO, CoO, respectively, which indicates that the decomposing processes are not complete due to the use of nitrogen protection or the insufficient heating temperature.

For 1, the χMT value at 300 K is 5.678 cm3 K mol−1 (6.740 μB; Fig. 5a), which is much higher than the expected value (3.75 cm3 K mol−1, 5.477 μB) of two isolated spin-only Co(II) ion (S = 3/2, g = 2.0), which can be ascribed to the strong orbital contribution to the magnetic moment of Co(II) centres [24]. Upon cooling, χMT gradually decreases to a local minimum of 4.406 cm3 K mol−1 at 21.9 K, and then increases to a value of 4.447 cm3 K mol−1 at 14.3 K, before dropping quickly to 3.081 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K. The magnetic behavior of 1 is unusual and interesting, indicative of a strong antiferromagnetic interaction admixture with a very weak ferromagnetic interaction [25].

For 3, the χMT value at 300 K is 3.282 cm3 K mol−1 (5.124 μB; Fig. 5b), which is higher than the expected value (2.000 cm3 K mol−1, 4.000 μB) of two isolated spin-only Ni(II) ion (S = 1, g = 2.0). As T is lowered, χMT continually increases to a maximum value of 12.894 cm3 K mol−1 at 15.4 K. This high temperature regime is typically observed for paramagnetic systems which exhibit dominating ferromagnetic interactions in the Ni(II) dimer. Finally, it decreases to 3.962 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K, which shows the signature of weak antiferromagnetic interaction between Ni(II) metal ions and/or a Zero-Field Splitting (ZFS) effect. Based on the structure analysis of 3, the ferromagnetic magnetic interaction between Ni centres is expected to be through the syn-anti carboxylate bridges.

For 5, the χMT value at 300 K is 2.541 cm3 K mol−1 (4.509 μB; Fig. 5c), which is much higher than the expected value (1.875 cm3 K mol−1, 3.873 μB) of one isolated spin-only Co(II) ion (S = 3/2, g = 2.0), which can be ascribed to the strong orbital contribution to the magnetic moment of Co(II) centres [24]. As T is lowered, χMT regularly decreases to a value of 2.415 cm3 K mol−1 at 39.8 K, and then increases to a maximum of 3.005 cm3 K mol−1 at 3.0 K. Below the temperature, χMT decreases sharply and reaches 2.897 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K. The magnetic behavior of 5 might be contributed to the competition between zero-field splitting effects of Co(II) and a very weak ferromagnetic coupling among distantly separated Co(II) ions [26]. Additionally, the behavior of compound 5 suggests a dominant single ion effect with only very weak magnetic interactions [26].

3.5. Photoluminescence properties

Taking into account the excellent luminescent properties of d10 metal complexes, the solid-state luminescence of the compounds (2, 4 and 6) as well as the bibp ligand were investigated. As shown in Fig. 6, the emission peaks of compounds 2, 4 and 6 are located at 446 nm (λex = 372 nm), 423 nm (λex = 328 nm) and 446 nm (λex = 365 nm). The bibp ligand exhibits a fluorescent emission band at 386 nm (λex = 313 nm). According to previous documents, 1,2-bdc and H3btc display fluorescence emission peaks at about 362 and 398 nm, respectively [27]. In contrast with the bands of 1,2-H2bdc and bibp, the emission spectra for 2 and 4 have bathochromic shift. Similarly, in contrast with the bands of H3btc and bibp, the emission of 6 is highly red-shifted. According to the previous literature [28], these results may be attributed to the ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT).

4. Conclusion

Six new coordination polymers, based on a long flexible N-donor ligand (bibp) and two aromatic polycarboxylate ligands, have been synthesized under hydrothermal conditions. Compounds 1–6 display different 2D → 3D interdigitated structures. The successful isolation of these species enriches the realm of 2D → 3D interdigitated networks.

  • Synthetic approaches for the incorporation of free amine functionalities in porous coordination polymers for enhanced CO<inf>2</inf> sorption

    2018, Coordination Chemistry Reviews

    Porous coordination polymers, or metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), provide a unique materials platform due to their regular structure that allows for almost unlimited tunability in terms of pore size, shape, dimensionality and chemical functionalisation. The ability of this class of material to act as agents for the selective capture or separation of small molecules has driven much of the research in the field over the past two decades.

    One of the major driving forces in recent years has been the ability of porous coordination polymers to act as selective hosts for carbon dioxide, with obvious potential environmental implications for successful materials. Efforts to enhance the selectivity for CO2 over other gases (particularly those present in exhaust flues) have explored chemical functionalisation of the interior pore space using amines, to give materials that to some extent mimic the standard solution-based processing methods of flue gas that are currently employed.

The relative merits and shortcomings of these different synthetic approaches are discussed alongside examples of the materials generated using each method.
