Introduction

During the past two decades, the rational design and syntheses of novel coordination frameworks have attracted much interest due to their promising applications in gas storage/separation, catalysis, luminescence, drug delivery, sensors and magnetic materials.1–3 The successful construction of coordination frameworks usually depends on judicious choice of polytopic ligands, metal ions or secondary building units (SBUs) as well as reaction conditions.4 Generally, flexible linkers can give rise to materials that demonstrate crystal-to-crystal ‘breathing’ and complex adsorption effects such as gating and sensing, where metal–organic frameworks respond to the nature and/or pressure of the adsorbate.5 Linker-derived dynamic behaviour necessitates careful control of the flexibility of the ligand; thus, linkers with limited degrees of freedom have most successfully been employed in the synthesis of dynamic metal–organic frameworks.6–8 The integration of materials displaying advanced framework flexibility with electronically or magnetically responsive SBUs underlies one approach for sensing and responding to small molecule adsorbents.9

On the other hand, among these crystalline coordination frameworks, single-crystal-to-single-crystal (SC-SC) structural transformation is a particularly interesting phenomenon that has attracted considerable attention in recent years since the process can directly provide the accurate relationship between the structures and their properties.10,11 Through SC-SC transformations, new coordination frameworks that cannot be formed under conventional conditions may be easily obtained in high yield, giving a deeper insight into the relationship between the host and the guest molecules and also offering ways to fabricate systems with enhanced functionalities and novel applications.12 However, a SC-SC transformation of coordination frameworks is still difficult to achieve, partly because SC-SC transformation in the solid state can simultaneously occur in more than one direction, and/or hardly retain crystallinity in the solid-state rearrangement of atoms. The exploration of new coordination frameworks that can endure SC-SC transformations still remains a challenge.13–15

It has been well-known that five-membered heterocycles, such as imidazole, triazole, pyrazole and tetrazole, are good candidate ligands for the construction of functional coordination polymers.16 Among them, 1,2,4-triazole, especially its derivatives, has the potential to bridge in μ1,2-, μ2,4-, and μ1,2,4-bridging fashions. These triazole derivatives have strong σ donor as well as weak π acceptor properties, which are expected to be used for the construction of novel functional coordination polymers with intriguing structural motifs and novel functional properties. We are also interested in exploiting the coordination chemistry and application of 1,2,4-triazole and its derivatives.17Trans-4,4′-azo-1,2,4-triazole (atrz) contains two triazole groups and one azo bridge. As a matter of fact, atrz has four potentially coordinated N atoms in a molecule, which may exhibit various bridging modes (Schemes 1 and 2). In spite of its apparent simplicity, atrz is a versatile ligand and has wide applications as energetic materials and magnetic materials (such as spin transition materials and single-chain magnets), as recently reported by chemists.18 In this work, the flexible atrz building block has been employed, and a series of novel coordination polymers, namely [Fe(μ-atrz)(NCS)2(H2O)2]n (1), [Fe(μ-atrz)(NCS)2(H2O)2]n (2), {[Fe(μ-atrz)(μ-Cl)(H2O)2]·Cl·H2O}n (3), {[Fe(μ-atrz)(atrz)2(H2O)2]·2Br·4H2O}n (4), {[Fe(μ-atrz)(atrz)2(H2O)2]·(BF4)2·2H2O}n (5), {[Cu(μ-atrz)3]·(BF4)2·2H2O}n (6), {[Cu(μ-atrz)3]·(BF4)2}n (7), {[Cu2(μ4-atrz)(μ2-OH)2(H2O)4]·SO4·3.75H2O}n (8), {[Zn(μ-atrz)(NCS)2]·0.5H2O}n (9) and {[Zn(μ-atrz)(NCS)2]}n (10) have been isolated (Scheme 3). 1 and 2 present 1D polymorphic iron(ii)–triazole frameworks, in which bicycle-pedal motion of the azo moieties of atrz can be observed. The 3D nano-porous CuII framework 6 exhibits a guest induced SC-SC structural transformation to obtain another 3D nano-porous CuII framework compound 7. Further anion exchange experiment also indicates an anion-induced structural transformation from the 3D CuII framework 6 to the 2D CuII coordination polymer 8 confirmed by PXRD patterns. Additionally, the 1D zinc(ii) compound 9 exhibits guest induced SC-SC transformation to obtain 10 accompanied by a color change from red (9) to pale pink (10). Variable temperature magnetic susceptibility measurements (2–300 K) reveals weak anti-ferromagnetic interactions between central iron(ii) ions for 1–5. The different coordination modes of atrz and different dihedral angles between two triazole rings within atrz are also briefly discussed, which indicate that the atrz building block has great potential for the construction of these flexible dynamic coordination frameworks.

Experimental section

General methods

The trans-4,4′-azo-1,2,4-triazole ligand was prepared by literature methods.18b (Caution! Azo complexes are potentially explosive in the presence of organic compounds. Only a small amount of the material which must be handled with care should be prepared.) All the other reagents were purchased commercially and used without further purification. Deionized water was used as the solvent in this work. C, H, and N microanalyses were carried out with a Perkin-Elmer 240 elemental analyzer. FT-IR spectra were recorded from KBr pellets in the range of 4000–400 cm−1 on a Bio-Rad FTS 135 spectrometer. Powder X-ray diffraction measurements were recorded on a D/Max-2500 X-ray diffractometer using Cu-Kα radiation. Variable-temperature magnetic susceptibilities were measured using a MPMS-7 SQUID magnetometer. Diamagnetic corrections were applied with Pascal's constants for all constituent atoms.

Synthesis of trans-4,4′-azo-1,2,4-triazole (atrz)

The atrz (atrz = trans-4,4′-azo-1,2,4-triazole) ligand was synthesized by mixing 4-amino-4H-1,2,4-triazole (2 g, 24 mmol) and KBrO3 (6 g, 36 mmol) in 50 mL of distilled water. The resulting solution was stirred until all chemicals were dissolved and then 6 mL of HCl was slowly dripped into the solution. Continuous stirring was carried out until the solution color turns orange and the required product appeared as a yellow precipitate. The precipitate was then filtered out and washed with water. Re-crystallization from water yields rod-like yellow crystals. Mp: 236–240 °C.

X-ray crystallography

Diffraction intensities for complexes 1–10 were collected on a Bruker SMART 1000 CCD diffractometer with graphite-monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) by using the ω–φ scan technique. Lorentz polarization and absorption corrections were applied. The structures were solved by direct methods and refined with the full-matrix least-squares technique using the SHELXS-97 and SHELXL-97 programs.19,20 Anisotropic thermal parameters were assigned to all non-hydrogen atoms. The organic hydrogen atoms were generated geometrically; the hydrogen atoms of the water molecules were located from difference maps and refined with isotropic temperature factors. Analytical expressions of neutral-atom scattering factors were employed, and anomalous dispersion corrections were incorporated. Results and discussion

Complexes 1–10 are air-stable and can retain their structural integrity at room temperature for a considerable length of time. Notably, hydrothermal reaction conditions are essential for the preparation of the final 2D and 3D cationic CuII frameworks 6 and 8. Though the trans-4,4′-azo-1,2,4-triazole ligand contains six nitrogen atoms, only four nitrogen atoms of triazole rings can participate in the coordination and the steric effect prevents azo-nitrogen atoms from metal coordination. Because both N1 and N2 in the triazole ring can coordinate with metal ions, there are four different coordination modes in 1–10, as shown in Scheme 2. For 1, 2, 6, 7, 9 and 10, the atrz ligands in these coordination polymers are coordinated in bridging mode c, while the atrz ligands in 3 are coordinated only in mode b, whereas the atrz ligands in 4 and 5 are simultaneously coordinated in modes a and c. Interestingly, the 2D cationic CuII framework 8 exhibited the unique tetra-dentate μ4-bridging mode, which is scarcely reported for metal–atrz coordination frameworks. The different coordination modes draw the self-assembly of coordination polymers to form 1D, 2D and 3D coordination polymers. It is noted that the dihedral angles between two triazole rings of atrz are also flexible. For example, as listed in Table S2,† the dihedral angles between two triazole rings within one atrz ligand are 2.41(6)° in 3, and the dihedral angles within one atrz ligand in 4 are 12.45(8)° while the dihedral angles within one atrz ligand in 5 are 18.03(1)°. The flexible dihedral angles also indicate a slightly spatial distortion effect. Four coordination modes together with flexible dihedral angles make the fact that we could not succeed to anticipate the self-assembly results of atrz and metal ions. The results also reveal that the atrz building block has great potential for the construction of these flexible dynamic coordination frameworks.

Structure of 1D polymorphic frameworks [Fe(μ-atrz)(NCS)2(H2O)2]n (1) and [Fe(μ-atrz)(NCS)2(H2O)2]n (2)

Bright yellow crystals of 1D polymorphic frameworks, [Fe(μ-atrz)(NCS)2(H2O)2]n (1) and [Fe(μ-atrz)(NCS)2(H2O)2]n (2), can be obtained by slow evaporation after mixing FeCl2·4H2O, NH4NCS and the atrz ligand in a 1 : 2 : 1 ratio in aqueous solutions at different reaction temperatures (25 °C for 1 and 80 °C for 2). 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic C2/c space group while 2 crystallizes in the triclinic P space group. As shown in Fig. 1 and 2, each symmetric unit of the 1D chain framework [Fe(μ-atrz)(NCS)2(H2O)2]n (1) contains one FeII atom in an octahedral geometry, two water molecules and one atrz ligand. The central FeII atom is six-coordinated to two water molecules, two thiocyanate nitrogen atoms and two atrz nitrogen atoms forming a FeN4O2 donor set. The Fe–N distances are 2.115(2)–2.223(2) Å and the Fe–O distances are 2.108(2) Å, while all the N–Fe–N and N–Fe–O angles are in the range of 88.29(9)–91.71(9)°; such coordination geometry is in accordance with octahedrally coordinated FeII centers in the HS state. The bridging μ-atrz ligands connect adjacent FeII ions forming a linear chain. The Fe(ii)⋯Fe(ii) distance across a bridging atrz ligand in 1 is 11.974(36) Å. The coordinated water molecules strongly interact with the nitrogen of the atrz ligand, with an O1–H1B–N2 lattice distance of 2.794(3) Å. The shortest separated interplanar center-to-center distances of two neighboring triazole rings are 5.816(7) Å, and no obvious π–π stacking interactions in 1 are observed. The bridging atrz ligands in this 1D structure are strictly planar in the 1D iron(ii)–triazole framework [Fe(μ-atrz)(NCS)2(H2O)2]n (1).

2 is a polymorphic 1D iron(ii)–triazole framework of 1, in which central FeII ions are also linked by bridging atrz ligands (Fig. 3). Most interestingly, as shown in Scheme 1, structural analysis of 1 and its polymorphic product 2 reveal the bicycle-pedal motion of the azo group within the bridging atrz ligands. The shortest separated interplanar center-to-center distances of two neighboring triazole rings are 4.826(4) Å, therefore no obvious π–π stacking interactions in 2 are observed. Recently, Bharadwaj et al. have reported a novel dynamic open framework exhibiting a bicycle-pedal motion in single-crystal-to-single-crystal transformation of {[Co(azim)2(DMF)2]·(ClO4)2·2DMF}n.21 Molecular motions in crystalline solids have received attention in recent years due to their potential uses in fabricating nanoscale devices. Such motions in crystalline solids in response to outside stimuli such as light, heat, etc. are particularly intriguing. As a result of the molecular motions, the significant structural change is that Fe(ii)⋯Fe(ii) distances across bridging atrz ligands vary from 11.974(36) Å in 1 to 11.433(4) Å in 2.

Structure of {[Fe(μ-atrz)(μ-Cl)(H2O)2]·Cl·H2O}n (3)

When FeCl2 is used to replace Fe(NCS)2, bright yellow crystals of the 1D chain coordination polymer, {[Fe(μ-atrz)(μ-Cl)(H2O)2]·Cl·H2O}n (3), can be isolated by slow evaporation after mixing FeCl2·4H2O and the atrz ligand in a 1 : 1 ratio in aqueous solution. As shown in Fig. 4, the coordination polymer 3 crystallizes in the monoclinic P21/c space group with one formula unit in the asymmetric unit. Each asymmetric unit contains one FeII atom in an octahedral geometry, two coordinated water molecules, one lattice water molecule, one free lattice Cl− anion, one bridging μ-Cl− anion and one bridging atrz ligand. The Fe–N distances are 2.2162(15) Å, the Fe–O distances are 2.0791(13) Å, the Fe–Cl distances are 2.4865(4) Å, and all the N–Fe–N and N–Fe–O angles are in the range of 88.52(5)–91.48(5)°. Such coordination geometry is in accordance with octahedrally coordinated FeII centers in the HS state.22 The central FeII atom thus adopts an FeN2O2Cl2 donor set.

The bridging atrz ligand and bridging μ-Cl− anions connect adjacent FeII ions forming a linear FeII coordination framework. As shown in Fig. 5, the coordination mode of bridging atrz in 3 is quite different from that in Fe(NCS)2–atrz frameworks 1 and 2. For the atrz ligand, two nitrogen atoms (N2, N3) of its one triazole group are both involved in the coordination, while two other nitrogen atoms (N7, N8) of its other triazole group within the same atrz ligand are not involved in the coordination. As a result, the Fe(ii)⋯Fe(ii) distance across the atrz ligand in 3 is 4.024(0) Å, which is much shorter than that in 1 and 2. The coordinated water molecules (O1, O2) and lattice water molecule (O3) strongly interact with atrz ligands as well as chloride atoms, with O–H⋯N and O–H⋯Cl inter-molecular interactions. Neighboring aromatic triazole moieties aligned face-to-face with a separated interplanar center-to-center distance of 3.529(8) Å exhibits typical π–π stacking interactions. These weak interactions are important for the stabilization of the whole metal–organic framework. It is noteworthy that unlike the essentially planar geometry of atrz in the 1D coordination polymers [Fe(μ-atrz)(NCS)2(H2O)2]n (1 and 2), the atrz ligands in 3 are non-planar with torsion angles (between the two triazole rings) of 2.4° for the bridging atrz ligand indicating a slightly spatial effect.23

Structure of {[Fe(μ-atrz)(atrz)2(H2O)2]·2Br·4H2O}n (4) and {[Fe(μ-atrz)(atrz)2(H2O)2]·(BF4)2·2H2O}n (5)

When FeBr2 and FeBF4 are used to replace FeCl2, bright yellow crystals of 1D isostructural FeII coordination frameworks, {[Fe(μ-atrz)(atrz)2(H2O)2]·2Br·4H2O}n (4) and {[Fe(μ-atrz)(atrz)2(H2O)2]·(BF4)2·2H2O}n (5), can be isolated. Because 4 and 5 are isostructural, therefore herein, only the structure of 4 is described. Coordination polymer 4 crystallizes in the triclinic P space group with one formula unit in the asymmetric unit. The asymmetric unit contains one FeII atom in an octahedral geometry, two coordinated water molecules, four lattice water molecules, two free lattice Br− anions, two terminal mono-dentate coordinating atrz ligands and one bridging atrz ligand (Fig. 6). The bridging atrz ligand connects adjacent FeII ions forming a linear chain. Different from 1D iron(ii)–triazole frameworks 1–3, there exist two different coordination modes for atrz; one atrz ligand adopts the bridging coordination mode while the other two atrz ligands adopt the mono-dentate coordination mode. The central FeII atom thus adopts a FeN4O2 donor set. The Fe(ii)⋯Fe(ii) distances across the atrz ligands in 4 and 5 are 11.369(11) Å and 11.433(4) Å, respectively. These distances can be compared with those large Fe(ii)⋯Fe(ii) distances in 1 and 2.

The 1D linear chain framework structure of 4 along the crystallographic a-axis is shown in Fig. 7. The Fe–N distances vary from 2.189(2) Å to 2.207(2) Å and the Fe–O distances are 2.0455(19) Å, while all the N–Fe–N and N–Fe–O angles are in the range of 88.80(9)–91.20(9)°; such a coordination geometry is in accordance with octahedrally coordinated FeII centers in the HS state. The coordinated water molecule (O1) and lattice water molecules (O2 and O3) strongly interact with atrz ligands as well as bromide atoms, with O–H⋯N and O–H⋯Br inter-molecular interactions. The separated interplanar center-to-center distances of 3.798(3) Å (for 4) and 3.718(0) Å (for 5) between neighboring aromatic triazole moieties exhibit relatively weak π–π stacking interactions. These weak interactions are important for the stabilization of the whole metal–organic framework. For 1D coordination polymers 4 and 5, the bridging atrz ligands are essentially planar. However, it is noteworthy that unlike bridging atrz ligands, the terminal coordinated atrz ligands in the 1D coordination frameworks are non-planar with torsion angles (between the two triazole rings) of 12.5° in 4 and 6.77° in 5.

Structure of the 3D CuII coordination polymers {[Cu(μ-atrz)3]·(BF4)2·2H2O}n (6) and {[Cu(μ-atrz)3]·(BF4)2}n (7)

Blue block-shaped crystals of {[Cu(μ-atrz)3]·(BF4)2·2H2O}n (6) suitable for X-ray structural determination can be isolated by a simple and one-step hydrothermal reaction of Cu(BF4)2·4H2O with atrz in aqueous solution. Complex 6 crystallizes in the monoclinic P2(1)/n space group. As depicted in Fig. 8, the asymmetric unit is made up of one CuII atom, three bridging atrz ligands, two BF4− anions and two aqua ligands. The CuII atom is six-coordinated to six atrz nitrogen atoms in a regular octahedron with Cu–N bond lengths varying from 2.0134(16) Å to 2.3896(16) Å. All the CuII atoms have the same coordination environment with an octahedral geometry. The neighboring Cu⋯Cu distances are 11.709(1) Å and 12.201(1) Å. For the three-dimensional framework structure, each atrz serves as a bidentate bridge connecting two CuII centers to form a 3D nano-porous framework with dimensions of 11.709(1) Å × 12.201(1) Å (Fig. 9). If the central CuII ions are defined as nodes and the atrz lgiands are defined as linkers, the 3D framework of 6 has a 6-connected α-Po topology. In the 3D porous framework of 6, along the crystallographic a axis, it can be seen that the channels in 6 are filled with free BF4− anions and free water molecules. The calculation of the effective free volume of the 3D coordination polymer using PLATON revealed that there is approximately 33.3% of the crystal volume accessible to guest molecules/anions in 6.24 Due the large pores of the 3D nano-porous framework, a two-fold interpenetrating 3D porous framework of CuBF4–L encapsulating free BF4− anions and free water molecules can ultimately be observed in 6.

Recently, we have obtained a 2D crystalline coordination framework {[Cu(tatrz)2(NO3)2]·(CH3OH)·4H2O}n (tatrz = 1-(9-(1H-1,2,4-triazol-1-yl)anthracen-10-yl)-1H-1,2,4-triazole), in which anion exchange experiment indicates that NO3− anions can be completely exchanged with ClO4− anions, as evidenced by the anion-exchanged crystalline product.17g Taking inspiration from the aforementioned points, in this work, we investigated the guest solvent loaded host–guest complex in a SC-SC fashion. A crystal of 6 was heated at 60 °C for 2 h under vacuum to obtain 7. Single crystal X-ray diffraction revealed that the space group changed from monoclinic P21/n (6) to triclinic P (7). For 7, the central CuII atom is also six-coordinated to six atrz nitrogen atoms in a regular octahedron with Cu–N bond lengths from 2.020(3) Å to 2.423(4) Å, which are slightly longer than that in 6. The adjacent Cu⋯Cu distance (Cu1–Cu1A) is 11.621(6) Å and 16.674(1) Å. For the three-dimensional structure, each atrz also serves as a bidentate bridge connecting two CuII centers to form a 3D nano-porous framework with dimensions of 11.621(6) Å × 16.674(1) Å (Fig. 10). If the central CuII ions are defined as nodes and the atrz ligands are defined as linkers, the 3D framework of 7 also has a 6-connected α-Po topology. In the 3D architecture of 7, the channels along the crystallographic a axis are filled with free BF4− anions. The shortest separated interplanar center-to-center distances of two neighboring triazole rings are 4.379(8) Å (for 6) and 4.377(8) Å (for 7), therefore no obvious π–π stacking interactions are observed. The calculation of the effective free volume of the 3D MOF using PLATON revealed that there is approximately 33.9% of the crystal volume accessible to guest molecules/anions in 7.24 The effective free volume of 7 has a slightly larger accessible volume compared to that of 6. Similarly, due the large pores of the 3D nano-porous framework, a two-fold interpenetrating 3D porous framework of CuBF4–L encapsulating free BF4− anions is ultimately observed in 7.

Structure of the 2D CuII coordination polymer {[Cu2(μ4-atrz)(μ2-OH)2(H2O)4]·SO4·3.75H2O}n (8)

As shown in Fig. 11, the fundamental structural unit of {[Cu2(μ4-atrz)(μ2-OH)2(H2O)4]·SO4·3.75H2O}n (8) contains two central CuII ions (Cu1 and Cu2), one μ4-bridging atrz ligand, two μ2-bridging OH− groups (O7 and O10), four terminal coordinated water molecules (O5, O6, O8 and O9), one lattice SO42− anion and four lattice water molecules (O11, O12, O13 and O14). The coordination geometry around the central CuII ion can be described as a regular elongated octahedron. The bridging triazole ligands and bridging OH− groups occupy the square plane. The terminal ligands, such as water molecules, weakly coordinate in the two axial positions because of the Jahn–Teller effect. It is noted that the atrz ligands in 8 adopt a unique μ4-bridging coordination mode as shown in Scheme 2(d). In general, such a tetradentate bridging coordination mode of atrz is also scarcely reported in previously reported metal–atrz frameworks.

As shown in Fig. 12, the central CuII ions are connected through oxygen atoms from hydroxyl groups to construct infinite zigzag Cu–O–Cu inorganic chains. The Cu–O–Cu angle is 124.90° and the shortest Cu⋯Cu distance is 3.455(1) Å. These 1D (CuO)n inorganic chains are further linked via μ4-bridging atrz ligands to form a 2D cationic CuII framework. The shortest separated interplanar center-to-center distances of two neighboring triazole rings are 5.931(2) Å, therefore no obvious π–π stacking interactions in 8 are observed. It is noted that the free sulfate anions and lattice water molecules are located between these 2D cationic frameworks. These free sulfate anions and lattice water molecules are interlinked via intermolecular hydrogen bonding interactions forming 2D supra-molecular sheets. As shown in Fig. 13, the 2D metal–atrz layers and 2D supra-molecular sheets are interlinked, which ultimately form a 3D supra-molecular architecture of 8.

Structure of {[Zn(μ-atrz)(NCS)2]·0.5H2O}n (9) and {[Zn(μ-atrz)(NCS)2]}n (10)

As shown in Fig. 14, the red 1D Zn–atrz framework {[Zn(μ-atrz)(NCS)2]·0.5H2O}n (9) crystallizes in the triclinic P space group. The fundamental structural unit contains one central ZnII ion, one μ2-bridging atrz ligand, two terminal coordinated thiocyanate ligands and half of a free lattice water molecule. The coordination geometry around central ZnII ions can be described as a regular tetrahedral coordination geometry. Two bridging atrz ligands and two terminal coordinated thiocyanate ligands complete a ZnN4 coordination sphere. The neighboring Zn(ii)⋯Zn(ii) distances across these bridging atrz ligands are 11.582(4) Å.

It is interesting that the 1D zinc(ii)–atrz framework {[Zn(μ-atrz)(NCS)2]·0.5H2O}n (9) exhibits interesting solvent-induced SC-SC transformations accompanied by a color change from red (9) to pale pink (10). The triclinic P space group of 9 can be transformed into the monoclinic C2/c space group of 10. 10 adopts a 1D chain zinc(ii)–triazole framework similar to that of 9 (Fig. 14). The shortest separated interplanar center-to-center distances of two neighboring triazole rings are 4.265(7) Å (for 9) and 4.638(5) Å (for 10), therefore no obvious π–π stacking interactions are observed in 9 and 10. However, a significant structural change is that the neighboring Zn(ii)⋯Zn(ii) distances across the bridging atrz ligands vary from 11.582(4) Å to 10.967(10) Å. It is also noteworthy that the neighboring Zn(ii)⋯Zn(ii)⋯Zn(ii) angles are 93.37(0)° in 9 while these angles are 116.22(0)° in 10, indicating a dramatic change in these 1D zinc(ii)–atrz chain frameworks.

Magnetic properties of 1–5

The temperature-dependent magnetic susceptibility data for complexes 1–5 have been measured in the temperature range of 2–300 K. As shown in Fig. 15, the magnetic properties of 1–5 are shown in the form of χMT vs. T plots where χM is the molar magnetic susceptibility and T is the temperature. For 1–5, the χMT values at room temperature are close to ca. 3.54 cm3 K mol−1, corresponding to one uncoupled octahedral Fe(ii) d6 center in the high-spin state. When the temperature is lowered, the χMT values remain constant and then smoothly decrease and drop rapidly below ca. 20 K reaching the minimum values at 2 K. The decrease below ca. 20 K can be attributed to weak anti-ferromagnetic interactions as indicated by the large Fe(ii)⋯Fe(ii) distances across the bridging atrz ligands or the presence of zero-field splitting or both. By fitting the magnetic susceptibility data with the Curie–Weiss law χM = C/(T − θ), where C and θ represent the Curie constant and the Weiss temperature, respectively, θ values of −1.44 K (for 1), −2.29 K (for 2), −19.7 K (for 3), −3.87 K (for 4) and −2.67 K (for 5) can be obtained. These θ values indicate anti-ferromagnetic interactions between central iron(ii) ions for 1–5. The relatively stronger anti-ferromagnetic interactions between central iron(ii) ions for 3 may be attributed to short chloride and atrz bridges. Obviously, complexes 1–5 showed no detectable thermal spin crossover magnetic behaviors. In general, FeN6 coordination geometries facilitate the formation of spin crossover behaviors;25 such a lack of spin crossover behavior in 1–5 may be correlated with N4O2 or N2O2Cl2 coordination spheres around central FeII ions.

Anion-induced structural transformation

A profound role in directing the structures of coordination polymers has been found for guest molecules in the coordination space. Especially in the domain of dynamic coordination frameworks, guest molecules residing in the porous aperture can regulate structures to impart flexibility to the framework.26 Soft porous coordination frameworks are well known to exhibit such guest dependent structural dynamism. Moreover, framework flexibility may depend upon the nature of guest molecules. Particularly, imparting structural dynamism/flexibility to cationic MOFs is very easy compared to other MOFs. By employing appropriate solvent systems for synthesis, the combination of a neutral N-donor ligand with metal ions yields cationic MOFs which usually are occluded with low boiling solvents and extra framework anions. On keeping such MOFs away from the mother liquor, the confined guests may easily leave the framework and induce the possibility of structural changes in the system. Such structural modulations upon guest removal at room temperature render inherent dynamism to cationic MOFs.27

As demonstrated in the crystal structure of 6, the counteranions are located in the channels of the structures. In addition, compound 6 is insoluble in common organic solvents, therefore anion-exchange experiment based on the crystalline samples of 6 has been investigated. As shown in Fig. 16(a) and (b), the comparison between powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) and a simulated analysis from the crystal data of 6 confirm the pure crystal phases. After suspension of the crystalline samples of 6 in an aqueous solution of Na2SO4 at room temperature for two weeks, the anion-exchanged product was isolated by filtration, washed with MeOH several times, and dried in air. As shown in Fig. 16(c) and (d), the powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns of the anion-exchanged product match well with PXRD patterns calculated on the basis of the single-crystal X-ray diffraction structure of 8. The results indicate complete anion-induced structural transformation from the 3D CuII framework 6 to the 2D CuII framework 8. We also try to accomplish SC-SC transformation from 6 to 8, however, it is not successful, which may be correlated with the dramatic structural change from the 3D CuII framework 6 to the 2D CuII framework 8.

Conclusions and perspectives

In conclusion, in this work, a flexible multi-dentate trans-4,4′-azo-1,2,4-triazole (atrz) ligand has been employed and a series of novel coordination polymers, namely [Fe(μ-atrz)(NCS)2(H2O)2]n (1), [Fe(μ-atrz)(NCS)2(H2O)2]n (2), {[Fe(μ-atrz)(μ-Cl)(H2O)2]·Cl·H2O}n (3), {[Fe(μ-atrz)(atrz)2(H2O)2]·2Br·4H2O}n (4), {[Fe(μ-atrz)(atrz)2(H2O)2]·(BF4)2·2H2O}n (5), {[Cu(μ-atrz)3]·(BF4)2·2H2O}n (6), {[Cu(μ-atrz)3]·(BF4)2}n (7), {[Cu2(μ4-atrz)(μ2-OH)2(H2O)4]·SO4·3.75H2O}n (8), {[Zn(μ-atrz)(NCS)2]·0.5H2O}n (9) and {[Zn(μ-atrz)(NCS)2]}n (10) have been isolated. 1 and 2 present 1D polymorphic iron(ii)–triazole frameworks, in which bicycle-pedal motion of the azo moieties of atrz can be observed, while 6 presents a 3D cationic CuII nano-porous framework with dimensions of 11.709(1) Å × 12.201(1) Å. Interestingly, 6 exhibits guest induced single-crystal-to-single-crystal (SC-SC) structural transformation to obtain the dehydrated compound 7, which presents another 3D nano-porous framework with dimensions of 11.621(6) Å × 16.674(1) Å. 8 presents a 2D cationic CuII framework constructed with infinite zigzag (CuO)n chains and unique μ4-bridging atrz ligands. Further, when Zn(NCS)2 is used in the reaction system, 9 can be isolated which exhibits guest induced SC-SC transformation to obtain the dehydrated compound 10, accompanied by a color change from red (9) to pale pink (10). The variable temperature magnetic susceptibility study reveals weak anti-ferromagnetic interactions between the central iron(ii) ions for 1–5. Anion-exchange experiment indicates an anion-induced structural transformation from the 3D CuII framework 6 to the 2D CuII framework 8 confirmed by PXRD patterns. The polymorphic iron(ii) frameworks, guest induced single-crystal-to-single-crystal transformation and solvatochromism are interesting, revealing great potential for the construction of dynamic frameworks employing 4,4′-azo-1,2,4-triazole as the building blocks. On the basis of this work, further synthesis, structural and property studies of these coordination polymers using 4,4′-azo-1,2,4-triazole as basic building blocks are also under way in our laboratory.
