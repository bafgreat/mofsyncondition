Introduction

Crystal engineering based on metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) has recently attracted great interest for their versatile architectures as well as their potential applications as functional materials in areas of gas sorption,1 luminescence,2 molecular magnetism,3 non-linear optics,4 catalysis5 and ion-exchange.6 The construction of molecular architecture could be varied by the selection of solvent system, the temperature,7 organic ligands,8 different metal ions,9 the counterions,10 and so on. The reported research work showed that those multidentate ligands containing rich coordination sites (N- or/and O-donors) are useful to produce polymeric networks with structural diversity.11 Especially, ligands with flexible carboxylategroups have been widely used, owing to the diverse multidimensional architectures and interesting topologies caused by their flexible multicarboxylic groups and different coordination modes.12

Taking these points into account, we chose 2,2′-(naphthalene-2,7-diylbis(oxy)) diacetic acid (H2L1) and 2,2′-(biphenyl-4,4′-diylbis(oxy)) diacetic acid (H2L2), which had been studied as flexible carboxylic acids in previous reports,13 and applied them into self-assembly with different metals to get new complexes. The –O–CH2– spacers between the naphthyl/biphenyl rings and –COO donor groups offer flexible orientations of the carboxylate arms. On one hand, the two ligands both have two same arms which are arranged at each side of the naphthyl/biphenyl rings, respectively. On the other hand, the aromatic rings of ligands are different from each other, naphthyl has a steric hindrance effect more obvious than biphenyl rings which would influence the coordination modes of flexible arms. It is interesting to find out the effect of the flexible groups and steric interference of aromatic rings on the structures. Based on the above ideas, eight new coordination complexes with intriguing structures obtained from H2L1 and H2L2 are reported here, [ZnL1(H2O)2]n (1), [CdL1(H2O)2]n (2), [CoL1(H2O)3]n (3), [EuL1(NO2)(H2O)]n (4), [SmL1(NO2)(H2O)]n (5), [CdL2(H2O)]n (6), [CoL2(H2O)4]n (7) and [NiL2(H2O)4]n (8). All complexes were characterized by elemental analysis, IR spectra and X-ray crystallography. In addition, their luminescence and magnetic properties are reported below in detail.

Experimental

Materials and methods

2,2′-(Naphthalene-2,7-diylbis(oxy)) diacetic acid (H2L1) and 2,2′-(biphenyl-4,4′-diylbis(oxy))diacetic acid (H2L2) were synthesized according to a reported literature procedure.13 Other chemicals were obtained from commercial sources and used without further purification. The IRabsorption spectra of the complexes were recorded in the range of 400–4000 cm−1 by means of a Nicolet (Impact 410) spectrometer with KBr pellets. C, H and N analyses were carried out with a Perkin Elmer 240C elemental analyzer. PXRD measurements were performed on a Bruker D8 Advance X-ray diffractometer using Cu-Kα radiation (0.15418 nm), in which the X-ray tube was operated at 40 kV and 40 mA. Luminescent spectra were recorded with a SHIMAZU VF-320 X-ray fluorescence spectrophotometer at room temperature (25 °C). The fluorescence decay lifetimes of the solid samples 1, 2, 4 and 6 were recorded on an Edinburgh Analytical Instruments FLS920 spectrometer at room temperature. The as-synthesized samples were characterized by thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) on a Perkin Elmer thermogravimetric analyzer Pyris 1 TGA up to 1023 K using a heating rate of 10 K min−1 under a N2 atmosphere. Temperature dependent magnetic susceptibility data for polycrystalline complexes 3, 4, 7 and 8 were obtained on a MPMS XL-7 SQUID magnetometer under applied fields of 2000 Oe (3, 7 and 8) and 100 Oe (4) over the temperature range of 1.8–300 K, respectively.

X-Ray crystallography†

X-Ray crystallographic data of 1–8 were collected on a Bruker Apex Smart CCD diffractometer with graphite-monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). Structure solutions were solved by direct methods and the non-hydrogen atoms were located from the trial structure and then refined anisotropically with SHELXTL using full-matrix least-squares procedures based on F2 values using the SHELXTL (version 6.10) package of crystallographic software.14 The hydrogen atom positions were fixed geometrically at calculated distances and allowed to ride on the parent atoms. Results and discussion

Structural description of 1

The single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis revealed that the structure of 1 contains one Zn(ii) ion, one deprotonated H2L1 ligand and two coordinated water molecules in the asymmetric unit. Each Zn(ii) ion is five coordinated by three oxygen atoms (O2, O5#1 and O1#2) from three different L12− ligands and two oxygen atoms (O7 and O8) from coordinated water molecules (Fig. 1). The Zn–O distances ranged from 1.953(2) Å to 2.132(3) Å, which are comparable to those values found in other reported Zn(ii)-carboxylate complexes.15 As shown in Fig. 2 (top), L12− act as bidentate bridging ligands to connect zinc ions into a one-dimensional chain which contains 8-membered rings and 28-membered rings ranged alternatively with the Zn⋯Zn distances of 3.7538(9) Å and 12.5787(34) Å, respectively. One carboxylategroup adopts bis-monodentate coordination mode to bridge two Zn(ii) centers, and another carboxylategroup acts as a monodentate ligand (Scheme 1A). The –OCH2COO– groups along each arm of the L12− ligands are indeed twisted at an angle of 164.968(2)° and −173.737(2)° away from the plane of the central naphthyl ring in complex 1, respectively. As shown in the bottom of Fig. 2, the adjacent 1D chains are connected into a 2D planar structure by hydrogen bonds [O8–H8C⋯O6a, O7–H7B⋯O2b and O7–H7C⋯O6c] (listed in Table 2). Furthermore, the hydrogen bonds O8–H8B⋯O5d result in a 3D supramolecular structure of 1 (Fig. 2, middle).

Structural description of 2

The asymmetric unit of 2 contains one Cd(ii) cation, one deprotonated H2L1 ligand and two coordinated water molecules. As shown in Fig. 3, all Cd atoms are seven-coordinated by five oxygen atoms (O1, O6, O2#1, O5#3 and O6#3) from three different L12− ligands and two oxygen atoms from coordination water molecules (O7 and O8), forming a slightly distorted pentagonal bipyramidal geometry. The distances of Cd–O bonds are in the range of 2.265(3) Å to 2.507(3) Å.16 As shown in Fig. 4 (top), L12− also act as bidentate bridging ligands, which are similar to that in complex 1, to connect metal ions into one-dimensional chains with the Cd⋯Cd distances of 3.6435(6) Å and 13.5096(17) Å, respectively. There are also two kinds of rings lined alternatively in the chains: one kind is 4-membered ring, the other kind is 28-membered ring. One carboxylategroup adopts chelate-bridging bis-bidentate coordination mode to connect two Cd(ii) centers, and another carboxylategroup acts as a chelate ligand (Scheme 1B). Compared with complex 1, the torsion angles of –OCH2COO– groups increased to 171.198(3)° and −179.184(3)°, respectively. As a result of the torsion angles change, the adjacent 1D chains are connected to furnish a 2D planar structure by both hydrogen bonds and π–π interactions which is different with complex 1. As illustrated in Fig. 4 (right), the neighboring naphthyl rings exhibit offset face-to-face stacking mode with the distance between two naphthyl rings centers (d1) of 3.749(2) Å, the vertical distance between two naphthyl rings (d2) of 3.520(2) Å and the angle between d1 and d2 of 21.734(7)°. Moreover, the adjacent layers are thus cross-linked into a 3D network by extensive hydrogen bonds (Fig. 4, left). The geometrical parameters pertaining to the hydrogen bonds are collected in Table 2.

Structural description of 3

There are two distinct crystallographically independent cobalt atoms (Co1 and Co2) in complex 3 (Fig. 5). Co1 is coordinated to four oxygen atoms (O1, O1#2, O2#3, and O2#4) from four L12− ligands, and two oxygen atoms from two coordinated water molecules (O9 and O9#2), forming a slightly distorted octahedral geometry with the average Co–O bond length of 2.1045 Å. Co2 also adopts a slightly distorted octahedral geometry. Four oxygen atoms (O7, O8, O7#5 and O8#5) from four water molecules occupy the equator plane and the axis position is completed by two oxygen atoms (O6 and O6#5) from two L12− ligands with the average Co–O bond length of 2.0879 Å. In complex 3, the L12− ligand adopts the same coordination mode as which in complex 1 (Scheme 1A). Co2 atoms are linked by carboxyl bridges to generate 1D chains with the Co⋯Co distance of 4.6809(9) Å, and all cobalt atoms are bridged by L12− ligands to form a 2D layer (Fig. 6). The large quantities of coordinated water give plentiful hydrogen bonds (listed in Table 2). As shown in Fig. 7, the 2D layers are connected by hydrogen bonds to give rise to a 3D structure.

Structural descriptions of 4 and 5

Complexes 4 and 5 all crystallize in the monoclinic crystal system, space groupP21/c and exhibit similar structures. Here, only the structure of 4 is described in detail. The coordination environment of complex 4, shown in Fig. 8, consists of one Eu(iii) ion, one L12− ligand, one nitroso group17 and one coordinated water molecule in each unit cell. The metal centers are nine-coordinated by five carboxylateoxygen atoms (O3, O4, O4#1, O5#5 and O6#7) from four L12− ligands, three oxygen atoms (O7, O9#2 and O9#6) of nitroso groups, and one oxygen atom from one coordinated water. The distances of Eu–O bonds ranged from 2.335(5) Å to 2.609(5) Å. One carboxylategroup adopts bis-monodentate coordination mode to bridge two Eu(iii) centers, and another carboxylategroup acts as a chelating ligand (Scheme 1C). All Eu atoms are lined up by oxygen atoms in b direction and generated a 1D chain. Similar to complexes 1, 2 and 3, L12− act as bidentate bridging ligands to connect parallel 1D chains into 2D layers which contain two types of channels. As shown in Fig. 9(a) and 9(b), all the channels can be classified to A type (a) and B type (b). Viewing perpendicular to the ab plane, the directions of naphthyl rings in A type are all downward, while those in B type are all upward. All 2D layers are combined by nitroso groups as pillars to give rise to a 3D framework, and the channels are arranged in “–A–B–A–B–” model (Fig. 9(c) and 9(d)).

Structural description of 6

There is only one hexacoordinated crystallographically unique cadmium(ii) ion in the fundamental building unit of complex 6. As illustrated in Fig. 10, the Cd center presents a slightly distorted octahedral geometry, with three oxygen atoms (O6, O6#4, O5#3) from three carboxylategroups of three separated L22− ligands and one oxygen atom (O2) from –OCH2– group composing the equatorial plane, while one oxygen atom (O3) from carboxylategroup of L22− ligand and one aqua molecule (O7) located at the axial apical positions. The distances of Cd–O bonds ranged from 2.1502(44) Å to 2.5351(35) Å in the rational extent.18 The two arms of L22− ligand show significant deviation from the central biphenyl rings attributing to its flexibility. One is arranged above the plane of biphenyl rings, and the other is below. In addition, two different coordination modes are observed: one is monodentate-bridging mode named G type shown by green color, in which the oxygen atom of –OCH2– group of L22− ligand does not participate in the coordination (Scheme 1D). Another one named P type in pink color showed more complicated mode, in which one carboxylateoxygen atom acts as a μ2-bridge linking two Cd(ii) atoms, and the other carboxylateoxygen atom just coordinates to one Cd(ii) atom. Moreover, a five-membered ring (Cd1, O6, C9, C10, O2) is formed by the oxygen of –OCH2– group coordinated to one Cd(ii) atom with the μ2-bridging oxygen together (Scheme 1E). It is interesting to note that the aromatic carboxyls in two types of L22− ligands are alternately slanting up and down the biphenyl plane resulting from the presence of ether groups in both two arms. In G type the carbon atom of carboxyl is deviated from the biphenyl plane at a distance about 1.122(2) Å, and in P type the distance is about 0.949(2) Å, which agree with the fact that in G type the –CH2COO– groups deviating from the plane of biphenyl rings more obvious than in P type.

A view of complex 6 along the b axis is shown in Fig. 11(a). All Cd atoms are linked by carboxylate bridges to generate a 1D chain of [Cd2C4O4] SBUs, and the P type L22− ligands bridge the 1D chains into 2D layer. Viewing along the a axis, the G type connect each 2D layer into the 3D framework with octagonal channel as can be seen in Fig. 11(b) and (c). From a topological perspective, the SBU [Cd2O6] acts as a 6-connected node, and complex 6 represents pcu α-Po topology by using flexible L22− ligands as linkers (Fig. 12).

Structural descriptions of 7 and 8

Complexes 7 and 8 crystallize in the triclinic space groupP and form 3D supramolecular structures by hydrogen-bonded and π–π stacking interactions. The complexes are isostructural. Thus, only the structure of 7 is discussed here as a representation. The asymmetric unit comprises half a cobalt atom, half a L22− ligand and two coordinated water molecules. As shown in Fig. 13, Co(ii) atom is six-coordinated by two carboxylateoxygen atoms (O3, O3#2) from two different ligands in the axial direction, and four water molecules filled the vacant coordination sites to construct a slightly distorted octahedra. The distances of Co–O bonds are in the range of 2.0630(22) Å to 2.1056(21) Å. It is worthy of noting that the angles around the Co center (O1–Co1–O1#2, O2–Co1–O2#2, O3–Co1–O3#2) are all 180°. Compared with complex 6, the L22− ligand also adopts the coordination mode of monodentate-bridging just like the G type L22− ligand (Scheme 1D). However, the –OCH2COO– groups are nearly coplanar with the biphenyl plane in complex 7, which is in accordance with the deviation distance of 0.168(1) Å from the carboxyl carbon atoms to the plane of biphenyl rings. All Co(ii) atoms are bridged by L22− ligands to form 1D chains, and the 1D chains are combined to 2D layer by hydrogen bonds [O1b–H1B⋯O3c, O1b–H1B⋯O2a and O2d–H2Cd⋯O1e] and π–π interactions. As illustrated in Fig. 14(a), the parallel biphenyl rings exhibit offset face-to-face stacking mode with the distances of 4.923(6) Å (d1) and 3.589(0) Å (d2), and the angle between d1 and d2 is 43.202(5)°. Moreover, the adjacent layers are linked into a 3D supramolecular structure by hydrogen bonds O1c–H1Ae⋯O4a and O2d–H2Cd⋯O2a as shown in Fig. 14(b) and (c). The details of hydrogen bonds are listed in Table 2.

Luminescent properties

The emission spectra of free ligand H2L1, complexes 1, 2 and 4 in the solid state at room temperature are investigated upon the same excitation at 375 nm, as depicted in Fig. S3.† It can be observed that free H2L1 ligand gives strong fluorescent emission at 360 nm and 375 nm, respectively. Emission peak at 355 nm in 1 shows a small blue shift (5 nm) compared with that of the H2L1 ligand. The emission at 375 nm quenched, which is probably due to the spin–orbit coupling effects through M–L bonds to the π-system of the fluorophores.19 Complex 2 exhibits the similar emission character to the free H2L1 ligand. The decreased intensity of 1 and 2 are perhaps caused by amounts of coordinated water. The spectrum of complex 3 exhibits the characteristic transitions of 5D0 → 7Fn(n = 0 → 4) of Eu(iii) ions at 579, 590, 618, 650 and 692 nm, respectively. The absence of the ligand-based emission suggests that the energy transfer from the ligand to the Eu(iii) center is effective (LMCT).20 The luminescent properties of free ligand H2L2 and complex 6 are also investigated upon the same excitation at 350 nm, as depicted in Fig. S4.† The free H2L2 ligand exhibits strong fluorescent emission at 387 nm, and complex 6 shows similar emission character to the free H2L2 ligand with a small blue shift (5 nm). The coordination water in 6 may be responsible for the small reduction of intensity. The emission decay curves of complexes 1, 2, 4 and 6 are best fit by bi-exponentials in solid, respectively (Fig. S5–S8†). All the corresponding lifetime data are collected in Table 3.

Magnetic properties of 3, 4, 7 and 8

Temperature dependent magnetic susceptibility data for polycrystalline complex 3 was obtained on a SQUID magnetometer under an applied field of 2000 Oe over the temperature range of 1.8–300 K. The χMT and χMversusT are displayed in Fig. 15. The value of χMT at 300 K is 2.85 cm3K mol−1 which is greater than that expected for one isolated spin-only ion (χMT = 1.876 cm3 K mol−1 for one isolated S = 3/2 ion) indicating that a significant orbital contribution is involved. Along with the lowering temperature, the χMT value decreases slowly and then more rapidly below 80 K to reach the value of 0.542 cm3 K mol−1 at 1.8 K. There are maximums in the χMversusT curve and the shapes of the χMTversusT curve are characteristic of the occurrence of weak antiferromagnetic interactions between the Co(ii) centers. The data can be fit by an expression (eqn (1)–(4)) for S = 3/2 systems with dominant zero field splitting effects:21 (1) (2) χ′ = (χ∥ + 2χ⊥)/3 (3) (4) where x is D/(kT) and D is the magnitude of the ZFS, and all symbols have their normal meanings, zj′ represents the interaction between Co(ii) ions. The best fitting gave D = −68.9 cm−1, zj′ = −1.71 cm−1 and g = 2.53 with R = [(χMT)calcd−(χMT)obsd]2/(χMT)obsd2 = 0.6 × 10−5.

The temperature dependence of the χMT product for 4 under an applied field of 100 Oe in the range of 1.8–300 K is shown in Fig. 16. The χMT value of 1.44 cm3 K mol−1 at 300 K is slightly less than the theoretical value of 1.50 cm3K mol−1 corresponding to one Eu(iii) ion with the populated exited states. Upon further cooling, the population of the excited levels deflates and χMT continuously decreases reaching 0.20 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K. This behavior indicates a ground state 7F0 for Eu(iii) ions. For only the non-magnetic ground state 7F0 of Eu(iii) is populated, χEu can be analyzed with the expression as follows (eqn (5)–(6)):22 χEu = (Nβ2/3kTx)[24 + (13.5x − 1.5)e−x + (67.5x − 2.5)e−3x + (189x − 3.5)e−6x + (405x − 4.5)e−10x + (742.5x − 5.5)e−15x + (1228.5x − 6.5)e−21x]/(1 + 3e−x + 5e−3x + 7e−6x + 9e−10x + 11e−15x + 13e−21x) (5) χM = χEu/(1 − (2zj′/NgJβ2)χEu) (6) where x = λ/kT, λ is the spin–orbit coupling parameter, gJ is the Landé factor and zj′ is the coupling interaction between EuIII ions within chain. The best fitting results gave: gJ = 1.99, λ = 311 and zj′ = −0.2 cm−1 with R = [(χMT)calcd − (χMT)obsd]2/(χMT)obsd2 = 2.4 × 10−4.23 The negative value of zj′ suggests the weak antiferromagnetic coupling between the paramagnetic EuIII ions in 4.

The χMT and χMversusT for complex 7 are displayed in Fig. 17 which are similar to the curves of 3. The χMT value at 300 K (3.20 cm3 K mol−1) is larger than the spin-only value for a high-spin Co(ii) center (1.88 cm3K mol−1 for S = 3/2 and g = 2.0) indicating that a significant orbital contribution is involved. The value of χMT decreases slowly to 2.86 cm3 K mol−1 at 100 K. Below this temperature, the χMT value decreases more rapidly and reaches a minimum of 0.98 cm3 K mol−1 at 1.8 K. The data can also be fit by the expression (eqn (1)–(4)) for S = 3/2 systems with dominant zero field splitting effects. 21 The best fitting gave D = 80.9 cm−1, zj′ = −0.75 cm−1 and g = 2.66 with R = [(χMT)calcd − (χMT)obsd]2/(χMT)obsd2 = 0.3 × 10−5. The negative zj′ value suggests the antiferromagnetic interactions between the Co(ii) centers.

At 300 K, χMT is 1.28 cm3K mol−1 which is slightly higher than the theoretical value of 1.00 cm3 K mol−1 (S = 1 and g = 2.0). As the temperature decreases, the value remains essentially constant until 100 K. However, the value decreases rapidly as the temperature decreases below 100 K, and then reaches a value of 0.26 cm3 K mol−1 at 1.8 K. Thermal analysis and PXRD results

To study the thermal stability of the complexes, thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were performed on polycrystalline samples of complexes 1–8 (Fig. S9 and S10†). Thermogravimetric analysis of 1 indicates that the first weight loss of 9.86% (calcd: 9.59%) at 191 °C corresponding to the loss of two coordinated water molecules, then the structure was decomposed since 369 °C. The TGA curve for 2 shows the initial weight loss in the temperature range of 141–212 °C, which can be ascribed to the removal of two coordinated water molecules (observed: 8.52% and calcd: 8.35%). Further weight loss indicates the decomposition of coordination framework from 313 °C. Complex 3 is less stable with the weight loss of one coordinated H2O molecules 13.95% (calcd 13.84%) before 210 °C, and then the framework was decomposed since 332 °C. Complexes 4 and 5 have similar structures. We take complex 4 as an example. The TGA curve for 4 displays a gradual weight loss of 3.67% (calcd 3.71%) from 230 to 275 °C which may be attributed to the loss of one coordinated water molecule. The framework lost their stability around 318 °C. A gradual weight loss of 4.19% (calcd 4.18%) from 113 to 206 °C may suggest the loss of the coordinated water molecule in complex 6, and the decomposition occurred at 385 °C. While for complex 7, the loss of the four coordinated water molecules from 86 to 151 °C is significant with 16.78% (calcd 16.71%), and the sharp weight loss at 407 °C indicates that the whole structure decomposed. Complex 8 shows a curve similar to that of 7 for they are isostructural. Thus it is not described here in detail. To confirm whether the crystal structures are truly representative of the bulk materials, XRD experiments were carried out for complexes 1–8. The PXRD patterns for 1–8 are presented in Fig. S11–S18.† The diffraction peaks of both simulated and experimental patterns match well in key positions, indicating the phase purities of complexes 1–8.

Conclusions

In summary, we have presented a detailed examination of the structures and properties of eight new complexes based on flexible carboxyl ligands H2L1 and H2L2 with different metals. The ligands display various coordination modes owing to the flexibility of –OCH2COO– group and the steric hindrance of aromatic rings. The thermal stability of these eight complexes and luminescent properties of 1, 2, 4 and 6 have also been studied. In addition, weak antiferromagnetic exchange interactions are observed in 3, 4, 7 and 8. Ongoing work is aimed at finding out more regulations in constructing metal–organic frameworks based on H2L1 and H2L2 ligands.
