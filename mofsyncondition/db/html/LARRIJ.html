Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have attracted a great deal of attention because of their highly porous, tunable structures, and good stability.1,2 In the field of MOF chemistry, strategies involving metal-containing secondary building units (SBUs) have been widely implemented for constructing new structures.3 Some metal-containing SBUs have been applied for construction of MOFs with expandable frameworks.4 Among them, the selected SBUs are predominately discrete clusters, such as [Zn4O(COO)6], [Fe3O(COO)6]+, [Zr6O4(OH)4(COO)12], and so on.5 On the other hand, SBUs can also be infinite in one-dimension (1D) or two-dimension.6,7 In particular, MOFs with 1D rod-shaped SBUs (rod MOFs) are attracting increasing attention because of their potential applications in gas adsorption, dye separation, and fluorescence sensing.8–11 It can be expected that rod MOFs can be endowed with some special functions when a unique type of SBU was introduced. For instance, chiral MOFs can be prepared by introducing homochiral helical SBUs.12 However, these rod-shaped SBUs can hardly be controlled and their structures are difficult to predict.13 Thus, it is meaningful but with challenges to construct rod MOFs based on new types of rod-shaped SBUs.

Heavy metal ions can cause great harm to human health.14 For example, too much Cu2+ can cause copper poisoning and liver cirrhosis, whereas excess Fe3+ may lead to mental decline, heart damage, iron poisoning and other diseases.15 In addition, nitroaromatic compounds (NACs) are highly toxic and potentially explosive, which will also pose a great threat to ecological civilization and human health.16,17 Therefore, it is necessary to monitor the concentration of these substances in the environment. For these detection purposes, fluorescence sensing technologies have attracted great attention due to their advantages of high sensitivity, good selectivity, and rapid response time.18,19 So far, various sensing materials have been investigated for detecting these pollutants.20,21 In this field, MOFs have gained intensive attention because of their large specific surface area, adjustable spectral performance, and rapid response.22 For instance, Cao and co-workers constructed an In-MOF with chiral channels for detecting Cu2+ ions.23 It is shown that the empty orbitals of Cu2+ could cause the energy/charge transfer and luminescence quenching of the MOF. Hou et al. synthesized two anionic MOFs for selectively detecting nitrofurazone in water with a detection limit as low as 0.2 ppm.24 Biswas and collaborators reported a thienothiophene-based MOF for Fe3+ ion sensing. It is shown that electron transfer from the MOF to the half-filled 3d orbitals of Fe3+ ions leads to efficient emission quenching.25 These results show that MOFs can serve as effective fluorescence sensors for sensing ions and NACs.

Based on the above considerations, we aimed to construct new rod MOFs for the detection of metal ions and NACs. Indium(iii) was selected due to its advantages of multiple coordination modes, while H3TCA was selected due to its flexible coordination configuration and good fluorescence performance. Herein, a new MOF, namely [In5(TCA)2(HTCA)2(OH)5]·6DMF·H2O (JOU-25), has been successfully synthesized under solvothermal conditions (H3TCA = 4,4′,4′′-triphenylamine tricarboxylic acid). Structural analyses show that JOU-25 displays a three-dimensional (3D) framework with unique homochiral In–O–In helical chains. It is shown that JOU-25 can be used as an effective fluorescence sensor for selectively sensing Fe3+, Cu2+ and nitroaromatics (NACs) in water by virtue of its highly porous structure, strong fluorescence emission, exposed uncoordinated carboxyl groups, and good stability. Moreover, the quenching mechanism is also discussed briefly.

Results and discussion

Crystal structure of MOFs

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction (SCXRD) analyses reveal that the single crystals of JOU-25 display a 3D framework with two types of homochiral In–O–In helical chains. The crystal products of JOU-25 contain a pair of enantiomers, which are denoted as JOU-25-R and JOU-25-S, respectively. JOU-25-R crystallizes in the hexagonal space group P61, whereas JOU-25-S crystallizes in the hexagonal space group P65. The CCDC numbers for JOU-25-R and JOU-25-S are 2103362 and 2103363, respectively. For clarification, only the structure of JOU-25-R will be described in detail. The asymmetric unit of JOU-25-R consists of five crystallographic independent In3+ ions, four TCA ligands and five hydroxyls. As shown in Fig. 1a and b, all In3+ ions are coordinated by four oxygen atoms from four carboxylic groups and two bridged oxygen atoms from hydroxyl groups in distorted octahedral coordination geometries. The length of In–O bonds ranges from 2.07 to 2.206 Å. Interestingly, the In1, In2, In3 and In4 ions of JOU-25-R are connected into a 1D right-handed helical chain (H1), while the In5 ions are connected into another type of 1D right-handed helical chain (H2) (Fig. 1c and d). H1 has an outer diameter of 11 Å, an inner diameter of 4.6 Å, and a screw pitch of 16.9 Å (Fig. S1a†). H2 has an outer diameter of 7.4 Å, an inner diameter of 1.8 Å, and a screw pitch of 16.9 Å (Fig. S1b†). These helical chains are further connected by [HTCA]2− and [TCA]3− to form a 3D framework (Fig. 1e, i and j). As viewed along the c axis, two H1 and one H2 chains are connected into an irregular channel with a window size of about 7.6 Å × 7.1 Å (Fig. S2a†). The uncoordinated carboxyl groups are exposed in the channel. Meanwhile, three H1 chains are connected by [TCA]3− to form another irregular channel with a window size of about 10.2 Å × 9.5 Å (Fig. S2b†). As shown in Fig. 1f–h, JOU-25-S also displays a 3D framework but with only left-handed In–O–In helical chains. PLATON calculations show that the porosities of JOU-25-R and JOU-25-S are 53% and 54.2%, respectively.26 The solvent molecules are disorderly distributed in the pores of JOU-25 and cannot be determined from the SCXRD data. Therefore, the whole formula of JOU-25 was supported by the results of elemental analysis and thermogravimetric analysis (Fig. S3†). To investigate the thermal stability of JOU-25, thermal gravimetric analysis was carried out from 50 to 800 °C at a heating rate of 5 °C min−1. As shown in Fig. S3,† the first weight loss in the temperature range 50–240 °C was attributed to the removal of lattice DMF and water molecules. The second weight loss started at about 400 °C may be attributed to the decomposition of the framework. The powder X-ray diffraction pattern of the as-synthesized crystals is consistent with the simulated one, indicating that the phase purity of the synthesized samples is high (Fig. S4†). In addition, JOU-25 can keep its stability in DMF and DMF : H2O (1 : 1) solutions over one day (Fig. S5†).

The topological structure of JOU-25 was also investigated according to the tutorial method proposed by O'Keeffe et al.27 As shown in Fig. 2, when the carbon atoms of the carboxylate groups are considered as points of extension, both helical chains can be defined as lengthy SBUs composed of edge-sharing distorted tetrahedra. Herein, these carbon atoms can be considered as six-connected nodes. Meanwhile, [TCA]3− ligands can be reduced to 3-connected nodes, and [HTCA]2− ligands can be regarded as rods (Fig. 2c and d). Then, the structure of JOU-25 can be reduced to a (3,6)-connected net with a Schläfli symbol of {63}{36·44·62·72·8}{36·44·62·73}2{36·45·52·62}2 (Fig. 2e). As far as we know, JOU-25 not only provides a new topology in MOF chemistry, but also represents the first example of a MOF containing two types of different homochiral helical metal–oxygen chains.

The solid-state luminescence properties of TCA and JOU-25 were studied at room temperature. As shown in Fig. 3, free TCA shows a maximum emission peak at 439 nm (λex = 340 nm), whereas JOU-25 displays a maximum emission peak at about 443 nm (λex = 340 nm), indicating that the emission of JOU-25 may mainly originate from ligand-based luminescence.28 In addition, JOU-25 dispersed in DMF/H2O (V/V = 1 : 1) also shows a strong emission peak at 418 nm (λex = 340 nm) (Fig. S6†).

Based on the good fluorescence properties of JOU-25, it was developed as a potential metal ion sensor. To investigate the sensing performance of JOU-25, it was firstly ground into fine powder and then dispersed into DMF/H2O (V : V = 1 : 1) mixed solution with a concentration of 1 mg mL−1. A certain amount of DMF makes JOU-25 disperse better. The dispersion was ultrasonicated over 1 hour and aged overnight before use. Sixteen metal ions were selected for sensing experiments, including Pb2+, Zn2+, Cr3+, Cd2+, K+, Ag+, Al3+, Ba2+, Co2+, Ni2+, Mg2+, Na+, Ca2+, Mn2+, Fe3+, and Cu2+. As shown in Fig. S7,† when different metal ion solutions with the same concentration (1 × 10−5 M) were added to the MOF dispersion, the emission intensity of the JOU-25 dispersion was obviously quenched when Cu2+ or Fe3+ was added. In contrast, there was no obvious fluorescence change after adding other metal ions. These results show that JOU-25 may be used as a fluorescence sensor for selectively sensing Cu2+/Fe3+ ions. After that, the quenching effect function of the Cu2+/Fe3+ concentration on JOU-25 was studied (Fig. 4a and c). The relationship between the emission peak intensity of JOU-25 and the metal ion concentration can be fitted as I0/I = 0.9169 + 392 867 [Cu2+] (R2 = 0.98) for Cu2+ and I0/I = 0.9536 + 313 907 [Fe3+] (R2 = 0.99) for Fe3+, respectively. These results are consistent with the Stern–Volmer equation: I0/I = 1 + Ksv[M],29 where [M] is the concentration of Cu2+/Fe3+, I0 is the initial emission intensity of the JOU-25 dispersion, and I is the emission intensity of JOU-25 after adding a certain amount of metal ions. The detection limits were respectively calculated to be 3.05 × 10−7 mol L−1 for Cu2+ ions and 3.82 × 10−7 mol L−1 for Fe3+ ions using the equation LOD = 3s/Ksv, where s is the standard deviation of the blank.30 As shown in Table S4 and S5,† the Ksv value and the detection limit of JOU-25 toward Cu2+/Fe3+ sensing are comparable to those of other reported MOF-based materials. In order to determine whether the presence of other ions will affect the sensing ability of JOU-25, 14 other ions of the same concentration were added to the MOF dispersion. It was found that the fluorescence of JOU-25 only changed slightly, but the emission was obviously quenched when Cu2+ or Fe3+ was added (Fig. S8†). The recyclability tests of JOU-25 toward Cu2+/Fe3+ detection have also been carried out (Fig. S9a and b†). The fluorescence emission of JOU-25 after five cycles only had a slight decay compared with the initial fluorescence intensity, suggesting its good reusability. These results showed the high sensing performance of JOU-25 toward Cu2+/Fe3+ sensing. Then, JOU-25 can be used as a potential fluorescence sensor for selective Cu2+/Fe3+ sensing.

To explore the underlying quenching mechanism of JOU-25, the following experiments were carried out. Firstly, in order to exclude the possibility of emission quenching caused by structural collapse, the crystals of JOU-25 were immersed in Cu2+/Fe3+ solution (DMF : H2O = 1 : 1) for one day. After that, the crystals of JOU-25 were filtered out and analyzed by the PXRD technique. The PXRD patterns of Cu2+/Fe3+@JOU-25 were well consistent with the simulated one, indicating that the MOF can keep its stability (Fig. S10a†).31 Thus, the emission quenching phenomenon was not attributed to the structural collapse. Thereafter, the crystals of JOU-25 were immersed in different metal ion solutions. It can be found that only when the crystals were immersed in Cu2+ or Fe3+ solution, their colour changed. Then, the UV-vis absorption spectra of JOU-25 and Cu2+/Fe3+@JOU-25 were measured. As shown in Fig. S10c,† the UV-vis absorption spectra of both Cu2+@JOU-25 and Fe3+@JOU-25 show new absorption bands. This may be attributed to the presence of charge transfer between JOU-25 and the adsorbed Cu2+ or Fe3+ ions.32 This charge transfer process may cause emission quenching.33,34 On the other hand, it is found that both the absorption bands of the Cu2+ and Fe3+ solutions cover the emission band of JOU-25, indicating that the inner filter effect of metal ions may also occur (Fig. S10b†).35,36 The emission light of JOU-25 may be absorbed by Cu2+/Fe3+ ions, leading to emission quenching. A fluorescence lifetime study shows that the lifetime of JOU-25 exhibits an obvious shift after adding Fe3+ or Cu2+ ions, suggesting the presence of a dynamic quenching process (Fig. S11†). In summary, the emission quenching of JOU-25 in the presence of Cu2+ or Fe3+ may be attributed to the combination of the charge transfer, the inner filter effect of Cu2+/Fe3+ ions and dynamic quenching.

JOU-25 can also be utilized for selective recognition of organic solvents. Sixteen different organic solvents were selected for the study, including ethylene glycol (EG), tetrahydrofuran (THF), acetonitrile (MeCN), ethanol, diethyl ether (Et2O), dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO), methanol (MT), ethyl acetate (EA), N-hexane (NYH), triethanolamine (TEA), petroleum ether (PE), cyclohexane (CYH), dichloromethane (DCM), isopropyl alcohol (IPA), chloroform (CHCl3), and nitrobenzene (NB). As shown in Fig. S12a,† the emission of the JOU-25 dispersion was quenched only when NB was added. Then, the fluorescence change of JOU-25 in different concentrations of NB was investigated. When 7.5 × 10−5 M NB was added, the emission of JOU-25 was almost completely quenched (Fig. 5b). The relationship between the emission intensity of JOU-25 and the NB concentration can be fitted as I0/I = 0.959 + 49803[NB] (R2 = 0.986) (inset of Fig. 5a). These results are also consistent with the Stern–Volmer equation: I0/I = 1 + Ksv[M], where [M] is the concentration of NB and I is the fluorescence intensity of JOU-25 after addition of NB. As shown in Table S6,† the quenching Ksv value of JOU-25 toward NB sensing was compared with those of other reported MOFs. In order to determine whether the existence of other organic solvents will interfere with the quenching ability of JOU-25, 14 other solvents of the same concentration were added to the JOU-25 dispersion, but the emission intensity of JOU-25 showed almost no change (Fig. S12b†). In contrast, when NB was added, the emission intensity of JOU-25 was obviously quenched, indicating the high sensing selectivity of JOU-25. The recyclability tests indicate that JOU-25 has good reusability for NB detection (Fig. S9c†). All these results indicate that JOU-25 can be used as a potential fluorescence sensor for NB sensing.

To explore the quenching mechanism of JOU-25 for NB detection, the following experiments were carried out. Firstly, in order to exclude the possibility of fluorescence quenching caused by structural collapse, JOU-25 were immersed in NB solution (DMF : H2O, V : V = 1 : 1) for one day. It is found that the PXRD pattern of NB@JOU-25 is consistent with the experimental one, indicating the stability of JOU-25 (Fig. S13†). Thereafter, the UV-vis absorption spectra of JOU-25 and NB were studied. It is found that the absorption band of NB almost does not cover the emission band of JOU-25. Then, the emission quenching of JOU-25 in the presence of NB may not be due to the FRET effect.37 As the absorption band of JOU-25 mostly overlapped with that of NB, an excitation energy absorption competition may occur (Fig. S14†).38 Meanwhile, the LUMO energy of H3TCA ligands is calculated to be about 1.8 eV,39 which is higher than those of NB and other NACs (Fig. S15†),40–48 indicating that the quenching phenomenon may also be attributed to electron transfer between H3TCA and the electron withdrawing group NB.49–51 Therefore, the emission quenching of JOU-25 in the presence of NB may be attributed to the combination of the competition of excitation energy absorption and the electron transfer effect. In addition, we also investigated the sensing ability of JOU-25 for other nitroaromatic compounds, including 4-nitroaniline (4-NA), 4-nitrophenol (4-NP), 4-nitrobenzonitrile (4-NBN), 4-nitrotoluene (4-NT), m-nitroacetophenone (3-NAP), 4-nitroacetophenone (4-NAP), 1-bromo-4-nitrobenzene (1-Br-4-NB), and 4-nitrochlorobenzene (4-NCB). When 7.5 × 10−5 M different nitroaromatics were respectively added into the JOU-25 dispersion, the order of the quenching efficiency was NB (90%) > 4-NA (89.1%) > 4-NP (88.4%) > 1-Br-4-NB (39.3%) > 4-NT (20%) > 4-NCB (12.7%) > 4-NBN (4%) > 3-NAP (3%) > 4-NAP (2%) (Fig. S16†). These results indicate that JOU-25 can also be used for detecting other nitroaromatics, such as 4-NA and 4-NP.

Conclusions

In conclusion, a novel chiral In-MOF (JOU-25) constructed from an achiral ligand has been solvothermally synthesized. Interestingly, structural analyses reveal that JOU-25 not only provides a new topology in MOF chemistry, but also represents the first example of a MOF containing two types of different homochiral helical metal–oxygen–metal chains. Due to the good fluorescence performance, JOU-25 was developed as an excellent fluorescence sensor for selective detection of Cu2+, Fe3+ and NB in water. Further studies show that the quenching performance of JOU-25 for Cu2+/Fe3+ sensing may be attributed to the charge transfer, the inner filter effect of Cu2+/Fe3+ ions and dynamic quenching. Meanwhile, the quenching performance of JOU-25 for NB sensing may be attributed to the competition of excitation energy absorption and the electron transfer effect. In addition, JOU-25 can also be applied for detecting other nitroaromatics, such as 4-NA and 4-NP. These results indicate that JOU-25 can be applied as a potential functional material for sensing Cu2+, Fe3+ and nitroaromatics in water.

Experimental section

Synthesis of JOU-25

A mixture of In(NO3)3·4.5H2O (0.02 mmol, 7.5 mg), H3TCA (0.02 mmol, 7.5 mg), DMF (1 ml), H2O (0.5 ml) and HNO3 (60 μL, 65 wt%) was ultrasonically treated for 3 minutes, transferred into a 10 mL pressure-resistant glass bottle, and then heated at 85 °C in an oven for 24 hours. Yellow block crystals were obtained by filtration and hand-picking. Yield: about 10% (calculated by H3TCA). Anal. calcd for C87H64In5N5O31 (%): C, 46.45; H, 2.87; N, 3.11. Found: C, 46.74; H, 2.52; N, 2.85. IR (KBr, cm−1): ν = 3426 (s), 2923 (w), 1648 (w), 1594 (s), 1536 (w), 1392 (s), 1315 (m), 1270 (w), 1176 (m), 1097 (m), 1008 (w), 849 (w), 783 (m), 705 (w), 667 (w), 534 (w).

Experiments for metal ion sensing

Freshly prepared crystals of JOU-25 were finely ground. Then, 3 mg of the obtained powder was immersed in 3 mL of DMF/H2O (V : V = 1 : 1) solutions with different metal nitrates (0.01 mmol L−1). The mixed solutions were ultrasonically treated for 30 min and aged overnight for luminescence studies. In the metal ion sensing experiments, the excitation wavelengths were set to 340 nm, and the luminescence intensity was monitored in the range of 350–650 nm.

For recycling experiments: 3 mg of the JOU-25 powder was immersed in a cuvette with 3 mL of DMF/H2O (V : V = 1 : 1) solution, in which a certain amount of Cu2+ (Fe3+ or NB) was added. After the fluorescence emission test, the powder of JOU-25 was centrifugally separated and washed with DMF. Then, the powder was redispersed in DMF for the next test.

Experiments for organic molecule sensing

Different organic solvents were diluted to 0.05 M with DMF/H2O (V : V = 1 : 1), and 30 μL of the diluted solutions were added to the stock dispersion of JOU-25. Under the excitation wavelength of 340 nm, the luminescence intensity was monitored in the range of 350–650 nm.
