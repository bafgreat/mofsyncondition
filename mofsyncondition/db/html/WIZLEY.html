Synthesis, crystal structure, solid state electronic spectra and thermal study of three cobalt(II)–selenocyanate complexes: In situ room temperature transformation of 4,4′-dipyridyldisulfide to 4,4′-dipyridylsulfide

Abstract

Three new supramolecular networks of cobalt(II), [Co(phen)2(SeCN)2] (1), [Co(bpe)2(SeCN)2]n (2), and {[Co(bps)2(SeCN)2]·H2O}n (3) [phen = 1,10-phenanthroline; bpe = 1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)ethane; bps = 4,4-bipyridylsulfide] have been synthesized and characterized by X-ray single crystal structure determination, solid state electronic spectroscopy and thermogravimetric analysis. Complex 1 is a neutral discrete cobalt(II) complex with cis located selenocyanate anions and forms 2D supramolecular layers through π−π interactions. Complexes 2 and 3 have a comparable structure based on metal center bridged by two bpe or bps ligands trough pyridinyl N-donor to give rise to a linear chain. The selenocyanate anions (SeCN−) bind the metal centers through nitrogen and occupy trans axial positions of the octahedral metal coordination environment. These pendant pseudohalides located on both sides of the 1D polymeric chain help to reinforce the packing through weak interactions. Solid state electronic spectra of all the complexes corroborate the presence of octahedral high spin cobalt(II) centers in all complexes.

Graphical abstract

Three coordination complexes of Co(II) with selenocyanide (SeCN−) and N-donor neutral ligands have been synthesized and characterized by X-ray single crystal structure determination. All the complexes form supramolecular structure through weak interactions. Solid state electronic spectra corroborate the presence of high spin octahedral Co(II) centers in complexes. The selenocyanide moieties help to reinforce the packing through hydrogen bonding.

1. Introduction

Crystal engineering of coordination compounds has attracted great attention during recent times due to the potentiality of these compounds as functional material [1], [2], [3], [4], [5] and also for the challenge of achieving various possible architectures and dimensionalities [6], [7], [8], [9]. Successful design often depends on suitable choice of ligands and metals [10]. Whereas it is easier to achieve a targeted synthesis with rigid ligands, flexible and semi-flexible ligands often introduce irregularity due to their conformational freedom that opens up the possibility towards unpredictable arrangements [11], [12], [13]. In addition self-assembly of a multi component system with more than one ligand and metal ions is often more complex and the outcome is almost impossible to be predicted at our current state of understanding. N-donor dipyridyl ligands are among the most explored ones in the field of metal organic framework. Various rigid and flexible analogs of 4,4′-bipyridine have been used for this purpose [14], [15], [16], [17], [18]. During recent past we have been engaged in exploring the effect of weak interactions on coordination frameworks and one of our design strategies has been the use of linear pseudo halides in combination with dipyridyl ligands [19], [20]. This was motivated by the fact that rigid linear pseudohalides have a limited number of coordination modes, which do not complicate much the self-assembly process in combination with dipyridyl ligands and yet make the multi-component system and its self-assembly exploration easier to be interpreted.

4,4-bipyridyldisulfide (bpds) has attracted much attention in crystal engineering not only for its twisted conformation (with a C–S–S–C torsion angle of ca. 90°) and axial chirality that leads the potential ability to generate M and P chiral enantiomers [21], but also for the facile cleavage of the central S–S bond resulting in different in situ ligand reactions [22], [23], [24], [25], [26]. In fact, the starting reagent 4,4-bipyridyldisulfide was shown to convert to 4,4-bipyridylsulfide (bps), 4,4-bipyridyltrisulfide (bpts), 1-(4-pyridyl)-4-thiopyridine (ptp), or pyridine-4-thiolate (pt) [27], [28], [29], [30] (Chart 1). In addition, 1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)ethane (bpe) has been observed to exhibit anti and gauche conformations, causing to be remarkably versatile as spacer in extended systems [12], [31]. In contrast to bpe, the disulfide moiety in bpds is rigid and does not adopt an anti conformation.

As an extension of our continuing interest on exploring the effect of weak interactions on coordination frameworks of linear pseudo halides in combination with neutral N-donor ligands, three compounds of formula [Co(phen)2(SeCN)2] (1), [Co(bpe)2(SeCN)2]n (2), and {[Co(bps)2(SeCN)2]·H2O}n (3) have been prepared with the aim to study the crystal packing and weak interactions present therein. Mononuclear complex 1 with chelating phen forms a 2D supramolecular layered structure through π–π interactions. On the other hand both complexes 2 and 3 form 1D polymeric chains with identical topology through bridging bpe and bps, respectively, but different supramolecular architectures. It is interesting to note that for the synthesis of complex 3 we have used bpds as starting material but an unexpected in situ ligand synthesis at room temperature led bpds to undergo transformation to bps. To the best of our knowledge this is the first report of such a conversion occurred at room temperature.

2. Experimental

2.1. Materials and physical measurements

High purity cobalt nitrate hexahydrate, 1,10-phenanthroline monohydrate, 1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)ethane (bpe); 4,4-bipyridyldisulfide (bpds) and potassium selenocyanate (KSeCN) were purchased from Aldrich Chemical Company and were used as received. All other chemicals were of analytical grade.

Elemental analyses (C, H, N) were performed using a Perkin–Elmer 240C elemental analyzer. IR spectra were recorded as KBr pellets on a Bruker Vector 22FT IR spectrophotometer operating from 400 to 4000 cm−1. The UV–Vis reflectance spectra were recorded between 300 and 1400 nm with a UV-3600, Shimadzu UV–Vis–NIR spectrophotometer at room temperature in the solid state. Thermal analysis was carried out on a Shimadzu TGA 60 instrument under nitrogen environment.

2.2. Synthesis

The complexes have been synthesized adopting the procedures which are schematically given in Scheme 1.

2.2.1. [Co(phen)2(SeCN)2] (1)

An aqueous solution (5 mL) of potassium selenocyanate (0.287 g, 2 mmol) was added dropwise to an aqueous solution (5 mL) of cobalt nitrate hexahydrate (0.291 g, 1 mmol) under stirring condition. To this mixture a methanolic solution of 1,10-phenanthroline monohydrate (0.198 g, 1 mmol) was added and stirred for 1 h. The filtrate was kept in a CaCl2 desiccator, and brown single crystals suitable for X-ray structure determination were obtained after a week. They were separated, washed with ethanol and dried. Yield: 0.472 g (75%). Anal. data: Found: C, 49.56; H, 2.55; N, 13.33. Calc. for C26H16CoN6Se2 (629.30): C, 49.57; H, 2.54; N, 13.34%. μeff at 300 K = 5.02 BM.

2.2.2. [Co(bpe)2(SeCN)2]n (2)

An aqueous solution (5 mL) of potassium selenocyanate (0.287 g, 2 mmol) was added to an aqueous solution (5 mL) of cobalt nitrate hexahydrate (0.291 g, 1 mmol) with constant stirring. To the resulting deep pink colored reaction mixture a methanolic solution (10 mL) of 1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)ethane (bpe) (0.184 g, 1 mmol) was added and stirred for 30 min. A brown colored compound was separated out. Brown crystals of good quality were obtained by diffusing a methanolic solution (10 mL) of bpe on an aqueous (10 mL) layer containing Co(NO3)2·6H2O and KSeCN (1:2) in a tube. The brown colored crystals were deposited at the junction of the layers after a few days. Yield: 0.459 g (72%). Anal. data: Found: C, 48.94; H, 3.78; N, 13.16. Calc. for C26H24CoN6Se2 (637.36): C, 48.95; H, 3.76; N, 13.17%. μeff at 300 K = 5.04 BM.

2.2.3. {[Co(bps)2(SeCN)2]·H2O}n (3)

Complex 3 was prepared adopting the same procedure as for complex 2 using 4,4-bipyridyldisulfide (bpds) (0.220 g, 1 mmol) instead of bpe. Single crystals suitable for X-ray analysis were obtained by diffusing a methanolic solution (10 mL) of bpds on an aqueous layer (10 mL) containing Co(NO3)2·6H2O and KSeCN (1:2) in a tube. Deep pink colored crystals were deposited at the junction of the layers after a few days. Yield: 0.475 g (70%). Anal. data: Found: C, 38.84; H, 2.63; N, 12.34. Calc. for C22H18CoN6O2S2Se2 (679.39): C, 38.85; H, 2.64; N, 12.36%. μeff at 300 K = 5.00 BM.

2.3. Crystallographic data collection, structure solution and refinement

The X-ray data collections for complexes 1–3 were carried with Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) on a Bruker Smart Apex diffractometer equipped with CCD. The experiments were performed at 100 K for 1 and 2, and at room temperature for 3. Cell refinement, indexing and scaling of the data sets were done by using programs Bruker Smart Apex and Bruker Saint packages [32]. All the structures were solved by direct methods and subsequent Fourier analyses [33] and refined by the full-matrix least-squares method based on F2 with all observed reflections [33]. Hydrogen atoms were placed at calculated positions. In 3 the terminal selenium was found disordered on two positions (refined occupancy of 0.856(13)/0.144(13)), and a residual was isotropically refined as an oxygen of lattice water molecule (occupancy 0.5, H atoms not assigned). All the calculations were performed using the WinGX System, Ver 1.80.05 [34]. Crystal data and details of refinements are given in Table 1.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Crystal structure descriptions

3.1.1. [Co(phen)2(SeCN)2] (1)

Complex 1 is a neutral discrete octahedral cobalt molecule as depicted in Fig. 1 and 1S. The cobalt(II) ion is located on a crystallographic two fold axis and is bound to two cis located selenocyanate anions and chelated by two phenanthroline ligands. The Co–NCSe bond distance of 2.062(7) Å is significantly shorter than the Co–N(phen) bonds, which average to 2.158(6) Å, due to the different N donor hybridization. The isoselenocyanate ligand is practically linear [Se–C–N angle of 179.4(7)°] and is almost linearly coordinated being the C–N–Co angle of 162.6(6)°. The crystal packing shows the complexes interacting through π–π interactions (centroid ring distance of 3.76 Å) to form a 2D layered arrangement in the xy plane (Fig. 2). The compound is isomorphous with the corresponding SCN derivative [35].

3.1.2. [Co(bpe)2(SeCN)2] (2)

The structure of complex [Co(bpe)2(NCSe)2] (2) consists of linear –[Co-(bpe)2]n– coordination chains, extending along the [0 1 0] crystallographic direction, and lying in the xy plane (Fig. 3). The metallic Co(II) ions located on a twofold axis are bridged through two N,N′-bidentate bpe ligands. The coordination sphere around each metal center is a CoN6 octahedron where the equatorial positions are occupied by four N(bpe) donor atoms and the axial positions by terminal N-bonded and almost linear isoselenocyanate anions. The Co–NCSe bond distances [2.096(4) Å] are shorter than the Co–N(bpe) bonds of 2.182(4) and 2.184(4) Å, giving rise to a compressed octahedral coordination sphere in which all the bond angles are close to the ideal values. Table 2 summarizes the coordination bond distances and angles for compound 2. The chains are connected through non-conventional hydrogen bonds between Se(1)and C(25)–H of a pyridyl ring of symmetry related chain (C⋯Se distance of 3.531 Å, Fig. 4). The unique crystallographic independent bpe ligand adopts a gauche conformation [C–CH2–CH2–C torsion angle is 70.35°] providing an intermetallic distance of 10.006(3) Å, which is the longest among those measured in compounds containing Co-(bpe)2–Co fragments [31], [36], [37], [38], [39], [40], [41], [42] displayed in Table 3. From these data it is possible to derive that the intermetallic distance has a linear relationship with the decrease of the torsion angle about the bpe ethylene group (Fig. 5). The differences may be dictated by packing forces and or by the electronic properties of the additional ligands bound at cobalt center. The trend is confirmed also inside the [Co(g-bpe)2(a-bpe)(NO3)2]n chain where metals are alternatively connected by pair of gauche-bpe ligands (related by an inversion center) and single anti-bpe ligands [39]. It is worth of note the considerable short Co⋯Co distance of 9.293(5) Å measured in the cationic polymer {[Co(bpe)2(H2O)2]2+}n of Ref. [40], which has the smallest torsion angle about the bpe ethylene bridge (63.4°).

3.1.3. {[Co(bps)2(SeCN)2]·H2O}n (3)

The X-ray structural characterization of 3 confirms the formation of 4,4-bipyridylsulfide (bps) ligand from the 4,4-bipyridyldisulfide. It has almost isomorphous structure to 2 consisting of linear –[Co-(bps)2]n-polymeric chains elongated parallel to the b-axis (Fig. 6). Each octahedral Co(II) ion is located on a twofold axis and is bound at equatorial positions to four N atoms of bps ligands, with the metals spaced by 10.175(3) Å. The metal completes its octahedral coordination sphere with two NCSe anions, at axial positions. Although in this case the structural accuracy of compound 3 is lower with respect to 2, the Co–NCSe bond distance (2.091(13) Å) is confirmed to be shorter than the Co–N(bps) bond values of 2.148(13) and 2.166(12) Å (Table 2). The disordered of selenium atom (see Section 2) can be explained due to the possibility for the two sites (of ca. 85% and 15% occupancy) to give rise to C(py)–H hydrogen bonds. The C(5)–H⋯Se(1) distance (for the selenium species at higher occupancy) is 3.686(17) Å.

In both the polymeric chains of complexes 2 and 3 the NCSe ligands are linear [N–C–Se angle of 178.5(4)° and175.2(16)°, respectively] and are bound to the cobalt ion with a comparable Co–N–C angle of 158.0(4)° and 159.7(13)°, respectively. The chains are rather fairly connected by C–H–π interactions which lead to the formation of a 2D supramolecular network. The only suitable oriented interaction involves the C(2)–H fragment with ring N1/C6/C7/C8/C9/C10 (H⋯ring distance of 3.00 Å and C–H⋯centroid ring angle of 152°, Fig. 2S). In addition non-classic C(5)–H⋯Se(1) hydrogen bonds (C⋯Se distance of 3.686 Å) are detected to form a festoon architecture (Fig. 7) leading the network to a 3D arrangement. Complex 3 is isomorphous to that of isothyocyanate [42] where the Co⋯Co separation is only slightly longer.

3.2. IR spectroscopy

The main application of IR spectroscopy in pseudohalide containing compounds can provide valuable information about the linkage of these possible ambidentate ligands. In particular for N-bonded selenocyanate the most intense band, which appears at about 2080 cm−1, is related to the asymmetric stretching mode of the CN bond, while for Se-bonded selenocyanate, this band can be found around 2146 cm−1 [43]. The integrated intensity of the ν(CN) band is larger for the N-bonded than for the Se-bonded group [44]. A summary of the most important IR bands observed for complexes 1–3 together with their tentative assignments [45] is given in Table 1S. IR spectra shows intense band at 2074, 2064, and 2073 cm−1 for complexes 1, 2 and 3, respectively, which are associated with the νasym stretching mode of the CN bond of the N-coordinated selenocyanate (Figs. 3S–5S). The frequencies of the IR bands related to the bpe ligand in compound 2 are very close to those of the free ligand. [38].

3.3. Electronic spectroscopy and magnetic properties

The solid state reflectance spectra of all the complexes have been recorded (Fig. 8), and the data were collected in Table 4. The band in the region 9041–9680 cm−1 (ν¯, 9680 cm−1; λ, 1033 nm for 1; ν¯, 9041 cm−1; λ, 1106 nm for 2 and ν¯, 9478 cm−1; λ, 1055 cm−1 for 3) can be assigned to the spin allowed d–d transition 4T1g(F) → 4T2g(F). In addition a multiple band, observed in the visible region at 494 nm (20 242 cm−1), 520 nm (19 230 cm−1) and 485 nm (20 618 cm−1) for 1–3, respectively, can be assigned to another spin allowed d–d transition 4T1g(F) → 4T1g(P) admixed with spin forbidden transition to doublet state mainly derived from the free 2G and 2H ion terms [46]. A very weak band ν¯2 at 646 nm (15 479 cm−1), 654 nm (15 290 cm−1) and 642 nm (15 576 cm−1) for 1, 2 and 3 respectively, corresponds to the spin allowed 4T1g(F) → 4A2g(F) transition. From the Orgel diagram (ignoring configuration interaction) [46] for weak field octahedral cobalt(II) (d7) compounds it can be shown that ν¯1=8Dq, ν¯2=18Dq and ν¯3=6Dq+15B. This relation allowed to calculate the 10 Dq and B values for the three compounds. The former values (10 Dq) of 5799, 6249 and 6098 cm−1 for 1, 2 and 3, respectively, (Table 4) indicate that the ligand field strength for 2 is relatively higher with respect to those of 1 and 3. The corresponding calculated B values are 1117, 1032 and 1130 cm−1.

Room temperature magnetic moment values are 5.02, 5.04 and 5.00 BM for 1, 2 and 3, respectively. These results indicate the presence of high spin octahedral Co(II) (d7, t2g5eg2) centers in all complexes with three unpaired electrons. The observed magnetic moments are the combined result of spin magnetic moment of three unpaired electrons and of orbital contribution to the magnetic moment due to partly filled t2g set of orbitals.

3.4. Thermal analysis

Thermogravimetric (TG) analysis of complexes 1–3 has been conducted at a rate of 5 °C min−1 under N2 atmosphere up to 500 °C (Fig. 9). Upon heating complexes 1 and 2 do not show any mass change up to ∼166 and ∼204 °C, respectively, and on further heating the frameworks gradually collapse. On the other hand TG analysis of complex 3 reveals the release of water molecules at ∼50 °C and the process completes at ∼116 °C. Observed weight decrease of 2.46% corresponds to the loss of one lattice water molecule (theoretical weight loss 2.72%). The desolvated species collapses immediately upon further heating.

4. Conclusion

In summary we have presented here the synthesis, crystal structure, solid state electronic spectra and thermal study of three cobalt(II)–NCSe networks using different N,N donor ligands. Cobalt(II)–NCSe in combination with chelating phen (1) generates mononuclear compounds, extending to a 2D supramolecular layer through π−π interactions. On the other hand, combination with flexible bpe (2) and bps (3) generates 1D coordination polymeric chains, which extend their dimension via noncovalent interactions. It is interesting to note that for the synthesis of 3 we have used 4,4′-bipyridyldisulfide (bpds) as starting material but in the reaction medium bpds converted to bps, representing the first example of this transformation at room temperature. A comparison of available –Co(II)-(bpe)2–Co(II)–structures reveals that the inter metallic distance shows a linear relationship with the torsion angle about the bpe ethylene group. Solid state electronic spectral results and room temperature magnetic moment data corroborate the presence of high spin cobalt(II) centers in all complexes.

  • A new homobimetallic cobalt(II) complex based on the tetradentate 3,5-bis(2-pyridyl)-1H-1,2,4-triazole ligand: Synthesis, crystal structure, Hirshfeld analysis, spectroscopic characterization, magnetic properties and antimicrobial activities

    2020, Polyhedron

    A novel homobimetallic cobalt(II) complex, [Co2(bpt)2(H2O)4](NO3)2·2H2O (bpt = 3,5-bis(2-pyridyl)-1,2,4-triazolate), has been synthesized and characterized by single crystal X-ray diffraction, Hirshfeld surface analysis, as well as UV–Visible and FTIR techniques. The crystal structure determination shows that the dinuclear complex crystallizes in the triclinic crystal system with the P1- space group. The (N′,N1,N2,N″)2 double bridging coordination mode is realised with a distorted octahedral N4O2-coordinated cobalt centre. The structure cohesion is ensured by different intermolecular hydrogen bonds, π–π stacking interactions and intermolecular lone-pair···π interactions. The structure consists of [Co2(bpt)2]2+ dimeric units with a cobalt-cobalt distance of 4.170(2) Å. Magnetic measurements carried out on the homobimetallic complex indicate a relatively weak antiferromagnetic interaction between the two cobalt centres. This complex exhibited antimicrobial activity against several strains of the fungal phytopathogens Verticillium dahliae and Fusarium oxysporum fsp. melonis, as well as against the phytopathogenic bacteria Agrobacterium tumefaciens, suggesting its use in crop protection.

  • Crystal structures and magnetic properties of two Co(II) coordination polymers created via in situ ligand synthesis

    2020, Journal of Solid State Chemistry

    In this work, we present two new coordination polymers, [Co3(iBuOIPA)3(bps)(H2O)]n (1) and [Co2(iPrIPA)2(bps)]n (2) (bps ​= ​4,4′-bipyridyl sulfide, iBuOIPA ​= ​5-i-butoxyisophthalate, iPrIPA ​= ​5-i-propoxyisophthalate), by the hydrothermal reactions of Co(OAc)2 4H2O and 4,4′-bipyridyl disulfide (bpds) with iBuOIPA or iPrIPA, respectively.   • Cobalt(II) coordination complex with 2,5-bis(pyridine-2-yl)-1,3,4-thiadiazole and thiocyanate as co-ligand: Synthesis, crystal structure, Hirshfeld surface analysis, spectroscopic, thermal and magnetic properties

    2020, Journal of Molecular Structure

    The bis(2,5-di(pyridin-2-yl)-1,3,4-thiadiazole-κ2N,N′)-bis(thiocyanato-κ1N)cobalt(II) complex has been synthetized from the reaction of 2,5-bis(pyridin-2-yl)-1,3,4-thiadiazole (L) with metallic salt CoCl2.6H2O and thiocyanate ion (SCN−) as coligand in H2O/CH3CN at room temperature. The synthetized complex (CoL2(SCN)2) has been fully characterized by single crystal X-ray diffraction, Hirshfeld surface analysis, as well as UV–Visible, FTIR, Raman, and NMR spectroscopy. TGA analysis and magnetic measurements were also performed. CoL2(SCN)2 crystallizes in monoclinic symmetry and P 21/c space group with two independent cobalt crystallographic sites, where each cobalt atom is localized in a distorted octahedral environment CoN6, with the thiadiazole molecules (L) as bidentate ligands in equatorial sites and terminal SCN− ions in axial positions. The crystal cohesion is assured by intermolecular hydrogen bonding, C–H⋯π and π–π stacking; in addition to the N–Cu coordination bonds. This has been confirmed by the three dimensional Hirshfeld surface analysis and the two dimensional fingerprint plots that highlight the dominance of intermolecular interactions C⋯H/H⋯C and S⋯H/H⋯S. The thermal analysis of CoL2(SCN)2 reveals that this complex is thermally stable up to 200 °C. Variable-temperature magnetic susceptibility measurements on CoL2(SCN)2 complex indicated an antiferromagnetic exchange between the two non-equivalent cobalt(II) ions with a ferrimagnetic behaviour.
