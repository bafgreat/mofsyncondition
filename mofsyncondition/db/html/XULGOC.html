Ferromagnetic interactions and slow magnetic relaxation behaviors of two lanthanide coordination polymers bridged by 2,6-naphthalenedicarboxylate ligand

Abstract

Two lanthanide-based frameworks: {Ln(phen)(NDA)1.5(H2O)}n (Ln=Gd(1), NDA=2,6-naphthalenedicarboxylate anion, phen=1,10-phenanthroline), and {[Dy(phen)(NDA)1.5]·0.5H2NDA}n (2) were structurally and magnetically characterized. Compound 1 exhibits 2D layer structure, belonging to the triclinic system with space group P−1, while compound 2 features a 3D framework with space group P−1. The magnetic studies revealed that ferromagnetic coupling existed between adjacent lanthanide ions in 1 and 2, and frequency-dependence out-of-phase signals in the measurement of alternate-current susceptibilities were observed for 2, albeit without reaching the characteristic maxima above 2 K, implying slow magnetic relaxation behavior in 2. After the application of a dc field, good peak shapes of ac signal were obtained and got the energy barrier ΔE/kB=29 K and the pre-exponential factor τ0=4.47×10−7 s at 2000 Oe field; and when the dc field was in 5000 Oe, giving ΔE/kB=40 K and τ0=2.82×10−6.

Graphical abstract

Two novel lanthanide-based frameworks 1 and 2 were structurally and magnetically characterized. The results revealed that ferromagnetic coupling exists between adjacent lanthanide ions in 1 and 2, and 2 displayed slow magnetic relaxation behavior with the energy barrier of 29 K.

1. Introduction

In recent years, scientists pay great attentions to designing and synthesizing of low-dimensional magnets including single-molecule magnet (SMM) and single-chain magnet (SCM), because of their unique properties and potential applications in high-density data storage technologies and molecular spintronics [1]. It is well-known in SMM the energy barrier (U) for the reversal of the magnetization derives from a combination of two features, namely a high-spin ground state (S) and an easy axis type of magneto-anisotropy (negative zero-field splitting parameter, D). Therefore, Co2+ and Mn3+ with significant magneto-anisotropy were frequently employed to design and construct SMMs. More recently, significant attention has been directed towards the incorporation of 4f ions into SMMs, in both heterometallic 3d/4f and homometallic 4f compounds, since many lanthanide (Ln) ions possess large unquenched orbital angular momenta which impart significant anisotropy [2]. Up to now, some impressive lanthanide-based SMMs have been reported, including 3d–4f SMMs [3], [4], [5] and lanthanide-only SMMs, [6], [7], [8] and most of them show good magnetic properties. Nevertheless, most complexes with SMM behaviors focused on the discrete clusters, and the investigations of two- or three-dimensional frameworks featuring slow magnetic relaxation were less developed [9]. Furthermore, it was also hardly observed that ferromagnetic coupling and slow magnetic relaxation coexisted in lanthanide-only compounds [10].

In this contribution, two lanthanide-only frameworks: {(Ln(phen)(NDA)1.5(H2O))}n (Ln=Gd (1), NDA=2,6-naphthalenedicarboxylate ions, phen=1,10-phenanthroline) and {(Dy(phen)(NDA)1.5)·0.5H2NDA}n (2) were fabricated and structurally characterized. Magnetic measurements were performed on compounds 1–2, and the results revealed that ferromagnetic coupling existed between adjacent lanthanide ions in 1 and 2. Interestingly, further magnetic analysis reveals that 2 displays slow magnetic relaxation behavior.

2. Experiment section

2.1. Materials and physical measurements

All chemicals purchased were of reagent grade and used without further purification. Water used in the reactions is distilled water. The elemental analyses (C, H and N) were carried out a Perkin-Elmer elemental analyzer. Powder X-ray diffraction measurements were recorded on a D/Max-2500 X-ray diffractometer using CuKα radiation. Variable-temperature magnetic susceptibilities under 1000 Oe were performed on a Quantum Design MPMS-XL7 magnetometer, the alternating current (ac) magnetic measurements were performed on a PPMS-9 ACMS magnetometer. Diamagnetic corrections were made with Pascal׳s constants for all the constituent atoms.

2.2. Crystallographic studies

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction measurements of 1 and 2 were carried out at room temperature on a Rigaku SCXmini X-ray diffractometer equipped with graphitemonochromated MoKα radiation (λ=0.71073 Å). Lorentz polarization and absorption corrections were applied. The structures were solved by direct methods and refined by full-matrix least-squares techniques using the SHELXS-97 and SHELXL-97 programs. All the nonhydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic parameters while H atoms were placed in calculated positions and refined using a riding model. Crystallographic data for 1 and 2 were summarized in Table 1. Selected bond lengths and angles were summarized in Table S1.

2.3. Synthesis of {(Ln(phen)(NDA)1.5(H2O))}n (Ln=Gd(1) and {(Dy(phen)(NDA)1.5)·0.5H2NDA}n (2)

A mixture of H2NDA (0.0216 g, 0.1 mmol), 1,10-phen (0.0180 g, 0.1 mmol), LiOH·H2O (0.0126 g, 0.3 mmol), 0.1 mmol Ln(NO3)3·6H2O (Ln=Gd (1, 0.0451 g) and Dy (2, 0.0456 g) and water (10 mL) was sealed in a 25 mL stainless steel reactor with a Teflon-liner and heated at 180 °C for 72 h, then cooled to 100 °C in 24 h and kept at 100 °C for 24 h, then cooled to room temperature slowly. The crystals of 1 and 2 were obtained in 38% and 53% yields (based on H2NDA), respectively. Anal. Calcd for C30H19N2O7Gd (1): C, 53.24; H, 2.83; N, 4.14. 3. Results and discussion

3.1. Crystal structure of {Gd(phen)(NDA)1.5(H2O)}n (1)

Single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that compounds 1 crystallized in space group P−1, which is similar with the structure containing Eu3+ reported by us [11]. Two nitrogen atoms from a bidentate chelating phen ligand, five oxygen atoms from five NDA anions and one water molecule complete the coordinated sphere of Gd3+, as shown in Fig. 1a. The coordination geometry for the eight-coordinated Gd3+ can be described as a slightly distorted square-antiprism (Fig. 1a). The Gd–N bond distance is 2.572(8) and 2.625(8) Å, respectively, and the Gd–O distances are in the range of 2.306(6)–2.415(6) Å. Four of the five ligands coordinated to the metal center act as bridges to connect with another Gd3+, thus two Gd3+ are bridged by four carboxyl groups to form a paddle–wheel binuclear unit (Fig. 1b). The binuclear units are further connected by NDA ligands, forming a regular rhombus with the dimension of about 17×12 Å2 (defined by the distances of the binuclear unit). The rhombus as a unit is further assembled into a 2D lattice structure (Fig. 2).

3.2. Crystal structure of {[Dy(phen)(NDA)1.5]·0.5H2NDA}n (2)

The self-assembly of H2NDA with Dy(III) salt and phen gave a 3D coordination polymer 2. The X-ray structural analysis reveals that 2 belong to triclinic crystal system with space group P−1. The coordinated sphere of Dy3+ consists of two nitrogen atoms from a phen ligand and six oxygen atoms from four NDA anions, and the coordination geometry of Dy3+ can be described as a distorted bicapped trigonal prism in which O3 and O5 act as the two capping atoms (Fig. 3a). The Dy–N bond distance is 2.512(5) and 2.517(5) Å, respectively, and the Dy–O distances are in the range of 2.236(4)–2.455(4) Å. Two Dy3+ are bridged by two carboxyl groups from NDA ligands, forming a binuclear unit (Fig. 3b), and the binuclear unit is further connected into a 3D framework by NDA anions (Fig. 4).

3.3. Comparison of structures

In the construction of compounds 1 and 2, the same reaction condition got two different structures which may origin from the lanthanide contraction effect. Compound 1 feature a novel 2D lattice structure, and 2 is a 3D microporous framework, although all of them belong to triclinic crystal system with space group P−1. Both the metal centers of the two compounds are eight-coordinated with bidentate chelating phen ligands, however, the coordination geometries for 1 and 2 are different: for 1, the coordination geometry is a slightly distorted square-antiprism, while that for 2 is a distorted bicapped trigonal prism. The Ln3+ ions are connected into a binuclear unit by carboxyl groups in both 1 and 2. However, Gd3+ in 1 are bridged by four carboxyl groups to form a paddle–wheel binuclear unit, while in 2 a binuclear Dy2 unit only has two carboxyl group bridges.

3.4. Magnetic properties

Powder X-ray diffraction measurements were performed to confirm the purities of these compounds, as shown in Fig. S1 and S2. The magnetic susceptibilities of 1 and 2 were measured on the microcrystalline samples in the temperature range from 2 to 300 K and under 1000 Oe field, as shown in Fig. 5.

At room temperature, the χMT value for 1 is 15.40 cm3 K mol−1, which is close to the theoretical value of 15.76 cm3 K mol−1 expected for two magnetically insulated Gd3+ ions in the 8S7/2 ground state (g=2) [12]. As the temperature is lowered, the χMT values of 1 almost remain constant between 300 and 10 K, and then increase quickly to 16.97 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K, as shown in Fig. 5. The behaviors indicate the presence of weak ferromagnetic coupling between adjacent Gd3+ in the binuclear unit for 1. Based on the Hamiltonian Ĥ=−JŜAŜB with ŜA=ŜB=7/2, a corresponding equation as following was deduced, and the magnetic properties of 1 can be appropriately described by it [13].χM=2Ng2β2kT×eJ/kT+5e3J/kT+14e6J/kT+30e10J/kT+55e15J/kT+91e21J/kT+140e28J/kT1+3eJ/kT+5e3J/kT+7e6J/kT+9e10J/kT+11e15J/kT+13e21J/kT+15e28J/kT

Least-squares fitting of the experimental data leads to J=0.027 cm−1, g=1.99, and the agreement factor R, defined as R=∑(χobsd–χcalcd)2/∑(χobsd)2, is 1.76×10−2. The positive and small J value indicates very weak ferromagnetic coupling interaction between adjacent Gd3+. For lanthanide ions, the 4f electrons are well shielded by outer-shell electrons, compared with 3d electrons, the interaction of 4f electrons from adjacent lanthanide ions is rather weak. Additionally, three atoms carboxylic bridges between adjacent lanthanide ions are longer, and pass on a weaker magnetic coupling. As a result, the magnetic coupling between Gd3+ ions in this work is very weak, which falls into the normal range of J value in all reported examples for the magnetic properties of Gd3+ [14].

For 2, the χMT value is 28.59 cm3 K mol−1 at room temperature which is close to theoretical value of 28.34 cm3 K mol−1 expected for two magnetically insulated Dy3+ in the 6H15/2 ground state (g=4/3) for 2 [12]. Upon cooling, the χMT value decreases gradually from 28.59 cm3 K mol−1 at 300 K to 24.37 cm3 K mol−1 at 5 K, then increases quickly to 24.58 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K with further cooling. The χMT value decreases with lowing temperature at high temperature accounts for the competition between depopulated of Stark sublevels of single Dy3+ ion and ferromagnetic interaction in the binuclear unit, the former dominates the magnetic properties. The increase of χMT value in the low-temperature indicates weak ferromagnetic coupling between adjacent Dy3+ dominates the magnetic properties, which are also observed in some pervious Dy-compounds [10a].

The field dependence of the magnetization of compounds 1 and 2 were measured at 2 K in the field between 0 and 70 kOe. For 1, the magnetization increases gradually with increasing field, reaching a saturation value of 13.98 μB at 70 kOe. The experimental values of magnetization show a compromise between the calculated curves according to the Brillouin functions for (1) two isolated centers with S=7/2, g=2, and for (2) one system with S=7, g=2. The result confirms the weak ferromagnetic coupling between the two gadolinium ions (Fig. 6). For 2, the magnetization shows a gradual increase at low field, reaching a value of 10.5 μB at 70 kOe (Fig. 7). The saturation magnetization is not reached even at 70 kOe, which may indicates the presence of magnetic anisotropy and/or the lack of a well-defined ground state, suggesting the presence of low-lying excited states that might be populated when a field is applied.

To further investigate the dynamics of magnetization, the alternating current (ac) magnetic measurements were performed on 2 in the frequency range of 111−2311 Hz and under temperature between 2 and 20 K, and the frequency dependent out-of-phase signals were observed (Fig. 8 and Fig. S4), indicative of a SMM behavior, albeit without reaching the characteristic maxima above 2 K, and the case often exists in some Dy-based SMMs [15].

In SMM behaviour with a relaxation partially influenced by quantum effects especially for lanthanide ions, the application of a direct current (dc) field can remove the ground state degeneracy and thus decrease the possibility of quantum tunneling, inducing a slowing down of the magnetization relaxation [16]. To investigate the dynamic properties of the slow magnetic relaxation and obtain an effective energy barrier, ac measurements under the direct-current (dc) field of 2000 and 5000 Oe were applied. When the dc field was in 2000 Oe, the ac measurements give good peak shapes in both in- and out of-phase (Fig. 9 and Fig. S5). Fitting the peak temperatures, TP, and their corresponding frequencies to an Arrhenius law (τ=τ0 exp(ΔE/kBT)) can give the energy barrier ΔE/kB=29 K and the pre-exponential factor τ0=4.47×10−7. When the dc field was in 5000 Oe (Fig. S6), the ac measurements also give good peak shapes in both in- and out of- phase, and giving the energy barrier ΔE/kB=40 K and the pre-exponential factor τ0=2.82×10−6 (Fig. S7). The good peak shapes of ac signal after the application of a dc field indicate there is a quantum tunneling of the magnetization at zero field, and the quantum tunneling was quenched by a dc field. And the energy barrier at 2000 Oe is lower than the one at 5000 Oe, which may indicate the quantum tunneling effect are not totally quenched at 2000 Oe. To explore whether the magnetic behavior of 2 belongs to spin glass, the shift of the peak temperature (TP) of χ″ is measured by a parameter Φ=(ΔTp/Tp)/Δ(logf). The value of 0.26 at 2000 Oe and 0.33 at 5000 Oe were obtained, which fall in the range of a normal value for a superparamagnet. The result suggests that the magnetic behavior of 2 does not originate from the spin glass [17].

4. Conclusion

In summary, two lanthanide-only complexes have been constructed and structurally characterized. Systematic magnetic studies for them have been performed, exhibiting ferromagnetic coupling between adjacent lanthanide ions in 1 and 2. Magnetic studies reveal that compound 2 shows clear frequency dependence of the out-of-phase component of ac susceptibility and a slow magnetic relaxation behavior, which is rare in 3D coordination polymers. In the presence of external dc field (2000 and 5000 Oe), good peak shapes of the out-of-phase and in-phase signal were obtained because the dc field inhibits quantum tunneling of the magnetization.

  • Magnetic Relaxation of Lanthanide-Based Molecular Magnets

    2017, Handbook of Magnetic Materials

    Molecular magnets containing lanthanide ions have been receiving an increasing level of attention during the last years. The applicability of molecular magnets on foreseeable devices and new technologies, such as spintronic components, spin valves, or quantum bits, relies on the achievement of magnetic bistability with long relaxation times, such that the molecule allows the designed operation. The use of lanthanides as ingredients in molecular magnetism facilitates the achievement of high-energy barrier for magnetization reversal, a parameter related to bistability, and long relaxation times, thanks to their unquenched orbital momentum which enhances the magnetic anisotropy of the molecule.

    The understanding and tailoring of the diverse magnetic relaxation mechanisms through adequate chemical design are at the core of this multidisciplinary research field. Nowadays, it is mandatory to use up-to-date ab initio computational tools, providing a new input for the next round of chemical design, which is nowadays involving concepts such as new topologies and interaction dimensionality.

    In this chapter, we describe the theoretical basis as well as the experimental and computational tools used in the determination of the magnetic relaxation times and the identification of the relevant relaxation processes in lanthanide-based magnetic molecules. We review the recent progress on this class of compounds, from single ion molecules, to homo- and heteronuclear dimers and clusters (both containing only lanthanides and combined with 3d magnetic ions). Recent developments on systems with higher magnetic dimension in the interactions, such as chains, planes, and 3D compounds, are also reviewed. Finally, we focus on the recent progress on lanthanide-based molecules grafted to different substrates and how the relaxation processes are affected by the molecule–surface interaction.
