Introduction

Over the past a few decades, second-order nonlinear optical (NLO) materials have attracted significant attention due to their pivotal role in the domains of photoelectronics and photonics.1 In recent years, with the development of crystal engineering, various metal complexes exhibiting a second-order NLO effect have also been explored.2 The essential requirement for such materials is that the second-order NLO chromophores designed at the molecular level must be assembled into a noncentrosymmetric (NCS) structure. However, the stacking of these NLO-phore units in a bulk structure is challenging because they are often inclined to arrange in opposite directions, resulting in a centrosymmetric structure. To induce NCS structures, the introduction of an asymmetric bridging ligand has been found to be a convenient and efficient method.2a,3

To date, most of the asymmetric bridging ligands employed for the construction of second-harmonic-generation-(SHG)-active metal complexes only contain carboxylate and/or aza-aromatic moieties for ligation.2,4 Alternatively, we, as well as others, have also utilized phosphonate (R–PO3H2) for the above purpose.5 However, on seeking a more effective coordination group with which to create NCS structures, we found that phosphinate (R1R2PO2H) may be an especial candidate. Compared with the mono-organic-group-attached carboxylate or phosphonate moieties, the phosphinate moiety is further bonded to an additional organic group, which can be readily designed as an asymmetric ligand.

As an initial research work to address the above issue, we selected 2-carboxyethyl(phenyl)phosphinic acid (H2L) as a phosphinate ligand, due to its following characteristics: (a) the direct combination of the phosphinate group with a phenyl ring can be viewed as a dipolar or push–pull chromophore, which may be a source of the second-order NLO effect; (b) the connection of the phosphinate and carboxylate moieties by a flexible –CH2CH2– spacer leads to the formation of an asymmetric ligand, which can exhibit rich coordination modes via its four potential O donors; (c) the phosphinate-containing ligands have been less explored so far6,7 and, as far as we are aware, a metal phosphinate showing SHG activity has never been studied. Herein, we report a novel SHG-active lead(ii) phosphinate assembled from the above phosphinate ligand, which contains two enantiomers as a racemic mixture, namely, [Pb(HL)2] (Λ- and Δ-1).

Experimental

Materials and methods

2-Carboxyethyl(phenyl)phosphinic acid (H2L) was synthesized using a published procedure.8 All other chemicals were obtained from commercial sources and used without further purification. Elemental analyses were performed on a German Elementary Vario EL III instrument. The FT-IR spectra were recorded on a Nicolet Magna 750 FT-IR spectrometer using KBr pellets in the range 4000–400 cm−1. Thermogravimetric analysis was carried out on a Diamond TG/DTA 6000 unit at a heating rate of 15 °C min−1 under a nitrogen atmosphere. X-Ray powder diffraction (XRD) patterns (Cu-Kα) were collected on a Bruker Advance D8 θ–2θdiffractometer. The measurements of the powder frequency-doubling effects were carried out by means of the method of Kurtz and Perry.9 A 1064 nm radiation generated by a Q-switched Nd : YAG solid-state laser was used as the fundamental frequency light. The crystal sample was ground and sieved into several distinct particle size ranges (25–45, 45–53, 53–75, 75–105, 105–150, 150–210 and 210–300 μm). The samples with distinct particle size ranges were packed in round glass boxes with a uniform diameter of 8.0 mm and then they were exposed to laser radiation. Second harmonic radiation generated by the randomly oriented microcrystals was detected by a photomultiplier tube and displayed on an oscilloscope. Samples of KH2PO4 were prepared as reference materials in an identical fashion to assume the SHG effect.

Synthesis of 1

A mixture of PbCl2 (0.2 mmol, 0.0556 g) and H2L (0.4 mmol, 0.0857 g) in 12 mL H2O was sealed into a Parr Teflon-lined autoclave (23 mL) and heated at 150 °C for 3 days. The final pH value was about 2.0 and colorless plate-shaped crystals of 1 were obtained in a ca. 81% yield based on Pb (ESI, Fig. S1).† Its purity has also been confirmed by the XRD measurement (Fig. S2).† Anal. calcd for C18H20O8P2Pb1: C 34.13, H 3.18%. Crystal structure determination for complexes Λ- and Δ-1

Data collection for complexes Λ- and Δ-1 were performed on a Smart ApexII CCD diffractometer equipped with a graphite-monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). Intensity data for both crystals were collected using φ and ω scans at 296 K. The data sets were corrected for Lorentz and polarization factors, as well as for absorption, by the SADABS program.10 Both structures were solved by direct methods and refined by full-matrix least-squares fitting on F2 by SHELX-97.11 All non-hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic thermal parameters, whereas all hydrogen atoms were generated geometrically and refined isotropically. Results and discussion

Description of structure

Single crystal structural determination reveals that Λ-1 crystallizes in the chiral monoclinic space groupC2,‡ although the phosphinate ligand is achiral. The asymmetric unit contains half of a Pb2+ ion lying on a crystallographic two-fold axis and one HL− anion located in a general position. The Pb(1) ion is four-coordinate with four phosphonite O atoms from four HL− anions (Fig. 1). The geometry around it can be described as a distorted ψ-PbO4 trigonal bipyramid, with one site occupied by its lone pair electrons. The Pb–O distances [2.328(4)–2.512(4) Å] are comparable to those reported for other lead(ii) phosphinates.7

The unique HL−ligand is bidentate and it bridges two Pb2+ ions via its two phosphinate O atoms. The remaining non-coordinated carboxyl group of it is not deprotonated as the requirement for charge balance and also as indicated by a much longer C–O bond (Table 1). The fact that the phosphinate O atoms rather than the carboxylate O atoms in the HL−ligand are coordinated to the Pb2+ centers is attributed to the low pH value of the reaction system.

The interconnection of Pb2+ ions by the above HL−ligands leads to a two-fold symmetrical chiral chain along the c-axis, in which the adjacent Pb2+ ions are doubly bridged by pairs of the HL− anions (Fig. 2). Furthermore, these chiral 1D chains stack along the ab-plane by edge-to-face π⋯π interactions, with adjacent chains related only by 21 symmetry parallel to the b-axis (Fig. 3). Consequently, complex 1 crystallizes in the chiral C2 space group.

Second-order nonlinear optical property

In view of the fact that 1 crystallizes in a NCS space group (C2), we investigated the SHG measurement on sieved powdered samples of 1. Fig. 4 shows the relative SHG signal intensity vs. particle size for ground crystals of 1, which indicates that the complex was phase-matchable, according to the rule proposed by Kurtz and Perry.9 A comparison of the second-harmonic signal produced by samples of 1 and KDP (KH2PO4) in the same particle range 150–210 μm reveals that 1 exhibits a SHG efficiency ∼2 times that of KDP. The SHG efficiency can be mainly attributed to the uni-oriented arrangement of the chiral chains in 1, where the lone pair-containing Pb2+ ion and the HL−ligand are the two potential NLO-phore units.

Luminescent property

In addition, the solid-state luminescent spectrum of 1 was investigated at room temperature (Fig. S3†). The free 2-carboxyethyl(phenyl)phosphinate ligand shows only a fluorescent emission band at λmax = 293 nm under 225 nm excitation. Upon complexion of the HL−ligands with the Pb2+ ions, complex 1 displays a slight red-shift fluorescent emission band at λmax = 307 nm (λexcitation = 261 nm).

Thermogravimetric analysis

The TGA curve (Fig. S4†) shows that 1 is stable up to 230 °C, whereupon the decomposition of the organic ligand occurs, with a rapid weight loss of 29.7% and a subsequent overlapped weight loss of 20.5% in the range 316–540 °C.

Conclusions

In summary, by hydrothermal reaction of PbCl2 with 2-carboxyethyl(phenyl)phosphinate, a novel SHG-active lead(ii) phosphinate with the formula of [Pb(HL)2] was obtained. The complex features a two-fold symmetrical chiral chain, and the uni-oriented stacking of such 1D chains by edge-to-face π⋯π interactions results in its crystallization in the chiral C2 space group. The present work serves as an elegant example in which second-order NLO materials can be rationally designed based on an asymmetric phosphinate ligand. We are currently extending such a synthetic technique to explore other SHG-active metal phosphinates.
