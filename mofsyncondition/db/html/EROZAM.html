Neutral coordination frameworks with host–guest properties constructed by dicopper(II) units and 4,7-phenanthroline

Abstract

Reaction of [Cu2(O2CR)4] [R=Me(1), Ph(2)] with 4,7-phenanthroline produced neutral coordination frameworks {[Cu2(O2CR)4](C12N2H8)}n showing inclusion behavior toward solvent molecules; through variation of the equatorial ligands of the dimetal units, the channels size and host–guest properties of the coordination polymers can be controlled.

1. Introduction

Crystal engineering of coordination polymers is of considerable interest in the area of materials chemistry [1]. The coordination-driven assembly of these polymers has shown high promise in various potential applications, such as molecular recognition, catalysis, gas storage and molecular magnetization [2], [3]. However, most of the assembled structures are complicated and restricted by the choice of counter cations or anions, which can occupy part of the space within the framework [4]. To avoid such drawback, attention has been paid to construct neutral coordination frameworks with dimetal units [5]. Notably, constructing channels with various size and shapes in this type of polymers can be finely tuned by a suitable choice of both equatorial and axial ligands on the dimetal units. While much study is focused on changing the axial ligands, such as 4,4-bipyridine [3d], less attention has been paid to altering the equatorial ligands. The latter approach has many advantages: (1) as the equatorial ligands can be selective in the synthesis of dimetal units, it is very easy to design the size and the shapes of the channels; (2) it is a very efficient way to extend the size of the channels because the distance between adjacent layers can be increased by using lucky equatorial ligands. Herein, we report the preparation, crystal structures and properties of a novel coordination framework constructed with the neutral dimetal units [Cu2(O2CR)4] (R=Me, Ph) and the rigid aromatic ligand 4,7-phenanthroline (L) [6]. Furthermore, this ligand has been successfully employed in self-assembly of molecular crowns in our laboratory [7].

2. Experimental

4,7-Phenanthroline and Cu(CH3COO)2 were obtained commercially. Cu(C6H5COO)2 was prepared according to a published method [8]. Other materials were of the highest available purity and used without further treatment.

2.1. Synthesis

2.1.1. {[Cu2(O2CCH3)4](C12N2H8)}n (1)

A buffer layer of p-xylene (5 ml) was carefully layered over a solution of 4,7-phenanthroline (14.4 mg, 0.080 mmol) in p-xylene (10 ml), and a layer of Cu(O2CCH3)2 · H2O (16.0 mg, 0.08 mmol) in methanol (10 ml) was carefully placed on top of the buffer layer. Green, cube-like crystals were harvested after several days in ca. 60% yield based on Cu(O2CCH3)2 · H2O. Anal. Calc. for C20H20Cu2N2O8: C, 44.20, H, 3.71, N, 5.14. Found: C, 44.11, H, 3.62, N, 5.17%.

2.1.2. {[Cu2(O2CC6H5)4](C12N2H8)(C8H10)}n (2)

As above, a buffer of p-xylene (5 ml) was carefully layered over a solution of 4,7-phenanthroline (14.4 mg, 0.080 mmol) in p-xylene (10 ml), and a layer of Cu(O2CC6H5)2 (36.9 mg, 0.080 mmol) in methanol (10 ml) was carefully placed on top of the buffer layer. Green, cube-like crystals were harvested after several days in ca. 65% yield based on Cu(O2CC6H5)2. Anal. Calc. for C24H19CuNO4: C, 59.92, H, 4.78, N, 3.49. Found: C, 59.88, H, 4.71, N, 3.52%.

2.2. X-ray single-crystal analysis

Intensity data were collected on a Rigaku R-AXIS RAPID IP area detector diffractometer with graphite monochromated Mo Kα radiation (50 kV, 300 mA). Crystal parameters and refinement results for both compounds are summarized in Table 1. Empirical absorption corrections were employed. The structure was solved by shelxtl [9]. The final refinement was done by full-matrix least squares based on F2 with anisotropic thermal parameters for all non-hydrogen atoms, and the hydrogen atoms were generated geometrically.

3. Results and discussion

Fig. 1(a) shows the structural unit of 1. In the centrosymmetric dicopper units, each Cu (II) ion has four oxygens of different carboxylate groups in the equatorial positions, and an infinite chain along the [−1, 1, −1] axis is formed by linking consecutive dimetal units with bridging 4,7-phenanthroline ligands. Fig. 2(a) shows that along the chain each Cu2(OOCCH3)4 paddle wheel is rotated by ca. 45° with respect to its nearest neighbor.

The molecular packing of complex 1 is shown in Fig. 3(a). Normally, the arrangement of paddle wheels belonging to neighboring layers give rise to a channel system, and the size of the void is ≈8.0(5)×5.57(6) Å, which can accommodate some solvent molecules as guests [10]. However, the channels in the framework of 1 are void. Owing to the alternate rotation of the paddle wheels, the actual size of the channel is even smaller. Thus, the size of the channel is not large enough to accommodate solvent molecules as guests.

For controlling the actual size of the channel in the framework to absorb some guests, we substituted R=Me with R=Ph in order to enlarge the distance between two adjacent layers. The X-ray structure of 2 (Fig. 1(b)) shows that this structure also contains centrosymmetric dinuclear Cu units with the paddle-wheel cage unit. The bridging 4,7-phenanthroline ligand coordinates two neighbor dimetal units to form an extended structure along the c-axis. Along the chain, the conformations of successive [Cu2(O2CPh)4] units are alike, as distinct from the conformation of the chain in 1.

Fig. 3(b) shows the molecular packing of 2. The p-xylene molecules are trapped in the channels formed by the paddle wheels between adjacent layers. The size of the void is ≈14.22(6)×5.57(5) Å, which is sufficiently large to include the solvent molecules as guests.

However, not only p-xylene molecules but also methanol molecules exist in the mixture during preparation. It is very notable that the neutral coordination framework of 2 can selectively absorb p-xylene molecules as guests, which may involve favorable edge to face C–H interactions. The distance between the center of the p-xylene molecule and the center of the just adjacent phenylene ring to which the p-xylene molecule is about perpendicular is 5.026 Å. It suggests a strong edge-to-face interaction (the standard distance of T-shaped orientation is 4.99 Å and the energy of that that interaction is −2.3 kcal mol−1) [11].

In conclusion, we have succeeded in controlling the size of the pore and the host–guest properties in a neutral coordination framework by changing the substituents in a [Cu2(O2CR)4] unit. Further investigation will be extended to other multiple-bonded dimetal units [6] (e.g. Mo24+, Rh24+, Ru24+, etc.) with larger substituted aromatic R groups so as to constitute larger channels for the accommodation of various organic guests.

    2019, Journal of Molecular Structure

    Three novel coordination polymers containing Hg(II) and/or Cd(II) with 4,7-phenanthroline ligand (4,7-phen) have been synthesized and structurally characterized by single-crystal X-ray diffraction. Complexes [Hg(η2-4,7-phen)Br2]n (1), [Hg(η2-4,7-phen)I2]n (2) and [Cd(η2-4,7-phen)I2]n (3) were prepared from the reaction of one equivalent of 4,7-phenanthroline ligand with one equivalent of HgBr2 (1), HgI2 (2) and CdI2 (3), in methanol, respectively. Suitable crystals of title compounds were obtained for X-ray diffraction measurement by methanol diffusion into a DMSO solution. All compounds have linear polymeric structures which extend parallel to the c axis and contain Hg(II) and Cd(II) centers coordinated by two bridging 4,7-phenanthroline ligands, and two coordinated halogen molecules, completing the Hg(II) and Cd(II) coordination spheres, and resulting in a distorted tetrahedral geometry. Furthermore, π-π (in 1–3) and M-I⋯π (in 2 and 3) interactions play crucial role on stabilization of the crystal packing. The luminescence properties of the free ligand as well as the complexes 1–3 were investigated in solution. Thermal stabilities of these complexes were also studied by TGA/DTA analyses.

  • Crystal structures and spectroscopic behavior of monomeric, dimeric and polymeric copper(II) chloroacetate adducts with isonicotinamide, N-methylnicotinamide and N,N-diethylnicotinamide

    2007, Inorganica Chimica Acta

    Substituted pyridines provide structural rigidity and thus permit the metal coordination geometry to guide the direction of propagation of the hydrogen-bonded links between building blocks. In this paper we present the crystal structures and spectroscopic properties of monomeric, dimeric and polymeric copper(II) chloroacetates with isonicotinamide (INA), N-methylnicotinamide (MNA) and N,N-diethylnicotinamide (DENA). The molecular structure of [Cu(ClCH2CO2)2(INA)2]2 (1) consists of a rather interesting dinuclear molecule with copper atoms bridged by anti, anti-O,O′ bridging oxygens of two chloroacetate anions. Each copper atom is octahedrally coordinated thus forming a CuN2O4 core with two nitrogens, originating from two different isonicotinamide molecules, in trans positions. This complex is one of a very few examples of this rare type of structure in which both carboxylate oxygen anions are coordinated to two copper metal ions. The crystal structure of 1 revealed an infinite 1-D linear hydrogen-bonded chain formed by discrete molecules [Cu(ClCH2CO2)2(INA)2]2 connected by strong hydrogen bonds between two amide groups. This structure is the first example, where two pairs of amide groups are involved in hydrogen bonding connecting two molecules. The X-ray structure of the complex [Cu(CCl3CO2)2(INA)2]n (3) revealed a tetragonal bipyramidal environment about the copper(II) atom. This structure represents the first example of copper(II) complex, where isonicotinamide acts as a bridging ligand. Strong intramolecular hydrogen bonds, N–H⋯O, create two eight-membered metallocycle rings which stabilizes the molecular structure. The crystal structure of 3 consists of 2-D sheets of a metal–organic framework. The coordination environment of the copper(II) atom in [Cu(CCl3CO2)2(MNA)2(H2O)2] · 2H2O (6 · 2H2O) is an elongated tetragonal bipyramid. Strong intramolecular hydrogen bond interactions involving an axial coordinated water molecule and a carboxylic oxygen atom stabilize the molecular structure. The crystal structure of [Cu2(ClCH2CO2)4(DENA)]n (7) shows that the complex is an extended zigzag coordination chain of alternating binuclear paddle-wheel units of the bridging tetracarboxylate type Cu2(ClCH2CO2)4 and N,N-diethylnicotinamide molecules. This complex represents the first example of copper(II) carboxylates where N,N-diethylnicotinamide molecule acts as a bidentate bridging ligand connecting binuclear paddle-wheel units. The variation in DENA coordination in the polymeric chain can be described by the following formula: –[Cu2(ClCH2CO2)4]–(DENA-N,O)– [Cu2(ClCH2CO2)4]–(DENA-O,N)–. All complexes were characterized by electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) spectroscopy and IR spectroscopy. The present study shows that the pyridine-carboxyamides are very suitable molecules that can be employed as ligands in the construction of extended arrays of transition metal-containing molecules linked via hydrogen bonds.
