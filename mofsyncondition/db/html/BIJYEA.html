Abstract

Six coordination polymers, [Cu(μI)(L1)]n (1), [Cu(μI)(L2)]n (2), [Cu(μI)(L3)]n (3), [Cu(μI)(L4)]n (4), [Ag(L4)(NO3)]n (5) and {[Ag(L5)](ClO4)·4CH3CN)}n (6) based on coinage metals Cu(I)/Ag(I) and a set of five imino-pyridyl ligands (L1–L5) have been synthesized. The self-assembling behaviour of the metals and the ligands and photo-physical properties of these coordination polymers have been described. Cu2I2 secondary building unit (SBU) is a robust feature of the Cu and Iodine self-assembly. This SBU along with the ligands provides 1D coordination polymers in 1, 2 and 4 and 2D coordination polymer in 3. The Ag(I) complex 5 is a helical coordination polymer and 6 is a 2D coordination polymer with (4, 4′) net architecture with rhombic grids.

Coinage metals Cu and Ag have been used to synthesize coordination complexes with novel pyridine-azine ligands those show tendency towards helical self-assembly. In Cu complexes Cu2I2 unit act as robust self-assembled secondary building unit.

1. Introduction

Studies of coordination polymers have assumed a phenomenal growth during recent past. This class of complexes can be synthesised in a controlled manner by adopting crystal engineering methodology [1]. In designing higher dimensional coordination polymers one utilises the stereo-chemical as well as electronic information encoded in metal nodes and various N, O, S donor ligands as spacers to direct coordination assembly with particular architectures/topologies [2] and desired functional properties [3]. It is the functional property of the resultant coordination polymer that is of practical interest but the topology of the architecture in many cases is responsible for the functional behaviour [4]. In this respect semi-rigid imino pyridyl ligands have been utilised by different groups to design coordination assembly of various transition metals yielding materials with diverse topologies such as helicates, 1D and 2D coordination polymers [5]. The N donor ligands are easily customizable due to the relative ease with which the length at the central part of the ligand can be tailored and also various substituents can be easily incorporated. Also the relative positioning of N atoms both on the terminal pyridyl rings and in central part of the ligand can easily be modified as is needed in designing. In the shortest imine-based bis-pyridyl ligand, 2-pyridine ketazine (pyridylmethylketazine), L1 (Scheme 1) two pyridyl units are linked directly through the imine nitrogen atoms. This apparently simple ligand exhibits rich coordination modes toward metal ions and particularly suitable for preparing helicates [5]. The imino-pyridyl ligands L2 and L3 are excellent building blocks for the preparation of various architectures. The azino-pyridyl ligands L4 and L5 have been synthesized for the first time and in designing these ligands it has been kept in mind how the effect of substituents and the donor atoms’ position on the ligand frame would affect coordination mode towards metal ions. Though ligand L1 can chelate metal ions due to close proximity of pyridyl N atom and imine N atom on the ligand body, other ligands are expected to be incapable of chelation due to wider spacing of N atoms and steric congestion due to phenyl and methyl substituents on the ligand frame. It is expected that L2–L5 would generally act as spacer for metal ions by coordinating them through the pyridyl terminals. Herein we report six new coordination assemblies of coinage metals Cu(I) and Ag(I) with imino-pyridine ligands L1–L5. As precursor of Cu(I) ion we have chosen Iodide salts of Copper with a preconception that the self-assembling behaviour of Cu+ and I− can lead to the robust Cu2I2 – secondary building unit (SBU). In the design of coordination polymer the SBU approach [6] is very useful one, which generally utilised to design robust and strong coordination polymers having high thermal stabilities. Silver(I) ions have also been utilised in the design of organic–inorganic hybrid materials, since as soft acids they favour stable coordination to soft bases (like unsaturated nitrogen atom containing ligands and have been widely utilised in the context of azino-pyridine ligands), most of the cases producing helical assemblies [7]. Targeted synthesis of helical coordination polymer still remains a great challenge in the field of crystal engineering as helical topology is important for designing optical materials and is also associated with the molecules of life [8], [9]. In the present report we have studied the self-assembling behaviour of pyridine-ethylene base ligands L2 and L3 with Cu(I). Whereas L2 gives rise to a 1D coordination polymer L3 gives rise to a 2D coordination polymer effected by a change in the pyridine N atom position. This study also reveals that the azino-pyridyl based ligands L1, L4 and L5 (Scheme 1) are predisposed for helical architecture in combination with coinage metals. Whereas L1, L2, L3 and L4 were studied in combination with Cu(I) to see the effect of ligand flexibility and spacer length; L4 and L5 in combination with Ag(I) were studied to explore the effect of pyridyl N-atoms’ position and substituent effects.

2. Experimental

2.1. Materials and physical measurements

All the reagents were procured commercially from Aldrich. Microanalyses were performed by Perkin-Elmer 2400II elemental analyzer. The melting point was determined by an electro-thermal IA9000 series digital melting point apparatus and is uncorrected. IR spectra (KBr disc) were recorded on a Nicolet Magna-IR spectrophotometer (Series II), UV–Vis spectra on a Shimadzu UV-160A spectrophotometer, 1H and 13C NMR spectra by a Brucker DPX200 spectrometer. EI mass spectra on a VG Autospec M-250 instrument. Fluorescence spectra were recorded on a Perkin Elmer LS55 Luminescence Spectrometer.

Caution! Though while working with the perchlorate complex described here we have not met with any incident, care should be taken in handling them as perchlorates are potentially explosive. They should not be prepared and stored in large amounts.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Synthetic and structural aspects

3.1.1. Compound 1

The shortest imine-based bis-pyridyl ligand L1 is a 2-pyridine ketazine (pyridylmethylketazine) in which the two pyridyl units are linked directly through the imine nitrogen atoms. The rotational degrees of freedom about the central N–N bond are somewhat restricted due to presence –CH3 groups on both side of the N–N bond. L1 was first prepared by Stratton and Busch in 1958, but only in 1974 Sheldrick et al. [5e] first reported the X-ray crystal structure of its dinuclear triple helical cobalt complex. Its Ag(I) complex is a dinuclear double helix [5f] and Cu(I) complex is of trinuclear circular helical architecture [5g]. Michael D Ward et al. [5h] reported the structure of the mononuclear Re complex of this ligand in 2003. From the achiral diazine ligand, chiral coordination polymer of Mn is obtained [5i]. We have synthesised the dark red CuI complex, 1 of L1, by reacting the ligand with CuI in a 1:1 ligand to metal ratio. Complex 1 is stable in air in the solid state for 2–3 weeks. It is sparingly soluble in DMF, and the reddish brown solution of 1 gradually turns green in air.

The X-ray crystallographic studies reveal the presence of 1D coordination polymeric chains running along crystallographic a-axis (Fig. 1a) in 1. In the building up of the chain, Cu+ and I− ions self-assemble to form Cu2I2 secondary building units (SBUs). These SBUs are responsible for joining successive azino pyridyl ligands into a 1D chain having helical turn (Fig. S1). Each azino pyridine ligand L1 acts as tetradentate N-donor ligand. Two of the nitrogen atoms come from the azino group at the middle of the ligand and the other two are contributed from the two nitrogen atoms of the pyridine rings on either side. The ligand L1 binds to two Cu2I2 units along its length. Pyridine nitrogen atom on one side of the ligand and the azino nitrogen adjacent to it are donated to one of the Cu atoms of the Cu2I2 unit forming a 5-membered chelate ring. Each half of the ligand is attached to two different Cu atoms on consecutive Cu2I2 units and successive L1 ligand units are bound from opposite side of the chain leading to the extension of the chain along the a-axis.

The steric hindrance due to the presence of –CH3 groups on adjacent carbon atoms on either side of the azino group of the ligand forces the pyridine rings on both sides to twist by an angle ∼70°, leading to the induction of helicity into the chain. Cu atoms have tetrahedral coordination environment. Two N-atoms coming from two ligand units and two I atoms satisfy tetrahedral coordination. Cu-N distances are ∼2.1 Å and Cu–I distances are ∼2.58 A0 and ∼2.62 Å (see Table 2). The packing of helical chains are governed by dispersive forces and π-stacking interactions. The helical chains pack in columnar fashion, each column is encircled by six helical columns (Fig. 1b).

3.1.2. Compound 2

The ligand L2 has been synthesized by condensing ethylenediamine (en) with 4-pyridinecarboxaldehyde in anhydrous methanol in 1:2 M ratio. The Cu(I) complex of L2, [Cu(μI)(L2)]n (2), has been prepared by reacting the ligand with Cu2I2 in a 1:1 ligand to metal ratio. Complex 2 is stable in air in the solid state for 2–3 weeks. However, its stability in open solutions is solvent dependent; e.g. 2 reveals considerably higher stability in CH3CN (for approximately 4 h) than in DMF (1 h).

Single crystal X-ray structure reveals that compound 2 consists of 1D coordination polymer (Fig. 2a) having ladder like architecture that run along the crystallographic c-axis. Ligand L2 acts as two rails of the ladder and Cu2I2 secondary building unit (SBU) act as steps of the ladder (Fig. S2). Two Cu(I) cations and two I- ions form this rhombic loop like SBU through self-assembly in which two I- ions bridge two Cu(I) ions. Two sets of L2 ligands organise on both sides of these SBUs generate the coordination ladder. Each L2 ligand coordinates simultaneously to three SBUs along its length. Each Cu(I) ion coordinates singly to two SBUs through terminal pyridine N atoms and doubly through central ethylene N-atoms.

The SBU that binds the L2 ligand at its middle by double coordination on one side of the ladder through Cu1 atom, also bind to two different L2 ligands on the other side of the ladder by Cu2 atom. Thus in forming the two arms of the ladder the L2 ligands are shifted through half of their length. The L2 ligands assume arc shape in the complex and to comply with this conformation successive SBUs instead of aligning themselves in a line, are displaced alternately towards the left and right side of the ladder. This renders a zig–zag topology of the ladder instead of a straight one. Each Cu(I) ion has a tetrahedral coordination geometry. Two terminal pyridyl N atoms of L2 and two I− ions satisfy four tetrahedral coordination of Cu1 ion. On the other hand two ethylene N atoms from the middle of L2 and two I− ions satisfy four coordination of Cu2 ion. The Cu–I distances are 2.589(3) Å (Cu1–I1) and 2.639(3) Å (Cu2–I1). Cu2–N(pyridine) distance is 2.086(5) Å and Cu1–N(ethylene) distance is 2.126(5) Å (Table 2). The diagonal Cu1–Cu2 distance within a SBU is 2.687(4) Å. Successive coordination ladders run parallel to each other in the bc plane (Fig. 2b). These planes are stacked along the crystallographic a-axis.

3.1.3. Compound 3

The tetradentate relatively rigid Schiff-base ligand L3, 1, 2-bis(3′-pyridyl)ethylenediamine is a 1:2 condensate of ethylenediamine and pyridine 3-carboxaldehyde. It is selective and sensitive towards various metal ions. Its polymeric Cu(I) complex [Cu(μI)(L3)]n (3), is readily obtained by the reaction of CuI with the ligand L3 in a molar ratio of 1:1 under mild conditions. The bright yellow complex 3 is stable in the solid state, but not very stable in solution.

Single crystal X-ray structure of compound 3 reveals that a subtle engineering in the ligand framework through a shift in the pyridine N position leads to a drastic change in the coordination assembly – instead of a 1D coordination ladder in 2 now we have a 2D coordination polymer (Fig. 3a, Fig. S3) having (4, 4′) grid architecture in 3. While in L2 the pyridine N atoms are farthest apart, positioned at the two opposite ends, in L3, the N atoms are at the γ-position with respect to the imino C atom. The choices of L2 and L3 were based on the presupposition that while the terminal pyridyle N in L2 would possibly promote an infinite 1D coordination chain, a slight angular disposition of N in L3 is expected to generate a finite converging coordination polygon. Contrary to our expectation, L3 in association with the same secondary building unit Cu2I2 give rise to a 2D coordination polymer due to an interesting mode of self-assembly of these tectons. Similar to the case of compound 2, in 3 also Cu(I) cations and I− anions form Cu2I2 SBU through self-assembly. Interestingly the tetradentate L3 ligand aligns four different Cu2I2 SBUs along its entire length, two each on either side. In doing so, two terminal pyridine groups of L3 twist around the central ethylene –C–C– group in such a way that pyridyl N atoms are positioned on the opposite side of the ligand back-bone. The ‘Z’ shaped twisting (Fig. 3a) also brings imine N atoms opposite to each other. Thus along the length of L3 one pyridine N atom and the N atom adjacent to ethylene moity are oppositely disposed for metal coordination. It is interesting to note that due the free rotation around the central –C–C– group L3 assumes a linear shape instead of the arc shape. The arch shape that is adopted by L2 is also a possibility for L3 but the linear shape is preferred in this case as this allowed simultaneous coordination to four SBUs. The expectation of converging polygonal self-assembly by L3 was not completely belied as one can visualise two loop like assembly (R1 and R2, [Fig. 3a]) on the coordination polymer. One bigger loop (R1) and a smaller loop (R2) results due to opposite curvature of each half of L3. Oppositely curved parts of two different ligands in association with two SBUs give rise to a converging tetragon. Two SBUs and each half of two ligands form the four arms in the bigger loop R1. On the other hand in the smaller loop R2, SBUs just provide a connexion point, four arms are formed by two halves of two different L3 ligands. Here two pyridine groups form two opposite arms and the –C–N–Cu bonds form smaller arms of this rectangular tetragon (Fig. 3a).The coordination network constitute the (011¯) plane of the complex. Each Cu(I) ion in the SBU has a tetrahedral coordination geometry. One of the pyridyl N atoms of L3 and two I− ions satisfy four tetrahedral coordination of Cu1 ion. The Cu–I distance is 2.706(15) Å, Cu–N(pyridine) distance is 2.024(7) Å and Cu–N(ethylene) distance is 2.036(7) Å (Table 2). The diagonal Cu1–Cu2 distance within a SBU is 3.372(6) Å.Successive coordination planes are stacked side by side through π⋯π interaction between the pyridine rings and are stacked along the [011¯ ] direction (Fig. 3b).

3.1.4. Compound 4

Ligand L4 is a 1:2 condensate of benzil dihydrazone and pyridine 3-carboxaldehyde (Scheme 2). It has two azine fragments and two pyridyl nitrogen donor sites. These are readily accessible for coordination. The orange yellow polymeric Cu(I) complex of L4, [Cu(μI)(L4)]n (4) is prepared by reacting L4 with CuI in air in 1:1 ligand-to-metal ratio. The complex 4 is fairly stable at room temperature in air in solid state. It is sparingly soluble in acetonitrile.

The X-ray crystallographic studies of 4 reveals the presence of 1D coordination polymeric chains running along crystallographic c-axis. One of the building blocks of the chain (Fig. 4 and Fig. S4) is the Cu2I2 secondary building units (SBUs, formed due to self-assembly of Cu+ and I− ions). These SBUs are responsible for joining two sets of ligands L4 into a 1D chain, having ladder architecture. Cu2I2 – SBUs form the steps of the ladder whereas the ligands L4 form two side arms of the ladder. Two pyridine N-atoms of two adjacent ligands L4 satisfy two out of four tetrahedral coordination sites of one of the Cu(I) in a SBU whereas two iodine atoms satisfy the other two tetrahedral coordination of the other Cu(I) ion. Two iodine atoms simultaneously satisfy two coordination sites of the other Cu atom in the SBU. Two L4 ligand units on the other side of the SBU satisfy other two coordination of this Cu atom. The Cu–Cu separation within a SBU is 2.57 Å. The Cu-N distances fall in the range 2.07–2.09 Å and Cu–I distances fall within 2.63–2.65 Å (Table 2). The distance between successive SBUs along the chain is 11.94 Å.

3.1.5. Compound 5

Reaction of L4 with AgNO3 in methanol at room temperature in equi-molar proportion yields the yellow polymeric complex [Ag(L4)(NO3)]n (5). This polymeric Ag(I) complex, 5 is stable in air at room temperature for about 2–4 weeks; on prolonged exposure to air they turn black.

The X-ray crystallographic studies of 5 reveals that in the presence Ag(I) ions ligand L4 give rise to 1D helical coordination polymer (Fig. 5 and Fig. S5). The helical chains run along the crystallographic a-axis. Each Ag(I) ion is tri-coordinated. Two terminal pyridine N-atoms of the L4 ligand from two adjacent units satisfy two of the coordination sites of the Ag(I) ion and the rest is satisfied by an O-atom of a coordinated nitrate anion.

The Ag–Ag separation along the chain is 10.45 Å. The Ag–N distances fall in the range 2.20–2.21 Å and Ag⋯O distance is 2.65 Å (Table 2). The two terminal pyridine groups of the L4 ligand are disposed nearly perpendicular to each other due to the stereo structure of the ligand and this gives rise to helical chain in the presence of Ag(I) ion. Two oppositely running helical chains are interdigitated by π-stacking interactions and form a columnar assembly.

3.1.6. Compound 6

Ligand L5 is a double Schiff base of 4-acetyl pyridine and benzil dihydrazone. This is considerably a less flexible ligand. Upon reaction with AgClO4 in acetonitrile it gives a off white, moderately stable polymeric complex {[Ag(L5)](ClO4)·4CH3CN)}n (6) in good yield. Crystals of 6 rapidly lose solvent molecules when they are isolated from the reaction mixture. The methyl fragments of L5 impose some steric strain into the structure. In the observed structure of 6, the ligand molecules are located on crystallographic C2 axes. The inner imino N-atoms are oriented in different directions which make them inert to metal ion coordination. As a result, only on the peripheral pyridyl nitrogen atoms coordinates to the silver ions, a 2D Ag coordination polymer with rhombic grid architecture is obtained.

X-ray crystallographic studies of 6 reveals a 2D coordination polymers where tetrahedral Ag(I) ions act as nodes and ligands L5 act as spacers (Fig. 6 and Fig. S6). Terminal pyridine N-atoms of four L5 ligands satisfy the tetrahedral coordination of the Ag(I) ion. Uncoordinated perchlorate anion plays the charge balancing as well as void filling role. Ag–N bond distances fall in the range 2.25–2.40 Å (Table 2). Tetrahedral Ag(I) ions act as nodes of a (4, 4′) grid network and the L5 ligands play the role of spacers. Additional steric repulsion generated due the presence of –CH3 groups in the L5 ligand is possibly responsible for the near 180° twist of two terminal pyridine atoms on the L5 ligand giving rise to the ligands linear spacing action. Successive 2D polymeric sheets are packed by π–π interaction and the void spaces are also occupied by four acetonitrile molecules.

3.2. IR Spectroscopy

The complexation process of the ligands has been monitored by IR spectroscopy. The IR spectrum of the ligands L1, L2, L4 and L5 show characteristic bands at 1561 and 1614 cm−1 (for L1), 1576, 1648 cm−1 (for L2), 1568 and 1610 cm−1 (for L4) and 1562 and 1600 cm−1 (for L5) which we assign to the pyridine ring and imine CN stretching frequencies respectively. In the IR spectrum of the Cu(I) and Ag(I) complexes these bands are shifted to the higher energies, (1592 and 1620 cm−1 for 1, 1606 and 1626 cm−1 for 2, 1592 and 1618 cm−1 for 4, 1599 and 1618 cm−1 for 5 and 1574 and 1608 cm−1 for 6). The stretching frequency of the coordinated NO3- ion in 5 appears at 1382 cm−1. The typical strong bands due to the stretching vibrations of the non-coordinated ClO4- ion in 6 appear at expected region; 1090 and 623 cm−1.

3.3. UV–Vis spectroscopy

The absorption spectra of the ligands L1, L2, L3, L4 and L5 in methanol show intra-ligand charge transfer bands at 335 and 270 nm (for L1); 275 and 230 nm (for L2), 271 and 235 nm (for L3), 334 and 272 nm (for L4) and 331 and 274 nm (for L5) respectively with high molar extinction coefficient values. These bands are little bit shifted in their metal complexes. The CuI complex, 1 displays additional unstructured bands at 375 nm, which is supposed to be MLCT in origin. An unstructured band at 532 nm of complex 2, might be responsible for the red colour of the complex. All data also demonstrate the coordination of the metal ions to the ligands.

3.4. Photoluminescent properties

Organic–inorganic hybrid coordination polymers have been investigated for fluorescence properties and for potential applications as luminescence materials, light-emitting diodes (LEDs) and sensors [16]. 3.5. Comparative discussion of the self-assembling behaviour of the ligands toward Cu(I) and Ag(I) ions

Ligand L2 and L3 are ethylene based whereas L1, L4 and L5 are azine based ligands. The ethylene group itself has flexibility and in L2 and L3 this flexibility has been utilised. While in L2 due to bow shaped nature of the ligand and disposition of the pyridine N atoms at the symmetrically opposite postions a ladder like topology of the coordination chain appeared. On the other hand in L3 the ‘Z’ shaped conformation of the central ethylene group along with the pyridine N atom led to end wise chelating functionality of the ligand and this gives rise to a 2D coordination polymer instead of 1D zig-zag chain. In L2 the chelating functionality is implemented at the central part of the ligand but in L3 chelating functionality is implemented at both the ends of the ligand unit of L3 and consequently the topology of the coordination network could be controlled.

Ligand L1 is the shortest iminie based rigid pyridine ligand. Though it is length wise rigid but terminal pyridyl rings can easily rotate around the central N–N bond. These rotational degrees of freedom allow L1 to chelate two metal centres simultaneously either in cis or in trans fashion. The chelation is through one imino nitrogen atom and the nearby pyridyl nitrogen atom. The optimised geometry of the ligand calculated using the GAMESS package [18] reveals that in the lowest energy conformation of the ligand, terminal pyridyl N atoms are trans to each other. Ligand L1 and it’s close analogue L2 in which no methyl substitution is present have been studied by a Quing-Jin’s group [5b] and Michael J. Hannon’s group [5g] which show that these ligands are particularly suitable for construction of self-assembled triple helicates in which two metal nodes are wound by a set of three ligands (L1 or L2), each chelating two metal nodes through the pyridyl N and adjacent imino N atoms. The helical twist is allowed as the ligand can rotate somewhat freely around the cental N–N bond. To the best of our knowledge so far no infinite helical coordination polymer based on ligand L1 is reported in the literature and also the finite helicates of L1 is based on Ag(I) ion only. Complex 1 is the first infinite helix based on L1 in which instead of single metal nodes we have used Cu2I2 secondary building units those are wound in a helical fashion by L1.

Two new ligands L4 and L5 were designed to study the effect of widening the separation between the terminal pyridyl groups by inserting a benzil dihydrazone moiety instead of just the azine group in L1. Interestingly the ligands no longer remain linear, but posses a sharp near to 90° bend around the central C–C bond (Fig. 8). Terminal pyridyl groups still posses the rotational degrees of freedom with N–N bond as rotation axis. In L4 the pyridyl N atom is at the 3 position and in L5 the pyridyl N atom is at the 4 position on the pyridyl ring. In L5 a –CH3 substitution is present on the carbon atom connecting the imino group with the pyridyl group. In complex 4, ligand L4 doubly bridges to successive Cu2I2 units giving a one dimensional coordination polymer with ladder architecture. The angular shape of L4 as well as the presence of N atoms at the 3-position facilitates this assembly. Changing the metal nodes to Ag(I) in complex 5 gives rise to infinite helical architecture of L4. Here successive Ag(I) nodes are helically wound around a common axis facilitated by the angular nature as well as 3-position pyridyl N atoms of L4. Presence of N-atoms at the 3-position allows tri-coordination of Ag(I). While two of the coordination sites of Ag(I) is occupied by pyridyl N-atoms the third coordination site is occupied by O atom of a nitrate anion. The 144.5° (N–Ag–N) angle around Ag(I) ion facilitates the continuing helical turn of the polymer chain. In complex 6, L5 along with Ag(I) produces a (4,4′) grid network. Here Ag(I) is tetra coordinated and acts as a four connecting node. The N-atom at farthest position facilitates this networking and L5 ligand acts as end to end connector.

4. Conclusions

In this study we report the self-assembling behaviour of ethylene based and azine based pyridine ligands. In case of ethylene based ligands the torsional degrees of freedom around the central ethylene moiety in L2 and L3 was utilised by positioning the pyridine N atoms at suitable positions to control the topology of the coordination polymers. Using L2 a 1D zig–zag ladder topology was induced where as L3 induced a 2D coordination polymer. We report two new azino pyridine ligands (L4 and L5) along with the shortest azino pyridine ligand (L1) towards Cu(I) and Ag(I) ions. In using Cu(I) we have utilised the self-assembled secondary building unit (Cu2I2) of Cu(I) and I−. This secondary building unit gives rise to infinite helix of L1 for the first time. L4 and L5 are 90° bent connectors for metal nodes. L4 produces 1D coordination polymer along with Cu2I2 units having ladder architecture but an infinite helix with tri coordinated Ag(I) ions. Tetra coordinated Ag(I) with L5 produces a rhombic grid (4, 4′) net architecture. Herein we have demonstrated that both convergent and divergent disposition of the N-donor sites of pyridyl fragments of the ligands would promote a facile construction of 1D and 2D polymeric material with the coinage metal ion auxiliaries. While the coordination bonds and chelating rings provide the major cohesive force in the complexes, π–π stacking interactions also play an important role in packing of the coordination polymers.
