Structure and photocatalytic property of a new Cu(II) based framework with jsm topology

Abstract

A new coordination polymer [Cu(mip)(bpy)0.5]n (1) was obtained through reaction of 5-methylisophthalic acid (H2mip) and 4,4′-bipyridine (bpy) with copper nitrate under hydrothermal condition. X-ray single crystal diffraction analysis reveals that complex 1 exhibits a 3D network with jsm topology. Photocatalytic activity of the complex has also been characterized. Notably, complex 1 represents the rare example of visible-light-driven photocatalyst based on coordination polymer and shows good degradation ability over methylene blue (MB).

Coordination polymers (CPs) are a class of hybrid crystalline materials synthesized from reactions between inorganic cations and organic linkers [1], [2], [3], [4], [5]. CPs with desired structures and properties can be obtained through ingenious selection and skilled assemble of the organic and inorganic components under certain condition [6], [7], [8]. This tunable feature makes CPs attractive candidates for application in many fields as chemical sensor, gas storage, catalyst and so on [9], [10], [11], [12], [13], [14], [15], [16]. Recently, a particularly interesting and challenging area in this field is the synthesis of photocatalytic coordination polymers. Application of CPs as catalysts for the decomposition of dye contaminants under irradiation has recently attained great attention due to the high thermal and chemical stabilities, facile synthesis and tunable nature. On the other hand, various kinds of charge transfer between organic linker and metal center enable CPs to exhibit adsorption band in the visible region, indicating that they may undergo photochemical process upon visible light irradiation and be good candidate for photocatalytic material [17], [18], [19]. However, until recently, limited works focused on the development of such new CPs as photocatalytic materials have been reported. As an extension of our studies on the synthesis of functional CPs based on mixed multicarboxylate and pyridyl-functioned ligand system [20], [21], [22], we now try to synthesize CPs with interesting photocatalytic ability based on this mixed ligand system. Copper nitrate was chosen as the source of the metal center for the good redox ability of the Cu(II) ion in CPs. A novel complex [Cu(mip)(bpy)0.5]n (1) was synthesized through reaction of Cu(NO3)2·2.5H2O, H2mip and bpy ligand under hydrothermal condition. Complex 1 was further characterized by IR spectrum, PXRD, TG (see the Supporting information). The photocatalytic activity of complex 1 was also investigated by degradation of methylene blue under visible light.

Single crystal X-ray diffraction experiments revealed that 1 holds a three-dimensional (3D) structure with jsm topology. The asymmetric unit of 1 contains one Cu(II) ion, one mip2 − ligand and half a bpy ligand. As shown in Fig. 1a, the Cu(II) center is coordinated by four oxygen atoms from four carboxyl groups and a nitrogen atom from pyridyl group. Each pair of Cu(II) ions is bridged by four carboxylate groups (Cu2(COO)4) to generate a well-known paddle-wheel SBU (Fig. 1b). Ten such Cu2(COO)4 SBUs are then linked by twelve mip2 − ligands forming a diamond cage as shown in Fig. 2a. These diamond cages are connected to each other to form a dia net with the Cu2(COO)4 SBUs simplified as nodes and the mip2 − ligands as linkers as shown in Fig. 2b. Since the central Cu(II) is five-coordinated, every Cu2(COO)4 SBU is then coordinated by two bpy molecules in the axial positions as shown in Fig. 2c. In whole, if the paddle-wheel SBUs were simplified as nodes and the mip2− and bpy ligands were treated as linkers, complex 1 goes to a six-connected 3D structure with jsm topology as shown in Fig. 2d and the point symbol of the structure is {510·64·7}.

The experimental and computer-simulated powder X-ray diffraction patterns of complex 1 are given in Fig. S2. The overall agreement in peak positions indicates the phase purity of the complex, and the differences in relative intensity could be due to preferential orientation of the samples. Thermal analysis was carried out from 30 °C to 800 °C for 1 under nitrogen atmosphere with heat rate of 10 °C min− 1. As shown in Fig. S3, there is only one step of weight loss in the temperature region of 280–450 °C which corresponds to decomposition of the whole 3D framework (observed: 75.69%; expected 75.13%). This result indicates that the complex's stability is up to about 300 °C. The remaining residue is assumed to be CuO (observed: 24.31%; expected 24.87%).

The UV–vis absorption spectrum of complex 1 was investigated in a solid state. As depicted in Fig. S4, complex 1 consists of absorption bands both in the UV and visible regions. The broad absorption band in the region of 200–300 nm could be attributed to π → π* transition of the ligand. As for the broad absorption band in the visible region from about 530 to 730 nm, it probably originates from the d–d spin-allowed transition of the central Cu(II) ions [23]. Methylene blue is widely used in the chemical industry and causes serious water contamination. As complex 1 shows good absorption ability in visible region, it was used as a catalyst for degradation of MB under visible light irradiation. In the typical experiment, powdered complex 1 (50 mg) was added into 50 mL aqueous MB solution (10 mg·L− 1). Then the pH value was adjusted to 5 and the mixture was stirred in dark for 1 h to establish an adsorption–desorption equilibrium of MB on the surface of complex 1. Then the reaction was illuminated by a tungsten filament lamp while stirring and 1 mL aliquot was withdrawn every half an hour which was then centrifuged and tracked by a UV–vis spectrophotometer. For comparison, the photodegradation process of MB without complex 1 has also been carried out under the same conditions. As shown in Fig. S5, about 18% of the MB decomposes under visible light irradiation without complex 1 in 3 h. However, when complex 1 was employed in the degradation process, it can be found that decomposition percentage of MB increases to about 90% within 3 h (Fig. 3, Fig. 4). The result indicates that complex 1 has a good photocatalytic activity for the decomposition of MB under visible light irradiation. Previous reports have proposed the possible reaction mechanism for photodegradation of CPs on dyes [24], [25], [26]. Irradiation of complex 1 under visible light causes excitation of electrons from valance band to the conduction band of the Cu(II) ions, and thus electron holes are generated. These holes can accept electrons and induce water molecules to generate hydroxyl radicals, which exhibit high oxidation ability to decompose the organic dyes.

In conclusion, the synthesis of a novel coordination polymer has been accomplished by employing hydrothermal method. X-ray single crystal diffraction analysis reveals that complex 1 holds a novel 3D structure with jsm topology. Remarkably, this coordination polymer shows good photocatalytic activity under visible light irradiation for the degradation of methylene blue. This work may provide important information for the synthesis of new types of visible-light-driven active photocatalysts for the degradation of organic dye pollutants.
