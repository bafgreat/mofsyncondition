Introduction

Coordination polymers constructed from organic linkers and inorganic nodes have seen rapid development in the field of supramolecular catalysis, attributed to their features such as easy isolation and potentially high atom economy.1–4 The intrinsic characteristics of coordination polymers, for example low solubility in common solvents, highly ordered structures and easy functionalization through design and modification, make them perfect candidates for heterogeneous catalysis. However, their applications in photocatalytic organic transformations have been scarce.5–9

In recent years, visible-light driven organic transformations have attracted intense research interest owing to the natural abundance of light which is renewable and sustainable.10–14 Metal (RuII, IrIII, ReI and OsII) polypyridine complexes15–17 and organic dyes18,19 represent two typical categories of photocatalysts for visible-light driven photoredox processes, and have been extensively studied in homogeneous synthetic organic systems. Heterogeneous photocatalysts sensitizing visible light, including MOFs20,21 and semiconductive metal oxides,22 have also been investigated for their unique feature of easy isolation and the potential of recycled usage. However, most of these photocatalysts suffer from disadvantages in aspects such as difficulties in efficient recovering and significant loss of activity during recycling. It is still highly desirable to develop efficient heterogeneous photocatalysts with good recyclability in terms of green chemistry.

In seeking photocatalysts that are recyclable, our attention has been focused in the development of coordination polymers with functionalized ligands, which might provide the possibility of heterogeneous photocatalysis. Carboxylate groups represent a very important category of binding sites in organic ligands for coordination polymers with desired structures and thus become an inevitable choice of our ligand design.23 The idea of ligand functionalization was inspired by the important role of quinones in biological systems as photosensitizers24,25 and their rich redox properties involving processes such as single electron transfer and proton-coupled electron transfer.26,27 Therefore, ligand 9,10-anthraquinone-1,4-dicarboxylic acid (H2AQDC) has been designed and synthesized. The expectation is that the anthraquinone portion behaves as a photosensitizer to harvest light and carboxylate groups bind metal ions to form coordination polymers. Subsequently, a series of transition metal complexes based on this ligand have been synthesized as potential photocatalysts.

To investigate the photocatalytic properties of the metal complexes thus synthesized, the reaction of the photocatalytic oxidation of diarylethyne analogues into 1,2-diketones has been selected as the substrate reaction. 1,2-Diketones are important organic molecules for synthesizing heterocyclic compounds with biological activity, which have attracted intense synthetic interest using catalysts such as metal complexes involving Pd,28,29 Ru,30,31 Au,32 and Cu,33 and organic dye eosin Y as well.34 But the same issue exists that catalysts are not recyclable, despite a large amount of effort in this area. Our investigation has demonstrated that both the H2AQDC ligand and its transition metal complexes are able to catalyze the air-oxidation reactions of alkynes to produce 1,2-diketones under the irradiation of visible light, in which the Ni(ii) complex exhibits the best catalytic activity. This catalyst can be readily recovered without the loss of mass and change in the structure, and remarkably, it can be efficiently recycled to sustain at least five catalytic cycles without the loss of activity.

Experimental section

General materials and measurements

All reagents and solvents were purchased from commercial sources and used without further purification. The ligand H2AQDC was synthesized using a modified procedure as reported.35 Elemental analyses were performed using a PE-240C elemental analyzer. Thermal analyses were carried out on a Mettler-Toledo TGA/DSC STARe system in the temperature range of 28 to 800 °C with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1, under a nitrogen flow. Infrared spectra were recorded on a VECTOR 22 spectrometer with pressed KBr pellets in the range of 4000 to 400 cm−1. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) was performed using a MiniFlex 600 X-ray powder diffractometer equipped with a Cu sealed tube (λ = 1.54178 Å) at 40 kV and 40 mA. 1H and 13C NMR spectra were obtained in the CDCl3 solvent on a Bruker 400 MHz spectrometer.

Synthesis of metal complexes

{Co(AQDC)(H2O)3·2H2O}n (Co-AQDC)

A mixture of Co(OAc)2·4H2O (12.4 mg, 0.05 mmol) and H2AQDC (14.8 mg, 0.05 mmol) was dissolved in 5.0 mL water. After stirring, the resulting solution was allowed to evaporate at room temperature for 15 days to afford pink needle-like crystals, which were collected by filtration and washed with water and acetone (15.1 mg, yield: 68%). Anal. calcd for Co-AQDC (C16H16O11Co): C, 43.36; H 3.64. Found: C, 43.38; H, 3.69. IR (KBr cm−1): 1672 (s, CO), 1573 (vs, carboxylate CO), 1265 (s, C–O).

{Ni(AQDC)(H2O)3·2H2O}n (Ni-AQDC)

This complex was obtained by the same procedure as that for Co-AQDC except that Ni(OAc)2·4H2O was used instead of Co(OAc)2·4H2O as a starting material. Green crystals were obtained by filtration and washed with water and acetone (11.7 mg, yield: 53%). Anal. calcd for Ni-AQDC (C16H16O11Ni): C 43.38, H 3.64; found: C 43.37, H 3.97. IR (KBr, cm−1): 1670 (s, CO), 1591 (s, carboxylate CO), 1265 (s, C–O).

{[Cu(AQDC)(H2O)3][Cu(AQDC)(H2O)2(DMF)]·(H2O)4}n (Cu-AQDC)

To a 3.0 mL DMF solution of CuCl2·2H2O (17.1 mg, 0.1 mmol) was added H2AQDC (29.6 mg, 0.1 mmol) in 3.0 mL DMF. Filtration followed by ether diffusion afforded green crystals (34.7 mg, yield: 64%). Anal. calcd for Cu-AQDC (C35H37O22NCu2): C 44.22, H 3.92. Found: C 44.18, H 3.89. IR (KBr, cm−1): 1672 (s, CO), 1585 (s, carboxylate CO), 1265 (s, C–O).

{Zn1.5(AQDC)(OH)(H2O)2·H2O}n (Zn-AQDC)

Aqueous solution of Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (11.9 mg, 0.05 mmol), H2AQDC (14.8 mg, 0.05 mmol) and NaOH (4.0 mg, 0.10 mmol) were mixed together, followed by acetone diffusion to give orange crystals (17.4 mg, yield: 75%). Anal. calcd for Zn-AQDC (C16H13O10Zn1.5): C 41.48, H 2.83. Found: C 41.43, H 2.83. IR (KBr, cm−1): 1670 (s, CO), 1583 (s, carboxylate CO), 1265 (s, C–O).

{Ag2(AQDC)(CH3OH)}n (Ag-AQDC)

The ligand H2AQDC (14.8 mg, 0.05 mmol) was dissolved in 3.0 mL methanol and this solution was carefully layered over 2.0 mL aqueous solution of AgClO4 (20.7 mg, 0.1 mmol), with a 5.0 mL buffer layer (1 : 1 water and methanol). Light yellow needle-like crystals were obtained after two days (26.1 mg, yield: 55%). Anal. calcd for Ag-AQDC (C17H10O7Ag2): C 37.67, H 1.86. Found: C 37.70, H 1.86. IR (KBr, cm−1): 1664 (s, CO), 1591 (s, carboxylate CO), 1269 (s, C–O).

Typical procedure for the photocatalytic experiment

A mixture of photocatalyst (0.02 mmol), 1,2-diarylethyne (0.2 mmol) and 4-chlorobenzenethiol (57.8 mg, 0.4 mmol) was dissolved in 2.0 mL MeCN. The reaction mixture was stirred at room temperature under the irradiation of blue LEDs for 72 h in ambient air. Filtration was performed after the reaction is complete. The insoluble photocatalyst thus isolated is ready for recycled use after dichloromethane washing and vacuum drying. The filtrate containing the final product was evaporated to dryness and subjected to column chromatography (silica gel, petroleum ether/ethyl acetate) to afford a pure 1,2-diketone product. The yields reported are based on the isolated pure product and characterization was performed by 1H and 13C NMR spectroscopy (see the ESI† for spectral and product details).

Single crystal X-ray crystallography

Single crystal X-ray diffraction data of all five complexes were collected at 293 K using a Bruker APEX II CCD diffractometer operating at 50 kV and 30 mA using Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). Data were integrated using SAINT and scaled with either a numerical or multi-scan absorption correction using SADABS.36 All structures were solved by direct methods or Patterson maps37 and refined by full-matrix least squares on F2 using the SHELXL-201438 and OLEX239 programs. All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. Non-water hydrogen atoms were added at calculated positions and refined using a riding model. Hydrogen atoms of water molecules were located by difference Fourier maps and refined using a riding model at a restrained distance of 0.86 Å. Results and discussion

Design and synthesis

Quinones are important photosensitive compounds in biological systems.24,25 Natural benzoquinones and anthraquinones participate in Type I and Type II photosensitization to produce singlet oxygen and superoxide anions for photo-oxidation reactions.40 The nature and position of substituents determine the photochemical properties of anthraquinone derivatives, by affecting the electronic states and electron transitions. For example, 2-Cl-anthraquinone has shown exceptional reactivity over its analogs as a photosensitizer in catalyzing certain organic transformations under the irradiation of a fluorescent lamp.41–44 The anthraquinone moiety has thus been chosen as the functional group of our designed ligand for developing the metal–organic coordination polymer with photosensitizing activity.

On the other hand, the ligand with carboxylate groups has proven to be very successful in constructing coordination polymers such as metal–organic frameworks.19 So the combination of carboxylate groups with the anthraquinone moiety might constitute a perfect candidate for our designed ligand. Regarding the positions of substituting carboxylate groups, a preferable arrangement is para-positioned for the polymeric coordination. Furthermore, a close arrangement of the carboxylate and carbonyl groups will lead to a significant steric hindrance between them, so that carboxylate groups will hold angles to the phenyl rings. As a result, the conjugation between the anthraquinone plane and carboxylate groups will be broken, and the photochemical properties of the anthraquinone portion might be better maintained.

Therefore, the functionalized ligand 9,10-anthraquinone-1,4-dicarboxylic acid was synthesized as a white solid in three steps using a modified procedure of the literature method.31 Transition metal complexes involving Co(ii), Ni(ii), Cu(ii), Zn(ii) and Ag(i) based on the AQDC2− ligand were prepared as a crystalline solid by a common solution method for synthesizing coordination compounds. All metal complexes were characterized by means of single-crystal and powder X-ray diffractions, infrared spectra and elemental analyses and their thermo-stabilities were studied (see the ESI† for PXRD patterns and TGA curves).

Structure analysis

A common feature of these coordination complexes is that they are all polymeric, indicating less solubility in common organic solvents and therefore dictating the possibility of heterogeneous catalysis and potential of easy recycling. Ascribed to rich coordination chemistry of carboxylate groups, the AQDC2− ligand exhibits various coordination modes in these series of transition metal complexes (Scheme 1), resulting in versatile polymeric structures.

Complexes Co-AQDC and Ni-AQDC are isostructural, crystallizing in the monoclinic space group P21/c. In both complexes, each metal center adopts a distorted octahedral geometry, coordinating to two carboxylate oxygen atoms from two symmetry related AQDC2− ligands and four coordinated water molecules (Fig. 1a). The AQDC2− ligands take a μ2-bridging mode with both carboxylate groups participating in coordination. 2D layers in the (010) plane were formed through the connection of AQDC2− ligands and μ2-bridging water molecules, which further packed together through the interactions of hydrogen bonding and π–π stacking (Fig. 1b and c).

As shown in Fig. 2, the Cu(ii) centers in Cu-AQDC adopt a pyramidal coordination mode, with water ligands located on the apex of the pyramid at elongated coordination distances (Fig. 2a). Two carboxylate O atoms from oppositely positioned AQDC2− ligands and one water molecule occupy three of the four corners of the pyramid base. The other corner is occupied by water and dimethylformamide alternatively to form pseudo-isostructural motifs, which are connected alternatively to form the 1D chain structure along the [010] direction (Fig. 2b). Hydrogen bonding and π–π stacking further assemble the chains into a higher dimension (Fig. 2c).

There are two symmetry independent Zn(ii) cations in Zn-AQDC, both taking slightly distorted octahedral geometries. Four symmetry related AQDC2− ligands and two hydroxyl ions constitute the coordination sphere of one Zn(ii) ion and the other Zn(ii) ion binds with two symmetry related AQDC2− ligands, two water molecules and two hydroxyl ions (Fig. 3a). The hydroxyl ion exhibits a μ3-bridging mode, connecting three adjacent Zn(ii) atoms to form a Zn3 motif, which are further linked together by the AQDC2− ligand to generate a 3D non-porous supramolecular architecture (Fig. 3b). The AQDC2− ligand links four Zn(ii) cations through its carboxylate groups (Scheme 1).

Complex Ag-AQDC crystallizes in the orthorhombic space group Pbca, in which a short argentophilic interaction at 2.869 Å has been observed (Fig. 4a). The dimeric Ag–Ag moiety containing two crystallographically independent Ag(i) cations is surrounded by five symmetry related AQDC2− ligands and one coordinated methanol molecule, which link the dimeric moieties into a 2D bilayer sheet (Fig. 4b). The layered structures further stack by van der Waals interactions into the 3D structure (Fig. 4c).

In accordance with our ligand design, the two carboxylate groups of the AQDC2− ligand are not in the plane of the conjugated system due to the steric hindrance with neighboring carbonyl groups, bearing dihedral angles ranging from 55.4° to 89.2° for this family of coordination polymers (Table 2). The ligand is slightly bent in it coordination polymers, as indicated by the dihedral angles of the two carbonyl groups and the central six-member ring and the deviation of atoms of the anthraquinone plane (Table 2).

Ascribed to the existence of coordinated and lattice water molecules, rich intra- and inter-molecular hydrogen bonds exist in the crystal packing of these compounds except that of the Ag(i) complex, leading to more stabilized stacking as solid materials (Table 3). Some of the hydrogen bonds are pretty strong with donor to acceptor distances between 2.5 to 2.6 Å.

Catalytic studies

Diphenylethyne is used as the substrate to optimize the reaction conditions (Table 4). An initial investigation revealed that the ligand itself is capable of catalyzing the reaction in 63% yield (entry 1). Screening of different solvents as a reaction medium revealed that acetonitrile (MeCN) performs much better than tetrahydrofuran (THF), dichloromethane (DCM), and methanol (MeOH) in mediating the reaction (entries 2–5). Water contents of the solvent were found to affect the yield of the reaction significantly. Neither anhydrous acetonitrile (entry 6) nor those doped with an excess amount of water (entries 7 and 8) as the reaction solvent performs better than commercially available acetonitrile with 0.13% water content (determined by Karl-Fischer titration). A minimum amount of the photocatalyst required for a satisfactory yield is 0.1 equivalent (entries 2, 9–11). Further studies indicate that almost no reactions occur in the absence of any one of the elements including photocatalyst, 4-chlorobenzenethiol, blue LED and air (entries 12–15).

As shown in Table 5, screening of transition metal complexes of the AQDC2− ligand shows that Zn-AQDC and Ag-AQDC exhibit comparable catalytic activities (57–68%, entries 18 and 19) to that for the ligand, while Co-AQDC and Cu-AQDC complexes catalyze the reaction at only 35–37% yields (entries 16 and 17). It is notable that isostructural pair Co-AQDC and Ni-AQDC behaves very differently in promoting the photo-oxidation reaction, and therefore we can conclude that structures of these complexes and coordination modes of metal centers/ligands are not decisive factors for the catalytic activity of the complexes. There might be some synergistic effect of the AQDC2− ligand and the Ni(ii) center in Ni-AQDC that enhances the catalytic activity of the complex.

Under the optimized reaction conditions, the generality and scope of this photocatalytic system using Ni-AQDC as a catalyst were then examined with a series of diarylethynes as substrates (Table 6). Moderate to good yields were obtained for a series of reactions with products as listed in Table 6. It is notable that the easily oxidized formyl group remains intact during the oxidation process (2l in Table 6), indicating good oxidizing selectivity in functional groups. Diarylethyne comprising the thienyl group could also be oxidized into the corresponding 1,2-diketones at 72% yield.

The Ni-AQDC catalyst therefore represents an efficient heterogeneous catalyst for this reaction system and can easily be recovered by filtration after the reaction, attributed to very poor solubility in the reaction medium. To determine the amount of catalyst loss during the catalytic process, we determined the concentration of the Ni element remaining in the solution after the reaction, through the inductive coupled plasma emission spectrum (ICP). This result shows that only 0.04 μg mL−1 of the Ni element presents in the filtrate, accounting for 0.0068% loss of the catalyst, which means that the photocatalyst can be efficiently recovered. Powder X-ray diffraction patterns of the Ni-AQDC catalyst before and after the catalytic reaction reveal no change in the material structure, indicating the fact that the catalyst stays intact during the catalytic process. The high recovery yield and the good stability of the catalyst dictate the possibility of recycled use.

Consequently, the recyclability and reusability of the Ni-AQDC catalyst are evaluated by such oxidation reactions using diphenylethyne as the substrate. The catalyst is recovered from the reaction system by filtration, washed with dichloromethane, dried under vacuum, and subjected to catalytic use again without any operation of regeneration. Upon four times recycled use, the Ni-AQDC complex is still able to efficiently catalyze the reaction without a significant loss of activity, demonstrating good recyclability as a photocatalyst (Fig. 5a). The yields with the recycled catalyst are comparable to that with the as-synthesized catalyst, all at ∼80% level. The powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) pattern of the photocatalyst after five catalytic cycles is in good agreement with that of the as-synthesized Ni-AQDC complex (Fig. 5b).

In the presence of 2.0 equivalents of 2,2,6,6-tetramethyl-1-piperidinyloxy (TEMPO), no product was detected from the reaction system under standard conditions, being a sign of possible radical pathway for the transformation. Based on this and the abovementioned observations, a plausible reaction mechanism has been proposed (Scheme 2). Upon irradiation by visible light, the Ni-AQDC catalyst converts to the excited state, which undergoes reductive quenching by thiophenol to generate its semiquinone form accompanied by the formation of the thiophenyl radical (A). Radical addition involving thiophenyl radicals, water and diphenylethyne affords intermediate B, which reacts with O2 to generate intermediate C. The redox reaction between semiquinone Ni-AQDC(H)˙ and intermediate C gives rise to the formation of 1,2-diketones and ground state Ni-AQDC.

Conclusions

To develop novel heterogeneous photocatalysts, the quinone functionalized dicarboxylate ligand 9,10-anthraquinone-1,4-dicarboxylate acid was designed and synthesized, followed by the syntheses of a family of transition metal complexes. The ligand shows versatile coordination modes in these metal complexes to result in various polymeric structures. These coordination polymers are able to catalyze the reaction of the visible-light driven aerobic oxidation of alkynes into 1,2-diketones under ambient conditions. The Ni-AQDC complex exhibits enhanced catalytic activity as compared to the ligand itself, representing the best one among the family of metal complexes over a broad scope of substrates with moderate to good yields. The recovery of the catalyst is as simple as filtration, washing and drying, with only 0.0068% loss in solution determined by ICP. Remarkably, the recovered catalyst can be used again to catalyze the reaction without any need for regeneration for at least five times with persistently good activity. X-ray powder diffractions before and after five catalytic runs reveal no structure change of the Ni-AQDC catalyst, demonstrating good stability. This catalyst represents an environmentally friendly, economical and recyclable photocatalyst with good performance, which might be used conveniently in light-irradiated flow reactors as a fixed bed catalyst. This ligand design strategy is potentially useful in the development of functional metal–organic hybrid materials with versatile applications.
