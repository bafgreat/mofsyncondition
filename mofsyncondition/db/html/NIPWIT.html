Introduction

In the area of organic–inorganic hybrid materials, metal–organic coordination architectures built from metal clusters and multifunctional carboxylates have been extensively investigated for their structural diversity and potential applications in catalysis, nonlinear optics, ion exchange, gas storage, magnetism and molecular recognition.1 Among these metal–organic complexes, zinc coordination polymers have attracted much attention owing to their thermal stability and photoluminescent properties, a feature common to the closed-shell 3d10 zinc(ii) complexes.2 The multinuclear zinc clusters, which act as building blocks, can be used to build up extended frameworks,3 and to date, the zinc coordination polymers with discrete multinuclear clusters Znn (n = 2–4, 6–8) have been successfully isolated.4 However, the coordination polymers based on the assembly of infinite multinuclear zinc clusters are very limited.2c,5 It has been demonstrated that the presence of hydroxy or oxo groups facilitates the formation of discrete clusters by M–OH(or O)–M linkage3b,4f and this is also true for the case of infinite building blocks. To our knowledge, there are three examples in which the Zn–OH–Zn connectivity participates in the formation of one-dimensional (1-D) rod-like building blocks,5 but 2-D sheet-like building blocks with Zn–OH–Zn linkage have not been reported so far.

Supramolecular isomerism, the existence of more than one type of network superstructure for a given set of components,6 represents an intriguing phenomenon that is particularly important because the superstructure plays an essential role in determining the properties of crystalline solids. In some instances, supramolecular isomerism could be synonymous with polymorphism, which is also an enigmatic phenomenon in crystal engineering. However, in other cases, supramolecular isomers are not true polymorphs since guest or solvent molecules are present in the lattice. Compared to the supramolecular isomers with different chemical compositions due to the coexistence of guest molecules,6,7 polymorphs with a fixed stoichiometry for all components have been rarely observed for coordination polymers,8,9 although it is widely encountered for organic compounds.10 In this study, two polymorphs with the same stoichiometry Zn2(OH)2(2,4-pydc) (2,4-pydcH2 = pyridine-2,4-dicarboxylic acid, Chart 1) were hydro(solvo)thermally synthesized and characterized by single-crystal X-ray diffraction. The two compounds, expressed as Zn2(μ2-OH)2(2,4-pydc) (1) and Zn2(μ2-OH)(μ3-OH)(2,4-pydc) (2), are constructed by infinite 1-D and 2-D zinc clusters and unsymmetric bridging ligands, thus generating 2-D and 3-D coordination networks, respectively. Compound 2 is an exceptionally stable acentric coordination polymer and both compounds show intense fluorescence properties.

Results and discussion

Syntheses

Recent researches have revealed that hydro(solvo)thermal reaction is a promising technique in preparing complexes with novel structures and special properties. However, the controlled synthesis of supramolecular isomers is still a great challenge. Small changes in one or more of the variables of hydrothermal reaction such as temperature, pH value, stoichiometry, template and duration can have a profound influence on the reaction outcome.11 The polymorphs 1 and 2 were hydro(solvo)thermally prepared from Zn(BF4)2 and 2,4-pydcH2 in the presence of 1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)ethylene (bpe) or 1,3-bis(4-pyridyl)propane (bpp) at 150 °C for 1 and 165 °C for 2. Interestingly, although bpe and bpp do not participate in the structure construction, we cannot obtain the products 1 and 2 in the absence of them. Many trials failed when only reacting Zn(BF4)2 with 2,4-pydcH2 in a wide pH range and at different temperatures while the reaction in the presence of 4,4′-bipyridine gave polymorph 1 in a poor yield. Tong et al. have prepared two supramolecular isomers with a formula as Cd2(μ3-OH)2(2,4-pydc) by reacting Cd(NO3)2 with 2,4-pydcH2 at higher temperature (175–180 °C for one polymorph and 190–200 °C for the other).9 This fact strongly suggests that bpe and bpp, as Lewis bases, could effectively promote deprotonation of the aqua ligands into hydroxy groups at relatively low temperature, leading to the formation of different superstructures. The absence of the expected characteristic bands at 1730–1690 cm−1 (IR) attributed to protonated carboxylate groups indicates the complete deprotonation of 2,4-pydc ligand in the reactions. 1 and 2 are stable in air and insoluble in water and common organic solvents.

Crystal structures

The selected bond lengths and angles are listed in Table 1. The 2,4-pydc ligand exhibits the same coordination mode in both 1 and 2 (Fig. 1). The pyridyl N atom and one 2-position carboxylate O atom are bonded to a Zn atom in a chelating mode, and this O atom in a bridging mode is also coordinated to another Zn atom. The 4-position carboxylate acts as a bridging bidentate ligand in a usual syn–syn configuration to link two Zn atoms. This coordination mode has recently been found in a 2-D coordination network (UO2)3(μ3-O)(μ3-OH)(μ2-OH)(2,4-pydc)(H2O)2]·H2O12 and a 3-D coordination network Cd2(μ3-OH)2(2,4-pydc).9

Compound 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/c. The asymmetric unit consists of two crystallographically unique zinc centers, one 2,4-pydc ligand and two μ2-hydroxy groups (O5 and O6). Assignment of the bridging O atoms as a μ2-OH group was confirmed by bond valence calculations13 for the corresponding oxygen atoms with values of 1.13 and 1.06 for O5 and O6, respectively. The H atoms attached to O5 and O6 can be located from a difference Fourier map and the hydrogen-bonding interactions support the presence of these H atoms. There are two distinct Zn centers in 1 (Fig. 1a). The Zn1 atom in a distorted trigonal bipyramidal geometry is coordinated by two carboxylate O atoms (Zn–O = 2.078(2) and 2.160(2) Å) and one N atom (Zn–N = 2.191(3) Å) from two 2,4-pydc ligands, and two O atoms from µ2-OH groups (Zn–O = 1.930(2) and 1.952(2) Å), while the Zn2 atom adopts a tetrahedral geometry, coordinated by two carboxylate O atoms (Zn–O = 1.975(2) and 2.009(2) Å) from two 2,4-pydc ligands, and two μ2-OH groups (Zn–O = 1.902(2) and 1.928(2) Å). The Zn1 and Zn2 centers are connected alternatively into an infinite 1-D Zn–OH–Zn chain by sharing O5 and O6 vertices. Such two chains are further linked to each other via the bifurcate bridging O1 atom, generating a centrosymmetric double-chain unit (Fig. 2a and b). The Zn⋯Zn separations are 3.364(1) and 3.425(1) Å in the single chain and 3.661(1) Å between the chains. The double-chain units are arranged in parallel in the [0 1 0] direction and connected by 2,4-pydc ligands to give a 2-D layer-like network (Fig. 2c). The layers are stacked in the [0 0 1] direction and associated via hydrogen-bonding interactions involving the hydroxy groups and uncoordinated carboxylate O2 atoms.

X-Ray structure analysis has revealed that compound 2 crystallizes in the orthorhombic space group Pna21 and has an acentric extended 3-D coordination network. The asymmetric unit contains two Zn centers, one 2,4-pydc ligand, and one μ2-OH(O5) and one μ3-OH(O6) group. The presence of hydroxy groups is in good agreement with bond valance calculations13 for the corresponding oxygen atoms with values of 1.01 and 1.29 for O5 and O6, respectively. The H atoms attached to O5 and O6 can be found in a difference Fourier map. The μ3-OH bridging configuration of O6 is also confirmed from an examination of the Zn–O–Zn angles (102.1(1)–123.3(1)°) and the Zn–O–H angles (101–109°), which are consistent with the expected tetrahedral geometry for such a μ3-OH group.14 The presence of the µ3-OH group results in an entirely different structure from that of 1, in spite of the same coordination mode of the 2,4-pydc ligand as that in 1. There are two different trigonal bipyramidal Zn centers in 2, namely a ZnO4N unit and a ZnO5 unit (Fig. 1b). The Zn1 atom is coordinated by two carboxylate O atoms (Zn–O = 2.018(2) and 2.207(3) Å) and one pyridyl N atom (Zn–N = 2.052(3) Å) from two 2,4-pydc ligands, and two hydroxy O atoms (Zn–O = 1.979(2) and 1.996(2) Å). The Zn2 atom is surrounded by two carboxylate O atoms from two 2,4-pydc ligands (Zn–O = 2.076(2) and 2.104(2) Å) and three hydroxy O atoms (Zn–O = 1.931(2)–2.034(2) Å). As shown in Fig. 3a, the fusion of the six-membered rings, each consisting of one µ2-OH, two µ3-OH groups and one Zn1 and two Zn2 atoms, generates a Zn–OH ribbon. The ribbon can also be described as two Zn–OH–Zn chains connected via µ3-OH groups with the shortest Zn⋯Zn separation of 3.149(1) Å. The carboxylate O1 atoms bridge between the ribbons, leading to a 2-D sheet parallel to the (0 1 0) plane. The 3-D coordination network of 2 is constructed by the pillaring of 2,4-pydc ligands between adjacent sheets (Fig. 4).

It is interesting to note that in the zinc–oxygen sheet, there are two distinct helices surrounding the crystallographic twofold screw axes along the c direction. One helix is made of Zn1–O1–Zn1 chain in a left-handed fashion (Fig. 3b), and the other formed by Zn2–O6–Zn2 chain is right-handed (Fig. 3c). The two kinds of helical chains have an identical pitch equal to the length of the c axis, and are connected to each other by sharing O5 and O6 vertices. Thus, the acentric structure of 2 can be viewed as a consequence of 2,4-pydc spacers connecting the acentric zinc–oxygen sheets. It has been shown that the coordination polymers with 2-D M–O–M building units have advantages in material properties compared to those with isolated metal atoms or discrete metal clusters. For instance, Co2(OH)2(1,4-benzenedicarboxylate) with 2-D Co–O–Co sheets exhibits unusual metamagnetism,15 while Cd2(OH)(1,2,4-benzenetricarboxylate) with chiral Cd–O–Cd sheets shows distinct NLO and fluorescence properties.2b This work provides an example that a 3-D network is built up from acentric Zn–O–Zn sheets pillared by unsymmetrical pyridinedicarboxylate ligands.

One of the advantages of the coordination polymers with 1-D rod-like building blocks is the potential to avoid interpenetration of coordination frameworks.5 In 1, there exist π–π interactions between adjacent pyridyl rings of the 2,4-pydc ligands that connect the double-chain units, with a mean atom-to-plane distance of 3.37 Å, which is a good match with the Zn⋯Zn separations (3.364(1) Å) in the Zn–OH–Zn chains. The π–π interactions result in an impenetrable wall. A robust porous material, Co3(μ3-OH)2(2,4-pydc)2, has been reported very recently,16 in which the Co–OH–Co chains act as rod-like building blocks around which the 2,4-pydc ligands provide walls of the channels. Thus, 1-D M–OH–M linkage can be a good candidate for constructing non-interpenetrating coordination polymers by using a rod-and-spacer approach.5a,17 Although there are no voids or channels in 2, it is evident that the close packed Zn–OH–Zn sheets connected by the 2,4-pydc spacers can never give interpenetrating structure. This may provide a clue that a sheet-and-spacer approach may be useful to construct 3-D metal–organic frameworks that do not interpenetrate.

In order to check the bulk purity of the compounds and whether or not there are concomitant polymorphs in the same preparation, X-ray powder diffraction (XRPD) patterns have been measured and compared with those simulated from the single-crystal structures. As can be seen in Fig. 5, the measured and simulated XRPD patterns for both 1 and 2 are quite similar, confirming the homogeneity of the bulk samples. This indicates that the reaction conditions used in the present study are effective to control the formation of the two polymorphs.

Photoluminescence

The photoluminescence spectra of 1 and 2 were measured in the solid state at room temperature (Fig. 6). Upon excitation at λex = 355 nm for 1 and 330 nm for 2, intense bands in the emission spectra are observed at 410 nm for 1 and 480 nm for 2. The origin of the emission can be attributed to ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT). As reported previously, solid 2,4-pydcH2 displays very weak photoluminescent property upon excitation at λex = 343 nm.12Ligand chelation to the metal center that effectively increases the rigidity of the ligand and reduces the loss of energy through a radiationless pathway may enhance luminescence in 1 and 2.18 Compared to 1, the emission peak of 2 is red-shifted by 70 nm. This shift is presumably a result of different structural motifs, 2-D vs. 3-D network. In contrast, very close emission bands at 425 and 420 nm have been observed for two polymorphs of Cd2(OH)2(2,4-pydc), both of which have 3D network structures.9 Moreover, the calculated densities of 2.523 g cm−1 for 1 and 2.634 g cm−1 for 2 indicate more compact packing for the high-dimensional polymorph. As a result of the 3D network, 2 is remarkably robust. Thermogravimetric analyses showed that 1 and 2 are stable up to 260 and 426 °C, respectively, at which point decomposition of the organic components occurred. These observations indicate that these condensed polymeric compounds, which are thermally stable and insoluble in common organic solvents, may be useful as potential photoactive materials.

Conclusions

Two polymorphs of zinc hydroxide coordination polymers with unsymmetrical 2,4-pydc ligands have been synthesized and crystallographically characterized. The two compounds show remarkably distinct structural features, a centrosymmetric 2-D network formed by 2,4-pydc ligands linking 1-D building blocks for 1 and an acentric 3-D network constructed by 2,4-pydc ligands pillaring 2-D building blocks for 2. Both 1-D and 2-D building blocks contain infinite Zn–OH–Zn linkage. Our results suggest that the architectures of these superstructures can be controlled by varying reaction conditions, especially the addition of pyridyl-like ligands such as bpe and bpp, although they do not take part in the superstructures. Moreover, it has been documented by several authors that unsymmetrical ligands have a significant effect on the formation of chiral or acentric coordination polymers.2b,19 This work based on zinc(II)–2,4-pydc system provides very rare examples of two polymorphs which crystallize in the centrosymmetric and non-centrosymmetric space groups, respectively. Our further effort focuses on other metal–unsymmetrical ligand systems to explore supramolecular isomerism in such coordination polymers and understand the relationships between structures and properties.

Experimental

Materials and general methods

All chemicals used in syntheses were of reagent grade and used as purchased without further purification. Infrared (IR) spectra were recorded from KBr pellets in the range of 400–4000 cm−1 on a Bruker VERTEX70 FTIR spectrometer. Elemental analyses were performed on a Perkin-Elmer 2400 elemental analyzer. Thermogravimetric analyses were carried out on a Perkin-Elmer TGA7 thermogravimetric analyzer in an N2 stream with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. XRPD pattens were recorded on a Philips PW1700 diffractometer. Fluorescence spectra were obtained with an LS55 fluorescence/phosphorescence spectrophotometer at room temperature.

Zn2(μ2-OH)2(2,4-pydc) (1)

A mixture of Zn(BF4)2 (0.2 mmol), 2,4-pydcH2 (0.15 mmol), bpe (0.1 mmol), methanol (3 mL) and distilled water (8 mL) in a molar ratio of 2 : 1.5 : 1 : 740 : 4444, with a pH value of 6.0, was stirred for 20 min at room temperature and then sealed in a 23 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel autoclave at 150 °C for 72 h. After cooling to room temperature, colorless crystals were obtained and washed with water, filtered and dried in air. Yield: ∼32% based on Zn. Anal. Calcd. (%) for C7H5NO6Zn2: C 25.49, H 1.53, N 4.25. Found (%): C 24.92, H 1.46, N 4.39. IR (KBr, cm−1): 3500 s, 2975 w, 2939 s, 1609 s, 1549 w, 1475 w, 1364 s, 1222 w, 1091 vs, 1036 m, 941 w, 880 w, 807 m, 782 m, 730 s, 628 vs, 548 w, 457 w.

Zn2(μ2-OH)(μ3-OH)(2,4-pydc) (2)

A mixture of Zn(BF4)2 (0.2 mmol), 2,4-pydcH2 (0.15 mmol), bpp (0.1 mmol), methanol (3 mL) and distilled water (8 mL) in a molar ratio of 2 : 1.5 : 1 : 740 : 4444, with a pH value of 7.8, was stirred for 20 min at room temperature and then sealed in a 23 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel autoclave at 165 °C for 72 h. After cooling to room temperature, colorless crystals were obtained and washed with water, filtered and dried in air. Yield: ∼25% based on Zn. Anal. Calcd. (%) for C7H5NO6Zn2: C 25.49, H 1.53, N 4.25. Found (%): C 25.18, H 1.64, N 4.47. IR (KBr, cm−1): 3419 vs, 3077 w, 3052 w, 1655 vs, 1611 s, 1476 m, 1399 vs, 1255 m, 1191 m, 1088 m, 936 w, 833 s, 786 s, 736 s, 591 w, 551 w, 461 m, 433 w.

X-Ray crystallography

Diffraction intensities of 1 and 2 were collected on a Bruker Smart APEX CCD area-detectordiffractometer (MoKα radiation, λ = 0.71073 Å) at −120 °C. Absorption corrections were applied by using the multi-scan program SADABS.20 The structures were solved by direct methods and refined on F2 by full-matrix least squares technique with the SHELXTL program package.21 Anisotropic displacement parameters were applied to all non-hydrogen atoms. The organic hydrogen atoms were positioned geometrically and refined as riding atoms. The hydrogen atoms of hydroxy groups were located in difference Fourier maps and fixed in the final refinements.
