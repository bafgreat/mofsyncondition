Introduction

Interest in the crystal engineering of coordination polymers (CPs) is increasing not only due to their intriguing architectures, but also because of their fascinating potential applications in luminescent materials, heterogeneous catalysis, magnetism, and electrochemistry.1–4 In this area, one important branch is the construction of lanthanide-based CPs. Lanthanide-based CPs have intense luminescence because of the long-lived excited states of Ln3+ ions. These CPs have played important roles in lighting and light conversion technologies such as lasers, cathode-ray tubes, plasma displays, and light-emitting diodes, leading to the most interesting developments in the coordination chemistry of lanthanide ions.5,6 It is well known that lanthanide ions have an affinity to hard donor atoms, and thus ligands containing oxygen atoms have been extensively used in the synthesis of lanthanide-based CPs. Several classes of ligands such as cryptands,7 β-diketone,8 carboxylic acid,9 and phosphoric acid10 have been utilized. The first lanthanide phosphonate complex was prepared in 1985 by McCabe.10 Up to now, many phosphonate ligands have been designed and synthesized, in which most of them contain a secondary functional group, such as carboxyl,11 sulfonic,12 hydroxyl,13 and pyridyl.14 It is believed that the presence of a secondary functional group could improve the crystallinity of the lanthanide phosphonate complexes.15

The derivatives of pyridyl-N-oxide phosphonates were first reported by Paine in 2002, and were designed as effective liquid–liquid extraction (LLE) reagents for nuclear materials separations.16 In comparison with the pyridyl-containing phosphonates, the pyridyl-N-oxide phosphonates have more abundant coordination modes, higher solubility, and higher coordination ability even at low pH values. Based on the aforementioned considerations, these pyridyl-N-oxide phosphonates could represent a significant type of ligand for construction of lanthanide-based CPs. However, reports on them are limited in comparison with others.17 Accordingly, in this work, we selected 2-(pyridyl-N-oxide)methylphosphonic acid (H2L), and investigated it in combination with oxalic acid for the construction of lanthanide-based CPs. Two series of nine complexes were synthesized and characterized, which show three-dimensional (3D) structures. In addition, the photoluminescence and magnetic properties were studied.

Experimental section

Materials and measurements

All commercially available chemicals were reagent grade and used as received. The ligand 2-(pyridyl-N-oxide)methylphosphonic acid (H2L) was synthesized according to a previous reference.17a LnCl3·6H2O was synthesized by dissolving lanthanide oxide in a slight excess of hydrochloric acid. Elemental analyses of C, H, and N were performed on a Perkin-Elmer 2400 elemental analyzer. The IR spectra were recorded on a Perkin Elmer Spectrum 100 FT-IR spectrometer equipped with a DGTS detector (32 scans) by using KBr disks in the range of 4000 to 500 cm−1. Thermal analyses were conducted on a Perkin-Elmer STA 6000 with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 in a temperature range from 30 to 800 °C under air. The luminescence spectra for the powdered solid samples were recorded at room temperature on an Edinburgh FLS 920 fluorescence spectrophotometer. Magnetic susceptibilities were determined using a Quantum Design MPMS XL-7 SQUID-VSM magnetometer. Diamagnetic corrections were made with Pascal's constants for all the constituent atoms and sample holders.

X-ray crystallography

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction data for complexes 1–9 were collected on a Rigaku R-AXIS RAPID imaging plate diffractometer or an Oxford Xcalibur Gemini Ultra diffractometer, both with graphite-monochromated Mo Kα (λ = 0.71073 Å) at room temperature. Empirical absorption corrections based on equivalent reflections were applied. The structures of 1–9 were solved by direct methods and refined by full-matrix least-squares methods on F2 using SHELXS-97 crystallographic software package.18 The crystal parameters, data collection and refinement results for 1–9 are summarized in Table 1. Results and discussion

Synthesis

The crystals of complexes 1–9 were synthesized by hydrothermal reactions in a low pH range of 1.0–2.0, where the pure crystal products can be obtained. However, when the pH was higher than 2.0, the products would be a mixture of the crystals and unknown powders. In this series of complexes, one obvious feature is that the yield of the products would decrease when the atomic number is increased. The other Ln metals, such as La, Ce, Pr, Nd and Sm, were also utilized to prepare the corresponding complexes. However, the products were simple oxalate lanthanide complexes: {[Ln((C2O4)0.5)3(H2O)2]·2H2O}n. The Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre (CCDC) indicates that the crystal structures of Pr and Nd complexes had been reported.19

Crystal structures of complexes 1–5

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis shows that complexes 1–5 are isomorphic. In a typical structure of 1–5, complex 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic system with a P21/c space group. The asymmetric unit of 1 has one Eu(iii) cation, one HL− anion, two halves of oxalate anions, one coordinated water molecule, and three lattice water molecules (Fig. 1a). The Eu(iii) cation is eight-coordinated in a distorted square antiprismatic geometry defined by three O atoms from two HL− anions, four O atoms from two oxalic acid anions, and one O atom from the coordinated water molecule. The Eu−O (HL−) bond distances are in the range of 2.298(3)−2.397(3) Å with the average bond length of 2.336 Å, which is significantly shorter than the Eu−O(oxalate) bond distances (in the range of 2.425(3)−2.474(3) Å with the average bond length of 2.450 Å). It suggests that the phosphonate group–metal interactions are stronger than the carboxyl group–metal interactions. This phenomenon is also found in other complexes constructed from carboxyl-containing phosphonate ligands.20 The HL− anion serves as a tridentate ligand coordinating with two Eu(iii) cations involving two O atoms of the phosphate group and one O atom of the pyridine N–O group, in which one O atom of the phosphate group does not participate in the coordination due to the protonation (Scheme 1).

The HL− anions link the Eu(iii) cations to form a chain structure along the b axis with an Eu⋯Eu separation of 7.061(1) Å. Furthermore, the oxalate anions serve as the chelated ligands connecting the Eu(iii) cations to form another chain along the a axis with the closest Eu⋯Eu distance of 6.297(1) Å. In the packing structure, these HL–Eu and oxalate–Eu chains interweave each other by sharing the Eu(iii) cations to construct a porous three-dimensional (3D) framework (Fig. 1b). PLATON21 indicates that the effective free volume is 28.4% of the crystal volume after removing the lattice and coordinated water molecules. The porous framework is filled with water molecules, and it is also stable after removing them, as was proved by the TG and PXRD analyses. In this framework, the HL− and oxalate anions both act as the bidentate bridging ligands, which could be simplified to a line, meanwhile, each Eu(iii) cation connects two HL− anions and two oxalate anions, which could be simplified to a 4-connected node. Therefore, the framework could be considered as a 3D 4-connected dia topological framework with a point symbol of {66} calculated by TOPOS (Fig. 1c).22

Crystal structures of complexes 6–9

Complexes 6–9 are also isomorphic, which crystallize in the monoclinic system with a P212121 space group. In a typical structure of 6–9, the asymmetric unit of 8 consists of two Yb(iii) cations, one L2− ligand, two oxalate anions, two coordinated water molecules and one lattice water molecule (Fig. 2a). The Yb1 atom is seven-coordinated in a pentagonal bipyramid environment defined by three O atoms from three different L2− anions, two O atoms from one oxalate anion, and two O atoms from two coordinated water molecules. The Yb1–O distances range from 2.171(3) to 2.352(4) Å with an average bond length of 2.273 Å.

However, the Yb2 atom is eight-coordinated in a distorted square antiprismatic environment defined by one O atom from the L2− anion, six O atoms from three oxalate anions, and one O atom from the coordinated water molecule. The Yb2–O distances range from 2.187(4) to 2.399(4) Å with an average bond length of 2.325 Å. The difference in the bond length is probably caused by the difference in the number of L2− anions that participate in the coordination of Yb1 and Yb2, namely, the more L2− anions that participate, the shorter the bond lengths become. In 8, the L2− anion serves as a tetradentate ligand coordinating with three Yb atoms involving all the O atoms (Scheme 1).

The L2− and the oxalate anions connect the Yb(iii) cations to build up the coordination chains both along the a axis. These chains also interweave each other by sharing the Yb(iii) cations to construct a 3D framework with the closest Yb⋯Yb separation of 5.574(1) Å (Fig. 2b). In comparison with complex 1, complex 8 shows a close-packed structure which only has a free volume of 5.4% of the crystal volume not including the lattice and coordinated water molecules. Interestingly, it is different from that of complex 1; in 8, each L2− anion as the tetradentate ligand links three Yb atoms, which should be considered as a 3-connected node. The oxalate anion serves as a bidentate bridging ligand, which should be considered as a line. And the Yb1 and Yb2 ions could be considered as the 3- and 4-connected nodes, respectively, based on their coordination modes. Hence, this framework could be simplified as a new 3D (3,4)-connected topological structure with a point symbol of {72·8}2{73·83} calculated by TOPOS (Fig. 2c).

Notably, according to the order of the lanthanide contraction, the bond distances of Ln–O and Ln⋯Ln decrease regularly in 1–5 and 6–9, respectively. The average bond lengths and the closest Ln⋯Ln distances of complexes 1–9 are listed in Table 2.

PXRD and thermal gravimetric analyses

Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) and thermogravimetric (TG) analyses were carried out to investigate the phase purity and stability of these complexes. The PXRD patterns for complexes 1–9 are presented in Fig. S2.† The diffraction peaks of both calculated and experimental patterns match well, indicating the phase purities of complexes 1–9.

The TG measurements of complexes 1–9 were performed under air atmosphere in the temperature range of 50–800 °C (Fig. S3†). Complexes 1–5 and 6–9 exhibit similar weight loss processes. Thus, the curves of 1 and 8 as the typical examples will be discussed in detail. As is shown in Fig. S3,† the weight loss of 13.4% for 1 in the range of 90–200 °C corresponds to the loss of three lattice and one coordinated water molecules (calcd.: 14.4%). Further heating to 201–380 °C shows no weight loss, which indicates that the dehydrated framework of 1 is thermally stable over this temperature range. The observed final mass residue of 50.2% can be ascribed to the generation of EuPO4 (calcd.: 49.3%). In complex 8, the weight loss of 10.1% in the range of 90–350 °C corresponds to the loss of one lattice and three coordinated water molecules (calcd.: 9.22%). There is no obvious stable platform for the framework of 8, which indicates that the framework collapses quickly after the dehydration process. The observed final mass residue of 59.5% is likely ascribed to a mixture of Yb2O3 and YbPO4 in a molar ratio of 1 : 1 (calcd.: 60.7%).

Photoluminescence properties

The solid-state luminescence properties of H2L and complexes 1–3 were investigated. Upon excitation at 300 nm, the free H2L ligand exhibits two broad emissions at 444 and 470 nm due to π–π* and/or n–π* transitions of the H2L molecules (Fig. S4†). In comparison to that of the H2L molecule, Gd-containing complex 2 shows significantly enhanced emissions at 494 and 522 nm (excitation at 360 nm) and an obviously red shift. It is well known that the lowest excited state, 6P7/2, of Gd(iii) ions is too high to accept energy from the ligand. Therefore, the enhancement and about 50 nm red-shift of the emission in 2 could be attributed to the coordination effect, which enables the rigidity of the aromatic backbones and reduces the loss of energy by radiationless decay of the intraligand emissions.23

The excitation spectrum of 1 was recorded at room temperature, which consists of a broad band and some narrow bands (Fig. 3a). The broad band at 330 nm can be assigned to the π–π* or n–π* transitions of the N-oxide ligands, while the narrow bands at 394 and 464 nm can be attributed to the intra-4f6 electronic transitions of Eu(iii) ions.24 As is expected, complex 1 displays the characteristic emission bands of Eu(iii) ions at 579 (very weak), 590, 610, 650 and 700 nm under excitation at 330 nm, which are attributed to the transition 5D0 → 7FJ (J: 0 to 4). The 5D0 → 7F1 transition is a magnetic dipole which is insensitive to the site symmetry. Only one component is found for the 5D0 → 7F0 transition, indicating that there is only one crystallographically independent Eu(iii) ion in complex 1.25 However, the 5D0 → 7F2 transition is allowed by electric dipole and is hypersensitive to the environment of the Eu(iii) ion, which is stronger than that of other transitions leading to a bright red emission and indicating that the coordination geometry around the Eu(iii) ion is low. Complex 3 exhibits four characteristic emission bands of Tb(iii) ions at 488, 545, 584 and 622 nm, attributed to the transition 5D4 → 7FJ (J: 6 to 3) (Fig. 3b). The strongest peak at 545 nm from the 5D4 → 7F5 transition results in a green light emission.

The room-temperature luminescence decay profiles (Fig. S5†) of complexes 1 and 3 are found to be single-exponential functions, thus implying the presence of only one emissive Eu(iii) or Tb(iii) center. The lifetimes of 671.5 and 697.5 μs were determined by monitoring the emission decay curves within the 5D0 → 7F2 transition at 610 nm for 1, and the 5D4 → 7F5 transition at 545 nm for 3.

Magnetic properties

The temperature dependence of the magnetic susceptibilities of 2–5 is recorded in the range of 1.8–300 K at 1000 Oe (Fig. 4). The χmT values at 300 K for 2–5 are 7.78 (2-Gd), 11.44 (3-Tb), 14.07 (4-Dy), and 14.02 (5-Ho) cm3 K mol−1, respectively, which are close to the theoretical values of 7.88 (Gd), 11.82 (Tb), 14.17 (Dy), and 14.06 (Ho) cm3 K mol−1 for the isolated Ln(iii) ions (Gd(iii), 8S7/2, g = 2; Tb(iii), 7F6, g = 3/2; Dy(iii), 6H15/2, g = 4/3, Ho(iii), 8I5, g = 5/4). Moreover, the curves of χm−1vs. T from 1.8–300 K obey the Curie–Weiss law (C = 30.3 cm3 K mol−1 and θ = −3.84 K (Gd); C = 16.67 cm3 K mol−1 and θ = −5.83 K (Tb); C = 16.73 cm3 K mol−1 and θ = −2.22 K (Dy); C = 23.73 cm3 K mol−1 and θ = −3.16 K (Ho)), which indicates the occurrence of a weak antiferromagnetic coupling between adjacent Ln(iii) ions in 2–5. Upon decreasing the temperature, the χmT values almost remain constant until 40.0 K and then decrease to reach the minimum of 7.30, 4.63, 12.47, and 4.79 cm3 K mol−1 for 2–5, respectively, at 1.8 K. The decreases are most likely to be attributed to the thermal depopulation of the Stark sublevels under the ligand-field effect.26

Conclusion

Two series of nine lanthanide-based coordination polymers, constructed from 2-(pyridyl-N-oxide)methylphosphonic acid, oxalic acid and lanthanide ions, have been isolated, which show interesting 3D frameworks. Generally, the poor solubility of high-dimensional lanthanide phosphonate complexes causes their preparation to be difficult. In the titled ligand, the utilization of the pyridyl-N-oxide group not only improves the coordination ability, but also increases the solubility of its complexes. In addition, the complexes are prepared in the presence of oxalic acid to promote the crystallization process. Complexes 1–5 exhibit 3D porous dia topological frameworks with free volumes of about 28% after removing the solvent molecules, meanwhile, 1–5 also show characteristic photoluminescence and magnetic properties of lanthanides, which imply that these complexes deserve further study as potential multifunctional lanthanide-based CPs. Complexes 6–9 show close-packed structures with free volumes of only about 5% after removing the solvent molecules; however, topological analysis indicates that they are new 3D 3,4-connected frameworks with the point symbol of {72·8}2{73·83}.
