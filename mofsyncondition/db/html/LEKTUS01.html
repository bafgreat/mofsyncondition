Three tetranuclear copper(II) cluster-based complexes constructed from 4-amino-1,2,4-triazole and different aromatic carboxylates: Assembly, structures, electrochemical and magnetic properties

Author links open overlay panel
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jssc.2012.10.003Get rights and content

Abstract

Three new tetranuclear copper(II) cluster-based complexes constructed from 4-amino-1,2,4-triazole (atrz) and three types of aromatic carboxylates, [Cu4(μ3-OH)2(atrz)2(DNBA)6] (1), [Cu4(μ3-OH)2(atrz)2(1,3-BDC)3]·2H2O (2) and [Cu4(μ3-OH)2(atrz)2(SIP)2]·4H2O (3) (HDNBA=3,5-dinitrobenzoic acid, 1,3-H2BDC=1,3-benzenedicarboxylic acid and NaH2SIP=sodium 5-sulfoisophthalate), have been hydrothermally synthesized and structurally characterized. Complex 1 displays a single-molecular CuII4 cluster structure, which is further connected by the intermolecular hydrogen-bonding interactions to form a 2D supramolecular layer. In 2, there also exist tetranuclear CuII4 clusters, which are linked by the 1,3-BDC anions to give a 3D NaCl-type framework. In 3, the CuII4 clusters are connected by the carboxyl and sulfo groups of SIP anions to generate 3D (4,8)-connected framework with a (410·614·84)(45·6)2 topology. The atrz ligand conduces to the construction of tetranuclear copper(II) clusters and the carboxylates with different non-carboxyl substituent show important effects on the final structures of the title complexes. The electrochemical and magnetic properties of 1–3 have been investigated.

1. Introduction

The rational design and synthesis of novel polynuclear metal cluster-based coordination polymers (MCCPs) is currently attracting considerable attention because of their intriguing topologies and potential applications as functional materials in conductivity, magnetism, luminescence, porosity, and so on [1]. Among the diverse construction methods of MCCPs, a combination of triazole/tetrazole or their derivatives as the main ligands and aromatic carboxylates as the auxiliary ligands is one of the effective strategies [2]. Firstly, the triazole/tetrazole or their derivatives and carboxyl groups can hold the metal ions into a polynuclear cluster through a network of bridges. On the other hand, the aromatic carboxylates with different coordination groups can extend the polynuclear metal clusters into high-dimensional frameworks. Yang and Zhao et al. reported a series of polynuclear metal cluster-based coordination polymers based on the triazole/tetrazole or their derivatives and aromatic carboxylate ligands, and investigated the effect of number, position and deprotonation of the carboxyl groups for aromatic multicarboxylates on the structures and properties of MCCPs [3], [4]. However, the exploration on the influence of non-carboxyl substituents for aromatic carboxylate ligands on the structures and properties of MCCPs based on the triazole derivatives is still limited [5], to our knowledge.

Herein, in order to investigate the role of non-carboxyl substituent for aromatic carboxylates in the assembly of MCCPs based on the triazole derivative, we selected 4-amino-1,2,4-triazole (atrz) as the main ligand and three types of aromatic carboxylic acids, namely, 3,5-dinitrobenzoic acid (HDNBA), 1,3-benzenedicarboxylic acid (1,3-H2BDC) and 5-sulfoisophthalic acid (H3SIP) as auxiliary ligands (Scheme 1), and combine them with CuII ions to construct the target complexes. As a result, three new tetranuclear copper(II) cluster-based complexes, namely, [Cu4(μ3-OH)2(atrz)2(DNBA)6] (1), [Cu4(μ3-OH)2(atrz)2(1,3-BDC)3]·2H2O (2) and [Cu4(μ3-OH)2(atrz)2(SIP)2]·4H2O (3) have been obtained, and the electrochemical and magnetic properties of 1–3 have been investigated.

2. Experimental

2.1. Materials and measurements

All chemicals were of reagent grade, commercially available, and used without further purification. FT-IR spectra were taken on a Magna FT-IR 560 spectrometer (KBr pellets). Elemental analyses (C, H and N) were performed on a Perkin–Elmer 2400 CHN elemental analyzer. Thermogravimetric data were measured on a Pyris Diamond thermal analyzer. Powder XRD investigations were carried out with a Bruker AXS D8-Advanced diffractometer at 40 kV, 40 mA with CuΚα (λ=1.5406 Å) radiation. Electrochemical measurements were carried out on a CHI 440 electrochemical workstation. A conventional three-electrode system was used at room temperature. A chemically bulk-modified carbon-paste electrode (CPE) was used as the working electrode. A saturated calomel electrode (SCE) and a platinum wire were used as the reference and auxiliary electrodes, respectively. Magnetic data were collected on a Quantum Design MPMSXL7 (SQUID) magnetometer. The diamagnetic corrections were made by using Pascal's constants.

2.2. Synthesis of [Cu4(μ3-OH)2(atrz)2(DNBA)6] (1)

A mixture of CuCl2·2H2O (0.034 g, 0.2 mmol), atrz (0.008 g, 0.1 mmol), HDNBA (0.032 g, 0.15 mmol), H2O (8.0 mL) and NaOH (0.1 mol L−1, 1.5 mL) was stirred for 1 h in air, and then sealed in a 25 mL Teflon-lined stainless-steel autoclave at 120 °C for 72 h. After slowly cooling to room temperature, blue block crystals of 1 were obtained. Yield: 50% (based on Cu). Anal. Calcd for C46H28Cu4N20O38 (1723.08): C, 32.07; H, 1.64; N, 16.26%. Found: C, 31.82; H, 1.76; N, 16.38%. IR (KBr, cm−1): 3452 w, 3082 w, 1614 s, 1541 s, 1454 m, 1350 s, 1269 w, 1191 w, 1145 w, 1085 m, 931 w, 779 m, 731 s, 686 s, 628 s, 582 s.

2.3. Synthesis of [Cu4(μ3-OH)2(atrz)2(1,3-BDC)3]·2H2O (2)

A mixture of CuSO4·5H2O (0.050 g, 0.2 mmol), atrz (0.008 g, 0.1 mmol), 1,3-H2BDC (0.025 g, 0.15 mmol), H2O (8.0 mL) and NaOH (0.1 mol L–1, 3.0 mL) was stirred for 1 h in air, and then sealed in a 25 mL Teflon-lined stainless-steel autoclave at 120 °C for 72 h. After slowly cooling to room temperature, blue block crystals of 2 were obtained. Yield: 60% (based on Cu). Anal. Calcd for C28H26Cu4N8O16 (984.74): C, 34.15; H, 2.66; N, 11.38%. Found: C, 34.01; H, 2.71; N, 11.57%. IR (KBr, cm–1): 3325 w, 3118 w, 1606 s, 1558 s, 1438 m, 1371 s, 1271 w, 1191 w, 1149 w, 1058 m, 966 w, 731 s, 686 s, 628 s, 584 s.

2.4. Synthesis of [Cu4(μ3-OH)2(atrz)2(SIP)2]·4H2O (3)

A mixture of CuNO3·3H2O (0.048 g, 0.2 mmol), atrz (0.008 g, 0.1 mmol), NaH2SIP (0.040 g, 0.15 mmol), H2O (8.0 mL) and NaOH (0.1 mol L–1, 3.0 mL) was stirred for 1 h in air, and then sealed in a 25 mL Teflon-lined stainless-steel autoclave at 120 °C for 72 h. After slowly cooling to room temperature, blue block crystals of 3 were obtained. Yield: 60% (based on Cu). Anal. Calcd for C20H24Cu4N8O20S2 (1014.82): C, 23.67; H, 2.38; N, 11.04%. Found: C, 23.49; H, 2.35; N, 11.22%. IR (KBr, cm–1): 3333 w, 3132 w, 1603 s, 1570 s, 1433 s, 1408 s, 1366 s, 1258 s, 1198 m, 1165 s, 1109 m, 1084 m, 1059 w, 1028 s, 918 w, 772 m, 727 s, 621 s.

2.5. Preparation of 1−, 2− and 3−CPEs

Complex 1 bulk-modified carbon-paste electrode (1-CPE) was fabricated as follows: 0.030 g of 1 and 0.500 g of graphite powder were mixed and ground together by the agate mortar and pestle for approximately 30 min to achieve an even mixture, and then 0.15 mL of paraffin oil was added into the mixture with stirring. The resulting mixture was packed into a glass tube (3 mm inner diameter) to a length of 8 mm, and the tube surface was wiped with the weighing paper. The electrical contact was established with a copper stick [6]. A similar procedure was used for the preparation of 2- and 3-CPE.

2.6. X-ray crystallographic study

X-ray diffraction data for 1–3 were collected on a Bruker Smart Apex CCD diffractometer with Mo-Kα radiation (λ=0.71073 Å). The structures were solved by direct methods using the program SHELXS-97 and all non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically on F2 by the full-matrix least-squares technique using the SHELXL-97 crystallographic software package [7]. Pertinent crystal data and structure refinements for 1–3 are listed in Table 1. Selected bond lengths (Å) and angles (°) for 1–3 are given in Table S1–Table S3. Crystallographic data for 1–3 have been deposited in the Cambridge Crystallographic Data Center with CCDC reference numbers 882562–882564.

3. Result and discussion

3.1. Synthesis

Many factors can influence the crystallization of products under hydrothermal conditions. In order to obtain 1–3 at maximum yield, we carried out many parallel experiments by changing the pH value of reaction media, time and temperature. The results indicate that 1–3 can be obtained at 120 °C for 3 days in the pH ranges of 3.4–6.5 for 1, 3.1–6.2 for 2 and 4.0–6.8 for 3, respectively. When the pH values are 5.8 for 1, 4.5 for 2 and 6.3 for 3, respectively, we can get the maximum yield for 1–3. When we extended the pH range, only blue sediment was obtained and no crystalline products appeared. Thus, the pH value plays an important role in the synthesis of 1–3. In addition, when the reaction time was less than 3 days, the crystalline products cannot be observed. If the temperature was below 120 °C, we also cannot obtain the crystalline products.

3.2. Structure description

Single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that complex 1 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P−1. The molecular structure of 1 consists of four CuII ions, two μ3-OH groups, two atrz ligands and six DNBA anions. As shown in Fig. 1, there exist two crystallographically independent CuII ions in 1. The Cu1 ion is five-coordinated by one nitrogen atom from one atrz ligand and four oxygen atoms from two μ3-OH groups and two DNBA anions in a distorted square-pyramidal geometry. The Cu2 ion is also five-coordinated by one nitrogen atom from one atrz ligand and four oxygen atoms from one μ3-OH group and three DNBA anions, showing a distorted square-pyramidal geometry. The Cu–N bond lengths are 1.983(3) (Cu1–N1) and 2.005(3) (Cu2–N2) Å, and the Cu–O bond lengths are in the range of 1.928(3)–2.502(3) Å (Table S1), which are comparable to those of the similar copper(II) complexes [8]. The DNBA anion adopts three types of coordination modes (Scheme S1) in 1, that is, monodentate, bridging monodentate, and bridging bismonodentate modes, which have been observed in the reported DNBA metal complexes [9]. Four CuII ions are supported by two μ3-OH groups, two atrz ligands and four DNBA anions to form a tetranuclear CuII4 cluster with the intermetallic distances of 3.233(3), 3.422(3), 3.133(3) and 5.710(3) Å for Cu1···Cu1A, Cu1···Cu2, Cu1···Cu2A and Cu2···Cu2A, respectively. The adjacent CuII4 clusters are further extended into a 2D supramolecular layer by the intermolecular hydrogen-bonding interactions (Fig. S1). The CuII4 cluster and 2D supramolecular layer of 1 are similar to those of [Cu4(μ3-OH)2(atrz)2(NB)6]·2H2O (HNB=4-nitrobenzoic acid) [4e].

Complex 2 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/c. Fig. 2 shows the coordination environments of two pairs of crystallographically independent CuII ions in 2. Similar to that of 1, each CuII ion is five-coordinated by one nitrogen atom from one atrz ligand and four oxygen atoms from the μ3-OH groups and 1,3-BDC anions in a distorted square-pyramidal geometry. The Cu–N bond lengths are 2.014(6) (Cu1–N1) and 1.988(6) (Cu2–N2A) Å, and the Cu–O bond lengths are in the range of 1.924(4)–2.349(5) Å (Table S2). Two μ3-OH groups, two atrz ligands and four 1,3-BDC anions hold four CuII ions into a tetranuclear CuII4 cluster with the intermetallic distances of 3.198(3), 3.135(3), 3.365(3) and 5.664(3) Å for Cu1···Cu1A, Cu1···Cu2, Cu1···Cu2A and Cu2···Cu2A, respectively, which are also similar to those of 1.

In 2, each CuII4 cluster is connected by four 1,3-BDC anions to form a 2D (4,4) network structure along the bc plane (Fig. 3a). The adjacent 2D networks are further linked by the 1,3-BDC anions to generate a 3D framework structure along the ac plane (Fig. 3b). If the CuII4 cluster is considered as a six-connected node, and the 1,3-BDC anion as a linker, complex 2 exhibits a 3D NaCl-type topological structure (Fig. 4). The 1,3-BDC anion adopts two types of coordination modes (Scheme S1) in 2, which have been observed in the reported 1,3-BDC metal complexes [10].

Complex 3 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/c. Fig. 5 shows the coordination environments of two pairs of crystallographically independent CuII ions in 3. Cu1 is five-coordinated by one nitrogen atom from one atrz ligand and four oxygen atoms from one μ3-OH group and three SIP anions in a distorted square-pyramidal geometry. Cu2 is six-coordinated by one nitrogen atom from one atrz ligand and five oxygen atoms from two μ3-OH groups and three SIP anions in a distorted octahedron geometry. Similar to those of 1 and 2, two pairs of CuII ions are held by two μ3-OH groups, two atrz ligands and four SIP anions into a tetranuclear CuII4 cluster with the intermetallic distances of 5.812(3), 3.380(3), 3.212(3) and 3.115(3) Å for Cu1B···Cu1C, Cu1B···Cu2, Cu1B···Cu2A and Cu2···Cu2A, respectively. The Cu–N bond lengths are 1.970(3) (Cu1B–N3B) and 2.026(3) (Cu2–N4B) Å, and the Cu–O bond lengths are in the range of 1.938(2)–2.521(3) Å (Table S3), which are similar to those of 1 and 2.

In 3, each SIP anion connects three CuII4 clusters to form a 2D (3,6) network structure along the bc plane (Fig. 6a). In addition, there are two oxygen atoms from the sulfo group of SIP anion to coordinate with the CuII ions. One is used to construct the 2D network along the bc plane, and the other links the adjacent 2D networks to generate a 3D framework structure along the ac plane (Fig. 6b). If the SIP anion is considered as a four-connected node, and the CuII4 cluster as a eight-connected node, complex 3 displays a 3D (4,8)-connected topological structure (Fig. 7) with a (410·614·84)(45·6)2 Schläfli symbol analyzed by TOPOS software [11]. The SIP anion only adopts one type of coordination mode (Scheme S1) in 3, which has not been observed in the reported SIP metal complexes, to our knowledge.

3.3. Effect of aromatic carboxylate anions on the structures of 1–3

In the syntheses of 1–3, atrz was used as the main ligand and three type of aromatic carboxylate anions as the auxiliary ligands, aiming at exploring the effect of different aromatic carboxylate anions on the assembly and structures of 1–3. According to the above structural descriptions, atrz uniformly behaves as the bidentate-bridging ligand to construct the CuII4 cluster in 1–3. For 1, the DNBA anion only has a coordination site of carboxyl group, which leads to the zero-dimensional CuII4 cluster structure of 1. In order to extend the dimension, the 1,3-BDC anion with two carboxyl groups was employed in the synthesis of 2, and a 3D NaCl-type framework was obtained, which indicates that the number of coordination site plays an important role in the 3D structure of 2. When the H atom in meta-position of 1,3-BDC anion is replaced by the sulfo group, a 3D (4,8)-connected framework of 3 was constructed, which further shows that the number of coordination sites and different substituent have a great effect on the structures of 2 and 3. Thus, the assembly and structures of 1–3 are mainly influenced by the aromatic carboxylates with different non-carboxyl substituent groups (Scheme 2). These results, to some extent, are similar to the previous literatures [4] containing the CuII4-based complexes constructed from the aminotriazole and carboxylate ligands, which reported the effect of number, position and deprotonation of the carboxyl groups for aromatic multicarboxylates on the assembly and structures.

3.4. Thermogravimetric analyses and X-ray powder diffraction

Thermogravimetric (TG) analyses of 1–3 were performed under N2 atmosphere with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 in the temperature range of 18–800 °C (Fig. S2). The TG curve of 1 only shows one weight loss step from 299 to 704 °C, which can be ascribed to the loss of organic ligands (Found 81.07%; Calcd. 81.43%). However, the TG curves of 2 and 3 show two weight loss steps. The first weight loss stages start at 70 °C up to 186 °C for 2, 71 °C up to 254 °C for 3, respectively, which correspond to the release of the water molecules (Found 3.65% for 2, 6.63% for 3; Calcd. 3.66% for 2, 7.10% for 3). The second weight losses occurring from 319 to 615 °C for 2, 347 to 629 °C for 3, respectively, suggest the decomposition of organic ligands (Found 63.42% for 2, 61.92% for 3; Calcd. 64.85% for 2, 61.37% for 3). The final products for 1–3 correspond to CuO. As shown in Fig. S3, the crystalline phase purities of 1–3 are evidenced by the similarity of simulated and experimental XRPD patterns. The as-synthesized patterns are in good agreement with the corresponding simulated ones, indicating the phase purities of samples.

3.5. Electrochemical property

Electrochemical properties of 1–3 were investigated in 1 M H2SO4 aqueous solution. The redox behaviors of the 1-CPE, 2-CPE (Fig. S4) and 3-CPE (Fig. S5) are similar except for some slight potential shifts. Thus, 1-CPE is described here as the representative example. Fig. 8 shows the cyclic voltammograms of the 1-CPE at different scan rates. In the potential range of +100 to +700 mV, a pair of reversible redox peaks attributed to the redox of CuII/CuI are observed at the cyclic voltammogram (100 mV s−1) of the 1-CPE, and the mean peak potential E1/2=(Epa+Epc)/2 is +358 mV [10], [12]. When the scan rates are varied from 60 to 450 mV s−1, the peak potentials gradually change: the cathodic peak potentials shift toward the negative direction, and the corresponding anodic peak potentials shift toward the positive direction. In addition, the anodic and cathodic peak currents are proportional to the scan rates, as shown in the inset of Fig. 8, which indicates the redox process of 1-CPE is surface-controlled.

3.6. Magnetic properties

The variable-temperature magnetic susceptibilities of 1–3 were measured in the temperature range of 2–300 K at an applied field of 1000 Oe, owing to the multiple superexchange pathways within the CuII4 cluster of 1–3. The χmT vs. T plot for 1 is shown in Fig. 9. The χmT value at 300 K is 2.26 emu mol−1 K, which is much higher than the theoretical value (1.50 emu mol−1 K) for four spin-only CuII (S=1/2, g=2) ions [13]. From 300 K to 2 K, χmT decreases gradually to a minimum of 0.01 emu mol−1 K, which suggests that the strong antiferromagnetic nearest-neighbor interactions within the CuII4 cluster of 1 lead to an S=0 ground state at low temperature. Such a spin ground state can be further confirmed by the nearly zero magnetization value of 1 even at 70 kOe (Fig. S6) [4a]. The χmT vs. T curve indicates that the dominant antiferromagnetic interactions between the paramagnetic centers exist in 1. The antiferromagnetic behavior should be originated from the superexchange interactions between Cu1 and Cu2 by the mixed μ-κ2N1:N2-atrz and μ3-OH pathways, as well as between Cu1 and Cu2A by the mixed syn,syn-μ2-η1:η1-COO−, μ2-η2:η0-COO− and μ3-OH pathways [4], [4]. In contrast, the superexchange interactions between Cu1 and Cu1A by the double μ3-OH pathways often result in a weak ferromagnetic coupling [4], [4], [14]. In addition, the sharp increase of χm for 1 below 50 K (Fig. 9) implies the presence of a paramagnetic impurity, as is usually observed in the antiferromagnetic clusters [4], [15]. The magnetic behaviors of 2 and 3 are similar to that of 1. The χmT vs. T plots for 2 and 3 are shown in Fig. 10 and Fig. 11. At 300 K, the χmT values are 1.19 emu mol−1 K for 2 and 0.79 emu mol−1 K for 3, which are much lower than the expected 1.50 emu mol−1 K for four magnetically isolated CuII ions, suggesting the very strong antiferromagnetic couplings between the paramagnetic ions even at room temperature [4d]. Upon cooling, the χmT products gradually decrease to a minimum of 0.01 emu mol−1 K for 2 and 3. The M versus H plots of 2 and 3 are shown in Fig. S7 and Fig. S8, which are similar to that of 1. Similar to 1, the χm values show a sharp increase for 2 below 70 K (Fig. 10) and for 3 below 100 K (Fig. 11).

4. Conclusion

In summary, three new tetranuclear copper(II) cluster-based complexes constructed from the atrz ligand and three types of aromatic carboxylate anions have been hydrothermally synthesized and structurally characterized. The results indicate that the atrz ligand plays an important role in the formation of tetranuclear copper(II) cluster, and the different aromatic carboxylate anions dominate the final structures of 1–3. The complexes 1–3 exhibit the good electrochemical and magnetic properties.
