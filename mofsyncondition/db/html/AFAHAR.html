Synthesis and characterization of binuclear rhenium(I) complexes containing bifunctional ligands

Abstract

The ligand exchange reaction of the anionic binuclear rhenium complexes Re2(CO)6(OR)3- (R = H (1), or Me (2)) has been studied with the bifunctional ligands 2-aminophenol (3), 4-hydroxypyridine (4), 3-hydroxybenzoic acid (5), and 3-pyridylcarbinol (6). The reactivity the pendant pyridyl group of 6 was studied in reactions with the Lewis acids ZnCl2 (7), and AgPF6 (8). Crystal structure determinations for several of these derivatives have been carried out which reveal both discrete and polymeric complexes upon addition of the Lewis base.

Graphical abstract

Several derivatives of Re2(CO)6 have been synthesized that contain bifunctional ligands. The reactivity of the these ligands toward additional Re fragments and the Lewis acids Zn(II) and Ag(I) have been explored.

1. Introduction

Rhenium(I) tricarbonyl complexes with ligand containing hydroxy –OH and carboxylate –COOH groups, or hydroxy –OH and an aromatic N donor atom comprise an interesting class of complexes that have the potential to exhibit medicinal (e.g. radiopharmaceutical) applications [1], [2], [3], [4], [5]. In the design of these rhenium containing complexes, the chemistry of [Re(CO)3]+ moiety is attracting significant activity, higher oxidation states of the metal are commonly employed, but the lower +1 state is receiving increasing attention [6], [7], [8], [9], [10], [11].

In our previous reports, we had described the reactions of the dirhenium(I) tricarbonyl complexes with aryloxide, alkoxide, carboxylate, and dicarboxylate type of ligands [12], [13]. We are now examining the reaction of [Et4N][Re2(CO)6(OR)3] (R = H (1), R = CH3 (2)) towards various bifunctional ligands such as hydroxy pyridines and phenol carboxylic acids in order to assess the stability and reactivity of the [Re2]+2 core to ligands of this type that have the potential to be used to incorporate the dimetal unit, and others of similar ilk, into mixed metal assemblies. We also report the interaction between the aromatic N atom of the hydroxy pyridine derivatives with Lewis acids, leading to the formation of discrete and polymeric structures depending on the ratio and acid type.

2. Experimental

2.1. General considerations

All reactions were carried out under air free conditions in an Argon filled glove box, or using standard Schlenk techniques. THF, diethyl ether, and hexane were freshly distilled from sodium/benzophenone ketyl, acetonitrile was freshly distilled from calcium hydride. Infrared spectra were recorded on a ThermoMattson IR300 spectrometer. 1H and 13C NMR spectra were recorded on a Bruker 300 MHz NMR spectrometer and referenced to the residual solvent peaks.

2.2. Syntheses

The compounds [Et4N][Re2(CO)6(OH)3] (1) and [Et4N][Re2(CO)6(OCH3)3] (2) were synthesized following published procedures [14]. Re2(CO)10 was purchased from Pressure Chemical. All other reagents were purchased from Aldrich Chemical and used as received.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. General characterizations

3.1.1. Synthesis and IR spectra

The complexes [Et4N][Re2(CO)6(OR)3] (R = H or CH3), have been shown to undergo ligand substitution in the presence of alcohols and carboxylic acids to afford novel complexes with a pendant functionality. The reaction of [Et4N][Re2(CO)6(OR)3] (R = H or CH3) with 2-hydroxypyridine, 4-hydroxypyridine, 3-hydroxybenzoic acid, 3-pyridyl carbinol and 3-pyridyl carbinol derivatives is the focus of this paper. No significant difference in the ligand exchange rate is observed when using different types of alcohols, however, when using carboxylate derivatives instead of alkoxides faster exchange reactions were observed.

The syntheses of complexes 3–8 were achieved by the ligand exchange reaction of complex 1 or 2 with the corresponding ligand in acetonitrile for complexes 3 and 6–8, and in refluxing ethanol for complex 4–5, for 10–12 h. The substitution of the hydroxide or methoxide ligand in complexes 1 and 2 for the new alkoxide or carboxylate ligand is indicated by a shift to higher wavenumbers in the IR spectrum for the C–O stretches of the carbonyl groups. The infrared data collected for all complexes is presented in Table 1.

The reaction of 2-hydroxypyridine with 1 gives as a product, 3 in which one hydroxide is replaced by 2-hydroxypyridonate in a bridging rather than chelating mode. The analogous methoxide derivative has been previously published.[15] Attempts at replacing additional hydroxide bridges by this ligand were unsuccessful. All efforts to obtain the 1:2 and 1:3 derivatives always resulted in 3 as the final product. The infrared spectrum of 3 presents two vibrational sets in the carbonyl region, both corresponding to the C3v symmetry around the rhenium metals which have different environment around them, one rhenium is surrounded by oxygens and carbonyls and the other is interacting with two bridging oxygens, one nitrogen, and three carbonyls. There are observed three signals at 2008, 1993 and 1874 cm−1, the last one being an overlapping of the E vibration.

In the case of complex 4, an interesting structure was obtained; it was expected that the 4-hydroxypyridine ligand would interact with the rhenium metals through the oxygen atom in a bridging fashion; however the observed interaction is between the nitrogen and the metal centers. In this case there is only one substitution of a hydroxide bridge. The complex is connected by hydrogen bonding to adjacent dirhenium units. The infrared spectrum of this complex consists of four bands at 2011, 1999, 1885, and 1870 cm−1.

Complex 5 was prepared by refluxing [Et4N][Re2(CO)6(OR)3] (R = H or CH3) with 0.5 equiv. of 3- hydroxybenzoic acid in ethanol for 12 h with 75% isolated yield. The infrared data corresponding to complex 5 showed a three bands which is attributed to the presence of two vibrational sets of A1 + E with the latter signal overlapped. The peak at 2009 cm−1, is attributed to the stretching of the three carbonyls bound to the rhenium-carboxylate side, while the 1997 cm−1 is the signal for the carbonyls associated to the metal-alkoxide part of the molecule. The broad signal that appears at 1886 cm−1 is assigned to the overlapping of the E bands.

With complex 6, new functionality in the form of a pyridyl nitrogen is introduced into the triply bridged complex [Et4N][Re2(CO)6(OH)3] generating additional reactivity. Reactions were carried out to study the possibility of using these complexes as building blocks in the synthesis of supramolecular assemblies.

Our efforts were directed to the reaction between the pyridyl-containing derivate 6, and the Lewis acids ZnCl2 and Ag+. The formation of a new complex, with ZnCl2 was confirmed by a shift in the carbonyl frequencies in the IR spectrum. The carbonyl peaks shift from 1994 and 1877 cm−1 to 2017, 1999, and 1902 cm−1 for the new compound.

The yielded adduct, a white solid, is insoluble after precipitation with ether, making characterization difficult. Despite this, we were able to grow crystals suitable for X-ray analysis and NMR studies; elemental analysis was hindered due to 7 being air sensitive. The complex has two rhenium (+1) and two ZnCl3- centers isolated from each other.

The reaction with the silver salt leads to the formation of complex 8. In this case, the crystals obtained showed the presence of three pyridyl carbinolate ligands instead of two as observed for complex 6. This is the only example in which we were able to observe complete substitution at the Re centers. The shift in the carbonyl region is not as pronounced as the one observed for complex 7, from 1994 and 1877 cm−1 to 1996 and 1879 cm−1. The yield obtained for the reaction is not as high as expected, only 31%, due to the fact of redox products also formed resulting in metallic silver. Elemental analysis for this compound was not possible; however, crystals of X-ray quality were obtained.

3.1.2. NMR spectra

The NMR spectra of these complexes are consistent with the molecular structure determined by X-ray crystallography. In the 1H spectrum of each of the compounds, a proper integration was attained versus the protons of the Et4N+ cation.

For complex 3, the 1H NMR spectrum shows the aromatic protons of the 2-hydroxypyridyl ligand as a set of doublet, triplet, doublet and triplet between 8.16 and 6.21 ppm, in a ratio 1:1:1:1 ratio. The 13C spectrum shows the metal carbonyl signal at 202.53 ppm, and the C(ring)–O of the ligand is at 171.70 ppm. The remaining four ring carbon atoms have single resonances from 152.82 to 109.83 ppm in the proper ratio.

The 1H NMR spectrum of 4 consists of a set of doublets at 7.74 and 6.33 ppm associated with the ring protons of the 4-hydroxypyridonate ligands. The corresponding peaks associated with the ethoxy groups show at 4.41 and 1.46 ppm with the expected quartet–triplet pattern. The 13C spectrum for the 4-hydroxypyridine derivative shows one resonance for the metal carbonyl carbons at 200.02 ppm and a series of three peaks between 175 and 140 ppm for the ring carbons of the bridging ligand.

In the 1H NMR spectrum for complex 5, the protons from the benzene rings appear as one singlet, two doublets, and one triplet between 8.15 and 7.21 ppm in a ratio of 1:1:1:1. The asymmetry of the molecule is displayed by the splitting of the methylene proton peak at 4.31 and 4.20 ppm. The signal corresponding to the methyl of the ethoxide groups appears as a triplet at 1.36 ppm. By observing the 13C spectrum of complex 5, we noticed the asymmetry present in the complex; there are two signals corresponding to the metal carbonyl peaks at 201.45 and 201.14 ppm. The carbonyl signal of the carboxylate ligand is at 172.40 ppm. The signal associated to the C(ring)–O is 166.14 ppm. The remaining carbons present in the ring are between 138.63 to 120.23 ppm. The hydroxide and carboxylate peaks showed at 76.83 and 72.90 ppm, and the methyl carbons appear at 21.03 and 19.98 ppm.

In the 1H NMR spectrum for complex 6 the proton from the pyridine ring appears as three doublets, and one multiplet, in a 2:2:2:2 ratio, between 8.69 and 7.64 ppm, and the proton signal for the methylene is a singlet at 5.32 ppm. The metal carbonyl carbons appear at 201.48 ppm in the 13C NMR spectrum, and the OCH2 appears as a singlet signal at 77.11 ppm. The carbons of the pyridine ring have a set of five peaks between 149.2 and 123.84 ppm.

The 1H NMR spectrum for complex 7 is similar to the one observed for complex 6. The interaction of zinc with the pyridine nitrogen caused a shift in the proton signals, between 8.79 and 7.65 ppm, with a singlet, doublet, doublet, multiplet pattern, in the same ratio as 7. Also, it is observed that the methylene shifted about 0.5 ppm upfield in the NMR scale when comparing with the precursor complex 6, 4.78 ppm. In the 13C NMR spectrum, the carbonyl carbons are at 201.18 ppm, and the ring carbons appear at 148.81, 147.52, 139.72, 135.04, and 122.98 ppm. The carbon corresponding to the methylene is slightly upfield from 6 at 76.98 ppm.

Complex 8 presents the pattern expected for the pyridylcarbinolate ligand, the peaks corresponding to the aromatic protons showed between 8.69 and 7.34 ppm, meanwhile the peaks corresponding to the methylene appear at 5.32 ppm. The 13C NMR spectrum showed that the carbonyl carbons are at 200.38 ppm. The associated signal to the ring carbons appear between 148.38 and 123.04 ppm, and the methylene is at 76.51 ppm. All the signals, as expected, are shifted up field due to the interaction between the metal, silver, and the ligand.

3.2.2. Structure of complex 4

Complex 4 presents a mixed ligand system with two hydroxide bridging the Re2(CO)6 core and two 4-hydroxypyridine ligands bound to the rhenium centers through the nitrogen atom instead of the oxygen, one of the ligands is deprotonated while the other still has its proton. Two unique complexes are present in the asymmetric unit and there are two Et4N+ cations balancing the charge. A thermal ellipsoid plot of this complex is shown in Fig. 2, and select bond distances and angles are given in Table 4. The pendent OH group from one 4-hydroxypyridine ligand is hydrogen bonded to an adjacent molecule with an O–O distance of 2.433(3) Å, this forms a one-dimensional hydrogen bonded network down the c axis. The Re1–Re2 distance in 4 is 3.349(4) Å and the Re–N average distance is 2.20 Å, similar to the value observed for rhenium(I) pyridine complexes [16], [17], [18].

3.2.3. Structure of complex 5

A thermal ellipsoid drawing of 5 is shown in Fig. 3, and selected bond lengths and angles are given in Table 5. Complex 5 contains a tetrametallic core or dimer of dimers with the Re2(CO)6 moieties held together by a bridging aryloxide on one side and a carboxylate on the other. The Re(CO)3 units are held together by two bridging ethoxide groups. The alkoxy functionality is bridging in its normal way of μ2, meanwhile, the carboxylate is bridging with the syn–syn type geometry, the same geometry observed for the carboxylate derivatives reported in previous work.[13] The Re–Re nonbonding distances are quite different as expected. The distance on the side that is bridged by the hydroxide is 3.1347(4) Å, while, the distance for the carboxylate side is 3.3951(5) Å; the dirhenium units are spanned by the ligand to a distance of 8.680(3) Å. The average Re–O(alkoxide) distance is 2.134 Å, which is similar to the one observed for the Re–O(carboxylate) of 2.136 Å. The average Re–O(ethoxy)–Re angle for the hydroxide side is 95.24°, which is smaller than the one observed for the carboxylate side of 106.25°.

3.2.4. Structure of complex 6

A thermal ellipsoid plot for complex 7 is provided in Fig. 4, and selected bond lengths and angles are provided in Table 6. The molecular structure of complex 6 consists a Re2(CO)6 unit with two opposite pyridylcarbinolate groups binding ZnCl3-. The sixth coordination site on the Re is taken by an acetonitrile solvent molecule. The overall complex is neutral with the Re2 core having a +2 charge that is balanced by the two ZnCl3- units. A crystallographic inversion center between the two Re atoms relates the two halves of the molecule. The tetrahedral geometry around the Zn is slightly distorted, as evidenced by the bond angles of N1–Zn–Cl (1, 2, and 3) (104.6°, 101.8°, and 111.4°).

The Re–Re nonbonding distance is 3.4666(6) Å, which is similar to the one observed for other dimethoxy-bridged rhenium complexes [19]. The vertex angle subtended by the Re2 unit is less than the ideal 90°, O(4)–Re(1)–O(4)#1 72.22°, however, it is similar to the one observed for dimethoxo-bridged tungsten derivatives [20]. The interesting feature about this complex is the transformation from a triply bridged Re–Re core to a doubly bridged one.

The coordination around the silver centers is an interesting feature of this molecule. Complex 8, consists of the coordination to Ag of three pyridylcarbinolate groups, which are bound to three different dirhenium centers, the fourth coordination site is occupied by an acetonitrile molecule. The geometry around the silver is a distorted tetrahedron. Interestingly the bond lengths around the Ag are not symmetric. Two are the same (N1–Ag 2.273 and N3–Ag 2.269 Å) while the others are considerably longer, N2–Ag 2.410, N4–Ag 2.433 Å. That the solvent acetonitrile–Ag distance is long is not unusual. What is unusual about the acetonitrile is the angle made to the Ag which is produces a Ag–N4–C25 angle of 147°, while there is no obvious steric reason for this large bend, it may be that due to the weak interaction with the Ag, it is able to maximize the space it occupies by bending over into the gap. The rhenium nonbonding distance is 3.1327 Å, appropriate for a triply bridged alkoxide [12]. A plot of the polymeric structure of 8 is presented in Fig. 6.

4. Conclusion

We have presented several new Re2(CO)62+ containing complexes that have incorporated additional functionality in the additional ligands. This functionality can be used to bridge to another Re2(CO)62+ or to bind to a Lewis base forming discrete and polymeric structures.
