Introduction

Recently, the investigation of molecular refrigerant materials has been a hotspot of inorganic chemistry and materials chemistry due to their potential application in cryogenic refrigeration.1 Among them, the study of GdIII-based coordination polymers (CPs) has attracted much more attention.2 On one hand, GdIII-based CPs including GdIII-based metal–organic frameworks usually have high flexibility in structural design and large magnetic densities.3 On the other hand, the large spin ground state S, Dion = 0 and low-lying excited spin states that exist in GdIII, help to enhance the refrigerant performance as described by the magnetocaloric effect (MCE).4 In addition, the magnetic entropy change (ΔSm) is an important parameter to evaluate the magnitude of the MCE, thus having a large ΔSm under a low magnetic field is essential for high-performance cryogenic magnetorefrigerants.5

As an important branch of GdIII-based CPs, chain-based structures have been well studied.6 A simple method to construct GdIII-based CPs with various chain structures is to choose suitable GdIII ion sources and multidentate ligands.7 Multidentate ligands with coordinating oxygen atoms such as sulfate, carboxylates and carbonate have been used extensively to prepare target GdIII-based CPs due to their excellent coordinating ability and variety of coordination modes, which is favorable for improving the MCE.8 Additionally, the magnetic interactions between GdIII ions conducted by multidentate O ligands are usually weak on account of the shielding of f orbitals.9

In this work, inorganic sulfate and organic dicarboxylates [oxydiacetic acid (H2oda) and glutaric acid (H2glu)] (see Scheme 1) have been used as primary ligands for the synthesis of GdIII-based magnetorefrigerants. To obtain a low-dimensional structure, the introduction of terminal ligands such as 1,10-phenanthroline and 2,2′-bipyridine is necessary.10 In addition, the use of long-chain-dicarboxylates is helpful for the construction of chain-based GdIII complexes.11 The synthesis, structures and magnetism of three GdIII CPs containing GdIII chains with the formulae of [Gd2(SO4)3(phen)2(H2O)2]n (1), [Gd2(oda)2(ox)(H2O)6]n (2) and {[Gd2(glu)3(H2O)2]·4H2O}n (3), have been studied in detail. Notably, complex 2 has been reported previously but its magnetocaloric properties have not been studied.12 These complexes add new members to GdIII complexes with significant MCEs.

Experimental

Materials and instrumentation

All starting materials were of analytical grade and obtained from commercial sources without further purification. IR spectra were measured on a Vector 22 (Bruker) FT-IR spectrometer with KBr pellets in the range 4000–400 cm−1. Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were carried out on a Rigaku standard TG-DTA analyzer (Thermo plus EVO2) under an air atmosphere with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1, using an empty Al2O3 crucible as a reference. The powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) spectra were recorded on an Empyrean (PANalytical B.V.) diffractometer for a Cu-target tube and a graphite monochromator. Simulation of the PXRD spectra was carried out by the single-crystal data and diffraction-crystal module of the Mercury (Hg) program available free of charge. About 20 mg samples of crushed micro crystals were used to measure PXRD patterns to confirm the phase purities, and further to measure the magnetic properties. Notably, the crushed crystalline sample was potted in wax to secure them. Magnetic data was collected using a Quantum Design MPMS superconducting quantum interference device (SQUID). Diamagnetic corrections were estimated by using Pascal constants and background corrections by experimental measurement on sample holders.

Synthesis of complexes

[Gd2(SO4)3(phen)2(H2O)2]n (1)

A mixture of Gd2(SO4)3 (0.25 mmol, 151 mg), pivalic acid (1 mmol, 102 mg), 1,10-phenanthroline monohydrate (phen, 0.25 mmol, 50 mg), and H2O (10 mL) was sealed in a 23 mL Teflon-lined autoclave and heated to 160 °C. After maintaining for 72 h, the reaction vessel was cooled to room temperature (RT) within 24 h. Colorless block crystals were collected with ∼40% yield based on GdIII. IR (KBr, cm−1): 3434m, 3103w, 1627w, 1584w, 1519m, 1422m, 1346w, 1300w, 1197s, 1129vs, 1059s, 998m, 845m, 776w, 726m, 664w, 634m, 592m, 504w, 460w, 429w (see Fig. S1a, ESI†).

[Gd2(oda)2(ox)(H2O)6]n (2)

A mixture of Cu(NO3)2·3H2O (2.5 mmol, 604 mg), Gd(NO3)3·3H2O (1 mmol, 451 mg), H2oda (2 mmol, 268 mg), and H2O (10 mL) was sealed in a 23 mL Teflon-lined autoclave and heated to 160 °C. After maintaining for 72 h, the reaction vessel was cooled to RT within 24 h. Colorless block crystals were collected with ∼45% yield based on GdIII. IR (KBr, cm−1): 3435m, 3255m, 2923w, 2361w, 1689s, 1596vs, 1434s, 1359w, 1310s, 1241w, 1123s, 1051m, 933w, 791w, 714m, 606m, 555m, 502w (see Fig. S1b, ESI†).

{[Gd2(glu)3(H2O)2]·4H2O}n (3)

H2glu (2 mmol, 264 mg) and Gd2O3 (0.5 mmol, 181 mg) were dissolved in 10 mL mixed solvent (Vwater : Vethanol = 4 : 1). The reaction mixture was then heated to 160 °C for 72 h sealed in a Teflon-lined stainless steel autoclave. Upon cooling to RT within 24 h, colorless strip crystals were obtained with a yield of ∼60% based on GdIII. IR (KBr, cm−1): 3590m, 3422m, 2958w, 2891w, 2360w, 1672m, 1543vs, 1457vs, 1414s, 1353m, 1274m, 1196w, 1151w, 1059m, 1010w, 966w, 926w, 880w, 816m, 755m, 700m, 653m, 543w, 477w (see Fig. S1c, ESI†).

Crystallographic data collection and refinement

X-ray single-crystal diffraction data were collected on a Bruker D8 QUEST diffractometer for complexes 1 and 3 under Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) by ω scan mode. The program SAINT was used for the integration of the diffraction profiles. The structures were solved using direct methods and refined by full-matrix least-squares methods using SHELXL-2016.13 Metal atoms were located from the E-maps and other non-hydrogen atoms were located in successive difference Fourier syntheses and refined with anisotropic thermal parameters on F2. The hydrogen atoms of the ligands were generated theoretically onto the specific atoms and refined isotropically with fixed thermal factors. The hydrogen atoms of water in 1 and 3 were added by the difference Fourier maps and refined with suitable constrains. The crystal of 1 was a non-merohedral twin with partially overlapping reflections, resulting in a high residual electron density (6.12 e Å−3). A summary of the crystal data and structural refinements for 1 and 3 is provided in Table 1. The selected bond lengths and angles are given in Tables S1 and S2 (ESI†).

Results and discussion

Syntheses

To obtain chain-based structures, two of the simplest and most effective methods are employed including the introduction of terminal ligands (or chelating ligands) and the selection of dicarboxyalte with a certain length. In a hydrothermal reaction, oxalate anions could be formed in situ when using dicarboxylates as organic ligands. In the synthesis, starting material copper nitrate did not appeared in 2, but related to the formation of this complex. Synthesis without copper nitrate has shown that we only obtained a clear solution and no single crystals of 2 were afforded for the structural analysis. One possible mechanism is that the copper(ii) ion formed coordination complexes with the flexible H2oda ligand as a structural template and constrained the configuration of the ligand, which favors the formation of products with an ordered structure. The synthetic methods of 1–3 are very different from those of the reported similar structures.12,14,15

Description of crystal structures

Similar structures of 1 and 3 have been reported in previous literature14,15 and complex 2 has been reported by another group,12 thus herein their structures are only discussed briefly. The one-dimensional (1D) structure of 1 contains inorganic Gd2(SO4)3 columnar chains decorated with phen ligands (see Fig. 1). Complex 1 crystallizes in the triclinic P group and the asymmetric unit of 1 consists of two GdIII ions, two SO42− anions, two phen ligands and three coordinated water molecules (O1W and O2W). Both Gd1 and Gd2 are nine-coordinated by six O atoms from five SO42− anions, two N atoms from a phen ligand and one water O atom. Gd1 and Gd2 are linked by SO42− anions and the terminal phen ligands prevent the formation of the high-dimensional structure. Therefore, a 1D chain structure is formed.

Complex 2 crystallizes as a monoclinic systems with the P21/c space group. Unlike 1, complex 2 exhibits a 1D double-chain ladder-shape structure, as shown in Fig. 2. The asymmetric unit consists of one GdIII ion, one oda2− anion, half an ox2− anion and three coordinated water molecules (O1W, O2W and O3W). Each GdIII ion is nine-coordinate with nine O donors, three from H2O, two from ox2− anions, and four from two different oda2− anions. The adjacent GdIII ions are connected by syn–anti carboxylate groups of oda2− anions to form a 1D chain. Two chains are further bridged by ox2− anions to obtain a double-chain ladder-shaped structure. Complex 3 forms a chain-based three-dimensional structure. It crystallizes in the monoclinic C2/c group and the asymmetric unit contains one GdIII ion (nine-coordinate), one and a half glu2− anions, one coordinated and two lattice water molecules (see Fig. 3a). The adjacent GdIII ions are linked by syn–syn-μ2-η1:η2 carboxylates of glu2− anions to form the 1D structure. As shown in Fig. 3b, the neighbouring 1D chains are further connected to form a chain-based 3D structure, which indicates that the use of long-chain-dicarboxylate is helpful for the construction of chain-based GdIII CPs.

TGAs and PXRD patterns

The thermal stability of 2 has been studied by the previous work, suggesting that the framework of 2 could be stable at about 370 °C. To investigate the thermal stabilities of 1 and 3, TGAs were carried out. The results suggest that no weight loss was observed up to 265 °C for 1 and 166 °C for 3, respectively (Fig. S2, ESI†). Additionally, the frameworks of 1 and 3 could be stable at ∼495 °C for 1 and 371 °C for 3. The PXRD patterns of 1–3 in a crushed crystalline state were performed to confirm their phase purities before magnetic measurements. As shown in Fig. S3 (ESI†), the coincidence of the experimental PXRD patterns at ambient temperature of 1–3 and the simulated ones confirms the phase purities of the samples. Notably, the peak differences in the intensity between the simulated and the experimental patterns may result from the preferred orientation of the lattice face.16

Magnetic properties

The magnetic properties of 1–3 were studied by solid state magnetic susceptibility measurements in the range of 2–300 K at a 1 kOe direct current field and isothermal field-dependent magnetizations M(H) at 2–10 K and 0–70 kOe. As shown in Fig. 4, the χmT values at room temperature (300 K) are 15.81, 15.79 and 15.68 cm3 mol−1 K, respectively, which agree with the theoretical value of 15.75 cm3 mol−1 K for two uncoupled GdIII ions (S = 7/2, g = 2). Upon cooling, the values of χmT remain almost unchanged above 50 K. Below 50 K, the χmT value decreases slowly to 14.80 and 14.72 cm3 mol−1 K for 1 and 2, respectively, suggesting antiferromagnetic coupling between the adjacent metal ions. While the χmT value of 3 rapidly increases at 34 K and reaches the maximum of 17.32 cm3 mol−1 K at 2 K, indicating the existence of the ferromagnetic coupling between neighbouring GdIII ions.

As shown in Fig. 4 and Table 2, the Curie–Weiss fitting of magnetic susceptibility data leads to the corresponding parameters. The Weiss constants are consistent with the above descriptions, suggesting weak magnetic couplings exist in all of them. The magnetization rapidly increases and the M values at 7 T and 2 K are 14.03, 14.26 and 13.91 Nβ, respectively, which are similar to those of other reported GdIII complexes (Fig. S4, ESI†). Moreover, the M vs. H curves could be well described by the Brillouin function with the parameters derived from the static susceptibility data, which confirms the weak interactions between the GdIII ions.

On account of the weak interactions and relatively high magnetic densities of 1–3, ΔSm was used to evaluate the MCEs of these three complexes. ΔSm can be calculated by employing the Maxwell equation . According to this equation, the −ΔSm values of 1–3 were obtained from the experimental magnetization data (Fig. S5, ESI†), and the plots of −ΔSm at different fields and temperatures are shown in Fig. 5. For 2, from 10 K to 2 K, the −ΔSm values slowly increase with the increase of the applied field. While for 1 and 3, the −ΔSm values follow a trend of increasing first then falling below 6 K and then increase in the range of high magnetic dc fields. −ΔSm per unit of mass reach the maximum values of 31.7, 44.5 and 36.0 J kg−1 K−1 for a field change ΔH = 7 T at 2 K, which are smaller than the theoretical values (34.6, 44.6 and 42.5 J kg−1 K−1) based on 2R ln(2S + 1), due to the existence of weak interactions. They are also relatively large values among the reported molecular cryogenic magnetorefrigerants (Table 3). Notably, the maximum entropy changes (−ΔS maxm) of 1–3 reach 24.8, 36.0 and 28.1 J kg−1 K−1 at 2 K for ΔH = 3 T, and 19.8, 29.9 and 22.7 J kg−1 K−1 at 2 K for ΔH = 2 T, respectively. In addition, the volumetric −ΔS maxm values are 74.1, 119.7 and 83.2 mJ cm−3 K−1 for ΔH = 7 T and T = 2 K, which are also relatively large among the molecular magnetorefrigerants, especially 2. From Table 3, we can safely assume that weaker interaction, higher magnetic density and higher physical density are propitious to enhance the MCEs described by mass −ΔS maxm and volumetric −ΔS maxm values, and most of the GdIII CPs exhibit relatively good thermal stabilities with decomposition temperatures higher than 320 °C.

Conclusions

By using three multidentate O ligands including inorganic sulfate and organic dicarboxylates, three 1D/3D chain-based GdIII complexes have been successfully prepared via hydro/solvothermal reactions. Weak magnetic interactions between adjacent GdIII ions exist in all of them, which is helpful for the enhancement of MCEs. In light of their good thermal stabilities and large MCEs, all of them could be considered as attractive magnetorefrigerants. Further studies of more GdIII CPs derived from multidentate O ligands, especially for Gd-based metal–organic frameworks, are underway in our group.
