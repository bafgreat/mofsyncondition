Mixed ligand chelates of copper(II) with substituted diamines

Author links open overlay panel
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.poly.2005.04.002Get rights and content

Abstract

The copper complexes, oxalato(N,N,N′N′-tetramethylethylenediamine)copper(II) tetrahydrate (1), oxalato(N,N,N′-trimethyl-1,3-propanediamine)copper(II) (2), and oxalato(N,N,N′-trimethylethylenediamine)copper(II) (3), have been synthesized and structurally characterised. Compounds 1 and 2 share a common zig-zag polymeric structure in which the oxalates are bridging as well as chelating. The copper is in a distorted octahedral position in both compounds. Compound 3 is monomeric and the oxalate is chelating only. The copper is in a distorted square planar position. Diffuse reflectance spectroscopy and ESR spectroscopy showed a clear difference between the two types of structures.

1. Introduction

The mixed ligand systems based on copper(II) salts form stable complexes of approximately square planar geometry and such complexes have been extensively studied [1]. Copper(II) ion forms stable ternary complexes with π-acceptor ligands such as 2,2′-bipyridyl (bipy) ligands and oxalate (C2O42-) ions [2], [3]. It has been shown that this complex is subject to polymorphism indicated by colour changes as well as changes in the electronic and ESR spectra.

Stone et al. [2] have prepared [Cu(bipy)(O4C2)] · 2H2O which turned green and then finally dehydrated to give an anhydrous compound; [Cu(bipy)2] [Cu(O4C2)2]. Sigel et al. [1] and Hathaway and co-workers [3] have prepared a blue-green anhydrous mixed ligand complex by a different method but no solid-state reactivity was observed. There have also been several studies of mixed ligand Cu(II) bipy complexes with carboxylic or amino-acids [4], [5], [6], [7].

The copper chelates with diamines have proven to be active catalysts for the hydrolysis of phosphate esters [8]. The stability of 1:1 copper(II) chelate complexes is controlled by the tendency to disproportionate to 2:1 chelate and copper hydroxide. Since the copper(II) chelate complexes are square planar, the substitution of alkyl groups in place of the amino groups should help the disproportionation reaction by steric repulsion. It has been reported that the soluble 1:1 copper(II) chelate complexes of TMEDA and N,N′-dimethylethylenediamine (DMEDA) did not disproportionate in aqueous solutions at pH 7 and above, but 1:1 copper(II) chelate complex of ethylenediamine was not stable, and did convert to 2:1 chelate complex and copper hydroxide [9].

However, Marjit and Sharma [10] have reported that 1:1 complexes are not only difficult to isolate but if isolated, their stability was low in the reaction medium. The copper(II) nitrate–TMEDA 1:1 complex became turbid in aqueous solution due to disproportionation reaction and form a more stable 1:2 complex. Previously, it has been shown that dimers of the chelate complexes tend to show negligible catalytic activity [11]. In order to better define the coordination chemistry of substituted diamines with copper(II), mixed ligand complexes of oxalate and diamines have been synthesized and discussed.

2. Experimental

CuSO4, K2(C2O4) and diamines were used as purchased from Aldrich company Limited. Cu(C2O4) · 1/2H2O was prepared according to the reported method [12].

2.1. Preparation of Cu(TMEDA)oxalate · 4H2O (1)

To a stirred solution of TMEDA (0.48 ml, 3.2 mmol) in water (2 ml), finely powdered Cu(C2O4) · 1/2H2O (0.25 g, 1.6 mmol) was added. Unreacted copper oxalate was removed by filtration. Ethanol (2 ml) was added to the filtrate and the solution was allowed to stand. After several days, a mixture of colourless and blue crystals formed. The blue crystals of 1 were separated for analysis; m.p. 232 °C; yield, 79%. Microanal. Calc. for CuC8H22O8N2: C, 28.3, H, 7.1, N, 8.2. Found: C, 28.3, H, 7.3, N, 8.1%. IR bands: 3407 (s), 2926 (m), 1673 (s), 1046 (m), 520 (m). UV/Vis bands: λmax = 15.0 × 103 cm−1 with a shoulder at 9.8 × 103 cm−1.

2.2. Preparation of Cu(TriMPDA)oxalate (2)

To a stirred solution of N,N,N′-trimethyl-1,3-propanediamine (0.37 ml, 1.6 mmol) in water (4 ml) was added Cu(C2O4) · 1/2H2O (0.25 g, 6.4 mmol). The excess copper oxalate was removed by filtration. The addition of ethanol immediately precipitated a blue solid which was dissolved in water (2 ml) and allowed to stand for crystallization. After several days blue crystals of 2 were formed which were separated by filtration, dried, m.p. 240 °C; yield, 68%. Microanal. Calc. for CuC8H16O4N2: C, 35.9, H, 6.0, N, 10.5. Found: C, 35.8, H, 5.8, N, 10.6%. IR bands: 3443 (m), 2930 (m), 1619 (s), 1044 (m), 495 (m). UV/Vis bands: λmax = 13.6 × 103 cm−1 with a shoulder at 9.6 × 103 cm−1.

2.3. Preparation of Cu(TriMEDA)oxalate (3)

To a stirred solution of Cu(C2O4) · 1/2H2O (0.5 g, 3.2 mmol) in ethanol (5 ml) and water (5 ml), was added N,N,N′-trimethylethylenediamine (0.81 ml, 6.4 mmol). The resulting solution was allowed to stand. Blue/violet crystals of 3 were grown after a few days; m.p. >240 °C; yield, 77%. Microanal. Calc. for CuC7H14O4N2: C, 33.1, H, 5.5, N, 11.0. Found: C, 33.2, H, 5.4, N, 11.4%. IR bands: 3424 (m), 2927 (m), 1619 (s), 1055 (m), 790 (s), 490 (m). UV bands: λmax = 18.4 × 103 cm−1.

2.4. Physical measurements

Infrared spectra were recorded on a Perkin–Elmer FT-IR 1720X spectrometer in the range 4000–200 cm−1. Samples were examined as nujol mulls between caesium iodide plates or as potassium bromide discs. Melting points were determined using a Gallenkamp melting-point apparatus. A Philips PU 8710 spectrophotometer was used to carry out the optical measurements, the samples were placed in silica cuvettes (1 cm path length). Elemental analyses were obtained from the microanalytical laboratory at University College London. Single-crystal X-ray diffraction data for the compounds were collected with a CAD-4 diffractometer and Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71069 Å) using ω–2θ scan. The unit cell parameters were determined by least-squares refinement on diffractometer angles (9.99° ⩽ θ ⩽ 12.51°) for 25 automatically centered reflections. The structures were solved by heavy-atom method using dirdif-99 programs, refined anisotropically (non-hydrogen atoms) by full-matrix least-squares on F2 using shelxl-97 program [13]. Crystallographic details and selected interatomic distances and angles are given in Table 1, Table 2, Table 3, Table 4.

3. Results and discussion

The compounds have been characterized by IR, UV, elemental analysis and X-ray single crystallography. All three compounds have ratios of 1:1:1 for the copper, oxalate and diamine ligand but the common structure of compounds 1, 2 is not shared by compound 3.

Both compounds 1 and 2 have a six coordinate CuN2O2O2′ chromophore with an elongated, rhombic, octahedral structure. The neutral Cu(L)ox units where L = diamine and ox = oxalate, involve bridging oxalate anions which gives a zig-zag polymeric chain structure. In 1, the water molecules are not coordinated to the copper atom but are present as lattice water (Fig. 1). The water molecules are hydrogen bonded to each other and to oxygen atoms, O(2) and O(4), of the oxalate groups (Fig. 2). In the IR spectrum of compound 1, ν(OH) has a single broad band at 3407 cm−1, indicating hydrogen bonding to other water molecules and to the oxygen of the bridging oxalate group [14]. In 2, hydrogen bonding between H(1) of the secondary amine group and the oxygen O(4) of the oxalate group within the chain, at a distance of 2.323 Å, has been suggested. Compound 2 contains no water. Generally, the diamine ligand is coordinated to the metal and the two oxalate ligands are asymmetrically coordinated with one long Cu–O bond and one short Cu–O bond (Fig. 3).

Table 2 shows selected bond lengths and angles in compound 1. Compound 1 has a symmetrically coordinated TMEDA (mean Cu–N 2.052 Å) and two asymmetrically coordinated bridging oxalate (Cu–O (1.999–2.383 Å)) that are centrosymmetric (Fig. 1). Cu–O bond lengths are comparable to those reported (1.940–2.345 Å) for [Cu(DMEDA)ox(H2O)]2 [Cu(DMEDA)2](ClO4)2 [15]. Compound 2 has an asymmetrically coordinated TriMPDA (Cu–N(1) 2.048 Å and Cu–N(2) 2.093 Å), the shorter bond being to the least substituted nitrogen N(1) and two asymmetrically coordinated bridging oxalate groups (shorter bonds Cu–O(1) 2.017 Å, Cu–O(3) 1.978 Å and longer bonds Cu–O(2) 2.379 Å, Cu–O (4) 2.396 Å) (Fig. 3). The oxalate groups are involved in an asymmetric bridging role between copper atoms. Selected bond lengths and angles of compound 2 are shown in Table 3.

The mean O–C–O angles within the oxalate in complex 1 is 125.8° and in complex 2 125.9° [16], [17], [18]. Similarly, the bond lengths and angles associated with TMEDA are normal for such complexes [19]. In complex 2, the six-membered ring Cu–N(1)–C(3)–C(4)–C(5)–N(2)– is in a chair conformation. The mean C–N bond length, (1.49 Å) in complex 2 is noticeably longer than the average C–N bond length of (1.47 Å). The mean C–N bond length of complex 1 is 1.481 Å. This difference is attributed to the increase in the size of ring from five- in 1 to six-membered in 2 [20].

The bite angle of TMEDA at copper is 86.1° which compares well with that observed in (Cu(TMEDA)Cl2)2, where the angle is 85.3° [19]. The bite angles of the two oxalate groups in compound 1 are 75.48° and 76.66°, which are significantly different to each other. These angles compare well with those observed in 2 and in Cu(bipy)ox · 2H2O, where the oxalate bite angles are 77.4° and 77.9° [12]. The bite angle of TriMPDA at the copper center is 91.33° and those of the oxalate groups in compound 2 are 74.67° and 76.05° which are similar to those reported elsewhere [21].

In compound 1, the N(1), N(2), O(1), O(2) atoms are distorted from a planar arrangement. The O(2)–Cu–O(4) angle of 156.90° is significantly reduced from 180°. Previously, this distortion has been attributed to the small bite angle of the oxalate groups [12]. In compound 2, the N(1), N(2), O(1), O(3) atoms are again distorted from a planar arrangement. The O(2)–Cu–O(4) angle of 168.05° is reduced from 180° but not to the same extent as in 1.

In compound 3, the copper is described as square planar with two nitrogen atoms from the diamine and two oxygen atoms from the oxalate (Fig. 4). Table 4 shows selected bond lengths and angles of compound 3. The copper deviates slightly out of the O(2), O(3), N(1), N(2) plane by ca. 0.031 Å. The TriMEDA is almost symmetrically coordinated with the bond lengths for Cu–N(1) and Cu–N(2) being 2.001 and 1.952 Å and the bite angle is 86.95°. This compares well with the reported bite angle of 86.4° for the diamine ligand in oxalato(N,N′-dimethylethylenediamine)copper(II) monohydrate [22]. This shortening of the bond to the least substituted nitrogen has been noted previously [23], [24]. The C(4)–N(1)–Cu(1) angle of 105.3° and C(3)–N(2)–Cu(1) of 109.5°, reflect the substitution on the nitrogen, with the largest angle at the least substituted nitrogen. The oxalate group is almost symmetrically coordinated to the copper through two oxygen atoms with Cu–O bond lengths of 1.922 and 1.948 Å in 1,2-cis-conformation. This contrasts with the asymmetry of the oxalate groups observed in compounds 1 and 2. Within the oxalate group, the C–C bond length of 1.555 Å and the average C–O bond length of 1.247 Å are in accord with those previously reported [21]. Two long and two short bond lengths have been observed. This reflects the difference between those that are chelating (mean C–O length 1.274 Å) and those that are terminal (mean C–O 1.218 Å). The O–C–O angles are unequal. The O(1)–C(2)–O(3) moiety, where only one of the oxygen interacts with a copper, has an angle of 127.0°. This agrees well with the value of 126–128° observed in the free ion and oxalic acid [16], [17], [18]. In the O(4)–C(2)–O(1) moiety, the angle is reduced to 124.0°. Both oxygen atoms are involved in interactions, O(2) with Cu and O(4) in hydrogen bonding. The bite angle of the oxalate group is 84.53°. This compares well with the value of 84.2°, reported for oxalate(N,N′-dimethylethylenediamine)copper(II) [23]. The Cu(L)(C2O4) units are linked together by hydrogen bonding between the hydrogen H(1) bonded to N(1) and an oxygen, O(4), of an oxalate group in a neighbouring unit to from winding chains (Fig. 5).

4. Electronic properties

The diffuse reflectance spectra of complexes 1, 2 and 3 were recorded between 8000 and 50 000 cm−1. The compounds 1 and 2 have a main band with a shoulder and compound 3 have a single broad band.

The reflectance spectra of 1 had previously been reported [25]. Fukuda et al. recorded the spectra between 10 000 and 29 400 cm−1. They reported a band at λmax = 14 030 cm−1. However, they failed to report a shoulder at this main band at lower frequency because their scan range was to too narrow.

Complex 1 has a main band at λmax = 15 × 103 cm−1 with a shoulder at 9.8 × 103 cm−1. Similarly, the spectrum of complex 2 has a main band at λ max = 13.6 × 103 cm−1 with a shoulder at 9.6 × 103 cm−1. These two-band spectra are common to the compounds that are isostructural. The spectrum of complex 3 has a single broad absorption at 18.4 × 103 cm−1.

This sensitivity of the electronic spectra to the two modes of coordination has been previously reported in compounds of type Cu(L)(C2O4) · xH2O where L = bipy and 1,10-phen [12], [26], [27]. The first modes contains an axially elongated N2O4 chromophore consisting of one bipy and two catena bridging oxalates which is comparable to complex 1 containing TMEDA and complex 2 containing TriMPDA. The second mode has an N2O2 chromophore of an oxalate and one bipy in a square plane at copper(II) which is comparable to complex 3 with TriMEDA. It can be concluded that the observation of two bands is consistent with the elongated tetragonal distortion seen in complexes 1 and 2. The electronic broad band for complex 3 is consistent with an N2O2, ca. square planar chromophore.

The electronic reflectance spectra of complexes 1 and 2 are clearly different from complex 3. This difference is associated with the local molecular structure of the complex. This local structure is shared by complexes 1 and 2 but not complex 3 and is best seen by the presence of the low energy shoulder in 1 and 2.

The compounds were also analyzed by electron spinning resonance (ESR) and observed g values for the crystalline samples lie in the range 2.08–2.34, with no absorptions at high or low fields (Table 5). The ESR spectra for both complexes 1 and 2 are clearly axial but a small rhombic component is indicated. This suggests a non-square planar geometry for complexes 1 and 2 as shown by the crystal structures obtained. The lowest observed g values for the complexes are greater than 2.08 and this is consistent with a dx2-y2 or dxy ground state.

5. Conclusion

Three new copper complexes have been synthesized and characterized by spectroscopic and X-ray single crystallographic methods. The structures of compound 1 and 2 are based on polymers where oxalato ligand is chelating and bridging between the metal centers. Both compounds share a common zig-zag structure with copper in a distorted octahedral configuration. Compound 3 is a monomer where oxalate is chelating only mode and the geometry around copper can be described as distorted square planar. Difference between the monomeric and polymeric structures was also shown by diffuse reflectance spectroscopy and ESR spectroscopy.

Special attention is paid to the syntheses, structural, reactivity, and magnetic properties of oxalato-centered copper(II)-polypyrazole inverse polynuclear complexes (IPCs) and multidimensional inverse coordination polymers (ICPs). Future trends in this area will be focused on the design and synthesis of a new class of mixed oxalato-centered ICPs with polytriazoles as additional bridging ligands featuring porous 3D net structures, referred to as inverse metal–organic frameworks (IMOFs), as prototypes of magnetic sensors for the chemical sensing of volatile organic compounds (VOCs) of biological, environmental or industrial interest.

    In this study, two new copper (II) carboxylate complexes, [Cu(4-mba)2(tmeda)](1) and [Cu(3-mba)2(H2O)2(tmeda)](2) (4-mbaH = 4-methylbenzoic acid, 3-mbaH = 3-methylbenzoic acid, tmeda = N,N,N',N'-tetramethylethyleneamine) were synthesized.   • Synthesis, characterization, single crystal structure and DFT calculations of [Cu(temed)(H<inf>2</inf>O)<inf>4</inf>](1,5-napthalenedisulphonate) ̇2H<inf>2</inf>O

    2014, Journal of Molecular Structure

    A new cationic species [Cu(temed)(H2O)4]2+ has been isolated in the form of a complex 1, [Cu(temed)(H2O)4](1,5-napthalenedisulphonate)⋅2H2O (1) (where temed = N,N,N′,N′-tetramethylethylenediamine).   • Synthesis, characterization and X-ray diffraction of [Cu(malonate)(phen) <inf>2</inf>]<inf>2</inf>·17H<inf>2</inf>O complex
