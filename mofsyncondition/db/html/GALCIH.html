Introduction

There is much current interest in studying the variety of products in the self-assembly processes between metal ions and flexible multidentate ligands in supramolecular chemistry.1 One of the most challenging topics within this field involves controlling the variable conformation of ligands, which is particularly susceptible to many factors, such as metal coordination preference,2solvent,3 temperature4 and anion.5 The last factor is particularly notable in Ag(i) coordination complexes, due to the versatile coordination sphere of Ag(i) and the relatively weak nature of many Ag(i)⋯ligand interactions.6 Indeed, the competitive metal⋯anion interaction can affect the configuration of the ligand to a great extent, and further alter the balance between some possible structures. Besides, other intrinsic properties of anions such as size, shape, hydrogen bonds, and even the anion–π interaction7 can also influence the final structure when the anion acts as a non-coordinating template.

On the other hand, the recognition, exchange and separation of the common anions have received more attention over the past decades,8 because of their crucial role in the biological and chemical processes. In fact, one of the early recognized properties in coordination networks was their anion-exchange ability, particularly for cationic networks intrinsically including charge-balancing anions, which usually preserve the structural similarities between the initial and final frameworks.9 Recently, another type of anion exchange with a distinct structural conversion has been observed, and this exchange can be achieved by introduction of a certain anion with a characteristic size, charge, shape or coordination ability into the system.10,11 Such controllable supramolecular materials may have the potential application in detecting specific ions and sequestering/releasing guest ions. For instance, it is especially important for food security to prepare materials for identification and separation of the nitrite anion from other common anions, as nitrites in meat can react with degradation products of amino acids, forming carcinogenic nitrosamines.12

Recently, we have reported a novel disilver(i) metallacycle species [Ag2L2]2+ in solution, which subsequently self-assembles into two distinct coordination complexes under the different solvent conditions.13 We were extending this work to use different counter anions and found that they can control the conformation of ligandL and lead to the formation of distinct complexes [Ag2L2]n·2n(ClO4)·7nH2O (1), [Ag2L2]n·2n(BF4)·5nH2O (1a), [Ag2L2]n·n[Ag2L2]·4n(NO3) (2) and [AgL(NO2)]n (3). Anion exchange of solid materials 1 and 1a was systematically studied. Interestingly, a fast and quantitative anion exchange accompanying a remarkable structural conversion from 1/1a/2 to 3 by introduction of the nitrite anion was observed. We investigated the mechanism of this exchange process by atomic force microscopy (AFM).

Results and discussion

Synthesis of precursory disilver macrocycles in solution

Reaction of ligandL and AgX at a ratio of 1 : 1 in CH3CN resulted in the formation of a dinuclear silver(i) macrocycle solution (X− = ClO4−, BF4− or NO3−, Chart 1). The measurements of 1H NMR spectra and ESI-MS have verified that the dinuclear macrocyclic Ag2L2 species are dominant in the solution.13 As indicated by our previous work, these macrocyclic moieties [Ag2L2]2+, which possess unsaturated silver(i) ions and uncoordinated thiadiazol moieties, can act as novel building blocks and further assemble into various coordination polymers.13

Solid-state structures modulated by anion

Taking the above dinuclear silver(i) macrocycle solution as the precursor, complexes 1, 1a, 2 and 3 were obtained under different anion conditions. When the disilver macrocycles [Ag2L2]2+·(ClO4)2 and [Ag2L2]2+·(BF4)2 in CH3CN was carefully layered in an H2O/methanol mixture, colorless needles of [Ag2L2]n·2n(ClO4)·7nH2O (1) and [Ag2L2]n·2n(BF4)·5nH2O (1a) were obtained, respectively. Single-crystal analysis reveals that 1a crystallizes in the monoclinic unit cell space group C2/m and is constructed by the disilver(i) macrocyclic motifs with chair conformation. As shown in Fig. 1a, each metallacycle is connected with two other of the same motif via a pair of Ag–Ntz bonds (2.470(4) Å, tz = thiadiazol ring), resulting in the formation of an infinite extended chain with a four-stranded pattern. Similar to 1 previously reported,13 the adjacent four chains pack into one-dimensional rectangular space, in which the water molecules and BF4− anions are alternately arranged as guests through the hydrogen bonding interactions (Fig. 1b).

When AgNO3 was used as the initial material, the corresponding disilver macrocycle solution was carefully layered in an H2O/methanol mixture to afford colorless block crystals of [Ag2L2]n·n[Ag2L2]·4n(NO3) (2) after two days. The X-ray crystallographic analysis reveals 2 crystallizes in the non-centrosymmetric orthorhombic unit cell space group Cmc21. As shown in Fig. 2, there are two types of disilver(i) metallacyclic subunits in different crystallographic positions, which can be named metallacycle I and II, respectively. The metallacycle I link to each other in one end through a pair of relatively weak Ag–Ntz bonds (2.581(9) Å, tz = thiadiazol ring) to afford a one dimensional backbone, and the discrete metallacycle II moieties can be accommodated by the clefts of backbone without any significant interactions between them. Besides, the residual spaces are occupied by the NO3− counteranions, which bind to the metallacycles via weak Ag(i)⋯NO3− interactions (the Ag⋯O contacts are 2.53 and 2.77 Å). It is worth noting that two types of metallacycles coexisting in 2 both adopt a boat conformation, different from the chair conformation in complexes 1/1a.

[AgL(NO2)]n (3) with a distinct two-dimensional network was obtained when the nitrite anions were introduced into the reaction system. As shown by Fig. 3, the slightly distorted linear [AgL]∞ chains of alternating Ag(i) ions and L join together by NO2− anions via two weak Ag⋯O interactions (2.613 (4) and 2.674(4) Å) and one Ag⋯N interaction (2.575(5) Å) to form puckered square (4,4) grids, which further stack by the long-range π–π interactions between pyridinyl and thiadiazol rings (the inter-plane distance is ca. 3.57 Å).15 In 3, the bridging L adopts an extended conformation with two pyridylnitrogen atoms pointing to opposite directions, which is in sharp contrast to that of dinuclear macrocycles in 1/1a/2.

In fact, complexes 1–3 can be easily synthesized in high yields by directly mixing the reactants in methanol or ethanol solution, which have been characterized by X-ray powder diffraction (XRPD). Therefore, the assembly process is very fast and probably controlled by the kinetics of crystallization. Obviously, the diversity of products is largely ascribed to the counter anion in the reaction system. As shown by Fig. 1 and Fig. 2, the disilver macrocyclic motif acting as the building block is retained during the self-assembly process of 1/1a and 2. In 1/1a, the metallacycles adopting a chair conformation link with each other by Ag(i)⋯thiadiaze interactions to form a four-stranded chain, leaving ClO4− or BF4− anions uncoordinated in the interstice. Compared to the four-coordinate environment in 1/1a, Ag(i) ions adopt unsaturated linear two- or T-shaped three-coordinate geometries in 2, which leads to the formation of a distinct structure. Furthermore, when introducing NO2− with a strong coordination ability, Ag(i) centers interact with them to form a [Ag(NO2)]∞ chain, and the flexible ligandL is adjusted to a extended conformation to construct a two-dimensional grid structure 3. Besides, in our previous work using the same AgClO4 as starting material, it has been demonstrated that the metallacycles adopt chair and boat conformations in the presence and absence of solventwater, respectively.13 Nevertheless, the present results show that the metallacycles choose the boat form to afford 2 when using AgNO3 as the reactant, despite the presence of solventwater. These structural variations are unambiguously affected by the counter anions, though it is difficult to determine how the influence is imposed on the crystallization process. The above results illustrate the complexity of conformation control and coordination network design and how challenging it can be to evaluate the influence of the competing factors in the synthesis of supramolecular architectures.

Anion exchange of weakly coordinating anions

As the anions that occupy the channels in 1 and 1a are held only via relatively weak hydrogen bonding and anion–π interactions, we attempted to test whether they could be exchanged as the previous reports.16 As shown in the IR spectrum of Fig. 4a, for the sample of 1 immersed into a solution of NH4PF6 at 60 °C, the characteristic ClO4− peaks were diminished and the new characteristic PF6− peaks (837 cm−1 and 559 cm−1) were present, indicating the exchange of ClO4− in 1 by PF6−, and the reverse process was not observed under the analogous conditions. The XRPD reflections confirm the maintenance of the framework after anion exchanges, though a slight shift towards lower 2θ degree of several reflections appeared, which probably suggests the augmentation of the unit cell derived from the larger radii of the PF6− anion (Fig. 4b). Additionally, elemental microanalysis (EA) also indicates that ClO4− in 1 can be largely exchanged with the PF6−. In contrast, the samples of 1 immersed in solutions of NaNO3 and NaBF4 had not undergone ion exchange even when the systems were heated to 70 °C. When complex 1 was replaced by 1a, experimental results displayed that BF4− in 1a could also be partially exchanged with the PF6− and ClO4− at 60 °C (Fig. S2, S3 and EA, ESI†), but not vice versa. It should be pointed out that these ion exchanges can not be observed at lower temperatures. From these results, the anion affinity order of the solids can be determined as PF6− > ClO4− > BF4− > NO3−, and it is coincidental with the Hofmeister series,17 which is largely dependent on the hydration energy (ΔGh) values proportional to −Z2/r− (where Z is the charge and r− is the thermochemical radius of the anions).

Nitrite-induced structural conversion in the crystalline state

Interestingly, another anion exchange with remarkable structural conversion was observed when the samples 1, 1a or 2 were immersed into NaNO2 solution and left undisturbed at ambient temperature. For the IR spectrum of ion-exchanged sample 1, the complete disappearance of the characteristic ClO4− peaks and the emergence of NO2− peaks indicate that the ClO4− anion is quantitatively displaced by NO2− (Fig. 5a). Furthermore, the XRPD reflection of the resultant product is found to be in good agreement with that of 3, which is prepared by introducing NaNO2 solution into the reaction system (Fig. 5b). This conversion is readily achieved even in dilute 0.005 M NaNO2 solution for 24 h. To understand the process of the anion exchange, the samples immersed in 0.10 M NaNO2 solution were obtained after 5, 10, 20, 40, 80 and 160 min, respectively. During this process, single macro-crystals retained their size and shape, but rapidly lost their single crystallinities, only resulting in opaque material. As shown in Fig. 6, the XRPD measurements of these samples indicate that the anion exchange reactions cause a rapid structural change. Moreover, it may afford a simple way to separate the carcinogens nitrite from mixed anion solutions, such as a NO2−/NO3− mixture.

In the previous reports, some coordination complexes have demonstrated a structural conversion to give rise to the changes in the unit cell dimensions when they were treated with diverse anions.10,11 However, most of them would firstly be completely dissolved in solvent, which could be visually detected, and then recrystallize with the aid of added anions, affording the different structures.11 In fact, only a few examples are known where the whole structural change with anion exchange occurred in a seemingly insoluble solid state.10 In these complexes, the structural conversion was usually achieved just by the coordination or uncoordination of the ligand/anion without significant changes of the location of metal centers and ligands. Also, the conformation of ligands in transformation usually does not change because of their rigidity. Whereas, the quantitative anion exchange in the present work not only accompanies the supramolecular structural change, but also induces a dramatic conformational change of the ligandL from the previous cis, cis, syn form in complexes 1, 1a and 2 to the trans, trans, syn form in the newly built crystalline solid 3 (Scheme 1).

Although the single macro-crystals retained their size and shape during the exchange process, this conformational alteration of L to a surprising extent should not merely undergo a solid-state exchange process. In order to identify the mechanism of this structural variation, the single crystal samples before and after anion exchange were used for in situatomic force microscopy (AFM) experiments. In this study, AFM scans provide images of profile and nanoscopic features on the surface, which indicate that the crystal surface profile undergoes significant transformations, implying a clear restructuring of the crystal surface (Fig. 7). After several minutes, the relatively flat surface of the single crystal becomes quite rough, resulting in the emergence of keen-edged pleats. Interestingly, the restructuring continues until the final surface of the sample becomes more regular and consists of nanocrystallites. These observations are typical for solvent-mediated transformation, which forms a new crystalline phase on the surface. According to the previous work, the anion exchange in coordination polymers without substantial water solubilities may also involve dissolution of one crystalline phase and precipitation of the new phase, which is driven by differences in solubility between the initial and final phases.9,10a In the resultant complex 3 of our case, the NO2− anion acts as a linear bridging mode, which is usually adopted in the previously reported Ag(i)-NO2− complexes,18 facilitating the formation of a two-dimensional (4,4) grid network (Fig. 3). Indeed, the higher framework dimensions in 3 probably leads to a decrease of water solubility in comparison to one-dimensional networks in 1/1a/2, which would control the direction of the anion exchange.

Conclusion

In summary, a series of coordination networks 1, 1a, 2 and 3 have been constructed from precursory disilver(i) macrocyclic motifs in the presence of different anions. Complexes 1 and 1a with weakly coordinating ClO4− and BF4− are comprised of chair-form dinuclear metallacycles, whereas 2 consists of boat-form metallacycles in the presence of NO3−. In contrast to 1/1a/2, the flexible ligandL in 3, influenced by the NO2− anion, adopts an extended conformation to construct a two-dimensional (4,4) grid structure. These results illustrate the significant role of anions in the control of conformation and the complexity to evaluate the influence of the competing factors in the synthesis of supramolecular architectures.

For weakly coordinating anions, the affinity order of the solids 1/1a can be determined as PF6− > ClO4− > BF4− > NO3−, in agreement with the Hofmeister series. Whereas for strongly coordinating NO2− anions, a fast and quantitative anion exchange with remarkable conformational transformation of ligandL was observed. Investigation of the mechanism by AFM technique suggests a solvent-mediated rather than a solid-state mechanism for the exchange process. It is proposed that the fixed coordination mode of the silver⋯nitrite anion interaction, leading to a higher dimension of the framework and lower water solubility, facilitates the conversion in the crystalline state. This obvious correlation between structure and anion could provide new insight to the supramolecular isomerism. Further investigations into the application in treating nitrous wastewater using these solid materials is being undertaken.

Experimental

Materials and general methods

All chemicals were of reagent grade from commercial sources and used without further purification. The ligand2,5-bis(4-pyridinylmethylthio)-1,3,4-thiadiazole (L) and complex 1 was synthesized according to the reported literature.13 The C, H, N and Cl analyses were performed on an Elementary Vario ELIII elemental analyzer. The P analysis was carried out on an Ultima 2 ICP optical emission spectrometer. The IR spectra as a KBr disk were recorded on a Magna 750 FT-IRspectrophotometer in the 4000–400 cm−1 region. X-ray power diffraction (XRPD) patterns were collected in a sealed glass capillary on a Rigaku DMAX 2500 power diffractometer with ultra 18 KW Cu radiation. AFM images were taken by using a silicon nitride scanning tip (Nanoprobe, Veeco Metrology Group, Santa Barbara, CA).

Syntheses of the complexes

Synthesis of dinuclear silver(i) macrocycles solution (solution DSM·X2, X = ClO4− or BF4−; DSM = dinuclear silver(i) macrocycles)

A solution of AgX (1.0 mmol, X = BF4− or NO3−) in CH3CN (10 mL) was added dropwise to a stirred solution of L (332 mg, 1 mmol) in CH3CN (10 mL). The mixed solution was stirred for a further two hours and filtered. The solution DSM·X2 can also be synthesized by dissolving complex 1 or 1a in CH3CN. (ESI-MS: for ClO4−: m/z 978.9 [Ag2L2(ClO4)]+, 771.1 [AgL2]+, 441.3 [AgL]+, 333.5 [L + H]+; for BF4−: m/z 967.1 [Ag2L2(BF4)]+, 771.1 [AgL2]+, 441.3 [AgL]+, 333.5 [L + H]+).

Anion exchange in the solid state

To investigate the anion-exchange properties, the excellent single crystals of 1 and 1a were immersed in solutions of NH4NO3, NaBF4, NaClO4, NH4PF6, and NaNO2 (2.0 mmol salt in a mixture of 10 mL H2O), respectively. The samples were placed at variable temperatures 25, 50, 60 and 70 °C for 1–60 h, and then filtered out, washed with distilled water, and dried under vacuum. The products were characterized by FT-IR spectra, XRPD pattern and Elemental microanalysis (EA). EA: For the PF6−-exchanged sample of 1 at 60 °C: C, 28.56; N, 8.99; H, 2.55; P, 4.51%; exchange ratio, ca. 93%. For the PF6−-exchanged sample of 1a at 60 °C: C, 27.57; H, 2.79; N, 9.21; P, 2.98%; exchange ratio, ca. 59%. For the ClO4−-exchanged sample of 1a at 60 °C: C, 29.03; H, 2.94; N, 9.78; Cl, 3.79%; exchange ratio, ca. 63%. For the NO2−-exchanged sample of 1: C, 34.68; H, 2.44; N, 14.45%.

X-ray data collection and structure determination

Data collections for 1a and 3 were performed on a Rigaku-CCD diffractometer equipped with a graphite monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) by using the ω-scan mode at 293 K. Data for 2 were collected by using a Bruker Smart APEX II CCD-based diffractometer. An absorption correction was applied by using the SADABS program.19 The structures were solved by direct methods and refined on F2 by full-matrix least-squares techniques by using the SHELXTL-97 program package.20 All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically with the exception of O3 in structure 1a, where hydrogen atoms were placed in idealized positions and allowed to ride on the relevant carbon atoms. No attempt was made to locate the hydrogen atoms of water molecules positioned at special sites. For 1a, the oxygen atoms O1 and O2 of water molecules were treated with refinement of disorder using the PART instructions, which leads to the short contacts between water molecules. Additionally, O1, O1′, O2, O2′ and the fluorine atoms were refined using restraint ISOR, while O3 was only refined with isotropic thermal parameters because of the large thermal parameter and partial occupancy. For 2, the data were of relatively poor quality because of the high thermal motion of the NO3− anion and thiadiazol moiety uncoordinated, thus led to some large deviations from generally observed bond distances and high Uiso values of some O and N atoms. We thus used geometrical restraints DFIX and FLAT for NO3− and restraint ISOR on displacement parameters for some O and N atoms with high a Uiso.
