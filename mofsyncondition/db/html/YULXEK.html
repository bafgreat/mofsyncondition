Introduction

The design and synthesis of metal–organic coordination polymers are of great importance in supramolecular chemistry and crystal engineering due to their intriguing structures and potential application as functional materials.1–4 An effective approach for the synthesis of coordination polymers is the judicial selection of bridging ligands, which can bind metal ions in different modes. Multidentate organic ligands containing nitrogen and carboxylate groups have been extensively used, and they play different roles in the assembly of coordination polymers.5 Generally, carboxylates link metal ions or metal clusters to charge-neutral coordination frameworks, and further coordination of nitrogen atoms may satisfy the coordination geometry of metal ions, which generate coordination polymers with novel structures and functions. In this context, 5-aminoisophthalate (AIP) is one of the most promising ligands, in which its two V-shaped carboxylate groups may bridge metal ions in multiple coordination modes similar to that of isophthalate, and its amino group either may take part in coordination in a nonlinear mode, or may serve as a hydrogen bonding donor to form strong hydrogen bonds.6 The assembly of AIP with metal ions generates various coordination polymers, which range from discrete oligonuclear species to one- (1-D), two- (2-D) and three-dimensional (3-D) networks, but it is still difficult to exactly predict the structures and properties of target products based on AIP. One of the main reasons for this is that the assembly process of AIP and metal ions is also influenced by many other factors such as the coordination geometry of metal ions, counter anions, temperature, solvent and pH value.7–9 Furthermore, some unexpected organic reactions may occur in the amino group of AIP. It is well known that amino is a reactive group, and is readily protected and deprotected in organic reactions.10 Amino protection and deprotection in the assembly process of organic ligands and metal ions may generate unanticipated coordination polymers, which cannot be accessible through direct reactions.11 Although various coordination polymers from AIP have been reported, those based on in situ deprotected AIP are seldom investigated.

In the construction of coordination polymers, the mixed use of carboxylate ligands and bipyridyl ligands represents an attractive route. The assembly of AIP and bipyridyl ligands with metal ions forms various coordination polymers.12 In comparison with bipyridyl ligands, bis(imidazolyl) ligands are more readily available and they possess stronger coordination ability toward transition metal ions.13,14 Surprisingly, no coordination polymers from the mixed use of AIP and bis(imidazolyl) ligands have been reported hitherto.

In our previous work, we primarily focused on the synthesis of coordination polymers based on coordination-inert 5-substituted isophthalate ligands and flexible bis(imidazolyl) ligands with alkyl linkers between imidazolyl rings.15 Among them, the 5-positioned substituents of isophthalate derivatives do not participate in coordination, and alternatively, their steric and electric natures may impose important effects on the structures and properties of target products. As a continuation of our research in coordination polymers based on 5-substituted isophthalates, we are interested in 5-(4-oxopentan-2-ylideneamino)isophthalate (OAIP) and 5-(2-pyridylmethyl)aminoisophthalate (PAIP) (Scheme 1), in which the five-positioned substituents of isophthalate are inclined to chelate with metal ions to form a stable chelating ring, which results in strong competition with the imidazolyl nitrogen atoms of the flexible bis(imidazolyl) ligands when coordinating to metal centers. Herein, we report the syntheses and characterizations of six coordination polymers, [Co(BIMB)(AIP)]n (1), [Co(BIMB)0.5(H2O)(AIP)]n (2), [Co(BMIB)0.5(AIP)]n·(H2O)n (3), [Mn2(BIMB)2(AIP)2]n (4), [Mn(BMIB)(H2O)2(AIP)]n·(DMF)n (5) and [Mn(BIMB)0.5(PAIP)]n·(H2O)n (6) (BIMB = 1,4-bis(imidazol-1-yl-methyl)benzene, BMIB = 1,4-bis(2-methylimidazol-1-yl-methyl)benzene), in which AIP originates from the in situ deprotection of H2OAIP or H2PAIP.

Results and discussion

Synthesis

H2OAIP is readily available through the condensation reaction of H2AIP and 2,4-pentanedione. It is known that the 5-positioned 4-oxopentan-2-ylideneamino of H2OAIP either may form a stable six-membered ring with metal ions, or may be deprotected to form AIP under hydrothermal reactions. Sometimes, the deprotection of H2OAIP in the assembly process may generate new coordination polymers. BIMB and BMIB are known to possess gauche and anti-conformations, which may induce different structural topologies and properties of coordination polymers.16 Furthermore, competitive coordination between OAIP and BIMB or BMIB may result in incoordination of the amino in deprotected OAIP, which generates novel supramolecular networks through the directional hydrogen bonds between the amino and carboxylate oxygen atoms. In addition, the deprotection of OAIP and subsequent amine coordination to metal ions may be adjusted by changing reaction conditions. The hydrothermal reaction of H2OAIP, BIMB and Co(CH3COO)2·4H2O gives rise to complex 1, in which H2OAIP is deprotected to form AIP, and the amino in AIP is not involved in the coordination of four-coordinated Co(ii), while the use of Co(NO3)2·6H2O under similar conditions generates complex 2, in which the deprotected amino takes part in the coordination of the octahedral Co(ii) ion (Scheme 2). Different from complex 1, the hydrothermal reaction of H2OAIP, BMIB and Co(CH3COO)2·4H2O produces complex 3, and the deprotected amino participates in the coordination of four-coordinated Co(ii). The variations in Co(ii) salts and reaction temperatures have no detectable effect on the structure of 3. The reaction of H2OAIP and BIMB/BMIB with Mn(CH3COO)2·4H2O produces 4 and 5, in which OAIP is deprotected to form AIP. It should be mentioned that we failed to obtain un-deprotected coordination polymers from H2OAIP, which prompts us to further investigate the assembly of the more stable H2PAIP with metal ions. Recent research has shown that PAIP may form a stable five-membered chelating ring when coordinated to metal ions,17 but no coordination polymers based on the mixed ligands of H2PAIP and nitrogen-donor bridging ligands are reported hitherto. Encouragingly, the reaction of H2PAIP, BIMB and Mn(CH3COO)2·4H2O produces complex 6, in which PAIP is un-deprotected, and the 5-positioned substituent of PAIP forms a stable five-membered ring with Mn(ii). To our surprise, the reaction of PAIP, BMIB and Mn(CH3COO)2·4H2O also affords complex 5, in which PAIP is in situ deprotected to give rise to AIP.

Structural descriptions

[Co(BIMB)(AIP)]n (1)

Single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis shows that complex 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/c and has a 2D → 3D interdigitating network. The asymmetric unit consists of one Co(ii) ion, one AIP and one BIMB. As shown in Fig. 1a, Co1 has a distorted tetrahedral geometry, and is coordinated by two carboxylate oxygen atoms from different AIP and two imidazolyl nitrogen atoms from different BIMB. The average Co–O and Co–N bond distances are 1.993 and 2.009 Å, respectively. AIP adopts a bis-monodentate bridging mode (Scheme 2a), and the twisting angles of the two carboxylate groups with the central phenyl ring are 2.678(4)° and 14.177(4)°. AIP connects Co(ii) into a charge-neutral 1-D [Co(AIP)]n chain, where the adjacent Co⋯Co separation is 9.287 Å. BIMB adopts an anti-conformation, two imidazolyl rings are almost perpendicular to the central phenyl ring, and the dihedral angles between the imidazolyl rings and phenyl rings are 85.406° and 74.313°. BIMB further links 1-D chains into a corrugated 2-D layer (Fig. 1b), in which the Co⋯Co distance across BIMB is 14.241 Å. The long Co⋯Co separations across BIMB and AIP generate large voids in the individual 2-D network, and the absence of guest molecules in the voids results in the interdigitation of each 2-D layer with the adjacent independent 2-D layers, which finally form a 2D → 3D interdigitating network (Fig. S1, ESI†).18 It should be mentioned that these 2-D layers in the 3-D framework are packed in the ABAB mode (Fig. S2, ESI†). The amino of AIP is not involved in the coordination with Co(ii), but it forms hydrogen bonds with the coordinated oxygen atoms from the adjacent layer [N1–H1A⋯O4i 3.056(3) Å, N1–H1B⋯O1ii 2.956(3) Å, symmetry code: (i) −x, −y + 2, −z + 2, (ii) −x + 1, −y + 2, −z + 2], which further stabilize the whole structural framework. In order to better understand the interdigitating framework, a topological analysis was carried out. Each window of the 2-D network can be regarded as encircling two rods passing through it, which gives rise to an increment of overall dimensions from 2-D to 3-D (Fig. 1c).

[Co(BIMB)0.5(H2O)(AIP)]n (2)

Complex 2 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P. As shown in Fig. 2a, the Co(ii) ion is in a distorted octahedral geometry and is coordinated by one monodentate carboxylate oxygen atom, two chelating carboxylate oxygen atoms and one amino from three different AIP, one imidazolyl nitrogen atom and one water molecule to complete the six-coordinated environment of Co1. The equatorial plane is defined by three oxygen atoms from AIP [O1, O2 and O4A] and water (O1W), and the mean deviation of Co1 from the equatorial plane is 0.0827 Å. The amino nitrogen atom (N3B) and imidazolyl nitrogen atom (N1) occupy the axial positions and the N1–Co1–N3B bond angle is 172.57(7)°. The Co1–N1 bond distance of 2.087(2) Å is shorter than the Co1–N3B bond distance of 2.198(2) Å, which suggests that imidazolyl possesses stronger coordination ability than the amino group. Each AIP bridges three Co(ii) ions through the amino group, monodentate and chelating carboxylate groups (Scheme 2b), resulting in the formation of a charge-neutral [Co(AIP)]n double chain, in which two symmetry-related Co(ii) and two AIP form a 14-membered macrocycle with the Co⋯Co separation of 8.088 Å (Fig. 2b). The monodentate and chelating carboxylate groups in AIP are highly distorted with respect to the central phenyl ring, and the twisting angles between the carboxylate and phenyl ring are 27.179(2) and 24.255(2)°. BIMB adopts an anti-conformation, two imidazolyl rings are parallel with each other due to center symmetry, and they are almost perpendicular to the central phenyl ring with the dihedral angle between the imidazolyl and phenyl ring being 84.091(2)°. BIMB bridges the adjacent double chains to generate a 2-D layer (Fig. 2b), in which two BIMB, two AIP and two Co(ii) ions form a macrocycle. The Co⋯Co separation across BIMB in the macrocycle is 12.555 Å, which is longer than that in complex 1. Interestingly, water molecules form a strong intralayer hydrogen bond with the adjacent uncoordinated oxygen atom of the monodentate carboxylate [O1W–H1W1⋯O3i 2.713(3) Å, symmetry code: (i) x, y + 1, z], hydrogen bonds among water, amino and carboxylate oxygen atoms [O1W–H1W2⋯O1ii 2.844(3) Å, N3–H3A⋯O2iii 3.028(3) Å, symmetry code: (ii) x − 1, y, z, (iii) x + 1, y, z] extend 2-D layers into a 3-D supramolecular network (Fig. 2c).

[Co(BMIB)0.5(AIP)]n·(H2O)n (3)

Complex 3 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/c and has a 2-D pillared-bilayer structure. The asymmetric unit consists of one Co(ii) ion, one AIP, half of BMIB and one lattice water molecule. As shown in Fig. 3a, Co1 is in a distorted tetrahedral geometry, and is coordinated by one 2-methylimidazolyl nitrogen atom from BMIB, two carboxylate oxygen atoms and one amino nitrogen atom from three different AIP. The Co–O/N bond distances are in the range of 1.9566(12)–2.0922(14) Å, which are comparable with those in 1 and 2. AIP bridges three Co(ii) ions through two monodentate carboxylate groups and the amino nitrogen atom (Scheme 2c). The two carboxylate groups are almost coplanar with the central phenyl ring and the dihedral angles between the carboxylate and phenyl ring are 7.580(2)° and 2.481(1)°. Different from complex 2, AIP connects the Co(ii) ions to a 2-D [Co3(AIP)3]n layer (Fig. 3b). The layer contains 21-membered macrocycles, which consist of three Co(ii) and three AIP. The adjacent Co⋯Co separations in the macrocycle are 7.875, 8.789 and 8.950 Å. Notably, a pair of such 2-D layers are pillared by BMIB to form a pillared-bilayer network.22 The bilayer network contains 1-D channels, which are occupied by guest water molecules through hydrogen bonds between water and the uncoordinated carboxylate oxygen atom [O1W–H1W1⋯O2 2.963(3) Å]. BMIB adopts an anti-conformation, the two 2-methylimidazolyl rings are parallel with each other due to center symmetry, and the dihedral angle between the 2-methylimidazolyl ring and the central phenyl ring is 85.835(7)°. The Co⋯Co distance across BMIB is 13.611 Å. Interestingly, the neighboring bilayers are connected with each other by hydrogen bonds between the amino and carboxylate oxygen atoms [N1–H1A⋯O1i 3.0291(18) Å, N1–H1B⋯O3ii 3.0920(18) Å, symmetry code: (i) −x + 1, y − 1/2, −z + 5/2. (ii) −x + 1, −y − 1, −z + 2], which result in the formation of a 3-D supramolecular network (Fig. 3c).

[Mn2(BIMB)2(AIP)2]n (4)

Complex 4 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P and has a 2-D layer, which consists of dinuclear Mn(ii)-carboxylate units. The asymmetric unit is composed of two crystallographically independent Mn(ii) ions, two AIP and two BIMB. As shown in Fig. 4a, both Mn1 and Mn2 are in a distorted octahedral geometry and are coordinated by two imidazolyl nitrogen atoms from different BIMB at the axial positions. The N7–Mn1–N3 and N10C–Mn2–N6A bond angles are 176.223(58)° and 176.345(57)°, respectively. Two oxygen atoms from one chelating carboxylate group and two oxygen atoms from different μ2,η2-carboxylate groups comprise the equatorial plane. Mn1 and Mn2 are bridged by two μ2,η2-carboxylate groups from different AIP to form a dinuclear Mn(ii)-carboxylate unit. The Mn1⋯Mn2 distance in the dinuclear unit is 4.483 Å. In AIP, the amino is not involved in coordination, and the μ2,η2-carboxylate and chelating carboxylate groups bridge three Mn(ii) ions (Scheme 2d), thus generating a charge-neutral [Mn2(AIP)2]n chain, in which two Mn(ii) and two AIP form a 14-membered macrocycle. These 1-D chains are connected by BIMB into a 2-D layer (Fig. 4b). The Mn⋯Mn separations across BIMB are 14.143 and 14.261 Å. The amino is located on the two sides of the 2-D layer and forms hydrogen bonds with carboxylate oxygen atoms from the adjacent layers [N1–H1B⋯O4i 3.284 Å, symmetry code: (i) −x + 3, −y + 1, −z + 1], which result in the formation of a 3-D supramolecular network (Fig. 4c).

[Mn(BMIB)(H2O)2(AIP)]n·(DMF)n (5)

Complex 5 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/c and has a 1-D chain, which consist of 21-membered dinuclear macrocycles. The asymmetric unit contains two crystallographically independent halves of Mn(ii), one AIP, one BMIB, two coordinated water and one lattice DMF. As shown in Fig. 5a, both Mn1 and Mn2 are in a distorted octahedral geometry and are equivalently coordinated by two carboxylate oxygen atoms from different AIP, two 2-methylimidazolyl nitrogen atoms from different BMIB and two water molecules. The Mn–N/O bond distances fall in the range of 2.150(2)–2.284(3) Å. The amino in AIP does not participate in coordination, and two carboxylate groups bridge two Mn(ii) ions in a bis-monodentate mode (Scheme 2e). Interestingly, BMIB shows a gauche configuration, which is considerably different from that in complex 3. The two 2-methylimidazolyl rings in BMIB are almost perpendicular to the central benzyl ring, and the twisting angles between the 2-methylimidazolyl rings and phenyl ring are 82.839(13)° and 84.806(12)°. AIP and BMIB link Mn(ii) ions to a 1-D chain, in which two AIP, two BMIB bridge two Mn(ii) ions to form a 21-membered macrocycle. The Mn⋯Mn distance in the macrocycle is 9.934 Å. In the chain, the coordinated water molecules and uncoordinated carboxylate oxygen atoms form strong intra-chain hydrogen bonds [O1W–H2W⋯O2 2.74(3) Å, O2W–H3W⋯O4 2.658(3) Å], while hydrogen bonds between the amino and uncoordinated carboxylate oxygen atom [N5–H5B⋯O4i 3.000(4) Å, symmetry code: (i) x, −y + 3/2, z − 1/2] as well as between the coordinated water and uncoordinated carboxylate oxygen atom [O2W–H4W⋯O2ii 2.820(3) Å, symmetry code: (ii) x, −y + 3/2, z + 1/2] extend 1-D chains a 3-D supramolecular network (Fig. 5b). Notably, this 3-D network contains 1-D channels. DMF molecules fill the voids of the channels through hydrogen bonds between water and DMF [O1W–H1W⋯O5iii 2.841(4) Å, symmetry code: (iii) −x + 1, −y + 2, −z + 1], which further stabilizes the whole framework. The accessible void volume is 20.6% of the per unit cell volume after the removal of DMF molecules as calculated by PLATON.

[Mn(BIMB)0.5(PAIP)]n (6)

Complex 6 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/c and has a 3-D pillared-layer network. As shown in Fig. 6a, Mn1 is in a distorted MnN3O3 octahedral geometry and is coordinated by one imidazolyl nitrogen atom from BIMB, two chelating nitrogen atoms, two chelating carboxylate oxygen atoms and one monodentate carboxylate oxygen atom from three PAIP. The equatorial plane is defined by three carboxylate oxygen atoms (O1A, O2A and O4B) and the amino nitrogen atom (N4), and the mean deviation of Mn1 from the equatorial plane is 0.1342 Å. The pyridyl nitrogen atom (N3) and imidazolyl nitrogen atom (N2) occupy the axial positions and the N2–Mn1–N3 bond angle is 172.45(6)°. PAIP bridges three Mn(ii) ions through monodentate carboxylate, chelating carboxylate and two chelating nitrogen atoms (Scheme 2f). The pyridyl ring is almost perpendicular to the central phenyl ring and the dihedral angle between them is 86.641°. As shown in Fig. 6b, PAIP connects Mn(ii) ions into a corrugated [Mn3(PAIP)3]n layer, in which three Mn(ii) and three PAIP form a 63-membered macrocycle with the Mn⋯Mn distances of 7.779–10.464 Å. BIMB serves as a pillar between the adjacent layers and extends the 2-D layers into a 3-D coordination network. The Mn⋯Mn separation across BIMB is 15.570 Å. The two imidazolyl rings in BIMB are parallel with each other due to center symmetry, and the dihedral angle between the imidazolyl ring and phenyl ring is 79.390°. The hydrogen bonds between the amino and uncoordinated monodentate carboxylate oxygen atom [N4–H4⋯O3i 2.966(2) Å, symmetry code: (i) −x + 1, y + 1/2, −z + 1/2] further stabilize the whole framework.

Thermogravimetric analysis

The thermal stabilities of complexes 1–6 were investigated using as-synthesized samples under a nitrogen atmosphere. The thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) curves are shown in Fig. 7, and there is no evident weight loss before 370 °C in complex 1. For complex 2, there is a weight loss of 5.12% from 150 to 210 °C, which corresponds to the release of coordinated water molecules (calcd 4.80%), and the framework starts to collapse after 350 °C. A weight loss of 4.28% (calcd 4.62%) was observed from 30 to 215 °C in 3, which is attributed to the removal of lattice water molecules. The structural frameworks of 3, 4 and 6 are stable up to 400, 405 and 415 °C, respectively. In 5, the coordinated water and lattice DMF molecules are released from 110 to 240 °C, and the weight loss of 18.21% is close to the theoretical value of 17.90%, and subsequent decomposition of host framework was observed.

Powder X-ray diffraction

In order to check the purity of complexes 1–6, the as-synthesized samples were characterized using powder X-ray diffraction (XRD) at room temperature. As shown in Fig. S3 in the ESI,† the peak positions of the experimental patterns are in good agreement with the simulated patterns, which clearly demonstrates the good purity of these complexes. Notably, the as-synthesized samples of 2 and 3 were heated to 240 °C in order to remove coordinated and lattice water molecules and then put into water for one week. XRD studies show that their structural frameworks have no evident change.

Magnetic properties

Complex 4 consists of dinuclear Mn(ii)-carboxylate units, and the Mn(ii)⋯Mn(ii) distance in the dinuclear unit is 4.483 Å, which suggests there is magnetic exchange within the dinuclear Mn(ii) ions. However, for the other Mn(ii) and Co(ii) coordination polymers, the single metal ions are well separated by AIP and bis(imidazolyl) ligands, and magnetic exchange between the metal ions can be neglected; thus their magnetic behavior is similar to mononuclear metal ions. As a result, the temperature-dependent magnetic susceptibilities of only complex 4 were measured under a 1000 Oe external field at 2–300 K. The plots of χmT and χm−1versus T are shown in Fig. 8. The χmT value at 300 K is 8.96 cm3 K mol−1, which is in agreement with the expected value for two non-interacting Mn(ii) ions with g = 2.0 and S = 5/2. As the temperature decreases, the χmT value decreases slightly due to the Boltzmann depopulation of the excited states and population of the ground state, which suggest predominant antiferromagnetic coupling. It should be mentioned that the χmT value quickly increases below 50 K, reaches the maximum value of 8.87 cm3 K mol−1 at 37 K, and then sharply drops to 1.19 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K, which indicate the occurrence of the onset of a weakly ferromagnetic state caused by spin-canting.19 The reciprocal value of magnetic susceptibility follows the Curie–Weiss law above 50 K with the Curie constant C = 5.94 cm3 K mol−1 and Weiss constant θ = −15.77 K. The negative Weiss constant value further indicates predominately antiferromagnetic coupling above this temperature.

Discussion

It is known that the 2-methylimidazolyl ring in BMIB possesses bulkier steric hindrance than the imidazolyl ring in BIMB, and the bulky hindrance between the coordination groups either reduces the coordination number of the metal ions or results in the coordination of small guest molecules. The Co(ii) ions in 1 and 3 adopt a distorted CoN2O2 tetrahedral geometry, but Co(ii) in 1 is coordinated by two nitrogen atoms from the imidazole rings of different BIMB, while the Co(ii) in 3 is coordinated by two nitrogen atoms from 2-methylimidazoyl and AIP, which is probably ascribed to the smaller steric hindrance of the amino in AIP. The Co(ii) in 2 and Mn(ii) in 5 are six-coordinated. One water molecule and the amino of AIP take part in coordination in 2, while the coordination of two small water molecules occurs in 5. It should be mentioned that the structural framework of 2 is well maintained after the removal of coordinated water, and the visual color of the crystals changed from claret to purple (Fig. 9), which is attributed to the change of coordination geometry of Co(ii) from the distorted octahedron to the distorted trigonal bipyramid after dehydration and results in the variation of the crystal field splitting of Co(ii) ions.20 After the coordinated water molecules were removed, the resultant sample readily absorbed water molecules. In the TGA curve of dehydrated complex 2 (complex 2′), there is a weight loss of 2.42% from 120 to 160 °C, which corresponds to half of the coordinated water molecules; moreover, the temperature of the weight loss is lower than that in complex 2. The decomposition temperatures of the structural framework in complexes 2 and 2′ are identical with each other (Fig. 7). Interestingly, when complex 2′ was put into water for one week, complex 2 could be re-formed. The whole process is reversible, as confirmed by XRD patterns (Fig. S3, ESI†). Notably, there is no obvious color change in complex 3 after the removal of lattice water molecules. In the TGA curve of complex 3′, no weight loss is observed before its structural framework starts to collapse at 400 °C. Both the XRD pattern and TGA curve show that the structural framework of complex 3 is stable after the removal of lattice water molecules. However, complex 5 decomposes after the removal of coordinated water molecules.

Conclusions

Six Co(ii) and Mn(ii) coordination polymers were synthesized using amino-substituted 5-aminoisophthalates and flexible bis(imidazolyl) ligands. The five-positioned substitutent of OAIP does not form a six-membered chelating ring with metal ions, and alternatively, it is deprotected to in situ form AIP. The amino of AIP in 1, 3, 4 and 5 does not participate in coordination, but AIP connects Co(ii) and Mn(ii) into different structures. The coordinated amino and two carboxylate groups of AIP in 2 and 3 connect three Co(ii) ions into a 1-D double chain and 2-D layer, respectively. The structural frameworks of 2 and 3 are maintained after the removal of coordinated water and guest molecules. In addition, a visual color change is accompanied in 2. The five-positioned substitutent of PAIP in 6 forms a stable five-membered chelating ring with Mn(ii), while the replacement of BIMB by BMIB under the same conditions generates complex 5, in which PAIP is in situ deprotected just as OAIP. It should be mentioned that BMIB in complex 5 shows a gauche conformation, while BIMB and BMIB in other complexes adopt an anti-conformation, and the steric hindrance of BIMB and BMIB shows evident effects on the structures of the final products. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first report of coordination polymers based on AIP and bis(imidazolyl) ligands. In summary, this research has demonstrated that 5-positioned chelating substitutes in AIP derivatives may be in situ deprotected to form interesting coordination polymers, which provides a new approach for the assembly of coordination polymers with new structures and functions.

Experimental section

Materials and general methods

H2PAIP,21 BIMB22 and BMIB22 were synthesized according to the previously reported methods. Other chemicals were obtained from commercial sources and were used without further purification. IR spectra (KBr pellets) were recorded using a Magna 750 FT-IR spectrophotometer in the range of 400–4000 cm−1. Powder X-ray diffraction data were recorded using a Philips X'Pert-MPD diffractometer with CuKα radiation (λ = 1.54056 Å). Thermal stability studies were carried out using a NETSCHZ STA 449C thermoanalyzer under N2 at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 from room temperature to 800 °C. Magnetic susceptibility data were collected using a Quantum Design MPMS model 6000 magnetometer in the temperature range of 2–300 K. C, H and N elemental analyses were determined using an EA1110 CHNS-0 CE element analyzer.

Synthesis of H2OAIP

A mixture of H2AIP (1.81 g 10 mmol), 2,4-pentanedione (5.00 g, 50 mmol) and triethylamine (5 mL) in ethanol (50 mL) was stirred for 12 h, and the resultant solution was concentrated under reduced pressure. Water (50 mL) was added to the residue to produce a white precipitate. After filtration and washing with ethanol, a pale-yellow product was obtained. Yield: 0.82 g [31% based on H2AIP]. Anal. calcd for C13H13N5O5 (263.25): C, 59.31; H, 4.98; N, 5.32. Found: C, 59.10; H, 4.68; N, 5.26. 1H NMR (DMSO-d6) δ: 12.58 (s, 1H), 8.24 (s, 1H), 7.86 (s, 2H), 5.67 (s, 1H), 2.08 (s, 3H), 2.03 (s, 3H). 13C NMR (100 MHZ, DMSO) δ: 196.37, 166.57, 159.28, 139.71, 132.94, 128.04, 126.32, 99.28, 29.41, 19.98. IR (KBr, cm−1): 3516(w), 3134(w), 2925(vw), 1674(vs), 1617(vs), 1589(vs), 1569(vs), 1527(vs), 1426(vs), 1360(vs), 1274(m), 1240(s), 1105(s), 1023(w), 981(vw), 951(m), 901(w), 826(w), 781(vs), 740(s), 701(w), 651(s), 597(w), 538(m).

Synthesis of [Co(BIMB)(AIP)]n (1)

A mixture of H2OAIP (43 mg, 0.16 mmol), BIMB (60 mg, 0.25 mmol), Co(CH3COO)2·4H2O (63 mg, 0.25 mmol) and H2O (10 mL) was placed in a Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel (30 mL) and then heated to 120 °C for 3 days. After cooling the mixture to room temperature at a rate of 3 °C h−1, purple crystals of 1 were obtained. Yield: 40 mg (53% based on H2OAIP). Anal. calcd for C22H19N5O4Co (476.35): C, 55.47; H, 4.02; N, 14.70. Found: C, 55.53; H, 3.94; N, 14.84. IR (KBr, cm−1): 3407(vw), 3357(w), 3257(vw), 3113(w), 3037(vw), 2925(vw), 1622(m), 1581(vs), 1443(w), 1343(s), 1307(vs), 1245(m), 1112(m), 1032(vw), 955(vw), 878(m), 775(m), 730(m), 660(m), 609(vw), 474(w).

Synthesis of [Co(BIMB)0.5(H2O)(AIP)]n (2)

X-Ray crystallography

Single crystals of complexes 1–6 were mounted on a glass fiber for X-ray diffraction analysis. Data sets were collected using a Rigaku AFC7R equipped with graphite-monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) from a rotating anode generator at 293 K. Intensities were corrected for LP factors and empirical absorption using the ψ scan technique. The structures were solved by direct methods and refined on F2 with full-matrix least-squares techniques using the SHELX-97 program package.23 All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. The hydrogen atoms of water molecules in these complexes were located from the difference Fourier map and refined isotropically, and the positions of the other hydrogen atoms were generated geometrically (C–H bond fixed at 0.96 Å), assigned isotropic thermal parameters, and allowed to ride on their parent carbon atoms before the final cycle of refinement. Crystal data as well as the details of data collection and refinement for complexes 1–6 are summarized in Table S1 (ESI†). Selected bond distances and bond angles are given in Table S2 in the ESI.† CCDC 1037113–1037118.
