Introduction

In the past few decades, metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have gained considerable attention due to their intriguing topological structures and potential applications as functional materials in the areas of catalysis, luminescence, gas storage, nonlinear optics, magnetism and so on.1 Against this background, many MOFs have been successfully synthesized by the self-assembly of metal centers with well-defined coordination geometries and multifunctional organic ligands containing O- and/or N-donor ligands.2 However, the controllable synthesis of desired MOFs remains a far-reaching challenge to chemists since there are many factors influencing the structures of the resulting MOFs, including the reaction solvent and temperature, counterions, pH value, molar ratio of raw materials, and so on.3 In addition, the introduction of the secondary metal ions into the reaction systems not only can tune the structural variations of the final MOFs, but also may be able to obtain new heterometallic MOFs with promising properties.4 Therefore, understanding the relationship between the structures of MOFs and various external stimuli is still important.

On the other hand, employing the secondary building subunits (SBUs) strategy has also been proven to be a very successful example to fabricate MOFs with desired topological structures.5 Therefore, construction of the SBUs will not only play a key role in the formation of the required frameworks, but also can help us to improve our understanding of MOF nets. Usually, polycarboxylate ligands are the first choice to construct new MOFs, because abundant coordination modes can make them easily generate various SBUs (such as di-, tri-, tetranuclear clusters, rod-shaped chains and so on).6 Alkaline-earth metal ions, compared to transition metal ions, have a relatively larger radius, and have a high affinity for oxygen atoms, especially for water molecules, so the introduction of alkaline-earth metal ions into the reaction systems will readily generate heterometallic rod-shaped inorganic chains or polynuclear clusters as SBUs.7 Considering this, different alkaline-earth metal ions were introduced into the Cd(ii)–H3BTC system, and four new structurally different MOFs tuned by M(ii) (M = Mg, Ca, Sr, Ba), {[Cd2.07Mg0.93(BTC)2(H2O)4·2H2O]}n (1), {[Cd2Ca(BTC)2(H2O)4]·2H2O}n (2), {[Cd2Sr(BTC)2(H2O)5]·H2O}n (3) and [Cd6Ba3(BTC)3(H2O)10]n (4) (H3BTC = 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylic acid), were successfully obtained (Scheme 1). Single-crystal X-ray analysis revealed that compound 1 is a 3D framework based on heterometallic Cd–O–Cd–O–Mg inorganic chain subunits, compounds 2 and 3 are basically isostructural and feature a 3D framework based on Ca(ii) or Sr(ii) polyhedrons decorated with Cd–O–Cd inorganic chains. Compound 4 is a 3D framework templated by inorganic hydrate Ba(ii) ions, and also features inorganic Cd–O–Cd chains, which are connected together through corner sharing with the {Cd1O6} polyhedrons by {Ba1O6} polyhedrons, giving rise to a rare heterometallic 3D inorganic skeleton. Moreover, the introduction of alkaline-earth metal ions (Ca, Sr or Ba) resulted in three new acentric frameworks (compounds 2–4), which may be considered as potential candidates of NLO materials.

Experimental section

General considerations

All reagents were purchased commercially and used without further purification. All syntheses were carried out in 23 ml polytetrafluoroethylene lined stainless steel containers under an autogenous pressure. Elemental analyses were performed on an EA1110 CHNS–0 CE elemental analyzer. FT-IR spectra were measured as KBr pellets on a Nicolet Magna 750 FT-IR spectrometer in the range 400–4000 cm−1. All powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) analyses were recorded on a Rigaku Dmax2500 diffractometer with Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.54056 Å) with a step size of 0.05°. The fluorescence spectra were measured on polycrystalline or powder samples at room temperature using an Edinburgh FLS920 TCSPCfluorescence spectrophotometer. Thermal stability studies were carried out on a NETSCHZ STA–449C thermoanalyzer with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 under an air atmosphere. The powder second-harmonic generation test was carried out on the sample by the Kurtz and Perry method. Second-harmonic generation intensity data were obtained by placing a sieved (70–100 mesh) powder sample in an intense fundamental beam from a Q-switched Nd:YAG laser of wavelength 1064 nm. The output was filtered, first to remove the multiplier and was then displayed on an oscilloscope. This procedure was then repeated using the standard NLO material (microcrystalline KDP), and the ratio of the second-harmonic intensity output was calculated.

X-ray crystallography

Suitable single crystals of compounds 1–4 were carefully selected under an optical microscope and glued to thin glass fibers. Single-crystal X-ray diffraction data for compounds 1–4 were recorded on Oxford Xcalibur E diffractometer (Mo-Kα radiation, λ = 0.71073, graphite monochromator) at 293(2) K. All diffractometers were equipped with graphite-monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). Empirical absorption corrections were applied to the data using the SADABS program.8 The structures were solved by the direct method and refined by the full-matrix least-squares on F2 using the SHELXTL-97 program.9 All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically and the hydrogen atoms bound to the carbon were located by geometrical calculations and their positions and thermal parameters were fixed during the structure refinement. In compound 1, Mg1 and Cd3 are statistical distributions with an occupancy of 0.067 for Cd3 and 0.933 for Mg1. The Ba2 ion is disordered into three sites with an occupancy of 0.17, and the O3w is disordered into three sites with an occupancy of 0.3333. The hydrogen atoms of O2w and O3w in compound 4 were not included in the final refinement. All calculations were performed with the SHELXL-97 package. Results and discussion

Description of crystal structures

Crystal structure of 1

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis revealed that compound 1 crystallizes in the centrosymmetric space group P21/c, and features a 3D framework based on heterometallic inorganic Cd–O–Cd–O–Mg chain subunits. The asymmetric unit of 1 contains two crystallographically independent Cd(ii) ions, one Mg(ii) ion, two BTC ligands, four aqua ligands, as well as two free water molecules. The Cd1 center is five-coordinated with a trigonal bipyramidal coordination geometry, which is formed by three carboxylate oxygen atoms (O8, O4d and O12e) from three different BTC ligands defining the equatorial plane, one aqua ligand (O1w) and one carboxylate oxygen atom (O1) from another BTC3−ligand at the two remaining apical positions. The Cd2 center is six-coordinated and located in the center of a slightly distorted octahedron. The equatorial plane of this octahedron is defined by four carboxylate oxygen atoms (O5, O6, O2a and O10c) from three different BTC ligands, and the apical positions are occupied by two carboxylate oxygen atoms (O11 and O3b) from another two different BTC ligands. The Mg1 center is in an octahedral geometry, composed by three carboxylate oxygen atoms (O9, O6c and O7c) from three different BTC3−ligands and three aqua ligands (O1w, O2w and O3w). The Cd–O and Mg–O distances are in the range 2.240(3)–2.477(3) Å, 2.014(3)–2.113(3) Å, respectively, which are all in the normal range. Although there are two independent BTC3−ligands in the asymmetric unit of 1, both of which adopt the μ6-(η2-O, O′), O, O′′, O′′′, O′′′′, O′′′′′ coordination mode linking six different metal ions (five Cd(ii) centers and one Mg(ii) centers). Interestingly, despite of numerous BTC3− complexes reported, these two coordination modes have not been reported in heterometallic BTC3− complexes.10 It is noteworthy that three different kinds of polyhedrons ({Cd1O5}, {Cd1O6} and {Mg1O8}) are connected into a 1D hybrid inorganic zigzag chain by corner sharing. Although a 1D inorganic M–O–M (M = metal) chain is very common in a metal–organic framework, however, the hybrid 1D inorganic chain reported here, to the best of our knowledge, is rarely reported.11 Finally, all of these 1D zigzag chains are connected together by the BTC3−ligands to give rise to the final 3D pillar-layer structure (Fig. 1).

Crystal structure of 2 and 3

When the Ca(ii) or Sr(ii) ion was introduced, a new framework, compound 2 or 3, was obtained. Crystallographical analysis revealed that compounds 2 and 3 are basically isostructural, the differences are in the different coordination environments of the alkaline ions in 2 and 3, and the different numbers of lattice water molecules in the asymmetric unit of 2 and 3. As examples, only the structure of 2 was described in detail. Compound 2 is a 3D coordination polymer which crystallized in the noncentrosymmetric space group Pna21. There are two crystallographically independent Cd(ii) ions, one Ca(ii) ion, two BTC3−ligands, four coordinated water molecules, and two free water molecules in the asymmetric unit. As is shown in Fig. 2a, each Cd1 atom adopts a distorted octahedral coordination geometry surrounded by six carboxylate oxygen atoms from five different BTC3−ligands. The Cd1–O bond distances in the {Cd1O6} sites range from 2.156(5) to 2.680(5) Å, and the O–Cd1–O bond angles range from 56.18(2)–165.4(2)°. The Cd2 atom is coordinated by six carboxylate oxygen atoms from four different BTC3−ligands, forming another distorted octahedron (Cd2–O = 2.210(5)–2.424(5) Å, O–Cd2–O = 53.58(2)–162.46(2)°). The Ca1 is seven-coordinated by four coordinated water molecules and three carboxylate oxygen atoms from three different BTC3−ligands (Ca1–O = 2.318(6)–2.462(6) Å, O–Ca1–O = 69.9(2)–161.1(2)°). Although the BTC3−ligands in 2 all act as hexadentate ligands linking six different metal centers, they adopt two kinds of different coordination modes: one adopts the μ6-(η2-O, O′), O, O′′, O′′′, O′′′′, O′′′′′ coordination mode (linking five Cd(ii) centers and one Ca(ii) center), that is similar to that in 1, the other adopts the μ6-(η2-O, O′), (η2-O′′, O′′′′), O′′, O′′′, O′′′′, O′′′′′ coordination mode (linking five Cd(ii) centers and one Ca(ii) center). Interestingly, each {Cd1O6} polyhedron shares two corners with the adjacent {Cd2O6} polyhedrons, extending into a 1D inorganic chain along the crystallographical a axis. The {Ca1O7} polyhedrons are anchored on both sides of the chain through corner sharing with {Cd1O6} polyhedrons, resulting in the final rare inorganic chain decorated by {Ca1O7} polyhedrons (Fig. 2b). These rare inorganic chains decorated with alkaline-earth metal ions (Ca(ii)) polyhedrons are further interconnected by the BTC3−ligands, generating the final 3D framework.

Crystal structure of 4

When the Ba(ii) was introduced, another new framework, compound 4, was obtained, which crystallized in the noncentrosymmetric space group P31c. Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis revealed that it is a 3D framework with the 1D channels occupied by disordered hydrated barium ions. The asymmetric unit of 4 consists of one Cd(ii) ion, two Ba(ii) ions (the site occupancies of 0.333 for Ba1, and 0.166 for Ba2), one BTC3−ligand, two coordinated water molecules (one coordinated to Ba1 and the other coordinated to Ba2), and one free water molecule (the site occupancy of 0.333). As shown in Fig. 3a, each Cd1 ion adopts a distorted pentagonal–bipyramidal coordination geometry surrounded by seven carboxylate oxygen atoms from four different BTC3−ligands. The Cd1–O bond distances are in the range 2.327(9)–2.674(9) Å, and the O–Cd1–O bond angles are in the range 53.3(4)–172.6(4)°. The Ba1 ion lying in a crystallographical 3-axis with one third occupation is coordinated by three coordinated water molecules and three carboxylate oxygen atoms from three different BTC3−ligands. The Ba1–O bond distances range from 2.699(9)–2.8627(1) Å, and the O–Ba1–O bond angles range from 67.240(3)–137.294(3)°. The Ba2 ion is disordered over three positions with each site occupation of 0.166, and is only coordinated by six water molecules. It is noteworthy that each {Cd1O7} shares two corners with the adjacent ones, forming a 1D Cd–O–Cd inorganic chain along the crystallographical c axis (Fig. 3b). Each {Ba1O6} polyhedron shares three corners with the adjacent {Cd1O7} polyhedrons from three different Cd–O–Cd chains, giving rise to a rare heterometallic 3D anionic inorganic connectivity (Fig. 3c). This 3D anionic inorganic framework is further consolidated by the organic ligand BTC. Interestingly, in order to balance the charge, hydrated Ba(ii) ions, which act as templates, are filled in the 1D channels of the 3D framework (Fig. 3d). In addition, each BTC3−ligand in 4 links five different metal centers (four different Cd(ii) ions and one Ba(ii) ion) in the μ5-(η2-O, O′), (η2-O′′, O′′′), (η2-O′′′′, O′′′′′), O′′′, O′′′′ coordination mode (Scheme 2).

PXRD patterns and thermogravimetric analysis

The synthesized products of compounds 1–4 have been characterized by PXRD (Fig. S1†). The experimental PXRD patterns correspond well with the results simulated from the single crystal data, indicating the high purity of the synthesized samples. The difference in reflection intensities between the simulated and experimental patterns were caused by the variation in the preferred orientation of the powder samples during the collection of the experimental PXRD data.

Thermal stabilities of all four compounds were examined (Fig. 4). Compound 1 exhibits a weight loss of 13.33% in the temperature range 50–258 °C, corresponding to the release of four coordinated water molecules and two free water molecules (Cal. 13.99%). The skeleton of 1 is stable up to 400 °C, after that, decomposition of 1 occurs. Compounds 2 and 3 show similar thermal stability owing to their isostructural framework. The observed weight loss (8.07% for 2 and 10.88% for 3) occurs in the temperature range 67–224 °C for 2 and 65–250 °C for 3, corresponding to the loss of four coordinated water molecules and two free water molecules for 2 (Cal. 13.72%), and five coordinated water molecules and one free water molecule for 3 (Cal. 12.94%). The frameworks are stable upto 325 °C for 2 and 385 °C for 3. Compound 4 shows a weight loss of 10.31% in the temperature range 40–233 °C, corresponding to the release of two coordinated water molecules (Cal. 10.31%). Then, the framework is stable upto 383 °C. Notably, the observed weight losses of compounds 1–4 are lower than the calculated ones, probably due to the deliquescence of the compounds when exposed to air.

Photoluminescence property

Metal–organic frameworks constructed from closed-shell d10 metal centers and organic ligands with delocalized π-systems are promising candidates for hybrid photo-active materials with potential applications, such as light-emitting diodes (LEDs).12 Therefore, the solid-state luminescent properties of 1–4 and the free ligand (H3BTC) have been investigated at room temperature and their emission spectra are given in Fig. 5. The free ligand (H3BTC) exhibits a strong emission with a maximum at 448 nm upon excitation at 331 nm. The luminescent behavior of the free ligand can be probably assigned to π or n to π* orbital transitions.13 For the compounds, the emission bands are 427 nm (λex = 325 nm) for 1, 419 nm (λex = 325 nm) for 2, 421 nm (λex = 325 nm) for 3 and 420 nm (λex = 320 nm) for 4, respectively. It is noteworthy that the emission bands of the compounds are similar to that of the free ligand, which suggested that the emission bands for the compounds are neither ligand-to-metal charge transfers (LMCT) nor metal-to-ligand charge transfers (MLCT), and can probably be attributed to the intraligand fluorescent emission. In addition, compared with the emission peak of H3BTC, the blue shifts (21 nm for 1, 29 nm for 2, 27 nm for 3, 28 nm for 4) may result from the ligand chelating and/or bridging effect upon metal coordination, which can effectively modify the rigidity and conjugation, and then reduce the energy loss via a non-radiative pathway. In addition, the emission lifetimes at an ambient temperature are 4.3 ns for 1, 8.64 ns for 2, 4.91 ns for 3 and 3.74 ns for 4, respectively.

Second harmonic generation (SHG) measurement

Compounds 2–4 crystallize in the acentric space group (Pna21 for 2 and 3, P31c for 4). Hence, their second-order non-linear optical (NLO) properties are worth studying. Approximate estimations were carried out using a pulsed Q-switched Nd:YAG laser at a wave length of 1064 nm. The intensity of the green light (frequency-doubled output: λ = 532 nm) produced by the powder samples is about 1.53 times for 2, 1.54 times for 3 and 1.73 times for 4, respectively, of that produced by a potassium dihydrogen phosphate (KDP) powder, indicating that compounds 2–4 have an SHG effect that is stronger than that of KDP, making them potential candidates as new NLO materials.

Conclusions

In summary, four new heterometallic 3D MOF materials tuned using alkaline-earth metal ions have been successfully synthesized and structurally determined. The variable structures illustrate that the secondary metal ions play an important role in the formation of the final structures. Compound 1 is a 3D framework based on heterometallic Cd–O–Cd–O–Mg inorganic chain subunits. Compounds 2 and 3 are basically isostructural and feature a 3D framework based on Ca(ii) or Sr(ii) polyhedrons decorated with Cd–O–Cd inorganic chains. Compound 4 is a 3D framework templated by disordered inorganic hydrate Ba(ii) ions, and also features inorganic Cd–O–Cd chains, which are connected together through corner sharing with the {Cd1O6} polyhedron by {Ba1O6} polyhedrons, giving rise to a rare heterometallic 3D inorganic skeleton. Moreover, the introduction of Ca(ii), Sr(ii) or Ba(ii) into the reaction systems resulted in three new acentric frameworks, which exhibit obvious SHG effects, and may serve as potential candidates as new NLO materials.
