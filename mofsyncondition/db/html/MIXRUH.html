Introduction

Currently, extensive experimental and theoretical efforts have been devoted to crystal engineering of coordination polymers from pre-designed bridging ligands and metal ions, because such hybrid materials can display a variety of regulated architectures and thus many potential functions.1–3 At present, it has been realized that the apparent properties of these crystalline products critically rely on their network structures, and several empirical strategies have been concluded for rationally constructing coordination polymers. For instance, porous coordination frameworks with adjustable cavities can accommodate guest molecules,4 and a challenge in designing such materials with available channels is the avoidance of forming interpenetrating arrays.5Pyridyl and/or carboxylate containing organic ligands have been mostly utilized in the preparation of coordination polymers.6,7 In this regard, exo-bidentate N,N′-dipyridyl-type building blocks have been testified to be a type of excellent rod-like connectors, which can engender a wide range of coordination frameworks from one- to three-dimensional upon metalation.8 And also, polycarboxylate species, especially phenyldicarboxylate, have been extensively explored to produce robust crystalline materials due to their exclusive and versatile coordination ability.1d,9

In this context, numerous examples of such inorganic–organic hybrids on the basis of typical building blocks phenyldicarboxylate,10trimesate,11 and pyromellitate12 have been reported so far, some of which show intriguing structural motifs and further applications. Also, these ligands can exhibit diverse binding modes in the resultant coordination solids, mainly depending on their degree of deprotonation and the nature of metal ion. On the other hand, some analogous compounds possessing coordination-unfavored substituents, such as nitro (–NO2) and hydroxyl (–OH) groups in conjunction with carboxylate, have seldom been explored to this day.13,14 It is anticipated that these modified linkers may afford different supramolecular assemblies, in view of their particular ability to form hydrogen bonding as well as the steric and/or electronic effect. On this condition, one essential purpose of this research is to survey the influence of such substituents on the resultant structural fabrication, which may offer new understanding of crystal engineering.

Previous works on the modification of 4,4′-dipyridyl by introducing different spacers between the terminal pyridyl groups have presented abundant supramolecular systems via coordinative and/or noncovalent forces.15,16 Very recently, an angular dipyridyl derivative, viz.4-amino-3,5-bis(4-pyridyl)-1,2,4-triazole (4-bpt), has been employed in the construction of new functional coordination networks. We have systematically explored a series of mixed-ligand coordination polymers with 4-bpt and aromatic polycarboxylate co-ligands. Their multiform network structures and properties could be readily achieved and regulated, relying on the choice of co-ligand, metal ion, and even reaction route.17 In comparison to 4-bpt, its 3-N-donor analogue, namely 4-amino-3,5-bis(3-pyridyl)-1,2,4-triazole (3-bpt), is of further interest due to its potential tendency to show three typical conformations under appropriate surroundings (Scheme 1). Hence, in our continued study of molecular tectonics of coordination polymers,17,18 we choose 3-bpt incorporated with isophthalic acid (H2ip), 5-nitroisophthalic acid (H2nip), and trimesic acid (H3tma), respectively, as the building blocks. As a matter of fact, these polycarboxyl compounds are chemically pertinent (see Scheme 1) and differentiated by the 5-position substituents. In this contribution, we will present a series of CoII, CuII, and CdII mixed-ligand complexes, aiming to explore the intrinsic factor of these substituents (with different geometrical features and chemical functions) in the direction of coordination crystalline solids.

Experimental

General materials and methods

The ligand 3-bpt was synthesized according to the reported method,19 and all other reagents and solvents for synthesis and analysis were commercially available. C, H, and N analyses were taken on a CE-440 (Leemanlabs) analyzer. Fourier transform IR spectra (KBr pellets) were recorded on an AVATAR-370 (Nicolet) spectrometer. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) experiments were carried out by using a NETZSCH TG209 (Siemens) thermal analyzer from 25 to 600 °C under N2 atmosphere at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. Solid-state fluorescence spectra were recorded on a Cary Eclipse spectrofluorimeter (Varian) equipped with a xenon lamp and quartz carrier at room temperature.

X-Ray crystallography

X-Ray single-crystal diffraction data for all complexes were collected on a Bruker Apex II CCD diffractometer with Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at room temperature. There was no evidence of crystal decay during data collection. A semiempirical absorption correction was applied (SADABS), and the program SAINT was used for integration of the diffraction profiles.20 All structures were solved by direct methods and refined (on F2) anisotropically for all non-hydrogen atoms by full-matrix least-squares methods with the SHELXTL software.21 Generally, C- and N-bound hydrogen atoms were placed geometrically and refined as riding. For compounds 1c, 2c and 3c, the starting positions for hydrogen atoms of carboxyl were located in difference Fourier syntheses, and then refined by using a rigid model, allowed to rotate but not tip. As a rule, the hydrogen atoms of water were first located in difference Fourier maps, and then fixed at the calculated sites. In the structure of 2b, partial hydrogen atoms of the lattice water were not located. One lattice aqua moiety in 3a is assigned to half occupancy to achieve an appropriate thermal parameter, and one in 3b is disordered over three positions with partial site-occupancies of 0.50, 0.25, and 0.25, respectively. As a result, their affiliated hydrogen atoms were not located. As for 3c, the hydrogen atoms for the lattice water molecules (one of which was divided into two equivalent proportions) were not determined. Isotropic displacement parameters of all hydrogen atoms were derived from their parent atoms. A summary of the crystallographic details is listed in Table 1.

X-Ray powder diffraction (XRPD) patterns of all crystalline products were recorded on a Rigaku RU200 diffractometer for Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5406 Å), with a scan speed of 2 ° min−1 and a step size of 0.02° in 2θ. The experimental XRPD patterns agree well with those produced from single-crystal X-ray reflection data, which confirm the phase purity of the bulk samples.

Results and discussion

Structural analysis of the complexes

Most of these complexes were prepared by using the hydrothermal route except for 3c (no crystalline product was observed in this instance and thus a conventional synthetic method was applied), and their compositions were confirmed by microanalytical and IR techniques. Single crystal X-ray diffraction results indicate that they usually display a variety of infinite 1-D and 2-D coordination frameworks (with a molar ratio of metal : 3-bpt : dicarboxylate = 1 : 1 : 1) except for the discrete monomeric ion-pair product 1c (with a molar ratio of Co : 3-bpt : H2tma = 1 : 2 : 2). Selected bond distances and angles, and important hydrogen-bonding parameters are listed in Tables S1–S3 and Tables S4–S6, respectively (see ESI).‡

{[Co(3-bpt)(ip)(H2O)2]·(H2O)2}n (1a)

The asymmetric coordination unit of 1a (see Fig. 1a) consists of a pair of CoII ions, one 3-bpt, one ip dianion and two aqua ligands. Both crystallographically independent CoII ions lie on the inversion centers and display similar octahedral environments, which are provided with two pyridyl N donors and four carboxylate/water O atoms. The neighboring CoII centers are interlinked by mixed 3-bpt/ip bridges to generate a 1-D double-strand chain along [101] (see Fig. 1b). In this array, both 3-bpt (cisoid-I) and ip act as bidentate bridges and the nearest Co⋯Co separation is 9.822(3) Å; strong O5–H5B⋯O3 and O6–H6B⋯O2 H-bonds between the aqua and ip ligands are found. These polymeric chains are further linked through interchain O6–H6A⋯O3 interactions to result in a layered net (see Fig. S1a).‡ Further analysis of crystal packing reveals that the lattice water molecules connect such hydrogen-bonding layers via multiple O–H⋯O/N and N–H⋯O contacts to generate a 3-D supramolecular architecture (see Fig. S1b).‡

{[Co(3-bpt)(nip)(H2O)2]·(H2O)}n (1b)

The local structure and composition of complex 1b (see Fig. 2a) are quite similar to those of 1a, with the exception of the ip ion being replaced with nip, and only one lattice water is being observed in this case. The nip dianion also adopts the simple bidentate bridging fashion to connect the CoII ions with a Co⋯Co distance of 9.447(3) Å. Remarkably, the crooked [Co(nip)]n coordination chains are further extended via 3-bpt to generate a 2-D layered network with (4,4) topology along the (011) plane (see Fig. 2b). Obviously, the structural transformation in the case of 1b should be ascribed to the transoid configuration of the 3-bpt molecules, which leads to a significantly longer Co⋯Co separation of 12.015(5) Å. The uncoordinated nitro groups are nearly perpendicular to the 2-D motif, and this facilitates the formation of interlayer N5–H5A⋯O5 hydrogen bonding. As a consequence, the neighboring layers are joined into a 3-D network via these as well as O8–H8A⋯O2 interactions (see Fig. S2),‡ in which the lattice aqua moieties are captured via N5–H5B⋯O9, O9–H9A⋯O7, and O9–H9B⋯N3 contacts. Also, multiple intralayer hydrogen bonds such as O7–H7A⋯N2, O7–H7B⋯O2, O8–H8B⋯O3, and N5–H5A⋯O3, are observed (see Table S4 and Fig. S2).‡

[Co(3-bpt)2(H2O)4]·(H2TMA)2(H2O)2 (1c)

The structure of compound 1c reveals a distinct ion-pair crystalline product, the fundamental building unit of which contains the centrosymmetric [Co(3-bpt)2(H2O)4]2+cation and the uncoordinated H2TMA ion (see Fig. 3a). Each octahedral CoII ion is coordinated by a pair of 3-bpt using their pyridylnitrogen and four aqua molecules. Such monomeric subunits are connected to generate a 1-D hydrogen-bonding tape through O7–H7A⋯N3 and O8–H8A⋯N2 bonds. Interestingly, these tapes are further recognized by the H2TMA anions via O6–H6⋯N6 interactions between pyridyl and carboxyl (see Fig. 3b). Meanwhile, each carboxylate group of the H2TMA moiety is linked to such a 1-D array via O7–H7B⋯O1, O8–H8B⋯O2, and N5–H5B⋯O1 bonds, resulting in a complicated 3-D structure, in which the O3–H3⋯O1 interactions between the H2TMA ions are also observed along [010]. As for the lattice aqua molecules, they are fixed in this 3-D network as tri-connectors viahydrogen bonds such as O9–H9A⋯O5, O9–H9B⋯O1, and N5–H5A⋯O9 (see Table S4).‡

{[Cu(3-bpt)(ip)]·(H2O)2}n (2a)

Complex 2a has a double-layer coordination framework, in which each octahedral CuII center is surrounded by four oxygen atoms from one chelating and two bridging carboxylate groups and a pair of pyridylnitrogen donors of two 3-bpt (see Fig. 4a). Each ip ligand acts as a µ3-bridge to link three metal ions, and this results in a 1-D ribbon along [010] with the commonly observed [Cu2(COO)2] dimeric subunits, in which the Cu⋯Cu distance is 4.297(2) Å. The 1-D arrays are further extended through the paired 3-bpt linkers to generate a 2-D (4,4) network if considering these dimeric motifs as single nodes (see Fig. 4b). Notably, each 3-bpt connects two CuII ions in a transoid style with the Cu⋯Cu separation of 12.532(5) Å. This 2-D double-layer motif resembles those of [{Co(dpe)(nip)}·0.5(dpe)]n·nH2O with nanoscale rectangular channels for the inclusion of 1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)ethylene (dpe) molecules,14d and a more related complex {[Co(4-bpt)(ip)]·(H2O)3}n.17c The adjacent coordination frameworks are arranged in a parallel and interdigitated fashion along [100], with the presence of interlayer π–π stacking interactions between the phenyl planes of ip (the centroid-to-centroid and centroid-to-face distances are 3.76 and 3.47 Å, see Fig. S3a).‡

A notable structural feature of 2a is that the lattice water molecules are assembled into ordered water tetramers. As shown in Fig. S3b,‡ each water molecule of O6 forms a pair of O6–H6A⋯O5 and O6–H6B⋯O5 H-bonds with the adjacent aqua molecule of O5. As a result, a centrosymmetric tetrameric water cluster with planar configuration is afforded, which has a notation of R 44(8) with respect to its hydrogen-bonding pattern.22 The intracyclic O⋯O distances and O⋯O⋯O angles are 2.843(3)/2.893(3) Å and 95.99(8)/84.01(8)°, respectively. As for the water molecules of O5, they are also hydrogen bonded to the coordination frameworks via O5–H5A⋯N2 and O5–H5B⋯O2, which thus anchor the water clusters in the 1-D channels of the host structure along [010] (see Fig. S3c‡). A computation of these interlayer voids by using the PLATON program23 suggests a value of 101 Å3, corresponding to 9.9% of the unit-cell volume. Notably, X-ray crystallographic studies on water clusters, which largely rely on the special organic or metal–organic host surroundings, have attracted intense attention lately, for the sake of further understanding the structures and properties of bulk water.24

{[Cu4(3-bpt)4(nip)4(H2O)6]·(H2O)6}n (2b)

In the structure of 2b, four independent CuII ions are observed (see Fig. 5a). Cu1 and Cu2 are five-coordinated with τ values25 of 0.059 and 0.054, respectively, suggesting a slightly distorted square-pyramidal geometry in which one waterligand occupies the axial site. The Cu3 ion adopts an elongated octahedral sphere, which shows a Jahn–Teller distortion with the significantly longer axial Cu–Owater lengths (see Table S2).‡ As for Cu4, the basal coordination environment is similar to those of Cu1 and Cu2, with a τ value of 0.028. However, a weak Cu⋯Owater interaction with the distance of 2.852(9) Å is also found. Both 3-bpt molecules (in transoid style) and nip anions are bidentate and as a result, the CuII ions are connected to form a highly contorted coordination layer with (4,4) topology (see Fig. 5b). Each repeating rhombic mesh contains four metal nodes from Cu1 to Cu4; the distances of Cu1⋯Cu4 and Cu2⋯Cu3 separated by nip are 9.625(3) and 9.649(3) Å, and those of Cu1⋯Cu2 and Cu3⋯Cu4 separated by 3-bpt are 11.744(3) and 11.803(3) Å, respectively. These parallel layers take an interdigitated stacking fashion (see Fig. S4),‡ which are extended to a 3-D network via interlayer H-bonding such as O25–H25A⋯O5, N5–H5B⋯O4, N11–H11B⋯O24, and N17–H17B⋯O11. Also, intralayer O–H⋯O/N contacts are observed. Notably, dispersed interlayer interstices (217 Å3) are found for accommodation of the lattice water molecules. As expected, there exist multiple N–H⋯O and O–H⋯O/N hydrogen bonds between the coordination frameworks and aqua guests (see Table S5 for details)‡ to further stabilize this structure.

{[Cu(3-bpt)(Htma)(H2O)]·(H2O)}n (2c)

The CuII ion in this case shows a distorted square-pyramidal sphere with a τ value of 0.310, which is defined by a pair of pyridyl N atoms from distinct 3-bpt molecules and two carboxylate O donors from Htma anions that consist of the basal plane, as well as one waterligand at the axial site (see Fig. 6a). The bidentate 3-bpt ligands with cisoid-I configuration and the Htma dianions link the CuII centers to furnish a layered coordination network with a simple (4,4) topology, in which the Cu⋯Cu distances bridged by 3-bpt and Htma are 10.331(3) and 9.896(2) Å, respectively (see Fig. 6b). The adjacent 2-D wavelike layers are antiparallel (see Fig. S5a),‡ and are thus linked via paired head-to-tail hydrogen-bonding motifs [O3–H3⋯O4, synthon R 22(8),22 see Fig. S5c‡] between the uncoordinated carboxyl groups of Htma to generate a 3-D network. This can also be clearly seen from Fig. S5b: each Htma anion acts as a tri-connected node via Cu–O coordination and hydrogen bonding, and thus, a 2-D honeycomb (6,3) network with large voids is afforded. In fact, these nets are interlinked by the 3-bpt ligands, which also fill in the hexagonal channels to give a compact lattice. Furthermore, each lattice water inhabits the inside of the coordination layer which, together with the waterligand, generates a R 24(8) H-bonding ring with two carboxylate oxygen atoms (see Fig. S5c and Table S5 for details). In addition, this aqua moiety is also fixed by the amino group of 3-bpt via a N5–H5A⋯O8 contact.‡

{[Cd(3-bpt)(ip)(H2O)]·(H2O)1.5}n (3a)

Complex 3a displays a 1-D molecular ladder array, in which the asymmetric coordination unit is made up of one CdII ion, one 3-bpt, one ip anion, and one waterligand (see Fig. 7a). The distorted pentagonal-dipyramidal sphere of CdII is defined by a pair of chelated carboxylate groups and a terminal pyridylnitrogen from 3-bpt in the equatorial plane, as well as one aqua ligand and one triazolylnitrogen atom at the apical positions with O5–Cd1–N2 angle of 169.02(6)°. Notably, the 3-bpt ligand takes the unusual cisoid–II configuration, in which the terminal pyridyl N6 does not participate in metal coordination, as generally observed in other structures. As a result, two 3-bpt ligands join a pair of CdII ions [Cd⋯Cd = 6.227(1) Å] to generate a dimeric subunit; such building units are further propagated through ip bridges to afford a molecular ladder along [010] (see Fig. 7b), in which the Cd⋯Cd separation by ip is 10.304(1) Å. The hydrogen bonds such as N5–H5A⋯O1 and O5–H5A′⋯O2 between the 1-D arrays connect them to form a 2-D network (see Fig. S6).‡ Furthermore, the guest aqua moieties form multiple H-bonding interactions with the coordination frameworks (see Table S6‡ for details), which also consolidate the crystalline lattice.

{[Cd2(3-bpt)2(nip)2(H2O)4]·(H2O)2}n (3b)

As depicted in Fig. 8a, the fundamental building unit of compound 3b contains two independent CdII ions. With respect to Cd1, the distorted pentagonal-dipyramidal sphere is provided by two axial pyridylnitrogens from different 3-bpt molecules (N1–Cd1–N7 = 177.86(1)°) as well as five oxygen atoms from carboxylate and water that define the equatorial plane. The Cd2 ion also shows a distorted pentagonal–dipyramidal geometry, which is completed by four carboxylate O and one pyridyl N in the equatorial orientation as well as two aqua ligands at the apical positions with O15–Cd2–O16 angle of 174.54(3)°. There exist two types of nip anions in this case, and they connect the CdII centers via two terminal carboxylate groups thereof in chelating/unidentate and chelating/chelating modes [Cd1⋯Cd2 separations: 9.042(2) and 9.903(2) Å], respectively, to generate a crooked 1-D chain along [001]. Notably, the 3-bpt ligands also exhibit different configurations and coordination modes. As shown in Fig. 8b, the transoid 3-bpt linkers (Cd⋯Cd = 12.566(2) Å) further connect the [Cd(nip)] chains to afford a 2-D coordination layer, in which all CdII ions behave as tri-connected nodes to give a (6,3) net topology. As for the monodentate cisoid–I 3-bpt terminals, they are procumbent on one side of each layer, and form intralayer O16–H16A⋯N6 hydrogen bonds with the aqua ligands using the uncoordinated pyridyl nitrogen atoms. There also exist intralayer interactions of O13–H13A⋯O1 and O13–H13B⋯N2. These 2-D patterns are parallel to the ac plane and take an antiparallel stacking along [010] (see Fig. S7),‡ which are linked to afford a 3-D net via interlayer H-bonding such as O16–H16B⋯O1, N5–H5B⋯O9, O14–H14A⋯O15, and O15–H15B⋯N8. Also, multiple H-bonds between the lattice water molecules and the coordination layers, including O17–H17A⋯N9, O17–H17B⋯O5, N11–H11A⋯O17, O14–H14B⋯O17, and N5–H5A⋯O18, are observed in this structure (see Table S6 for details).‡

{[Cd(3-bpt)(Htma)(H2O)2]·(H2O)2}n (3c)

The CdII ion in 3c also shows a distorted pentagonal–dipyramidal sphere, via coordinating to five equatorial carboxylate/water O atoms and two axial pyridyl N donors. The CdII centers are connected viacisoid-I 3-bpt and Htma bridges to generate a 1-D double-strand array (see Fig. 9a) along [010], being similar to that in 1a, and the adjacent Cd⋯Cd distance is 9.532(2) Å. An intrachain O7–H7B⋯O4 hydrogen bond is found. Interestingly, the familiar dimeric hydrogen-bonding synthons [O6–H6A⋯O5, R 22(8)] between each pair of protrudent carboxyl of Htma extend the 1-D arrays to produce a 2-D honeycomb network with large cavities (see Fig. 9b). These 2-D aggregations stack in a staggered fashion, with the lateral 3-bpt components of each layer penetrating into the voids of two adjacent ones. Thus, three such 2-D motifs are entangled to afford a novel 3-D polythreading architecture (see Fig. 9c). Analogous examples have been reported by us and others very recently.26 The guest water molecules are located in the interlayer interstices of the 3-D lattice and fixed via multiform hydrogen bonding (see Table S6 for details).‡

Structural diversity and substituent effect

Structural transformation and diversification are evidently observed in the present series of mixed-ligand coordination complexes. This could be ascribed to the choice of organic ligands as well as metal centers, since most of them were synthesized under similar reaction conditions. As stated above, one dominating purpose of this research is to elucidate the influence of the substituted groups (from –H, to –NO2 and –COOH) of phenyldicarboxyl ligands on the structures and properties of the resultant crystalline materials. On the one hand, the nitro group is seldom engaged in coordination with familiar metal ions, such as CuII, CoII, and CdII in this work. However, as a strong electron withdrawing group27 that will significantly impose on the electronic density of the whole ligand, it can not only behave as a reliable hydrogen-bonding acceptor, but also show the spatial effect. On the other hand, trimesic acid may be presented as the trianion, dianion, or monoanion under different conditions and its degree of deprotonation will greatly affect the assemblies of such supramolecular solids.17b For example, the monodeprotonated nature of H2tma leads to the generation of a distinct ion-pair product 1c, and in this case, the H2tma component may be considered as a counterion to balance the charge of the monomeric CoII complex unit.

As for the other complexes which are coordination polymers, all acidic components are doubly deprotonated and most carboxylate groups take the simple chelating or unidentate fashion except for a portion in 2a (bridging). As a consequence, these ligands function as exo-extended two-connectors (the 5-position –NO2 and –COOH groups are not involved in metal–ligand binding) in directing the coordination frameworks, and this will provide a nice opportunity to further explore the substituent effect. As a matter of fact, we can clearly discover the structural changes (vide supra) from 1a (1-D double-strand chain) to 1b (2-D (4,4) layer), from 2a (2-D double-layer) to 2b and 2c (2-D (4,4) layer), as well as from 3a (1-D molecular ladder), to 3b (2-D (6,3) layer), and then to 3c (1-D double-strand chain). Evidently, the results demonstrate that product diversity of such coordination polymers can be well regulated by the nature of the phenyldicarboxylate ligands with different substituent groups. Notably, the structural discrepancy is practically achieved through the versatility of the 3-bpt co-ligand in the construction of these coordination arrays, as discussed below.

Beyond coordination contacts, noncovalent interactions, especially H-bonding ones, are also effective driving forces for structural assemblies of higher-dimensional networks. Typically, the coordinated layers and chains in complexes 2c and 3c are further extended via head-to-tail H-bonding dimers between the carboxyl groups of Htma, and this results in a 3-D network of 2c or a 2-D porous (6,3) layer of 3c. In the 2-D coordination polymers of nip (1b, 2b, and 3b), the layered frameworks are similarly interlinked via interlayer H-bonding to form 3-D architectures. Invariably, the nitro groups act as the H-bonding acceptors of amino from 3-bpt in the circumstances, and the interactions between aqua ligands and carboxylate oxygen atoms of nip are also reliable. As far as compounds containing ip spacer are concerned, due to the lack of 5-position H-bonding participator, the coordination patterns of 1a and 3a are extended to higher-dimensional supramolecular networks viaH-bonding of waterligand⋯carboxylate as well as amido⋯carboxylate (only in 3a). Notably, the waterligand is absent in the double-layer coordination array of 2a, and as a result, aromatic stacking interactions are found to fabricate the final 3-D crystalline lattice.

Metal-controlled assembly and configuration/binding of 3-bpt

It is widely known that the coordination preference of the metal ion is one of the decisive factors in coordination polymerassembly. This can also be confirmed by this work, for example, the structural transformations of 1a → 2a → 3a, 1b/2b → 3b, and 2c → 3c are quite obvious. Further, in the net structures of these coordination polymers, the connectivity of benzenedicarboxylate building blocks is quite similar (single chain) except for 2a (1-D ribbon). In this regard (at a molecular level), we can consider that their structural discrepancy should be attributed to the coordination versatility of the 3-bpt ligand.

As stated above, the 3-bpt molecule may exhibit three typical configurations, all of which have been observed in this work. A comparison of the structural features of 3-bpt in these complexes is given in Table 2. As can be seen, the cisoid-I and transoid styles of 3-bpt always emerge (coexistence in complex 3b) except in the case of 3a, and the µ-Npy, Npy coordination mode is usually observed in such structures except for the unidentate ones in the ion-pair compound 1c and in 3b (a portion). Significantly, the 3-bpt connector in 3a presents an unusual µ-Npy, Ntriazole binding fashion, which may be exclusively relevant to its unique cisoid-II configuration. It should be interesting to compare the structural parameters in this series of related complexes for the sake of summarizing some uniform rules. As for the N–X–N angles (see the definition in Table 2) for that determined by the configuration of 3-bpt, the values increase obviously along with the transformation from cisoid-I (120.9–127.3°), to transoid (141.0–154.7°), and to cisoid-II (168.9°). As for the M–X–M angles and the M⋯M distances (see also the definition in Table 2), the values for transoid cases (134.9–158.8° and 11.712–12.566 Å) are significantly larger than those for cisoid-I ones (91.9–103.9° and 9.532–10.331 Å). Notably, such values in 3a are not comparable due to the distinct bridging mode of 3-bpt in this structure as stated above. On the other hand, the dihedral angles φ and θ (see Table 2) are distributed in a quite board range, even if the 3-bpt molecules display similar configurations and binding fashions. This also indicates the considerable structural flexibility of 3-bpt when binding to the metal ions.

In summary, from the viewpoint of crystal engineering, we can reveal that for coordination polymers presented herein, the doubly deprotonated R-isophthalate components usually compensate for the charges of divalent metal ions and also link them to form 1-D arrays (ribbon in 2a and single chains in others). Thus, the versatility of the 3-bpt molecule will be principally responsible for their structural diversity, that is, the bidentate 3-bpt connectors in cisoid fashions are inclined to afford 1-D frameworks such as double-strand chain (1a and 3c) or molecular ladder (3a), whereas the transoid ones prefer the production of 2-D coordination networks. The only exception of 2c is due to the cis-arrangement of two Htma anions around the same metal center, which is contrary to the trans-style in 1a and 3c.

Thermal stability

All compounds are air stable and can retain the crystallinity at room temperature for at least several weeks. Hence, TGA experiments were carried out to explore their thermal stability (see Fig. S8–S10 in the ESI for TGA curves),‡ which is an important parameter for coordination polymers. In the TGA curve of 1a, the first weight loss of 12.12% in 110–170 °C (peak: 146 °C) corresponds to the expulsion of both lattice and coordination water (calculated: 13.51%). The remaining substance is thermally stable upon heating to 250 °C, with that, a series of weight loss occurs (peaks: 266, 320, and 387 °C), which do not end until 600 °C. Similar to that of 1a, the TGA curve of 1b shows the first weight loss of 10.15% in the 145–215 °C range (peak: 204 °C), which is attributed to the removal of both lattice and coordination water (calculated: 9.64%). The residue is stable up to 280 °C, following by a series of weight losses (peaking positions: 401, 460, and 586 °C). As for the ion-pair product 1c, it undergoes consecutive steps of weight loss (peaks: 82, 291, and 359 °C) from room temperature, which does not stop until 600 °C. The CuII coordination polymers2a–2c show similar thermal behaviors (see Fig. S9).‡ They release all water molecules (both lattice and coordination water for 2b and 2c) in the temperature range of 65–130 °C for 2a (observed/calculated: 6.88/7.18%), 55–135 °C for 2b (observed/calculated: 9.29/9.56%), and 50–240 °C for 2c (observed/calculated: 6.28/6.60%), and the peak positions appear at 98, 114, and 136 °C, respectively. Subsequently, a sharp weight loss in each case (starting and peaking at 220 and 293 °C for 2a, 290 and 325 °C for 2b, and 275 and 310 °C for 2c) indicates the decomposition of residual component. Further heating to 600 °C reveals a continuous weight loss of the sample. The TGA curves of the polymeric CdII species 3a–3c are also analogous (see Fig. S10).‡ The first loss of all lattice and coordination water molecules occurs from room temperature to 100 °C for 3a (observed/calculated: 6.97/8.04%), to 115 °C for 3b (observed/calculated: 8.67/8.80%), and to 170 °C for 3c (observed/calculated: 11.08/11.38%), peaking at 87, 75, and 117 °C, respectively. Pyrolysis of the residue occurs upon further heating to 245 °C for 3a (peaks: 277, 341, 376, and 502 °C), to 320 °C for 3b (peaks: 405 and 443 °C), and to 300 °C for 3c (peaks: 368, 395, 448, and 522 °C), with several steps of weight loss, which does not stop until the heating ends at 600 °C. Anyway, for all coordination polymers in this study, both lattice and coordination water molecules can be facilely excluded upon heating, and as expected, the residuary coordination frameworks have moderate thermal stability with degradation temperatures of 220–320 °C.

Fluorescent properties

It has been well established28 that coordination polymers with conjugated organic ligands are promising candidates for photoactive materials due to their thermal stability and regulable photoluminescence. Consequently, solid-state fluorescent properties of the polymeric CdII complexes (with d10 electronic configuration) have been investigated at room temperature. Excitation of the microcrystalline samples of complexes 1a–1c at 285 nm results in similar luminescence emissions with maximal peaks at 388 nm. In addition, two shoulder peaks at ca. 360 and ca. 414 nm are also observed in each case. To further explore the origin of these emission bands, the fluorescence of the free ligand 3-bpt has also been analyzed under similar conditions (λex = 290 nm), which shows two emission maxima at 360 and 392 nm, respectively. Thus, it can be presumed that the peaks appearing at ca. 360 and 388 nm for these complexes should be assigned to the intraligand π → π* transitions of 3-bpt, whereas the additional bands at ca. 414 nm may come from the ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT). On the other hand, an obvious increase of intensity is observed for these emission bands as compared to that of the free ligand. This may be ascribed to the greater rigidity of the ligand backbone in the CdII complexes in virtue of the coordinative forces, which can effectively reduce the loss of energy. Furthermore, the fluorescent behavior of 1a–1c is also comparable to that of the CdII coordination polymers with 4-bpt and benzenedicarboxylate.17

Conclusions

Three groups of CoII, CuII, and CdII complexes are designed, based on R-isophthalate and 4-amino-3,5-bis(3-pyridyl)-1,2,4-triazole (3-bpt) building blocks, which show various infinite coordination frameworks except for a monomeric ion-pair complex 1c due to the lower deprotonated degree of trimesic acid. Hydrothermal technology is again confirmed to be a reliable and facile route to achieve such crystalline materials. Apparently, the structural diversification of the coordination species largely depends on the selection of R-isophthalate modules and metal ions. Further, when exploring these coordination arrays at a molecular level, we can find that they behave on quite common ground: all the doubly deprotonated R-isophthalate ligands act as the bent linkers to bridge the metal centers using their two divergent carboxylate groups (with an approximate angle of 120°), and the net structures are mainly differentiated by the binding modes and configurations of the versatile 3-bpt connector. On the other hand, the notable substituent effect of R-isophthalate on the overall crystalline lattices is also present due to their different H-bonding functionality which, as a result, affords distinct supramolecular architectures.

In conclusion, this work represents a rational strategy to modulate and control the network structures of coordination polymers through a systematic change of the organic ligands. The results promote us to make further research on this attractive subject, which may provide profound impetus and insight into the crystal engineering of supramolecular solids with desired structures and useful properties.
