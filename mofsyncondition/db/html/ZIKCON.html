Introduction

Over the past three decades, strong and continuous attention on coordination polymers (CPs) has been inspired by the aesthetics of crystalline architectures, as well as the potential applications in ion exchange,1 catalysis,2 gas storage,3 nonlinear optics,4 magnetism,5 luminescence,6 solvatochromic behavior7 and so on. However, it is still a far-reaching challenge to predict the assembly of CPs up to now, owing to various influence factors such as pH values, molar ratios of raw materials, reaction solvents and temperatures.8 Even so, the coordination trends of metal centers and the nature of the organic ligands can affect the assembly of CPs to a considerable extent.9

After multiple exploration, flexible imidazole dicarboxylate ligands that contain N,O-multidentate coordination sites, have been shown to be excellent candidates which can display various bridging modes.10 Moreover, according to experiences gathered previously, it will be highly feasible to introduce additional groups on the basis of original ligands to obtain more desirable multifunctional linkers.11 In general, the modifications cause little effect on the ligands, which are beneficial for the predictability of final structures.11 As is well known, bulky aromatic carboxylate groups are regarded as valuable tools to tune structures, not just because bulky ligands usually lead to large pores and surface areas,12 but even more importantly, the carboxylate groups can adopt versatile bonding modes to metal ions and tend to generate metal-rich clusters, which can create a wide diversity of CPs.13

Here, we introduce carboxylphenyl groups into the 2-position of the imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid (H3IDC) system. To enable systematic investigations, 2-carboxyphenyl, 3-carboxyphenyl and 4-carboxyphenyl groups are employed for the modifications, respectively. Thus, three promising ligands, 2-(2-carboxylphenyl)-1H-imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid (o-CPhH4IDC), 2-(3-carboxylphenyl)-1H-imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid (m-CPhH4IDC) and 2-(4-carboxylphenyl)-1H-imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid (p-CPhH4IDC) were designed and synthesized by us. It should be pointed that only one Mn(ii) mononuclear complex bearing the o-CPhH4IDC ligand has been prepared by us.14Ligandsm-CPhH4IDC and p-CPhH4IDC were newly adopted in the assembly of CPs. Importantly, we will explore their coordination features including the coordination abilities and modes from both theoretical and experimental aspects.

Additionally, despite the selection and design of promising ligands, the choice of metal centers as nodes can also play a significant role in the assembly of CPs.15 In the light of previous literature, due to the well-defined coordination geometries, transition-metal ions attract wide attention in the researches of CPs, while alkaline-earth (AE) metal ions are often ignored mainly for the following reasons. Compared with transition-metal ions, AE atoms have larger radii which make it harder to predict their coordination numbers and geometries. Moreover, AE metal ions show high affinity for oxygen donors, especially waterligands,16 which is adverse to metal–ligand cross-linking for the extension of high-dimensional structures. However, the unique coordination properties of AE atoms mentioned above attracted our interest greatly. We suppose that the designed flexible ligandso-CPhH4IDC, m-CPhH4IDC and p-CPhH4IDC which possess abundant electron donors especially O atoms are highly promising to meet the binding preferences of AE atoms well. Therefore, the coordination properties of Sr(ii) ions with o-CPhH4IDC, m-CPhH4IDC and p-CPhH4IDC ligands are researched here. Further, the coordination properties of Cd(ii) ions with well-defined coordination geometry are investigated as well.

In this paper, five coordination polymers, [Sr(o-CPhH2IDC)]n (1), {[Sr3(m-CPhHIDC)2(H2O)]·2H2O}n (2), [Sr(p-CPhH2IDC)(H2O)]n (3), [Cd(o-CPhH2IDC)(H2O)2]n (4) and {[Cd3(m-CPhHIDC)2(H2O)8]·2H2O}n (5), have been hydro(solvo)thermally synthesized based on o-CPhH4IDC, m-CPhH4IDC and p-CPhH4IDC ligands and structurally characterized. Their thermal and solid-state photoluminescence properties have also been investigated. Moreover, the comparisons of the polymeric structures and coordination features of the three imidazole dicarboxylate ligands have been discussed in detail.

Experimental section

Materials and physical measurements

All chemicals were of reagent grade quality obtained from commercial sources and used without further purification. The organic ligandso-CPhH4IDC, m-CPhH4IDC and p-CPhH4IDC were prepared according to the literature procedure.17

Elemental analyses (C, H and N) were performed on a FLASH EA 1112 analyzer. Infrared (IR) spectra were recorded on a BRUKER TENSOR 27 spectrophotometer as KBr pellets in the 400–4000 cm−1 region. TG measurements were performed by heating the crystalline samples from 25 to 1300 °C at a rate of 10 °C min−1 in air on a Netzsch STA 409PC differential thermal analyzer. Fluorescence spectra were characterized at room temperature by a F-4500 fluorescence spectrophotometer.

Crystal structure determinations

Suitable single crystals of polymers1–5 were carefully selected under a microscope and glued to thin glass fibers. Single-crystal data for 1–5 were obtained on a Bruker smart APEXII CCD diffractometer with a graphite-monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.710 73 Å). All data were collected at room temperature using the ω–2θ scan technique and corrected for Lorenz-polarization effects. Furthermore, a correction for secondary extinction was applied.

The five structures were solved using the direct methods and expanded using the Fourier technique. The non-hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic thermal parameters. Hydrogen atoms were included but not refined. The final cycle of full-matrix least-squares refinement was based on 2688 observed reflections and 211 variable parameters for 1, 4991 observed reflections and 422 variable parameters for 2, 2062 observed reflections and 204 variable parameters for 3, 3170 observed reflections and 232 variable parameters for 4, 2812 observed reflections and 244 variable parameters for 5. All calculations were performed using the SHELX-97 system of computer programs.18Crystallographic data and structural refinements are summarized in Table 1, selected bond lengths are listed in Table 2, and the hydrogen bond parameters of complexes 2, 4 and 5 are provided in Table 3.

Results and discussion

Ligand design and syntheses of the polymers

Previously, several outstanding imidazole dicarboxylate ligands bearing 2-position aromatic groups have been investigated by our group, such as 2-phenyl-1H-imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid (PhH3IDC), 2-(p-methylphenyl)-1H-imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid (p-MePhH3IDC), 2-(p-hydroxyphenyl)-1H-imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid (p-OHPhH3IDC), 2-(3,4-dimethylphenyl)-1H-imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid (DMPhH3IDC), 2-p-methoxyphenyl-1H-imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid (p-MOPhH3IDC), 2-(3-methoxyphenyl)-1H-imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid (m-H3MOPhIDC) and 2-(p-bromophenyl)-1H-imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid (p-BrPhH3IDC).11 It should be noted that in the above organic ligands, we only introduced the groups which have no coordination abilities (p-methylphenyl and 3,4-dimethylphenyl groups) or weak coordination ability (p-hydroxyphenyl group), to modify the H3IDC ligand. According to the natural bond orbital (NBO) charge distributions of these ligands, their negative charges are mainly distributed on the oxygen and nitrogen atoms from the imidazole dicarboxylate part and the substituent groups introduced show little effect on the NBO charge distributions. That is to say, if we want to obtain more coordination sites on the H3IDC derivatives, we should consider introducing other substituted groups that have strong coordination ability. Thus phenylcarboxylate groups have attracted our attention. Then, three kinds of ligands, o-CPhH4IDC, m-CPhH4IDC and p-CPhH4IDC have been designed and prepared.

Although the potential strong coordination abilities of the carboxylphenyl groups introduced here can be easily predictable, we still hoped to explore theoretically the coordination features of the three organic ligands. Thus, the optimized geometry and NBO charge distributions of o-CPhH4IDC, m-CPhH4IDC and p-CPhH4IDC ligands were calculated by the B3LYP/6-311++G(d,p) level (Scheme 1).19 For the four O atoms from imidazole carboxylate groups, the NBO charges are −0.711, −0.614, −0.704 and −0.548 a.u. for o-CPhH4IDC ligand, −0.634, −0.645, −0.663 and −0.597 a.u. for m-CPhH4IDC ligand and −0.634, −0.644, −0.663 and −0.595 a.u. for p-CPhH4IDC ligand. The NBO charges distributed on two imidazolenitrogen atoms are −0.529 and −0.499 a.u., −0.454 and −0.498 a.u., and −0.449 and −0.495 a.u. for o-CPhH4IDC, m-CPhH4IDC and for p-CPhH4IDC ligands, respectively. Moreover, negative NBO charges for the O atoms on the introduced carboxylate groups are −0.700 and −0.594 a.u., −0.684 and −0.594 a.u., and −0.689 and −0.592 a.u. for o-CPhH4IDC, m-CPhH4IDC and p-CPhH4IDC ligands, respectively. The abundant negative charges on the N and O atoms indicate that the three organic ligands may show flexible coordination modes under appropriate reaction conditions. The above conjectures can be confirmed by our present experimental results. In compounds 1 and 4, the imidazole COO–H and phenyl COO–H are removed from o-CPhH4IDC to form the o-CPhH2IDC2− unit, which connects five Sr ions or three Cd ions in the modes of μ5-κO:κO:κO′,O′′,O′′′N:κO′′:κO′′′ (Scheme 2a) or μ3-κO:κON:κO′O′′ (Scheme 2d), respectively. Two imidazole COO–H and one phenyl COO–H are deprotonated in 2, leading to an m-CPhHIDC3− unit, which adopts the μ8-κO:κO:κO′:κN,O′′:κO′′,O′′′:κO′′′,O′′′′:κO′′′′,O′′′′′:κO′′′′′ mode to connect eight Sr ions (Scheme 2b). For the m-CPhH4IDC ligand in compound 5, it loses one imidazole COO–H and one phenyl COO–H to bridge three Cd ions in the mode of μ3-κO:κO′N:κO′′N′ (Scheme 2e). The p-CPhH2IDC2− unit in 3 is formed by the removing of one imidazole COO–H and one phenyl COO–H and adopts the mode namely μ4-κO:κO′:κO′′,O′′′:κN,O′′′′ to coordinate to four Sr(ii) ions (Scheme 2c).

Compared with the reported imidazole-based dicarboxylate ligands,11 which always exhibit 2-, 3-, 4- and 5-connected coordination modes to form 1D to 3D structures, the organic ligands here show more complicated coordination modes. Clearly, the carboxylate group of the introduced group in the ligandso-CPhH4IDC, m-CPhH4IDC, and p-CPhH4IDC exhibit strong coordination ability and diverse coordination modes. This drives the newly prepared ligands into 3-, 4-, 5- and 8-connected coordination modes. To the best of our knowledge, these five unique coordination modes are first reported, which further confirm the strong coordination ability of the imidazole multi-carboxylate ligands.

It is important to note that the reaction solvents, pH values, and stoichiometries of the starting materials are crucial for the formations of complexes 1–5. The reaction solvents are C2H5OH–H2O = 4 : 3 for polymers1 and 2, C2H5OH–H2O = 3 : 4 for polymer3, and CH3CN–H2O = 3 : 4 for polymer5. Polymer4 was hydrothermally synthesized. The pH values before reactions are all adjusted by Et3N. The pH values of the solutions are ca. 6 and 5 before and after reactions for polymers1 and 5, respectively, and 7 and 6 for compounds 2–4. The reactions of metal salts and ligands are in a molar ratio of 1 : 2 for compounds 1–5. The other conditions we tried led to poor quality blue crystals which are unsuitable for X-ray analyses.

In conclusion, by fine control over synthetic conditions such as solvent, pH values, metal/ligand molar ratio, temperature and coligands, the reproducibility of our experiments is good.

Crystal structure of crystalline polymer [Sr(o-CPhH2IDC)(H2O)]n (1)

A single-crystal X-ray diffraction study shows that compound 1 is a 3D highly porous framework. The structure of 1 contains two kinds of 1D infinite channels, which are further linked by o-CPhH2IDC2−ligands to form a (5,5)-connected 3D framework. Unusual irregular cages emerged in one type of channels while the other type of channels presented a fascinating “H-shape”.

As shown in Fig. 1a, each Sr(ii) atom in 1 is eight-coordinated with five individual o-CPhH2IDC2− anions, generating the distorted dodecahedron. Thereinto, six carboxylate groups provide seven carboxylate oxygen atoms (O2, O10f, O10g, O11, O11a, O12 and O12c) to coordinate with Sr(ii) ion and one imidazolenitrogen atom (N1) is located at the apical position, to provide a [SrNO7] geometry. Notably, despite the high affinity of Sr(ii) ions for waterligands, there is no waterligand in the asymmetric unit, which may be attributed to the abundant O donors in the o-CPhH2IDC2−ligand. The Sr–O distances are in the range of 2.443(2)–3.240(3) Å, and the Sr–N distance is 2.894(2) Å, which are compatible with the values of previously published complexes.11a–g The bond angles around Sr(ii) ions vary from 42.99(7) to 173.66(6)°.

Each p-CPhH2IDC2− anion in 1 adopts a μ5-κO:κO:κO′,O′′,O′′′N:κO′′:κO′′′ mode (Scheme 2a). As is well known, the torsions generated within the organic ligands are very crucial for the construction of high-dimensional frameworks. For the flexibility of o-CPhH2IDC2− units, they coordinate with center atoms in changeable directions. Within the ligand, the three carboxylate groups all participate in the coordination and the dihedral angles among them are 83.192, 83.932 and 23.673°, respectively.

It is worth noting that many unusual irregular cages [Sr4(o-CPhH2IDC)6] (Fig. 1b) are constituted by the linkage of four Sr(ii) ions and six o-CPhH2IDC2− units. These cages further construct 1D channels m via the sharing of their surfaces (Fig. 1c). The four adjacent channels m are parallelly located at the apexes of a quadrilateral, which are interlinked with each other via the bridge of o-CPhH2IDC2−ligands. In the process of interlinking, the second type of channels is generated and named n (Fig. 1d). Interestingly, the channels n present an “H” shape. The two types of channels cross in the space to build a 3D reticular framework (Fig. 1d). For a clearer understanding, the distributed configuration of the H-shape channels is depicted in Fig. 1e.

To understand the framework better, it is necessary to simplify the blinding blocks of compound 1. Each Sr atom is surrounded by five ligands, which can be represented by a 5-connected unit, and the five connected ligands can be represented by the 5-connected units. Therefore the whole structure can be simplified as a (5,5)-connected net (Fig. 1f).

Crystal structure of crystalline polymer {[Sr3(m-CPhHIDC)2(H2O)]·2H2O}n (2)

Compound 2 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P with three crystallographically different Sr(ii) ions (Fig. 2a) in the asymmetric unit, revealing unexpected coordination properties of the Sr atoms. Surprisingly, the structure of 2 possesses two types of fascinating irregular cages (Fig. 2a and b).

Polymer 2 shows three different coordination environments around Sr atoms (Fig. 2a). First, the Sr1 ion is in a seven-coordinated environment with the coordination sphere all defined by O atoms. The oxygen atoms of the Sr1 center belong to two carboxylphenyl groups and four imidazole carboxylate groups. Second, the geometry around the Sr2 center is best portrayed as a slightly distorted [SrO6N2] enneahedral environment. Thereinto, two m-CPhHIDC3−ligands chelate with Sr2 atom with N,O-bidentate sites and the remaining vertices are defined by four oxygen atoms from one carboxylphenyl group and two imidazole carboxyl groups in three individual m-CPhHIDC3−ligands. It should be pointed out that no waterligand participates in the coordination with Sr1 and Sr2 atoms. Third, regarding the coordination sphere of Sr3 ion, it is surrounded by five m-CPhHIDC3−ligands and one waterligand, which provide eight oxygen atoms to generate a 8-coordinate environment. The Sr–O bond distances lie in the range of 2.478(3)–2.958(4) Å. For all the crystallographically determined Sr atoms, only Sr2 ions coordinate with N atoms, with distances of 2.659(4) and 2.765(3) Å. The bond angles of L–Sr–L (L = N or O) vary from 45.00(10) to 152.07(12)°. All of the bond distances and angles around Sr(ii) ions are well-matched to those observed in similar complexes.11a–g

Each m-CPhHIDC3− adopts a complicated coordination mode, namely μ8-κO:κO:κO′:κN,O′′:κO′′,O′′′:κO′′′, O′′′′:κO′′′′,O′′′′′:κO′′′′′ to connect eight Sr(ii) ions (Scheme 2b). To complete the unique mode, the coordination in the changeable directions is very necessary, which is ensured by the flexibility of m-CPhHIDC3−ligand well. Within the ligand, the dihedral angles among the three carboxyl groups are 41.837, 56.698 and 57.579°, respectively.

Interestingly, with the participation of m-CPhHIDC3−ligands, Sr1 and Sr3 atoms build two types of irregular cages [Sr4(m-CPhHIDC)6] (Fig. 1b) and [Sr4(m-CPhHIDC)5] (Fig. 1c). The coordination of carboxylate groups with Sr ions plays the crucial role in the formation of these cages. The two types of cages are located in line alternately and share Sr(ii) ions with each other to give rise to a 1D chain (Fig. 1d), which is very rare. Moreover, two Sr2 ions construct a parallelogram with the bridge of carboxylphenyl groups. Finally, these parallelograms built by Sr2 ions act as the linker to bridge the 1D chains into a 2D structure (Fig. 2e).

Crystal structure of crystalline polymer [Sr(p-CPhH2IDC)(H2O)]n (3)

Compound 3 is a 3D polymeric structure. It should be noted that the waterligands play a crucial role in the formation of 1D helix chains, which are further linked by p-CPhH2IDC2− to form a (4,6)-connected 3D framework.

As shown in Fig. 3a, the Sr(ii) metal center shows an eight-coordinated coordination environment with one nitrogen atom (N2), five carboxylate oxygen atoms (O1, O11g, O10g, O3f and O4e) from four individual p-CPhH2IDC2− anions, and two oxygen atoms (O12 and O12d) from two coordinated water ligands to generate a decahedral geometry [SrNO7]. The Sr–O distances are in the range of 2.5697(18)–2.962(2) Å and the Sr–N distance is 2.915(2) Å. The O–Sr–O bond angles vary from 46.03(5) to 164.46(5)°. All of these values are comparable to those reported imidazole dicarboxylate-based Sr(ii) complexes.11a–g

In the resultant 3D framework, each p-CPhH2IDC2−ligand adopts the same coordination mode, namely μ4-κO:κO′:κO′′,O′′′:κN,O′′′′ (Scheme 2c). Through the coordinated water ligands, Sr(ii) ions are connected to form 1D helix chains with repeat distance of 5.246 Å, which are further bridged by carboxylate groups to build 2D layers (Fig. 3b). Moreover, via the linkage of the p-CPhH2IDC2− units, these layers are further interlaced into a 3D framework (Fig. 3c). From another perspective, the deprotonated ligandsp-CPhH2IDC2− connect eight coplanar Sr(ii) atoms to construct a cluster [Sr8(p-CPhH2IDC)2]. Two organic ligands intervene into the cluster, forming two types of distorted quadrilaterals. Through the interlacing and accumulating, the configuration of compound 3 embodies three kinds of channels which are staggered in the 3D framework (Fig. 3c).

To understand the 3D structure better, the simplification of the building blocks for polymer3 is necessary. From a topological viewpoint, the p-CPhH2IDC2− coordinates to four Sr ions, so it can be viewed a 4-connected node. The Sr atom is surrounded by four p-CPhH2IDC2−ligands and two waterligands, which can be represented by a 6-connected node. Then the 3D framework of 1 can be described as a (4,6)-connected topology (Fig. 3d). Owing to the three types of channels, polymer3 shows a difference in comparison with the frameworks constructed from imidazole dicarboxylate previously, especially as one kind of 1D channels therein is constructed by Sr atoms and coordinated waters.

Crystal structure of crystalline polymer [Cd(o-CPhH2IDC)(H2O)2]n (4)

To our surprise, the 2D framework 4 contains 1D channels composed of Cd atoms and imidazole dicarboxylate units. The asymmetric unit of 4 contains one Cd(ii) ion, one o-CPhH2IDC2−ligand, and two coordinated water ligands. As shown in Fig. 4a, each Cd(ii) ion resides in an octahedral coordination geometry with the apical positions occupied by one imidazolate nitrogen atom (N1) and three carboxylate oxygen atoms (O3, O7 and O7a) from three surrounding o-CPhH2IDC2− anions, as well as two oxygen atoms (O5 and O8) from two waterligands. The Cd1–N1 length is 2.3198(23) Å and the Cd–O distances are in the range of 2.1931(21)–2.4021(22) Å. The L–Cd–L bond angles are in the range of 69.22(9)–170.26(8)°. The bond distances and angles are all within the normal range found in reported values.10a,20

In polymer4, each o-CPhH2IDC2−ligand is formed by losing two protons on one imidazole COO–H and one phenyl COO–H, adopting the coordination mode μ3-κO:κON:κO′O′′ (Scheme 2d). The aromatic group is twisted by an angle of 27.198° from the imidazole ring. The dihedral angle between two coordinated carboxyl groups is 58.543°, leading to coordination in different directions. The two Cd(ii) ions in two adjacent units are connected into a parallelogram [Cd2(o-CPhH2IDC)2]·2H2O by two imidazole carboxylate groups from two individual p-CPhH2IDC2−ligands. The four neighboring parallelograms are bridged via the coordination of the ligands. When we replace these parallelograms with their centers, they constitute a rhombus (Fig. 4b). In the rhombus, the parallelograms located in opposite positions are parallel to each other. The rhombuses share the vertexes and edges to build up a fascinating 2D framework. As depicted in Fig. 4c, channels along the b-axis can be observed in the structure. The interactions of intermolecular hydrogen bonds further extend the network into a 3D configuration (Fig. 4d).

Crystal structure of crystalline polymer {[Cd3(m-CPhHIDC)2(H2O)8]·2H2O}n (5)

For polymer5, there are one and half Cd(ii) ions, one m-CPhHIDC3−ligand, four waterligands and one lattice water in the asymmetric unit. The octahedral geometries [CdN2O4] and [CdNO5] are generated around two crystallographically independent Cd(ii) ions with their distance of 6.687 Å.

As shown in Fig. 5a, the m-CPhHIDC3−ligands bridge the Cd1 atom in a bidentate fashion through N,O-heterocoordination at the vertexes of geometry [CdN2O4], and its remaining positions are occupied by two H2O molecules. The Cd2 ion with [CdNO5] distorted octahedral geometry is chelated by one N,O-bidentate site of one m-CPhHIDC3−ligand, as well as one carboxylate oxygen atom and three O atoms from waterligands (Fig. 5a). The Cd–O bond distances lie in the range of 2.260(3)–2.451(3) Å and Cd–N bond distances are 2.275(3)–2.277(4) Å. All of these bond distances are similar to those found in other Cd(ii) complexes.10a,20

Each m-CPhH2IDC3−ligand adopts the same coordination mode in bridging fashion namely μ3-κO:κO′N:κO′′N′ (Scheme 2e). The dihedral angles among three chelation planes are 57.545, 51.717, 22.765°, respectively, which show the multiple-directional coordination. For ease of description, we regard the V-shape Cd3(m-CPhHIDC)·8H2O cluster as a unit which consists of one Cd1 and two Cd2 ions bridged by the N,O-bidentate site of two individual m-CPhHIDC3−ligands (Fig. 5b). The m-CPhHIDC3− anion in one unit connects the Cd2 ion in adjacent units via the coordination of a carboxylphenyl group, leading to the formation of 1D zigzag chains (Fig. 5c). Then, still through the coordination effects of carboxylphenyl groups, the 2D network is generated (Fig. 5d). Notably, the carboxylphenyl groups which act as the vectors play a significant role in the extension of higher dimensional structure.

Structural discussion of complexes 1–5

As is well known, Sr(ii) ions with large radius tend to form high coordination environments. Additionally, they show high affinity for oxygen donors especially waterligands, which is adverse to metal–ligand cross-linking for the extension of high-dimensional structures. Owing to its unpredictable coordination numbers and geometries, researches about Sr are relatively less than for transition atoms. Despite the long-term researches for imidazole dicarboxylate-based polymers, only two 3D and seven 2D Sr(ii) polymers have been constructed from imidazole dicarboxylate ligands bearing 2-position aromatic substituents.11a–g For the employed ligands in the reported Sr polymers, the aromatic groups have no or only weak coordination ability, and do not participate in the coordination consistently. Hence, the changes in the coordination modes of these ligands can only happen in the part of imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylate. Four kinds of coordination modes in all are presented in the reported Sr polymers.11a–g In this work, the additional carboxyphenyl groups in the designed ligandso-CPhH4IDC, m-CPhH4IDC, p-CPhH4IDC are all involved in the coordination. The multiple oxygen donors satisfy the high affinity for oxygen donors of Sr atoms well and three new unique coordination modes are presented here. Furthermore, it should be noted that among the Sr polymers aforementioned, only in two examples the Sr ions coordinate with N atoms.11a,b However, the Sr ions in polymers1, 2 and 3 all coordinate with nitrogen atoms. The uncommon phenomenon demonstrates the strong coordination abilities of o-CPhH4IDC, m-CPhH4IDC, p-CPhH4IDC ligands.

Relatively speaking, Sr polymers present more complicated coordination modes than Cd polymers in this work. As previously described, Sr has high affinity for oxygen donors while Cd prefers nitrogen donors. As there are more O donors than N donors in the ligands for the multiple carboxylate groups in the ligands, this is ideal for the coordination with Sr atoms. In addition, Sr(ii) ions have a larger radius than Cd(ii) ions and can receive more electron donors in the ligands. These may be the reasons why relatively complicated coordination modes are revealed in Sr polymers here.

To our delight, unusual cages are constituted in the polymers1 and 2 and three types of regular infinite channels are formed in polymer3. According to previous experiences, the structures of these polymers are much connected with the distribution of coordination groups in the employed ligands. For the p-CPhH4IDC ligand, the electron donors are almost symmetrically distributed along a straight line and make for a regular structure. However, the coordination groups for o-CPhH4IDC and m-CPhH4IDC ligands are located in curved chains, which are conducive to the formation of intriguing cages.

Infrared spectra, thermal analyses and luminescent properties

Polymers1–5, once isolated, are stable in air and insoluble in water and common organic solvents. The IR spectra display characteristic absorption bands for water molecules, carboxylate, imidazole and phenyl units. Compounds 1–5 show strong and broad absorption bands in the range of 3400–3500 cm−1, which indicates the presence of νN–H and the νO–H stretching frequencies of imidazole rings and water molecules. The carboxyl can be observed from the absorption bands in the frequency range 1380–1704 cm−1 as a result of νas(COO−) and νs(COO−) vibrations, respectively. The characteristic IR band of the phenyl ring at 676–881 cm−1 due to δ(C–H) vibrations, are found at 746 cm−1 for 1; 854, 781, 734 cm−1 for 2; 783 cm−1 for 3; 749 cm−1 for 4; 881, 789, 676 cm−1 for 5. In conclusion, the infrared spectral data of the complexes 1–5 are consistent with crystal structure analyses.

The thermal behaviors of 1–5 were investigated using thermogravimetric analysis in air (Fig. 6).

Polymer 1 exhibits a high thermal stability and shows a two main step weight loss process above 400 °C. First, a gradual loss weight of 57.75% occurs from 400 to 530 °C. Subsequently, the samples loses remaining weight from 840–944 °C (observed 12.94%). Finally, a plateau region is observed from 944 to 1070 °C. The amorphous residue is identified as SrO (observed 29.31%, calculated 28.64%).

Polymer 2 shows a low thermal stability and a three main step loss weight. A first loss weight of 6.23% from 45 to 125 °C (calculated 6.26%) corresponds to the release of two lattice water molecules and one coordinated water molecule. The second and third steps gross weight loss is 57.54%, which occurs in the range of 140.0–1269.0 °C corresponding to the decomposition of the remaining complex. The residual weight of 36.20% corresponds to the percentage (calculated 36.00%) of the Sr and O components, indicating that the final product is SrO.

The TGA curve of 3 shows a weight loss of 3.98% (calculated 4.74%) from 83 to 224 °C corresponding to the release of a coordinated water molecule, then it keeps losing the remaining complex in three steps from 220 to 1000 °C (observed 68.57%, calculated 67.94%). Finally, a plateau region is observed from 1000 to 1174 °C. The remaining SrO residue accounts for 27.25% (calculated 27.32%).

For polymer4, a gradual weight loss is observed from 100 to 537 °C. It first loses two coordinated water molecules in the temperature range of 100–259 °C (observed 8.03%, calculated 8.53%) and further loses the remaining part of the complex from 284 to 537 °C (observed 61.37%, calculated 61.09%). Finally, a weight of 30.60% remains which corresponds to the Cd and O components (calculated 30.38%), indicating that the final product is CdO.

Polymer 5 shows a relatively low stability up to 80 °C. After this temperature the sample shows three main steeps weight loss stages. Initially, it loses weight from 80 to 277 °C (16.82%) attributed to the loss of ten coordination water molecules (calculated 16.97%). Then it keeps losing the remaining complex in two main steps from 280 to 500 °C (observed 47.58%, calculated 46.75%), leaving a solid residue of CdO (found 35.60%, calculated 36.28%).

Photoluminescent properties of coordination polymers have attracted much attention due to their potential optical applications.21 It has been reported that the emission wavelength and intensity of the organic material can be affected through the coordination with metal. The solid-state photoluminescent spectra of polymers1–5 and the corresponding free ligands are investigated at room temperature under the same experimental condition (Fig. 7).

The free ligands show luminescence with emission maximum at 498 nm (λex = 445 nm) for o-CPhH4IDC, 462 nm (λex = 412 nm) for m-CPhH4IDC, 492 nm (λex = 412 nm) for p-CPhH4IDC, which may be attributed to ligand-centered π*→n or π→π* electronic transitions. Emission bands are observed at 376 nm (λex = 325 nm) for 1, 452 nm (λex = 350 nm) for 2, 469 nm (λex = 430 nm) for 3, 408 nm (λex = 315 nm) for 4, 458 nm (λex = 400 nm) for 5. The emission spectra of the five complexes show various degrees of blue shift in comparison with their corresponding ligands, which may because that the coordination of multi-carboxylate ligands to metal cations affect the energy levels of the ligands.22 The blue shift of compounds 4 and 5 may also be ascribed to a mixture characteristic of intraligand and ligand-to-ligand charge transition (LLCT), as reported for other d10 metal complexes with N-donor ligands.23

It is interesting to note that the emission intensities for polymers1–3 present different changes with the sequence of free ligands. Polymers1 and 2 show obvious increase, but for polymer3, it shows similar emission intensities with the free ligand except for a slight blue shift of 23 nm. There are many factors that can influence the results, for instance, the increasing intensity of the complexes is probably attributable to the formation of the polymeric structures, which effectively restrict the flexibility and increase the rigidity of the free ligands, reducing the loss of energy, and the different coordination environment around metal ions.

Both polymers4 and 5 show an increased intensity. As is well known, CPs constructed by d10 transition-metal ions are typically based on the linkers and charge transfer between the metal ions and linkers. So the main influence of emission of polymers4 and 5 should be from ligand-centered luminescence24 and ligand-to-metal charge-transfer (LMCT).25

Conclusion

In summary, five 2D or 3D polymers1–5 based on Sr(ii) or Cd(ii) ions are derived from the designed multifunctional organic ligands, o-CPhH4IDC, m-CPhH4IDC and p-CPhH4IDC. The introduced carboxylate groups all participate in the coordination, leading to diversified structures. These designed ligands possess abundant coordination sites especially O atoms, which satisfy the high affinity for oxygen donors of Sr atoms well. In intriguing Sr polymers1–3, the N atoms all coordinate with the Sr atoms, and the uncommon phenomenon reveals the strong coordination ability of o-CPhH4IDC, m-CPhH4IDC and p-CPhH4IDC ligands. For comparisons among polymers with different atoms, Sr polymers present relatively more complicated coordination modes than Cd polymers and the reasons are discussed in detail. Further investigations for the coordination properties of AE metal ions and these designed ligands are underway in our laboratory.
