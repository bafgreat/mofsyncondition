One pot synthesis, structure and magnetic property of a pseudo-interpenetrated 2D copper framework based on coordinated 1,1′-biphenyl-3,3′,5,5′-tetracarboxylate and synthon

Abstract

A mixture of 1,1′-biphenyl-3,3′,5,5′-tetracarboxylate acid (abbreviated as H4bpta), 4,4′-bipyridine (abbreviated as 4,4′-bipy), and CuCl2·2H2O generates co-crystallization supramolecular solids [Cu2(cis-H2bpta)2(4,4′-bipy)2]n·nH4bpta·2nH2O (I) under hydrothermal condition and form of partly deprotonated cis-H2bpta2− firstly emerges in this work. Crystallographically, a 1:1 co-crystal is formed by co-crystallization of [Cu2(cis-H2bpta)2(4,4′-bipy)2] and H4bpta in self-assembled process. Structurally, the compound (I) exhibits two-dimensional (2D) double-layered nets with a pseudo-interpenetrated skeleton, one layer is characteristic of metal-organic moiety linked via cis-H2bpta2− and 4,4′-bipy spaces, and the other formed by O–H···O H-bonds presented in neutral H4bpta ligands. Interestingly, its mechanically interlocked structure is formed by hydrogen bonds, which results in an interesting interweaving framework with a new (3,4,5)-connected topological structure. Magnetic couplings between adjacent Cu(II) centers are mediated through double syn-syn carboxylate bridges. Magnetic susceptibility measurement indicates that (I) exhibits a dinuclear Cu(II) behavior with antiferromagnetic coupling (J = −35.69(1) cm−1).

1. Introduction

The design and construction of entangled networks have attracted widespread interests of many investigators in crystal engineering and supramolecular chemistry for both esthetic [1], [2] and practical applications in various areas [3], [4], [5]. Interpenetrated structures are very common in metal-organic architectures [6], [7], [8]; especially to the flexible ligands systems, which will promote the possibility in the formation of unusual supramolecular entanglements [9], [10], [11]. For examples, the flexible ligand N,N′-[(2,3,5,6-tetrafluoro-1,4-phenylene)bis(methylene)]bis(pyridine-4-car-box-amide) [12] with multifunctional binding sites for coordination and H-bonding interactions are confirmed to form varied entangled networks. Another interpenetrating example constructed by only hydrogen-bonds was 4 or 5-fold diamond structures based on 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethyl-4,4′-bipyrazole [13]. Compared with the widely reported interpenetrated infinite networks, there are few examples involving hetero-molecules entangle via hydrogen bonds [1]. Apart from their intrinsic esthetic appeal, the interest in these compounds is that their mechanically interlocked structures are more flexible than the usual networks entirely based on coordination bonds. Thus, introduction of the term co-crystal into metal-organic materials will extend topologies and functions of metal-organic materials. Co-crystals are considered as solids that are crystalline and single phase materials composed of two or more different molecules held together by noncovalent forces such as hydrogen bonding, π-stacking, Vander Waals forces, and etc [14]. A strategy of using hydrogen bonds as auxiliary linkage forces has been proven successfully to establish many supramolecular systems with fascinating structures and outstanding properties [15], [16], [17].

Many co-crystals have been constructed by organic matters in the supramolecular synthon approach or structural resemblance strategy [18], [19], which has rarely come into metalorganic materials. As is referred, organic ligands play important roles in directing the final structures and properties. Based on our previous works [20], [21], [22], aromatic polycarboxylate ligands, which are characterized by multiple bridging moieties and multidentate chelating modes with metal centers, are possible to provide abundant structural motifs [23], [24]. Among these ligands, the 3,3′5,5′-biphenyl-tetracarboxylic acid (abbreviated as H4bpta) and its derivatives are of special importance because the H4bpta ligand possesses a variety of coordination modes with possible syn- and anti-conformations by partly deprotonated carboxylate groups. and they can act as rigid organic linkers to connect metal secondary building units (SBUs), then construct porous frameworks [25], [26], [27]. A search result from Cambridge Structural Database (CSD; Version 5.36, updated in February 2015) [28] pointed that the H4bpta ligand is mainly fully deprotonated and partly deprotonated (trans-H2bpta2−) between the functional groups. However, partly deprotonated cis-H2bpta2− firstly emerges in this work (Scheme 1). In addition, the deliberate use of N-donor auxiliary ligands, such as 4,4′-bipyridine (abbreviated as 4,4′-bipy), could effectively promote and control the entangled networks in the self-assembly process.

According to the above facts, we reported a case of 1:1 synthon polymorphism in [Cu2(cis-H2bpta)2(4,4′-bipy)2]n·nH4bpta·2nH2O (I) based on 1,1′-biphenyl-3,3′,5,5′-tetracarboxylate ligand. Here, the H2bpta2− ligands exhibit partly cis-deprotonated coordination mode to construct metalorganic moiety. Moreover, neutral H4bpta ligands entangle the metal-organic framework to form an interesting interweaving structure via O–H···O H-bonds. Magnetic susceptibility measurement indicates that (I) exhibits a dinuclear Cu(II) behavior with antiferromagnetic coupling (J = −35.69(1) cm−1) for the S = 1/2 system arising from spin interactions between the two d9 Cu(II) ions via the carboxylate bridges. The antiferromagnetic coupling between adjacent Cu(II) centers is in agreement with the well-known ability of the synsyn carboxylate bridge which mediates dominantly antiferromagnetic interactions [29], [30].

2. Experimental section

2.1. Material and measurements

H4bpta, 1,1′-biphenyl-3,3′,5,5′-tetracarboxylate acid, was received from Jinan Camolai Trading Company, China. Other reagents and solvents were of standard commercial grade and directly used without further purification. The IR spectra were recorded from KBr pellets in the range 4000−400 cm−1 on a BRUKER TENSOR27 spectrometer. Elemental analysis was carried out using a CHNO-Rapid instrument. Powder X-ray diffraction (XRD) data was collected on a Bruker D8 Advance X-ray diffractometer with Cu-Kα radiation (λ = 1.5418 Å). Thermogravimetric (TG) study was carried out on a Dupont thermal analyzer with temperature range 25–825 °C under N2 flow with a heating rate of 20 °C min−1. Magnetic susceptibility data was obtained with a SQUID magnetometer (Quantum MPMS) in the range of 2.0–300 K by using an applied field of 2000 Oe.

2.2. Synthesis of [Cu2(cis-H2bpta)2(4,4′-bipy)2]n・nH4bpta・2nH2O (I)

A mixture of H4bpta (33.3 mg, 0.1 mmol), 4,4′-bipy (31.2 mg, 0.2 mmol), CuCl2·2H2O (34.2 mg, 0.2 mmol) and 8 mL of H2O was adjusted to approximately pH 6.5 with dilute NaOH solution, and then transferred to 13 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel and heated under autogenous pressure at 403 K for 72 h and then cooled to room temperature naturally. Blue block-shaped crystals were collected by filtration, washed with H2O, and dried in air (yield ∼47%, based on H4bpta). Analysis calculated for C40H32Cd2N8O11 (I): C 55.86, H 3.17, N 3.83%; found: C 56.23, H 3.41, N 3.15%. IR (KBr, ν, cm−1): 3436m, 1629s, 1608s, 1543s, 1488w, 1419s, 1307s, 1369s, 1217w, 1085w, 897w, 814m, 781m, 722m, 711m, 656m, 631m, 461w (see Fig. S1, ESI†).

2.3. X-ray crystal structure determination

The data for compound (I) was collected on a Bruker Smart Apex II diffractometer equipped with a 1000 CCD instrument by using a graphite monochromator utilizing Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at room temperature. Cell parameters were determined using SMART software [31]. Data reduction and correction were performed using SAINTPlus [32]. Absorption correction was made via SADABS [33]. The structure was solved via direct methods employed in the SHELXS-97 [34] program and refined by full-matrix least-squares methods against F2 with SHELXL-97 [34]. After all non-H atoms were refined anisotropically, hydrogen atoms attached to C atoms were placed geometrically and refined using a riding model approximation, with C–H = 0.93 Å and Uiso(H) = 1.2Ueq(C). H atoms attached to O atom were located from difference Fourier maps; their bond lengths were restrained in the range from 0.82(2) to 0.87(2) Å, and they were refined using a riding model, with Uiso(H) = 1.5Ueq(O). The solvent water molecule was found to be disordered and isotropically restraint was imposed on its anisotropic displacement parameters. Crystal data and structure refinement details were summarized in Table 1.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Synthesis and characterization of (I)

The self-assembly process of compound (I) was achieved through synthetic route in Scheme 2. Particularly, it was obtained from H4bpta and 4,4′-bipy reacting with the copper salts under hydrothermal condition. IR spectra of the compound (I) and H4bptc ligand were given in Fig. S1a, and IR spectra of 4,4′-bipy ligand was in Fig. S1b (ESI†). In the IR spectrum of (I), the broad band in 3436–3200 cm−1 region is characteristic of the O–H stretching of the lattice water molecules and carboxylate groups O–H. The characteristic band at 1690 cm−1 is assigned as the COO– stretching vibrations of the lattice H4bptc ligand, which is slight shift relative to the free H4bptc ligand. In addition, the characteristic two strong bands of the carboxylate groups appeared in the usual region at 1610 and 1563 cm−1 for the antisymmetric stretching vibrations and 1409 and 1343 cm−1 for the symmetric stretching vibrations. The Δ(ν(OCO)asym−ν(OCO)sym) was round 201 and 220 cm−1, showing the presence of bidentate and bridging of carboxylate ions in the complex [35], [36], which is consistent with the features of crystal structure (I). The band observed at 645 cm−1 could be assigned to COO− vibrations of the bridging carboxylate. Further comparing with the IR spectrum of 4,4′-bipy ligand, the characteristic bands in the region from 816, 970, 1076, 1219 and 1481 cm−1 can be regarded as features of the 4,4′-bipy molecule in (I).

3.2. Description of the crystal structure (I)

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that compound (I) crystallizes in the triclinic space group P1¯ , and shows a unique 2D structure. It is a 1:1 co-crystal and composed of two parts, namely, metal-organic moiety and neutral H4bpta ligand. The asymmetric unit consists of one crystallographically independent Cu(II) cation, one H2bpta2− anion, one 4,4′-bipy ligand, half a free H4bpta ligand and one water molecule. As shown in Fig. 1, the penta-coordinated Cu1 with the trigonal bipyramidal geometry (CuN2O3) coordinates to three H2bpta2− ligands in a monodentate fashion through three oxygen atoms (O1, O2ii & O6iii) coordination at the equatorial positions, while its axial positions are occupied by two nitrogen atoms(N1i & N2iv) from two 4,4′-bipy ligands (Symmetry codes: (i) −x, −y + 2, −z; (ii) −x + 1, −y + 1, −z + 1; (iii) −x + 1, −y, −z + 1; (iv) x − 1, y, z). The Cu–O bond lengths range from 1.979 to 2.248 Å, and the Cu–N bond distances are 2.023 and 2.012 Å (Table S1, ESI†), which are all within the normal ranges [37].

The partly deprotonated cis-H2bpta2− ligand has a μ2-bridging mode, connecting two Cu1 atoms to construct a dimeric Cu(II) subunit [Cu2(CO2)2] with Cu1–Cu1ii distances of 4.375(2) Å, and one carboxylate group (5′-COO−) adopts monodentate mode to link one Cu1 atom. Interestingly, the motifs of the dimeric cluster units are linked by H2bpta2− ligands in the [010] direction to form an infinite alternating subloop chains consisting of 8- and 22-membered rings in the metal-organic fragment, as shown in Fig. 2. Moreover, the rings are successively connected by 4,4′-bipy ligands along the a axes to yield a 1D chains; further give rise to a (4,4) rhomboid grid layer (A) with the dimensions of about 12.13(2)Å × 11.11(2) Å (Fig. 3a).

An interesting and peculiar structural feature of (I) is that free H4bpta ligands as guests are threaded within the crystal structure when considering the hydrogen bond bridges involving lattice water molecules and carboxylate groups. In compound (I), the free H4bpta ligands together each other are linked into a 2D (4,4)-sheet (Fig. 3b) with rectangular windows through hydrogen-bonding interactions (O12–H12A···O11 2.544(4) Å, O10–H10···O9 2.555(4) Å) in the bc plane. The adjacent layers are stacked to form a large rectangular channels with an interlayer separation of 12.17(2) Å running in the crystallographic a axis, which are connected via O–H···O and C–H···O hydrogen bonds from crystallization water molecules (Symmetry codes: (ii) −x + 1, −y + 1, −z + 1; (x) x, y − 1, z + 1; (xiii) −x + 1, −y + 2, −z + 1). In the structure of (I), numerous intermolecular hydrogen bonds (Table S2, ESI†) play important roles in stabilizing the structure.

Ultimately, the two motifs (layers A and B) are entangled in such a way that the larger cavities of the A-layers are threaded by B-supramolecular-layers, giving a fascinating heterotwofold pseudo-interpenetration architecture, as illustrated in Fig. 4a. Thus, the interest of the compound (I) has been heightened by the fact that its mechanically interlocked structure is assembled by hydrogen bonds. This result suggests that the molar ratio of starting materials play a significant role in tuning the entangled modes of coordination networks. To gain insight into the supermolecule network, the metal ions are defined 5-connected, and the cis-H2bpta2− ligand is defined 3-connected. H4bpta ligand is defined 4-connected. Therefore, the structure can be simplified by the application of a (3,4,5)-connected topological approach (Fig. 4b).

3.3. Powder X-ray diffraction and thermal analyses

The powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) experiment was carried out for the compound (I). As shown in the PXRD pattern (Fig. 5, left), the experimental patterns of the bulk solids of (I) match well with the simulated patterns from its singlecrystal data, indicating the presence of mainly one crystalline phase in the corresponding compound (I). The TG analysis (Fig. 5, right) indicates that the first step occurred weight loss of 2.20% in the range of 25–115 °C, corresponding to the removal of the one lattice water molecule (calcd. 2.46%); and the second step weight loss of 20.8% (Calcd 21.3%) in the temperature range of 390–443 °C is due to the decomposition of 4,4′-bipy ligand, corresponding to the distinct endothermic peaks in the DTA curve. Then the decomposition of half a free H4bpta ligand occurred rapidly and weight loss is 21.5% (Calcd 22.6%) in the range of 443–496 °C. Continuously, the remaining H2bpta2− ligand (Calcd 45.1%) burning above 496 °C, and the final residue is CuO.

3.4. Magnetic properties

As carboxylate bridging Cu(II) ions can propagate magnetic properties, the compound (I) was subjected to variabletemperature magnetic susceptibility measurements in the 2.0–300 K range with an applied magnetic field of 2000 Oe. The curves of χM and χMT versus T in (I) are shown in Fig. 6a; the χMT value at 300 K is 0.92 cm3 mol−1 K, which is slightly larger than the spin-only value (0.75 cm3 K mol−1, g = 2.0) expected for two magnetically isolated S = 1/2 spin carriers. Upon cooling, the χMT product decreases from 300 to about 20 K, remaining approximately constant below 20 K, which indicates spin coupling between the Cu(II) centers and the presence of antiferromagnetism. Additionally, the χM values continuously increase to attain a maximum 0.0061 cm3 mol−1 at 80 K, and then decrease rapidly to 0.0027 cm3 mol−1 at 9.0 K, finally sharply rise to extreme values at 2 K. The characteristics of χM in the low-temperature range for compound (I) might be attributed to the presence of trace of paramagnetic impurities. The data in the range of 60–300 K follows the Curie–Weiss law χ = C/(T − θ) with C = 1.26(1) cm3 mol−1 K, and θ = −110.29(1) K (Fig. 6b).

According to the magnetic structure of the compound (I) (Fig. 2), it has been interpreted as isolated antiferromagnetically coupled dinuclear copper units. Whereas the magnetic interactions of adjacent dinuclear units should be negligible due to the coupling of the long bridging spaces (distances of H2bpta2−and 4,4′-bipy spaces, 12.13(2) and 11.11(2) Å, respectively) being in a quasi negligible strength or molecular field approximation. Thus, the coupling is given by the isotropic exchange Hamiltonian (H→=−J∑S→1S→2) for two interacting S = l/2 centers. Best fits for two interacting Cu(II) ions were obtained by using the modified Bleaney–Bowers equation [38] (Eq. (1)):(1)χM=2Nβ2g23k(T−θ)[1+1/3exp(−2J/kT)]−1(1−ρ)+[Nβ2g2]ρ3kT+Nαwhere Na is the temperature-independent paramagnetism (60 × 10−6 cgsu per copper), ρ is the fraction of monomeric impurity, and θ is a corrective term for interdimer interactions) and a nonlinear regression procedure. The best fit well reproduces the experimental data over the entire temperature range with g = 2.43(2), J = −35.69(1) cm−1, θ = −32.78(1) cm−1, and ρ = 0.032(1) and the agreement factor defined byR=∑(χMTexp−xMTcal)2/∑(χMTexp)2 is 5.0 × 10−5. The g value is also in accord with that expected for Cu(II) complexes [39]. The negative θ and J values indicate the presence of antiferromagnetic interaction between adjacent Cu(II) ions. As previously noted, the exchange coupling in carboxylatebridged complexes is largely determined by the conformation of the bridge and the interaction between the d orbits of the metal ion and the bridge [29]. The antiferromagnetic coupling (J) between adjacent Cu(II) centers is in agreement with the well-known ability of the syn-syn carboxylate bridged to mediate dominantly antiferromagnetic interactions [40], [41]. In addition, the Cu···Cu distances are small by bridged in a syn-syn manner and the exchange coupling constants are very large. From the structural analysis, the magnetic orbital (dx2−y2) for dinuclear Cu(II) ions with trigonal bipyramidal geometry lies at the basal plane, the basal−basal bridges can efficiently induce magnetic exchange [42]. For (I), the magnetic interaction between Cu(II) ions is strongly antiferromagnetic through sole carboxylate bridges, because unpaired electron density from both Cu(II) ions is transferred to the same orbital of the bridging carboxylate ligands.

4. Conclusions

In summary, we have successfully constructed a new 1:1 co-crystal with two parts of [Cu2(cis-H2bpta)2(4,4′-bipy)2] and H4bpta ligand under hydrothermal condition. The compound (I) exhibits 2D interweaving layered nets via hydrogen bonds based on the two types hetero-molecules, whereas its mechanically interlocked structure is assembled by hydrogen bonds. Magnetic susceptibility measurement indicates that the anti-ferromagnetic exchange interactions (J = −35.69(1) cm−1) through the syn-syn carboxylate bridges exist between adjacent Cu(II) centers in compound (I). The magnetic coupling is mediated through double syn-syn carboxylate bridges in the dimer, which is in accord with the view that the exchange interaction between two M(II) ions bridged by the syn-syn carboxylate bridge is dominantly antiferromagnetic interactions.

  • Syntheses, structures, corresponding fluorescence and magnetism of Zn(II) and Mn(II) coordination complexes based on 1-(3,5-dicarboxylatobenzyl)-1,2,4-1-H-triazole-3-carboxylic acid

    2022, Inorganica Chimica Acta

    Two new coordination complexes [Zn2(HL)2(phen)2]·4H2O (1) and [Mn(HL)(H2O)2]n (2) (H3L = 1-(3,5-dicarboxylatobenzyl)-1,2,4-1-H-triazole-3-carboxylic acid, phen = 1,10-phenanthroline) have been synthesized under solvothermal condition, and characterized by elemental analysis, and infrared spectroscopy, X-ray single-crystal and power diffraction analyses. Both crystal structures of complexes 1 and 2 belong to the monoclinic system with space group P21/c. In the two complexes, abundant weak intermolecular interactions (hydrogen bonds and π···π stacking) make their structure more stable. The thermal decomposition of the complexes was also studied by TGA techniques. Moreover, the solid-stated luminescent spectrum of complex 1 shows the obvious emission peak at 438 nm, and the intensity of the emission peak is stronger than those of free H3L and phen. DFT and TDDFT analyses explain that the fluorescence property of complex 1 derive from the π* - n transition from phen to the adjacent carbonyl of HL2-. Magnetic study reveals that complex 2 exists weak ferromagnetic coupling between manganese(II) centers from 2 K to 300 K.

  • An acid–base resistant paddle-wheel Cu(II) coordination polymer for visible-light-driven photodegradation of organic dyes

    2019, Polyhedron

    A new Cu(II)-based metal–organic framework with open metal sites, {[Cu(L-NO2)(H2O)]·0.25(CH3CN)}n (1) (L-NO2 = 4,4′-dicarboxy-4″-nitrotriphenylamine), has been solvothermally synthesized and characterized, which exhibits a 3-D porous network assembled by 2-D two-fold interpenetrating sheet comprising helical chains. Compound 1 shows outstanding chemical robustness which can withstand acidic or basic aqueous solution as well as various organic solvents. In particular, compound 1 displays a notable visible-light photocatalytic activity for degrading organic dyes (e.g. crystal violet (CV), rhodamine 6G (R6G), congo red (CR) and basic red 2 (BR2)) with the assistance of H2O2.
