Co-crystals are at the forefront of the quest for novel crystal forms. There is a strong interest in their potential use in the pharmaceutical field and in all areas where the final products are commercialized and utilized in their solid state forms.1,2 The great potential of co-crystals resides in their different properties (solubility, dissolution rate, melting point, colour, etc.) with respect to those of the co-formers; at the same time—and this is particularly relevant to the pharmaceutical field—the process of co-crystal formation does not compromise the chemical integrity of the component of interest, e.g. an API (active pharmaceutical ingredient). In the academic world co-crystals are intensively investigated also because they provide new insights into mechanisms of recognition, assembly and packing.3–5

The definition of a co-crystal is still controversial, because the borderline between solvates, molecular salts (e.g. when proton transfer along a hydrogen bond is involved) and molecular complexes is somewhat difficult to define;6–13 however, the general expression that a co-crystal is a “multicomponent assembly held together by reversible, non-covalent interactions”1 is herein accepted. Usually co-formers are organic molecules chosen on the basis of their ability to form supramolecular aggregates with the main component,14–17 or charge transfer complexes.18–20 Electrostatic interactions such as those present in alkaline salts, on the contrary, are not usually employed, and the design of co-crystals where an inorganic salt is used as a co-former is still an unexplored field. Interestingly, though, the oldest example of intentional co-crystallization of an organic molecule with an alkaline salt dates back to 1946, when Beevers and Cochran21 were successful in determining the structure of sucrose by co-crystallizing it with NaCl.

In this communication we report on a new family of ionic co-crystals, formed by an organic molecule (barbituric acid) and an ionic salt (an alkali halide). We also show that ionic co-crystal formation can be exploited as a viable route to improve the dissolution properties of the organic system in an aqueous medium, an observation that may have relevant implications with APIs. Pure barbituric acid (BA) is not pharmacologically active, but substitution at C(5) results in the barbiturate family of hypnotic, sedative and anticonvulsantdrugs, such as barbital and phenobarbital.

In a recent paper by Griesser et al.22 the IR-spectrum of the anhydrous form II of BA as KBr-disks was reported: the IR spectrum shows an additional absorption band at 3500 cm−1 which could only be explained by assuming that some solid state transformation has occurred. Alkali halides, such as KBr and CsI, used for IR pellets, are known to react in some cases with the embedded sample,23–25 causing an alteration of the absorption frequencies with respect to those measured in solution or Nujol. For these reasons we decided to investigate the possibility of co-crystallization reactions between solid BA and the solid alkali halides. Four types of approaches were used: grinding of the solid reactants in a 1 ∶ 1 stoichiometric ratio, kneading, i.e.grinding with the addition of a drop of MeOH,26vapour digestion,27i.e. exposure of the 1 ∶ 1 stoichiometric mixtures to vapours of MeOH, and direct crystallization from EtOH or MeOH solutions. The formation of BA·KBr·2H2O could also be observed if the reaction was conducted mechanochemically with form III of BA as well as with the hydrated form of barbituric acid.28

In all the ionic co-crystals BA is present as a neutral molecule, with intramolecular bond distances and angles that are strictly comparable to those of its crystal structure. In addition to this, 13C CPMASSSNMRspectra show no tautomeric shift for the signal around 43 ppm, which is typical of the presence of a CH2group.28,29 All these observations concur to establish the ketonic nature of barbituric acid.

The hydrated co-crystals BA·MBr·2H2O (M = Na, K, Rb) are isomorphous, and isomorphism is observed also for the two anhydrous BA·MBr (M = Rb, Cs) co-crystals. The formation of hydrated or anhydrous co-crystals depends on the radius of the metal ion: the coordination sphere for the smaller Na+ and K+ cations is made of BA and water molecules, while in the case of Cs+ it is made of BA and Br ions. The Rb+ cation clearly represents a borderline condition, because both the hydrated and the anhydrous co-crystals can be obtained.

In the BA·MBr·2H2O (M = Na, K, Rb) co-crystals the water molecules act as bridges between alkali cations and bromide anions: together they form all-inorganic sheets that are “intercalated” between layers of discrete BA molecules (see Fig. 1).

The anhydrous ionic co-crystals BA·MBr (M = Rb, Cs), on the other hand, are characterized by the presence of BA dimers linked via N–H⋯O hydrogen bonds and interacting with the Br− and M+ ions viaO⋯M+ and H⋯Br− interactions (see Fig. 2).

The formation of ionic co-crystals is not a phenomenon limited to alkali bromides30: BA has been co-crystallized also with CsI, another salt frequently used in the preparation of pellets for FTIR. The resulting BA·CsI co-crystal is anhydrous, but is not isomorphous with the bromide analogue, as it can be seen in Fig. 3. The BA molecules in BaCsI also form dimers via N–H⋯O hydrogen bonds, as observed in anhydrous BAMBr. The overall structure is characterized by the alternation of BAhydrogen bonded dimers and caesium and iodide ions.

The ATR-IRspectra of the ionic co-crystals BA·MBr·2H2O are reported in Fig. 4. The absorption bands at ca. 3500 and 1600 cm−1 in the spectra of the hydrates can be attributed to νOH stretching and δOH2 bending of the water molecules, respectively. As expected, these features are not present in the anhydrous co-crystals.

It is worth noting that the spectrum of BA·KBr·2H2O is superimposable with those—recorded as KBr pellets—previously reported by us28 for the forms II and III of barbituric acid and later discussed by Griesser,22 who proposed a tautomeric transformation of barbituric acid, in the solid-state, triggered by the presence of KBr; we can now confidently assign those spectra to the ionic co-crystal BA·KBr·2H2O.

The DSC curves for the hydrated ionic co-crystals all show an endothermic peak due to loss of the two water molecules and transformation into the anhydrous co-crystal, as confirmed by TGA traces (see ESI†). All crystals show incongruent melting followed by decomposition: while for the solid containing sodium and potassium this is observed at temperatures (onset values 255 and 249 °C, respectively) very close to the melting point of pure barbituric acid (245 °C), for the co-crystals containing RbBr, CsBr and CsI the value is much higher (307, 298 and 308 °C, respectively) (see ESI†).

In addition to being a means to modify the thermal stability of a given solid component, co-crystallization can represent a strategy to alter its dissolution properties.31 As a matter of fact the dissolution rates of the anhydrous co-crystals of BA‡ are all higher than for crystalline BA (form II) (see Table 2).

The possibility of increasing the thermal stability of an organic molecule or that of changing the dissolution and solubility properties by ionic co-crystallization may have important implications in all areas, beside the pharmaceutical field,31 where crystal forms are investigated and utilized, e.g. pigments, explosives, agrochemicalsetc.

One may wonder, however, what could be the driving force for the formation of the ionic co-crystals between BA and alkali cations. Beside an entropic contribution to the crystal free energy arising from the multi-component nature of the co-crystal, the BA–alkali salt interactions must obviously (over)compensate for the loss of packing energy of the pure ionic salts. The interaction between the inorganic ions and the organic moiety (with intervention of the water molecules in the case of the smaller cations) ought to be regarded as a special kind of solvation process with barbituric acid behaving like a solvent molecule (in competition with water) with respect to the ions. Insight in the phenomenon requires theoretical evaluation of the different enthalpic and entropic contributions, which we hope this communication will stimulate.
