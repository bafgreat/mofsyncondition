Introduction

In the rapidly growing field of metal–organic polymer chemistry, the design and synthesis of compounds with unique structure and function can be regarded as a long-term challenge. A major reason for this interest is the possibility of being able to generate new materials with intriguing architectures1 and potential applications.2 Usually, the construction of molecular architecture depends on the coordination geometry of metal ions, the organic ligands and counterions, the temperature and the ratio between metal salt and a ligand.3 Considerable research effort has been directed towards the use of the rigid bridging ligandsterephthalic acid and trimesic acid as well as related ligands in which various spacer groups connect carboxylic pairs.4 Nevertheless, the exploration of new ligand systems, for which certain design principles have been followed, can lead to directed supramolecular motifs through the interplay of structure-directing effects of the metal ion's coordination preferences and the introduction of specific non-coordination groups on to the ligand backbone. For example, we demonstrated this principle using a dinuclear FeIII building block and a set of iminodiacetic acid substituted phenols/naphthols where the shape and functionality of aromatic ligands was shown to determine the dimensions and architecture of the inorganic regions of the resulting hybrid materials.5

At the same time, various theoretical and experimental studies6 have considerably advanced the understanding of water structures and provided novel structural aspects of water and new insights into the behaviour of water with implications in biological and environmental contexts. Small water clusters (H2O)n (n = 4–10) are crucial building units for extended water morphologies including tapes7 and layers8 whose physical properties are closely associated with those of bulk water.9 So far, a variety of tetramer,10apentamer,10bhexamer,10cheptamer,10d,eoctamer10f and decamer10gwater clusters have been isolated in different solid crystalline hosts.10h–j

Our approach introduces more groups to the rigid aromatic scaffold that allow for extra metal–ligand interactions and for the build-up of higher-dimensionality motifs. In previous work on the build-up of CuII aggregates we used 2-(carboxyphenyl)iminodiacetic acid (H3cpida) to isolate a series of Cu12 aggregates supramolecularly connected through secondary interactions of the carboxylate moieties with counterions.11 As an extension of this work we have synthesized two ligands closely related to H3cpida, 2-(carboxyphenyl)iminodipropanoic acid (H3cpidp) and 2-(carboxyphenyl)iminoacetic propanoic acid (H3cpiap) (Scheme 1) which have been employed in one-pot reactions with Cu(NO3)2·3H2O. H3cpidp is similar to H3cpiap (Scheme 1) differing only in the symmetry of the tripodal iminocarboxylate arms. While H3cpidp has equal length carboxylate arms, hence symmetrical tripodal moiety, H3cpiap is asymmetric presenting one longer and one shorter carboxylate arm. The interest here was to investigate the influence of such difference in the length of carboxylate arm in coordination to CuII ions.

Herein, we describe the synthesis of these two new ligands and their use in the preparation of two 1D coordination polymers {[Cu3(cpiap)2(H2O)3](µ-H2O)}n·7nH2O (1) and [Cu(H2cpidp)2(H2O)2]n·4nH2O (2). The structures of compounds 1 and 2 as well as the magnetic properties of 1 are presented. We found a T5(2) and a C4 water tape formed viahydrogen-bonding interactions of the lattice water molecules in 1 and 2, respectively.

Experimental

Materials and methods

All chemicals and solvents used for synthesis were obtained from commercial sources and were used as received without further purification. All reactions were carried out under aerobic conditions. FTIR spectra were measured on a Perkin-Elmer Spectrum One spectrometer with samples prepared as KBr discs. 1H NMR analyses were obtained using a Bruker AC 400 spectrometer with samples in solutions of DMSO-d6.

Synthesis of 2-(carboxyphenyl)iminoacetic propanoic acid (H3cpiap)

The ligand was prepared by condensing 2-(carboxyphenyl)iminopropanoic acid (8.30 g, 0.040 mol) in 5 ml water in a three-necked flask. A 5-N sodium hydroxide aqueous solution (8 ml, 0.040 mol) was added with vigorous stirring. Once the 2-(carboxyphenyl)iminopropanoic acid had dissolved, chloroacetic acid (11.1 g, 0.120 mol) was added. The solution was heated to 60 °C on an oil bath while stirring and a 5-N sodium hydroxide aqueous solution (23 ml) was added dropwise. After heating and stirring for 20 h, the temperature was lowered to room temperature, and the solution volume was concentrated. Hydrochloric acid (0.134 mol) was added, and the reaction left to stand overnight. The pale brown product was obtained as brick-shaped crystals in 75% yield. Found: C, 50.03; H, 5.30; N, 4.94; Calc.: C, 50.52; H, 5.26; N, 4.91%. IR (KBr disk, ν/cm−1): 3344 w, 2973 w, 2500 w, 1942 w, 1720 s, 1598 m, 1564 w, 1491 m, 1424 m, 1384 s, 1234 w, 998 m, 913 s, 838 m, 761 m and 511 m. 1H-NMR (DMSO-d6): δ = 2.46 (tr, 2H), 3.40 (tr, 2H), 4.29 (s, 2H), 7.4 (m, 4H).

Synthesis of 2-(carboxyphenyl)iminodipropanoic acid (H3cpidp)

The ligand was prepared by condensing anthranilic acid (7.0 g, 0.51 mol) in 8 ml water in a three-necked flask. A 5-N sodium hydroxide aqueous solution (10.2 ml, 0.051 mol) was added while stirring well. After dissolving the anthranilic acid, chloropropanoic acid (18.29 g, 0.157 mol) was added. The solution was then heated to 60 °C on an oil bath while stirring and a 5-N sodium hydroxide aqueous solution (31.5 ml) was added dropwise. After heating and stirring for 20 h, the temperature was lowered to room temperature, and the solution volume was concentrated. Hydrochloric acid (0.174 mol) was added, the turbid solution was filtered and the reaction left to stand overnight. The product was obtained as needle-shaped crystals in 40% yield. Found: C, 55.43; H, 5.40; N, 5.18; Calc.: C, 55.52; H, 5.33; N, 4.98%. IR (KBr disk, ν/cm−1): 3025 s, 2924 w, 2529 w, 1955 w, 1734 s, 1556 s, 1498 s, 1457 m, 1378 w, 1252 m, 1184 m, 1077 w, 974 s, 849 s, 816 s, 767 m, 703 s, 662 s, 586 m. 1H-NMR (DMSO-d6): δ = 2.35 (tr, 4H), 3.7 (tr, 4H), 7.4 (m, 4H).

Synthesis of {[Cu3(cpiap)2(H2O)3](µ-H2O)}n·7nH2O, (1)

To a solution of Cu(NO3)2·3(H2O) (0.120 g, 0.5 mmol) in 10 ml of water, a solution of H3cpiap (0.067 g, 0.25 mmol) and diisopropyl amine (0.100 ml, 0.75 mmol) in 10 ml of water was added with stirring. Filtration and slow evaporation of the resulting blue solution gave blue crystals of 1 in 3 weeks in about 10% yield. Elemental analysis (%) Found: C, 31.94; H, 4.61; N, 3.42; Calc. for C24H40Cu3N2O22 (loss of 1 H2O): C, 32.06; H, 4.48; N, 3.12%. Synthesis of [Cu(H2cpidp)2(H2O)2]n·4nH2O, (2)

Compound 2 was prepared in a similar way to that described for 1, using H3cpidp in place of H3cpiap. Slow evaporation of the resulting blue solution gave pale blue plate crystals of 2 in a week. Yield: 37.6%. Elemental analysis (%) Found: C, 43.40; H, 5.66; N, 4.09; Calc. for C26H38.6CuN2O17.3 (loss of 0.7 H2O): C, 43.40; H, 5.41; N, 3.89%. Magnetic measurements

Magnetic susceptibility measurements were obtained using a Quantum Design SQUID MPMS-XL susceptometer. This magnetometer works between 1.8 and 300 K for dc applied fields ranging from −7 to +7 T. Measurements for 1 were performed on a polycrystalline sample of 22.2 mg. The magnetic data were corrected for the sample holder and the diamagnetic contribution. The samples were first checked for the presence of ferromagnetic impurities by measuring the magnetization as a function of the field at 100 K. No ferromagnetic impurities have been detected.

X-Ray data collection and structure determination

Data were collected on Stoe Sapphire CCD (1) and Stoe IPDS II (2) diffractometers using graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation. Semi-empirical absorption corrections were made using XPREP in SHELXTL.12 The structures were solved using direct methods, followed by full-matrix least-squares refinement against F2 (all data) using SHELXTL.12 Anisotropic refinement was used for all ordered non-H atoms; organic H atoms were placed in calculated positions, while coordinates of hydroxo or water H atoms were refined. Results and discussion

Synthesis

Compounds 1 and 2 were prepared by the reaction of the corresponding ligand, and Cu(NO3)2·3H2O with diisopropyl amine in aqueous solution at room temperature. Both compounds are quite stable and retain their crystallinity even when exposed to air.

Structure description

The structure of {[Cu3(cpiap)2(H2O)3](µH2O)}n·7nH2O (1) is based on 1D chains, which interact with each other through hydrogen bonding and C–H⋯π interactions. The trinuclear complex unit has two-fold rotational symmetry, with one crystal two-fold axis passing through Cu(2) and the midpoint of Cu(1)⋯Cu(1′), and another through the bridging waterligand O(7). Cu(1) is six-coordinated in a Jahn–Teller-elongated octahedral environment. It is chelated by a (cpiap)3−ligandvia its iminonitrogen and one oxygen of each of the three carboxylate groups. The remaining two cis-related coordination sites are occupied by O(1′), a propionate oxygen from the second ligand chelating Cu(1′), and the bridging aqua ligand O(7). The axial positions on Cu(1) are occupied by the carboxylate oxygen atom O(1) belonging to its own (cpiap)3−ligand and by O(7). Cu(1) and Cu(1′) are therefore linked by two equivalent alkoxy (eq-ax) bridges. Cu(2) is five-coordinated. The equatorial plane of the five-coordinate Cu(2) is occupied by two water molecules, O(9) and O(9′), and by the two benzoate oxygen atoms, O(6) and O(6′), from the two ligands (O6 and O6′), while the apical position is occupied by a water molecule, O(8). Analysis of the angles using the approach of Reedijk et al. yields a value for τ of 0.15, so that the environment of Cu(2) can be described as a slightly-distorted square pyramidal geometry.13Cu(1) and Cu(1′) are thus each linked to Cu(2)via a syn(eq)-anti(eq) carboxylate bridge. All the carboxylic groups of the ligands are deprotonated and coordinated in different modes (monodentate, monodentate–bridging, and bidentate in a syn–anti mode, Scheme 2), forming a trinuclear copper unit (Fig. 1).

The two independent metal–metal distances within this trinuclear unit are 3.4266(6) Å (Cu(1)⋯Cu(1′)) and 4.5304(6) Å (Cu(1)⋯Cu(2)). These units are linked into infinite 1D chains via the oxygen atom O(7) of the aqua ligand which bridges Cu(1) and Cu(1″) at {−x, 1 − y, z}, with Cu(1)–O(7) 2.3776(7) Å and Cu(1)–O(7)–Cu(1″) 152.18(13)° (Fig. 1). Bridging water molecules occur in several crystal hydrates,14 but they are rather rare in coordination polymers.15 The Cu⋯Cu distance through the water bridge is 4.6158(6) Å.

Further analysis of the crystal packing reveals that this structure also contains extensive hydrogen bonding between the lattice water molecules forming a water tape. A classification of the water tapes by Infantes and Motherwell revealed 23 different patterns.10i Among all these patterns, three are more dominant than others, namely T5(2), T4(2)6(2) and T6(2). Accordingly, T5(2) water clusters are stabilized either in organic hosts16 or more rarely in metal–organic networks.17

Interestingly, a T5(2) water tape appears in the 3D supramolecular structure of 1. The five lattice water molecules O(12), O(13)c, O(14)d, O(12)b and O(14)a are arranged in a non planar cyclic pentamer. (Fig. 2). The O⋯O⋯O angles are in the range 88.90–122.65°, while the five O⋯O⋯O⋯O torsional angles in the ring are −28.2, 25.1, −7.1, −14.2 and 25.3° indicating that the water pentamer conformation is similar to cyclopentane with one water molecule O(14)e above the plane formed by the other four oxygens by 0.819 Å. The O⋯O distances within the pentamer range from 2.735 to 3.013 Å (Table 2), which are comparable with the values obtained from ab initio calculations,18 and the distances in ice Ih (2.759 Å at 200 K).19 Within the pentamer, all the oxygen atoms act as single donors and single acceptors. Furthermore, adjacent pentamers are fused together by sharing one edge, forming a T5(2) water tape along the b axis, with the O⋯O distance of the shared edge at 2.770 Å.

Moreover, the [Cu3(cpiap)2(H2O)3](μ-H2O), coordination chains are linked by the adjacent water chains to form a 3D supramolecular architecture (Fig. 3). Various O–H⋯O hydrogen bonding and C–H⋯π interactions can be identified. The first involves the water clusters and the [Cu3(cpiap)2(H2O)3](μ-H2O) chains, while the second involves the aryl rings of the 1D coordination hosts, which both further stabilize the overall 3D supramolecular framework of 1. On the other hand, the pentameric water cluster shows a 5-connected mode to bridge three trinuclear units and two adjacent pentamers. Each trinuclear unit links six pentameric water clustersvia the lattice water molecules O(14)d, O(13)c and O(14)a and two adjacent trinuclear units via the cpiap3− ligand bridges to result in an 8-connected node. Thus, complex 1 forms a novel binodal (5,8)-connected topological net with a Schläfli symbol of (32.42.55.6)2(32.44.510.610.72).20 It should also be noted that the compound crystallises in the chiral space groupP21212. From the Flack parameter of −0.024(11) it is clear that we have the correct absolute configuration with, in this case, the chiral cpiap3−ligand in S-conformation. This means that creating and crystallising this coordination polymer has led to a chiral separation and we can expect an equal number of crystals with the ligand in R-configuration to be formed.

Compound [Cu(H2cpidp)2(H2O)2]n·4nH2O, 2, crystallises in the P21/c space group and is based on 1-D zig–zag polymeric chains, which interact with each other through hydrogen bonding (Fig. 4).

The CuII center Cu(1) in 2 lies on an inversion centre, and is six-coordinated in a distorted (Jahn–Teller elongated) octahedral geometry. In addition to two oxygen atoms from two trans-related water molecules, O(7) and O(7a) (for symmetry codes, see Fig. 4), Cu(1) is ligated by four carboxylate oxygens, unusually each from a different-ligand molecule. O(1) and O(1a), together with the two waterligands, occupy the equatorial sites, with Cu(1)–O(1) and Cu(1)–O(7) 1.9797(13) and 1.9274(14) Å, respectively. The two axial sites are occupied by two protonated carboxylate oxygens O(3′′) and O(3′′′), with Cu(1)–O(3′′) 2.6144(15) Å. Each ligand is monoanionic (Scheme 2 right), carrying two protons, one on the nitrogen, where it makes an intraligand hydrogen bond to the benzoatecarboxylate oxygen O(6) instead of coordinating to Cu(1), and the other on O(3). It is unusual for a carboxylate oxygen coordinated to a transition metal to be protonated, particularly when there are other potentially more basic uncoordinated oxygens present as alternatives, but the protonation site is supported by the C–O bond lengths: C(6)–O(3) and C(6)–O(4) are 1.313(2) and 1.218(2) Å, respectively, while those in the other carboxylate groups are all in the range of 1.231–1.286 Å. Both hydrogens could be located and refined in the structure.

In this way, the structure of 2 builds into one-dimensional chains along the b-axis. The CuII centres along the chain can be considered as magnetically isolated from each other, with the Cu⋯Cu distance of 7.3389(7) Å, and no clear magnetic pathway between them. In the structure there are two lattice water molecules, which make hydrogen bonds between adjacent 1-D chains, forming hydrogen-bonded layers parallel to the [1 0 −1] plane. These 2-D layers then interact with each other through π-stacking interactions between the aryl moieties, forming a 3-D supramolecular architecture (Fig. 5). Interestingly, through a symmetry operation the two lattice water molecules form an infinite C4 water tape, running parallel to the b-axis (Fig. 5) similar to a previously reported example.7f

Magnetic properties

The magnetic susceptibility of compound 1 was studied from 300 to 1.8 K at 1000 Oe and the χTvs. T plot is shown in Fig. 6. The χT product at room temperature is 1.37 cm3K mol−1. This value is in good agreement with the expected Curie constant for the presence of three Cu(II) ions in compound 1 (S = ½, g = 2.2, C = 0.457 cm3K mol−1). When the temperature is lowered, the χT product at 1000 Oe stays roughly constant down to 50 K and then continuously increases to reach 2.1 cm3K mol−1 at 1.8 K that corresponds to an S = 3/2 ground state. This type of behaviour indicates dominant ferromagnetic interactions in this trinuclear Cu(II) complex. To model the temperature dependence of the magnetic susceptibility, a simple S = ½ triangular model as shown in Scheme 3 was used.

A least-squares fit of the data leads to the following set of parameters: J1/kB = +2.0(1) K, J2/kB = +8.6(5) K and g = 2.17. In line with the structural analysis of 1, the magnitude of the magnetic interaction (J2) through the double alkoxy (eq-ax) bridges is higher than that (J1) through the syn(eq)–anti(eq) linkage of the carboxylate ligand. While the presence of ferromagnetic interactions between the double alkoxy bridge in an eq-ax mode is well known and well understood in terms of the orthogonality of the Cu(II) dx2−y2 magnetic orbitals,21,23 there is much less work done on syn(eq)–anti(eq) carboxylate bridges between Cu(II) metal ions, although it usually stabilizes ferromagnetic interactions.24 It is worth mentioning that the magnitude of the magnetic interactions deduced above from our fitting approach falls very well in the range of magnetic interactions previously estimated.21,23,24

The field dependence of magnetization at low temperatures (Fig. 7), reveals a true saturation above 4 T indicative of weak or absent magnetic anisotropy. The saturation of M at 3.2 µB is very close to the value of 3 µB expected for three CuII (S = 1/2) ions. This result has been confirmed by the MvsH/T plots which are superposed on to a single master-curve as expected for an isotropic system and thus for Cu(II) metal ions. The MvsH/T data between 1.8–4 K are very well reproduced by an S = 3/2 Brillouin function with a g value around 2.16 that is in very good agreement with the value deduced from the χTvsT analysis. This result confirms the ST = 3/2 ground state for this complex.

Theoretical study

The stability of the water tapes in 1 was probed using a theoretical approach. All calculations were carried out with the TURBOMOLE program package.25 Combinations of several DFT functionals and basis sets were tested against the more accurate MP2/aug-cc-pVQZ method on the global minima of various water clusters (H2O)n, n = 1,…, 6. Among these combinations, the TPSS functional in combination with the def2-TZVPP was found to yield results of the same quality as the MP2/aug-cc-pVQZ method. DFT and MP2 calculations were performed using the resolution of the identity (RI) approximation. All energies were corrected for the basis-set superposition error (BSSE) by means of counterpoise calculations. The dissociation energies of various water clusters with respect to dissociation into the monomers were calculated as follows: (1) −4.54 and −4.86 kcal mol−1 for the dimer, (2) −14.91 and −15.44 kcal mol−1 for the trimer, (3) −27.19 and −27.02 kcal mol−1 for the tetramer, (4) −36.09 and −35.51 kcal mol−1 for the pentamer and (5) −44.53 and −43.86 kcal mol−1 for the hexamer, from TPSS/def2-TZVPP and MP2/aug-cc-pVQZ calculations, respectively. Based on these results, the combination of the TPSS functional with the def2-TZVPP basis set was chosen for the calculations on the water tape structures.

In the first step, the structure of the water tapes was optimized. It was found that the structures do not correspond to minima on the potential energy surface, and upon optimization, they transformed into various common water clusters. In order to estimate the relative stability of the water tapes with respect to the global minima of the water clusters, the crystal environment must be included in the simulation. For this reason, a cluster-model approximation was used. A large enough part of the crystal was isolated and treated as an individual system. The crystal model contained the carboxylate groups and some watersolvent molecules with which the water tape forms hydrogen bonds. Dangling bonds were saturated with hydrogen atoms. The effect of the Cu2+ ions was simulated by substitution with Li+ ions. Partial optimizations of the water tapes inside the crystal were performed. The atoms of the crystal model were kept frozen and only the water-tape molecules were allowed to relax, so that the bonds and angles took their optimum values. For the water tape molecules, the def2-TZVPP basis set was used but for the atoms of the crystal environment we used the smaller def2-SVP basis set. Two-ring and four-ring T5(2) water tapes were studied, that is, T5(2) water tapes with two and four water pentamers fused in rings, as depicted in Fig. 2. After the partial optimization, the interaction energies of the two water tapes with the crystal environment were calculated. Single-point energy calculations were performed with the larger def2-TZVPP basis set applied to all atoms, and the interaction energies (IE) were calculated as IE = E (crystal − water tape) − E (crystal, ghost) − E (water tape, ghost) where a term such as E(water tape, ghost) denotes that the energy of the water tape was calculated at the complex geometry in the presence of the ghost basis of the crystal-model molecules.

In the second and final step, the relative stabilities of the two-ring and four-ring water tapes were calculated for the dissociation into the corresponding monomers. After the partial geometry optimizations, the water tape structures retained their starting configuration due to the confinement from the environment and they did not collapse into various common water clusters. The interaction energies of the water tapes with the crystal environment were calculated to be −36.46 and −34.32 kcal mol−1 for the two-ring and four-ring, respectively. The two-ring T5(2) is isomeric to a water octamer of cubic conformation. The relative energy difference between the tape and the cubic structure is 14.95 kcal mol−1. The dissociation energy of the two-ring T5(2) water tape into eight water monomers was calculated to be −53.26 kcal mol−1, whereas it is −68.21 kcal mol−1 for the cubic structure. The dissociation energy of the four-ring water tape into fourteen water molecules was calculated to be −103.87 kcal mol−1, that is, 7.42 kcal mol−1 for each water molecule, whereas the corresponding value for the two-ring case is 6.66 kcal mol−1. If a comparison is made with respect to the number of the hydrogen bonds in each water tape, then the mean binding energy per hydrogen bond is 5.92 and 6.11 kcal mol−1 for the two-ring and four-ring tapes, respectively.

Conclusion

In this article we have described the synthesis and characterization of two new ligands 2-(carboxyphenyl)iminoacetic propanoic acid (H3cpiap) and 2-(carboxyphenyl)iminodipropanoic acid, (H3cpidp), which can be utilized for the synthesis of coordination polymers. The reaction of copper nitrate and H3cpiap at room temperature results in the formation of 1, which consists of trinuclear units, which are extended to 1D chains through a water bridge, a rare phenomenon in metal–organic polymer chemistry. Moreover, a well-resolved five-membered fused water tape T5(2) is formed in the crystal structure and the stability of this was probed using a theoretical approach. This tape interacts with the coordination chains forming a metallo supramolecular architecture which is a novel 3D binodal (5,8)-connected topological net with a Schläfli symbol of (32.42.55.6)2(32.44.510.610.72). In contrast, a reaction of copper nitrate and H3cpidp at room temperature results in the formation of 2, which consists of 1D zig–zag coordination polymers. The influence of the ligand on the crystal growth of both compounds is reflected in the formation of trinuclear repeating units in 1, while compound 2 is a simple 1D coordination polymer. Magnetic studies carried out in 1 are consistent with dominant ferromagnetic interactions between the CuII centers within the complex, resulting in a spin ground state of S = 3/2. Experiments with other 3d transition metal and 4f lanthanides are in progress.
