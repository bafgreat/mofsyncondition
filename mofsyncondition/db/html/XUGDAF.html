Construction of three one-dimensional zinc(II) complexes containing pyrazine-2,3-dicarboxylic acid

Author links open overlay panel
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ica.2008.11.033Get rights and content

Abstract

Three one-dimensional zinc complexes, namely, [Zn(pzdc) · 3H2O] · H2O (1), [Zn2(pzdc)2 · 4H2O] · 2.5H2O (2), and [Zn(pzdc)(phen) · 4H2O]n (3) (H2pzdc, pyrazine-2,3-dicarboxylic acid, phen = 1,10-phenanthroline), have been synthesized successfully under hydrothermal condition. X-ray diffraction analyses reveal that complex 1 is a square-wave-like chain and complex 2 shows a 1D ladder-like infinite chain, while complex 3 has 1D zigzag chain structure. In all cases, the Zn(II) centers have octahedral coordination geometries. Through hydrogen bonding (such as O–H···O, O–H···N and C–H···O) and/or π–π stacking interactions, three-dimensional supramolecular networks are constructed in three complexes. Furthermore, the IR, TGA and luminescent properties are also investigated in this work.

1. Introduction

Current interest in metal–organic polymers (MOPs) or metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) with pyridine dicarboxylate is rapidly expanding for their potential applications and intriguing structural features, so that a large number of different dimensional coordination polymers containing such kind of ligands have been recently prepared and characterized [1], [2], [3], [4]. Even so, the prediction and synthesis of the solid-state architectures still remain a long-term challenge for the chemists [5]. On the other hand, the development of supramolecular assembled chemistry has allowed the possibility to rationally design and prepare supramolecular architectures [6], [7], [8]. Meanwhile, many investigations indicate that the combination of metal coordination and supramolecular interaction (hydrogen bonding and π···π stacking) is a powerful force for supramolecular structural assembly in crystal engineering [9].

Compared with pyridine dicarboxylic acids, pyrazine 2,3-dicarboxylic acid (H2pzdc) has advantages over the former such as having richer coordination modes, being the hydrogen-bond donors and acceptors [10]. Pyrazine 2,3-dicarboxylic acid is therefore regarded as excellent candidates for the construction of MOPs/MOFs [11], [12], [13], [14], so that recently many complexes containing Cu2+, Cd2+, Ln3+, Ag+, Mn2+, etc. are reported [15], [16], [17], [18], [19], [20]. However, by comparison, its zinc(II) coordination chemistry has not been extensively explored until now [21], [22], [23], [24], [25], especially, few efforts have been devoted to the investigation on assembly of metal–organic coordination polymers induced by the external factors, such as temperature, pH and auxiliary ligand, governing the crystallization process and the stability of the overall crystals. Our interest focuses on the d10 metal-based H2pzdc for the self-assemblies of metal–organic coordinations and supramolecular architectures under different reaction conditions.

In the past years, we have prepared a series of new metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) in the presence of heterocyclic multi-carboxylic acids via the hydrothermal synthesis, and their supramolecular architectures have also been investigated [26], [27]. In order to continue and extend our research work, we have recently synthesized three new Zn(II) complexes with pyrazine 2,3-dicarboxylic acid, that is, [Zn(pzdc) · 3H2O] · H2O (1), [Zn2(pzdc)2 · 4H2O] · 2.5H2O (2), and [Zn(pzdc)(phen) · 4H2O]n (3). Although all compounds display 1D chain structure, shapes of 1D chains, namely, square-wave-like for 1, ladder-like for 2 and zigzag-like for 3, may be tuned by changing the reaction temperature and the auxiliary ligand. Here we like to report the synthesis, characteristics and properties of the three 1D Zn(II)-based complexes with different shapes based on pyrazine 2,3-dicarboxylate (pzdc2−).

2. Experimental

2.1. Materials and measurements

All materials were reagent grade, obtained from commercial sources and used without further purification. Elemental analyses were performed on a Perkin–Elmer 240C analytical instrument. The metal analysis was performed on an ICP AES Liberty Series II Varian apparatus. IR spectra in the 4000–400 cm−1 range were measured with a Thermo Nicolet 320 FT-IR spectrometer using KBr disks. Thermal analyses (under nitrogenated atmosphere, heating rate of 5 °C/min) were carried out in a Labsys NETZSCH TG 209 Setaram apparatus. The luminescent spectra for the solid state were recorded at room temperature on an Aminco Bowman Series 2 spectrofluorometer with a xenon arc lamp as the light source. In the measurements of emission and excitation spectra the pass width is 5.0 nm.

2.2. Preparation of complex { [Zn(pzdc) · 3H2O] · H2O}n (1)

A mixture of Zn(ClO4)2 · 6H2O (0.079 g, 0.3 mmol), pyrazine-2,3-dicarboxylic acid (0.05 g, 0.3 mmol) and H2O (10 ml) was sealed in a 20 cm3 stainless steel reactor with Teflon liner and kept under autogenous pressure at 100 °C for 78 h, and then cooled to room temperature at a rate of 0.5 °C min−1. Colorless block crystals of 1 suited for single crystal X-ray diffraction analyses formed with a yield of approximate 65% (based on Zn). Anal. calcd. for C6H10N2O8Zn: C, 23.69; H, 3.41; N, 9.25; Zn, 21.43. Found: C, 23.72; H, 3.29; N, 9.22; Zn, 21.41%. Selected IR data (KBr, cm−1): 3465(br), 3176(s, 2884(m, 2828(s), 1614(vs), 1569(vs), 1437(m), 1367(vs), 1232(w), 1168(m), 846(s), 760(m), 445(m).

2.3. Preparation of complex {[Zn2(pzdc)2 · 4H2O] · 2.5H2O}n (2)

Similar to the preparation of 1, the reaction containing the same reactants sealed in a 20 cm3 Teflon reactor under autogenous pressure was performed at 150 °C for 72 h and then was cooled to room temperature at a rate of ca. 0.5 °C min−1 to give colorless prism crystals of 2 (yield: 51% based on Zn). Anal. calcd. for C12H17N4O14.5Zn2: C, 24.78; H, 3.05; N, 9.63; Zn, 22.32. Found: C, 24.83; H, 2.93; N, 9.65; Zn, 22.41%. IR (KBr pellet)/cm−1: 3479(br), 3071(vs), 1669(vs), 1576(vs), 1467(s), 1442(m), 1359(s), 1369(vs), 1205(w), 1130(s), 844(s), 448(m).

2.4. Preparation of complex [Zn(pzdc)(phen) · 4H2O]n (3)

A solution of Zn(OAc)2 · 2H2O (0.066 g, 0.3 mmol), pyrazine-2,3-dicarboxylic acid (0.05 g, 0.3 mmol), phen (0.027 g, 0.15 mmol) and H2O (10 cm3) was sealed in a stainless steel reactor with Teflon liner and, respectively, heated at both 100 and 150 °C for 78 h and then cooled to room temperature. After several days, colorless crystals of 3 were always obtained in about 58% yield (based on Zn). Anal. calcd. for C18H18N4O8Zn: C, 44.62; H, 11.69; Zn, 13.42. Found: C, 44.65; H, 3.72; N, 11.58; Zn, 13.44%. Selected IR data (KBr pellet)/cm−1: 3390(br), 3017(m), 1622(vs), 1573(s), 1384(vs), 1243(m), 1123(s), 852(m), 643(m), 607(w), 468(vw).

2.5. X-ray crystallography

The three complexes 1–3 were determined by single crystal X-ray diffraction. Suitable single crystals were mounted on a glass fiber and the intensity data were collected on a Bruker APEX II diffractometer at 298 K using graphite monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). Absorption corrections were performed using the sadabs program [28]. The structures were solved by direct methods and refined by full-matrix least-squares against F2 of data using shelxtl software [29]. Anisotropic thermal parameters were applied to all non-hydrogen atoms except O1W, O2W, O3W and O4W atoms in compound 3. The organic hydrogen atoms were generated geometrically, and the aqua hydrogen atoms in compounds 1 and 2 were located from difference maps and refined with isotropic temperature factors; however, the hydrogen atoms of water molecules in compound 3 are not added. A summary of parameters for the data collection and refinements for three complexes is given in Table 1. Selected bond lengths and angles for complexes 1–3 are listed in Table 2. Hydrogen-bonding data of complexes 1-3 are listed in Table 3. Further details are provided in Supporting Information.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Description of crystal structures

3.1.1. [Zn(pzdc) · 3H2O] · H2O (1)

X-ray crystallographic data reveal that complex 1 is made up of a one-dimensional square-wave-like chain of [Zn(pzdc) · 3H2O]n (Fig. 1) and one solvated water molecule. The ball and stick drawing of the repeat unit of chain with atom labeling scheme is shown in Fig. 2. Here each Zn(II) atom is coordinated by three oxygen atoms from three water molecules, two carboxyl oxygen atoms (O1, O3a) and one nitrogen atom N1 from one pzdc2− ligand, forming a distorted octahedral coordination geometry. The Zn–O bond lengths range from 2.064(2) to 2.158(3) Å, and the Zn–N distance is 2.191(3), comparable to those in the six coordinated ZnII complexes [30], [31].

It is noteworthy that two carboxyl groups of the pzdc2− ligand have the different relationship of configurations relevant to the pyrazine core in 1, the carboxyl group involved in chelation is almost coplanar with the pyrazine ring (dihedral angle of 8.53(6)°), the other results bent by 86.86(1)°, a conformation already observed in a series of transition metal complexes formed with pzdc2− [32]. Such a coordination fashion of the pzdc2− ligand may be described into μ2-(η2-N,O),O′′, indicated in Scheme 1(a) [15].

The 1D chains of 1 are further joined together by O–H···O and O–H···N hydrogen bonds between the coordinated/non-coordinated water molecules and the carboxyl oxygen/pyrazine nitrogen atoms from pzdc ligands (Table 2) to form the 3D frameworks (Fig. 3) containing 1D channels constructed by the cooperative association of covalent and hydrogen bonds along the a-axis direction with dimension of ca. 5.5 × 3.2 Å. No water molecules are included within the channels, instead, they are filled between the chains and fixed by hydrogen bonds. The platon program [33] analysis suggests that there is approximately 41.5 Å3 spare space available in the unit cell volume of 1035.2 Å3 (4.0%).

3.1.2. [Zn2(pzdc)2 · 4H2O] · 2.5H2O (2)

To evaluate the effect of temperature during the construction of the supramolecular architectures, at 150 °C, the same reactants as 1 were employed to give 2. Compound 2 has the same metal/ligand ratio as that in 1. Single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that each asymmetric unit consists of one Zn2+ ion, one pzdc2− ligand as well as three and one-quarter of water molecules (including two coordinated water molecules, one and one-quarter of uncoordinated water molecules) in complex 2. As shown in Fig. 4, each zinc(II) center is six-coordinated, residing in a distorted octahedral environment surrounded by one nitrogen atom(N1), three oxygen atoms (O1, O3, O4) from three discrete pzdz2− ligands and two oxygen atoms (O5, O6) from two coordinated water molecules. The bond distances and angles around the zinc ions are 2.1401(15) Å for Zn–N, 2.0583(13)–2.1456(13) Å for Zn–O, 79.10(5)–169.94(5)° for N–Zn–O, and 81.18(5)–172.51(5)° for O–Zn–O, respectively, which are a little shorter than the ones in complex 1 (Table 2).

Each 2,3-pyrazinedicarboxylic ligand provides six potential coordination sites of which four are involved in the coordination, that is, μ3-(η2-N,O),O′′,O′′′ coordination fashion as illustrated in Scheme 1(b). The carboxyl group C(1)O(1)O(2) coordinates to zinc with one oxygen O(1) in a monodentate fashion, the other carboxyl group C(6)O(3)O(4) of the same ligand coordinates to two zinc(II) atoms as a bridging bidentate ligand in an anti–anti fashion, where the two carboxyl groups are almost perpendicular to each other with the dihedral angles of 89.2°, similar to 86.9° in 1. The planes of the pzdc2− unit are parallel to each other and form a ladder-like polymeric complex with two infinite chains running along the a-axis direction, as shown in Fig. 5, in which the motif of one carboxyl group and pyrazine ring is the ‘‘middle rail” of the ladder, and the binuclear [Zn2(pzdc)2(H2O)4] circuit is the basic motif (as the repeat unit) of the chains. The distance of the nearest two zinc atoms Zn(1) and Zn1(1b) bridged by the carboxylate group C(6)O(3)O(4) is 6.436 Å. The shorter distance between the two zinc atoms Zn(1) and Zn(1a) bridged by pzdc2− is 6.490 Å.

The hydrogen contacts listed in Table 3 mainly determine the crystal packing shown in Figure S1. These parallel polymeric ladder-like chains are connected through extensive hydrogen bonds O–H···O/O–H···N involving water molecules, pyrazine nitrogen atoms, and carboxyl oxygen atoms to form a three-dimensional (3D) supramolecular network containing channels perpendicular to the plane of the ladders with dimensions ca. 6.4 × 3.3 Å. Similar to compound 1, no water molecules are also included within the channels, and they are filled between the ladders and fixed by hydrogen bonds. The platon program [33] analysis suggests that there is approximately 32.9 Å3 spare space available in the unit cell volume of 476.3 Å3 (6.9%).

3.1.3. {[Zn(pzdc)(phen)] · 4H2O}n (3)

When the chelating ligand phen is used, a one-dimensional zigzag chain-like structure of complex 3 was obtained at the temperature of 100/150 °C under the reaction conditions similar to 1 or 2. As indicated in Fig. 6, the structure of compound 3 can be viewed as composed of 1D neutral zigzag [Zn(pzdc)(phen)]n chains and four non-coordinated water molecules. The selected bond angles and lengths are depicted in Table 2. The coordination geometry of Zn(II) ion, which is similar to the ones in compounds 1 and 2, is a slightly distorted octahedron with two nitrogen atoms from one phen molecule and the other two oxygen atoms as well as two nitrogen atoms from two different ligands pzdc2−. The bond angles around the Zn(II) ion are in the range of 74.04–64.12°, and the Zn–O bond and Zn–N lengths range from 2.141(3)–2.166(3) to 2.076(2)–2.107(2) Å, respectively, as observed in many zinc(II) complexes with three 1,2-bidentate chelating ligands [34].

In complex 3, both the two bridging ligands pzdc2− and the one terminal ligand phen adopt chelating modes (OˆN/NˆN) to coordinate to one Zn(II) ion, forming the neutral repeat unit [Zn(pzdc)(phen)] with three bite angles of O1–Zn1–N1 74.07°, O4–Zn1–N2 74.65° and N3–Zn1–N4 78.63° as secondary building units (SBUs), and then these SBUs are further linked by OˆN chelations of pzdc2− ligand to Zn(II) ions of the adjacent SBUs to give rise to 1D zigzag-like chain structure which is illustrated in Fig. 7, Fig. 8, which show all atoms of pzdc2− ligand are almost in the same plane with a mean deviation of 0.0396 Å differing from those of 1 and 2. In the case, both carboxyl groups of each pzdc2− ligand have the same chelating coordination modes c (Scheme 1).

The packing of complex 3 in the crystal lattice is worth mentioning. The introduction of phen molecule in the coordination geometry facilitates the formation of the π···π stacking. The zigzag-like chains in 3 are arranged alternately and linked with each other through π···π stacking and C–H···O hydrogen bonding interactions (Table 3), meanwhile these chains are also arranged in an offset fashion to maximize the π···π interactions between ligated phen molecules with the perpendicular distances between centroids of the interacting phen rings being in the range of 3.416–3.571 Å. Therefore, the packing diagram of 3 exhibits a 3D supramolecular architecture containing 1D channels with the pore size of 10 × 12 Å2 along the crystallographic a-axis with lattice water molecules inside (Fig. 8). The platon program [33] analysis suggests that there is approximately 558.5 Å3 spare space available in the unit cell volume of 1978.2 Å3 (28.2%).

It is obvious from the above descriptions and discussions that temperature has certain effect on the architecture of the resulted 1D chain-like Zn(II) complexes 1 and 2 (Scheme 2). For complex 1, at 100 °C, pzdc2− ligand adopts μ2-(η2-N,O),O′′ fashion (mode a in Scheme 1) to interact with Zn(II) ions, resulting in the assembly of complex 1 with 1D square-wave-like chain structure, in which one uncoordinated oxygen atom of each carboxyl group involves with the formation of hydrogen bonds constructing 3D supramolecular network. However, at 150 °C, the same reactants gave compound 2 with 1D ladder-like chain structure, which may be attributed to the enhancement of the metal–ligand interactions rather than metal–water coordination at high temperature, where the pzdc2− ligand presents μ3-(η2-N,O),O′′,O′′′ coordination fashion (mode b in Scheme 2), finally resulting in construction of a different shape 1D chain of 2. In compound 3, the addition of terminal chelating ligand phen not only prevents the structure from being extended into higher dimensionality, but also excludes water molecules from approach. In order to evaluate temperature effect, the same reactions were employed at 100 and 150 °C, respectively, and the final results show that only compound 3 is obtained. In this case, chelating interaction of phen obviously plays a very important role in the formation of the stable compound 3, in which pzdc2− ligand adopts μ2-(η2-N,O),(η2-N′,O′′) fashion (mode c in Scheme 2) to coordinate to Zn(II) ions. On the other hand, there exist different driving forces for the assemblies of 3D supramolecular networks in complexes 1–3. Existence of water molecules in the coordination geometries of Zn(II) ions in 1 and 2 facilitates the formation of the hydrogen bonds O–H···O/O–H···N between the coordinated/non-coordinated water molecules and the uncoordinated carboxyl oxygen/the pyrazine nitrogen atoms, driving architectures of 3D supramolecular frameworks. However, it is also clear that owing to the introduction of auxiliary phen ligand in the coordination geometries of Zn(II) ions in 3, the C–H···O hydrogen bonds and π···π stacking interactions formed drive the construction of 3D supramolecular network, and the crystal structure is further stabilized. In comparison with 1 and 2, no O–H···O/O–H···N hydrogen bonds are observed in the structure of 3.

3.2. Thermal properties and infrared spectroscopic analysis

To examine the thermal stability of the complexes, the thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) of complexes 1–3 was carried out in nitrogen from 20 to 800 °C. As shown in Figure S1, the thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) shows that compound 1 exhibits three steps of weight loss. At the first step, there is a 6.02% weight loss from 66 to 109 °C, which can be attributed to the loss of one non-coordinated water molecule (calcd.: 5.93%). The second weight loss (17.52%) is in the range of 135–188 °C, corresponding to the thermal decomposition and loss of three coordinated water molecules (calcd.: 17.79%). The third stage occurs above 263 °C, corresponding to the thermal decomposition and loss of pzdc2− ligand in 1. For compound 2, the TGA curve presents that a weight loss of 20.98% occurred over the temperature range of 20–200 °C, which corresponds to the removal of two and half lattice and four coordinated water molecules per formula unit (calcd. 20.17%). Weight loss occurred gradually beyond 200 °C, which was followed by a multi-step decomposition process of the compound. The TG curve for 3 shows the loss of four guest water molecules in the temperature range from 20 to 251 °C (found, 15.31%; calcd. 14.88%). And then the further weight loss of 36.79% (calcd. 37.21%) from 357 to 500 °C ascribes to the loss of one terminal phen ligand. Another major weight loss occurred in the range 500–800 °C, which may correspond to complete destruction of the network.

The broad bands centered at 3400 cm−1 in the IR spectra of 1 to 3 may be due to the –OH stretching vibration of the coordinated water molecules and the guest water molecules. It is well known that the difference Δ between the asymmetric stretching vibration and the symmetric one (Δνas − Δνsym) of the carboxylate group depends on the coordination mode [35]. In complex 1, the asymmetric stretching vibration is at 1668.51 cm−1, and the symmetric stretching vibration is at 1466.94 cm−1. The Δν = 201.57 cm−1 indicates that the carboxylate groups are coordinated in monodentate fashion to the Zn ions, which is consistent with the single-crystal X-ray diffraction. The asymmetric stretching and symmetric stretching vibrations of compounds 1 and 3 are similar to those of 2. The absence of the characteristic bands at around 1700 cm−1 in compound 3 attributed to the protonated carboxylic group indicates the complete protonation of the carboxylate group [36].

3.3. Luminescent properties

The study of luminescent properties of MOFs is of great current interest owing to their higher thermal stability than the pure organic ligand and the ability to affect the emission wavelength and intensity of the organic material by metal coordination [37]. The solid-state photoluminescent behaviors of complexes 1 to 3 are investigated at room temperature, as shown in Fig. 9. It can be seen that these complexes exhibit intense photoluminescent emission at 412 nm for 1 (λex = 326 nm), 417 nm for 2 (λex = 326 nm), and 424 nm for 3 (λex = 370 nm). Contrastively, the neutral ligand H2pzdc displays very weak emission at 390 nm, corresponding to the excited light at 244 nm. The enhanced fluorescence efficiency of compounds 1 to 3 is attributed to the coordination of the pzdc2− ligand to the Zn(II) ions that effectively increases the rigidity of the ligand and reduces the loss of energy by radiationless thermal vibrations. The energy difference caused by the coordination environment makes the red shift of their emissions. The high luminescence efficiency in the blue light region indicates compounds 1–3 are excellent candidates for blue fluorescent materials.

In summary, three new one-dimensional complexes containing the same pzdc2− ligand and metal Zn(II) centers with the identically octahedral configuration have been successfully synthesized under hydrothermal condition. X-ray single crystal diffraction displays that though compounds 1–3 have the 1D chain-like topologies and 3D supramolecular architectures, the shapes of 1D chains are clearly different, that is, square-wave-like for 1, ladder-like for 2 and zigzag-like for 3, which may be attributed to the diverse coordination modes of pzdc2− ligand caused by different reaction temperatures or the second ligand phen. Obviously the results presented here offer the intriguing strategy of constructing the 1D chain-like complexes with the different shapes. Furthermore, the intense blue luminescence properties of complexes 1–3 at room temperature suggest that they have great potential application as luminescent materials.
