Introduction

In recent years, the design and construction of lanthanide metal–organic frameworks (Ln-MOFs) has attracted increasing attention not only for the intriguing structural versatility but also for the potential applications in magnetism,1 gas storage and separation,2,3 sensing,4 optics,5 and catalysis.6 In particular, lanthanide ions are very attractive luminescent centers for the high color purity and long lifetimes of their excited states, and their compounds have found applications in lighting, photonics, and optical communication as well as luminescent probes and sensors.7 However, Laporte-forbidden 4f–4f transition prevents direct excitation of lanthanide luminescence, and the luminescence of Ln-MOFs is commonly sensitized through energy transfer from π-conjugated organic chromophores to Ln3+ centers (the so-called antenna effect).8 Recently, aromatic carboxylate coordination complexes that exhibit unique photophysical properties and intriguing structural features have attracted considerable interest.9 With regard to the construction of functionalized Ln-MOFs, the aromatic multicarboxylate is one of the most important organic ligands with good “antenna effect” because of its strong binding tendency toward Ln3+ ions as well as serving potentially as a sensitizer to enhance the Ln3+ luminescence.10

Lanthanide metal–organic frameworks (Ln-MOFs) with exceptional tenability and structural diversity also offer a unique platform for the development of white-light emitting materials.11 Nowadays, the color tuning and white-light emitting Ln-MOFs are being developed because of their potential applications in solid-state lighting, large-panel displays, full-color displays and low-cost backlighting,12–14 and several approaches for tunable white-light emitting Ln-MOFs have been reported, such as La:Eu,Tb, Gd:Eu,Tb, Gd:Dy,Sm, Gd:Dy,Eu and Gd:Eu co-doped Ln-MOFs for white-light emission, where Eu3+, Tb3+, Sm3+ and Dy3+ ions emit metal-centered red, green, orange and yellow light, respectively, while the frameworks of La3+ and Gd3+ ions exhibit ligand-centered blue emission.15 As a result, white-light emitting materials and devices are realized by doping with luminescent Ln3+ ions into the La3+ or Gd3+ ion framework. However, it is still a challenge to develop suitable Ln-MOFs capable of generating white light because the different colored light emitters should compensate exactly through the corresponding chromatic approaches.

Although several approaches, such as monochromatic, dichromatic, trichromatic, and tetrachromatic methods,16 have been utilized to obtain white-light emitting materials, the trichromatic (blue, red and green) method has been paid more attention since it is typically superior to others in terms of the trichromatic white-light emitting materials having better color rendering properties and higher luminous efficiency. Considering that the isostructural Ln-MOFs with mixed lanthanide ions may be achieved via in situ doping and the color of the light emission may be modulated depending on the types and relative concentration of the constituent lanthanide ions, trichromatic white-light emitting materials can be obtained by incorporating the blue, red and green light-emitting components into the resulting mixed Ln-MOFs by carefully tuning the trichromatic concentration.

Actually, besides the emitter components, the choice of suitable ligands for white-light emitting materials is crucial. The ligand that binds to Ln(iii) should itself act as a light harvesting part for efficient emission sensitization with high absorption.17 Recently, the use of trigonal-planar 4,4′,4′′-s-triazine-2,4,6-triyl-tribenzoate (TATB3−) with transition metals has received great attention,18,19 and highly stable (up to 480 °C) three-dimensional (3D) Ln-MOFs (PCN-9) constructed by the connection of ytterbium and H3TATB ligand was also reported.20 However, the authors mainly focused on the MOFs' structures and the basic gas adsorption, especially for transition metals; the systematic study of H3TATB with lanthanide metals and the relevant photoluminescence properties is still unexplored, let alone white-light emitting materials. Considering that TATB3− has an extraordinary ability to increase the framework stability, which is one of the main concerns in MOF material application, and owns a strong advantage in absorption and transformation of light energy to result in a preferential enhancement of luminescence, we selected it as a ligand to synthesize a series of Ln-MOFs so as to systematically investigate their photoluminescence properties and attempt to obtain white-light emitting materials. Moreover, compared with the more commonly used H3BTB, TATB3− is expected to be flatter than BTB3− since the trigonal-planar TATB3− ligand has an electron-deficient triazine moiety without C–H bonds on the central ring, which is not subject to the repulsions between the C–H bonds on the central ring and those on the peripheral rings in BTB3−. In addition, the triazine ring is better suited for π–π stacking than the central ring in BTB3− due to the increased quadrupole moment21 and thus may contribute to highly thermally stable MOFs with novel structural types.

Herein we synthesized eight new highly thermally stable Ln-MOFs [Ln = La (1), Ce (2), Pr (3), Sm (4), Eu (5), Nd (6), Gd (7), Tb (8)] based on TATB3− and systematically studied their crystal structures and photoluminescence properties. Moreover, we also successfully obtained tunable white-light emitting materials through the doping of Eu3+ and Tb3+ ions in the La3+ complex. Placing emphasis on the white-light emitting materials, the corresponding Ln-MOFs [Ln = La (1), Eu (5) and Tb (8)] are discussed in detail in the main text, while the other Ln-MOFs [Ln = Ce (2), Pr (3), Sm (4), Nd (6) and Gd (7)] are mainly described in the ESI.†

Experimental section

Chemicals and reagents

The ligand 4,4′,4′′-s-triazine-2,4,6-triyl-tribenzoate (H3TATB) was prepared according to the literature procedure.22 The Ln(NO3)3·6H2O (Ln = La, Ce, Pr, Nd, Sm, Eu, Gd and Tb) compounds were prepared by dissolving the corresponding rare earth oxides in an aqueous solution of HNO3 (6.0 M) (several drops of H2O2 were added when preparing Tb(NO3)3·6H2O), and then were evaporated at 80 °C. N,N-Dimethylformamide (DMF) and dimethylsulfoxide (DMSO) were dried and distilled according to standard procedures. Other reagents were purchased commercially and used without further purification.

Physical measurements

Elemental analyses (C, H, N) were performed on a Perkin-Elmer 240 elemental analyzer. Thermal gravimetric analyses (TGA) were performed using a DTA-TGA 2960 thermogravimetric analyzer in a nitrogen atmosphere at a heating rate of 20 °C min−1. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) data were recorded on a Shimadzu XRD-6000 diffractometer with Cu Kα (λ = 1.54056 Å) radiation at room temperature at a scan speed of 5° min−1 and a step size of 0.01° in 2θ. The IR spectra were obtained on a NICOLET iS10 spectrometer using KBr pellets in the 4000–400 cm−1 region. The absorption spectra were obtained using a Shimadzu UV-3600 spectrometer for the solid samples. The morphology, elemental mapping images and composition of the samples were inspected using a scanning electron microscope (SEM, S-4800, Hitachi) equipped with an energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscope (EDX, S 4800). Luminescence spectra of the solid samples were recorded at room temperature on F-7000 FL and RF-5301PC spectrophotometers equipped with a 150 W xenon lamp as an excitation source. The photomultiplier tube (PMT) voltage was 400 V in all the measurements. The scan speed was 1200 nm min−1. The photoluminescence lifetime was measured using an Edinburgh Instruments FLS920P fluorescence spectrometer.

Preparation and characterization of Ln-MOFs

[La2(TATB)2(DMSO)6]·DMF·DMSO·H2O (1). A mixture of La(NO3)3·6H2O (10 mg, 0.023 mmol), H3TATB (5 mg, 0.011 mmol), DMF (0.2 mL), DMSO (0.2 ml ) and H2O (0.1 mL) was placed in a Teflon reactor (15 mL) and heated at 90 °C for 48 h. The mixture was gradually cooled to room temperature at a rate of 5 °C h−1 to obtain colorless block-shaped crystals. The above-mentioned analogous procedure was used for the preparation of 2–5 by replacing La(NO3)3·6H2O with other Ln(NO3)3·6H2O (Ln = Ce3+, Pr3+, Sm3+, Eu3+).

[Tb2(TATB)2(DMSO)5(CH3OH)]·DMF·DMSO·2H2O (8). A mixture of Tb(NO3)3·6H2O (10 mg, 0.022 mmol), H3TATB (5 mg, 0.011 mmol), DMF (0.3 mL), DMSO (0.3 mL), CH3OH (0.02 mL) and H2O (0.1 mL) was placed in a Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel (15 mL), which was heated at 90 °C for 48 h and then cooled to room temperature at a rate of 5 °C h−1 to give colorless block-shaped crystals. Compounds 6 and 7 were obtained in the same manner, replacing Tb(NO3)3·6H2O by Nd(NO3)3·6H2O and Gd(NO3)3·6H2O, respectively. The microanalysis, yield and main IR bands of compounds 1–8 are summarized in Table S1 in the ESI.†

The co-doped compounds EuxTbyLa1−x−y–TATB (A–E) were prepared by using a mixture containing the desired Ln(NO3)3·6H2O as the starting material in stoichiometric ratios, and the preparation process is the same as that of homometallic Ln-MOFs mentioned above. The purities of the compounds were confirmed by powder XRD (Fig. S1, ESI†) and elemental analysis of C, H and N (Table S2, ESI†); energy dispersive X-ray (EDX) elemental mapping analysis for Eu0.25Tb0.045La0.705–TATB proved that this kind of doped compounds is not a simple mixture but a single phase of coordination polymer (Fig. S2, ESI†).

Crystallographic analyses

Single crystal X-ray diffraction data were collected on a Bruker Smart Apex II CCD diffractometer at 293 K using graphite monochromated Mo/Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). Data reductions and absorption corrections were performed using the SAINT and SADABS software packages,23 respectively. Structures were solved by a direct method using the SHELXL-97 software package.24 The non-hydrogen atoms were anisotropically refined using the full-matrix least-squares method on F2. All hydrogen atoms were placed at the calculated positions and refined riding on the parent atoms. In compounds 1–8, solvent molecules in the structure were highly disordered and were impossible to refine using conventional discrete-atom models. To resolve this issue, the contribution of solvent electron density was removed using the SQUEEZE routine in PLATON.25 Crystallographic data were obtained for 1–8, and the corresponding data of collection and refinement details are presented in Table 1 for 1, 5, and 8 and Table S3 (ESI†) for 2–4, 6 and 7. The CCDC reference numbers for 1–8 are 1470930–1470937, respectively.

Results and discussion

Crystal structures

Single crystal X-ray diffraction analyses revealed that compounds 1–8 are isomorphous, crystallizing in the triclinic space group 1 and exhibiting a 2D framework; herein, only the structures of [La2(TATB)2(DMSO)6]·DMF·DMSO·H2O (1) and [Tb2(TATB)2(DMSO)5(CH3OH)]·DMF·DMSO·2H2O (8) are representatively described in detail. The structures of 2–7 are shown in Fig. S3 (ESI†). Selected bond distances and angles for compounds 1–8, which are the most suitable for single-crystal X-ray analyses, are listed in Tables S4 and S5 (ESI†). Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) analysis of compounds 1–8 was performed at room temperature. As shown in Fig. 1 and Fig. S4 (ESI†), the curves of the eight compounds are similar, also revealing that 1–8 are isostructural with each other. The peak positions of the simulated and experimental PXRD patterns are in agreement with each other, demonstrating the good phase purity of the complexes. Besides this, the IR spectra of 1–8 are almost identical (Fig. S5, ESI†). In addition, the PXRD patterns of the doped materials EuxTbyLa1−x−y–TATB (A–E) coincide with that of the original complex 1, suggesting that they are also isostructural with each other (Fig. S1, ESI†).

Crystals of 1 belong to the triclinic space group 1. As shown in Fig. 2a, its asymmetric unit cell consists of two nine-coordinated La3+ ions, two fully deprotonated TATB3− ligands and six coordinated DMSO molecules. Two crystallographically independent La3+ ions (labeled as La1 and La2) exhibit different coordination geometries although they are both coordinated with nine oxygen atoms, six from three bidentately coordinating carboxyl groups and three from three DMSO molecules. The La1 ion is nine-coordinated to form a distorted tetrakaidecahedron coordination geometry, in which six oxygen atoms (O1, O2, O7, O8, O11a and O12a) come from three TATB3− ligands and three oxygen atoms (O13, O14 and O15) from three DMSO molecules to result in a [La1O9] unit. The La2 ion is also nine-coordinated with a [La2O9] unit, which adopts a distorted tetrakaidecahedron coordination geometry bonding to six oxygen donors (O5, O6, O3b, O4b, O9c and O10c) from three TATB3− ligands and another three (O16, O17 and O18) from three DMSO molecules (Fig. 2b). The La–O bond distances range from 2.473(9) to 2.689(7) Å (Table S4, ESI†), and the O–La–O bond angles span from 49.8(2)° to 164.3(2)° (Table S5, ESI†), which are in the ranges compatible with the values of the previously published lanthanide complexes with carboxylates as bridging ligands.26 Each TATB3− ligand directly links three La3+ ions through its three carboxylate groups, adopting bidentately chelated coordination modes. Meanwhile, each La3+ ion is surrounded by three TATB3− ligands, generating a two-dimensional (2D) (3,3)-connected net-like layered structure (Fig. 2c). As shown in Fig. 2d, the layers are parallel with each other. The dihedral angles between the central triazine A and the three benzene rings B, C and D are 14.41°, 20.69° and 18.84°, respectively, and the dihedral angles among the three benzene rings B and C, B and D, and C and D are 16.90°, 23.19° and 37.41°, respectively (Fig. 2c). These dihedral data indicate that the TATB3− is extremely unsymmetrical and not as flat as the reported ones.21 The guest DMF, DMSO and water molecules, being disordered, locate in the channels. All DMF, DMSO and water molecules can be completely removed upon heating. The dimension of the free window along the b-axis is calculated to be 7.5 × 4.2 Å when the van der Waals radii of the atoms at the wall of the channel are subtracted. The total potential void volume is 49.1% calculated from PLATON/SOLV25 if the guest DMF, DMSO, water and coordinated DMSO molecules are removed (Fig. 2e). From the viewpoint of topology, the 2-D structure of 1 can be described as a (3,3)-connected network with the Schläfli symbol {63} by considering each La3+ ion as a 3-connected node and the organic ligand serves as a 3-connected node (Fig. 2f).

Compounds 8 and 1 are isomorphous. As for 8, its asymmetric unit is almost the same as that of 1 except that the coordinated solvents of Ln1 are different. In compound 8, Tb1 is also coordinated with nine oxygen atoms, six from three bidentately coordinating carboxyl groups, two from two DMSO molecules and one from one methanol molecule (Fig. S6, ESI†).

TG analyses

The isomorphous compounds 1–8 exhibit similar thermal behavior (Fig. S7, ESI†). Considering the isomorphism of type I for compounds 1–5 and type II for compounds 6–8, compounds 5 and 8 are discussed in detail as the representative examples. As shown in Fig. 3a and b, compounds 5 and 8 are stable up to 90 °C and then exhibit two main steps of weight losses. The first weight loss of 9.1% for 5 and 9.9% for 8 occurs from 90 °C to 150 °C, which corresponds to the loss of all guest water, DMF and DMSO molecules per formula unit (calcd 9.2% for 5 and 10.0% for 8). The second weight loss of 25.8% for 5 in the temperature range 150–380 °C and 23.9% for 8 in the temperature range 150–395 °C corresponds to the release of all coordinated solvent molecules (calcd 25.8% for 5 relating to six coordinated DMSO molecules and calcd 23.8% for 8 relating to five coordinated DMSO molecules and one coordinated methanol molecule per formula unit). After that, a plateau appears and the desolvated sample is thermally stable up to 560 °C. Above 560 °C, the framework gradually begins to break down with the decomposition of TATB3− ligands. It is worth noting that compound 5 still showed very good crystallinity at 550 °C. At 600 °C, the amorphous phases were formed (Fig. 4). To the best of our knowledge, there are only a few Ln-MOFs reported to be stable at about 550 °C, which may highly facilitate their functional applications. Their unusual thermal stabilities may be attributed to the coordinatively linked net-like layered structures, which are among the highest of reported porous Ln-MOFs.27

Photoluminescence properties

Lanthanide metal–organic frameworks are potential luminescent materials since the trivalent lanthanide ions are well-known photoluminescence ions, which are due to the splitting of the 4f1–13 configuration into 2S+1LJ spectroscopic levels, caused by Coulomb repulsion and spin–orbit coupling. Each of these states is further split into 2J + 1 Stark sublevels by the crystal field. In general, except for Ce3+, the excitation and emission spectra of Ln3+ are characterized by narrow bands originating from internal 4fn–4fn electronic transitions between the 2S+1LJ energy levels.28,29 Here in the main text, only the photoluminescence properties of compounds 1, 5, and 8 and co-doped compounds EuxTbyLa1−x−y–TATB (A–E) are discussed in detail; the others are discussed in the ESI.†

Excited at 350 nm in the solid state at room temperature, H3TATB exhibits a broad emission band at 458 nm (Fig. S8(b), ESI†), which can be ascribed to the intraligand π → π* transitions. Similarly, compound 1 shows blue luminescence (Fig. 7b) with the broadband emission centered at 466 nm under the excitation at 350 nm (Fig. 5; the excitation spectrum is given in Fig. S9(a), ESI†). Such a broadband emission is ascribed to the intraligand π ← π* transition of TATB3−. With respect to the emission of the pure ligand, the broad emission band of 1 exhibits a slight red shift; this phenomenon can be assigned to the ligand interactions upon coordination with the metal ions which increases the rigidity of the ligands and thus reduces the loss of energy by radiationless decay of the intraligand emission excited state.30

The excitation spectrum of compound 5 is obtained by monitoring its emission of the 5D0 → 7F2 transition at 619 nm (Fig. S9(b), ESI†). It clearly shows two broad bands centered at 287 and 350 nm that can be assigned to the π → π* electron transition of the organic bridging ligands and the charge-transfer between the ligand and the Eu(iii) ions, respectively. The other weak peaks at 375, 383 and 395 nm could be ascribed to 5D4 ← 7F0, 5G6,2,4 ← 7F0 and 5L6,7 ← 7F0 transitions, respectively.16 Upon excitation at 350 nm, 5 exhibits the characteristic red luminescence of Eu3+ ions in the visible region (Fig. 7b), whereas the emission band from the free ligand is not observed, indicating that there is energy transfer from the ligand to the Eu3+ ions during photoluminescence.31 The major peaks at 580, 594, 619, 652 and 702 nm arise from the characteristic emission of the 5D0 → 7FJ (J = 0–4) transitions (Fig. 5). The most intense emission at 619 nm is attributed to the electric dipole induced 5D0 → 7F2 transition, which is hypersensitive to the coordination environment of the Eu3+ ions, and its intensity increases with lower site symmetry of Eu3+ ions. The second intense emission at 594 nm corresponds to the magnetic dipole induced 5D0 → 7F1 transition, which is fairly insensitive to the environment of the Eu3+ ions. It is generally accepted that the fluorescence intensity ratio of 5D0 → 7F2 to 5D0 → 7F1 is very sensitive to the structural change in the vicinity of Eu3+ ions.32 In compound 5, the intensity ratio I(5D0 → 7F2)/I(5D0 → 7F1) is equal to ca. 5. Obviously, the intensity of the hypersensitive transition 5D0 → 7F2 is much stronger than that of 5D0 → 7F1, indicating that the Eu3+ ions are not located at the inversion center and the symmetry of the Eu3+ ions is low, which are in agreement with the crystal structure analysis. Moreover, the appearance of the weak symmetry forbidden 5D0 → 7F0 transition further suggests that the Eu3+ ions occupy low-symmetry coordination sites.33

The excitation spectrum of 8 is shown in Fig. S9(d) (ESI†). It displays a large broadband centered at 350 nm which is ascribed to the electronic transitions of organic ligands. The obtained crystals of 8 give characteristic Tb3+ emission at around 492, 546, 587 and 622 nm when excited at 350 nm (Fig. 5), which can be assigned to the 5D4 → 7 FJ (J = 6, 5, 4, and 3) transitions. Among them, the 5D4 → 7F5 transition is the most intense one, which results in a strong green luminescence output for the solid sample (Fig. 7b). It is worth noting that there is also no apparent residual ligand-based emission, indicating an efficient energy transfer from ligand p-excited states to Tb3+ f-excited states.

Upon pulsed excitation at 350 nm in the solid state at room temperature, the luminescence decay profiles of 5 and 8 were obtained, monitored at the main emission peaks of 619 and 546 nm, respectively (Fig. 6a and b). The process of luminescence decay follows a single exponential decay law, and as a result, eqn (1) was utilized for fitting the curves of luminescence decay: y = y0 + A1 × exp(−t/τ) (1)

In eqn (1), y0 and A1 are two constants, and τ is the decay constant representing luminescence lifetime. The black dots are the raw experimental data and the red lines represent the fitting curves. The best fits were performed to give τ = 0.69 ms for 5 and 0.70 ms for 8. The long excited state decay time is due to the forbidden f–f electronic transitions of lanthanide ions.34

With the doping of different lanthanide ions into one isostructural Ln-MOF, the color of the emission for the co-doped Ln-MOFs can be finely tuned, and ultimately, white-light emission may be achievable if the combination of different lanthanide ions in carefully adjusted molar ratios is appropriate. Considering that compounds 1, 5, and 8 act as the primary blue, red, and green emitter components, respectively (Fig. 5), the trichromatic white-light emission can be obtained through the doping of both Eu3+ and Tb3+ ions into the parent La-TATB MOFs. Since white-light emissions are usually produced when the emission intensities around the blue, red and green range are comparable,11 we made an effort to finely optimize the relative concentration of Eu3+ and Tb3+ ions in the co-doped EuxTbyLa1−x−y–TATB MOFs. As shown in Fig. 7a, a series of co-doped EuxTbyLa1−x−y–TATB compounds (A–E) with different doping ratios were obtained, and there exists a concentration effect on the luminescence intensity in these co-doped complexes. From A to E in the EuxTbyLa1−x−y–TATB co-doped complexes, with the relative amount of Eu3+ and Tb3+ ions decreasing, the f–f characteristic emission intensity of Eu3+ around 619 nm (corresponding to the transition 5D0 → 7F2) and Tb3+ around 546 nm (corresponding to the transition 5D4 → 7F5) decreased significantly, while the π ← π* transition arising from TATB3− around 466 nm increased gradually, resulting in different photoluminescence colors from pale yellow to pale yellow-green, near white, white and pale blue-green depending on the concentration of the Eu3+ and Tb3+ dopants upon excitation at 350 nm as shown in Fig. 7b. The corresponding CIE chromaticity coordinates are listed in Table S6.† All of the above reveal that the color of the co-doped complexes can be finely tuned through different combinations of the amounts of Eu3+ and Tb3+ ions, and the energy transfer is incomplete from the La3+ host to the doped Ln3+ ions excited at about 350 nm, which is the most significant feature to realize white-light emission in the co-doped system.35,36 Interestingly, white-light emission is readily produced when the emission peaks at 466, 546 and 619 nm are comparable in intensity (Fig. 7 and S11, ESI†), which is in agreement with other reports.11 The optimal concentrations of the co-dopants for white-light emission are 73 mol% La3+, 24 mol% Eu3+ and 3 mol% Tb3+ with the corresponding CIE coordinate (0.3323, 0.3349), which is very close to the coordinates for pure white-light (0.3333, 0.3333), and thus ideal for white-light emission according to the 1931 CIE coordinate diagram (Fig. 7 and S11, ESI†).

Moreover, it should be noted that the reduction of the effective amount of luminescence centers (Eu3+ and Tb3+ ions) results in the decrease in luminescence intensity, indicating that the concentration quenching effect stemming from electron–phonon coupling plays a much less important role in the process of deactivation of Eu3+ and Tb3+ ion luminescence in EuxTbyLa1−x−y–TATB co-doped compounds.37

Conclusion

In summary, eight isostructural highly thermally stable lanthanide metal–organic frameworks, [Ln2(TATB)2(DMSO)6]·DMF·DMSO·H2O [Ln = La (1), Ce (2), Pr (3), Sm (4), Eu (5)] and [Ln2(TATB)2(DMSO)5(CH3OH)]·DMF·DMSO·2H2O [Ln = Nd (6), Gd (7), Tb (8)], have been successfully constructed from the conjugated TATB3− ligand and lanthanide nitrate via a solvothermal reaction. The photoluminescence properties of the lanthanide complexes demonstrate that the conjugated TATB3− ligand exhibits a good “antenna effect” for Ln3+ cations and, especially, provides excellent sensitization of the Eu3+ and Tb3+ luminescence through a particularly efficient ligand-to-lanthanide energy transfer process. The compounds 5 (Ln = Eu) and 8 (Ln = Tb) not only exhibit intense red and green luminescence emission, respectively, but also possess millisecond level long solid-state luminescence lifetimes, showing potential application in the red and green luminescence materials field. All the compounds display good luminescence performance. With careful adjustment of the relative concentration of the doping Eu3+ and Tb3+ ions in the La3+ complex, the color of luminescence of the resulting isostructural materials can be finely-modulated, and tunable white-light emitting materials were successfully obtained. Such doped Ln-MOFs can be a strategy for obtaining white-emitting materials which have potential applications in optical devices.
