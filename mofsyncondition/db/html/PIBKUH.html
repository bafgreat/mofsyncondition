Solvent-induced supramolecular isomers: Two dimensional coordination polymers constructed by Cu(II) and fluconazole

Author links open overlay panel
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.inoche.2007.02.003Get rights and content

Abstract

Two Cu(II)–fluconazole supramolecular isomers 1 and 2 formulated as (flu)2Cu(SCN)2 (flu = fluconazole) have been synthesized and structurally characterized by single crystal X-ray diffraction. Different solvent media lead to the different binding modes of anion SCN− and the flexible C–C chain of fluconazole has been rotated in the two isomers. Complex 1 exhibits a two dimensional square grid-like layer and complex 2 exhibits a two dimensional framework with a parquet motif. Different H-bonds and stacking modes are observed in the two isomers. Complex 1 is nonemissive, whereas complex 2 exhibits photoluminescence property.

Much effort has been devoted to the design and controlled crystallization of coordination networks based on transition-metal atoms and multifunctional bridging ligands owing to their potential applications such as magnetism, electric conductivity, molecular adsorption, and heterogeneous catalysis. Supramolecular isomerism or polymorphism, the existence of more than one superstructure for given set of components [1], is of particular importance because the superstructure plays an essential role in determining the properties of crystalline materials [1], [2], [3], [4]. For example, in drug industry, the drug activity of a material might change abruptly from one polymorph to another [5]. Thus it is necessary to develop a robust method to obtain the favorable supramolecular isomer or polymorph consistently and reproducibly. However, it is not easy to be achieved because the existence of supramolecular isomerism or polymorphism implies that free energy differences between different crystalline forms are small (1–2 kcal mol−1) [6]. Molecular conformations, packing arrangements and hydrogen bond synthons of the same molecule can lead to different crystalline environments and superstructures.

In the previous work, solvent effect has received much attention in the construction of metal–organic polymers [7]. Generally, solvent is a crucial crystallization condition to favor or disfavor a particular supramolecular isomer [1a]. But if solvent molecule is included in the crystal structure, it is always called as pseudo-polymorphism, and in case of, e.g., water such crystal forms are generally called hydrates [1], [2], [3]. Most reported supramolecular isomers in coordination networks have different solvent molecules in the structures (pseudo-polymorphs) because solvents usually enter the lattice as coordinated or dissociated ligands, “true” supramolecular isomers with a fixed stoichiometry for all components are rarely reported [1](b), [4].

Generally, anion possesses small charge to radius ratio and relative high solvation free energies, and exhibits different binding ability in different solvents [8]. In an attempt to explore the relationship between the solvents and “true” supramolecular isomers, our strategy is to introduce anions into coordination networks, therefore anions will embody the effect of solvents.

Fluconazole (C13H12N6OF2), α-(2,4-difluorophenyl)-α-(1H-1,2,4-triazol-1-ylmethyl)-1H-1,2,4-triazole-1-ethanol, a good antifungal agent in the treatment of candidiasis [9], is a flexible organic ligand due to the flexible C–C chain in the structure, which first seized our attention because its two symmetrical 1,2,4-triazole ligands can combine metal ions in their neutral form (Scheme 1). Based on the principle of electroneutrality that all pure substances fulfill, anion must be incorporated into the metal–fluconazole complexes for the balance of charge. On the other hand, the big substituent, 2,4-difluorophenyl of fluconazole, would occupy the possible empty channel and block the introduction of solvent molecules into the structure. It is expected to obtain true supramolecular isomers of metal–fluconazole complexes by using same anion and different solvents.

In our previous work, in the presence of CH3COO− and DMF (N,N′-dimethyllformamide), a metal(II)–fluconazole complex (C13H11N6OF2)2Ni2(CH3COO)2 has been synthesized [10]. In the structure of the complex, solvent DMF has not entered the lattice, but the weak basicity of DMF leads to the deprotonation of hydroxyl group of fluconazole, indicating it is another case, which is different from common neutral solvents. In the presence of water, using Br−, ClO4- and Mo8O264-, we have ever synthesized three metal(II)–fluconazole complexes formulated as (flu)2CuBr2 · 2H2O, (flu)2Zn(ClO4)2 · 2H2O, (flu)4Ni2(H2O)4(Mo8O26) · 6H2O (flu = fluconazole) [11], in which solvent water molecules have entered the final structures as coordinated or dissociated molecules.

In the present work, we choose linear anion, SCN− as counter anion. That is because SCN−, possesses superior coordination ability, would be preferentially coordinated to metal centers in the presence of solvent molecules and other anions such as SO42-,ClO4-. Secondly, the linear geometrical configuration of SCN− would lead to the structural variety of metal complexes because its terminal S and N atoms are not only good coordination atoms, but also good H-bond donors.

Hydro(solvo)thermally technique was proven to be a powerful approach in the preparation of low-soluble organic–inorganic hybrid materials. Under such conditions, the different solubilities of the organic and inorganic starting materials are alleviated. In the present work, we have hydro(solvo)thermally synthesized two Cu(II)–fluconazole complexes. Complex 1 was obtained in good yields (no lower than 90% based on fluconazole) by hydrothermally treating the mixture of CuSO4, KSCN, fluconazole and water. When methanol was used instead of water, complex 2 was obtained.1 Complexes 1 and 2 are “true” supramolecular isomers and they possess the same composition formulated as (flu)2Cu(SCN)2.

In the reactions, the molar ratio of Cu2+/fluconazole/KSCN was controlled at 1:1:3. According to the chemical composition of complexes 1 and 2, the stoichiometric ratio of Cu2+/fluconazole/SCN− in complexes 1 and 2 is 2:1:2. It is indicated that both Cu2+ and SCN− were in excess with respect to fluconazole in the reactions and it is ensured that fluconazole was transformed as possible as totally in the self-assembly of complexes 1 and 2. The parallel experiments show that the molar amount of SCN− can be lowered to be equal to that of SO42-.

The preparations of complexes 1 and 2 were not susceptible to the reaction temperature and reaction time. Hydro(solvo)thermal reactions at 70–160 °C for 2–4 days can produce crystalline products in good yields. Complexes 1 and 2 also can be obtained when Cu(ClO4)2 was used as starting materials instead of CuSO4. However under such condition, we chose hydro(solvo)thermally reactions at 70 °C for 1–2 days for the sake of safety because Cu(ClO4)2 would be a potential oxidant and explosive at high temperature [12].

Single-crystal X-ray analysis has revealed that complex 1 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P1¯ (no. 2), its asymmetric unit contains one Cu(II), two fluconazole and two coordinated SCN−. The Cu(II) center is coordinated by four nitrogen atoms from four fluconazole [Cu–N 2.012(5)–2.038(5) Å, N–Cu–N 89.33(18)–178.32(19)°] and two nitrogen atoms from two SCN− to furnish an octahedral geometry (Fig. 1). The two SCN− exhibit inequivalent coordination modes and the Cu–N bond lengths are 2.474(5) and 2.654(6) Å, respectively. The relatively longer Cu–N bond lengths indicate that the binding strength of SCN− in complex 1 is weaker [13].

Complex 1 exhibits a two dimensional (2D) square grid-like layer with a uniform (4, 4) net topology (Fig. 1). The neighbouring Cu⋯Cu distances in a (4, 4) net are 12.717 and 12.236 Å, and it is estimated that the size of the square grid is ca. 155.6 Å2. The dihedral angles between the two triazole planes of the two crystallographically unique fluconazoles are 81.4° and 90.9°, respectively. The dihedral angles between one triazole plane and the aromatic phenyl ring of two crystallographically unique fluconazole are different. In one fluconazole, the angles are 115.5° and 49.7° and in another crystallographically unique fluconazole, the angles are 131.4° and 116.3°.

The 2,4-difluorophenyl ligands of two crystallographically unique fluconazoles orient above and below each layer, respectively. The centroid–centroid distances between aromatic planes (including triazole and phenyl planes) are no shorter than 3.958 Å, indicating that no strong π–π stacking interactions exist. Strong intermolecular H-bonds are observed between SCN− anions and the uncoordinated hydroxyl groups of fluconazole, for example, H(2)⋯O(2): 0.820 Å, H(2)⋯S(1A): 2.451 Å, S(1A)⋯O(2): 3.229 Å, ∠O(2)–H(2)⋯S(1A): 158.6° (Atom with additional label A refers to the symmetry operations: −x + 1, −y + 2, −z + 1). Then the 2D square grid-like layers are stacked each other in an AB manner, displaying a slipped geometry stabilized through the intermolecular O–H⋯S H-bonds (Fig. 2). Such non-interpenetrated stacking mode of square grid layers can effectively reduce the accessible channel volumes. Calculated by PLATON software, no accessible void is left. Actually, no solvent molecules are found in complex 1.

Complex 2 was obtained when methanol was used instead of water in the preparation of complex 1. Different solvent media lead to the different crystallizations of the compound. Complex 2 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/n space group (no. 14). Cu(1) locates in the inversion center (position coordinates: 0.5, 0, 0.5) and the asymmetric unit of complex 2 contains only one Cu(II), one fluconazole and one coordinated SCN−. The Cu(II) center also exhibits a distorted octahedral geometry and is six coordinated by six nitrogen atoms from four fluconazole [Cu–N 2.099(4)–2.109(4) Å, N–Cu–N 88.67(15)–180.00(10)°] and two SCN−. The two SCN− in complex 2 exhibit equivalent coordination mode and the Cu–N bond length is 2.059(4) Å, which is much shorter than those in complex 1. It is indicated that the binding strength of SCN− in complex 2 is much stronger than that in complex 1. Each fluconazole in complex 2 also links two Cu(II) centers via its two triazole ligands, but with a Cu⋯Cu separation of 12.008 Å, which is shorter than those in complex 1. Because the flexible C–C chain of fluconazole can be rotated for suitable position and angle to fit the different binding modes of SCN−, in complex 2 the dihedral angle between the two triazole planes of fluconazole is 107.6°. The dihedral angles between one triazole plane and the aromatic phenyl ring of fluconazole are 51.6° and 56.3°.

Complex 2 exhibits a 2D layer-like structure but with a parquet motif (Fig. 3). The 2,4-difluorophenyl ligands of two fluconazole are not above or below the layer, but orderly array within the layer. In one layer, the dihedral angles between the neighboring two phenyl rings are 77.2° and 102.8°. The centroid–centroid distances between aromatic planes are no shorter than 4.104 Å, indicating no strong π–π stacking interactions exist in complex 2.

Intermolecular H-bonds are observed between the triazole groups and the uncoordinated hydroxyl groups of fluconazole, for example, H(1)⋯O(1): 0.820 Å, H(1)⋯N(2B): 2.520 Å, N(5B)⋯O(1): 3.100 Å, ∠O(1)–H(1)⋯N(5B): 128.7° (Atom with additional label B refers to the symmetry operations: x − 1/2, −y + 1/2, z + 1/2). The 2D layers with parquet motif in complex 2 stacked on top of each other in a AA mode via intermolecular H-bonds and short S⋯S contacts (3.421 Å).

Complex 2 exhibits an AA stacking mode instead of AB stacking mode in complex 1. No solvent molecules are found in complex 2, too. Calculated by PLATON software, the accessible void is 13 Å3, which is not big enough to hold the solvent methanol, indicating the parquet motif of the layer in complex 2 is a more dense architecture than the square grid framework in complex 1[14].

The powder X-ray diffraction patterns of complexes 1 and 2 are shown in Fig. S1 in the supporting information. All the peaks of the two complexes can be indexed to their respective simulated XRD powder pattern, which indicates each of them is pure phase. The emission spectra of complexes 1 and 2 are investigated on a RF-5301PC spectrofluorometer equipped with a 450 W xenon lamp as the excitation source. As shown in Fig. S2a in the supporting informations, fluconazole displays one intense emission peak at 371 nm and three weak shoulder peaks at 397, 453, 470 nm. Whereas complex 1 is nonemissive at room temperature when excited in the range of 220–800 nm, indicating the total quenching of the emission of fluconazole [15]. Complex 2 exhibits two weak emission peaks at 358 and 428 nm when excited at 390 nm (Fig. S2b). When the excitation wavelength is decreased or increased, the emission peaks’ positions of complex 2 are shift a little and the curve’s shape almost has not be changed, but the overall emission intensities are lowered. It is indicated that complex 2 exhibits the strongest emission when excited at 390 nm. The result is in good agreement with the excitation spectrum of complex 2, in which it exhibits the excitation peak at 390 nm (Fig. S3).

Complexes 1 and 2 are supramolecular isomers, the minor variance in their structures leads to their different properties. The detailed mechanism is under investigation.

In summary, using Cu(II) salts, KSCN and fluconazole, two supramolecular isomers 1 and 2 have been obtained hydro(solvo)thermally. Solvents have not entered the structures of the two compounds, but the different solvents media in their preparations lead to the different binding modes of anion SCN−. The flexible C–C chain of fluconazole has been rotated for suitable position and angle to fit the different binding modes of SCN−. Complex 1 exhibits a 2D square grid-like layer and complex 2 exhibits a layer-like framework with a parquet motif. Different H-bonds and stacking modes are observed in the two compounds. Complex 1 is nonemissive and complex 2 exhibits weak photoluminescence property. The present work shows it is possible to achieve true supramolecular isomers via the selection of anions and the control of solvents, and the present work provides a robust and effective method to obtain the specific supramolecular isomers. However, due to the small free energy differences between supramolecular isomers, to investigate the detailed formation mechanism of supramolecular isomerism is still underway.

Crystallographic data for complexes 1 and 2 were collected on a Siemens SMART-CCD diffractometer with Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). An empirical absorption correction from ψ scan was applied. All the structure was solved by direct methods and expanded using Fourier techniques. The nonhydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. Hydrogen atoms were located in the calculated positions. All calculations were performed using the shelxtl-97 program.

Crystals of complex 1 were obtained by hydrothermal technique. A mixture of Cu(ClO4)2 (0.5 mmol, 0.132 g), fluconazole (0.5 mmol, 0.153 g), KSCN (1.5 mmol, 0.146 g) and water (10 mL) was heated at 120 °C in Teflon-lined autoclaves for three days, followed by slow cooling to room temperature. The resulting blue column crystals were filtered off (yield: ca. 90% based on fluconazole). Elemental Anal. Found: C, 42.50; H, 3.16; N, 24.68%.
