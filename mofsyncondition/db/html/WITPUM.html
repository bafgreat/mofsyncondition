Two luminescent metal − organic frameworks constructed by unsymmetric tricarboxylate

Abstract

Two metal–organic frameworks, Zn(Hbpt)(bpp) (1), [Cd(Hbpt)(bpp)(H2O)] (2), (H3bpt = biphenyl-3,4′,5-tricarboxylate, bpp = 1,3-bi(4-pyridyl)propane), have been solvothermally synthesized from unsymmetric tricarboxylate and nitrogen containing ligands, and characterized by single-crystal and powder X-ray diffractions, FTIR, and TGA. Single-crystal X-ray structural analyses show that 1 possesses a 2D grid layer consisting of Zn ions bridged by bpp ligands and partly deprotonated Hbpt2 − ligands, which is extended via hydrogen bonds to a two-fold interpenetrated framework. 2 contains 1D ladder-like chains, which are further linked by hydrogen bonds into the 3D supramolecular architecture. Furthermore, photoluminescence studies revealed that these coordination polymers display intense violet emission in the solid state at room temperature.

Graphical abstract

Two d10 metal − organic frameworks (MOFs) based on biphenyl-3,4′,5-tricarboxylate (H3bpt) and 1,3-bi(4-pyridyl)propane (bpp), Zn(Hbpt)(bpp) (1), [Cd(Hbpt)(bpp)(H2O)] (2), were obtained and characterized. 1 features a 2D grid layer and 2 affords a 1D ladder-like chain. The MOFs exhibit interesting fluorescent dual emission in the solid state.

Design and synthesis of metal − organic frameworks (MOFs) or coordination polymer have attracted immense current research interest not only for their fascinating structures but also more importantly for potential applications in various fields including gas storage and separation, heterogeneous catalysis, nonlinear optics, luminescent sensing, and drug delivery [1], [2]. MOFs are generally synthesized by the self-assembly of metal ions/clusters as coordination centers and organic ligands as linkers. The modular nature and mild synthesis condition allow for incorporating limitless diverse building blocks into the frameworks, endowing them with multifunctional properties. Thus, MOFs certainly are promising luminescent materials because both the inorganic and the organic components constituting the metal–organic frameworks can provide the excellent platforms to generate luminescence [1h].

Carboxylate linkers including dicarboxylate, tricarboxylate, tetracarboxyalte, and even dendritic multicarboxylate ligands are among the most important and widely used bridging ligands for constructing metal–organic frameworks [3]. Compared to the symmetric carboxylates, unsymmetric ones are less explored but recently have drawn great attention [4]. For example, Matzger group synthesized an unsymmetrically substituted linker H3BPT (H3BPT = biphenyl-3,4′,5-tricarboxyilc acid) and used it to construct a porous MOF with impressive gas sorption capacity [5]. Subsequently, the bpt linker has widely been utilized by other research groups to construct metal–organic frameworks because of its multiple coordination sites and unsymmetric structure. In addition, the d10 metal–organic frameworks exhibit photoluminescent properties, and some of them show interesting dual emission [6]. In light of these observations, we have chosen Zn(II) and Cd(II) as d10 metal ions, and the unsymmetric linker H3bpt and N-donor ligands (bpp = 1,3-bi(4-pyridyl)propane) as the first and second ligands, respectively, to construct new d10 metal–organic frameworks and investigated their luminescent properties. We herein report the synthesis [7] and structural characterization [8] of two luminescent metal–organic frameworks: Zn(Hbpt)(bpp) (1), and [Cd(Hbpt)(bpp)(H2O)] (2). The results show that the H3bpt ligand adopts different coordination modes in these new MOFs and more importantly, these MOFs exhibit the interesting dual emission.

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analyses revealed that 1 crystallizes in triclinic space group P1¯. The asymmetric unit of 1 consists of one Zn(II) center, one Hbpt2 − linker, and one bpp molecule. As shown in Fig. 1a, the Zn(II) atom is five-coordinated by three oxygen atoms (O(1), O(2), O(6)) from two carboxylates of two different Hbpt2 − ligands and two nitrogen atoms (N(1), N(2)) from two bpp ligands in a distorted trigonal bipyramid coordination geometry [ZnN2O3]. The ZnO bond lengths fall in the range of those reported in other Zn(II) complexes with N,O-mixed ligands [9]. One carboxylate group of the organic linker Hbpt2 − is not depronated, while the other two adopt monodentate (μ1-η1), and bidentate-chelating (μ1-η1:η1) coordination modes, respectively. The Zn(II) ions are bridged by two Hbpt2 − and two bpp ligands to afford a 2D (4,4)-grid structure (Fig. 1b) where the separations of Zn⋯Zn are 14.168 and 13.245 Å. The 2D layers are further extended to a 3D network via intermolecular hydrogen bonds between the protonated carboxylate O(3) atom and the carboxylate O(1) atoms of neighboring layer (Fig. 1c). Due to the large void in the single framework, two such frameworks interpenetrate each other via intermolecular π–π interactions (Fig. 1d). As a result, the porosity of the framework is further tuned. The large rectangular channels along the a axis within the single framework are dissected into the smaller one with dimension of 7.2 × 10.0 Å, taking into account of van der Waals radii.

2 crystallizes in triclinic space group P1¯. The asymmetric unit contains one Cd(II) center, one Hbpt2 − ligand, one bpp molecule and one water molecule. The Cd(1) atom is seven-coordinated by four oxygen atoms from two carboxylate groups of two different Hbpt2 − ligands, two nitrogen atoms from two bpp ligands and one water molecule to generate a distorted pentagonal–bipyramidal coordination geometry (Fig. 2a). One carboxylate group of the organic linker Hbpt2 − is not deprotonated, while the other two adopt the same bidentate-chelating (μ1-η1:η1) coordination mode. The Cd(II) ions are linked by Hbpt2 − and bpp linkers to form 1D ladder-like chain along the a axis (Fig. 2b). The ladder chains are extended to a 3D network along the c axis via intermolecular hydrogen bonds (Table S4, Fig. 2c).

From the above structure analysis, it can be seen the coordination mode of the bpt linker is distinctly different in the title MOFs: one of three carboxylate groups of the bpt linker is not deprotonated, while the other two adopt monodentate, and bidentate-chelating coordination modes for 1. One carboxylate is not deprotonated, while the other two adopt the same bidentate-chelating coordination modes for 2. The dihedral angles between two phenyl rings in the bpt linkers are 49.32° with trans–trans conformations for 1, and 34.38° for 2 with trans–cis ones. Clearly, different hydrothermal reaction conditions such as the metal/ligand ratios, pH etc., result in the distinct structures of the complexes 1–2.

The experimental PXRD patterns of the MOFs 1–2 match with their simulated ones, indicating the phase purity of the bulk materials. The difference in reflection intensities between the simulated and experimental patterns is due to the preferred orientation of the powder sample during collection of the experimental PXRD data (Fig. S1).

To study the thermal stability of these coordination polymers, thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) of the MOFs 1–2 were performed (Fig. S2). 1 has not obvious weight loss until 260 °C. The observed total weight loss from 260 to 510 °C corresponds to the decomposing of the organic ligands (obsd. 84.08%, calcd. 85.18%). The final residuals may be ZnO. 2 exhibits a weight loss of 34.32% from 60 to 370 °C, corresponding to the loss of the bpp molecules and coordinated water molecules (obsd. 34.32%, calcd. 35.28%). The further decomposition occurs in the range of 370 to 460 °C, which attributes to the elimination of the organic ligands. The final residuals may be CdO (obsd. 18.13%, calcd. 20.95%).

Given that the d10 metal complexes have interesting photoluminescent properties, the solid-state luminescent spectra of H3bpt ligand and its MOFs 1–2 were measured at room temperature. As shown in Fig. S3, the maximum emission band for the free ligand is at 407 nm upon excited at 359 nm, which is probably attributed to the π*-n or π*-π transitions. The two complexes 1–2 exhibit the similar emission character to the free H3BPT ligand upon the same excitation, indicating that the emission of the complexes may be assigned to the intraligand charge transfer transition [10]. Moreover, compared to the free ligand, a remarkable luminescence enhancement of the complexes was observed, which is considered as the consequence of increasing molecular rigidity due to the intermolecular interactions such as π–π packing, hydrogen bonding etc., upon metal complexation, thus slowing down non-radiative decay from excited state.

In conclusion, two new d10 metal–organic frameworks were successfully synthesized using an unsymmetric tricarboxylate and nitrogen-containing ligands. The resulting structures are clearly dependent on the hydrothermal reaction conditions such as metal/ligand ratios, pH etc., where bpt3 − ligands adopt different coordination modes. Moreover, these compounds have good thermal stability up to 400 °C, and exhibit the intense violet emission.
