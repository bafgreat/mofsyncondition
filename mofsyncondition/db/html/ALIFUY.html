Selective sensing of 2,4,6-trinitrophenol and detection of the ultralow temperature based on a dual-functional MOF as a luminescent sensor

Abstract

A novel 3D Cd(II) MOF, {[Cd(Ccbp−)(4.4′-bpy)·H2O]·(ClO4−)·2H2O} (1), (Ccbp− = 3-carboxy-1-(4-carboxybenzyl)pyridin-1-ium, 4.4′-bpy = 4.4′-bipyridine), has been successfully synthesized and characterized. Complex 1 possesses a 3D 4-fold interpenetrating dia network with a pyridine cation basic skeleton. This Cd-MOF 1 can be applied as the first potential dual-functional luminescent sensing material for high selective and recyclable sensing of 2,4,6-trinitrophenol and quantitatively detecting the ultralow temperature from 10 to 60 K.

A novel luminescent Cd(II) metal-organic framework with 3D 4-fold interpenetrating dia network has been successfully synthesized and characterized. In the past decades, the selective and rapid detection of the nitro compounds, such as nitromethane (NM), nitrobenzene (NB), nitrotoluene (NT) and 2,4,6-trinitrophenol (TNP), has drawn great interest due to its importance in homeland security, civilian safety, and environmental protection. Among such nitro compounds, TNP is the most well-known explosive and commonly used for military applications and terrorism all over the world [1]. Although many methods like gas chromatography [2], ion mobility spectrometry [3], mass spectrometry [4] and X-ray detection [5] have been developed for explosive detection, these detection techniques are limited by their equipment demands, time-consuming and cost drawbacks. Compared with those traditional methods, fluorescence detection has obvious advantages and gains more attentions owing to its high sensibility, simplicity, short response time and convenient detection [6]. Taking into account this kind of materials are still defective, so the synthesis and development of new materials for fluorescent detection of nitro compounds are still a challenge work. The immense interests in the development of luminescent materials based on metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) is of considerable increasing in recent years due to their intriguing structural topologies and potential applications in high selectivity and sensitivity for detecting anions [7], cations [8], explosives [9], small molecules [10] and vapor [11]. Recently, a large amount of luminescent MOF sensors for detecting the nitro compounds have been reported, most of which exhibit high selectivity and most of which were constructed by d10 transition metal ions and π-conjugated ligands [12]. However, those luminescent MOFs reported so far exhibit mainly mono-function detection. Only a few rare MOFs in dual functional luminescent detection have been reported [13].

Temperature is one of the most fundamental physical properties in many scientific fields, and temperature-dependent luminescence is a common phenomenon. Besides conventional thermometers, luminescent sensors based on MOFs are considered to be attractive alternatives for sensing and on-line monitoring temperature because of their accuracy, non-invasive nature, fast response, and working capability in strong electric or magnetic fields [14]. Over the past few years, a large number of temperature probes based on MOFs have been realized [15]. However, only a small number of these luminescent MOFs sensors have been reported which are capable of quantitative detection [16].

As an effective combination of the above mentioned aspects, we design a new 3D Cd-MOF, {[Cd(Ccbp−)(4.4′-bpy)·H2O]·(ClO4−)·2H2O}, as a luminescent sensor not only exhibits high selective sensing of TNP in the ethanol solution but also can quantify the temperature from 10 K to 60 K. To the best of our knowledge, no single Cd-MOF that exhibiting dual-function of detecting TNP and temperature at the same time has been reported. Thus, this is the first Cd-MOF that can selectively detect TNP and quantify the temperature.

The zwitterion ligand H2CcbpCl synthesized and characterized by 1H NMR and 13C NMR (Figs. S1 and S2) [17]. The single crystal of 1 suitable for X-ray diffraction studies was obtained by the solvothermal reaction of Cd(ClO4)2·6H2O (0.3 mml), H2CcbpCl (0.1 mml), NaOH (0.2 mml) and 4.4′-bipyridine (0.1 mml) in 10 ml H2O/ethanol (1:1). The detailed procedure of synthesis of ligand and complex 1 are given in the ESI.

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction study reveals that complex 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic system with P2(1)/n space group. As shown in Fig. 1a, Cd1 is coordinated by two nitrogen atoms from two different 4.4′-bipyridine, three oxygen atom from two carboxylate oxygen atoms of two different Ccbp− ligands and the other one oxygen atom of coordination water. The coordination environment of the central Cd2 + ion, represented by a coordination polyhedron, is a pseudo-octahedral coordination geometry with the CdN/O bond distances for Cd1 fall in the range between 2.276(4) and 2.405(3) Å (Table 1, Table 2, ESI), which are close to those reported in Cd-coordination polymers [18]. Next, each deprotonated Ccbp− ligand (μ2-η2:η1) and each the 4.4′-bipyridine ligand connect to the adjacent two Cd2 + ions, resulting in a 3D dia network with large cavities (Fig. 1b and c). Furthermore, the large diamondoid cage permits the adjacent equivalent dia nets to entangle each other, forming a 4-fold interpenetrating dia network (Fig. 1d), in which the ClO4− anions are located. It is noteworthy that there are pyridine cations pointing to the zwitterionic pyridinium ligand Ccbp− of 1, which can be used to explore the sensing properties. The TG curve for 1 shows a gradual weight loss of 9.1% between 80 and 186 °C, which can be ascribed to the removal of one coordinated water molecule and two free water molecules (Fig. S4). Furthermore, the PXRD patterns for complex 1 is shown in Fig. S3. The diffraction peaks of the experimental patterns are in good consistency with the simulated data, indicating that the synthesized bulk materials are consistent with the determined single-crystal structure.

Considering the previous studies have shown that Cd2 + coordination polymers exhibit luminescent properties [19], the luminescence spectra of the free Ccbp− ligand and the as-synthesized sample of 1 in solid state were investigated at room temperature under excitation at 325 and 296 nm, respectively. (Fig. 2) The emission spectrum of the free ligand exhibits two broad emission bands at 384 and 427 nm, which can be attributed to the ligand-centered electronic transition [20] (Fig. 2). Similar to the free Ccbp− ligand, the complex 1 displayed emission peak at 367 nm. Compared with the free ligand, the 1 showed a weaker emission band blue-shifted to different extents, which probably originates from the ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT) [21]. The luminescence of 1 motivated us to investigate its potential application in the detection of small molecules and temperature.

The luminescence responses of 1 to various nitro compounds were investigated by addition of 2,4,6-trinitrophenol (TNP), 4-nitrophenol (4-NP), 3-nitrophenol (3-NP), 2,4,6-trinitrotoluene (TNT), o-nitrotoluene (o-NT), m-nitrotoluene (m-NT), nitrobenzene (NB) and nitromethane (NM) to 1-ethanol suspensions, respectively, with the concentration of the nitro compound being 50 μM (Fig. 3a). All eight nitro compounds can weaken the luminescent intensity of 1 to a varying degree. Interestingly, a significant luminescence attenuation with a QP (quench percentage) of 79% is observed in the presence of TNP (Fig. 3a). However, the QP of 1 of 4-NP, 3-NP, TNT, o-NT, m-NT, NB and NM is 65%, 54%, 40%, 29%, 21%, 10%, 5%, respectively. These results indicate that 1 possesses a highly selective sensing ability to TNP compared to other nitroaromatics.

For better understanding of the sensing ability of 1 for TNP, the sensing properties of 1 for TNP were also investigated by recording the emissive spectra of the 1-ethanol suspension with the gradual addition of TNP. As shown in Fig. 3b, the luminescence intensity decreases clearly with the increasing TNP concentration. When the concentration of TNP in the 1-ethanol suspension reaches 85.2 μM, the fluorescence is almost completely quenched with a QP of 91%. Moreover, the quenching effect can be rationalized for the low concentrations by the Stern–Volmer equation: Io/I = 1 + Ksv × [M], where Io and I are the luminescence intensities of ethanol suspensions of complex 1 before and after the addition of TNP, respectively; Ksv is the quenching constant, and [M] is the molar concentration of TNP [22]. On the basis of the quenching experimental data, the linear correlation coefficient (R) in the Ksv curve of 1 with addition of TNP is 0.9971 (Fig. 3c), suggesting that the quenching effect of TNP on the fluorescence of 1 fits well the Stern–Volmer model. The Ksv is 7.34 × 104 M− 1 at low concentrations of TNP, which is comparable to those of previously reported MOF-based sensors, such as the [Cd3(TPT)2(DMF)2]·(H2O)0.5] (6.56 × 104 M− 1) [23], [Cd2(NDC)(PCA)2] (3.42 × 104 M− 1) [24], and [Eu(L)1.5(DEF)n] (6.56 × 104 M− 1) [25]. The possible reason for the luminescence quenching may be due to the photoinduced electron-transfer (PET) mechanism. The electron-transfer progress can be interpreted by inductive effect. The nitro compounds with electron-deficient property can obtain an electron from excited ligand, which is similar with that reported in previous work [26], [27]. The linear Stern–Volmer relationship was observed at low concentrations of TNP, which is speculated that a static quenching process is dominant, while a nonlinear and upward curve (Fig. 3c) at higher concentrations suggested that the fluorescence quenching mechanism can be attributed to the presence of simultaneous dynamic and static quenching [26]. This static quenching may be due to the electrostatic interactions between the pyridine cations of zwitterionic ligands and hydroxyl groups of TNP (Fig. S6), while the dynamic one may be due to the collisional encounters between the fluorene moieties and TNP [27].

Additionally, we also found that 1 can be regenerated and reused for five numbers of cycles by centrifuging of the dispersed crystals in ethanol after sensing and washing several times with ethanol (Fig. 4). The quenching efficiencies of every cycle are basically unchanged through monitoring the emission spectra of 1 dispersed in the presence of 50 μM TNP in ethanol. The PXRD patterns of the initial sample and recovered sample after five cycles of quenching and recovery also indicate the high stability of this compound (Fig. S7). The result reveals that 1 could be applied as a fluorescence sensor for TNP with high selectivity and recyclability.

The temperature-dependent luminescent intensity property of complex 1 was investigated without changing its original structure (Fig. S8) in order to establish their potentials as luminescent thermometer. Fig. 5 showed the temperature-dependent emission spectra of the Cd(II) coordination polymer 1 recorded at different temperatures (10–150 K). The intensities of the luminescent emissions monitored at 367 nm decrease gradually as the temperature increases because the cooling gradually limits the thermally activated intramolecular rotations and nonradiative-decay pathways [28]. Importantly, the luminescence intensity of complex 1 is decreased linearly corresponding to the temperature change from 10 to 60 K (R2 = 0.9982). Indeed, among reported the coordination polymers, only very rare examples can detect the temperature changes with such excellent linear from 10 to 60 K [16]. This suggests that the coordination polymer 1 is an excellent luminescent thermometer in this temperature range.

In summary, a new dual-functional luminescent sensor Cd-MOF 1 has been successfully designed and synthesized by the solvothermal method. This Cd-MOF 1 was utilized as chemosensor to detect the various nitro compounds to find that this one can exhibit a high selectivity and recyclability for TNP in ethanol solution, and 1 was also utilized as a luminescent thermometer to detect the temperature to find that 1 as has the potential to quantitatively detect ultralow temperature over a wide range from 10 K to 60 K. These results indicate that Cd-MOF 1 can be applied as the first potential dual-functional luminescent sensing material for high selectively sensing TNP and quantitatively detecting the ultralow temperature. Further studies are under way in our lab.

  • Detection of 2,4,6-trinitrophenol based on f–f transition of Eu<sup>2+</sup>

    2021, Journal of Rare Earths

    In this paper, we designed Eu2+-doped BaAlF5 and BaSiF6 downconversion nanophosphors with strong f–f transition emission to realize the quantitative detection of TNP. The surface of the as-prepared nanophosphors was modified by polyethylenimine (PEI) to increase the water solubility and to provide a pair of electrons to bind to the benzene ring in TNP, forming a Meisenheimer complex. The detection principle of TNP is based on luminescence resonance energy transfer (LRET) technology, where the PEI-functionalized BaAlF5:Eu2+ and BaSiF6:Eu2+ nanophosphors are used as energy donors and TNP is an energy acceptor. With increasing TNP concentrations, the luminescence intensity of the BaAlF5:Eu2+ and BaSiF6:Eu2+ nanophosphors at 359 nm [4f7(6P7/2)→4f7(8S7/2) transition of Eu2+] displays dramatic luminescent quenching. The BaAlF5:Eu2+@PEI or BaSiF6:Eu2+@PEI nanophosphor downconversion luminescent sensors show great sensitivity and selectivity and good linearity, and the detection limits of TNP are 0.57 and 2.82 ng/mL, respectively.
