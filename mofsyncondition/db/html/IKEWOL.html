Introduction

There has been intensive research in developing and designing self-assembly of functional materials for applications in electronic, magnetic, and optical devices over the past decade.1 Among the relevant approaches, the silver(i) complexes with mixed ligands particularly have been the subject of considerable interest because of their unusual structural diversity.2 This can be attributed to a lack of stereochemical preferences from a d10 configuration. Moreover, due to the weak bonding nature between the silver(i) ion and ligands, various weak interactions and crystal packing forces2d in the solid state may have greater influence on structure formation. In addition to the nature of metal atoms and ligands, the role of counter anions and solvents amid self-assembly processes has emerged as an increasingly active theme in recent studies.3 Accordingly, understanding the relationship between complexes structures and the factors affecting the formation of the framework, as well as the exploration of its perhaps unprecedented properties remain great interest and challenge.

On the other hand, flexible nitrogen-heterocyclic ligands have been shown to well suited for the coordination geometries in the assembly process.4 In the relevant approach, the oligo-α-pyridylamido ligands have proven to be good N-donors toward first to even third row transition metal ions; the resulting extended networks with different coordination mode have been achieved particularly by Peng's and Cotton's groups.5 In view of applications, several research groups have extended the oligo-α-pyridylamido ligands networks toward magnetic and electronic properties.6 Conversely, studies of structure-versus-luminescence behaviour, especially the optical non-linear properties, utilizing Ag(i) framework is relatively scant. During the past decade, much effort has been dedicated to the study of one-photon excitation properties of silver(i) complexes, and the latent applications as emitters or chemical sensors.7 Nevertheless, in several aspects, two-photon excitation renders certain advantages over one-photonspectroscopy in view of e.g. three-dimensional optical data storage,8 two-photon optical power limiting,9 micro-fabrication10a–c and 3D-bioimaging,10d–eetc.

Herein, we report on the self assembly of silver salts (AgX, X = ClO4−, CF3SO3−, PF6−) by using N,N′-di(pyrazin-2-yl)pyridine-2,6-diamine (H2dpzpda) as the bridging ligand. Based on these building blocks, four new complexes [Ag2(H2dpzpda)2](ClO4)2 (1), [Ag2(H2dpzpda)2(ClO4)2] (DMF)2 (2), [Ag2(H2dpzpda)2](CF3SO3)2 (3), and [Ag2(H2dpzpda)(DMF)4](PF6)2·DMF (4) have been synthesized and structurally characterized. The influences of counter anions on the resulting structures as well as photoluminescence properties are discussed. Moreover, to expand our long-term interest in metal organic frameworks (MOF) suitable for luminescent devices, two-photon excitation measurements are performed in this studied. As a result, two-photon absorption cross section for complex 2 is deduced to be as high as 43.4 ± 2.2 GM (800 nm, 1 GM = 10−50 cm4 s per photon).

Results and discussion

Structure description

To investigate the effect of counter anions on the structures of silver(i) complexes, the reactions of H2dpzpda with AgClO4·xH2O, AgCF3SO3 and AgPF6 in a 1 : 1 molar ratio were performed to afford complexes 1–4, respectively. The structures of 1–4 were subsequently characterized by elemental analyses, IR spectroscopy and single-crystal X-ray diffraction analyses. X-Ray structure determination revealed that the complexes 1, 3 and 4 are crystallized in the monoclinic P21/c space group, while compound 2 belongs to the triclinic P space group (see Table 1). [Ag2(H2dpzpda)2](ClO4)2 (1) and [Ag2(H2dpzpda)2(ClO4)2] (DMF)2 (2)

Reaction of AgClO4·xH2O and H2dpzpda with 1 : 1 molar ratio by diffusing diethyl ether into the DMF solution leads to the formation of two types of crystals with distinctly different morphologies, i.e. of needle for 1 and plate for 2. Also, the color appearance of complex 1 is yellow, whereas that for the complex 2 is light-yellow. The molecular structures of 1 and 2 are depicted in Fig. 1(a) and 2(a), respectively, in which H2dpzpda is used as a bridging ligand with bis-monodentate coordination mode, as shown in Scheme 1(b). As shown in Fig. 1 and 2, each silver(i) of 1 or 2 in the [2 : 2] metallomacrocycle was linearly coordinated by two N donors from the different H2dpzpda ligands with Ag–N7A = 2.140(3) Å, Ag–N6 = 2.148(3) Å and N–Ag–N angle = 168.64(9)° in 1; Ag–N7A = 2.166(5) Å, Ag–N6 = 2.175(5) Å and N–Ag–N angle = 170.3(2)° in 2. All the Ag–N distances are within the normal range (2.11–2.63 Å) reported in literatures.11 The dihedral angles between the planes of the pyrazinyl and pyridyl units are 11.4° (or 8.3°) in 1 and 7.3° (or 6.0°) in 2, respectively, whereas the two pyrazinyl rings are almost parallel with a dihedral angle of 14.2° in 1 and 9.2° in 2. Moreover, as shown in Fig. 1(a) and 1(b), it is apparent that amine atoms from H2dpzpda ligand in 1 are hydrogen bonded with perchlorateoxygen atoms with N2⋯O1 = 2.967(2) Å and N4⋯O3 = 3.120(3) Å. Slightly different hydrogen bonding configuration is resolved for 2, in which one of amine atoms of H2dpzpda forms hydrogen bond with perchlorateoxygen atom [N2⋯O2 = 2.879(8) Å], while the other is with DMFsolventoxygen atom [N4⋯O5 = 2.905(8) Å] (see Fig. 2(a)–2(c)).

The packing diagram (Fig. 1(b)) also shows that two ClO4− counter anions are located above and below the macrocycle plane perhaps through the Ag⋯O interaction with an Ag⋯O3 distance of 2.867(15) Å, which is shorter than sum of the van der Waals radii (3.24 Å).11d Adjacent macrocyclic rectangles in 1 are packed orderly by the intermolecular π–π stacking interaction between the pyridyl rings of H2dpzpda ligands with distance between the ring centroids of 3.426(13) Å, as well as stabilized by two sets of hydrogen bonds formed between the nitrogen atoms of aminegroups and oxygen atoms of ClO4− with N2⋯O1 = 2.967(2) and N4⋯O3 = 3.120(3) Å. As depicted in Fig. 1(c), the dimeric units are further linked by two sets of weak hydrogen bonds between carbon atom of pyrazinylgroup and the oxygen atom of ClO4− [C1–H1⋯O4 with C(1)⋯O(4) = 3.168(4) Å] in an ABAB fashion into a 3D supramolecular framework. The results manifest the important role of weak hydrogen bonds in governing the structure of metal complexes. Note that the contribution of hydrogen-bond interactions to the alignment of 1D tubes and its conversion into 3D framework has recently received much attention.12

Apart from complex 1, the packing of complex 2 is depicted in Fig. 2(b). Interestingly, the counter anion ClO4− in 2 not only is responsible for compensation of the electronic charge of the metals, but also serves as a bridge ligand to link adjacent dimeric units with Ag⋯O1 = 2.627(5) and AgB⋯O1 = 2.639(6) Å (the symmetry transformation of B is −x + 1, −y + 2, −z + 1). The Ag⋯AgB separation is 3.269(4) Å, which is shorter than the sum of the van der Waals radii for two silver atoms (3.44 Å),13 indicating a significant intermolecular Ag⋯Ag interaction, which assembles the rectangles to form a 1D chain-like array. Additionally, the face-to-face intercyclic π–π interactions between the pyrazinyl ring with ring centroid distances at 3.587(5) Å and two sets of hydrogen bonds [C2⋯O1 = 3.302(8) Å and N2⋯O2 = 2.879(8) Å] intensify the extension of macrocycles into an infinite 1D chain motif (denotes column A). Adjacent columns are then aligned in a parallel AA fashion in the unit cell (Fig. 2(c)). The relevant hydrogen-bonding geometries are tabulated in Table 3.

[Ag2(H2dpzpda)2](CF3SO3)2 (3)

Similar to compound 1, complex 3 also consists of one dimeric unit (see Fig. 3(a)–3(c)) and two coordinated CF3SO3− anions through Ag⋯O interaction with Ag–O3 = 2.775(2) Å and dual intramolecular hydrogen bonds [C11⋯O2 = 3.362(4), N4⋯O2 = 3.083(3) Å]. Each Ag(i) of 3 is almost linearly coordinated to two H2dpzpda ligands with N6–Ag–N7A angle, 170.97(8)°, Ag–N6 = 2.128(2) Å, Ag–N7A = 2.133(2) Å. The dihedral angles between the planes of the pyrazinyl and pyridyl units in the structure of 3 are 4.0 and 2.6°, respectively, whereas the two pyrazinyl rings are almost parallel with a dihedral angle of 1.6°. Two amine atoms are hydrogen bonded to the oxygen atoms (O1 and O2) from CF3SO3− anion with distances of N2⋯O1 = 3.049(3) Å and N4⋯O2 = 3.083(3) Å.

In complex 3, the CF3SO3− ligands act as bridging ligands, which connect another dimeric unit via intermolecular π–π stacking and intermolecular C–H⋯O and N–H⋯O hydrogen bonds with C6⋯O1 = 3.334(3), C12⋯O3 = 3.284(3), C2⋯O3 = 3.302(3) and N2⋯O1 = 3.049(3) Å. The distance of intermolecular π–π stacking between pyrazinyl from pyridyl rings of dimeric units, measured by centroid to centroid distance, is estimated to be 3.639(2) Å. The combination of intermolecular π–π interactions and hydrogen bonds assemble the dimeric units to form an ABAB supramolecular chain. The A or B chains are then stabilized by intermolecular hydrogen bond C–H⋯N interaction with C13⋯N1 = 3.327(4) Å to construct a 3D framework (see Fig. 3(b) and 3(c)).

[Ag2(H2dpzpda)(DMF)4](PF6)2·DMF (4)

Upon reacting AgPF6 with H2dpzpda, a new framework of complex 4 was obtained. The molecular structure of 4 is depicted in Fig. 4(a), and the selected bond distances and angles are given in Table 2. A 1D ladder-like infinite chain is formed through the connectivity of silver(i) and H2dpzpda as shown in Fig. 4(a). Although the topology is a ladder type,14 to our knowledge, [Ag2(H2dpzpda)2](PF6)2(DMF)2 represents a novel structure with respect to previously reported silver(i)-Schiff base complexes.15 In stark contrast to 1–3, the coordination of H2dpzpda ligand bonded to Ag(i) ions is in tetra-monodentate mode (see Scheme 1(c)). Ag(i) is four-coordinated and bonded to two nitrogen atoms from two different H2dpzpda ligands [Ag1–N1 = 2.247(3), Ag1–N5A = 2.241(3), Ag2–N6 = 2.213(3), Ag2–N7B = 2.218(3) Å] as well as two oxygen atoms from two different solventsDMF [Ag1–O1 = 2.535(3), Ag1–O2 = 2.408(6), Ag2–O3 = 2.449(4), Ag2–O4 = 2.460(4) Å], forming a distorted tetrahedral geometry (Fig. 4(b)). The Ag–Npyrazinyl bond lengths are very close to the corresponding bond distances found in the reported Ag(i)–Schiff base molecular complexes.16 The angles around the two types of Ag(i) are within 89–158° (The detail of bond angles are shown in Table 2), being responsible for the distorted tetrahedral geometry. Intriguingly, in this case, all nitrogen atoms in pyrazinylgroups of H2dpzpda that act as the bridge ligands are connected by center silver(i) atoms and extended into an infinite 1D chain. The pyridyl and pyrazinyl rings are not co-planar, as evidenced by the dihedral angles of 28.2 and 41.4°, while the two pyrazinyl rings are with a dihedral angle of 13.9°.

The crystal packing of 4 clearly indicates that the neighbouring layers are stacked in ABAB fashion (see Fig. 4(b)). Upon careful examination of the structure, hexafluorophosphate anion (PF6−) and a non-coordinated DMFsolvent are located between two adjacent layers. DMF then connects two adjacent PF6− ions through weak C⋯F hydrogen bonding [C26⋯F11ii = 3.151(9) Å, C26–H26⋯F11ii = 128°; C27⋯F12ii = 3.685(10) Å, C27–H27A⋯F12ii = 156°; C28⋯F8 iv = 3.799(9) Å, C28–H28C⋯F8iv = 175°] to form a linker, which further connects two pyrazinylgroups of H2dpzpda and two coordinated DMF from neighbour layers via weak C⋯F hydrogen bonding [C3⋯F9v = 3.129(6) Å, C3–H3⋯F9v = 125°; C23⋯F8v = 3.799(8) Å, C23–H23⋯F8v = 170°; C23⋯F9v = 3.566(9) Å, C23–H23⋯F9v = 142°; C23⋯F10v = 3.659(9) Å, C23–H23⋯F10v = 143°; C12⋯F8i = 3.205(7) Å, C12–H12⋯F8i = 119°; C12⋯F9i = 3.274(7) Å, C12–H12⋯F9i = 138°] (Symmetry codes: i −x, −y, −z; ii −x, 1/2 + y, 1/2 − z; iii −x, −1/2 + y, 1/2 − z, ivx, 1 + y, z; vx, y, z). On the other hand, PF6− ions and another non-coordinated DMF form hydrogen bonding [N4⋯O5i = 2.727(6) Å, N4–H4⋯O5i = 159°; C26⋯F11ii = 3.151(9) Å, C26–H26⋯F11ii = 128°; C27⋯F12ii = 3.685(10) Å, C27–H27A⋯F12ii = 156°; C24⋯F10v = 3.694(9) Å, C24–H24A⋯F10v = 155°] that bring the adjacent ladder of another side together. In addition, several weak C⋯F hydrogen bonds are also observed between adjacent chains. The distances C22⋯F7vi, C25⋯F8vii, C2⋯F4i, C6⋯F2ii, C22⋯F2i, C20⋯F4i, C20⋯F5i, C21⋯F3viii, C16⋯F3ii, C15⋯F2iii and C17⋯F3i are 3.546(8), 3.418(10), 3.314(6), 3.342(7), 3.619(8), 3.622(7), 3.643(6), 3.747(7), 3.715(6), 3.387(6), 3.564(9) Å, respectively. The angles C22–H22B⋯F7vi, C25–H25C⋯F8vii, C2–H2⋯F4i, C6–H6⋯F2ii, C22–H22A⋯F2i, C20–H20⋯F4i, C20–H20⋯F5i, C21–H21A⋯F3viii, C16–H16C⋯F3ii, C15–H15A⋯F2iii and C17–H17A⋯F3i are 153, 170, 135, 129, 150, 137, 155, 159, 153, 121, 141°, respectively (Symmetry codes: vix, −1/2 − y, −1/2 + z; vii −x, −1 − y, −z; viii −1 + x, 1/2 − y, −1/2 + z). As a result, these hydrogen bond networks assemble the 1D chain into 3D architecture shown in Fig. 4(b).

Photophysical properties

Taking the promising luminescence properties of d10 metal complexes into account,17 the photophysics of 1–4 at room temperature are then investigated. In solution such as DMF, complexes 1–4 all exhibit emission maximized at ∼487 nm, for which the peak wavelength, spectral feature as well as relaxation dynamics are similar to that of the free ligand H2dpzpda (see ESI, Fig. S2).† To further investigate the structure and the stability of 1–4 in solution, ESI-MS data in DMF–MeOH were collected. The results were all similar and showed that the dominate species were the [H2dpzpda-H]+, suggesting that coordination of ligands to the Ag(i) ion was weak in solution phase. In stark contrast, in the single crystal complexes 1–4 reveal remarkable structure-versus-luminescence properties elaborated as follows.

In this approach, the solid-state absorption spectrum is acquired with a diffuse reflectance UV spectrometer, while the corresponding luminescence is acquired with a confocal microscope equipped with a He–Cd laser as the excitation source (see experimental section for detail). Pertinent data are summarized in Table 4. Prior to the discussion, we have also performed a control experiment to acquire the spectrum of solid H2dpzpda under identical experimental condition. Upon excitation at 325 nm, H2dpzpda exhibits a very weak emission (Φf ∼ 0.015) with peak wavelength at 487 nm (Fig. 5, corresponding data are listed in Table 4). In sharp contrast, complexes 1–4 show intense emission with distinctly different peak wavelength at 524 nm (1), 490 nm (2), 564 nm (3) and 546 nm (4).

First of all, the same emission spectral features were resolved throughout different probing areas of the crystals, ruling out the possibility that the emission originates from any crystal defects. In addition, the crystal size of 1–4 selected is similar in size (∼100 μm × 100 μm × 50 μm), so that size effect can be excluded. According to the distinctly different single crystal structures, the difference in emission properties among 1–4 seems to be attributed to the different architectures of the associated crystal packing. In other words, the emission band should be sensitive to the local environment such as anions, solvents, and conformation changes upon coordination with silver metal ions,18 resulting in the red shift of emission relative to that of H2dpzpda.

Interestingly, while 1 and 3 exhibit similar coordination environment (vide supra), in which the anions ClO4− in 1 and CF3SO3− in 3 both display weak coordination to Ag(i), yet they exhibit quite different emission peaks, i.e. 524 nm (1) versus 564 nm (3). As for a rationalization, different stacking arrangement is likely to be the clue. As shown in Fig. 3(b), two sets of infinite intermolecular π–π stacking of pyrazinyl and pyridyl moieties between AB stacking fashion of 3 may permit efficient inter-ligand π–π* transition. Such arrangement is apparently lacking in complex 1 due to its one set of π–π stacking (see Fig. (1b)). The emission quantum yields for e.g.1 and 3 in single crystal were measured to be 0.065 and 0.038, respectively, which are larger than that of H2dpzpda by more than 3–4 folds, manifesting the role of Ag(i), which acts as a bridge to enhance the rigidity of the framework. More detail on the origin of transition will be elaborated in the section of theoretical approach.

Next, both 1 and 2 use the same ClO4− anions to coordinate with Ag+. However, the difference lies in the fact that ClO4− anions in 2 not only coordinate with the metal ions but also act as bridge connecting nearby dimeric units via ligand-supported Ag⋯Ag interactions, resulting in a 1D polymer (see Fig. 2(a)–(c)). Subsequently, the interchains of 1D polymer are further linked by solventDMF. As a result, in comparison to that of 1, the hypsochromic19 and more intensive emission (Φf ∼ 0.32) of 2 (see Fig. 5) may manifest the existence of Ag⋯Ag interactions that enhance the cooperative association of the intermolecular π–π interaction in a e.g. H-type of arrangement. Supplementary support of this viewpoint is provided by its substantially longer observed lifetime (0.98 ns) related to that of 1 (0.55 ns, see Table 4). In brief, the enhancement in luminescence for 1–3 (cf.H2dpzpda) can be attributed to the bridge connection of the ligand to that silver(i) atom, the result of which increases the conformational rigidity of the ligands,20 and hence reduces the non-radioactive deactivation pathways.

Finally, despite the similar weak anion coordination, the emission peak wavelength of 546 nm for complex 4 is bathochromically shifted related to complexes 1 and 2 as well as the free ligand, accompanied by a drastic decrease in the quantum yield (Φf ∼ 0.001) and lifetime (180 ps, see Table 4). The weak emission yield, on the one hand, signifies the lower energy gap of the emission so that the energy gap is perhaps operative.21 On the other hand, the structure of 4 is unique among the titled complexes where there is a lack of π–π stacking (Fig. 4(b)). Such a structural variation may result in differences of the lower lying transition. The latter viewpoint is supportive via the theoretical approach elaborated in the following section.

Theoretical approach

Supplementary supports of the above viewpoints are provided by the computational approaches. In this attempt, ab initio approach based on the full geometry optimized framework of 1–4 is formidable. Alternatively, we then truncate the single crystal complexes 1–4 (the ORTEP view at 150 K) to the suitable size where decent interaction/stacking still exist. To gain more insight into the detail of the electronic transition, time-dependent DFT using functional B3LYP and basis set of 6-31G(d)/LANL2DZ)22 based on the truncated crystal structures were then performed. In this approach, the first 15 lower-lying singlet states were calculated. Fig. 6 depicts the molecular frontier orbitals involved in the lowest lying, i.e. S0 → S1, transition. Evidently, the transition of 1 and 3 is mainly contributed by the intra-ligand charge transfer from the π-bonding orbital of pyridyl and pyrazinyl moieties (π, HOMO) to pyrazinyl part of H2dpzpda (π*, LUMO). As a result, 1 and 3 is ascribed to an intra-ligand-based π*–π transition. Conversely, due to the cooperative effect by silver atoms, the calculated electronic transition of 2 is attributed to a inter-ligand charge-transfer from the non-bonding mixed with π orbital of the perchlorate anions (n mixed with π, HOMO) to the pyrazinyl part of H2dpzpda (π*, LUMO). Last, the lowest-lying transition of 4 in the extended silver(i)-containing polymeric structure is calculated to be from the π-orbital of pyridyl and pyrazinyl moieties of H2dpzpda (π, HOMO) to the proximal pyrazinyl part of H2dpzpda (π*, LUMO). Although the theoretical approach presented here is relatively qualitative, the variation of lowest lying transition as a function of the associated structural properties is apparent, consistent with the differences in photophysical properties observed among the titled complexes.

Two-photon emission properties

According to above results, 1–4 have shown remarkable one-photon excited fluorescence (e.g.2) at the single-crystal level using a confocal microscope. In an aim to employ the as prepared complexes toward more versatile applications, such as bio-imaging, non-linear optics and light-emitting diode, etc., two-photon excitation becomes an obvious choice.23 Taking advantage of intense emission intensity and stability of crystalline solids,24 complex 2 was employed as a paradigm for the following two-photon excitation experiment.

As displayed in Fig. S2(b)† of the ESI, TPEF obtained after excitation at 800 nm showed emission colour of blue-green with a maximum at 490 nm using a 40× microscope objective with an incident laser power 5mW. This result is consistent with one-photon excitation emission upon excitation at 406 nm in respect to peak wavelength and spectral profile (see ESI, Fig. S2(a)),† suggesting that the same emitting states of complex 2 are reached regardless of the mode of the excitation. Fig. 7(a) shows the two-photonexcitation spectrum monitored through the two-photon excited luminescence at ambient temperature. For comparison, the one-photonexcitation spectrum for complex 2 is also drawn in Fig. 7(b). The two photonexcitation spectrum shows a peak at 825 nm with a rise at around 890 nm where the one-photonexcitation spectrum has negligible intensity. Subsequently, the corresponding pump power dependence of the emission intensity at 490 nm for 2, which reveals linear dependence on the incident laser power with a slope of 1.99 ± 0.04, firmly supports the occurrence of two-photon excitation (see Fig. 7(c)). Note that no changes of the fluorescence spectrum were observed upon scanning through different z-position.

Finally, open-aperture Z-scan method with 1 KHz femtosecond laser was performed to determine the two-photon absorption (TPA) cross section based on changes of the transmittance along the Z-scan (see Fig. 7(d) and experimental section for detail). The TPA cross section of complex 2 deduced from Z-scan method was 43.4 ± 2.2 GM (800 nm, 1 GM = 10−50 cm4 s per photon). Note that for the fitting process, the concentration of the single crystal of complex 2 is estimated from its density and molecular weight obtained from X-ray analyses, which is deduced to be 2.16 M. We also acknowledge that the Z-scan method might lead to some overestimation of TPA cross section due to the possible build up of population in the excited state. Accordingly, additional Z-scan experiment has been performed by reducing the repetition rate of laser to 10 Hz; the resulting σ value, within experimental error, remained unchanged. Knowing that the two-photon absorption originates perhaps from a local chromophore (vide infra) rather than the entire framework, the value of 43.4 ± 2.2 GM should be considered as a lower limit. Nevertheless, the σ value of complex 2 is large than several common dyes in the solution form.24

While the two-photon absorption property of complex 2 is clearly authentic, its origin is rather intriguing and deserves to be further explored. On the one hand, the free electrons of complex 2, due to the crosstalk among rectangle units, should exhibit great polarizability upon moving around the space intra- as well as inter-molecularly,24 giving rise to the non-linear property. On the other hand, the observation of non-linear effect in centrosymmetric crystal is commonly due to local non-centrosymmetry caused by defects, which may be generated via partial desolvation of the crystal or via ablation under laser radiation.25 Nevertheless, whether former, latter or both, or other mechanisms contributing to the two photon absorption property of complex 2 are pending for resolution.

Conclusions

In conclusion, several remarks can be summarized in this study: (1) four new silver(i) containing complexes 1–4 based on H2dpzpda ligand and Ag(i)X salts (X = ClO4−, CF3SO3−, PF6−) have been demonstrated, the structures of which depend greatly on the cooperative effects among the anions, solvent and ligand. (2) Single crystals of complexes 1–4 display remarkable structure-versus-luminescence properties, in which the emission of complexes 1 and 3 is ascribed to an intra-ligand-based π*–π transition, while the emission origin of 2 and 4 is likely attributed to a inter-ligand charge-transfer transition. However, due to the cooperative effect by silver atoms that strengthens two rectangle chromophores, complex 2 gives highly intensive emission of Φf ∼ 0.32. In parallel, a theoretical approach was executed to provide supplementary support on the origin of luminescence. (3) TPEF and two-photon absorption cross section of complex 2 in single crystal are measured viaconfocal microscope and Z-scan method, respectively. The results show promising TPA value that is competitive or even larger than many known fluorophores. It should be noted that silver(i) complexes with high emission intensity and notable two-photon absorption properties are obscure, especially in room temperature solid state. The results should exemplify a model to investigate the photophysical properties of relevant silver complexes, especially their non-linear optical properties and the associated applications.

Crystallographic data collection and refinement

The chosen crystals were mounted on glass capillaries. Data collection were carried out on a NONIUS KappaCCD diffractometer of complexes 1, 3, 4 and a BRUKER SMART APEX CCD diffractometer for complex 2, respectively, with Mo radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at 150(2) K. After data collection, the frames were integrated and absorption corrections were applied. The initial crystal structure was solved by direct method. The structure solution was expanded through successive least-squares cycles, and the final solution was determined. All of non-hydrogen atoms are refined anisotropically. Hydrogen atoms attached to the carbon and nitrogen were fixed at calculated positions and refined using a riding mode. Crystal data, data collection, and refinement parameters are summarized in Table 1.

Spectral measurement

UV-vis diffusive reflectance spectroscopyspectra of crystal 1–4 were obtained with a HITACHI U-3900H spectrophotometer equipped with an integrating sphere accessory (Al2O3 was used as a reference). In order to investigate the possible differences in photoluminescence among 1–4 due to the variation of topologies, 1–4 were measured with a confocal mode of a MonoVista confocal Raman microscope system (Princeton Instruments/Acton). Microscope (Olympus BX51) is with an upright-style frame in which all optics are modified to allow the passage of deep UV to infrared light. Simultaneously, a video camera (QImage 5.0 RTV) was used to capture images. In this approach, a 325 nm laser line (He–Cd laser, KIMMON IK3301R-G) was used as an excitation source throughout the measurement. A set of neutral density filters is then placed in the beam path to attenuate the laser power. The photoluminescence was separated from the scattering light of excitation pulse by an edge filter with a cut-off wavelength of 325 nm (333AELP, Omega Optical). Subsequently, the luminescence was collected by an optical assembly to the entrance slit of a polychromator (blazed at 500 nm), which is coupled with a sensitive charge coupled detector (CCD, Princeton Instruments, PI-MAX). The CCD was operated in a shutter mode and the measurements were performed with 100 ms exposure time. All spectra were accumulated over an average 1 scan. Quantum yields were measured using an absolute PL quantum yield measurement system (Hamamatsu Photonics C9920-02). Moreover, one-photonexcitation spectra of complex 2 were recorded on an Edinburgh (FLS920) fluorimeter.

Measurement of two-photon excitation fluorescence (TPEF) and fluorescence lifetime

Crystals 1–4 were placed on 0.14 mm thick glass coverslips, imaged on an inverted confocal microscope (Zeiss LSM 710) with 63X oil-immersion lens (Zeiss, 1.4 NA), and a femtosecond laser source (Mai-Tai DeepSee, Spectra-Physics), with a pulse duration <100 fs and a pulse repetition rate of 80 MHz. The excitation wavelength was tuned to 800–1050 nm (4.1% of 30 mW), passed through the LSM710microscope with HFT 650 dichroic (Carl Zeiss, Inc.) and focused on the crystals. Note that one-photon excitation emission was performed using an diode laser operating at 405 nm and also coupled to the microscope. In order to record two-photonexcitation spectra, the input power of the laser was kept constant and the wavelength of the laser was tuned between 750 and 900 nm at 10 nm intervals while emission was integrated over the band of 400–700 nm. The resulting excitation spectra subsequently were corrected for laser power. Lifetimes of the crystals 1–4 were obtained using a multi-spectral FLIM (Fluorescence life-time Imaging Microscopy) system, rendering a temporal resolution of ∼50 ps. A 16 channel, multi-wavelength, single photon counting detector (Simple-Tau 150, Becker & Hickl GmbH) was employed to resolve the lifetime of the fluorescence. Lifetime data at the desired wavelength was calculated by the software SPCImage, and the decay matrix was generated accordingly.

Measurement of the TPA cross section by an open aperture Z-scan method

The TPA cross section of complex 2 at 800 nm was measured by an open aperture Z-scan experiments.26 A mode-locked Ti:sapphire laser (Tsunami, Spectra Physics) produced single Gaussian pulse (800 nm), which was coupled to a regenerative amplifier that generated approximately 180 fs and 1 mJ pulse (760–840 nm, 1 KHz, Spitfire, Spectra Physics). To circumvent the buildup of excited-state populations, we further reduced the output of our amplifier to 10 Hz. After an appropriate attenuation, the pulse energy was reduced to 0.5–0.75 μJ and used to excite the solid crystal (2.16 M) through an f = 30 cm lens with a beam diameter of 5.0 × 10−3 cm.
