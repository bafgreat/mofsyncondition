Introduction

In recent years, tremendous interest has been paid to the design and synthesis of coordination polymers (CPs), stemming from not only their fascinating nature of molecular connectivities and topologies, but also their potential applications as functional materials in the areas of gas adsorption,1 catalysis,2 drug delivery,3 molecular magnetism4 and photoluminescent properties.5 Therein, for the practical application of CPs as solid-state functional materials in single-crystal to single-crystal transformations6 and separation,7 and especially in drug delivery and heterogeneous catalysis,8 the advantageous factor of a high level of solvent resistance properties, is very essential to be considered. Though a large number of metal–organic coordination polymers have been prepared, it is still a challenge to search for new approaches to attain applied CPs with good solvent resistance properties and predictable functional properties. This is due to many factors, such as the coordination geometry of the central metal ions, the connective modes of the organic ligands, the hydrogen bonding interaction, aperture diameters and the geometric matching of the metal ions with the ligands, which have great influences on the frameworks of the resulting structures and also the interaction of solvent and materials.9 It has been proposed that the organic ligands, which can meet the coordination request of metal ions with the slightest bend and twist, play important roles in determining the stability of the coordination frameworks and topologies of the CPs.10 In addition, the solvent resistance properties of the CPs can also be effectively improved and modulated by changing the property and aperture diameter of the cavities.11 The urge to synthesize CP-based materials with both a high level of solvent resistance and predictable functional properties has driven chemists to design and explore new ligands or mixed-ligands capable of forming extended networks upon complexation with transition metals.

In our continuing quest to develop new strategies necessary for the rational assembly of CPs with high solvent stabilities and intriguing structural topologies,10d–g a rigid V-shaped bicarboxylate ligand featuring two benzoic acid groups connected by a sulfonyl group, named 4,4′-sulfonyldibenzoic acid (H2sdba), captured our attention.12 The ligand H2sdba, as a multidentate O-donor ligand with versatile coordination conformations and a strong coordination ability, can adopt a variety of coordination modes to conform to the requirements of the coordination geometries of the metal centers and, thus, result in stable diverse multidimensional architectures. It also could exhibit regular orientations with the two benzoic acid groups bent at ca. 90° arising from the distorted tetrahedral geometry of SO2C2.12 The conformation is in favour of improving the stability of the formed frameworks because it can best meet the geometric requirement of the metal centers.10a,13 In addition, the sulfonyl group is a fascinating functional group because it tends to form hydrogen bonds among themselves and interact negligibly with guest molecules as well as the counter anions to further stabilize the framework. Furthermore, it was anticipated that the introduction of small organic N-donor ligands (Htrz, Hatz, and Hti) into the CPs based on H2sdba could further improve the solvent resistance properties and tune the structural topologies. These small organic molecules could adopt flexible bidentate chelating or bridging modes to link the center metal ions and increase the number of connected nodes. Furthermore, they could also form potential hydrogen bonds and decrease the aperture diameters and the intermolecular forces between the solvent and the crystals, consequentially, improving the solvent resistance properties of the final compounds.

Herein, as an extension of this methodology, seven new CPs based on H2sdba and ancillary ligands (Htrz, Hatz, and Hti) (Scheme 1), namely {[Co3(sdba)3·2H2O·DMF]·2DMF}n (1), {[Co2(sdba)·(μ3-OH)·(trz)·H2O]·H2O}n (2), {[Co2(sdba)·(μ3-OH)·(atz)·H2O]·H2O}n (3), {[Co(sdba)·(ti)2·H2O]·1.5H2O}n (4), {[Cu(sdba)·H2O]·1.5H2O}n (5), {[Cu2(sdba)·(μ3-OH)·(trz)·H2O]·H2O}n (6) and {[Zn2(sdba)·(atz)2]·0.5H2O}n (7), have been synthesized under solvothermal conditions. Their structures are characterized by IR spectroscopy, elemental analysis, X-ray crystallography, and X-ray powder diffraction. Their solvent resistance properties in boiling water, methanol, ethanol, acetonitrile, DMF, dilute acid and base solutions have been discussed. In addition, the luminescent properties of complexes 2, 6 and 7 were discussed, and the thermal stabilities of complexes 2 and 7 were also investigated.

Experimental section

Materials and physical measurements

All reagents and solvents were commercially available and used as received without further purification. The FT-IR spectra were recorded on a Bruker-ALPHA spectrophotometer with KBr pellets in the 400–4000 cm−1 region. Elemental analyses (C, H, and N) were carried out on a FLASH EA 1112 elemental analyzer. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns were recorded using Cu Kα1 radiation on a PANalytical X'Pert PRO diffractometer. Thermal analyses were performed on a Netzsch STA 449C thermal analyzer at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 in air. The luminescence spectra for the powdered solid samples were measured at room temperature on a Hitachi 850 fluorescence spectrophotometer. The excitation slit and emission slit were both 2.0 nm.

Crystal data collection and refinement

The data of 1–7 were collected on a Rigaku Saturn 724 CCD diffractomer (Mo-Kα, λ = 0.71073 Å) at a temperature of 20 ± 1 °C. The absorption corrections were applied by using a multi-scan program. The data were corrected for Lorentz and polarization effects. The structures were solved by direct methods and refined with a full-matrix least-squares technique based on F2 with the SHELXL-97 crystallographic software package.14 The hydrogen atoms were placed at calculated positions and refined as riding atoms with isotropic displacement parameters. The distribution of peaks in the channels of 1–7 were chemically featureless to refine using conventional discrete-atom models. To resolve these issues, the contributions of the electron density by the remaining DMF and water molecules in 1 and the water molecules in 2–7 were removed by the SQUEEZE routine in PLATON.15 The final formulas were determined by combing the single-crystal structures, elemental microanalyses, TGA and the electron count of the SQUEEZE results. Selected bond lengths and bond angles for 1–7 and parts of the complexes under specific conditions are listed in Table S1 (ESI†), respectively. The crystallographic data for 1–7 have been deposited at the Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre with CCDC reference numbers 960060–960066.

Results and discussion

The crystal structure of 1

Single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that complex 1, which crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/c, is a 3D coordination framework. Its asymmetric unit contains three Co(ii) ions, three sdba2− groups, one DMF molecule and two coordinated water molecules. As illustrated in Fig. 1a, there are three unique Co(ii) centers with different coordination environments. The Co1 ions possess a typical six-coordinated octahedral coordination geometry, which is coordinated by six oxygen atoms from six sdba2− ligands. Co2 is coordinated by four oxygen atoms from three sdba2− ligands, one oxygen atom from a DMF molecule and one oxygen atom from a coordinated water molecule, showing a distorted octahedral arrangement. While Co3 has a distorted tetrahedral coordination geometry, which is coordinated by three oxygen atoms from three sdba2− ligands and one oxygen atom from a coordinated water molecule. The distances of Co–O are 1.938(5)–2.294(12) Å, which are within the normal range.16

All the carboxyl groups from the H2sdba ligands are deprotonated and adopt two different coordination modes. One carboxyl group takes a bismonodetate coordination mode to link two Co centers (Co1 and Co3) while the other carboxyl group adopts a cheating/bridging bidentate coordination mode to bridge two Co centers (Co1 and Co2) (Fig. 1b). The coordination modes are different from that of [Zn3(μ-OH)2(sdba)2]n·2nH2O, in which the H2sdba ligands adopt a (κ1-κ1)-(κ1-κ1)-μ4 coordination mode with the torsional angle between the two phenyl rings being 81.441°.17 Three Co atoms are joined by three sdba2− ligands to form a trinuclear Co(ii) cluster as a secondary building unit (SBU) with a Co2⋯Co1⋯Co3 distance of 3.492 and 3.658 Å , respectively. These SBUs can be further extended by the carboxylate groups of the sdba2− ligands to give a 2D zigzag sheet, in which the two benzene rings bend at ca. 103.2(4)–103.8(3)° (C5–S1–C8, C19–S2–C22, and C36–S3–C39) (Fig. S1†). Interestingly, the sheets are connected in an alternating fashion by Co1 atoms to generate a 3D framework without an obvious cavity (Fig. 1c). From a topological point of view, if the trinuclear Co(ii) cluster is regarded as a connected node and the ligands are considered as linkers, the structure of 1 can be described as a 6-connected pcu topology with the point symbol of (412·63) (Fig. 1d).

The crystal structure of 2

Compared with 1, the structure of 2 changed enormously when importing Htrz. Compound 2 crystallizes in the tetragonal space group I4(1)/a and contains an interesting 3D “onion” polymeric architecture. The asymmetric unit consists of two Co(ii), one coordinated sdba2− ligand, one deprotonated trz− group, one coordinated water molecule and one μ3-OH group, as shown in Fig. 2a. The central Co1 ion has a slightly distorted trigonal bipyramid coordination geometry with two oxygen atoms (O4A and O8) from two sdba2− ligands, two oxygen atoms (O1 and O1A) from two μ3-OH groups and one nitrogen atom (N3) from one deprotonated trz− group. Co2 ion is six-coordinated by two oxygen atoms (O3 and O7) from two sdba2− ligands, one oxygen (O2) from a coordinated water molecule, one oxygen atom (O1) from μ3-OH and two nitrogen atoms (N1 and N2) from two deprotonated trz− groups. The Co–O bond distances vary in the range of 2.010(3)–2.196(3) Å, and the Co–N bond distances vary in the range of 2.018(3)–2.089(3) Å.

As shown in Fig. 2b, two pairs of symmetry-related Co(ii) atoms (Co1, Co1A; Co2, Co2A) are bridged together by two μ3-hydroxides to form a [Co4(μ3-OH)2] cluster (Co⋯Co 3.167–3.631 Å, Co–O–Co 79.20–117.59 Å) as a tetranuclear Co(ii) SBU. These SBUs can be further extended by the triazole groups and carboxylate groups of the symmetrical sdba2− ligands. The coordination mode of the ligand H2sdba was different from that found in 1. All of the carboxyl groups from H2sdba are deprotonated and adopt a bismonodentate coordination mode to bridge Co1 and Co2 of the tetracobalt SBU with the distance of 3.630 Å for Co1⋯Co2, generating a 3D framework (Fig. S2†). The two benzene rings bend at ca. 103.1(2)° (C7–S1–C10). Moreover, each trz− unit acts as a bridging linker and connect with the neighboring tetracobalt clusters to further stabilize the 3D framework (Fig. 2c). Compound 2 possesses 1D channels (void = 18.6%) along the c axis and all of the trz− groups point to the square-shaped channels, with the maximum and minimum aperture diameters of 3.8 and 2.9 Å, respectively.

From a topological point of view, if the trinuclear Co(ii) cluster is regarded as a connected node, this tetracobalt SBU can be defined as a uniondal 8-connected node, and the carboxylate acts as 2-connected linkers and triazole acts as 2-connected linkers. The topological analysis shows that the Schläfli symbol for this network is {36·410·56·66} named whc1 topology (Fig. 2d). This topology has been theoretically expected but not yet observed prior to this work.

The crystal structure of 3

In consideration of the fact that Hatz is similar with Htrz and contains a specific –NH2 group to form a fascinating hydrogen bond, complex 3 was obtained when Hatz was utilized in place of Htrz under appropriate reaction conditions. The single crystal X-ray analysis shows that complex 3 is isostructural with 2. Similar to 2, the two pairs of Co(ii) atoms are linked together by two μ3-hydroxides to construct a [Co4(μ3-OH)2] cluster as a tetranuclear Co(ii) SBU. The SBUs are bridged by atz− groups and H2sdba with the two benzene rings bent at ca. 103.1(5)° (C5–S1–C8) to form a 3D “onion” framework. (Fig. S3†). Compound 3 possesses 1D channels along the c axis. All of the atz− groups point to the channel and the hydrogen atoms of the amino groups are exposed on the pore surface, which further decrease the aperture diameters. As expected, the hydrogen bonding interactions [N4⋯O8 = 2.999(12) and N4⋯N3 = 3.129(12)] were formed by the –NH2 group, which further stabilizes the framework (Table S2†).

The crystal structure of 4

In contrast with 1, 2 and 3, the single crystal X-ray analysis shows that complex 4 crystallizes in the monoclinic crystal system space group C2/c, indicating 1D helix chains when the bidentate chelating ligand Hti is imported. The asymmetric unit contains one Co(ii) cation, one H2sdba ligand, one Hti and one coordinated water molecule (Fig. 3).

The Co(ii) is surrounded by three oxygen atoms (O1, O2 and O6) from two sdba2− groups, two nitrogen atoms (N1 and N3) from Hti and one oxygen atom (O7) from a water molecule, giving a distorted octahedral coordination geometry (Fig. 3a). The carboxylate groups of the ligands connect the adjacent Co(ii) cations to form a single-strand helix chain with the Co⋯Co distance of 13.1562(36) Å (Fig. 3b). The two benzene rings bend at ca. 104.9(2)° (C5–S1–C8). In addition, the adjacent 1D helix chains are further bridged by hydrogen bonding interactions [O7⋯O5 = 2.836(5) Å] (Table S2†) between the oxygen atoms of the coordination water molecule and the adjacent uncoordination oxygen atoms from the ligands to give a two-dimensional (2D) supramolecular framework (Fig. 3c). PLATON analysis gives the free void volume ratio of 11.5% in compound 4.

The crystal structure of 5

Complex 5 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/m and exhibits 1D ∞-like chains. The asymmetric unit contains one Cu(ii), one sdba2− and one coordinated water molecule. The Cu(ii) atom is coordinated by four oxygen atoms from four sdba2− [Cu–O1 = 1.961(3) and Cu–O2 = 1.959(3) Å] and one oxygen atom from one coordinated water molecule [Cu–O4 = 2.184(51) Å], giving a distorted square pyramidal coordination geometry, where the O4 atom locates at the top of the square pyramid and the four carboxylate oxygen atoms frame the bottom (Fig. S4†). The fully deprotonated H2sdba ligand indicates a bidentate coordination mode and the pairs of the Cu(ii) cations are linked by two symmetrical sdba2− ligands to form a dinuclear Cu(ii) “paddlewheel” type SBU with a Cu⋯Cu space of 2.6244(15) Å. These SBUs are further crossed through the second terminal carboxylate group of the symmetrical sdba2 ligands into 1D chains (Fig. 4a). Furthermore, there exist weak π⋯π stacking interactions (centroid-to-centroid distance of 3.8549(7) Å) among adjacent aromatic cycles of the sdba2− ligands, which give a two-dimensional (2D) supramolecular framework (Fig. 4b). PLATON analysis gives the free void volume ratio of 18.3% in compound 5.

The crystal structure of 6

Crystal structure determination reveals that 6 crystallizes in the tetragonal space group I4(1)/a and is isostructural with 2. Two pairs of symmetry neighbored Cu(ii) atoms are connected together by two μ3-hydroxides to develop a [Cu4(μ3-OH)2] cluster as a tetranuclear Cu(ii) SBU. Each tetranuclear Cu(ii) SBU is joined by four completely deprotonated H2sdba ligands and trz− units to form a 3D framework. Compound 6 possesses 1D channels (void = 18.6%) along the c axis and all of the trz− groups point to the square-shaped channels, with the maximum and minimum aperture diameters of 3.5 and 2.8 Å, respectively.

The crystal structure of 7

Complex 7 crystallizes in the orthorhombic space group Pbcn and exhibits a 3D coordination framework consisting of Zn-triazolate layers and dicarboxylate pillars. Its asymmetric unit contains two independent Zn(ii) ions, one sdba2− ligand and two atz− ligands (Fig. 5a). The Zn1 atom is four-coordinated by three nitrogen atoms (N1, N6 and N7) from three atz− ligands and one carboxylate oxygen atom (O2) from one sdba2− ligand, conforming a distorted tetrahedrally geometry. The Zn2 atom is also tetrahedrally coordinated by three nitrogen atoms (N4, N5 and N8) from three atz− and one carboxylate oxygen atom (O6) from one sdba2− ligand. The Zn–N bond length varies from 1.985 to 2.022 Å, and the Zn–O bond lengths are 1.931 and 1.954 Å, which are in the normal ranges in both cases.18 As shown in Fig. 5b, the Zn(ii) cations are linked through the atz− ligands to form a 2D layer structure. Such layers are further bridged by bent dicarboxylate pillars, in which the two carboxylate groups adopt a monodentate coordination mode, leading to the formation of a 3D framework (Fig. 5c). In order to better understand the whole structure, we can simplify the intricate structure as node-and-connecting nets. Hence, from the viewpoint of structural topology, each Zn(ii) unit can be viewed as a 4-connected node, the sdba2− ligand acts as a linear linker and each Htaz ligand can be reduced to a 3-connected node. So the whole framework possesses a new tetranodal 3,3,4,4-c net with the point symbol {46·82·1012}{46·83·10}{46·8}{48·2} named whc2 topology. (Fig. 5d). In addition, the 3D framework is further stabilized by hydrogen bonds (N2⋯O1 = 2.834(6), N6⋯O2 = 2.984(6), N6⋯O6 = 2.964(6), N2⋯O2 = 3.279(6) ), which are formed by the –NH2 group and the oxygen atoms of the carboxylate group (Table S2†). PLATON analysis gives the free void volume ratio of 18.2% in compound 7.

Analyses of the solvent resistance property

The solvent resistance property of the complexes 1–7 was examined by suspending samples in boiling water, methanol, ethanol, acetonitrile, DMF, dilute acid and base for 48 h, which imitated the typical industrial chemical processes. During this process, samples were regularly observed under an optical microscope.

It was found that the crystals of complex 2 could keep their original shapes in boiling organic solvents conditions for more than 48 h and dissolved in boiling water (Table 2). Complex 5 dissolved in boiling methanol, ethanol, and DMF and could just keep the original shapes under boiling water and acetonitrile for 48 h. Single crystal X-ray diffraction showed the unit cell parameters of complexes 2 and 5 after treatment basically did not change (Table S3†). Complexes 2 and 5 were directly assembled by metal ion with H2sdba. The solvent resistance property of 2 in boiling organic solvents can be ascribed to the rigidity of the linker, the dense structure, and its stable 3D framework without an obvious cavity. In addition, the ligand is a rigid V-shaped linker with different coordination modes to meet with the requirements of the coordination geometries of the center metal ions and provides a “pi-pocket” formed by two phenyl rings and rules the stability of the final structures.19 As with 5, its macrocyclic subunits are liable to be attacked by solvent molecules (Fig. 6).

Complexes 2–4 were prepared by the self-assembly of the Co(ii) ion and H2sdba, with Htrz, Hatz, and Hti, respectively. The structures of 2 and 3 showed a 8-connected 3D metal–organic framework with a new {36·410·56·66} topology, due to the bridging effect of the auxiliary ligands. Complex 4 showed 1D helix chains. Compared with 1, the crystals of complexes 2–4 could keep their original shapes under boiling methanol, ethanol, acetonitrile, DMF and water for more than 4 days. The single X-ray diffraction studies show that their unit cell parameters basically did not change (Table S4†). That is to say, complexes 2–4 have more excellent boiling water and organic solvents resistance than that of 1. This may be due to the introduction of small organic N-donor ligands (Htrz, Hatz, and Hti) into the framework, which could further improve the skeleton stability. The small organic N-donor ligands (Htrz, Hatz, and Hti) could adopt bridging and bidentate chelating modes to link the center metal ions, increase the coordination number of the center metal ions, and tune structural topologies. More importantly, the organic N-donor ligands point to the channels of CPs, occupy the cavity, form hydrogen bonds and decrease the aperture diameters, which will reduce the interaction between the solvent and the crystals and prevent the dissolution of the frameworks.

The same phenomenon had occurred in complexes 5 and 6. With the introduction of the auxiliary ligand Htrz, complex 6 exhibits a 3D “onion” framework and has a remarkable chemical resistance to boiling water and organic solvents. Complex 7 was synthesized by the self-assembly of the Zn(ii) ion, H2sdba and Hatz. It showed a 3D coordination framework consisting of Zn-triazolate layers and dicarboxylate pillars and indicated a good solvent resistance property. In addition, it was found that 2–4, 6 and 7 could keep their original shapes for several minutes when the samples were immersed in a 0.5 M hydrochloric acid solution or aqueous sodium hydroxide, but 1 and 5 were soluble immediately when the samples were immersed in the acid/base solution.

To confirm whether the crystal structures remained unchanged after treatment, the XRD power patterns for compounds 2 and 7 have been collected on a PANalytical X'prt Pro diffractometer using graphite-monochromated Cu Kα radiation in the angular range 2θ = 5–50°. The powder patterns of compounds 2 and 7 matched with the ones calculated from their single crystal structure data, which indicated that their crystals could retain their crystallinity in boiling water, methanol, ethanol, acetonitrile and DMF (Fig. 7).

Analyses of the thermal stability

The thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) experiments of 2 and 7 were carried out (Fig. S5†). The curve of compound 2 shows a weight loss of 6.23% in the 75–320 °C range which can be attributed to the departure of lattice water molecules (calculated 6.59%). After that, an additional weight loss of 60.22% up to 533 °C can be attributed to the gradual release of the coordinated H2sdba and Htrz ligands. The TG curve of 7 exhibits that it is stable up to 421.3 °C. The thermal stability of 7 is mainly due to the stable 3D coordination framework consisting of Zn-triazolate layers and dicarboxylate pillars. And then, the following weight loss may be attributed to the gradual release of the H2sdba and Htrz ligands ended at 608 °C with a retention of weight of 12.84% (calculated 13.28%), corresponding to ZnO.

Photoluminescence properties

The fluorescent properties of complexes 2, 6 and 7, together with the free H2sdba ligand and the co-ligands of Htrz and Hatz, were investigated at ambient temperature, as depicted in Fig. S6.† The free H2sdba ligand displays intense emission bands at 329 nm (λex = 277 nm), which are assigned to the π*→n or π*→π transition.20 As strong electron withdrawing groups, triazole and its derivatives almost have no significant contribution to the fluorescent emission.21 Thus, the emission of the coordination polymer is mainly associated with the presence of the H2sdba ligands.

Complexes 2 and 6 show no detectable fluorescence due to the fact that the Co(ii)/Cu(ii) ion provides an efficient pathway for quenching the fluorescence of the ligand because of the fluorescence quenching nature of paramagnetic Co(ii)/Cu(ii).22 While complex 7 exhibits an emission maxima at 404 nm (λex = 385), which is red-shifted 75 nm as compared with the free H2sdba ligand. This fluorescence behavior could be due to the metal ion, carboxylate ligand and encapsulated lattice molecule. However, the role of the structural complexity and framework robustness can not be ignored. Considering the above factors, it is neither the metal-to-ligand charge transfer (MLCT) nor the ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT), but it may be assigned to a change in the energy levels (HOMO–LUMO) of the carboxylate-based ligands. The profile of the emission bands suggests the possibility of intraligand (π→π*) transitions.23

Conclusions

In summary, we have successfully synthesized and characterized seven new CPs by the self-assembly of H2sdba, auxiliary ligands (Htrz, Hatz, and Hti), and Co(ii), Cu(ii), Zn(ii) cations under solvothermal conditions. The title complexes display versatile coordination features with 1D, 2D and 3D frameworks. Therein, with the introduction of rigid organic molecules (Htrz, Hatz, and Hti) as auxiliary ligands, complexes 2–3, 6 and 7 exhibit interesting 3D frameworks with the first reported topologies whc1 and whc2. The investigation of the solvent resistance properties had demonstrated that complexes 2–4, 6 and 7 have more excellent boiling water and organic solvents resistance than that of 1 and 5. Their excellent solvent resistance properties can be ascribed to the rigidity of the linker, the dense structure and the stable frameworks. In addition, the auxiliary ligands could adopt bridging and bidentate chelating modes to link the center metal ions, decrease the aperture diameters and form hydrogen bond to reduce the interaction between the solvent and crystal. Herein we have shown that a rational approach can be taken toward the construction of CPs with good solvent resistance properties and intriguing structural topologies. Actually, there is more work to be done to clarify the intermolecular forces between the solvent and the crystal. Further investigations about the solvent resistance properties of metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) are currently underway.
