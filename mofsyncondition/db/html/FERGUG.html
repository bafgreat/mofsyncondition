Introduction

As a new class of porous materials, namely, metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have been widely explored1 due to their novel structures, interesting properties, and tuneable and wide-ranging applications, such as gas storage,2 sensing,3 molecular magnets,4 and catalysis.5 Furthermore, luminescence6 has been observed as another interesting property due to the multifaceted nature of the structures of MOFs.7 Two principles can be distinguished in this regard: (a) processes triggered by an organic chromophore being responsible for both the excitation and the emission process,8 and (b) incorporation of metal ions as luminescence centres.9 Thus, luminescent properties can be achieved by modifying either the organic linkers or the metal ions as nodes.

In general, tuning of MOFs can be accomplished by modifying the linkers, and polycarboxylato ligands have often been used as mono-, bi- or multidentate ligands for coordination of transition metals, leading to the formation of moderately robust MOFs.10 Among these multicarboxylato ligands, benzene-1,4-dicarboxylate (BDC) and biphenyl-4,4′-dicarboxylate (BPDC) exhibit versatile bridging modes and have been employed as organic linkers in constructing MOFs with mono-, di- or polynuclear transition metal complexes as the secondary building unit (SBU) and different dimensionalities,11 and in some cases porous coordination frameworks and coordination polymers were obtained. [Zn4O(BDC)3] (MOF-5)12 and [Zn4O(BPDC)3] (IRMOF-9 (interpenetrated network) and IRMOF-10 (non-interpenetrated network))13 reported by Yaghi and co-workers are well-known examples of the bridging capabilities of BDC and BPDC. With the isomeric biphenyl-2,2′-dicarboxylate, the dianion of diphenic acid (DPA), helical chains are formed due to steric hindrance of the two carboxylato groups and non-coplanar phenyl rings.14

Various architectures are observed in Zn-based MOFs with BDC and BPDC as linkers and some also contain co-ligands such as 4,4′-bipyridine or 1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)ethane, containing metal–oxygen nodes15 like Zn4O(CO2)6 as an octahedral SBU,12,13,16 or other SBUs such as Zn-based paddle wheels,17 chains18 or polynuclear clusters.19

Similarly, Cd-based MOFs with dicarboxylato ligands as linkers exhibit structural diversity, and diverse architectures and functions.20 Burrows et al.20g observed that the structural features of Cd–BDC MOFs are highly dependent on the synthetic conditions, i.e., temperature, pressure and solvent, yielding for example [{Cd3(BPDC)3(DMF)}·5DMF·18H2O]n, in which a three-dimensional network with rare etb topology is generated from rod-shaped CdII carboxylate SBUs.20h In 2012, a Cd-based MOF [(Me2NH2)6(Cd3L4)2(CdL2)·18H2O·7Me2NH·5DMF]n (L = 4,4′-(CO2)2-2,2′-(OMe)2-1,1′-(C6H3)2) with two-fold hetero-interpenetrated nets, consisting of a 3D diamond network (mononuclear Cd(CO2)4 node) and a 3D CsCl framework (trinuclear Cd3(CO2)8 node), has been reported.21

Pb-based MOFs with BDC or BPDC as linkers have also been reported.22,23 The coordination chemistry of PbII is rather versatile, with varying coordination numbers, irregular geometries and a cooperative effect of the lone pair of electrons.24 Thus, the Pb–BDC MOFs form either infinite undulated 2D layers of [Pb(CO2)2]n22a,b or [Pb–O–C]n rods, which are constructed from 8-coordinated PbIIcations, with each cation being coordinated by the carboxyl groups of four BDC linkers generating 1D rhombic channels.22c The Pb–BPDC MOF is based on PbO7 polyhedra forming a 3D pillared-layer network.23

MOFs based on BDC or BPDC and other transition metals such as CuII,25 CoII,26 MnII,27 ZrIV,28 InIII,29 YIII,30 the rare earth metal GdIII,31 and even main group metals (AlIII,32 LiI,33 MgII)34 have been reported.35 Related linkers such as biphenyl-3,3′-dimethoxy-4,4′-dicarboxylate have also been employed in the synthesis of MOFs with several transition metal ions such as CdII, ZnII, CuII, CoII, MnII and lanthanide ions (EuIII, GdIII and DyIII).36 We now report the synthesis of three MOFs based on zinc(ii) (1), cadmium(ii) (2) or lead(ii) (3) and the functionalised biphenyl linker biphenyl-2,2′-dimethoxy-4,4′-dicarboxylate, [4,4′-(CO2)2-2,2′-(OMe)2-1,1′-(C6H3)2] (L). According to DTA–TG, 1–3 are thermally stable up to 420, 360 and 380 °C, respectively. Compounds 1–3 show bluish emissions, which could be assigned to S1,2 → S0 transitions of the aromatic units by comparison with the luminescence properties of the pure ligands.

Results and discussion

Chiral MOFs can be obtained by using chiral linkers. A modified BPDC, namely, axially chiral racemic biphenyl-2,2′-dimethoxy-4,4′-dicarboxylic acid (LH2),21,37 was synthesised from commercially available 2-amino-5-methylphenol (see ESI‡) and employed as the organic linker in the solvothermal synthesis of Zn-, Cd- and Pb-based MOFs (Scheme 1).

The precursor for the preparation of LH2, 4,4′-Me2-2,2′-(OMe)2-1,1′-(C6H3)2, was structurally characterised (see ESI‡). Density functional calculations showed that the methoxyl groups in LH2 are not sufficiently sterically demanding to hinder rotation around the central C–C bond that connects the two phenyl groups (see ESI‡ for a detailed discussion of the theoretical results). Related biphenyl-4,4′-dicarboxylates with alkoxyl or hydroxyl substituents in the 2,2′-positions have been reported37 and employed as linkers in MOFs.21,38 In contrast, related linkers with additional substituents in the 6,6′-positions, e.g., methyl groups, form stable axially chiral molecules.39 Homochiral (S)-biphenyl-2,2′-dihydroxy-6,6′-dimethyl-4,4′-dicarboxylate was employed as a linker in the synthesis of chiral MOFs and tested in asymmetric catalytic reactions after post-synthesis modification by replacement of the hydroxyl protons on the organic linkers with zinc(ii) and titanium(iv).39

As a racemic mixture of biphenyl-2,2′-dimethoxy-4,4′-dicarboxylic acid (LH2) was employed, the resulting frameworks are also racemic. [{(Zn4O)(L)3(H2O)}·4.5DMF]n (1) has the same topology as the IRMOF series described by Yaghi et al.12,13 and Burrows et al.,16c but in 1, one water molecule is additionally coordinated at one zinc(ii) cation of the Zn4O moiety. Two interpenetrated Cd–L networks of different topology are observed in [{(Cd3L4)2(CdL2)}·8DMF·8Me2NH2·2Cl]n (2), identical to the recently published [(Me2NH2)6(Cd3L4)2(CdL2)·18H2O·7Me2NH·5DMF]n (with L = 4,4′-(CO2)2-2,2′-(OMe)2-1,1′-(C6H3)2),21 but with different solvent molecules as well as cations and anions included in the pores of 2. The Pb2+-based MOF, [{(Pb6O2)L4}·2EtOH]n (3), exhibits an unprecedented novel Pb6O2(O2C)4 cluster as the SBU.

Solid-state structures

[{(Zn4O)(L)3(H2O)}·4.5DMF]n (1)

Compound 1 crystallises in the rhombohedral space group R with six molecules in the unit cell. The asymmetric unit and SBU are shown in Fig. 1 and Table 1 contains selected bond lengths and angles.

The SBU consists of a Zn4O(O2C)6(OH2) unit that is located on a crystallographic threefold axis passing through O(8), Zn(1) and O(7) (Fig. 1). Notably, one of the zinc cations, Zn(1), is additionally coordinated by one water molecule (O(8)) in contrast to a Zn-based MOF reported by Burrows et al.,16c which has two coordinated water molecules, resulting in a distorted trigonal–bipyramidal geometry around Zn(1) (Table 1) with (O(1)–Zn(1)–O(8)) and (O(1)–Zn(1)–O(7)) bond angles of 81.7(2) and 98.3(2)°, respectively. A topology is observed in which an oxide-centred Zn4O tetrahedron (Zn(1)–O(7)–Zn(2)) is edge-bridged by six carboxylates to give an octahedron-shaped secondary building unit. These extend into a three-dimensional primitive porous cubic network (Fig. 2) (pcu-type net, point (Schläfli) symbol {412.63}),40 similar to MOF-512 and IRMOF-10,13 but with a different pore size (10 × 10 Å2 in 1). The presence of the OMe group in the 2,2′-position results in different possible rotamers and thus in a highly disordered arrangement. The dihedral angle between two aromatic rings ranges from 86.1(6) to 86.3(6)°.

Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) and DTA–TG showed 1 to be thermally stable up to 420 °C even after removal of the incorporated solvent molecules (DMF)41 (see ESI‡). At 468 °C decomposition to ZnO occurred.42

The shape of the nitrogen sorption isotherm at 77 K of 1 obtained under solvothermal conditions corresponds to type I of the IUPAC adsorption isotherm classification43 for microporous materials (see Fig. S13 in ESI‡). By using the t-plot method,44 a micropore volume of 0.28 cm3 g−1 and a corresponding specific micropore surface area of 689 m2 g−1 were determined. An average pore diameter of 0.52 nm was obtained by using the model of Horvath and Kawazoe.45 This is, however, somewhat smaller than the crystallographic pore diameter of 1.0 nm (see Fig. 2). The total specific pore volume (from the adsorption branch at P/P0 = 0.99) of 0.32 cm3 g−1 is only slightly higher than the specific micropore volume. This additional pore volume is probably due to the interstitial space between the crystallites. The hysteresis for P/P0 > 0.42 corresponds to a cavitation effect. Limited desorption of nitrogen through narrower pores at the outer crystallite surface is a possible reason.

[{(Cd3L4)2(CdL2)}·8DMF·8Me2NH2·2Cl]n (2)

The Cd-based MOF 2 obtained here (Scheme 1) has a very similar structure to the recently reported compound [(Me2NH2)6(Cd3L4)2(CdL2)·18H2O·7Me2NH·5DMF]n with L = 4,4′-(CO2)2-2,2′-(OMe)2-1,1′-(C6H3)2, which has two-fold hetero-interpenetrated nets consisting of a 3D diamond network (mononuclear Cd(CO2)4 node) and a 3D CsCl framework (trinuclear Cd3(CO2)8 node).21 Here, the pores contain the counterions, i.e. dimethylammonium cations, as well as H2O, Me2NH, and DMF molecules, while in 2 the pores are filled with counterions (Me2NH2+), DMF, as well as 2 (Me2NH2)Cl (Fig. 3). Thus, the unit cell contains no residual solvent-accessible void. The dimethylammonium cations exhibit intermolecular hydrogen bonds with the organic linkers (O(2), O(12)) and DMF molecules (N(1)–H(1A)⋯O(102) 173.5°, d(H⋯A) 1.93 Å, d(D⋯A) 2.850(2) Å; N(1)–H(1B)⋯O(2) 134.3°, d(H⋯A) 2.20 Å, d(D⋯A) 2.916(2) Å; Fig. 3 and Table 2). Compound 2 is thermally stable up to 360 °C (DTA–TG, see ESI‡).

[{(Pb6O2)L4}·2EtOH]n (3)

Crystals of 3 were obtained under solvothermal conditions in H2O : EtOH (10 : 2) by reaction of Pb(NO3)2 with LH2 in the presence of 2,2′-bipyridine as base and 3–4 drops of triethylamine to reach pH 4–6. Compound 3 crystallises in the orthorhombic, space group Pbcn with four molecules in the unit cell. Lead(ii)-based MOFs with 1,4-BDC or with 4,4′-BPDC as ligands have been explored,22,23 and other Pb-based MOFs, coordination polymers and networks have been reported as well.47 However, 3 shows unprecedented Pb6O2(O2C)4 clusters as SBU (Fig. 4) consisting of two edge-sharing Pb4O tetrahedra in which the oxide anions (light blue) are in a distorted tetrahedral environment (Pb–O(13)–Pb 105.3(1)–112.3(1)°, Pb–O(13) 2.256(3)–2.348(3) Å) (Table 3).

The Pb6O2 clusters are interconnected by dianionic bis-carboxylato ligands in three different coordination modes (Fig. 5): (i) the two carboxylato groups of one linker adopt a μ2,η1:η2-bridging coordination mode, (ii) one carboxylato group of the second linker exhibits a μ3,η1:η1:η1 and the other a μ3,η2:η1:η1 chelating–bridging coordination mode. Pb–O(L) bond lengths, which range from 2.256(3) to 2.656(4) Å, are comparable to those in other lead(ii) carboxylates.48

This interconnection of the Pb6O2 clusters by bis-carboxylato ligands results in an infinite three-dimensional network for [{(Pb6O2)L4}]n, in which each pore is occupied by two ethanol molecules. The Pb6O2(O2C)4 zigzag chains extend along the c axis and the centres of these units are ca. 16.40 Å apart (Fig. 6). The topology of the resulting zigzag three-dimensional framework can be described by the unprecedented point symbol {440.626}{46}2. For the given point symbol, there is no assignment to a three-letter code available in the TOPOS & RCSR database.40 Simplified networks are shown in Fig. 7.

The total potential solvent-accessible void volume is 1695 Å3, total per unit cell 7815 Å3 [21.7%], calculated with PLATON.46 However, the calculated channel size of ca. 4 × 3 Å2 is very small, and a nitrogen adsorption isotherm revealed no adsorption capacity. Thermal analysis (DTA–TG) showed 3 to be thermally stable up to ca. 380 °C (loss of ethanol (ca. 1.8%) occurred in the temperature range of 60–100 °C).

Luminescence measurements

Compounds 1–3 show visible emission by excitation with a UV lamp. The respective room temperature emission spectra of the ligandLH2 as well as those of 1, 2, and 3 excited at 419 nm (23 866 cm−1) are depicted in Fig. 8. All bands are symmetrically or slightly unsymmetrically Gaussian shaped. It is remarkable that the maxima of the free ligandLH2 and 1 (λmax = 519 nm, = 19 278 cm−1) and those of 2 and 3 are identical (λmax = 534 nm, = 18 712 cm−1) suggesting that the emission bands originate from ligand transitions. The positions of the bands can probably be explained with the different dihedral angles (θ) of the aromatic rings which are in the same range for 2 (53.2(3), 54.1(3), 54.2(3) and 66.7(3)°) and 3 (60.1(4)° and 63.7(4)°), but differ remarkably from those of 1 (86.1(6)° to 86.3(6)°). In general, a larger dihedral angle results in reduced interactions of the π electrons shifting the emission bands to higher energies as observed for 1. While the crystal structure of the free ligandLH2 is not known, the dihedral angle could be assumed to be in the same range as for 1. Although this discussion is somewhat speculative, because the dihedral angles in the excited states are not known, it can be assumed that the conformations will not change remarkably during excitation due to the packing forces in the rigid crystal structures. The different positions of the emission bands leading to dark blue or light blue emission are also reflected in the changes in the colour coordinates (LH2: x = 0.1823, y = 0.1333; 1: x = 0.1762, y = 0.1133; 2: x = 0.2102, y = 0.2096, Fig. 9). Unfortunately, the emission of 3 was too weak to determine colour coordinates.

Since DFT calculations showed that the rotation of the two substituted phenyl rings around the central C–C bond in LH2 is practically unhindered and no clearly preferred conformation could be identified for LH2 (see ESI‡), it is not surprising that under different conditions LH2 and its metal complexes adopt conformations with different dihedral angles (θ). Furthermore, time-dependent DFT calculations confirmed that conformations of LH2 with θ around 60° give signals at lower wavenumbers than conformations with θ values around 90°.

The full width at half maximum (fwhm) of the emission bands also differs (LH2: fwhm = 4406 cm−1, 1: fwhm = 5423 cm−1, 2: fwhm = 5677 cm−1, 3: fwhm = 4830 cm−1). This observation can be explained by different changes of the interatomic distances of the excited states relative to the ground states for the compounds, but mainly by several crystallographically independent ligands all excitable at 419 nm with comparable dihedral angles, which are, moreover, disordered in the case of 1 and 3. Therefore, the observed emission bands are a superimposition of several bands. Because 2 has the largest range of dihedral angles for the crystallographically different ligands L, its emission band has the largest width.

The quantum yields (QY) of the solid compounds are rather different. While the QY of LH2 is only 2%, 1 has a QY of 10%. Although this is not a high value, this result is remarkable, because the quantum yield of ligands is generally higher. Obviously, the structure of 1 prevents radiationless transitions. In contrast, the QY of 2 is the same as for the ligand (2%) and the QY of 3 is 0.2% and, thus, extremely weak. Detailed investigations of the optical properties of the compounds would be necessary to understand this behaviour.

In the case of the Cd-based MOF 2 it is possible to detect emission bands at different energies using appropriate excitation energies, which excite ligands with different dihedral angles. In this case, three emission bands with maxima at 17 736, 18 513, and 18 711 cm−1 could be observed (Fig. 10). The excitation spectrum according to the high energy emission band is also depicted in Fig. 10. Due to the Stokes shift and the shape, the emission and excitation bands can be assigned to S1 → S0 transitions of the ligands.

Emission spectra excited at higher energies show some bands located around 25 000 cm−1 (LH2: max = 25 360 cm−1, 1: max = 25 880 cm−1, 2: max = 25 040 cm−1, 3: max = 25 020 cm−1; Fig. 11 shows those of 1 and 3). Again, the maxima of 2 and 3 are at comparable energies, which can be explained with the dihedral angles of the aromatic rings (see above). These bands can thus be again assigned to ligand transitions, but starting most probably from the higher singlet states S2 to the ground state. For LH2 and 3, the S1 → S0 transitions could also be detected with lower intensity, but not for 1 and 2. The excitation spectrum of 3 is also depicted in Fig. 11. It consists of several broad bands, with a low energy maximum at 28 333 cm−1 leading to a Stokes shift of about 3300 cm−1. The excitation spectra for compounds 1 and 2 (not shown here) are similar in position and shape. This supports the assignment of S2 → S0 transitions.

While Zn2+ as well as Cd2+ ions are not optically active in the visible range, Pb2+ ions show, in general, intense emission due to sp → s2 transitions.49 It is remarkable that no Pb transitions could be detected for 3. The reason for this might be that the excitation of the ligands is much more efficient and energy transfer L → Pb is much more inefficient than direct ligand emission.

A comparison of the optical properties in solution (DMF) and the solid state is only possible for the ligandLH2 as compounds 1–3 are not soluble in any solvent. As shown in Fig. 12, the ligand absorption spectrum shows an onset at about 375 nm and two maxima at 305 nm (32 787 cm−1) and 269 nm (37 175 cm−1), respectively, which is very comparable in shape and position to the excitation spectrum of compound 3 (see Fig. 11). This indicates that the conformation of the ligandLH2 dissolved in DMF is not very different to that in compound 3.

Experimental

Materials and general methods

Commercially purchased reagents were used without further purification. The infrared spectra were recorded on a Perkin-Elmer System 2000 FT-IR spectrometer using KBr disks in the range of 4000–400 cm−1. Elemental analysis was performed with a Heraeus VARIO EL instrument CHN-O analyser. Thermal analyses were performed on a NETZSCH STA/QMS system 409/429-403 thermal analyser from room temperature to 800 °C with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 under nitrogen flow. The nitrogenadsorption isotherm at 77 K of 1 was obtained on an ASAP-2000 (Micromeritics) instrument equipped with a high-resolution pressure sensor. Before the isotherm measurements, the sample was heated at 150 °C for 12 h.

X-ray crystallography

The X-ray data were collected with a Gemini-S CCD diffractometer (Agilent Technologies) using Mo Kα radiation and omega scan rotation. Data collection and data reduction were done with CrysAlisPro including the program SCALE3 ABSPACK for empirical absorption correction.50 Structures of 1 and 2 were solved by direct methods and that of 3 with Patterson methods.51 Anisotropic refinement of all non-hydrogen atoms, except for poorly defined solvent molecules in 2 and 3, was performed with SHELXL-97. Hydrogen atoms were calculated on idealised positions by using the riding model. The ligands were found to be highly disordered for 1 and 3. In the final refinement of 1 the contribution of non-coordinating, and most likely highly disordered, solvent molecules was removed from the data with the SQUEEZE routine implemented in PLATON.46 Structure figures were generated with Diamond-3.52Crystallographic data and other pertinent information for 1–3 are summarised in Table 4. CCDC 904635 (1), 904636 (2) and 904637 (3) contain the supplementary crystallographic data for this paper. The PXRD measurements were carried out on a STOE STADI–P diffractometer in the Debye–Scherrer mode using Cu Kα1 radiation (λ = 1.5406 Å). The samples for these measurements were prepared in glass capillaries (outer diameter 0.5 mm).

Synthesis of 4,4′-(CO2H)2-2,2′-(OMe)2-1,1′-(C6H3)2 (LH2)

The synthesis is reported in the literature.21,37 A modified synthesis is given in the ESI.‡

Synthesis of 1, 2, and 3

Solvothermal syntheses were carried out in steel autoclaves with appropriate Teflon vessel (PARR). The temperature programmes were applied in an oven ULE400 (MEMMERT) using the software Celsius 2005 (Version 6.1).

[{(Zn4O)(L)3(H2O)}·4.5DMF]n (1)

The synthesis was based on the publication by Li et al.53 for highly crystalline MOF-5 (ligand : Zn2+ : DMF = 1 : 2 : 258.5). The solution was placed in a Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel and heated at 105 °C for 24 h, and then cooled slowly to room temperature over 72 h. Yellow block-like crystals of 1 were obtained (0.3 g, 90% yield, based on LH2). Anal. calcd (after drying in the air) for C55.5H55.5N2.5O22.5Zn4 (1-2DMF) (1379.1): C, 48.34; H, 4.06; N, 2.54. Found: C, 48.56; H, 4.11; N, 2.36%. [{(Cd3L4)2(CdL2)}·8DMF·8Me2NH2·2Cl]n (2)

LH2 (0.200 g, 0.662 mmol) and Cd(NO3)2·4H2O (0.140 g, 0.463 mmol) were dissolved in DMF/C6H5Cl (8/2 mL) and 3 drops of triethylamine were added. The solution was placed in a Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel, heated at 165 °C for 47 h and then cooled slowly to room temperature over 63 h. The solution was filtered and yellow block-like crystals of 2 appeared after 4 weeks on slow evaporation at RT (0.2 g, 60% yield, based on LH2). [{(Pb6O2)L4}·2EtOH]n (3)

3–4 drops of triethylamine were added to a mixture of LH2 (0.151 g, 0.5 mmol), 2,2′bipyridine (0.078 g, 0.5 mmol) and Pb(NO3)2 (0.331 g, 1 mmol) in H2O : EtOH (10 : 2 mL) to reach pH 4–6. The mixture was placed in a Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel, at 150 °C for 48 h, and then cooled to room temperature over 21 h. Colourless needle-like crystals of 3 were obtained (0.21 g, 65% yield, based on LH2). Anal. calcd for C68H60O28Pb6 (2568.30): C, 31.80; H, 2.35; O, 17.44%. Found: C, 31.96; H, 2.42; O, 16.61. Luminescence measurements

Photoluminescence investigations at room temperature on solid samples sealed in silica ampoules and on LH2 dissolved in DMF were carried out with a Jobin–Yvon fluorescence spectrometer (Fluorolog 3) equipped with two 0.22 m double monochromators (SPEX, 1680) and a 450 W xenon lamp. The emission spectra were corrected for photomultiplier sensitivity, the excitation spectra for lamp intensity and both for the transmission of the monochromators. Quantum yields were measured by using the same equipment with an additional Ulbricht sphere. For absorption measurements in the UV/vis range, a Cary 5000 (Varian) was used.

Conclusions

The zinc(ii)- (1) and lead(ii)-based (3) MOFs with the functionalised biphenyl linker 4,4′-(CO2)2-2,2′-(OMe)2-1,1′-(C6H3)2 (L) exhibit different networks, with Zn4O(O2C)6(H2O) clusters as the secondary building unit (SBU) in the three-dimensional Zn-based MOF [{(Zn4O)(L)3(H2O)}·4.5DMF]n (1) and Pb6O2(O2C)4 clusters as SBU in the Pb-based MOF [{(Pb6O2)L4}·2EtOH]n (3). The basic network of 2 was reported previously; here, the pores are filled with DMF, 8Me2NH2+ and 2Cl−. 1–3 are thermally stable up to 360 to 420 °C and show intense emissions which could be assigned to S1,2 → S0 transitions of the ligands.
