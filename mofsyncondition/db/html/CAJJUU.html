Keggin polyoxometalate-templated chair-like heptanuclear silver complex

Author links open overlay panel, , , , Xiang Wang a, , , ,
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ica.2011.09.032Get rights and content

Abstract

Two isostructural Keggin-templated compounds containing a chair-like heptanuclear silver complex, [{Ag7(Hbtz)2(btz)4}{PM12O40}]·H2O (M = W, 1; Mo, 2) (Hbtz = benzotriazolate), have been hydrothermally synthesized and characterized by routine methods and single crystal X-ray diffraction. In the compounds 1 and 2, the Hbtz ligands present two types of coordination modes. A pentameric silver chain with two kinds of Ag⋯Ag weak interactions exists in the heptanuclear silver complex. The heptanuclear silver cations connect the polyoxoanions PM12O403− to a two-dimensional layer, and the adjacent layers are fused together by the pseudo wave-like chains, H-bonds and AgI–π interactions, constructing a three-dimensional net. Furthermore, electrochemical properties have been studied for compounds 1 and 2 and the two compounds display excellent electrocatalytic activities to the reduction of nitrite.

1. Introduction

Polynuclear transition metal complexes can be employed as building blocks to design and construct coordination polymers with unique magnetic, optical, biological and pharmacological properties [1], [2], [3], [4], [5]. Ag has attracted considerable interest in generating polynuclear transition metal complexes. Silver centers not only provide abundant coordination modes, but also commonly form argentophilic Ag⋯Ag interactions, which lead to the formation of polynuclear silver complexes [5], [6], [7], [8]. Nevertheless, for target synthesis of a polynuclear transition metal complex, the selection of a suitable organic ligand is an important factor. The Hbtz (Hbtz = benzotriazolate) ligand displays an ability to bridge metal ions to supply polynuclear complexes with unique structure, as a result of its multiple coordination modes [9], [10].

Polyoxometalates (POMs), as versatile metal–oxygen clusters with potential applications in such as materials science, catalysis, photochemistry and medicinal chemistry [11], [12], [13], possess high charge, large size and abundant surface oxygen atoms. Consequently, POMs often act as inorganic anionic templates to direct the assemblies of metal polynuclear clusters with fascinating geometry and connectivity [14]. Meanwhile, the combination of POMs and metal polynuclear metal complexes may endow the polymers with brand-new properties [15], [16].

The templating effect of POMs has been also applied to build high-nuclearity silver complexes. For example, Gao and Wang et al. obtained silver-alkynyl cages based on Mo6O228− and V10O286− [17], [18]; Lu’s group synthesized Keggin-templated tetranuclear and hexanuclear silver complexes [19]. In this work, we choose H3PW12O40·12H2O, H3PMo12O40·14H2O, Hbtz ligand, AgNO3 as reactants to get two POM-templated high-nuclear silver complexes [{Ag7(Hbtz)2(btz)4}{PM12O40}]·H2O (M = W, 1; Mo, 2), in which the chair-like heptanuclear silver cations connect the polyanions into a grid-like 2D layer, and the adjacent layers further stack to a 3D structure through wave-like chains, H-bonds and AgI–π interactions.

2. Experimental

2.1. Materials and general methods

All reagents for synthesis were purchased from commercial sources and were used without further purification. H3PW12O40·12H2O and H3PMo12O40·14H2O were prepared according to the reported procedures and verified by IR spectroscopy [20]. Elemental analyses (C, H, and N) were performed on a Perkin-Elmer 2400 CHN Elemental Analyzer. The IR spectra were obtained on an Alpha Centaurt FT/IR spectrometer with KBr pallet in the 400–4000 cm−1 region. The TG analyses were performed on a DTG-60AH instrument in flowing N2 with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. The XRPD patterns were obtained with a Rigaku D/max 2500 V PC diffractometer with Cu Kα radiation, the scanning rate is 4°/s, 2θ ranging from 4° to 50°. Cyclic voltammograms were obtained with a CHI 660 electrochemical workstation at room temperature. Platinum gauze was used as a counter electrode and Ag/AgCl electrode was referenced. Chemically bulk-modified carbon paste electrodes (CPEs) were used as working electrodes.

2.2. Syntheses of compounds 1 and 2

[{Ag7(Hbtz)2(btz)4}{PW12O40}]·H2O (1): A mixture of H3PW12O40·12H2O (0.200 g, 0.065 mmol), AgNO3 (0.060 g, 0.353 mmol), Hbtz (0.040 g, 0.336 mmol) and 8.0 mL H2O was stirred for 1 h. The pH was adjusted to 2.0 with 1 M NaOH, and the suspension was transferred to a Teflon-lined reactor and kept under autogenous pressure at 160 °C for 5 days. After the autoclave was slowly cooled to room temperature over a period of 10 °C/h, orange crystals of 1 were obtained. The crystals were filtered, washed with distilled water and dried at room temperature (0.119 g, 55% yield based on Ag). Anal. Calc. for C36H28Ag7N18O41PW12: C, 9.92; H, 0.65; N, 5.78. Found: C, 9.89; H, 0.62; N, 5.80%.

[{Ag7(Hbtz)2(btz)4}{PMo12O40}]·H2O (2): 2 was prepared by following an analogous procedure to that used for the synthesis of 1 except that H3PW12O40·12H2O was replaced by H3PMo12O40·14H2O. Red crystals of 2 were obtained (0.094 g, 57% yield based on Ag). Calc. for C36H28Ag7N18O41PMo12: C, 13.08; H, 0.85; N, 7.63. Found: C, 13.26; H, 0.77; N, 7.66%.

2.3. Preparation of 1-, 2-CPEs

Compound 1(2)-modified carbon paste electrode (1,2-CPE) was prepared as follows: 90 mg of graphite powder and 8 mg title compound were mixed and ground together by agate mortar and pestle to achieve a uniform mixture, and then it was added 0.6 mL nujol with stirring. The homogenized mixture was packed into a glass tube with 1.2 mm inner diameter, and the tube surface was wiped with paper. Electrical contact was established with copper rod through the back of the electrode.

2.4. X-ray crystallography

Crystal data for compounds 1 and 2 were collected on a Bruker SMART-CCD diffractometer, with Mo Kα monochromatic radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at 293 K. The structures were solved by the directed methods and refined by full matrix least-squares on F2 using the shelxtl crystallographic software package [21], [22]. All the non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. Crystallographic data for the structures have been deposited with Cambridge Crystallographic Data Center with CCDC numbers: 798715 for 1 and 798716 for 2. Crystallographic data are given in Table 1. Selected bond lengths and angles of the compounds are listed in Table S1.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Syntheses

The isostructural compounds 1 and 2 were synthesized under almost identical hydrothermal conditions except for using different Keggin polyanions, which possess the same charges. We tried to replace H3PW12O40·12H2O or H3PMo12O40·14H2O with H4SiW12O40·14H2O, H4SiMo12O40 and K5BWI2O40·xH2O, but no crystals were obtained. Accordingly, the charge of Keggin polyoxometalates is a key factor influencing the assembly of compounds 1 and 2.

3.2. Crystal structure of compound 1

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that compounds 1 and 2 are isostructural, except that POM anion is {PW12O40}3− in compound 1 and {PMo12O40}3− in compound 2. Related bond lengths and angles are comparable in compounds 1 and 2 (Table S1). Therefore, only the structure of 1 is described here representatively. Compound 1 consists of one polyoxoanion {PW12O40}3− (abbreviated to PW12), one heptanuclear silver cation {Ag7(Hbtz)2(btz)4}3+ (abbreviated to {Ag7}3+), as shown in Fig. 1. The PW12 anion shows a classical α-Keggin configuration. The central atom P is disorderly surrounded by a cube of eight oxygen atoms, with each oxygen site half-occupied [23].

There are four independent silver atoms which exhibit two kinds of coordination numbers: tri-coordinated Ag1 and Ag2, and bi-coordinated Ag3 and Ag4. The Ag1 and Ag2 atoms reside in ‘‘T-type” geometry coordinated by two N atoms from two Hbtz ligands and one O atom from the PW12 anion, respectively. Ag3 and Ag4 achieve their linear geometry by coordinating with two N atoms of two Hbtz ligands. The bond distances around the Ag atoms are 2.7433(178)–2.7919(148) Å (Ag–O) and 2.089(12)–2.180(19) Å (Ag–N), and N–Ag–N angles are 153.0(7)–180.0(10)°. Hbtz ligands present two types of coordination modes: (a) as a deprotonated tridentate anionic ligand, three N atoms of a btz− bridge three Ag+ ions, almost located on a plane with the ligand btz, or located in a V-type on the two sides of the ligand plane; (b) as a neutral monodentate ligand, one N atom of a Hbtz molecule coordinates to one Ag+ ion, terminating the dimensional extension.

A remarkable structural feature of 1 is the chair-like heptanuclear silver complex, as shown in Fig. 2. Two aspects are noteworthy. First, the five silvers (Ag1, Ag1#, Ag2, Ag2#, Ag3) are in a plane. Second, a pentameric silver chain (Ag1, Ag1#, Ag4, Ag4#, Ag3) exists in the {Ag7}3+ units. There are two kinds of Ag⋯Ag distances among five silver atoms, namely, 3.165(3) and 3.377(3) Å, which are shorter than van der Waals contact distance (3.44 Å) [24]. The Ag⋯Ag interaction between Ag1 and Ag4 is stronger than that of Ag1 and Ag3.

The chair-like {Ag7}3+ cation as a tetra-dentate linker connects the polyanion PW12 by weak Ag⋯O bonds into a grid-like 2D layer (in the ac plane) as shown in Fig. 3 (left). The polyoxoanion PW12 as a template is wrapped by two chair-like {Ag7}3+ units in the grid-like layer, as shown in Fig. 3 (right). When compared with the compound AgBZT reported by Rajeswaranby et al., the template effect of POMs is significant for forming the chair-like {Ag7}3+ units, as in that case, without the presence of a polyoxoanion in the reaction system, all three nitrogens of the btz are bound to silver atoms and all silvers adopt tri-coordinated “trigonal-type” geometry. The self-assembly of Ag+ and Hbtz forms a highly cross-linked 2D network [25]. We thus deduce that the polyoxoanion PW12 impact on the coordination modes of the Ag+ and the ligand Hbtz, which result in the different Ag-btz structures.

Furthermore, the heptanuclear silver units between the layers are extended into a wave-like chain via AgI–π interactions (Ag4⋯C6, 2.985 Å) [26], [27] and π–π stacking of Hbtz ligands (3.670 Å). Finally, the neighboring layers are stacked to a 3D net through the wave-like chains, H-bonds [N2⋯O9, 3.00(2) Å; N2⋯O10, 3.08(2) Å; N2⋯O11, 3.04(2) Å; N2⋯O12, 3.16(3) Å] and AgI–π interactions between adjacent wave-like chains (Ag1⋯C11, 3.070 Å), as shown in Fig. 4.

3.3. FT-IR spectrum, TG analysis and XRD

In the IR spectrum (Fig. S1), characteristic bands of POMs are observed for ν(P–O), ν(W–Od), ν(W–Ob–W) and ν(W–Oc–W) at 1079, 966, 885, 806 cm−1 for 1 and for ν(P–O), ν(Mo–Od), ν(Mo–Ob–Mo) and ν(Mo–Oc–Mo) at 1058, 952, 875, 806 cm−1 for 2 [28]. A series of bands in the region from 1172 to 1918 cm–1 for 1 and 1172 to 1956 cm–1 for 2 are regarded as the bands of organic ligand Hbtz.

TG analyses of 1 and 2 were carried out under N2 atmosphere at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 in the range of 20–800 °C (Fig. S2). The TG curves of both 1 and 2 exhibit two weight loss steps. The total weight loss of 15.43% (calc. 16.71%) from 289 to 669 °C for 1 and 20.84% (calc. 22.06%) from 292 to 528 °C for 2 corresponds to the loss of one water molecule and six Hbtz molecules.

The XRPD patterns for compounds 1 and 2 are presented in Fig. S3. The diffraction peaks of both simulated and experimental patterns match in the key positions, indicating the phase purities of the compounds.

3.4. Cyclic voltammetric behaviors of 1-, 2-CPE

To study the electrochemical properties of compounds 1 and 2, the title compounds modified carbon paste electrodes (1-, 2-CPE) were prepared, owing to their insolubility in water and most organic solvents. The cyclic voltammetry of 1-CPE (2-CPE) is measured in 1 M H2SO4 aqueous solution. The CV curve of 1-CPE in the potential range from +450 to −675 mV shows three reversible with half-wave potentials E1/2 = (Epa + Epc)/2 at −537 (I–I′), −327.5 (II–II′) and −101 mV (III–III′), and one irreversible redox peaks (IV) at 290 mV (scan rate: 220 mV s−1). The redox peaks III–III′ and II–II′ could be ascribed to two consecutive one-electron processes of W centers, and that of I–I′ corresponds to one two-electron process [29]. And the fourth irreversible anodic peak IV at 285 mV is assigned to the oxidation of Ag (I) [30]. Three pairs of reversible redox peaks (I–I′, II–II′, III–III′) appeared for 2 in the potential range from +700 to −150 mV and the mean peak potentials E1/2 were 15.5, 237 and 403 mV (scan rate: 260 mV s−1), respectively, corresponding to three two-electron redox processes of Mo atoms [29], as shown in Fig. 5. Furthermore, the peak potentials change gradually with increasing scan rates: the cathodic peak potentials shift to the negative direction and corresponding anodic peak potentials to the positive direction.

3.5. Electrocatalytic reduction of nitrite on 1-, 2-CPE

It is well-known that POMs have been exploited extensively in electrocatalytic reduction for nitrite [31], [32]. However, the electroreduction of nitrite requires a high overpotential, and no obvious response is observed for nitrite at bare CPE in the potential range. The cyclic voltammograms of 1-CPE and 2-CPE display excellent electrocatalytic activities to the reduction of nitrite in an acid solution containing nitrite (Fig. 6). It can be observed that, with the addition of nitrite, all the three reduction peak currents increased while the corresponding oxidation peak currents of W or Mo dramatically decreased, suggesting that the reduction of nitrite involves one-, two- and four-electron reduced species for 1-CPE, two-, four- and six- electron reduced species for 2-CPE.

4. Conclusion

Compounds 1 and 2 have been synthesized by using the Keggin anions as templates which direct the formation of heptanuclear silver complex. The {Ag7}3+ unit acting as a synthon, plays a key role in construction of the 3D structure: the {Ag7}3+ units link the polyanions PW12 into a grid-like 2D layer in the ac plane and form a wave-like chain along the b axis via AgI–π and π–π stacking of Hbtz ligands. The successful syntheses of the Keggin-templated heptanuclear silver complexes provide more informative examples for designable construction of new POM-templated polynuclear metal complexes.

Keggin-type compounds with heteroatom X=P, Si, have been widely studied, while Keggin polymers involved transition metals (TM) as central atoms are rarely reported [11–15], the main problem is that the oxidation of transition metal is hard to determine in a macroanion, but also may introduce intriguing features such as magnetism; In addition, among numerous organic ligands of ability to diversify framework structure types associated with various transition metals, the aromatic amines have been supposed to be a perfect candidate in building supramolecular polymer through intermolecular interactions [16,17].

    Two Keggin-type heteropolytungstates, [Co(phen)3]3[CoW12O40]·9H2O 1 (phen=1,10-phenanthroline) and [Fe(phen)3]2[FeW12O40]·H3O·H2O 2, have been synthesized via the hydrothermal technique and characterized by single crystal X-ray diffraction analyses, IR, XPS, TG analysis, UV–DRS, XRD, thermal-dependent and magnetic-dependent 2D-COS IR (two-dimensional infrared correlation spectroscopy). Crystal structure analysis reveals that the polyanions in compound 1 are linked into 3D supramolecule through hydrogen bonding interactions between lattice water molecules and terminal oxygen atoms of polyanion units, and [Co(phen)3]2+ cations distributed in the polyanion framework with many hydrogen bonding interactions. The XPS spectra indicate that all the Co atoms in 1 are +2 oxidation state, the Fe atoms in 2 existing with +2 and +3 mixed oxidation states.

  • Fabrication of carbon paste electrode containing a new inorganic-organic hybrid based on [SiW<inf>12</inf>O<inf>40</inf>]<sup>4-</sup> polyoxoanion and Nile blue and its electrocatalytic activity toward nitrite reduction

    2013, Journal of Electroanalytical Chemistry
    Citation Excerpt :

    Polyoxometalates (POMs), as metal–oxygen cluster species, are a large and rapidly growing class of compounds, that have attracted much attention in the areas of catalysis, medicine, bioanalysis and materials science, owing to their excellent properties such as the possibility of tuning POM size, shape, charge density, acidity, redox potential, stability and solubility characteristics [1,2].

    In this study, a new inorganic–organic hybrid compound consisting of a Keggin type polyoxometalate, H4SiW12O40 (SiW12), and Nile blue (NB) was synthesized and characterized. The (NB)2H2SiW12O40 (NB2SiW12) hybrid compound was characterized by Fourier transform infrared (FT-IR), elemental (CHNS) analysis, UV–Vis and thermogravimetry techniques, and used as a bulk modifier to fabricate a chemically modified carbon paste electrode (CPE) by direct mixing. The electrochemical behavior of the newly NB2SiW12 composite CPE was analyzed by cyclic voltammetry, and the electrode was applied for nitrite reduction, exhibiting high electrocatalytic activity. Determination of nitrite by fixed potential amperometry was done under various experimental conditions and, at the optimum conditions, the linear response ranged from 5 to 1200 μM with a high sensitivity of and a low detection limit. The electrode has the remarkable advantage of surface renewal owing to bulk modification, as well as simple preparation, good mechanical and chemical stability and reproducibility. No noticeable interferences from other ionic species usually found in natural water were observed. The new developed electrode has been successfully applied for detection of nitrite in real water samples.
