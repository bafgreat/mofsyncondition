Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) are emerging porous crystalline materials used for various applications.1–7 In particular, flexible MOFs known as third-generation porous coordination polymers8 received significant attention as promising materials for storage and separation of various chemicals because their framework structure can be modulated by physical and chemical stimuli such as heat, pressure, photons, and guest molecules.9–14 The flexibility of three-dimensional (3D) MOFs is often observed in MOFs that can perform the hinge motion with organic linkers around metal nodes,15–17 or in MOFs that can twist the conformation of flexible organic linkers.18–20 Furthermore, the flexibility of 3D MOFs is also observed in MOFs with interpenetrating framework structures, where the two interpenetrating frameworks are at different positions.21

The flexibility of two-dimensional (2D) MOFs is distinctly different from that of 3D MOFs. Flexible 2D MOFs are either stacked 2D layered frameworks22–26 or interdigitated 2D layered frameworks.27,28 Therefore, the flexibility is mostly governed by weak interactions between the 2D layers. Following the report on the guest-responsive framework flexibility of the interdigitated fish-bone type 2D layered framework [Zn(ip)(bipy)], where ip = isophthalate and bipy = 4,4′-bipyridine,29 there have been extensive investigations of isoreticular interdigitated 2D layered frameworks. The studies exploited various interlayer interactions between combinations of metal(ii) ions,30–33 bent dicarboxylates,30,34–41 and dipyridyl linkers.36,39–43 Notably, the most important interaction between the interdigitated layers is the π–π stacking interaction between aromatic rings of rigid dicarboxylates of one-dimensional (1D) nodal chains made of M2(COO)4 clusters and bent dicarboxylates.

Herein, we report a new interdigitated 2D layered framework with switchable framework flexibility depending on the guest molecules in the solvent pores. The interdigitated 2D layered framework [Cd(dbda)(bipy)]·2DMA·2H2O (where dbda = 3,3′-((3,4-dioxocyclobut-1-ene-1,2-diyl)bis(azanediyl))dibenzoate and DMA = N,N-dimethylacetamide) (1) is prepared by solvothermal reaction of Cd(ii) ions, dbda as a highly flexible multifunctional dicarboxylate ligand, and bipy as a rigid dipyridyl linker in DMA–water mixed solvent (Scheme 1). The dbda ligand is a bent dicarboxylate linker, which is highly flexible due to a large number of rotational degrees of freedom. It is capable of various hydrogen bonding interactions using squaramide groups as hydrogen bonding donors and acceptors, in addition to π–π stacking interactions using both phenyl and squaramide groups. Switchable flexibility was demonstrated by 1, depending on the types and amounts of solvent molecules encapsulated in the solvent pores. The interlayer interactions in [Cd(dbda)(bipy)]·DMA (2), with only strongly bound DMA in the solvent pores, can be reversibly modulated and the major interactions are π–π stacking interactions between the phenyl groups. Activated 2a showed the adsorption of 5.0 CO2 molecules per Cd2(COO)4 cluster with gate-opening at ∼0.08 bar and hysteric desorption. On the other hand, the framework of [Cd(dbda)(bipy)]·H2O (3) with no DMA in the solvent pores did not show any flexibility during similar CO2 adsorption and desorption processes. Therefore, the interlayer interactions in 3 are significantly more extensive than those in 2. In addition to the π–π stacking interactions between the phenyl and squaramide groups, π–π stacking interaction between the phenyl group of dbda and the pyridyl group of bipy were observed, as were CH–π interactions between the phenyl groups of dbda. Furthermore, activated 3a, which does not contain any DMA molecules in the pores, only adsorbs a small amount of CO2 (0.27 CO2 molecules per Cd2(COO)4 cluster) without gate-opening.

Results and discussion

A solvothermal reaction of dbda and bipy with Cd(NO3)2·4H2O in DMA–water (2 : 1) mixed solvent yields interdigitated 2D framework 1 with large solvent pores, both within the 2D layers and between the 2D layers. In 1, dinuclear Cd2(COO)4 clusters are doubly connected with bent dbda ligands to form an ellipsoidal unit containing a pore that can accommodate up to two DMA molecules, and the ellipsoidal units are interlinked to form a 1D nodal chain (Fig. 1a). The 1D nodal chains are doubly linked by bipy through the dinuclear Cd2(COO)4 cluster as a 4-c secondary building unit to yield a fish-bone type 2D layer with sql net topology (Fig. 1b and c).44 The 2D layers are interdigitated with each other by both π–π stacking interactions involving dbda ligands and hydrogen bonding interactions involving solvent molecules encapsulated in the solvent pores. Two types of DMA molecules are encapsulated in the solvent pores of 1 (Fig. 2a and d). The first type of DMA molecule (one DMA molecule per Cd ion) is strongly bound in the pores of the 1D nodal chain (Fig. 2a) and is stabilized by strong hydrogen bonding interactions with the amido moiety of the squaramide group. The second type of DMA molecule (one DMA molecule per Cd ion) is encapsulated along with water molecules (at two different sites) in the solvent pores between the interdigitated layers (Fig. 2d and Fig. S1, S2, ESI†) that are stabilized by π–π stacking interactions between the phenyl groups of the dbda ligand (Fig. S3 and Table S1, ESI†). While the second type of DMA molecule and one water molecule in the solvent pores are involved in the formation of hydrogen bonding networks between adjacent interdigitated layers (Fig. S1 and Table S2, ESI†), the other water molecule is involved in the formation of hydrogen bonding networks between the next adjacent interdigitated layers (Fig. S2, ESI†). The total potential solvent pore volume of 1 is 788.6 Å3 (41.6% of the unit cell volume) and the solvent pore volume between the layers, occupied by the weakly bound DMA and water molecules, is 532.8 Å3 (28.1% of the total unit cell volume).

The bulk purity of 1 was confirmed by comparison of the observed and simulated powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns (Fig. S4, ESI†). Furthermore, thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) of 1 shows multistep weight losses (Fig. S5, ESI†). The first weight loss at 72 °C is the loss of a weakly bound water molecule (found 0.9%; calc. 0.9%, weight loss of 0.4 H2O molecule); the second weight loss at 162 °C corresponds to the combination of the loss of a weakly bound DMA molecule and the loss of a strongly bound water molecule (found 11.6%; calc. 10.8%, weight loss of 0.92 DMA molecule and 0.4H2O molecule); the third weight loss at 274 °C is assumed as the loss of a strongly bound DMA molecule (found 11.6%; calc. 10.7%, weight loss of 1.0 DMA molecule). The weight loss over 300 °C corresponds to the decomposition of the framework.

In situ variable temperature PXRD (VT-PXRD) data were measured in a temperature-controlled stream of N2 gas to determine under what heat treatment conditions the framework phase transition occurred. The PXRD patterns of 1 packed in a capillary were collected at 20 to 310 °C with 5 °C temperature intervals. The VT-PXRD patterns indicate that the continuous phase change of the framework, including two major phase transitions around 150 °C and 200 °C, respectively, occurs during heat treatment process before decomposition at about 300 °C (Fig. 3).

Based on the TGA and VT-PXRD results, a new crystalline phase, 2 ([Cd(dbda)(bipy)]·DMA), can be obtained by heating 1 at 150 °C for 1 h under flowing N2 (Fig. S6, ESI†). The 1H nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectrum of 2 also supports the removal of all the weakly bound DMA molecules from the solvent pores (Fig. S7, ESI†). Furthermore, single crystals of 2 can be obtained by heating 1 at 130 °C for 1 d under vacuum (Fig. S6, ESI†).

Single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis of 2 shows that it has the same interdigitated 2D layer structure as 1 (Fig. 2e). However, the weakly bound DMA and water molecules encapsulated between the layers are not present, but the strongly bound DMA molecules are present in the solvent pores of the 1D nodal chain (Fig. 2b). The removal of the weakly bound DMA and water molecules results in an 18.7% reduction of the unit cell volume of 2 compared to that of 1 (from 1896.6 Å3 to 1544.1 Å3). This is accompanied by the reduction of the interlayer distance from 10.201 Å to 9.564 Å (Fig. 2d and e). Thus, the interlayer interactions are significantly altered. The strong π–π interaction observed between the phenyl groups in 1 disappeared and a new π–π interaction appeared between the squaramide and phenyl groups in 2 (Fig. S3b, d and Table S1, ESI†). The 2D layer structure was also altered to accommodate the new interlayer interactions. In the 1D nodal chain, the ellipsoidal unit in 2 is slightly elongated compared to that in 1. Furthermore, the inter-cluster distance of 16.132 Å in 1 is elongated to 16.659 Å in 2. In the 2D layer with sql net topology, the parallelogram consisting of four dinuclear Cd2(COO)4 SBUs doubly interconnected by two dbda ligands and two bipy linkers is altered. It changes from a parallelogram with the dimensions, 11.638 × 16.132 Å2, and an adjacent angle of 82.05° to a parallelogram with the dimensions, 16.613 × 16.659 Å2, and an adjacent angle of 56.57° (Fig. S8a and b, ESI†). The DMA molecules are still strongly bound in the pores of the 1D nodal chains through the same hydrogen bonding interactions with the amido residue of the squaramide group, but they are oriented in a slightly different direction (Fig. 2b). All these structural changes can be easily accommodated without significant network strain by changing the torsion angles of the dbda ligand (Fig. S9 and Table S3, ESI†). The potential solvent pore volume of 2 calculated by Platon software is 343.7 Å3 (22.3% of the unit cell volume of 2).

Another crystalline phase, 3 ([Cd(dbda)(bipy)]·H2O), can be obtained by heating 1 at 275 °C for 1 h under flowing N2 and then exposing it to ambient conditions (Fig. S10, ESI†). The complete removal the DMA molecules in the solvent pores was confirmed by the 1H NMR spectrum of 3 digested in DMSO-d6/DCl (Fig. S11, ESI†). Additionally, 3 can also be obtained by heating 1 at 180 °C for 1 d under vacuum, as shown in Fig. S10 (ESI†). Single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis of 3 shows that the DMA molecules in the solvent pores were completely removed; however, 3 still retains the same interdigitated 2D layer structure (Fig. 2f). An approximate 16% reduction of the unit cell volume of 3, from 1544.1 Å3 for 2 to 1298.0 Å3, results in profound alterations on the layer structure and the interlayer interactions. Furthermore, the conformation of the dbda ligand is significantly altered. The squaramide group, which is flipped to an approximately opposite orientation (Fig. 2c and Fig. S9e, ESI†), and the carboxylate group of dbda are involved in strong hydrogen bonding interactions with a water molecule adsorbed from air when the crystal was exposed at ambient conditions (Fig. S12 and Table S2, ESI†). Two different π–π stacking interactions further stabilize the densely packed interlayer structure with an interlayer distance of 7.617 Å. This is 2.584 Å and 1.947 Å shorter than that of 1 and 2, respectively (Fig. 2f and Fig. S13, ESI†). The first π–π stacking interaction is observed between the squaramide and phenyl groups of dbda, and the second π–π stacking interaction occurs between the phenyl groups of dbda and the pyridyl groups of bipy (Fig. S13a–d, ESI†). The additional CH–π interaction between the phenyl groups of dbda stabilizes the interdigitated 2D layered framework (Fig. S13e and f, ESI†). The total potential solvent volume calculated by Platon software is only 114.5 Å3 (8.8% of the unit cell volume).

To evaluate the gas sorption behavior of the frameworks, N2 and CO2 sorption isotherms of the activated 1, 2, and 3 (1a, 2a, and 3a, respectively) were obtained. All frameworks showed a very small amount of N2 uptake (Fig. S14, ESI†), as none of the frameworks had any accessible permanent pores for N2. Furthermore, weakly polarizing N2 molecules did not induce breathing behaviors in 2 and 3. Meanwhile, 1a shows a type I CO2 adsorption isotherm at 195 K with a maximum CO2 uptake of 12 cm3 g−1 (0.82 CO2 molecules per dinuclear Cd2(COO)4 cluster) at 1 bar (Fig. 4). The hysteric desorption isotherm, with ∼10 cm3 g−1 CO2 remaining in the pores even at very low CO2 pressure, indicates that the encapsulated CO2 is strongly bound in the pores. In contrast, 2a shows an evident gate-opening at 195 K and very low CO2 pressure (0.08 bar), where a sudden increase of CO2 uptake occurs even with a slight pressure decrease, and then the uptake reaches the maximum of 73 cm3 g−1 (5.0 CO2 molecules per dinuclear Cd2(COO)4 cluster) at 1 bar. The pressure decrease right after the gate-opening indicates an expansion of the framework leading to the increase in pore volume which occurred in a very short period. The hysteric desorption isotherm shows that evaporation of CO2 starts at 0.03 bar, which is slightly lower than the gate-opening pressure, and the complete desorption of CO2 is observed. Even a very small amount of CO2 molecules in the pore can trigger the structural transition from a semi-closed-phase framework structure with no or small pore volume to an open-phase framework with large pore volume. The breathing behavior originates from the flexibility of dbda, in addition to its ability to participate in various intermolecular interactions such as hydrogen bonding interactions, π–π stacking interactions, and van der Waals interactions. However, CO2 adsorption of 3a does not show any gate-opening phenomenon at 195 K. It only shows reversible type I CO2 adsorption and desorption isotherms with the maximum CO2 uptake of 4 cm3 g−1 (0.27 CO2 molecules per dinuclear Cd2(COO)4 cluster) at 1 bar. The CO2 sorption behavior indicates that 3a has a very small volume of permanent micropores. The extensive π–π stacking interactions and CH–π interactions between the interdigitated 2D layers of the closed-phase framework of 3a does not allow the gate-opening of the layered framework structure at 195 K. The CO2 sorption of 1a, 2a and 3a is measured at 298 K and 323 K, respectively, to see the CO2 uptake behaviour of the interdigitated 2D MOFs at higher temperatures. However, all the interdigitated 2D MOFs neither show any significant amount of CO2 uptake nor exhibit gate-opening behavior (Fig. S15, ESI†).

The semi-closed-phase framework of 2 can be reversely transformed to 1 in DMA–water (2 : 1) mixed solvent. Soaking 2 in the mixed solvent at 50 °C for 3 d produced 1 (Fig. 5). Even though the closed-phase framework of 3 cannot be transformed to a more open-phase framework structures by either N2 or CO2, it can be reversely transformed to 1 in a DMA–water (2 : 1) mixed solvent. Soaking 3 in the mixed solvent at 50 °C for 7 d also produced 1. This result shows that even the densely packed framework of 3 can be expanded back to the framework structure of 1 under strongly interacting DMA and water molecules. This transformation is due to the flexibility of dbda and its ability to participate in various intermolecular interactions.

Conclusions

In conclusion, we synthesized a new interdigitated 2D framework using a multifunctional flexible ligand, dbda, which shows different flexibility depending on the types and amounts of solvents remaining in the solvent pores. The removal of the weakly bound solvent molecules in the pores causes framework contraction, altering the interlayer π–π stacking interactions between the phenyl groups of the dbda ligand to compensate for the absence of hydrogen bonding interactions involving the removed solvent molecules. In this partially contracted interdigitated 2D framework with only strongly bound DMA molecules in the pores of the 1D nodal chain, the new interlayer π–π stacking interaction between the phenyl groups and squaramide groups of the dbda ligand can be easily adopted by adjusting the conformation of the flexible dbda ligand without significant increase in the framework strain. This partially contracted interdigitated 2D framework shows different flexibility depending on the guest molecules. It cannot expand when exposed to N2 at 77 K but can reversibly expand and contract when exposed to CO2 at 195 K. In the DMA–water mixed solvent, which is the solvent used for the preparation of the framework, the framework returns to the as-synthesized interdigitated 2D framework. When all the DMA molecules are removed from the solvent pores, the framework contracts further, significantly altering the interlayer interactions. Not only the π–π interaction between the phenyl groups and the squaramide groups, but also the π–π interaction between the phenyl groups in dbda and the pyridyl groups in bipy, as well as the CH–π interaction between the phenyl groups are observed to change. To accommodate this wide range of interactions, the conformation of the dbda ligand has changed significantly. The orientation of the squaramide group of dbda is flipped in the opposite direction, which can be easily adopted by adjusting the conformation of the flexible dbda ligand without significantly increasing framework strain. The flexibility of the fully contracted interdigitated 2D framework is different from that of a partially contracted framework and framework expansion does not occur under CO2 at 195 K. Additionally, in DMA–water mixed solvent, the fully contracted framework returns to the as-synthesized interdigitated 2D framework, just like the partially contracted framework. Therefore, the framework flexibility of 2D layered MOF is not only governed by the intrinsic interlayer interactions but is also modulated by guest molecules encapsulated between the layers. This study will pave the way for deepening insights into the flexibility modulation of 2D layered MOFs.
