Ferroelectrics are materials in which positive and negative charge centers separate spontaneously even in the absence of an external electric field and the spontaneous electric polarization can be reversed by application of an external electric field. Ferroelectrics are used in a wide range of applications—random-access memories (FeRAM) in memorechnique, accelerometers in air bags or ink-jet printers, telecommunication signal-processing devices and high frequency devices for ultrasonic medical imaging.1 A potential application of ferroelectric materials lies in ultra-high-density memory devices, produced by controlling the ferroelectric domains at the nano-meter scale. Such storage devices would be non-volatile (and so able to retain the stored data for long periods of time without any power supply) and have short boot-up times.2

Materials with high dielectric constants that are closely related to their ferroelectric behaviors have found widespread high-technique applications in, e.g., dielectric resonators and filters to store and transfer microwave communication signals, makes possible wireless transmission through cell phones. In metals, the charge is delocalized and εo ≪ 0. In insulators, the charge is localized and εo > 0; materials with a dielectric constant greater than that of silicon nitride (εo > 7) are classified as high-dielectric constant materials. In general, a value of εo above 1000 is related to either a ferroelectric that displays a dipole moment in the absence of an external electric field or to a relaxor characterized by a ferroelectric response under high electric fields.3

Compared to perovskites–metal oxides with a structure like that of the natural mineral CaTiO3, that have been intensely studied as ferroelectric materials, metal–organic framework (MOF) or coordination polymers (MOCP) have received relatively rare attention.4 Most current studies of MOFs focus on their applications in gas storage, size- or shape-selective catalysis, separation, and molecular recognition.5 Herein we report two homochiral MOFs (1) and (2), prepared by the solvothermal reaction of CuCN with N-4-cyanobenzylquinidinium bromide (CBQ-Br) and N-4-cyanobenzylcinchonidinium bromide (CBC-Br), respectively (Scheme 1). (CBQ)CuI3(CN)3Br (1) reveals a 2D layered network, and (CBC)CuI2.5(CN)2Br1.5 (2) displays a 2-fold interpenetrating 3D framework with an unprecedented topology. Of practical interest is the fact that 1 is a ferroelectric compound with a dipolar relaxation and high dielectric constant (εo = 119.3). To the best of our knowledge, 1 is the first example of MOFs (see Fig. 1) with such a ferroelectric property. In IR spectra of both compounds, a strong peak at ca. 2300 cm−1 was observed, indicating the presence of a cyanide group.6

X-Ray crystal structural determination of MOF (1) reveals that there are three crystallographical independent Cu centers, three cyanide groups and one bromide anion in which all Cu centers displays a proximately trigonal pyramidal coordination geometry. However compositions of the three Cu centers are different: Cu1 is composed of an N atom of a quinicludine ring, C atom of a cyanide group and a Br atom; Cu2 consists of three N atoms from three different cyanide groups; Cu3 links two C atoms from two different cyanide groups and one Br anion. Thus, apart from the CBQ cation, the linking mode of three Cu atoms, three cyanide groups and one Br atom is a typical 2D hexagonal net in which CBQ cation only acts as a pillar to support this layer, while another layered CBQ cation pillar inserts the space between two layered pillars to result in the formation of ABAB packing mode (Fig. 2). Of interest is that CBQ cation only is a monodentate ligand while the cyano group of the 4-cyanobenzyl group failed to coordinate to Cu atoms.6

Quite different from that in MOF (1), the asymmetric unit in MOF (2) (Fig. 3), is composed of three crystallographically different Cu centers, two cyanide groups and two bromide anions in which two Cu centers displays a proximately trigonal pyramidal coordination geometry: the first 3-coordinated Cu center has three Br anions; the second 3-coordinated Cu center is formed by one Br anion, one C atom of the cyano group and one N atom of the quinuclidine ring of CBC; the third Cu center has a distorted tetrahedron composed of two N atoms from different cyanide groups, one N atom of the 4-cyanobenzyl group of the CBC cation. Of particular interest is the presence of a tetrahedral Cu center, where the CBC cation is used as a bidentate linker to bridge two Cu centers to yield a 3D framework (Fig. 4). It is interesting to note that the topology of this framework reveals a 2-fold interpenetrating net with an unprecedented “Chinese Ancient Harp-Typed” net (Fig. 5).7

Given that the MOFs 1 and 2 crystallize in a chiral space group (C2) that belongs to a polar point group (C2), their optical properties were studied. Preliminary examinations of powdered samples indicate that 1 and 2 are both SHG (second harmonic generation) active in which MOF 1 has approximate responses 20.0 times that of KDP (KH2PO4), and MOF 2 is similar to KDP. The coordination of the CBQ cation leads to a large separation of the charge in 1: The positive charge is sited on the N atom of the quinuclidine ring while the negative charge is delocalized in CuI3(CN)3(Br), in comparison to the free CBQ cation and Br− anion pair. This increased charge separation perhaps leads to a large dipole moment µ (µ = q × d, q is charge; d is distance) in 1, and consequently strong SHG activity of 1. Of particular interest is that MOF 2 adopts a 2-fold interpenetrating 3D topological net, so that µ rapidly decreases due to an anti-parallel arrangement. These two structural features were further confirmed by the ferroelectric measurements to be discussed below.

The space group C2 is associated with the point group C2, one of the 10 polar point groups (C1, C2, Cs, C2ν, C4, C4ν, C3, C3ν, C6, C6ν) required for ferroelectric behavior. Experimental results indicate that 1 does indeed display good ferroelectric behavior, while 2 also shows ferroelectric responses (see ESI‡). Fig. 6 clearly shows there is an electric hysteresis loop with a remanent polarization (Pr) of 0.0048 µC cm−2 and coercive field (Ec) of ca. 11 kV cm−1 in 1. It is a typical ferroelectric feature.7 The saturation spontaneous polarization (Ps) of 1 is ca. 0.0182 µC cm−2, significantly smaller than that found in ferroelectric KDP with a Ps of 5.0 µC cm−2.

Furthermore, in the dielectric measurement, a peak of dielectric loss was observed and the peak temperature shifts to higher temperature with increasing measurement frequency in MOF 1(Fig. 7). Where Tp is the temperature of the peak, ω = 2πf, while f is the measuring frequency. Thus activation energy H and relaxation time τo can be estimated from Fig. 7. The average H and τo are ca. 0.41 eV and 1.60 × 10−5 (s), respectively. These suggest that the relaxation process (peak) is probably associated with a dipolar layer vibration, in a good agreement with the presence of a 2D ordered layered structural framework. It is interesting to note that the permittivity at low frequencies reaches a maximum value (1.8 × 106, at −0.9 °C, 100 Hz, see ESI‡), while it rapidly drops by 1000-fold at a relatively high frequency (103 Hz). This is similar to that found in perovskite-related oxide CaCu3Ti4O12 (105) which displays a 1000-fold reduction.7 This discrepancy suggests the presence of a dipolar layer relaxation at low frequencies in a good agreement with the SHG and dielectric loss measurements because permittivity (ε) = ε1(ω) − iε2(ω), where ε1(ω) and ε2(ω) are the real (dielectric constant) and imaginary (dielectric loss) parts, respectively. The frequency dependence of the dielectric constant ε1 (ε0 = 119.3 as estimated from the slope of Fig. 8) at two temperatures indicates that ε1 rapidly drops with an increase in frequency, further supporting the presence of a dipolar layer relaxation process. A MOF with such a high dielectric constant, to the best of our knowledge, is unprecedented.

The present work points to a new approach to 2D ordered ferroelectric materials through the combination of charged building blocks and the use of hydrothermal techniques.
