Introduction

Single crystal to single crystal (SCSC) transformation has received considerable attention in the solid state reaction and crystal engineering.1,2 However, the instances of inorganic and metallo-organic compounds exhibiting reversible SCSC transformation are still scarce because they involve the cooperative movement of atoms or molecules in the solid state,2,3 leading to the loss of single crystallinity. Among them, 3D coordination polymers with high stability and porous crystalline have been widely used to examine the SCSC transformation,2 which are usually triggered by light, temperature, guest desorption/adsorption or anion exchange. There are a few reports on the 2D open framework materials that act as such flexible and dynamic porous materials when they respond to external stimulation and result in structural transformation either within the layer or between the layers.4,5 In the latter case, the slide between the 2D layers sometimes results in considerable increase in the dimensions of channels that are found to selectively adsorb/desorb guest molecules.4a This provides a new strategy for the design and synthesis of the flexible, open framework materials that can be applied in ion exchange,6 selective adsorption and separation of small molecules.7

Polypyridyl ligands have been widely used for the construction of metallo-supramolecular assembly.8 Among them, the rigid polydentate ligands containing a triazine ring as a bridge have attracted greatly our attention due to their coordination diversity.9 Although the symmetric ligand2,4,6-tris(4-pyridyl)-1,3,5-triazine (TPT, Scheme 1) has been widely observed in coordination chemistry by Fujita and others,10–12 the instances for such asymmetric ligand are rare. Champness et al. have reported the assembly of the angular ligand 2,4-bi-(4-pyiridyl)-1,3,5-triazine (BPT) with a rigid 120° angle between the central triazine ring and the two 4-pyridineN-donors that could potentially provide both discrete and divergent products upon metal complexation under appropriate conditions.13,14 Recently, a multidentate ligand4,6-bis(2-pyridyl)-1,3,5-triazin-2-ol (2-HOBPT) and its cobalt(II) complex, [Co(H2O)6⊂Co8(2-OBPT)12](NO3)6·12H2O, have been synthesized by us,15 in which the ligand offers a rare, bis-bipyridine-like binding mode with the capability of bridging two cobalt(II) ions in a chelating manner.16 Furthermore, the OH group accepts hydrogen bonding from the [Co(H2O)6]2+ ion that embeds in the central cavity as a guest and plays an indispensable role in the cage structure formation. As part of an ongoing study, we extend the insight into the asymmetric ligand4,6-bis(4-pyridyl)-1,3,5-triazin-2-ol (HOBPT), in which a potential coordination group OH is introduced. We hope that the HOBPT ligand could possibly generate new architectures that would exploit the new motifs of this kind of ligand. This is indeed the case. In this contribution, the synthesis and structural characterization of two new, isomorphous compounds [M(OBPT)2]·0.6H2O (M = Co (1) and Ni (2)) have been reported. Indeed, ligand HOBPT is deprotonated and coordinates to metal ions in an uncommon, N,O-exo-bidentate chelating mode. Markedly, they exhibit reversible SCSC transformation triggered by thermal stimulation involving adsorption/desorption of water. Furthermore, the dehydrated phase can selectively adsorb water over organic solvents.

Results and discussion

Crystal structures of the as-synthesized and rehydrated compounds

The crystal structures of the as-synthesized and rehydrated compounds are exactly the same, and this also confirms the complete reversibility of dehydration/rehydration process. Therefore, only the as-synthesized compounds are employed as representations to be described in detail. Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analyses reveal that complexes 1 and 2 are isomorphous structures and crystallize in the monoclinic P21/c space group. As shown in Fig. 1, each metal ion lies on an inversion center and is coordinated by two nitrogen atoms of triazine rings (2.174(2) Å for 1 and 2.127(2) Å for 2) and two oxygen atoms (2.083(2) Å for 1 and 2.074(2) Å for 2, see Table 1) from two OBPT ligands arranged trans to each other on the equatorial plane and two nitrogen atoms (2.145(2) Å for 1 and 2.090(2) Å for 2) of pyridine rings from the other two ligands occupying the apical coordination sites to furnish a distorted octahedral geometry. The two N,O chelating rings from two OBPT ligands are coplanar, and the metal ion is also on the plane. The main distortion of the resulting octahedral coordination geometry originates from the small bite angle of the chelating ligand (63.17(6)° for 1 and 64.46(8)° for 2).The bond length of M-N(py) is significantly shorter than that of M-N(triazine), indicating that the nitrogen atom of the pyridine ring has a stronger coordination ability. It should be pointed out that the ligand is deprotonated and tridentate, adopting an unusual N,O-exo-bidentate chelating and N-monodentate mode.17 The other nitrogen atom of the pyridine ring is uncoordinated, though it has a stronger coordination ability than the nitrogen atom of the triazine ring.

Each ligand bridges two metal ions and each metal ion links to four ligands, resulting in a (4,4) 2D framework with rhombic cavities,18 as shown in Fig. 2a. The M⋯M distances across the OBPT ligands and through the diagonals are 10.90, 12.60 and 17.80 Å for 1, and 10.83, 12.83 and 17.44 Å for 2, respectively. Furthermore, the uncoordinated pyridine rings are vertically positioned up and down the layer, which are further linked to each other into a 3D hydrogen-bonded network viahydrogen-bonded interaction (O–H⋯N = 2.83 Å for 1 and 2.79 Å for 2) between the lattice water molecules and the uncoordinated nitrogen atoms of the pyridine rings (see Fig. 2b). The interlayer distances linked by water molecules are 16.01 Å for 1 and 16.05 Å for 2. Interestingly, three sets of the hydrogen-bonded networks wrap each other generating a three-fold interpenetration structure (see Fig. 2c and 2d). Although two uncoordinated pyridine rings from the two neighbouring layers pass through each rhombic cavity, no significant π–π interaction was found between the aromatic rings because of their off-set stacking.

Crystal structures of the dehydrated compounds (1a and 2a).

Interestingly, heating the single crystals of 1 and 2 at 200 °C for 2 h under ambient atmosphere result in the formation of their dehydrated forms, 1a and 2a, respectively. Their crystal structures were determined by single-crystal X-ray diffraction and the completed removal of the water molecule was also confirmed by elemental and TG analyses (see Fig S1 and S2 in ESI†). Their key structural parameters are summarized in Table 2. They have identical space groups and coordination geometries with the as-synthesized compounds 1 and 2. However, they display certain extent differences in unit cells, 2D frameworks and interlayer distances. Upon dehydration, the a- and c-axes are slightly shrunk, in contrast, the b-axis is expanded. Finally, their volumes are shrunk about 1.8% for 1a and 2.6% for 2a. Although the M⋯M distances across the OBPT ligands (10.90 Å for 1a and 10.81 Å for 2a) are almost the same as the as-synthesized compounds 1 and 2, the internal angles of the rhombic frame are changed from 69.4 and 110.6° to 70.6 and 109.4° in 1a, and 70.7 and 109.3° to 72.7 and 107.3° in 2a. The uncoordinated pyridine rings are vertically set up and down the layer, and intercalate into the rhombic cavities, packing in an ABC motif. Additionally, the interlayer distances of A⋯A are shrunk to 15.83 Å for 1a and 15.84 Å for 2a, respectively. These observations are in agreement with the decrease of their volumes.19

Synthesis and characterization

The ligand HOBPT was prepared by the reaction of 4-cyanopyridine and urea in the presence of sodium hydride as a catalyst in dimethyl sulfoxide solution according to the synthetic procedure of 2-HOBPT.15 It slightly dissolves in methanol and ethanol, but has a good solubility in DMF and DMSO. A hydrothermal reaction of Co(NO3)2·6H2O or Ni(NO3)2·6H2O with HOBPT in a water–ethanol mixture solution at 100 °C for 3 d afforded yellow crystals of 1 or green crystals of 2, in which the ligand is deprotonated and bridges two metal ions into a (4,4) 2D coordination polymer. It should be pointed out that the ligand is tridentate, adopting an unusual N,O-exo-bidentate chelating and monodentate mode,17 and only one nitrogen atom of the pyridine ring is coordinated.

Furthermore, other factors, such as reaction temperature, different metal salts as the starting material and solvent, were observed to examine their effects on the reaction. Firstly, the optimal reaction temperature ranges from 90 to 120 °C. At this temperature range, the product crystallizes in a pure phase with high yield (≥90% on the basis of HOBPT). High temperature (above 140 °C) induced low yield because a part or all hydrolyzed ligands lead to the appearance of by-products. Secondly, other cobalt(II) or nickel(II) salts were employed as the starting material. When other bivalent metal salts such as MCl2, M(CH3CO2)2·4H2O and MSO4·xH2O (M = Co and Ni) were used as the source of metal salt under the reaction conditions, the same products were obtained. Finally, different solvents were also examined. Using water or the mixture solutions of water and ethanol as solvent, products were obtained in good yield. However, no crystal was obtained in the presence of an unique solvent of methanol or ethanol, and only microcrystals were afforded in the case of water as an unique reaction solvent. This seems to suggest that the mixture solvent is necessary to obtain the appropriate single crystal and the addition of alcohol is superior in promoting crystallinity in the products.

Thermal gravimetric analyses (TGA) were carried out from 25–800 °C to examine the lattice water molecule and stability. The weight losses of 2.5% for 1 and 2.3% for 2 in the range of 25∼200 °C (see Fig. S1 and S3 in the ESI†) correspond to the loss of a 0.6 lattice water molecule (calculated 2.0%), indicating that the guest water molecules were completely removed at 200 °C. Moreover, there is a plateau extending to approximately 450 °C. After that, the 2D frameworks begin to decompose, indicating that the dehydrated 2D coordination frameworks are stable up to 450 °C. TGA studies of 1a and 2a reveal that there is no loss in the range of 25∼450 °C (see Fig. S2 and S4 in the ESI†), indicating that the guest water molecules in 1 and 2 were completely removed by heating at 200 °C for 2 h. These are consistent with the result from single crystal structural analyses.

Reversible SCSC transformation triggered by thermal treatment

Powder X-ray diffraction (XRD) was used to check the structural identity and possible phase transition of the sample. The powder XRD patterns of the as-synthesized products closely match the simulated ones from the single-crystal data, indicating that they are pure. Furthermore, powder XRD measurements at different temperatures were also performed to examine the robustness and thermal stability of the 2D framework upon removal of the guest molecules. Fig. 3 shows the XRD patterns of the as-synthesized sample of 1 at 25, 150, 250, and 350 °C, respectively. It is clear that the diffraction profiles below 350 °C are almost the same, indicating that such 2D framework is stable under this temperature and the crystal lattice remains intact after removal of the guest water molecules. The high stability of the 2D framework prompts us to examine the reversible SCSC transformation triggered by thermal treatment. Although many 3D coordination polymers have exhibited such behavior,2 2D open framework material with reversible SCSC transformation is still rare.4,5 Indeed, when the as-synthesized single crystals 1 were heated at 200 °C for 2 h or at 350 °C for 15 min, the crystals remained single and transparent. Its XRD pattern measured at room temperature closely matches the simulated one based on the single-crystal data of dehydrated 1a (see Fig. 3). On the other hand, the thermal treatment single crystals 1a were exposed to ambient air (humidity ca. 60%) at room temperature for 3 d, 1′ was obtained. Its XRD pattern matches well that of 1, indicating 1a rehydrates to 1′. These reversible processes are repeated several times. Similar cases were found in complex 2 (see Fig. S4†).

Adsorption and desorption of water over organic solvents

To examine the adsorption and desorption properties of 1, two sets of experiments were designed. Firstly, the as-synthesized 1 was immersed in different solvents, such as EtOH, MeOH, CH3COCH3, THF, C6H6, CH3CN, CHCl3, to examine whether the lattice water molecules can be exchanged or not. Surprisingly, the powder XRD patterns of the crystals immersed in MeOH and EtOH change and match well that of the simulated 1a (see Fig. 4), however, the others are unaltered, indicating that 1 transforms to dehydrated 1a and the guest water molecules are extracted by alcohol. The single crystal structural analyses and TGA studies also confirm that they are dehydrated. This may be attributed to the formation of strong hydrogen bondings between the lattice water molecules and methanol or ethanol molecules, which pulls out the lattice water molecules.

On the other hand, crystalline samples of 1 were heated at 200 °C for 2 h, affording 1a and were then immersed in a variety of wet solvents (H2O, EtOH, MeOH, CH3COCH3, THF, C6H6, CH3CN, CHCl3, CH2Cl2, and H2O–MeOH) for 3 d. As shown in Fig. 5, the powder XRD patterns of the crystals immersed in H2O, CH3COCH3, THF, C6H6, CH3CN, CHCl3, and CH2Cl2 are almost the same as that of the as-synthesized 1, indicating 1a can adsorb guest water molecules over the organic solvents and recover the original framework. In contrast, the powder XRD patterns of the crystals immersed in EtOH and MeOH are sustained and match well that of the dehydrated 1a, indicating 1a can not adsorb water molecules from EtOH and MeOHsolvents. This is consistent with the aforementioned observation that methanol can extract the lattice water molecules from 1. However, the dehydrated 1a can retrieve water molecules from H2O–MeOH (1 : 1) solution, this was confirmed by single crystal structural determination and the TGA experiment. All these demonstrate that the dehydrated sample 1a can selectively adsorb water over organic solvents except alcohols. Although traditional zeolite desiccants have been widely utilized in the removal of water molecules from organic solvents and some 3D porous MOFs have exhibited the adsorption of water over organic solvents,20 compound 1 is a rare instance with a 2D open framework displaying reversible dehydration/rehydration over organic solvents, in particular, it can dehydrate in alcoholsolvent at room temperature.

Conclusions

Two (4,4) 2D frameworks with rhombic cavities have been successfully synthesized under hydrothermal conditions, in which the ligand is deprotonated and coordinates to metal ions in an uncommon, N,O-exo-bidentate chelating mode. They are highly stable up to 450 °C and exhibit reversible single crystal to single crystal transformation triggered by thermal treatment involving a dehydration and rehydration processes. Hydrogen-bonded interactions between the lattice water molecules and the uncoordinated pyridine rings are conceivably the key to the conversion of 2D coordination polymers1a and 2a to the corresponding 3D hydrogen-bonded frameworks 1 and 2. Moreover, they selectively adsorb/desorb water molecules over organic solvents.

Experimental

Materials and physical technique

The reagents and solvents employed were commercially available and were used as received without further purification. The C, H and N microanalyses were carried out with a Vario EL elemental analyzer. The FT-IR spectra were recorded from KBr pellets in the range of 400–4000 cm−1 on a Bruker TENSOR 27 spectrometer. 1H NMR spectra were recorded on a Varian 300 MHz spectrometer at 25 °C. Electron spray ionization (ESI) mass spectra were obtained on a LCQ DECA XP quadrupole ion trapmass spectrometer with methanol as the carrier solvent. Thermogravimetric data were collected on a Netzsch TGS-2 analyzer in nitrogen atmosphere at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. Powder X-ray diffraction patterns were recorded on a D8 ADVANCE diffractometer with Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5409 Å) at a scanning rate of 4° min−1 with 2θ ranging from 5 to 40°.

Synthesis of HOBPT

The title ligand was prepared according to previously reported procedures.15 Yield 78%. Synthesis of [M(OBPT)2]·0.6H2O (1 and 2)

The mixture was heated to 100 °C for 72 h, and then cooled to room temperature at a rate of 5 °C h−1. The yellow block crystals were collected by filtration and dried at ambient temperature. Dehydration and rehydration of 1 and 2

All experiments were carried out on single crystals of the compounds. It should be pointed out that none of the dehydration and rehydration experiments as well as single-crystal X-ray data collections were carried out on one and the same crystal. Different sets of single crystals of 1 and 2 were heated at 200 °C for 2 h under ambient atmosphere. After cooling, the dehydrated crystals 1a and 2a were immediately covered with Paratone-N oil for protection from rehydration and were measured by single-crystal X-ray diffraction. At this temperature, the crystals remained single and transparent. The chemical formulae of the dehydrated samples 1a and 2a derived from the structure determinations are [Co(OBPT)2] and [Ni(OBPT)2]. Anal. Calcd for C26H16CoN10O21a: C, 55.82, H, 2.88, N, 25.04%. Found: C, 55.70, H, 3.02, N, 24.89%. For C26H16NiN10O22a: C, 55.82, H, 2.88, N, 25.04%. Found: C, 55.67, H, 3.11, N, 24.92%. On the other hand, as-synthesied 1 and 2 were immersed in methanol solution for 1 d, and the dehydrated 1a and 2a were also obtained.

For the rehydration process the dehydrated single crystals 1a and 2a were exposed to ambient air (humidity ca. 60%) at room temperature, respectively. At these conditions they turned back to the original complexes 1′ and 2′ in 3 d. Moreover, the dehydrated crystals 1a and 2a were, respectively, immersed in a mixture solution of water–methanol (1 : 1), they also turned back to 1′ and 2′ in 2 d. Anal. Calcd for C26H17.2CoN10O2.61′: C, 54.76, H, 3.04, N, 24.56%. Found: C, 54.47, H, 3.16, N, 24.41%. For C26H18NiN10O32′: C, 54.78, H, 3.04, N, 24.57%. Found: C, 54.35, H, 3.23, N, 24.43%.

X-Ray crystallography

Diffraction intensities for 1, 1a, 1′, 2, 2a and 2′ were collected at 293 K on a Bruker Smart Apex CCD diffractometer with graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). Absorption corrections were applied using SADABS.21 The structures were solved by direct methods and refined with the full-matrix least-squares technique using SHELXS-97 and SHELXTL-97 programs, respectively.22 Anisotropic thermal parameters were applied to all non-hydrogen atoms. The organic hydrogen atoms were generated geometrically (C–H 0.96 Å and N–H 0.86 Å). The hydrogen atoms of the water molecules were located from difference maps and refined with isotropic temperature factors.
