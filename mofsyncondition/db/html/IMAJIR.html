Abstract

Although TTF coordination complexes with transition metals have been extensively studied, coordination chemistry of p-block metal ions with TTF ligands remains largely unexplored. In this study, two Pb(II) complexes of 2,6-bis(4′-pyridyl)tetrathiafulvalene (py-TTF-py) with co-coordinated 9-anthracenecarboxylate (AC), formulated as [Pb(AC)2(py-TTF-py)2] (1), {[Pb(AC)2(py-TTF-py)0.5]·CH3CN}n (2), and a transition metal Cd(II) compound [Cd(AC)2(py-TTF-py)2]n (3) for comparison are characterized by single-crystal X-ray diffraction. Compound 1 is a monomer and 3 is a 1-D compound. Compound 2 is a 2-D coordination polymer with planar mesh-like structure. They are metal linked electron donor and fluorescent acceptor system. Traced by the intensity of fluorescence quenching of AC ligand, it was found that Pb(II) ion is a good intermediary for ligand-to-ligand charge-transfer in this system.

1. Introduction

One of the currently active research areas to both chemists and physicists is to rationally design and construct multifunctional materials with novel structures and intriguing properties. Metal-organic coordination compounds provide an attracting approach in this area via combination of specific properties of metal ions and ligands. The chemical and physical characteristics of ligands play an important role in regulating the properties of novel functional compounds [1], [2]. Tetrathiafulvalene (TTF) and its derivatives are a sulfur-rich π-electron system and can be oxidized to the corresponding radical cation and dication species sequentially [3]. They are successfully used as building blocks for molecular conductors [4], molecular switches [5], field effect transistors [6] and solar-energy conversion systems [7].

For TTF-metal complexes, a type of commonly called “π–d” systems has been extensively studied in which TTF provides mobile π electrons and paramagnetic metal ions supply localized d or f electrons so that the synergy between π and d/f electron interactions could be reached to obtain conductive magnetic materials [4], [8]. In comparison with transition metals and lanthanide metals, coordination chemistry of p-block metal ions with TTF ligands remains largely unexplored. To our knowledge, structural data available are only limited to a TTF connected crown-Pb(II) complex [9a] and a TTF-Tin(IV) dithiolate complex [9b].

In order to study the properties of TTF main-group metal complexes, lead ion is chosen in our work because lead(II) compounds are particularly relevant in the coordination chemistry studies of heavy p-block elements. Lead(II) ion can adopt alterable coordination number and geometry with or without a stereochemically active lone pair electrons and therefore could exhibit interesting topological arrangements [10]. 2,9-Bis(4′-pyridyl)tetrathiafulvalene (py-TTF-py) is selected as a donor ligand which is an analogue of 4,4′-bipyridine but has effective intramolecular charge-transfer property [11]. Unlike the diversity of the crystal structures of the coordination compounds with the analogue ligand 4,4′-bipyridine [12], there are only four coordination compounds with py-TTF-py crystallographically characterized: two molecular complexes with Ir2 + and Co2 + and two one-dimensional (1-D) coordination polymers with Mn2 + and Cu2 + [13]. Herein, three py-TTF-py complexes 1–3 from 0-D to 2-D co-coordinated with 9-anthracenecarboxylate (AC) are synthesized by solvothermal technique and characterized by single-crystal X-ray diffraction (SI materials). 1 and 2 are Pb(II) complexes and 3 is a Cd(II) complex as a contrast to study the difference between main-group metal and transition metal coordination. The crystal data and structural refinement parameters of 1–3 are listed in SI-Table 1. Their structures are discussed focusing on the coordination geometries affected by the lone pair electrons of Pb(II). They are metal linked electron donor and fluorescent acceptor system. Luminescent properties of the Pb(II) complexes are investigated compared with that of the Cd(II) compound. This is the first study of the role of main-group metal ion on TTF charge-transfer property using 9-anthracenecarboxylate as a fluorescent indicator.

Compound 1 is a monomer, taking approximately C2v symmetry (Fig. 1). The Pb(II) is six-coordinated by two py-TTF-py ligands and two chelate carboxylate groups from AC molecules. The coordinated O and N atoms are confined mostly in one hemisphere (Fig. 1) due to the presence of 6s2 lone pair electrons of lead atom. The Pb1–O1 (2.326 Å) bond that is opposite to the position of the lone pair electrons is much shorter than the Pb1–O2 (2.763 Å) bond that is adjacent to the lone pair electrons in 1. The TTF moiety is almost a planar structure with the mean deviation from plane of 0.0286 Ǻ. The dihedral angles between least-squares planes of the TTF and pyridine ring are 23.04° for coordinated pyridine and 8.74° for uncoordinated pyridine. Except weak C–H ⋯ O and C–H ⋯ C intermolecular interactions, there is no other important interactions.

Compound 2 is a 2-D coordination polymer. The asymmetric unit is shown in Fig. S1a. The Pb(II) center is seven-coordinated by six O atoms from carboxylate groups and one N atom from py-TTF-py. The coordination geometry of 2 is also lopsided, but not confined strictly in a hemisphere as 1 due to the increased coordination member. Similar lone pair electrons effect on PbO bond distances for 2 was observed. The shorter PbO bond of about 2.38 Å in 2 also appear on the opposite side of the lone pair electrons position. The general PbO bond distances fall within the range of 2.379–2.843 Å and the PbN bond distance is 2.589 Å. Only μ2-η2:η1 mode of carboxylate group of AC is found in 2. The carboxylate groups of ligand AC in compound 2 connect Pb(II) atoms to form an infinite 1-D chain structure (Fig. 2a) and then the chains are joined by the pyridine groups on two sides of the py-TTF-py ligand forming a 2-D network in [6], [3] topology (Fig. 2b, c). The TTF moiety is a planar structure that links two pyridine groups with the dihedral angles between least-squares planes of the TTF and pyridine ring 33.47° and 19.70° for 2. There is short C···S contact (3.357 Å) between AC and py-TTF-py in 2.

The structure of Cd(II) compound 3 is a 1-D chain, in which the asymmetric units (Fig. S1b) are bridged through py-TTF-py as shown in Fig. 3. The d10 Cd(II) is six coordinated and the sphere takes a distorted octahedral geometry, which is completely different from the d10S2 Pb(II) compounds. A weak coordination is observed for the mono-coordinated AC ligand (bond length Cd1–O4, 2.897 Å). The CdN bond distances, 2.349 and 2.430 Å, in 3 are shorter than those in Pb(II) compounds for the smaller ionic radium of Cd(II). The mono coordinated py-TTF-py molecule in 3 is planar and stacked each other with those in the neighboring parallel chains to form a column with the inter py-TTF-py distances 3.48 and 3.76 Å (Fig. S2). There are short C ⋯ S interchain contacts (3.484 Å) between AC and py-TTF-py.

Crystal samples of 1–3 are all air stable and were used in general characterizations. (Fig. 3). The XRD patterns of microcrystal samples of 1–3 are in agreement with the simulated patterns from the crystal data of compounds (Fig. S3) to confirm the identity and purity of all the samples. Thermogravimetry analysis of 2 (Fig. S4) indicates that the cocrystallized acetonitrile molecule gradually loses before 200 °C and the compound is stable below 250 °C. A vigorous decomposition happens in the range of 250 to 400 °C, corresponding to the decomposition of the organic ligands. Two redox waves correspond to the TTF/TTF+ and TTF+/TTF2 + redox couples appeared (Fig. S5). Optical diffuse-reflection spectra of 1–3 together with free ligands py-TTF-py and AC as used for comparison in solid state were measured at room temperature (Fig. 4). The absorption of AC has two characteristic bands correspond to the π–π* transitions at 264 nm and 367–404 nm. The absorption of py-TTF-py ligand shows three set bands around 280, 340, and 480 nm, separately. The lowest energy band is assigned as the intramolecular π–π* transition, which is shifted to about 520 nm for compound 3, while for compounds 1 and 2, the band shows no remarkable shift. The red-shift recorded for compound 3 is related to the molecular packing structure, because stacked TTF columns are only found in the structure of compound 3. Energy gap of the frontier orbitals is significantly narrowed when the π electrons of py-TTF-py molecules are delocalized (overlapped). The bands of the py-TTF-py ligand at lower than 400 nm are changed due to the formation of coordination compounds with AC ligand.

The ligand 9-anthracenecarboxylate (AC) displays a strong fluorescence-emission featuring the band in the range of 375–550 nm, and its fluorescence quenching is widely used in fluorescence detectors and sensors. The fluorescence properties of compounds 1 and 3 are studied to understand the electron transfer within the compounds in which the ratio of py-TTF-py to AC is the same (1:1). The fluorescence spectra of 1 and 3 along with the free AC were measured at room temperature. In general, enhancement of luminescence is observed for the d10 metal complexes that is attributed to the chelating and/or bridging effects of the 9-AC to the metal centers, which effectively increases the rigidity of the ligands and reduces the loss of energy via luminescenceless pathway [14]. Upon photoexcitation, the fluorescence of all the two compounds is significantly quenched (Fig. 5a), which indicates that there is photoinduced charge-transfer from py-TTF-py to fluorescent AC ligands (LLCT) [15]. The stronger the CT between py-TTT-py and AC is, the weaker the fluorescence should be. The fluorescence intensity of Pb(II) compound 1 is lower than that of Cd(II) compound 3 measured in a strictly consistent condition. The result may be explained in two aspects: (1) Pb(II) ion is a more effective bridge for py-TTF-py to AC charge-transfer (LLCT) than Cd(II) ion, and (2) the heavy atom quenching effect of Pb(II) ion exists. Addition of Fe(ClO4)3 to the solution of 1 and 3 led to fluorescence recovery, and the fluorescence intensity increased with the increasing amounts of Fe(ClO4)3 (Fig. 5b, c), because the electron-donating ability of TTF unit is lowered upon the TTF being oxidized to TTF+. The fluorescence recovery suggests that the dominant quenching mechanism is (1): the intramolecular LLCT induced quenching. The fluorescence emission band at 450 nm disappears for solid-state crystal samples, which indicate that the CT induced quenching is nearly completed.

2. Conclusion

In order to study the properties of TTF main-group metal compounds, two Pb(II) complexes of py-TTF-py with co-coordinated 9-anthracenecarboxylate (AC), along with a transition metal Cd(II) analogue as a contrast, are synthesized by solvothermal technique synthesis and characterized by single-crystal X-ray diffraction. The coordination geometries of the Pb(II) ion is rather lopsided due to the effect of active 6s2 lone pair electrons. They are metal linked electron donor and fluorescent acceptor system. Traced by the intensity of fluorescence quenching of AC ligand, it was found that Pb(II) ion in d10S2 electron configuration is a good ligand-to-ligand charge-transfer intermediary in comparison with that of d10 Cd(II) ion for TTF system. This study might awake the interest in the charge-transfer chemistry of tetrathiafulvalene derivatives with the main-group metals.
