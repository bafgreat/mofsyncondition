Introduction

Thanks to the construction of inorganic metal connectors with organic linkers, metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have incomparable advantages in structural flexibility, tunability and charge variability.1 Recently, lanthanide MOFs (Ln-MOFs) have attracted interest due to their potential for applications in catalysis,2 adsorption,3 photoluminescence,4,5 sensing,6 light-emitting devices7,8 and so forth. Temperature (T), one of seven fundamental physical parameters, is used in all kinds of science. Among the methods of sensing T, luminescent T sensors are based on the use of luminescent probes and possess the unique advantages of contactless measurement and large-scale imaging. Luminescent probes can be incorporated into the sample by depositing them on the surface or containing them with a polymer host to form a thin film.9 MOF materials are important luminescent probes that show T-dependent emission and are capable of sensing T by detecting intensity or decay time. However, at present, the practical application front of research on MOFs, such as MOF sensors, is progressing rather slowly, mainly due to the low mechanical strength and brittleness of MOFs. In this regard, composite materials of MOFs with polymers offer promising routes to overcoming the inherent weaknesses of MOFs.10 Several methods of forming such MOF-polymer composites into films have been developed, including solution casting, spin-coating, direct growth from solvothermal mother solutions, MOF seeding, stepwise layer growth of MOFs deposited on a matrix, counter-diffusion, electrochemical deposition and gel-layer approaches.11,12

With the need of improving the MOF mechanical flexibility, we investigated how a lot of polymeric matrices blend with our MOF materials. Polyether sulfone (PES) distinguishes itself as a class of thermoplastic, specially engineered to have high thermal, oxidative and hydrolytic stability and good resistance to acids, alkalis, salts, oils and greases. Therefore, it is an ideal matrix material to host MOFs to form composites.13,14 Indeed, there are a few examples of MOF@PES composite films that have been reported for Uio-66,15 ZIF-8,16 MOF-5 and Zn/Co-MOF-74.17,18

In this contribution, Eu3+ reacted with 2,6-Naphthalene dicarboxylic acid (H2NDC) via ionothermal processes to form a 3D Ln-MOF, [Eu(NDC)(H2O)Cl] (1), which displays thermosensitive fluorescence and high physicochemical stability. 1 was processed with PES to prepare 1@PES composite films with various loadings of 1. The fluorescence and mechanical properties of the films showed systematic variation with the loading of 1. The possibility of applying 1@PES as a thermal sensor in an alarm device was tested.

Experimental

Materials and methods

The reagents and solvents were used as supplied commercially without further purification except that four 1-methyl-3-alkylimidazolium iodine ionic liquids (ILs) were synthesized from reactions of 1-alkyl iodine (alkyl = ethyl, propyl, butyl, amyl) with 1-methylimidazole according to literature processes.19 Degassed 1-alkyl iodine was refluxed with distilled 1-methylimidazole to give the four kinds of ILs. The ILs were washed with ethyl acetate and then dried under a vacuum for at least 10 h to give [EMI]I as a yellow solid and [PMI]I, [BMI]I and [AMI]I as yellow oils.

Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) data for the materials were collected at ambient temperature with a Rigaku MiniFlex 600 (Japan) diffractometer (Cu-Kα1,2 X-radiation, λ1 = 1.540598 Å and λ2 = 1.544426 Å), equipped with an X'Celerator detector and a flat plate sample holder in a Bragg–Brentano para-focusing optics configuration (40 kV, 15 mA). Intensity data were collected using the step counting method (a step being 0.02°) in continuous mode in the 2θ range of 5–60°. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was carried out under a N2 atmosphere on a SDT Q600 V8.3 Build 101 instrument with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 and a N2 flow rate of 20 cm3 min−1. Fluorescence spectra were acquired on a Hitachi F-4600 fluorescence spectrophotometer with the excitation and emission slits set at 5 nm. The temperature-dependent fluorescence spectra were recorded using the same spectrophotometer with the same setup used for solid-state fluorescence and the temperature was increased and decreased in the range of 25–200 °C. The morphologies of the 1@PES films were observed using a Hitachi TM3030 tabletop SEM using the conventional measurement mode with an accelerating voltage of 15 kV and a filament current of 1850 mA. Tensile strength and breaking elongation data were acquired on a Q800DMA dynamic viscoelastometer. Elemental analysis of C, H and N was carried out on a Vario EL III elemental analyzer. FT-IR spectra were collected from KBr pellets (Aldrich, >99%, FT-IR grade) with a Bruker Tensor 27 FT-IR spectrometer in the range of 4000–400 cm−1.

Synthesis of [Eu(NDC)(H2O)Cl] (1)

1.0 mmol, 0.3667 g EuCl3·6H2O and 0.5 mmol, 0.1083 g H2NDC were placed in a crystallisation vial placed in a 25 mL teflon-lined stainless-steel autoclave and mixed with 1.5 mL [PMI]I. The mixture was kept inside a furnace at 160 °C for 5 days and then cooled to ambient temperature. Colorless crystals suitable for X-ray diffraction were collected after washing with ethanol. Yield based on H2NDC: 0.1872 g, 89.2%. Elemental analysis (%), Found (calcd): C, 34.70(34.33); H, 2.35(1.91). FT-IR data (in KBr, cm−1) for 1: 3429(m), 3340(w), 1975(w), 1836(w), 1607(s), 1547(s), 1490(s), 1423(s), 1359(m), 1201(m), 922(m), 786(s), 572(m), 484(m), 441(m).

1 was also produced by the 1 : 1 molar ratio of EuCl3·6H2O and H2NDC with 1.5042 g [EMI]I, 1.5 mL [BMI]I or 1.5 mL [AMI]I.

Synthesis of 1@PES composite films

1@PES composite films were prepared using a solution casting approach according to the literature.20,21 3.52 g PES was dissolved into 22 mL dimethylacetamide (DMAc) with stirring for 45 min to obtain a PES solution of 0.16 g L−1. 1 mL PES solutions were mixed with 0.0178, 0.0400, 0.0686, 0.1067, 0.1600, 0.2400, 0.3733, and 0.6400 g of 1 to prepare suspensions of PES with 10–80% 1 with steps of 10%. 0.7 mL of the suspensions were casted onto glass sheets with a small brush and then left in the air to allow DMAc to evaporate in order to obtain 1@PES composite films with different loadings of 1.

Crystal structure determination

Single crystals of 1 were manually harvested from crystallisation vials and mounted on Hampton Research CryoLoops using FOMBLIN Y perfluoropolyether vacuum oil with the help of a Stemi 2000 stereomicroscope equipped with Carl Zeiss lenses. Data were collected on a Bruker D8 Quest CCD diffractometer equipped with a graphite-monochromated Mo-Kα radiation source (λ = 0.71073 Å) at 153(2) K and reduced using the ω scan technique and the Bruker XSCANS program. The crystal structure of 1 was solved using direct methods using the SHELXL-2014/7 package of crystallographic software and refined using the full-matrix least-squares technique on F2.22 All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. Hydrogen atoms were located at geometrically calculated positions relative to their carrier atoms and refined with isotropic thermal parameters included in the final stage of the refinement.

Crystallographic data (excluding structure factors) for 1 reported in this paper have been deposited with the Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre (the deposition CCDC number: 1828712†).

Results and discussion

Structural description

The ionothermal processes of EuCl3·6H2O reacting with H2NDC at ratios of 1 : 1 and 2 : 1 produced the same result of 1 with a range of 1-methyl-3-alkylimidazolium iodine ionic liquids as reaction media, which can be confirmed by the samples showing the same PXRD pattern (ESI, Fig. S1†). 1 is obtained as the only solid product with a high yield, whose structural model was solved using single crystal X-ray diffraction. A summary of the structural determination and refinement for 1 (ESI, Table S1†) and selected bond distances and angles are listed in the ESI, Table S2.†

The structural analysis indicates that 1 comprises one Eu(iii) center, one NDC2− ligand, one Cl− ion and one coordinated water molecule in its asymmetrical unit. The seven coordinated Eu1 center exhibits a monocapped octahedral geometry (ESI, Fig. S2†). The NDC2− ligand adopts a μ4-bis-bidentate bridging fashion binding four Eu(iii) centers (ESI, Fig. S3†). Two neighboring Eu1 centers are connected via two pairs of bidentate COO− bridges into a [Eu2(COO)2(H2O)2] subunit, and the [Eu2(COO)2(H2O)2] subunits are further connected by four μ2-Cl− bridges into a 2D (4,4) layer along the bc-plane (ESI, Fig. S4†). The remaining parts of the NDC2− ligands along the a-direction connect the 2D layers into a 3D architecture. The [Eu2(COO)2(H2O)2] subunit, Cl−, and the remaining part of the NDC2− ligand acted as 10-, 2- and 3-connected nodes respectively so that 1 can be simplified as a (2,3,10)-connected topological network with a Schläfli point symbol of {43}2{46·616·821·102}{6}2 (Fig. 1).

Characterization

PXRD

The experimental PXRD pattern for 1 matches well with the simulated one from the single crystal structure data, indicating that the bulk sample of 1 was isolated as a single crystal pure phase (ESI, Fig. S5†).

TG analysis

TGA was used to evaluate the thermal stability of 1, whose TG curve indicates that 1 remains stable up to 280 °C and that a two-step weight loss occurs in 1 (ESI, Fig. S6†). From 280–490 °C, a small weight loss of 5.5% happened first, which was a little higher than the calculated 4.3% as this weight loss is assigned to the loss of the coordinated water. Then, there is a major weight loss of 49.4% above 490 °C, indicating that the framework starts to decompose until 550 °C. The total weight loss of 54.9% from 280–550 °C is attributed to the loss of H2O and NDC2− ligands and is very close to the theoretical value of 55.3%. It also indicates that NDC2− ligands slightly decompose at 280–490 °C, but mainly at 490–550 °C.

Solid-state fluorescence

The solid-state emission spectrum of 1 was recorded at ambient temperature. Upon excitation at 271 nm, 1 displays broad emission at 450 nm due to intraligand charge transfer and sharp emissions at 580, 591, 613, 653 and 704 nm due to the 5D0 → 7F0, 5D0 → 7F1, 5D0 → 7F2, 5D0 → 7F3 and 5D0 → 7F4 transitions of the Eu3+ cation, respectively (Fig. 2).23,24 The red fluorescence of the Eu3+ center in 1 can be explained by the absorption by the NDC2− ligand and intramolecular ligand-to-metal energy transfer,5,25 thus causing the fluorescence of the Eu3+ center.

As is well known, broadband excitation is an advantage for a fluorescence sensor in potential commercial applications. The fluorescence intensity of 1 was measured by varying the excitation wavelength from 270 to 400 nm with steps of 10 nm (ESI, Fig. S7a†). The fluorescence intensity at 613 nm increased with the excitation wavelength and reached a maximum at 370 nm (ESI, Fig. S7b†). The broadband excitation of 1 suggests that it can be excited by a commercial UV lamp and is not limited to a specific excitation band.

T-Dependent fluorescence

As shown in the TGA curve, 1 is thermally stable up to 280 °C without weight loss. That allows T-dependent fluorescence measurements in the temperature range of 25–200 °C without causing structural changes. The fluorescence intensity of 1 at 613 nm decreases by 95% during heating to 125 °C but is fully recovered upon cooling to 25 °C (Fig. 3), indicating that 1 is a thermosensitive fluorescent compound. The sensitivity of the fluorescence intensity at 613 nm to T (defined as the slope of the signal change ΔS with T) is 1.18% K−1 in the range of 25–100 °C.26 As expected, the lifetime of Eu3+ in 1 decreases sharply to near 15 μs as the temperature increases to 150 °C (Fig. 3c and d) with the decay lifetime decreasing by 3.60 μs °C−1 due to the thermal activation of a nonradiative-decay pathway.27 To determine the luminescence mechanism of 1, a Gd-NDC MOF was synthesized to estimate the triple energy level of the H2NDC ligand. The low-temperature phosphorescence spectrum of Gd-NDC shows an emission at 478 nm under 340 nm excitation at 77 K (ESI, Fig. S8†).28 Therefore, the estimated triplet energy level of the H2NDC ligand in this work is 20 921 cm−1 calculated using time-dependent density functional theory. The fluorescence intensity and lifetime of Eu3+ in 1 decrease very fast at relatively low temperatures. This is related to the small energy difference of 3721 cm−1 between the excited triplet state of the H2NDC ligand (20 921 cm−1) and the 5D0 emitting level in Eu3+ (17 200 cm−1). This value is much smaller than those for other Eu-based MOFs, such as 8666 cm−1 for Eu-PIA (H2PIA = 5-(pyridin-4-yl)isophthalic acid),29 and 6106 cm−1 for Eu-DMBDC (H2DMBDC = 2,5-dimethoxy-1,4-benzenedicarboxylic acid).30 The small energy difference in 1 is the key factor in the acceleration of the thermally driven fluorescence quenching.29,31 The durability of the T-dependent fluorescence of 1 was tested over repeated heating and cooling cycles. The fluorescence intensity did not change during 10 cycles of quenching-recovery experiments, indicating its excellent repeatability (Fig. 4). The PXRD pattern confirms that 1 is stable for 10 cycles (ESI, Fig. S9†). In fact, most fluorescent materials lose intensity upon heating because of the altered Boltzmann distribution depending on temperature.32 However, 1 in this contribution is noteworthy in that it shows complete quenching at a relatively low temperature of 125 °C and complete recovery at ambient temperatures and is highly durable upon temperature cycling.

Stability

1 exhibits very high stability in water, organic solvents and alkaline solutions. Its PXRD pattern does not change upon immersion in water and 22 different organic solvents for 5 h, but not DMSO (ESI, Fig. S10†), and NaOH solutions of pH = 8–12 for 24 h (ESI, Fig. S11†). Remarkably, the XRD pattern of 1 kept in an NaOH solution at pH = 12 for 54 h did not show any sign of change (ESI, Fig. S12†). Meanwhile, after 15–30 days, though the crystals still kept good crystalline shapes, there were small impurity peaks at 2θ = 7.74° coming from solvation that causes hydrogen bonding between COO− and water. We can infer from this that 1 should more easily form hydrogen bonds in acid solutions containing more H+. 1 was tested by immersing it in HCl solutions at pH = 2 for periods of 3 hours to 7 days (ESI, Fig. S13†). The impurity peaks at around 2θ = 8° appeared after 3 h, though other main peaks remained the same as those of 1, indicating that 1 is less stable in acid solutions and that 1 more easily becomes amorphous.

Fluorescence and mechanical properties of 1@PES composite films

1@PES composite films with loadings of 1 ranging from 10 to 80% with steps of 10% were prepared using the solution casting method. High-resolution SEM images show that the particles of 1 are distributed uniformly in the PES matrix and the distribution of 1 becomes more uniform with increased loading up to 40%. Higher loadings of 1 ranging from 60 to 80% lead to aggregation of the microcrystals (Fig. 5). It is clear that the PXRD patterns of all 1@PES films are same, but slightly different to the pattern of 1 (ESI, Fig. S14†). These patterns comprise new PXRD peaks at 7.7° and 8.3° besides those of 1. The new peaks did not appear as 1 was ground with PES, which excludes impurities as the peak source (ESI, Fig. S15†). As 1 was soaked with DMAC overnight before PXRD analysis without drying, the PXRD peak at ca. 2θ = 7.7° appeared (Fig. S15†). However, the peak did not appear if 1 was dried (Fig. S10†), indicating the DAMC molecules remained in the structure of 1. Similar phenomena appeared in all 1@PES films: with the addition of 1, PXRD characterization of the 1@PES films showed similar peaks to 1, but some showed slight shifts to right and the extra peak at around 2θ = 8.3° appeared. This is due to the microcrystals of 1 being immersed in PES, whose interactions resulted in a slight left-shift.33 The new emerging peak at 2θ = 8.3° is possibly related to a slight structure change caused by PES.34 Therefore, 1 is basically intact in the composite films.

Fluorescence intensity at 613 nm as a function of the loading of 1, measured under the same conditions as pure 1, increases initially as the loading increases from 0 to 20% and then is basically saturated upon further increases in loading (Fig. 6). The dynamic mechanical analysis of 1@PES films shows a trend that the tensile strength of 1@PES films increase with loading up to 40% and then decrease. There is a very clear boundary that the tensile strengths of 1@PES with 10–50% loadings of 1 are higher than that of the pure PES film, and the others with 60–80% loading are lower than that of the pure PES film (Fig. 7). This suggests that 10–50% loading of 1 can improve the tensile strength of the PES matrix, due to the better MOF distribution. The noncovalent interactions between 1 and PES can enhance the tensile strength. Higher loadings decrease the tensile strength of the PES matrix because of the aggregation of 1 (Fig. 7). Clearly, the 1@PES with a loading of 40% (40% 1@PES) shows the highest tensile strength, combined with the highest fluorescence intensity among the films with 10–50% loading. Therefore, 40% 1@PES was selected for the following studies on the thermosensitive fluorescence properties of 1@PES films.

40% 1@PES film as a thermal sensor in an alarm device

The thermosensitive fluorescence of 40% 1@PES film was tested by placing the 40% 1@PES film on a thin aluminum plate and heating it with a lighter flame and cooling naturally, with a pure PES film as comparison. Fluorescence was quenched in 19 s upon heating and was recovered in 25 s upon cooling. The fluorescence intensity remained unchanged across 10 cycles of this quenching-recovery process (Fig. 8), showing an excellent fluorescence repeatability. The pure PES film shows blue emission at 390 nm, which can be neglected compared to the red fluorescence of Eu3+ in 40% 1@PES film (ESI, Fig. S16†).

40% 1@PES film was used as the thermal sensor in an alarm device which consisted of several basic parts of a single-chip microcomputer minimum system (51/AVR), a photosensitive resistance sensor module (PRSM) and a UV source (Fig. 9a). With T being low, the fluorescence of the 40% 1@PES sensor is on (namely, the input intensity is larger than 0, I > 0), which cannot alert the photosensitive resistance sensor and make the microcomputer ring the buzzer (Fig. 9b). Meanwhile, as T increases, the fluorescence of 40% 1@PES sensor will turn off (I = 0), which eventually will be detected by the photosensitive resistance sensor, which turns on the buzzer connected to the microcomputer to warn of the high temperature (Fig. 9c). The alarm device shows high sensitivity to temperature. In the contrast test, the fluorescence intensity of pure PES film was very weak, so it alerted the photosensitive resistance sensor to ring the buzzer in spite of T at ambient temperature. As such, it was not capable of sensing environmental temperature.

The durability of the 40% 1@PES sensor was tested by exposing it to UV light for 90 days. The fluorescence intensity decayed by about 60% in 4–19 days and stabilized at around 30% of the initial intensity after 20 days (Fig. 10). This 90-day-UV-exposed sensor still worked to ring the buzzer, indicating its high durability. Therefore, 1@PES films may act as a thermal sensor in alarm devices used in building safety or as security labels.

Conclusions

In summary, a 3D Eu-MOF [Eu(NDC)(H2O)Cl] (1) was synthesized using ionothermal processes in a range of 1-methyl-3-alkylimidazolium iodine ionic liquids. The structural construction of 1 is based on interweaving 2D (4,4) layers with a part of the NDC2− ligands. 1 exhibits high stability in water, organic solvents and NaOH solutions and less stability in HCl solutions. 1 can stay thermally stable up to 280 °C. Strong red fluorescence originating from the Eu3+ center can be emitted upon excitation by broadband UV light. Complete fluorescence thermal quenching occurred at about 125 °C. It is noteworthy that the fluorescence intensity can be fully recovered as the temperature falls back to 25 °C. The fluorescence intensity can remain unchanged after ten quenching-recovery cycles, indicating that 1 has high repeatability without intensity decay during the quenching-recovery cycles. Solution casting was used to prepare 1@PES composite films with loadings of 1 ranging from 10 to 80%. The dynamic mechanical analysis indicates that 1@PES films with a 10–50% loading of 1 have higher tensile strengths than pure PES film. The fluorescence intensity at 613 nm of 1@PES composite films also shows an initial increase depending on the loading from 0 to 20%, and then a smooth fluctuation upon further increases in loading. With the strongest fluorescence among 10–50% loaded films and the highest tensile strength, 40% 1@PES film was used as the thermal sensor in an alarm device, which shows high sensitivity to temperature. Upon exposure to UV light, the fluorescence decayed about 60% in the first twenty days, then remained stable for the following seventy days. The 90-day-UV-exposed 40% 1@PES film can still ring the buzzer, meaning that the thermal sensor may find applications in building safety or security labels.
