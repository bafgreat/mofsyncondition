Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) are a new class of crystalline porous materials that are formed by a metal or metal clusters connected by organic ligands. MOFs have potential applications in adsorption, separation and catalysis due to their high specific surface area, periodically arranged pore structure, tuneable pore size and pore chemical environment.1–6 On the other hand, environmental pollution is one of the major problems facing mankind today. The discharge of various pollutants causes serious harm to ecology and human health. Therefore, the removal of pollutants has become a hot topic. Under this background, increasing attention has been paid to the removal of pollutants by MOFs.7–12 Generally, due to the pore structure, metal centre and synergistic effect of the metal and ligands, MOFs can capture or degrade target pollutants through adsorption or catalysis in air or solution.13–17 Various host–guest interaction mechanisms between the MOF and pollutants, including hydrogen bonds, coordination bonds, π–π interactions, electrostatic interactions, anion exchange, and so on, have made MOFs multi-functional absorbents.

For adsorption in solution, MOFs should not only have suitable pore structures and interaction sites but also have high chemical stability, which can maintain the framework in the corresponding adsorption medium (especially in aqueous solution). At present, most high-stability MOFs are formed by the assembly of high-valent metal ions and carboxylic acid ligands,18,19 and there are relatively few types of stable MOFs based on nitrogen-donor ligands,20–22 except for the ZIF series. Unlike carboxylic acid ligands, which are prone to be negatively charged, some nitrogen-donor coordination groups are neutral groups, and thus, the charge state of the resulting framework may be different. For example, it may be easier to form a cationic porous framework based on nitrogen-donor ligands that are better suited for the adsorption of anionic pollutants.23–26 However, neutral nitrogen-donor groups such as imidazole and pyridine usually adopt only one coordination mode and form a relatively simple framework structure, which is also not conducive to the formation of multi-nuclear secondary building units that are usually observed in porous frameworks. On the other hand, nitrogen-donor groups with multidentate bridging modes, such as the tetrazole group, can easily form multi-nuclear SBUs but can also easily be deprotonated and produce negative charges. Therefore, in recent years, we have used multi-dentate N-donor heterotopic ligands that contain different types of nitrogen-donor groups to construct MOFs in order to combine the characteristics of the different types of coordination groups to develop new porous materials.27–30 In general, the tetrazole group provides the ability to form lots of multi-nuclear SBUs, while the pyridyl or imidazolyl group links SBUs and reduces the total negative charge of the ligand. It is found that some of the MOFs based on these types of ligands showed good chemical stability and are suitable for the adsorption of related pollutants in solution medium.27,28 Herein, as a continuation study on MOFs based on heterotopic N-containing ligands, a new hydrolytically cage-based Cu(ii) metal–organic framework, namely, SCNU-Z4, was constructed. The cage is composed of two kinds of multi-nuclear SBUs. Interestingly, the framework can adsorb CR with a high capacity and large AB dyes. The framework also shows the ability to absorb I2 in solution. The adsorption mechanisms were also studied.

Materials and methods

Materials and measurements

Ligand 4-[3,5-bis-(2H-tetrazol-5-yl)-phenyl]-pyridine hydro-chloride (H2BTPP·HCl) was obtained from Shanghai Kylpharm Co., Ltd. All other reagents were of analytical grade and were used without further purification. The organic solvents were purchased from Daomao Chemical Reagent Factory. Other reagents were available from Guangzhou Chemical Reagent Factory. IR spectra were recorded on a Nicolet FT-IR-170SX spectrophotometer in KBr pellets in the range from 4000 to 400 cm−1. TG curves were recorded on PerkinElmer TGA8000 analyzer at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 under a flowing air atmosphere. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) analysis was performed using an Ultima IV X-ray powder diffractometer (Kurary, Tokyo, Japan) at 40 kV, 40 mA using Cu Kα radiation (k = 1.5406 Å). N2, H2 and CO2 adsorption–desorption isotherms were measured on a Micromeritics 3Flex adsorption instrument. Zeta potentials were measured using a Zetasizer Nano ZS90 analyzer from Malvern Instruments at a liquid concentration of 0.75 mg mL−1. UV-vis spectra were recorded on a Shimadzu UV-2550 spectrophotometer equipped with a Labsphere diffuse reflectance accessory. EDX images were recorded on a MERLIN Compact scanning electron microscope at 15.0 kV.

Syntheses of SCNU-Z4

A mixture of H2BTPP·HCl (0.1 mmol), Cu(NO3)·3H2O (0.1 mmol), NH3·H2O (1 M, 0.1 mL) and DMF (6 mL) was placed into a Teflon-lined stainless-steel autoclave. The mixture was heated at 90 °C for 3 days, cooled to room temperature at 3 °C h−1 and allowed to stand for 20 h. Dark green crystals of SCNU-Z4 were filtered. Yield 80% (based on Cu). IR (KBr, ν/cm−1): 3618(m), 3449(m), 1674(s), 1617(m), 1495(m), 1446(m), 1406(s), 1389(s), 1251(m), 1089(m), 1072(m), 1031(w), 901(w), 845(m), 787(s), 755(m), 657(m), 503(m), 438(m).

X-ray data collection and structure refinement

Data collections were performed at 298 K on a CrysAlisPro 1.171.38.43f (Rigaku OD, 2015) with a SuperNova (Cu) X-ray source (λ = 1.54184 Å) for SCNU-Z4. Multi-scan empirical absorption correction was performed using spherical harmonics, implemented in the SCALE3 ABSPACK scaling algorithm.31 Structural solutions and refinements based on F2 were performed using the SHELXS-201632 and SHELXL-201632 program packages, respectively. The amounts of solvent and cations in the channels or cages were removed by SQUEEZE.33 All non-H atoms were refined with anisotropic thermal parameters. Hydrogen atoms of organic ligands were placed in idealized positions and refined isotropically. The hydrogen atoms of water molecules were not added but included in the formula. The crystal parameters, data collections, and refinements are provided in Table S1.† Selected bond lengths and angles are listed in Table S2.† CCDC reference number is 2032895.†

Iodine adsorption and desorption

The activated SCNU-Z4 (20 mg) was added to an iodine cyclohexane solution (200 ppm, 8 mL) at room temperature and stirred. The kinetics of iodine adsorption was studied using UV-vis spectroscopy. The adsorption isotherm was measured by immersing 5 mg of activated SCNU-Z4 in 10 mL of iodine cyclohexane solution at different concentrations from 25 to 300 ppm, and the concentration of I2 was measured after 48 h. For I2 desorption, 5 mg of I2@SCNU-Z4 was soaked in ethanol (10 mL). The residual concentration of iodine solution was determined by UV-vis absorption at room temperature. To perform iodine adsorption in aqueous solution, 30 mg of the sample was added to the aqueous solution containing I2 (4 mM, 20 mL). Solid KI was added to improve the solubility of I2 in water. The complete experiment was carried out at room temperature for 36 h. The concentration of I2 in water was determined by titration.

Dye adsorption and separation

In order to investigate the dye adsorption abilities of the activated SCNU-Z4, five dyes namely positively charged methylene blue (MLB), Rhodamine B (RhB), alcian blue 8GX (AB), negatively charged methyl orange (MO) and Congo red (CR) were selected. The structures of the dyes are shown in Fig. S1.† The adsorption kinetics were measured by adding activated SCNU-Z4 (10 mg) to organic dye aqueous solutions (20 ppm, 30 mL). The experiments were performed at room temperature and kept stirring under neutral conditions. After a given time, about 3 mL of the upper solution was taken for the detection of dye concentration by UV-vis spectroscopy, then it was poured back to the original solution. The dye separation was performed by a similar method except the single dye solution was replaced by a dye mixture of MLB & RhB, MLB & MO and MLB & CR. Adsorption isotherms were measured by adding activated SCNU-Z4 (5 mg) to 20 mL of dye aqueous solutions with various initial concentrations. After being stirred for 24 h, the solution was collected and the equilibrium concentration was measured.

Calculation of capacity

The adsorption capacities of the dye and I2 on SCNU-Z4 at a given time and equilibrium are defined as eqn (1) and (2), respectively: Qt = V(C0 − Ct)/m (1) Qe = V(C0 − Ce)/m (2) where V is the solution volume, C0, Ct and Ce are the solution concentrations at initial, a given time (t) and the equilibrium, respectively, and m is the mass of SCNU-Z4.

Results and discussion

Crystal structure depiction

The single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis showed that SCNU-Z4 crystallizes in the tetragonal space group Pqm2. In the asymmetric unit, one BTPP2− ligand, three independent Cu(ii) ions with occupancies of 1/2, 1/2 and 1/4, respectively, two Cl− ions with occupancies of 1/4 and 1/8, respectively, two coordinated water molecules with occupancies of 1/4 and 1/2, respectively, and some ambiguous solvent molecules and anions are found. The elements in SCNU-Z4 were also confirmed by EDS, indicating the existence of Cu and Cl (Fig. S2†). As shown in Fig. 1a, both Cu1 and Cu2 are six-coordinated and show distorted octahedral geometries. For Cu1, the coordination sites are occupied by four tetrazolyl N atoms (Ntz), one coordinated water molecule and one Cl− anion. Meanwhile, Cu2 is coordinated by two Ntz atoms, two pyridyl N atoms (Npy), one coordinated water molecule and one Cl− anion. Cu3 is five-coordinated with the sites occupied by four Ntz atoms and one coordinated water molecule. The BTPP2− ligand adopts a μ5-coordiantion mode that connects to four Cu(ii) ions by two tetrazolyl groups via the 2- and 3-positions of nitrogen atoms and another Cu(ii) ion via the mono-dentate pyridyl group. Four Cu1 ions are connected by eight BTPP2− ligands and one Cl− to generate a tetranuclear [Cu4Cl]7+ secondary building unit (SBU), as shown in Fig. 1b. Such an SBU is similar to that observed in the well-known M-BTT (H3BTT = 1,3,5-benzenetristetrazolate) framework based on the BTT ligand.34–36 On the other hand, two Cu2 ions and one Cu3 ion are bridged by four BTPP2− ligands and one Cl−, resulting in a trinuclear SBU. From the tetranuclear SBU to trinuclear SBU, it can be seen that half of the tetrazole groups are replaced by pyridyl groups, and thus, one of the Cu(ii) ions should leave due to which the number of coordination atoms for pyridyl groups decreased. Two tetranuclear SBUs and four trinuclear SBUs are connected together to form a distorted truncated octahedron cage with a diameter of 10.4 Å and a small window with a diameter of 10.8 Å. The cage is also similar to that in the Cu-BTT MOF; however, it is composed of two types of SBUs, and thus, it becomes distorted. The cage is connected to six adjacent cages via the shared trinuclear and tetranuclear units, resulting in a 3D framework (Fig. 1c). In addition, similar to the Cu-BTT framework, there are also channels with a diameter of 10.9 Å nm in the framework (Fig. 1c). The BTPP2− ligand can be seen as three-connected nodes, while both the tetrazole and trinuclear SUBs are four-connected nodes from a topological view. Thus, the entire framework can be seen as a (3,8)-connected “Moravia” net, as indicated by TOPOS.37

Stability and gas adsorption

The PXRD data of the as-synthesized SCNU-Z4 match well with the simulated data except that the relative intensities are different due to the preferred orientation, indicating the high crystalline phase purity. The PXRD pattern of activated SCNU-Z4 was similar to that of the as-synthesized SCNU-Z4, suggesting that the framework is retained after activation. The intensities of some peaks changed, and the appearance of a few new peaks may be due to the slight structural distortion resulting from the release of solvent molecules (Fig. S3†).

As shown in Fig. S4,† thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) of the as-synthesized SCNU-Z4 showed a gradual weight loss from room temperature to 280 °C, and the framework decomposes after this temperature, which may be due to the release of the guest solvent and coordinated water molecules. The TG curve of the activated sample showed less weight loss before 280 °C, which confirmed the removal of the guest molecules under heating at 120 °C for 24 h. The residue is CuO (a weight of approximately 22% was observed, while the calculated value is approximately 25%), which was confirmed by PXRD.

To determine the stability of SCNU-Z4 in solvents, the as-synthesized crystals were immersed in aqueous solutions with different pH values ranging from 3 to 11 as well as in various organic solvents for 72 h. From the PXRD results, it can be seen that the framework structure remains almost unchanged after immersion (Fig. 2 and Fig. S5†). The slight differences may be due to the loss of the original solvent molecules, which can be achieved via activation (see above) or solvent-exchange.

Nitrogen adsorption–desorption isotherms measured at 77 K on activated SCNU-Z4 are shown in Fig. 3a. The isotherm of activated SCNU-Z4 reveals a type I + IV feature.38 The sharp gas uptake at low pressure was attributed to the micropores in SCNU-Z4, while the second gas uptake at a relatively high pressure (above P/P0 = 0.8) occurring in a relatively wide pressure range with a large hysteresis was attributed to the presence of mesopore structures in the sample. Since no mesopores can be observed in the single crystal structure, the mesopores may be due to the formation of some defects. Langmuir and BET surface areas of 620.6 and 584.8 m2 g−1 were observed, respectively. The pore width was 1.1 nm, which corresponds well with that in the crystal structure. In addition, some mesopores with sizes in the range of 5 to 10 nm were also observed (Fig. S6†). The H2 adsorption–desorption isotherm measured at 77 K showed that the material adsorbs a maximum of 1.11 wt% at 77 K and 1 bar, which is lower than that for M-BTT. Considering that the theoretical study suggests that nitrogen atoms can enhance the adsorption energy of H2, the reason for lower capacity may be that the number of nitrogen atoms in the BTPP2− ligand is smaller than that in the BTT ligand. Since the uncoordinated N atoms in the framework is also expected to enhance interactions with CO2, and the CO2 isotherm at 298 K was also measured (Fig. 3b), which shows an uptake of 40 cm3 g−1 at 1 bar. For comparison, the N2 uptake at 298 K was also measured, which was only 2.3 cm3 g−1 at 1 bar. Thus, the calculated CO2/N2 selectivity was 17.4 : 1 at 298 K, which is comparable to those of some other MOFs based on BTT ligands.39

Iodine adsorption behavior

Considering the cages, channels, nitrogen-rich groups and uncoordinated nitrogen atoms in the framework of SCNU-Z4, it can be expected that it has the ability to adsorb iodine molecules.40–43 First, 20 mg of SCNU-Z4 was added into the cyclohexane solution of iodine (200 mg L−1), and the iodine concentration was measured from the UV spectra at different times. The results are shown in Fig. 4a. It can be seen that the concentration of iodine gradually decreases in approximately 24 h, and the removal rate is 99.5% when reaching equilibrium.

The colour of the solution changed from purple to colourless (Fig. 4a, insert figure), which also indicates the adsorption of I2. The pseudo-second-order kinetics model was found to better describe the time dependence of the I2 adsorption than the pseudo-first-order kinetics model (Fig. S7 and Table S3†), indicating that chemisorption is the rate controlling step.44,45 The isotherm experiments were performed to measure the adsorption capacity of I2 onto SCNU-Z4 in cyclohexane solution. As shown in Fig. 4b, the adsorption capacity is 237 mg g−1, which is moderate and comparable to those of the most recently reported Th-MOFs.41 Furthermore, the data can be better fitted using the Freundlich model than the Langmuir and Temkin models (Fig. S8 and Table S4†), which indicates that the adsorption of I2 may occur by chemical mechanisms. The surface-active sites of the SCNU-Z4 samples may be heterogeneously distributed and play a role in I2 adsorption.46

The release of I2 from the I2@SCNU-Z4 is achieved by immersing it in an EtOH solvent. Most of the captured I2 can be released rapidly, and approximately 82% of I2 was desorbed during 16 h (Fig. 4c and Fig. S9†). After sufficient desorption, the recovered SCNU-Z4 sample was used for the iodine adsorption in cyclohexane solution again. The results show that the relative adsorption capacity is 91% after the first cycle, and retain 89% after three cycles (Fig. S10†), which indicates that the material can be reused.

The PXRD results of the samples after iodine adsorption and three adsorption–desorption cycles showed that their frameworks did not change significantly (Fig. S11†), which indicated that the SCNU-Z4 framework was stable in such an adsorption process. The loading of I2 on SCNU-Z4 was also confirmed by EDS. From Fig. S12,† the I element is observed in the EDS after I2 adsorption. It has been observed that the charge transfer often occurred between the aromatic π-electrons of the host networks and the guest iodine molecules to form adsorbed I2 and I3−.42,43 To confirm this, XPS of I2@SCNU-Z4 was also performed. The I3d spectra of I2@SCNU-Z4 show two bands centred at ∼631 and ∼619 eV for I3d3/2 and I3d5/2, respectively. They were contributed by I3− (630.2 eV; 618.7 eV) and I2 (631.7 eV; 620.3 eV), and the fitted spectra are shown in Fig. 4d. The adsorption of I2 can be attributed to the following reasons. First, iodine may be adsorbed inside the cavities and pores of SCNU-Z4 via I–I⋯N bonds due the presence of uncoordinated N atoms on the tetrazole group.42 Second, the intermolecular interactions between the I2 molecule and π-electron walls are also crucial.40

Considering its hydrolytically stability, the adsorption of I2 in water was also performed. The solubility of I2 in water is low, so KI is added to promote its dissolution to form KI3 solution.47,48 30 mg of activated SCNU-Z4 was immersed in an aqueous KI3 solution (4 mM, 20 mL) and stirred for 36 h. The color of the solution become lighter, indicating the adsorption of I2 from water by SCNU-Z4. The accurate amounts of iodine in the initial and resulting solutions were determined by I2 titration (see the ESI for details). A maximum of 9.95 mg of I2/30 mg of SCNU-Z4, i.e. ∼33.2% of iodine, can be removed by the activated SCNU-Z4. The percentage of I2 uptake is moderate compared with other MOFs reported in the literature.47,48

Dye-adsorption and separation studies

Considering the high hydrolytic stability and porous structure of SCNU-Z4, it can be expected that SCNU-Z4 may be used for adsorption in solution medium. Therefore, its adsorption properties for dye molecules in water were investigated. Three cationic dyes (MLB, RhB and AB) and two anionic dyes (MO and CR) with different sizes and charges were selected. The results are shown in Fig. 5a–e. All of the selected dyes can be adsorbed by SCNU-Z4 to a certain extent. The removal rates of MLB, RhB, AB, MO and CR are 79.3%, 35.9%, 88.2%, 31.6% and 93.6%, respectively, when the adsorption reaches equilibrium. Based on these performances, the dye separation performance was studied based on three separation modes. For testing the charge-based separation mode, the separation of a mixture of the dyes MLB and MO was performed. The result is shown in Fig. 5g. Interestingly, the adsorptions of MLB and MO in their mixed dye solution were better than those in their single dye solutions, and not only the adsorption rate obviously accelerated but the removal rate also increased. This may be due to the fact that MLB molecules that adsorbed on the framework are beneficial to capture more MO molecules through electrostatic or/and π⋯π interactions and vice versa. This phenomenon of synergistic adsorption is relatively less explored.47 Dye separation based on the size-dependent mode was performed by using a mixture of MLB and RhB dyes. The result indicates that the adsorption of MLB in the dye mixture is better than that of RhB, and thus, the two dyes can be separated to some extent (Fig. 5h). To test the dye separation ability based on the kinetic-dependent mode, the mixed MLB and CR dye solution was selected. After 5 min, almost all of the CR molecules were absorbed, while the removal rate of MLB was only 36%, which means that they can be separated via the kinetic-dependent mode to some extent.28,48

The adsorption isotherms of several dyes with SCNU-Z4 reveal that the highest adsorption capacities of MLB, RhB, AB, MO and CR for SCNU-Z4 are 279, 157, 221, 95 and 1200 mg g−1, respectively, as shown in Fig. 5f and Fig. S13.† The following two points with regard to the dye adsorption performance should be noted: (i) among the selected dyes, SCNU-Z4 showed the best adsorption performance for CR. On the one hand, the adsorption rate is faster. It takes only approximately 20 min for CR to be adsorbed, but it takes more than 2 h for the other dyes. On the other hand, the adsorption capacity of CR is significantly higher than those of the other selected dyes. The adsorption capacity of CR was as high as 1200 mg g−1 (which is among the highest capacities for MOFs, as shown in Table S5†), while the capacities are just moderate (MLB and AB) or low (RhB and MO) for the other selected dyes; (ii) although the size of the AB molecule is larger than the size of cages and channels in the SCNU-Z4 framework, it can still be adsorbed by SCNU-Z4, which is seldom observed in MOF absorbents. In addition, there are only a few reports related to AB adsorption.49–53 Thus, the adsorptions of AB and CR were studied in more detail (see below).

The time-resolved adsorption capacity data for all of the selected dyes were analyzed using pseudo-first-order and pseudo-second-order equations. The results showed that the data are better fit by the pseudo-second-order equations except for AB, indicating a chemical adsorption process (Fig. S14–S18†). The equilibrium capacities, pseudo-second-order rate constants and correlation coefficients (R2) are listed in Table S6.† The data for AB adsorption cannot give a decent result for both the equations, implying a complicated adsorption process. Furthermore, the adsorption isomer data were analysed by the Langmuir, Freundlich and Temkin equations. The fitting results of the Langmuir model are not significantly better than the fitting results of the other two models (Fig. S19–S23 and Table S7†), which means it is not merely monomolecular-layer adsorption.

For CR adsorption, the PXRD pattern of CR@SCNU-Z4 shows the framework stability during adsorption (Fig. S24†). The EDS measured after CR adsorption shows the presence of the S element (Fig. S25†), which supports the loading of CR on SCNU-Z4.45 In addition, the solid state UV-Vis spectra showed that the peak for CR is blue-shifted after adsorption by SCNU-Z4 (Fig. 6a), which supports the π⋯π interactions between the host SCNU-Z4 and CR.54 It is also reasonable to expect that the NH2 group on the CR molecule may form N–H⋯N hydrogen bonds with the SCNU-Z4 framework considering the existence of uncoordinated N atoms on the tetrazole group. The zeta potential of SCNU-Z4 is negative; thus, the adsorption is not driven by electrostatic interactions. After adsorption, the zeta potential of CR@SCNU-Z4 becomes more negative (Fig. 6b), which is not conducive to the adsorption of more CR by electrostatic interactions. Thus, they are the multiple weak interactions (including hydrogen bonds, π⋯π interactions, and so on) rather than electrostatic interactions that contribute to the effective adsorption of CR.

It has been reported that AB can undergo self-decomposition in water,55 and thus, the change in the AB concentration in a solution without SCNU-Z4 was measured for comparison. However, it was shown that only a slight amount of AB (approximately 4%) decomposes after 4 h under our experimental conditions, which is much lower than that of the concentration decrease when SCNU-Z4 was added, and therefore indicating that the AB is removed by SCNU-Z4. To investigate the mechanism of the removal of AB by SCNU-Z4, the EDS (Fig. S26†) of the samples after interaction with AB were tested. The S element appeared in EDS preliminarily indicating that AB may be removed by SCNU-Z4 through adsorption. The PXRD pattern of AB@SCNU-Z4 (Fig. S24†) indicted that the framework is stable in the adsorption of AB, and thus, a chemical reaction did not occur between SCNU-Z4 and AB. Considering that the size of AB is larger than the micropores (cages and channels) in SCNU-Z4, it cannot be captured by these cavities. In addition, the zeta potential of SCNU-Z4 is −3.9 mV in water, indicating that the cationic AB dye molecules may be absorbed on the surface of SCNU-Z4. Indeed, AB@SCNU-Z4 shows a positive zeta potential (15.6 mV), which also supports the surface adsorption. Furthermore, the samples were separated into two parts with different particle sizes through a sieve (Fig. S27†), and their adsorption capacities in 20 ppm AB solution were compared. The results showed that the adsorption capacity of the larger particles was 11.9 mg g−1, and that of the smaller particles was 18.5 mg g−1 (Fig. S28†). Thus, this indicated that the increase of surface area of the particles could indeed increase the adsorption capacity. In addition, the large AB molecules may also be adsorbed by the mesoporous pores (see the BET results) that were observed in the SCNU-Z4 crystals. The proposed mechanisms of CR and AB adsorption on SCNU-Z4 are shown in Fig. 6c.

Conclusions

In summary, an MOF based on cages composed of two types of building blocks with high hydrolytic stability was synthesized. The uncoordinated nitrogen, the cage and channels and the hydrolytic stability in this MOF allow it to be used for the adsorption of I2 and organic dyes from solution, and it shows a high adsorption capacity towards CR as well as the unexpected adsorption of large AB dye molecules. The results show that a porous stable MOF framework based on N-rich ligands does show strong potential application in pollutant treatment.
