Novel lanthanide coordination polymers with Eu-compound exhibits warm white light emission: Synthesis, structure, and magnetic properties

Abstract

Five two-dimensional lanthanide coordination polymers: [Ln2(TDA)3(bipy)2(H2O)2]·bipy·2H2O (Ln = Nd (1), Sm (2), Eu (3), Tb (4), Yb (5), TDA = thiophene-2,5-dicarboxylic acid anion, bipy = 2,2′-bipyridine) were fabricated and structurally characterized. Compounds 1–5 are isostructural belonging to the triclinic system with space group P-1. Luminescence analyses were performed on coordination polymers containing Eu3 + and Tb3 +, and compound 3 shows a warm white light emission upon excitation.

As an emerging material, lanthanide coordination polymers have seen huge success due to their appealing potential applications in modern areas of materials science such as photoluminescence [1], magnetism [2], probes in biosciences [3], molecular adsorption [4]. Lanthanide coordination polymers are efficient luminescent materials and irreplaceable components of light-emitting devices owing to their intrinsic electronic properties that can show luminescence in either near-infrared (Yb3 +, Nd3 +, and Er3 +) or visible (Eu3 + and Tb3 +) regions [5]. In recent years, much attention has been paid to the generation of white-light-emitting materials and devices for a variety of applications, including solid-state multicolor three-dimensional (3D) displays, back light, illumination, and so on [6]. So far, most of the white-light sources depend on the incorporation of three (RGB: red-green-blue) or two (blue-orange) emissions from separate dopants or multiple components, which may cause some problems such as phase separation and color variation [7]. Therefore, to circumvent these disadvantages, the investigation of efficient, durable, and single-component white-light-emitting phosphors are drawing much attention. Therefore, design of single-phase white-light-emitting materials is of obvious interest and importance. However, single-phase white-light emitters based on lanthanide coordination polymers have been seldom achieved [8], although such metal–organic materials feature easy combination of inorganic and organic properties and straightforward syntheses.

In this contribution, five two-dimensional (2D) lanthanide coordination polymers: [Ln2(TDA)3(bipy)2(H2O)2]·bipy·2H2O (Ln = Nd (1), Sm (2), Eu (3), Tb (4), Yb (5), TDA = thiophene-2,5-dicarboxylic acid anion, bipy = 2,2′-bipyridine) [9] were fabricated and structurally characterized. Magnetic measurements were performed on compounds 4 and 5. Luminescence analyses were performed on compounds 3 and 4, which exhibited the characteristic transitions of corresponding lanthanide ions. And compound 3 exhibited warm white light emissions upon excitation.

Single crystal X-ray diffraction analyses reveal that compounds 1–5 are isomorphous, belonging to triclinic system with space group P-1 [10], and therefore, the structure of compound 1 is described here representatively. The two Nd3 + ions are crystallographically equal, so the asymmetric unit in 1 (Fig. 1) consists of one unique Nd3 + ion, one and a half TDA2 −, one coordinated water molecule, one coordinated and a half lattice bipy molecules and a lattice water molecule. Five O atoms from five TDA2 −, one coordination water molecule and two nitrogen atoms from one bipy molecule complete the eight-coordinated environment of Nd3 +, thus the coordination geometry of Nd1 can be described as a distorted square antiprism. The two nearby Nd3 + ions are connected by four carboxyl bridges into a paddle-wheel dinuclear unit. The distance of two Nd3 + ions is 4.525 Å. The Nd–OCOO bond lengths are in the range of 2.274(4)–2.520(5) Å, and that of Nd–Owater is 2.469(2) Å. The bond lengths of NdN are 2.661(2) and 2.646(2) Å. In compound 1, the ligand adopts two different coordination modes: mode A (μ4-η1:η1:η1:η1), each TDA2 − anion bridges four Ln3 + ions; mode B (μ3-η1:η1:η1), each ligand bridges three Ln3 + ions, as show in scheme 1. Viewed along the [110] direction, two TDA2 − ligands with mode B link two paddle-wheel dinuclear units, forming one-dimensional (1D) ladder-like double chains (Fig. 2). Viewed along the [010] direction, the 1D chains are further assembled into a 2D network by the linking of TDA2 − ligands with mode A (Fig. 3). Adjacent layers are further extended into a 3D supramolecular architecture via rich hydrogen bonding interactions including OH ⋅⋅⋅ O and CH ⋅⋅⋅ O hydrogen bonds and π-π stacking interactions between coordinated bipy molecules and the lattice ones (Fig. 4). The H ⋅⋅⋅ O bond lengths of the OH ⋅⋅⋅ O hydrogen bonds are 3.176, 2.919, 2.676, 2.084 and 1.895 Å, and the H ⋅⋅⋅ O bond lengths of the CH ⋅⋅⋅ O hydrogen bonds are 2.956, 2.873, 2.845, 2.800, 2.680, 2.624, 2.611 and 2.468 Å. The angels of corresponding OH ⋅⋅⋅ O hydrogen bonds are 51.9, 118.0, 143.5, 148.6 and 167.5°, and the angels of corresponding CH ⋅⋅⋅ O hydrogen bonds are 121.7, 144.3, 135.2, 129.5, 114.6, 171.3, 129.8, 168.2°.

Thermal gravimetric analyses (TGA) for crystal samples of 1–5 were performed from 20 to 800 °C (Fig. 5) under N2 atmosphere. Since compounds 1–5 are isomorphous, they have similar weight loss behavior, and the TGA of compound 1 is described here representatively. Between 40 and 80 °C, compound 1 loses weight of 1.99% corresponding to the loss of two free water molecules (calculated value 2.69%). Between 130 and 170 °C, it loses 4.97% weight due to the loss of two coordinated water molecules (calculated value 5.38%). Upon further heating, the structure loses the free bipy ligand until 340 °C corresponding to the weight loss of 16.95% (calculated value 17.04%). Above 340 °C, the mass loses sharply, which is attributed to the thermal decomposition of the framework. PXRD patterns were performed on crystalline powders of 3 and 4 (Fig. S1 in supplementary material), the experimental PXRD patterns are consistent with the corresponding simulated from the single crystal data, suggesting the phase purity of the samples.

The solid-state luminescent properties of compounds 3 and 4 were investigated in the visible region at room temperature. The two compounds were both excited at 324 nm and all displayed their characteristic emissions. The emission spectra of compound 3 exhibit four peaks at 593, 614, 652 and 702 nm correspond to the transitions of Eu3 + from 5D0 to 7FJ (J = 1, 2, 3, 4), as shown in Fig. 6. The intensity radio of the two emission peaks of 593 and 614 nm corresponding to 5D0 → 7F1 (magnetic dipole transition) and 5D0 → 7F2 (electric dipole transition) emission is about 1:3, which means electric dipole transition 5D0 → 7F2 dominates the luminescence emission. This behavior shows that the symmetry of the coordination environment of Eu3 + ions is low, which is consistent well with the crystal structure.

The emission spectra of 3 also exhibit an emission peak at 486 nm, which may be the emission peak of TDA2 − or bipy. Compared with the solid-state luminescent spectra of compounds H2TDA and bipy excited at 324 nm, of which H2TDA shows no peaks while bipy shows a peak at 528 nm, the emission peak of 3 at 486 nm has similar shape and intensity with emission peak of bipy at 528 nm, with blue shift of 42 nm, which may conclude that the emission peak at 486 nm is originated from the emission of bipy. The blue shift of bipy may be originated from that the coordination increases the rigidity of bipy, while reducing the vibration and rotation of bipy. The emission peak of the transitions of Eu3 + from 5D1 to 7FJ (J = 1, 2) were overlapped by the emission of the ligand. The CIE coordinates of (x, y) (0.40, 0.35) and the color temperature of 3387 K suggest that the emission of compound 3 is warm white light, close to that of an incandescent lamp. The luminescence of compound 3 indicates that ligand TDA2 − possesses significant “antenna effect”, which can transfer the energy from TDA2 − to Eu3 + ions efficiently, and the bipy may not transfer the energy to Eu3 + ions efficiently and emit luminescence itself. According to the principle of the three RGB colors, to implement a white light emission needs introduction of green and blue light to the red light of Eu3 + emission, and the luminescent emission of coordinated bipy is just blue, the combination of the red and blue emissions got a warm white light emission.

Compound 4 was excited at 324 nm, and the four peaks at 490, 545, 586, and 622 nm are assigned to the transition of 5D4 to 7FJ of Tb3 + (J = 6, 5, 4, 3), in which the emission of 5D4 → 7F5 is the strongest one, as shown in Fig. 7. The peak at 490 is overlapped with the peak of bipy. The CIE coordinates of (x, y) (0.28, 0.46) suggest that the emission of compound 4 is green light, which is the typical fluorescence of terbium ion.

The magnetic susceptibilities of 4 and 5 were measured on the microcrystalline samples in the temperature range from 2 to 300 K and under 1000 Oe field, as shown in Fig. 8. Due to the existence of inter-electronic repulsion and spin-orbit coupling, the 4fn configuration is split into 2S + 1LJ states. Moreover, influenced by the weak crystal field perturbation, each of these states can be further split into Stark sublevels which are thermally populated at room temperature. Progressive thermal depopulation of these sublevels leads to a χMT response that decreases as the temperature is lowered owing to Boltzmann depopulation of the excited states. The χMT value of 4 and 5 is equal to 11.60, 2.31 cm3 K mol− 1 at room temperature, which is close to the theoretical value of 11.82, 2.53 cm3 K mol− 1, respectively, expected for one Tb3 + in the 7F6 ground state (g = 3/2), or one Yb3 + in the 2F7/2 ground state (g = 8/7). For 4, the χMT values almost remain consistent between 300 and 80 K, and this behavior may accounts for the competition between ferromagnetic interaction and thermal depopulation of Stark sublevels of single Tb3 + ion, reflecting possible weak ferromagnetic interactions between Tb3 + ions. With further cooling, the χMT value decreases quickly to 6.28 cm3 K mol− 1 at 2 K, which may indicate the thermal depopulation of Stark sublevels dominates the magnetic properties at low temperature. For 5, the χMT value slowly decreases on cooling and reaches a minimum value of 1.23 cm3 K mol− 1 at 2 K. Therefore, the nature of the interaction between Yb3 + ions cannot be absolutely determined, since the corresponding χMT value reduces with the temperature decreasing.

In summary, five lanthanide coordination polymers based on thiophene-2,5-dicarboxylic acid and 2,2′-bipyridine have been constructed and structurally characterized. They all feature a 2D framework, belonging to triclinic crystal system with space group P-1. The luminescent properties show that 3 and 4 exhibit the characteristic bands of the corresponding lanthanide ions in solid state, and compound 3 shows a warm white light emission upon excitation. Magnetic studies for 3 and 4 have been performed and 4 show possible weak ferromagnetic interactions between Tb3 + ions.
