Diverse Zn(II) MOFs assembled from V-shaped asymmetric multicarboxylate and N-donor ligands

Highlights

•

Four compounds based on a new semirigid tricarboxylate ligand, which has been rarely reported before.

•

These compounds display four different structures varying from OD to 2D.

•

Compounds 1–4 exhibit strong blue luminescence at room temperature.

Abstract

By reacting an asymmetry semi-rigid V-shaped linker H3L (H3L = 3-(3-carboxyphenoxy) phthalic acid) and Zn(NO3)2·6H2O under different N-donor ligands in different solvents, four new Zn-based coordination polymers, [Zn(HL)(2,2′-bpy)(H2O)]n (1), [Zn(HL)(4,4′-bpy)]n·n(DMA) (2), [Zn3(L)2(phen)3(H2O)]n·n(H2O) (3) and [Zn(HL)(phen)(H2O)]2 (4) (2,2′-bpy = 2,2′-bipyridine; 4,4′-bpy = 4,4′-bipyridine; phen = 1,10-phenanthroline; DMA = N,N-dimethylacetamide) have been obtained. All of these compounds have been clearly identified by single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis. Compound 1 exhibits one-dimensional (1D) chain structure constructed from uninuclear Zn(II) motif, which further extends into 2D supramolecular architecture via intermolecular π–π interactions and hydrogen bonds. Structural analysis reveals that the structure of 2 and 3 can be described as a 2D hcb topology network with the point symbol of {63}. Compound 4 shows a 0D binuclear motif while its 3D packing network has a large potential solvent voids. The results of this research demonstrate that the solvent and the secondary ligands could co-regulate different structural coordination polymers with interesting properties. In addition, the thermal stabilities and solid-state luminescence properties of compounds 1–4 have also been investigated.

1. Introduction

The rational design and assembly of metal-organic coordination frameworks (MOFs) with appealing structures and properties have stimulated interests of chemists in recent decades [1], [2], [3], [4], [5], [6]. In particular, considerable efforts dedicated to complexes based on d10 metals, such as Zn(II) and Cd(II) ions, due to their attractive luminescence functionality and potential applications in light-emitting diodes [7], [8], [9], [10], [11]. Furthermore, MOFs with desired topologies can be rationally designed by taking advantage of many subtle factors such as reaction temperature and pressure, auxiliary ligands, solvents, pH, secondary metal ions and so on [12], [13], [14], [15], [16], [17]. As a result, a new multicarboxylate ligand is often employed to fabricate coordination polymers (CPs) with various familiar N-donor ligands because of their excellent crystallinity and the relatively simple coordination geometries of the coordinated nitrogen atom [12], [18], [19], [20].

Recent research has been devoted to the use of rigid and symmetric flexible carboxylate ligands. The former, such as 1,4-benzenedicarboxylate, 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylic acid and 1,3,5-tris(4-carboxyphenyl) benzene have been extensively used for fabricating porous materials [21], [22], [23], [24], [25], [26]. The latter, like 2,2′,3,3′-oxydiphthalic acid and 3,3′,4,4′-oxydiphthalic acid have proved to be good candidates for preparing interpenetrating structures and metal clusters with photoluminescence properties [27], [28]. However, the construction of CPs using asymmetric semi-rigid V-shaped multicarboxylate ligands have been relatively less investigated [29], [30], [31]. The asymmetric flexible polycarboxylate ligands with different numbers of carboxylic substituents at each benzene ring can have different conformations and coordination modes when they react with metal salts, and can thus form polynuclear metal complexes with great structural diversity and unusual topologies [14]. Fortunately, most of the reported work has clarified the effect of positions of asymmetrically attached carboxylic groups on the molecular structure and functional properties of MOFs using four asymmetric semi-rigid V-shaped multicarboxylate ligands including 4-(2-carboxyphenoxy) phthalic acid (H3L1), 3-(2-carboxyphenoxy) phthalic acid (H3L2), 3-(4-carboxyphenoxy) phthalic acid (H3L3), 4-(4-carboxyphenoxy) phthalic acid (H3L4) [29], [32], [33]. With the above consideration, we choose a new H3L (3-(3-carboxyphenoxy) phthalic acid) ligand containing two freely rotating benzene rings around an oxygen atom and three carboxyl groups attached to these two benzene rings to assemble four novel Zn(II) MOFs with the help N-donor ligands. According to previous literature, S.-Q. Zhang et al. synthesized two 1D and 3D Zn(II) MOFs using H3L4 ligand with 4,4′-bipyridine while we obtained a 2D Zn(II) MOFs based on H3L and 4,4′-bipyridine ligands [31]. We prepared another 2D Zn(II) MOFs with the help of 1,10-phen while S.-Q. Zhang et al. obtained a 0D Zn(II) MOFs with similar isomeric ligands [31]. The structure of these two 2D layers can be described as a 2D hcb topology network with the point symbol of {63}. Furthermore, it is worth noting that we firstly synthesized a 1D Zn(II) MOFs with 2,2′-bipyridine based on H3L ligand [9], [31]. An analysis of the Cambridge Structural Database (CSD) reveals only one trinuclear Co(II) complex [Co3(μ3-L)2(μ2-Py2Et)4] (Py2Et = 4,4′-ethane-1,2-diyldipyridine) involving H3L and Py2Et has been reported [30]. Thus, further efforts are required to provide strategy on the coordination behavior of H3L, in order to synthesize and obtain more new functional materials with such types of organic ligands.

In this work, one 1D chain CPs [Zn(HL)(2,2′-bpy)(H2O)]n (1), two 2D layer CPs [Zn(HL)(4,4′-bpy)]n·n(DMA) (2) and [Zn3(L)2(phen)3(H2O)]n·n(H2O) (3), and one 0D binuclear motif [Zn(HL)(phen)(H2O)]2 (4) have been synthesized with the help of different N-donor ligands including 2,2′-bpy, 4,4′-bpy and phen (Scheme 1). It is worth noting that the 3D supramolecular structures of these low-dimensional frameworks are reinforced by weak interactions such as hydrogen-bonding, π–π stacking, and Van der Waals interactions [32], [34], [35]. The Zn(II)-based MOFs often exhibit excellent luminescent properties, so the luminescent properties and thermal stabilities of these four CPs were comparatively investigated as well [15], [36].

2. Experimental

2.1. Materials and instrumentation

The metal salts, organic ligand and solvents for synthesis and analysis were of analytical grade and obtained from commercial sources without further purification. Complexes 1 and 2 were synthesized in 23 mL polytetrafluoroethylene glass containers while complexes 3 and 4 were obtained in 25 mL teflon-lined stainless steel containers. Elemental analyses of C, H and N were performed on an EA1110 CHNS-0 CE elemental analyzer. FT-IR spectra were measured as KBr pellets on a Nicolet Magna 750 FT-IR spectrometer in the range of 4000–400 cm−1. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) analyses were recorded on a Rigaku Dmax2500 diffractometer with Cu-Kα radiation (λ = 1.54056 Å) with a scan speed of 5°/min. TG data were collected at a heating rate of 10 K/min on a NETSCHZ STA-449C thermal analyzer in the temperature range 30–700 °C under nitrogen atmosphere. The luminescence spectra were measured at 293 K with a HITACHI F-4600 luminescence spectrophotometer.

2.2. Syntheses of complexes

2.2.1. [Zn(HL)(2,2′-bpy)(H2O)]n (1)

The starting materials of Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (0.060 g, 0.20 mmol), 2,2′-bpy (0.0312 g, 0.2 mmol) and H3L (0.0302 g, 0.10 mmol) were added in 23 mL polytetrafluoroethylene glass container. 2 mL DMA and 4 mL H2O were then added. The resulting mixture was stirred for 15 min at room temperature, and then the mixture was sealed and kept at 90 °C for two days, and then cooled naturally to the room temperature. Colorless piece crystals suitable for single-crystal X-ray diffraction were obtained by filtration with a yield of 0.0421 g, 78% (based on H3L). Anal. 2.3. Single-crystal X-ray diffraction determination

Suitable single crystals of 1–4 were carefully picked under an optical microscope and then glued to thin glass fibers. The X-ray intensity data for four compounds were collected on a computer-controlled Oxford Xcalibur E diffractometer with graphite monochromatic Mo-Kα radiation (λMo-Kα = 0.71073 Å) at 298 K. Empirical absorption corrections were applied using the SADABS program. The structures were solved primarily by the direct method (SHELXS-97) and refined by full-matrix least-squares (SHELXL-97) on F2 [37]. Anisotropic thermal parameters were used for the non-hydrogen atoms and isotropic parameters for the hydrogen atoms. Hydrogen atoms were added geometrically and refined using a riding model. The crystal data, as well as the details of the data collection and refinements of 1–4 are summarized in Table 1. Selected bond lengths and bond angles with their estimated standard deviation are listed in Table S1 (Supporting information).

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Description of crystal structures

3.1.1. Crystal structure of complex 1

As shown in Fig. 1a, single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that complex 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/n with one crystallographically independent Zn(II) ion, one 2,2′-bpy ligand, one HL2− ligand and one coordinated H2O molecule in the asymmetric unit. Zn1 locates in a six-coordinated environment completed by one oxygen atom from H2O molecule (Zn1–O1W = 2.0961(18) Å), two nitrogen atoms from 2,2′-bpy ligand (Zn1–N1 = 2.111(2) Å, Zn2–N2 = 2.124(2) Å), three oxygen atoms from two carboxyl groups of H3L ligand (Zn1–O1 = 2.0545(17) Å, Zn1–O3A = 2.0776(18) Å, Zn1–O4B = 2.420(2) Å). The neighboring Zn(II) ions are linked by the deprotonated carboxyl groups of HL2− ligands with the average distance of 5.6693(4) Å, generating a 1D infinite chain with the 2,2′-bpy ligands just chelated to Zn(II) ions (Fig. 1b). These neighboring 1D chains are further extended into a 2D supramolecular network via intermolecular π–π interactions (face–face distance amounting to 3.7928(18) Å and 3.6707(17) Å by Platon) between 2,2′-bpy ligands of neighboring chains (Fig. 1c and Table 2) [38]. In addition, the 2D structure is stabilized by intramolecular O–H⋯O3 hydrogen bonds, which originate from coordinated water and carboxylate oxygens (Table 3). These weak interactions consolidate the structure of 1 in cooperation with the coordination interaction.

3.1.2. Crystal structure of complex 2

Single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that compound 2 features a 2D architecture completed by the 4,4′-bpy ligands. There are one Zn(II) ion, one 4,4′-bpy molecule, one HL2− ligand, and one solvent DMA molecule in the asymmetric unit of 2. As can be found in Fig. 2a, Zn1 ion is in a trigonal bipyramid coordination environment built from two nitrogen atoms of two 4,4′-bpy ligands and three oxygen atoms of two HL2− ligands. The Zn–O and Zn–N bond lengths (1.908(3)–1.967(3) Å, 2.048(4)–2.063(4) Å) are in good agreement with those of previous studied (Table S1, Supporting information) [9], [31], [39]. It is worth noting that the basic building unit of 2 is a metallomacrocycle, which is constructed from six Zn(II) ions bridged by four HL2− ligands and four 4,4′-bpy linkers arranged in an alternate manner (Fig. 2b). The diagonal Zn1–Zn1A and Zn1–Zn1B separations are 5.0841(7) Å and 11.1061(8) Å. The adjacent metallomacrocycles are linked by Zn1 ions as node, forming a 2D layer in the ab plane (Fig. 2d). The neighboring 2D layers further extend into a 3D supramolecular framework via Van der Waals forces. Tubular channels are observed in 2 and possess a void volume (4.3%) per unit cell by PLATON analysis, which are occupied by the solvent DMA molecules [38]. Furthermore, we select Zn(II) ions as nodes and the two dimensional structure is simplified to a 3-connected underlying net with the point symbol of {63}, which belongs to the topological type hcb (Fig. 2c).

3.1.3. Crystal structure of complex 3

Complex 3 belongs to the monoclinic system with C2/c space group, which consists of two kinds of crystallographically different Zn(II) ions, one L3− ligand, one phen ligand, one coordinated water molecule and one free water molecule. Two kinds of crystallographically independent Zn(II) ions both locate in the octahedral coordination geometry formed by four oxygen atoms and two nitrogen atoms of phen ligand (Fig. 3a). However, all the oxygen atoms coordinating to Zn1 ion come from two L3− ligands while one oxygen atom of water molecule and three oxygen atoms of two L3− ligands coordinated to Zn2 ion. Every two Zn1 ions coordinate two phen ligand and four bidentate chelating carboxyl groups while the other carboxyl groups bridge the Zn2 ions, thus forming an infinite V-shaped chain along a-axis. On the basis of this conductive way, these neighboring 1D chain are further connected together by Zn(II) ions and L3− ligands to form a 2D layer in ab plane (Fig. 3b). It is worth noting that there are abundant intermolecular π–π interactions between phen ligands of neighboring layers to construct a 3D intricate network, and weak hydrogen bonding interactions originated from coordinated water molecules and carboxylate oxygen further reinforce the layers (Table 2, Table 3 and Fig. 3c). To the best of our knowledge, a complicated supramolecular architecture assembled via both hydrogen bond interactions and π–π interactions from simple layers still worth studying. From the topological view, H3L ligand can be considered as 3-connected node and the intricate framework of 3 can be simplified into a hcb topology network with the point symbol of {63} (Fig. 3d).

3.1.4. Crystal structure of complex 4

Complex 4 was obtained only by employing DMA instead of NaOH for preparing 3 as different solvent. The asymmetric unit of 4 contains one Zn(II) ion, one HL2− ligand, one phen ligand and one coordinated water molecule. As shown in Fig. 4a, Zn1 ion is in a distorted trigonal bipyramid coordination environment built from two nitrogen atoms of two 4,4′-bpy ligands, two oxygen atoms of two HL2− ligands and one oxygen atom of one coordinated water molecule. It is worth noting that complex 4 features a zero dimensional molecular structure with only two asymmetric units (Fig. 4b). However, its 3D packing network has a large potential solvent voids which occupy 19.3% of the crystal volume by calculations using PLATON (Fig. 4c, d) [38].

3.2. The effect of secondary N-donor ligands and solvents on the structures of 1–4

As can be seen in Scheme 2, three types of different coordination modes exist in 1–4. It is noteworthy that in complexes 1–2, H3L ligand displays the μ1-η1:μ1-η2 mode with one deprotonated carboxylate group adopting monodentate coordinated mode and the other adopting bidentate chelate coordinated modes. In 3, the H3L ligand exhibits the μ1-η1:μ1-η2:μ1-η2 mode to coordinate three metal ions. In 4, the coordination fashion of the H3L ligand is the μ1-η1:μ1-η1 mode with two of carboxylic groups are deprotonated and both adopt monodentate coordinated modes.

Compounds 1 and 2 are synthesized under similar conditions except different secondary N-donor ligands were used. However, compound 1 is an infinite chain while compound 2 is a 2D layer with a 3-connected {63} topology network. Most carboxylate ligands have been revealed to form isostructures of coordination polymers for most transition metals with the help of phen and 2,2′-bpy as secondary ligand due to their similar coordination modes [40], [41]. However, different framework structure observed among 1, 3 and 4 in the present case clearly indicates the size effect of the secondary N-donor ligand on the complex structure. In addition, compounds 3 and 4 are prepared under the similar conditions only with different solvent systems. In this case, three carboxylic groups of H3L ligand in 3 are all deprotonated while only two carboxylic groups have been deprotonated in 4, leading to a totally different structure between 3 and 4. Therefore, the differences in their molecular structures reveal that the significant effect of secondary N-donor ligands and solvents in tuning the molecular structures of coordination polymers as well as the bridging modes and conformations of H3L ligand [32], [42], [43], [44].

3.3. PXRD and thermal analysis

To check the purity and homogeneity of the complexes 1–4, the synthesized products of the four complexes were characterized by powder X-ray diffraction (RXPD) at room temperature. As shown in Figs. S1–S4 (Supporting information), the experimental PXRD patterns are in good agreement with the simulated ones, which clearly indicate the good purity and homogeneity of the complexes.

To investigate the thermal behaviors of 1–4, thermal gravimetric analyses (TGA) were performed in the temperature range of 30–700 °C under a flow of nitrogen (Fig. 5). The TG curve of 1 shows the weight loss owing to the release of lattice water molecule in the range from 30 to 195 °C (obsd: 3.29%, calcd: 3.33%). Then the organic spacers decompose from 245 to 558 °C. For 2, the weight loss of 12.14% in the range of 30–208 °C is attributed to the exclusion of one free DMA molecule (calcd: 11.99%). Both 3 and 4 show similar TGA curve due to their same ligands with only different molecular architecture. The TG curve of 3 indicates that there is a weight loss from 30 to 166 °C, which can be attributed to the loss of one water molecule (obsd: 1.32%, calcd: 1.28%). While the release of two water molecules observed in 4 is 3.28% in the range from 30 to 152 °C (calcd: 3.19%). Upon heating beyond 236 °C, the residual composition of this complex begins decomposing, leading to a collapse of the framework.

3.4. Luminescent property of the ligand and complexes 1–4

Taking into account the excellent luminescent properties of Zn-based complexes, the luminescent properties of complexes 1–4 have been investigated in the solid-state at room temperature. In order to compare these emission bands, we have also tested the luminescent property of the metal-free H3L and N-donor ligand. As shown in Fig. 6a, strong blue emission band is observed at 429 nm upon excitation of 350 nm for H3L, which can be probably assigned to the typical intraligand π∗ to π or n electronic transitions [15]. For the complexes (Table 4), the strong violet emission bands are 343 nm (λex = 250 nm) for 1, 395 nm (λex = 232 nm) for 2, 387 nm (λex = 220 nm) for 3, 370, 389 and 411 nm (λex = 225 nm) for 4, respectively (Fig. 6b). The emissions of the compounds may be a mixture of characters of intraland π* to π transition, namely, ligand-to-ligand charge transition (LLCT) [45], [46]. On the basis of previous result reported on the emission of metal-free H3L, 2,2′-bpy, 4,4′-bpy and phen ligands, the largely blue shift of the maximum emission wavelengths between the complexes and the ligands were considered to mainly originate from the influence of the coordination of the ligand to metal atom and ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT) [9], [32], [41], [47], [48]. The photoluminescence properties of these compounds indicate that they could be potentially used as luminescent materials.

4. Conclusions

In summary, four Zn(II) MOFs with asymmetric semi-rigid V-shaped tricarboxylate ligand and different N-donor auxiliary ligands have been synthesized under different solvent conditions. The diverse structures of the compounds revealed that different coordination modes of the central metal ions and the organic ligands play an important role in the formation of CPs, as well as co-regulation effect of auxiliary ligands and the reacting solvents. In addition, the non-covalent bonding interactions including hydrogen bonds and π⋯π stacking contribute to stabilize crystal structure and extend the low-dimensional entities into high-dimensional frameworks. Moreover, 1–4 exhibit intense ligand-centered fluorescence at room temperature, and may serve as candidates for luminescent materials.

    Three 1,10-phenantroline zinc halide complexes were successfully synthesized under solvothermal method and structurally characterized by single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis. Meanwhile, the molecules of Zn(1,10-phen)I2 and Zn(1,10-phen)2I2 further connect each other via the π-π interactions between the adjacent phenanthrolines to form a pseudo-two-dimensional layer which parallel to b-axis. It can be concluded that the number of ligand and the electron density on coordinated Zn atoms influence the emission wavelength through charge transfer from ligand to the metal. The emission wavelengths of 3 are also slightly red-shifted compared with the free 1,10-phen ligand, which is attributed to the coordination effects of the 1,10-phen ligands to the Zn cations and [C2O4]2− group.

  • Syntheses, crystal structures, and luminescence properties of three cadmium coordination polymers assembled from a semi-rigid tetracarboxylate ligand

    2022, Journal of Molecular Structure

    Crystal engineering is considerably useful in the preparation of functional coordination polymers. In the present study, a new semi-rigid tetracarboxylate, 4,5-bis(4-carboxyphenoxy)phthalic acid (H4L), was designed and applied for assembling metal coordination polymers. Self-assembling process involving Cd(II) metal ions, H4L and 4,4′-bipyridine (4,4′-bpy), (E)-1,2-bis(pyridin-4-yl)ethane (bpe) or 1,4-bis(1-imidazolyl)benzene (bib) resulted into the formation of coordination polymers, including [Cd2(L)(4,4′-bpy)1.5(H2O)]n·(H2O)n (1), [Cd2(L)(bpe)1.5(H2O)]n·(H2O)3.5n (2), and [Cd2(L)(bib)1.5]n·(H2O)n (3). Through X-ray single crystal diffraction analysis, the three compounds 1–3 were found to exhibit isostructural three-dimensional polymeric structures composed of two-dimensional secondary layers pillared by different rod-like N-donor ligands. Compared with the uncoordinated H4L exhibiting a symmetric crystal structure, the molecular configurations of L4− in 1–3 were tuned due to the complexation of cadmium ion and the introduction of different N-donors. Additionally, the coordination geometries of cadmium ions and luminescence properties in compounds 1–3 were finely tuned due to the introduction of different lengths of rod-like ligands. The thermostability and luminescence properties of compounds 1–3 were determined. The present results provide further understanding of the crystal engineering of semi-rigid ligands to a certain extent.
