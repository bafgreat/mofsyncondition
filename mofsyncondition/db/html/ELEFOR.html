Self-assembly of supramolecular networks based on biphosphopentamolybdate and copper–organic units

Author links open overlay panel
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.solidstatesciences.2010.12.004Get rights and content

Abstract

Two polyoxometalates (POMs)-based hybrid supramolecular materials have been synthesized based on biphosphopentamolybdate and copper–organic units under hydrothermal conditions, namely, [(HPO4)2Mo5O15][CuI(4,4′-bipy)]2[CuII(en)2]·6H2O (1) and [(HPO4)2Mo5O15][CuI(4,4′-bipy)]4·6H2O (2) (en = ethylenediamine and 4,4′-bipy = 4,4′-bipyridine). Their crystal structures have been characterized by elemental analysis, IR, thermal gravimetric analysis (TGA), and single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis. The novel 2D supramolecular sheet is formed via weak copper–oxygen interaction and hydrogen bonding in 1. The fascinating structural feature of 2 is that the biphosphopentamolybdate polyanion acts as a pillar to link CuI centers and shows six-connected nodes. The electrochemical behaviors of hybrid materials have been studied in detail.

1. Introduction

The study of supramolecular chemistry has attracted much interest due to their strong potential for a variety of applications including gas storage, catalytic properties, nonlinear optics, and magnetism [1], [2], [3], [4], [5], [6], [7]. One of the important targets in the preparation supramolecular materials is the extension of low-dimensional building blocks to high-dimensional networks between organic and/or inorganic molecular fragments by various forces including strong and directional interactions [8] and weak interactions, such as hydrogen-bonding, π–π stacking, CN⋯π interaction and CN⋯Cu interaction, halogen–halogen, Br⋯π interaction, and so on [9], [10], [11], [12], [13], [14], [15].

In recent years, extensive efforts have been focused on the assembly of supramolecular architectures POMs-based [16], [17], [18], [19], [20], [21]. However, self-assembly of supramolecular architectures based on molybdate and metal complex by weak metal–oxygen interactions has rarely been investigated. Up to now, only a limited number of hybrid materials have been observed. Such as [Cu(2,2′-bipy)2](HPMo12O40)·H2O and [CuII(bbi)2(α-Mo8O26)][CuI(bbi)]2 [22], [23]. Therefore, we are appealed that synthesis and exploration of supramolecular networks biphosphopentamolybdate-based via the weak metal–oxygen interactions.

The use of a suitable metal complex is expected to be a key factor in the formation of hybrid material with intriguing topologies. In terms of organic ligands, the geometric difference, such as donor group orientation, space length and topology, may produce dramatic changes in the arrays of metal complexes and POMs as well. In our strategy, biphosphopentamolybdate, 4,4′-bipy and en ligands and copper sulfate were chosen based on the following considerations: (i) biphosphopentamolybdate is an important branch in the POMs chemistry because of its varied coordination modes. (ii) The selection of the reducer plays a crucial role in the transformation of CuII ions into CuI ones. The CuII cations can be reduced to CuI cations by organic amine under hydrothermal conditions [24]. The paper provides molecular insights into the role of weak metal–oxygen interactions, dictating the weak metal–oxygen interactions between polyanions and copper–organic units are important for the formation of the different structures. Here, [(HPO4)2Mo5O15][CuI(4,4′-bipy)]2[CuII(en)]2·3H2O (1) and [(HPO4)2Mo5O15][CuI(4,4′-bipy)]4 ·6H2O (2) are successfully isolated by hydrothermal methods. In the paper, weak metal–oxygen interactions and hydrogen-bonding interactions leading to different supramolecular frameworks have been discussed.

2. Experimental

2.1. Materials and methods

All reagents were purchased commercially and used without further purification. Elemental analyses (C, H and N) were performed on a Perkin–Elmer 2400 CHN Elemental Analyzer. The IR spectra were obtained on an Alpha Centaurt FTIR spectrometer in the 400–4000 cm−1 region with a KBr pellet. The TG analyses were performed on a Perkin–Elmer TGA7 instrument under flowing N2 with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. Cyclic voltammograms were obtained with a CHI 660 electrochemical workstation at room temperature. A typical three-electrode cell having a CPE working electrode, platinum gauze was used as a counter electrode and Ag/AgCl electrode was used as the reference electrode.

2.2. Synthesis

2.2.1. [(HPO4)2Mo5O15][CuI(4,4′-bipy)]2[CuII(en)]2·3H2O 1

A mixture of Na2MoO4 ·2H2O (0.95 g, 3.92 mmol), CuSO4·5H2O (0.64 g, 2.57 mmol), H3PO4(0.8 ml, 12.76 mmol), en (0.5 ml, 6.65 mmol), 4,4′-bipy (0.12 g,0.78 mmol) and 14 ml H2O was stirred for 30 min in air (pH ≈ 5.0). The resulting gel was then transferred to a 20 mL Teflon-lined autoclave and kept at 140 °C for 5 days. After the mixture was slowly cooled to room temperature, green block crystals of 1 were filtered, washed with water and dried at room temperature. Yield: 22.8% based on Cu. Anal. Calc. for C24H46Cu3Mo5N8O29P2 (1642.95): calcd. C 17.54, H 2.82, N 6.825. Found C 17.57, H 2.59, N 6.87%. IR(KBr pellet): (cm−1) 3411(m), 3095(m), 1606(m), 1529(m), 1490(m), 1413(m), 1219(w), 1110(s), 1039(m), 1001(s), 892(s), 691(s), 660(s).

2.2.2. [(HPO4)2Mo5O15][CuI(4,4′-bipy)]4 ·6H2O 2

A mixture of Na2MoO4·2H2O (1.93 g, 7.98 mmol), CuSO4·5H2O (1.28 g, 5.13 mmol), H3PO4 (2.4 ml, 38.28 mmol), en (1.2 ml, 17.97 mmol), 4,4′-bipy (0.24 g, 1.56 mmol) and 28 ml H2O was stirred for 30 min in air. Then the pH was adjusted to 4.5 by en solution. The resulting gel was then transferred to a 30 mL Teflon-lined autoclave and kept at 140 °C for 6 days. After the mixture was slowly cooled to room temperature, yellow block crystals of 2 were filtered, washed with water and dried at room temperature. Yield: 41.5% based on Cu. Anal. Calc. for C40H46Cu4Mo5N8O29P2 (1898.65): C 25.30, H 2.44, N 5.90. Found C 25.28, H 2.47, N 5.92%. IR(KBr pellet): (cm−1) 3398(m), 3088(m), 1613(m), 1533(m), 1484(m), 1412(m), 1218(w), 1110(s), 1004(s), 897(s), 816(m), 687(s), 655(s).

2.3. X-ray crystallography study

Single-crystal X-ray data of 1 and 2 were collected on a Bruker SMART CCD diffractometer equipped with graphite monochromatized MoKa radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). Semi-empirical absorption corrections were applied using the SADABS program [25]. The structure was solved by direct methods and refined by the full-matrix least-squares methods on F2, which were performed using the SHELXTL-97 software package [26], [27]. All of the non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. Hydrogen atoms on carbon atoms and nitrogen atoms were included at calculated positions and refined with a riding model. All hydrogen atoms of the water molecules in compounds 1 and 2 could not be positioned reliably. The crystallographic data and structural determination of 1 and 2 are summarized in Table 1. Selected bond lengths and angles for 1 and 2 are listed in Table 2.

2.4. Preparation of 1 and 2 modified CPE (1-CPE and 2-CPE)

The 1-CPE (2-CPE) was prepared as follows: graphite powder (0.3 g) and compound 1 (2) (0.03 g) were mixed and ground together by an agate mortar and pestle to achieve a uniform mixture, and nujol (0.2 mL) was then added with stirring. The resulting paste was packed in a glass tube (3 mm diameter) and a copper rod was inserted through one end of the tube to make electrical contact, the other end was polished on a sheet of weighing paper.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Syntheses of the compounds

In compound 1, the presence of CuII and CuI indicates that the starting CuII ions are reduced by organic amine compositions partially. The different reaction pH values have an influence on the formation of final products. When the same raw starting materials are selected, different compounds are isolated at pH ≈ 5.0 for 1 and 4.5 for 2, respectively. The en should be the reducing agent, and it seems that higher pH value will promote the reduction. However, lower pH value for compound 2 results in more CuI, which may be integrated factors influence the outcome of “black-box” reaction, such as starting concentrations, reaction time, and temperature and so on. Compared with compounds 1 and 2, the molar ratio of H3PO4 and en may be plays an important effect on supramolecular architecture (see Scheme 1). Moreover, we also found that reaction systems of 1 and 2 are sensitive to reaction time. If the reaction time was less than 5 days, no or a few crystals could be obtained, the 5 and 6 days are the best reaction time for the quality and yield of 1 and 2, respectively.

3.2. Structural description

3.2.1. Crystal structure of 1

The single-crystal structural analysis suggests that compounds 1 and 2 are based on biphosphopentamolybdate [HxP2Mo5O23](6−x)−(x = 0,1,2) [denoted {P2Mo5}]. The {P2Mo5} cluster consists of edge and corner sharing MoO6 octahedra forming a Mo5O15 ring capped by two PO4 tetrahedra. The Mo–O distances vary from 1.695(3)–2.397(3)Å in 1, 1.698(2)–2.402(2)Å in 2, respectively. The P–O distances vary from 1.519(3)–1.557(3)Å in 1, 1.496(2)–1.571(2)Å in 2, respectively. Bond-valence calculation results indicate that polyanions are protonated in some terminal oxygen atoms of [PO4] tetrahedra (O2 and O2′ for 1; O21 and O23 for 2). Furthermore, the all the Mo and P centers of 1 and 2 display the +6 and +5 oxidation states [28]. These results are consistent with the charge balance considerations.

The structure of compound 1 is constructed from {Mo5P2O21(OH)2}4− anion, [CuI(4,4′-bipy)] and [CuII(en)2] complexes and lattice water molecules, as shown in Fig. 1a. There are three crystallographically unique Cu atoms in 1. The Cu(1) site is defined by two nitrogen donors from two 4,4′-bipy ligands, and Cu(2) site is defined by four nitrogen donors from two en ligands. The Cu–N distances are in the range 1.885(4)–2.023(4)Å. Each 4,4′-bipy ligand links two symmetrical Cu(1) atoms into a 1-D [CuI(4,4′-bipy)] complex chain along the b axis direction. Cu(2) cation has weak copper–oxygen interaction with terminal oxygen atom from {P2Mo5} cluster resulting to assemble the 1D infinite chain (Cu(2)#1–O(5) = 2.504 Å and Cu(2)–O(5)#2 = 2.504 Å, symmetry codes: #1 − 1 + x,y,z, #2 1 − x,1 − y,1 − z). (see Fig. 1b), which is stabilized by non-classic N⋯O and C⋯O hydrogen bonding between nitrogen or carbon atoms of en ligands and oxygen atoms of the {P2Mo5} clusters (Table S1). The interesting 2D supramolecular sheet is formed in the manner of “zipper” between chains (see Fig. 1c). The 2D sheet and [CuI(4,4′-bipy)] complex chains are alternatively arranged in an -ABAB- sequence along the c axis (see Fig. S1).

3.2.2. Crystal structure of 2

The structure of compound 2 is constructed from {Mo5P2O21(OH)2}4− anion, [CuI(4,4′-bipy)] complexes and lattice water molecules, as shown in Fig. 2a. There are four independent CuI centers in the asymmetric unit. The Cu(2) links through two 4,4′-bipy bridges with adjacent Cu(1) and Cu(3) atoms, exhibiting a linear geometry in the form of {CuN2}. The Cu(4) is coordinated by two nitrogen atoms of two 4,4′-bipy ligands to form other corrugation chain. Cu–N bond distances and N–Cu–N angles are 1.881(3)–1.941(3) Å and 161.15(14)–178.77(14)°, respectively. In addition, each polyanion has weak copper–oxygen interactions with distances of 2.536 Å (Cu(1)#1–O(8)), 2.456 Å(Cu(2)#2–O(4)), 2.466 Å (Cu(3)#3–O(3)), 2.854 Å (Cu(3)#4–O(11)), 2.562 Å (Cu(4)#5–O(6)), 2.829 Å (Cu(4)#6–O(14)) and 2.856 Å (Cu(4)#6–O(13)), (symmetry codes: #1 1 − x,1 − y,1 − z, #2 x,−1 + y,1 + z, #3–0.5 + x, 1.5 − y,0.5 + z, #4 − x,1 − y,1 − z, #5 1 − x,2 − y,−z, #6 1 − x,y,0.5 − z) (see Fig. 2b). Each polyanion as six-connected node, coordinates to six CuI cations to form a intricate 3D supramolecular framework (see Fig. 2c). The supramolecular framework of 2 is further stabilized by hydrogen bonding among water molecules, organic groups and polyoxoanions (Table S2).

3.3. Effect of the copper complexes and coordination modes of {P2Mo5} clusters

Further insight into the nature of the compounds 1 and 2 indicates that the different copper complexes and the coordination modes of {P2Mo5} clusters have significant effects on the final structures of compounds 1 and 2. The {P2Mo5} clusters can coordinate to metal ions in a variety of coordination modes to form many attractive compounds with different dimensions [29], [30], [31], [32], [33]. CuII cation tends to form the octahedral coordination geometry, CuI ion prefers a linear, “T-shaped”, or distorted tetrahedron coordination geometry. The interactions between POMs and copper complexes play an important role in forming the ultimate structures. In 1, metal–organic units and polyanions are linked through weak copper–oxygen interactions to form 1D chain. In 2, polyanions pillared metal–organic chains to generate six-connected network via weak copper–oxygen interactions. These results indicate that metal–organic units and polyanions with different coordination modes are linked to each other through weak copper–oxygen interactions to obtain different supramolecular framework of POM-based.

3.4. IR spectra

As shown in Figs. S2 and S3, the IR spectra of compounds 1 and 2 exhibit prominent characteristic peaks for the {P2Mo5} structure at 892, 691 and 660 cm−1 for 1, and 897, 687 and 655 cm−1 for 2, which can be attributed to the ν(Mo–Ot) and ν(Mo–Ob/c–Mo), respectively. The strong peaks in the 1001–1039 cm−1 range are attributed to ν(P–O) bands, while the bands in the 1110–1614 cm−1 region are assigned to the en and 4,4′-bipy ligands. The broad band at about 3088–3411 cm−1 range is associated with the water molecules.

3.5. TG analysis

In order to examine the thermal stability of compounds 1 and 2, their TGA were carried out in N2 atmosphere with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. In the TG curve of 1 (see Fig. S4), there exist two weight loss stages. Below 340 °C, weight loss of 6.68% corresponds to the successive release of water molecules in good agreement with the calculated value (6.93%). Weight loss of 26.66% (calculated 26.34%) observed from 350 to 500 °C is attributed to the release of en and 4,4′-bipy ligands. In the TG curve of 2 (see Fig. S5), there exist two weight loss stages. Below 350 °C, the initial weight loss of 5.65% corresponds to the release of water molecules (calculated 5.69%). The second weight loss of 33.08% occurring between 350 °C and 500 °C is release of the 4,4′-bipy ligands (32.90%). The whole weight loss (38.73%) is in good agreement with the calculated value (38.59%). TGA support the chemical composition.

3.6. Voltammetric behavior of 1-CPE and 2-CPE

The electrochemical studies of 1-CPE and 2-CPE were carried out in 1M H2SO4 aqueous solution. Fig. 3 shows the cyclic voltammogram of 1-CPE. It can be seen that in the potential range from +650 to −650 mV, there are three quasi-reversible redox peaks I–I′, II–II′ and III–III′ with the half-wave potentials E1/2 = (Epa + Epc)/2 at 33 (I–I′), −154(II–II′) and −495 mV(III–III′) (scan rate: 20 mV s−1), respectively. The redox peak I–I′ may be corresponds to CuII/Cu0 [34]. The redox peaks II–II′ and III–III′ may be attribute to MoVI/MoV [35], [36]. The behavior of CuII/Cu0 couple is because of the stability of the CuI forms in the present hydrothermal conditions and in the pH = 1.0 H2SO4 aqueous solution all CuI are effectively oxidated to CuII.

Fig. 4 shows the cyclic voltammogram of 2-CPE. It can be seen that in the potential range from +700 to −700 mV, there are two quasi-reversible redox peaks II–II′ and III–III′ with the half-wave potentials E1/2 = (Epa + Epc)/2 at −299(II–II′) and −559 mV(III–III′) (scan rate: 20 mV s−1), respectively. The redox peaks II–II′ and III–III′ may be attribute to MoVI/MoV. It is worth noting that two overlapped reduction peaks (I′ and I″) are observed at 52 and −69 mV, respectively, which are similar to the two one-electron reduction waves of CuII ions owing to the stabilization of CuI, then its further reduction will be retarded and two one-electron waves are observed [37]. Keita et al. also found the stepwise reduction of copper in CuII-substituted P2Mo2W15O61 complex aqueous solution but gave no detailed explanation [38]. The sharp stripping peak (I) corresponds to an anodic stripping process of Cu0 to CuII [39], [40]. By comparison, the potential shifts of the Mo and Cu redox peaks comparing reported in above may relate to the effect of polyanions and the organic–inorganic hybrid framework.

4. Conclusions

In conclusion, we have designed and synthesized two hybrid materials based on {P2Mo5} cluster and copper–organic units under hydrothermal conditions. The various copper–organic units which are formed by en and/or 4,4′-bipy ligands combined with CuII/CuI cations, {P2Mo5} cluster with different coordination modes, and the weak interactions between polyanions and copper–organic units are important for the formation of the different structures. The combination of in situ synthesization, supramolecular assembly, and POM chemistry, opens up new possibilities in pursuit of multifunctional materials.

  • Self-assembly of γ-octamolybdate-based frameworks decorated by 1,3-bis(4-pyridyl)propane through covalent bond

    2013, Inorganica Chimica Acta

    Two new octamolybdate-based coordination polymers constructed from Mo–N bond (Hbpp)4[Mo8O26(bpp)2]·6H2O (1) and (H2bpp)2[Mo8O26(bpp)] (2) (bpp = 1,3-bis(4-pyridyl)propane) were synthesized by hydrothermal method and characterized by elementary analysis, IR and single crystal X-ray diffraction. Both of the compounds are the first report of unique bisupported structure built from Mo-N bonds between [γ-Mo8O26]4- anions and bpp ligands, which come from environmentally friendly aqueous solutions and avoiding serious environmental problem. Compound 1 has 3D supramolecular framework based on hydrogen bonds between [γ-Mo8O26(bpp)2] fragment, free bpp ligand and water molecules. Compound 2 contains [γ-Mo8O26]-organic amine 1D chain by Mo–N bonds. The thermal stability and fluorescence properties of 1 and 2 were investigated in the solid state.

  • Self-assembly of four one-dimensional molybdenum(V) phosphates linked by strontium and transition metal

    2012, Inorganica Chimica Acta

    Four reduced molybdenum(V) phosphates, [(py)2(Himi)4][FeII2SrMo12O24(OH)6(H2O)8(H2PO4)2(HPO4)4(PO4)2]·4H2O 1, (H2bpp)2[CoII2Sr2Mo12O24(OH)6(H2O)8(H2PO4)2(HPO4)2(PO4)4]·6H2O 2, [(Hpy)(Himi)][Ni(H2O)6][Ni2Sr2Mo12O24(OH)6(H2O)8(H2PO4)2(HPO4)2(PO4)4]·4H2O 3, (H2imi)2[Zn2Sr2Mo12O24(OH)6(H2O)6(H2PO4)2(HPO4)2(PO4)4]·3H2O 4 (imi = imidazole, bpp = 1,3-bi(4-pyridyl)-propane, py = pyridine), have been obtained by hydrothermal methods. These structures were determined by single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis and were further characterized by elemental analysis, IR, and TG analysis. All the compounds contain the [P4Mo6O31]12− (abbreviated as [P4Mo6]) polyoxoanions as building blocks, which are further connected into the sandwich-type dimeric units by various transition metal (TM) ions. In compound 1, the dimeric {Fe[P4Mo6]2} units are bonded by an heterometallic {SrFe} binuclear unit to form 1-D string. In compounds 2–4, the dimeric {(TM)[P4Mo6]2} units are linked by new {Sr2TM} trinuclear fragments into one-dimensional (1-D) chains. All the 1-D chains are further packed into various three-dimensional (3-D) supramolecular assemblies via strong hydrogen-bonding interactions. Furthermore, the electrochemical activities of compounds 1–4 and magnetic properties of compounds 1–3 are reported.
