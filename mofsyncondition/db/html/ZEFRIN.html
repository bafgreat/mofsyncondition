Introduction

Over the past decade, research on multi-dimensional metal coordination polymers1 has raised increasing interest for various applications, including gas storage,2 separation,3 catalysis,4 magnetism,5 luminescence,6 and electrodes for Li-ion batteries.7 The synthesis or crystal engineering of metal coordination polymers is generally achieved by using metal ions as connectors and rigid or flexible organic ligands as linkers. The concept of secondary building units (SBUs) is an essential concept for the classification and construction of MOF structures.8,9 Inorganic SBUs contain only inorganic parts with discrete units or clusters, one-dimensional chains, two-dimensional layers, and even three-dimensional networks. These inorganic motifs are linked by organic ligands through various bridging models to produce robust structures and related properties. The topology is greatly influenced by the type and shape of these bridging organic ligands. A number of aromatic carboxylate ligands have been demonstrated to achieve open frameworks with diverse structures for emerging applications.9 We have recently investigated the use of V-shaped 4,4′-sulfonyldibenzoic acid (H2SBA) to generate two Ca and Sr metal–organic frameworks using microwave-assisted solvothermal reactions. Both compounds display high thermal stability up to approximately 450 °C and demonstrate gas adsorption of N2 and H2 at 77 K and of CO2 at room temperature.16h The 4,4′-oxybisbenzoic acid (H2OBA) is also a V-shaped dicarboxylate ligand and has been reported to form various complexes with transition metal ions8 and lanthanide ions.9 The s-block alkali and alkaline-earth metal coordination polymers are still rarely reported despite the possibility that they may possess varied coordination numbers and unexpected structural features.7,10–17 As a continuation of our extensive research on V-shaped dicarboxylate ligands, we adapted H2OBA to react with alkali and alkaline earth metal ions and generate eight metal coordination polymers.

There have been few studies on lithium coordination polymers with interesting electrochemical behaviors that could be used as electrodes in lithium ion batteries.7 A novel lithium coordination polymer, [Li2(C6H2O4)], was used as positive electrode material with an discharge capacity of 176 mAh g−1 and a coulombic efficiency of 93.18% in the first cycle.7a Two organic salts of lithium terephthalate and lithium trans–trans–muconate displayed higher electrode capacities up to 300 mAh g−1.7b These reported results show that those bonds between metal ions and organic ligands of lithium coordination polymers can act as redox centers during electrochemical reactions. Continuing this interesting research, a structural analysis and electrochemical properties for the lithium complex 1 are also presented.

Experimental section

Materials and measurements

Chemicals of reagent grade or better were used as received. Solvothermal reactions were conducted by heating the reaction mixtures in 23 mL Teflon-lined digestion bombs to the desired temperature under autogenous pressure, followed by slow cooling at 6 °C per hour to room temperature. Elemental analyses were performed to confirm the organic compositions of the copolymers. Thermal gravimetric analysis (TGA) was performed using a DuPont TA Q50 analyzer with powder samples under flowing nitrogen gas with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. Cyclic voltammetry (CV) was performed using an Autolab/PGSTAT-101 in combination with the powerful Nova software electrochemical analyzer (Metrohm Autolab) in the potential range of 0.1 to 3.0 V (V vs. Li/Li+) with a scan rate of 0.5 mV s−1. Electrochemical measurements were conducted in CR2032 coin cells. The electrode film was prepared with 60 wt.% of complex 1 as an active material, 30 wt.% of Super P carbon black as a conductive additive, and 10 wt.% of poly(vinylidene difluoride) (PVDF) as a binder. N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone (NMP) was used as the solvent to make a slurry. The slurry was uniformly coated onto a copper foil as a current collector with a doctor blade. The material was cut into circular electrodes with areas of 1.37 cm2 and dried under a vacuum at 100 °C for 6 h. Coin cells were assembled in an argon-filled glovebox with a lithium foil as the anode, Celgard 2320 as the separator, and a solution of 1.0 M LiPF6 in ethylene carbonate (EC)/dimethyl carbonate (DMC) (1 : 1 by volume) as the electrolyte. Galvanostatic discharge/charge tests were conducted using a Maccor 2200 battery test system. The cells were cycled between 0.1–3.0 V with different current densities for both the discharge and charge processes. Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) was performed in three-electrode mode after 0.13 mA cm−2 (0.1 C) charge and discharge by coupling the potentiostat with an Autolab frequency response analyzer. A sinusoidal amplitude modulation of ±10.0 mV was used over a frequency range of 0.01 Hz to 1 MHz.

Results and discussion

Structural description of [Li2(OBA)] (1)

Single crystal X-ray diffraction (Table 1) showed that the structure of compound 1 possesses a 3D network. The asymmetric unit contains one Li atom and one half of the OBA ligand. The Li(i) ion is coordinated by four oxygen atoms from four independent OBA ligands with typical Li–O bond values from 1.894(3) to 2.006(3) Å (Fig. 1a).7,10 The OBA anion bonds with eight different Li(i) ions and adopts a symmetric μ8 coordination mode (Fig. S1, Supporting Information†). As shown in Fig. 1b, the LiO4 distorted tetrahedra share edges and create inorganic motifs in the form of 1D chains running along the b-axis. These chains are linked together by μ8 links of the OBA ligands along the c-axis to generate the 3D network of 1. Topological analysis revealed that compound 1 can be described as a fluorite net (Fig. S3a†), as defined by the Reticular Chemistry Structure Resource,18 or as a 2-nodal 4,8-connected net with a Schläfli symbol of {412.612.84}{46}2, determined using TOPOS.19

Structural description of [Na2(OBA)(H2O)] (2)

Single crystal X-ray diffraction (Table 1) showed that the structure of compound 2 possesses an extended 3D network. The asymmetric unit contains two Na atoms, one OBA ligand, and one coordinated water molecule (Fig. 2a). The Na(1) site is six-coordinated by five oxygen atoms of the carboxylate groups of four OBA ligands and one oxygen atom of the coordinated water molecule. The Na(2) center is five-coordinated by four oxygen atoms of the carboxylate groups of four OBA ligands and one coordinated water molecule. All of the Na−O bonds distances range from 2.294(2) to 2.793(2) Å, which is comparable to distances reported for other sodium coordination polymers.11 The OBA anion bonds to eight sodium atoms and adopts an octadentate μ8 coordination mode (Fig. S1†). The NaO6 and NaO5 polyhedra share edges and corners to form an inorganic layer. These inorganic sheets form a (6,3)-net and are further connected by the OBA ligands along the a-axis to generate the 3D network of 2 (Fig. 2d). The topology of compound 2 can be described as a 2-nodal 5,8-connected net (Fig. S3b†) with a Schläfli symbol of {32.410.58.64.74}{32.46.52}2. Intermolecular O–H⋯O hydrogen bonds were found between the carboxylate oxygen atoms and coordinated water molecules (Fig. S2 and Table S2†).

Structural description of [K(HOBA)] (3), [Rb(HOBA)] (4), and [Cs(HOBA)] (5)

Single crystal X-ray diffraction (Table 1) showed that compounds 3–5 are isostructural and possess extended 3D structures similar to that of 2. In compound 3, the asymmetric unit contains half of the K atom and half of the HOBA ligand (Fig. 3a). The K(1) site is eight-coordinated by eight oxygen atoms of the carboxylate groups belonging to eight independent HOBA ligands. The K–O bond distances range from 2.719(1) to 3.396(1) Å.12 In compound 4, the Rb–O bond distances13 range from 2.882(1) to 3.284(1) Å, and in compound 5, the Cs–O bond distances range14 from 3.072(2) to 3.312(2) Å. In compound 3, the HOBA anion bonds to eight different potassium atoms and adopts a symmetric μ8 coordination mode (Fig. S1†). The KO8 polyhedra share corners to form an inorganic 2D layer. These inorganic sheets of a (4,4)-net and are further connected by μ8-links of the HOBA ligands along the b-axis to generate a 3D network (Fig. 3b). The topology of compounds 3–5 can be described as a 2-nodal 6,6-connected net (Fig. S3c†) with a Schläfli symbol of {413.62}{48.66.8}. The structure is further stabilized by O–H⋯O hydrogen bonds between the oxygen atoms of the neighboring carboxylate groups. The assignment of hydroxide in the carboxylate groups was consistent with the O(1)–C(1) bond length of 1.296(2) Å for 3, the O(1)–C(1) bond length of 1.294(2) Å for 4, and the O(2)–C(1) bond length of 1.291(3) Å for 5 (Fig. S2 and Table S2†).

Structural description of [Mg(OBA)(H2O)2] (6)

Compound 6 has a 2D sheet structure and crystallizes in the triclinic space group P21/c (Table 1). The asymmetric unit consists of one Mg center, one OBA ligand and two coordinated water molecules. The magnesium atoms are six-coordinated by four oxygen atoms of three OBA ligands and two oxygen atoms of coordinated water molecules, with typical Mg–O bond lengths ranging from 2.002(3) to 2.169(3) Å (Fig. 4a).15 The OBA anion bonds to three magnesium atoms and adopts a μ3 coordination mode (Fig. S1†). Each layer is formed by MgO6 octahedra which are linked by the OBA ligands along the ac plane through a (6,3)-connection. These layers of 6 stack along the b-axis (Fig. 4b). Additional hydrogen bonds between the layers are accomplished by the coordinated water oxygen atoms and oxygen atoms of the carboxylate groups (Fig. S2 and Table S2†).

Structural description of [Ca(OBA)(H2O)] (7)

Crystal structure analysis (Table 1) revealed that compound 7 possesses an extended 3D network. The asymmetric unit contains one Ca atom, one OBA ligand, and one water molecule of crystallization (Fig. 5a). The Ca(ii) ion is six-coordinated and binds five oxygen atoms from the carboxylate groups belonging to five OBA ligands and one oxygen atom from the coordinated water molecule. All Ca–O bond distances are within the range of 2.294(1) to 2.390(1) Å (Table S1†).16 The OBA anion bonds to five different calcium atoms and adopts a μ5 coordination mode (Fig. S1†). Two Ca(1) polyhedra share edges and thus form Ca2O10 dimers.16a These dimers are connected to one another through two carboxylic groups in a Ca(1)–O–C–O–Ca(1) bonding system. Consequently, a layer in the ac plane is formed. The layers are pillared by the OBA ligands to generate a 3D network (Fig. 5c). The resulting three-dimensional network is a 2-nodal 5-connected net with a Schläfli symbol of {45.65} (Fig. S3d†). In this structure, intermolecular O–H⋯O hydrogen bonds were also found between the carboxylate oxygen atoms and coordinated water molecules (Fig. S2 and Table S2†).

Structural description of [Sr(OBA)(H2O)] (8)

Crystal structure analysis (Table 1) revealed that the structure of compound 8 possesses a 3D network. The asymmetric unit contains one Sr atom, one OBA ligand, and one crystal water molecule (Fig. 6a). The Sr atom is seven-coordinated by six oxygen atoms of the carboxylate groups in four OBA ligands and one oxygen atom from the coordinated water molecule. The OBA anion bonds to five different Sr(ii) ions and adopts a μ5 coordination mode (Fig. S1†). All of the Sr– bond distances range from 2.496(2) to 2.673(4) Å (Table S1†).17 The inorganic motif displays a zigzag 1D chain formed by edge-sharing of SrO7 polyhedra. These 1D chains are connected by the organic ligands in two different directions to form a 3D network (Fig. 6b). The 3D network for complex 8 is also a 2-nodal 5-connected net with a Schläfli symbol of {44.64} (Fig. S3e†). Intermolecular O–H⋯O hydrogen bonds were found between the carboxylate oxygen atoms and coordinated water molecules (O1w⋯O5 and O1w⋯O4, Fig. S2 and Table S2†).

Thermogravimetric analysis

The TG curves (Fig. S12†) showed that compounds 2 and 6–8 have first weight losses below 200 °C, corresponding to the release of coordinated water molecules. The dehydrated phases are thermally stable up to approximately 500 °C. Compounds 1, 3, 4, and 5 are thermally stable up to approximately 500, 300, 200, and 370 °C, respectively. Further heating of all compounds up to 800 °C resulted in the decomposition of all the structures. The final residues of 6–8 were examined by XRD as mostly the metal oxides (Fig. S15†).

Electrochemical performance

The electrochemical properties of complex 1 were studied by cyclic voltammetry (CV), charge/discharge tests, rate capability tests, and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS). The cyclic voltammograms of [Li2(OBA)] (1) in the first five cycles are presented in Fig. 7. During the first Li+ insertion process, a very wide reduction ramp between 1.5 V and 0.1 V was observed, possibly due to the formation of a solid electrolyte interface (SEI). After one cycle, the SEI film completely covered the anode surface, and the broad ramp disappeared in the following cycles. In addition, three reversible redox peaks located at 0.33, 1.0 and 1.3 V were observed, indicating that complex 1 is a potential anode material for Li-ion batteries. Oxidation was observed in negative sweeping from the open circuit potentials of 0.5, 1.0 and 2.5 V, which corresponded to the extraction of Li+ ions from the structure. On the first negative sweep, we observed a clear reduction process at 0.3 V versus Li that was partially obscured by several other cathodic processes increasing toward the negative potential limit. The first process may be the reduction of the delocalized carboxylate groups as redox centres (the bond distances of C1–O1 and C1–O2 are about 1.26 Å) to form new Li–O bonds. The oxidation processes appearing in the subsequent positive sweeps, with three minor peaks at 0.4, 1.0, and 2.5 V, can be ascribed to the oxidation of the lithium salt to break the extra Li–O bonds. In the reduction process, each delocalized carboxylate group might accept two electrons and insert two Li ions to form lithium alkoxide, and the Li ions are extracted in the reverse oxidation process. Charge/discharge tests of the organic electrode containing 1 were performed in the range of 0.1 to 3.0 V at a rate of 1 Li+ 10 h−1versus Li0; the charge/discharge curves for the first 30 cycles are shown in Fig. 8. The first charge and discharge capacities were 327 mAh g−1 and 106 mAh g−1, respectively. The coulombic efficiency at the first cycle was determined to be 32.4%. After 30 cycles, the discharge capacity remained at 100 mAh g−1, illustrating the good cyclability of the electrode. In addition, the coulombic efficiency of the sample was at least 93%, which is in good agreement with the CV measurements. During the following recharge, the charge voltage traced back the discharge perfectly for the organic electrode with a small polarization (100–200 mV). This indicates a reversible Li uptake with an irreversible capacity between the first charge and discharge that does not exceed, at worst, 35%. All the cells displayed good capacity retentions after 30 cycles. The rate capability tests of 1 are presented in Fig. 9. The results show the ability of the material to achieve excellent specific storage capacities at different C-rates, which may be due to the channel-like structures enhancing the diffusion of Li+ ions. The reversible capacities were approximately 100, 80, 65, and 56 mAh g−1 at 0.1 C, 0.2 C, 0.5 C, and 1 C, respectively.

To further investigate the impedance in the interfaces, the material was studied by impedance spectroscopy (Fig. 10), and the results were compared with the behavior of previously described [Li2(BDC)] (where BDC = 1,4-benzenedicarboxylate).7b The AC impedance was measured after the cells were galvanostatically charged (0.1 C) to 0.1 V. In the complex impedance plot, the semicircle has a high-frequency intercept of the curve with the real impedance axis that corresponds to the ionic resistance of the electrolyte (Re) caused by the migration of the Li ions in the electrolyte. At the low-frequency intercept of the semicircle with the Z′ axis, the resistance related to the charge transfer resistance between the electrolyte and the active material (Rct) can be identified. The low-frequency tail represents the third region with typical Warburg behavior, which is related to the diffusion of lithium ions in the cathode material. At the high-frequency region, the related resistance was attributed to both inter-particle electronic contacts and ionic migration through the passivation layer, the polymer layer, and the conductive additives. As shown in Fig. 10, the semicircle resistances at the high-frequency region of the reported electrode, [Li2(BDC)], were lower than that of 1, while the diffused magnitudes of the lithium ions were larger than in 1. The abundance of resonance on the carboxylates enhanced the electrochemical activity for the lithium coordination polymers, as reported for [Li2(BDC)]. The results in this work might suggest that the abundance of resonance on the electrode in [Li2(BDC)] could reduce the resistance. However, the [Li2(OBA)] (1) possesses the oxygen atom, resulting in a loss of resonance on the Li coordination polymers and a resistance. This work demonstrates that the lithium carboxylates groups in the lithium coordination polymers complexes might also have the capacity and electrochemical properties to be potential electrodes in Li-ion batteries.

To gain further insights into the Li insertion–extraction mechanism within this [Li2(OBA)] (1) organic electrode, we followed the crystal structure change of the electrode material by ex-situ XRD during one discharge and charge cycle for the discharged and recharged samples recovered from the cycled cells. On Li uptake, two extra peaks with 2θ values at 14° and 17° appeared in the powder X-ray diffraction patterns of the [Li2(OBA)] electrode (Fig. 11), whereas the main peaks of 1 remained the same. Thus, despite these changes, the fully recharged material showed an XRD pattern that was fully superimposed on the pristine material, unambiguously indicating a fully reversible process in which compound 1 maintains its original structure.

Conclusions

In summary, we have successfully synthesized and characterized eight alkali and alkaline earth metal coordination polymers by solvothermal reactions. The various inorganic motifs were constructed with the dicarboxylate ligand OBA to form 2D and 3D structures. The coordination variability of OBA is even richer with polycarboxylate anions used as linkers with bridging modes μn, where subscript n varies between 3, 5, 6, and 8. Indeed, the investigation of metal coordination polymers based on carboxylates with alkaline earth metals represents an interesting and fruitful part of solid state and materials chemistry. The present results showed a preliminary light upon the electrochemical studies of lithium coordination polymers. Moreover, the possibility of employing lithium coordination polymers as an electrode material for Li-ion batteries was introduced. Further work will proceed with a continued investigation of ion-exchanged forms. The more different organic ligands with functional groups like carboxylate or sulfonate are of great interest and worth consideration to achieve higher capacities or potentials.
