Ring-shaped molecules have attracted significant scientific interest due to their intriguing structural features (e.g., discrete molecular entities, confined cyclic spaces, and well-defined sizes), relevance in biological systems, and wide potential applications in materials science.1–18 As such, particular attention has been paid to develop molecular rings with novel composition and topology. Metallacycles, formed by coordination between metal ions and organic ligands, are of intense current interest in supramolecular chemistry and are acknowledged as an important class of molecular rings, as they have particularly diverse structural types (e.g., triangles, rectangles, rhomboids, and hexagons) with tunable size, shape, and functionality and hold great promise for applications in sensing, optics, catalysis, and host–guest chemistry.1–8

Polyoxometalate macrocycles, built by covalent bond interaction between high valent early transition metal ions and oxygen atoms, are another important class of molecular rings. Their nanoscale size, high metal nuclearity and structural beauty, combined with rich electronic and magnetic properties, have motivated intensive synthetic research and the creation of many kinds of fascinating POM macrocycles, such as polyoxovanadate macrocycles,9,10 polyoxotungstate macrocycles,11,12 and well-known polyoxomolybdate macrocycles.13–18

The fusion of different research areas can create innovative materials and even new research areas though it is often challenging at the beginning. Unifying metallacycles and POM macrocycles in the same molecule would lead to brand-new composite molecular macrocycles with greater compositional and topological diversity and interesting properties not displayed by either component. However, despite great success having been achieved in both metallacycles and POM macrocycles, their integration into an ordered molecular entity is still a significant challenge because of the lack of feasible synthetic routes for rationally interfacing these two remarkably different materials.

With our ongoing research on polyoxoniobates (PONbs),19,20 here, we report for the first time the integration of metallacycles and PONb macrocycles into a molecule, forming a family of unprecedented bi-/tri-layer POM-metallacycle composite macrocycles, including 48-Nb-12-Cu-containing {[Cu12(mtz)6(etz)6(en)6][H4Nb48O138(H2O)6]}20− (1), 48-Nb-6-Cu-containing {[Cu6(atz)6(H2O)6][H13Nb48O138(H2O)6]}17− (2), 64-Nb-12-Cu-containing {[Cu12(tz)8(en)8][H10Nb64O184(H2O)8]}22− (3), and 48-Nb-12-Cu-containing{[Cu12(OH)6(atz)6(enMe)6][H5Nb48O138-(H2O)6]}19− (4) (Hmtz = methyl-1,2,4-triazole, Hetz = 3-ethyl-1,2,4-triazole, Hatz = amine-1,2,4-triazole, Htz = 1,2,4-triazole, en = ethanediamine, enMe = 1,2-diaminopropane). These novel composite materials comprise various PONb macrocyclic motifs, including 24-nuclearity {Nb24O69(H2O)3} (1.5 nm), larger 32-nuclearity {Nb32O92(H2O)4} (2.0 nm), and the largest 48-nuclearity {Nb48O138(H2O)6} (2.5 nm) known to date. Additionally, they also comprise diverse metallacyclic motifs, including hexagonal {Cu12(mtz)6 (etz)6(en)6} and {Cu6(atz)6(H2O)6}, square {Cu12(tz)8(en)8}, and circular {Cu12(OH)6(atz)6(enMe)6}. With the exception of the triangular motif {Nb24O69(H2O)3},21 all of the above PONb macrocyclic and metallacyclic motifs have not been known prior to this work.

Composite macrocycle 1, obtained by the reaction of the hexaniobate precursor K7HNb6O19·13H2O with Cu(Ac)2·H2O, en, Hmtz, and Hetz at 90 °C and pH = 9.4, crystallizes as a Cu-complex salt [Cu(en)2]10[1]·solvent. Its molecular structure comprises one PONb macrocycle {Nb48O138(H2O)6} (Nb48) and one copper metallacycle {Cu12(mtz)6(etz)6(en)6} (Cu12-A). The former is an anionic inorganic ring, while the latter is a cationic organic–inorganic hybrid ring. As shown in Fig. 1, the high-nuclearity Nb48 wheel comprises six Lindqvist hexaniobate clusters {Nb6O19}, which are arranged in a hexagonal geometry and held together by a large Nb–O 12-ring {Nb12O54(H2O)6}, forming an intriguing C6v-symmetric 48-nuclearity PONb macrocycle with inner and outer diameters of ca. 1.1 and 2.5 nm, respectively. The nanosized Nb–O 12-ring is made of alternate corner-sharing Nb–O octahedra of six NbO6 and six NbO5(H2O). Each Nb center of NbO6 octahedra bonds to three μ3-O atoms from one triangular face of a {Nb6O19} cluster, two μ2-O atoms sharing with its two adjacent NbO5(H2O) octahedra, and one terminal oxo, while each Nb center of NbO5(H2O) octahedra acts as a hinge between two {Nb6O19} clusters, with its equatorial positions occupied by four μ2-O atoms sharing with two {Nb6O19} clusters and two NbO6 octahedra and its axial positions occupied by one terminal oxo atom and one terminal H2O ligand (Nb–H2O: 2.455 Å). Up to now, only two types of PONb macrocycles of 24-nuclearity trigonal {Nb24O69(H2O)3}21 (Nb24) and 32-nuclearity tetragonal {Nb32O92(H2O)4}22 (denoted here as α-Nb32) have been known. Here, Nb48 shows not only a rare new-type PONb macrocycle, but also by far the largest macrocycle observed in PONb chemistry.

Cu12-A with C6-symmetry consists of 12 Cu2+, 6 mtz−, 6 etz−, and 6 en ligands (Fig. 1). Every two Cu2+ ions are bridged by one μ2-mtz ligand to form a bimetallic {Cu2(mtz)} subunit. Six such subunits are interlinked via six μ3-etz ligands into a watermill-propeller-like metallacycle with the six exterior Cu2+ ions further decorated by six bidentate chelating en ligands, leading to a hexagonal mixed-ligand macrocycle with inner and outer diameters of ca. 1.0 and 2.2 nm, respectively.

Interestingly, the coexistence of the PONb macrocycle and metallacycle yields a unique ring-on-ring-shaped bi-layer composite macrocycle 1, where Cu12-A is overlapped with a Nb48 wheel via coordination of Cu2+ ions with six terminal oxo atoms from six Lindqvist {Nb6O19} clusters and six terminal oxo atoms from the 12-ring {Nb12O54(H2O)6} (Fig. 1). Thus, 1 itself represents a C6-symmetry chiral macrocycle with dimensions of 1.1 × 2.5 × 2.5 nm3, while both enantiomers are present in the unit cell, forming a racemic crystal 1.

The use of mixed ligands here creates extra freedom to tune the macrocycle composition and topology because either the bridging azole ligand or decorating organoamine ligand can be varied. The replacement of mixed pro-ligands Hmtz and Hetz with Hatz led to the synthesis of a rare macrocycle-based two-dimensional (2D) compound Na13[Cu(en)2]2[2]·solvent.

The macrocyclic secondary building unit 2 shares the same 48-nuclearity hexagonal PONb macrocycle Nb48 as that of 1, but with different hexagonal copper metallacycles. As shown in Fig. 1, the hexagonal metallacycle {Cu6(atz)6(H2O)6} (Cu6) of 2 is made of only six Cu2+ ions interlinked via six atz ligands. By the coordination of Cu2+ ions with 12 μ2-oxo atoms, Cu6 is fixed onto one side of the Nb48 wheel to yield a new bi-layer inorganic–organic hybrid composite macrocycle 2, where all Cu2+ ions adopt a 5-coordinate square pyramid geometry with two N atoms from two atz linkers, two μ2-oxo atoms from PONbs, and one terminal water ligand. Without considering the alkaline metal linkers, each nanoscale macrocycle 2 can be crosslinked to four adjacent ones by four [Cu(en)2]2+ bridges to generate a macrocycle-based 2D layer that is parallel to the bc-plane (Fig. S4†). Different layers are parallelly stacked along the a-axis in an ABAB mode with interlayer pores occupied by Na+ ions and neutral guests (Fig. S5†), leading to the 3D structure 2 with cyclic and trigonal channels along the a-axis direction (Fig. S4†). Clearly, the introduction of POMs endow the POM–metallacycle composite macrocycles with rich terminal oxo atoms for further bonding of metal cations, which offers the possibility of the construction of rare extended frameworks derived from molecular macrocycles.

Furthermore, the use of pro-ligand Htz without any substituents on the azole ring led to a novel tri-layer composite macrocycle 3 isolated as the Na/K/Cu-complex salt Na2K2[Cu(en)2]9[3]·solvent. As shown in Fig. 2a, macrocycle 3 shows a hamburger-like tri-layer structure built from a new 12-Cu-containing metallacycle {Cu12(tz)8(en)8} (Cu12-B) sandwiched by two square PONb macrocycles {β-Nb32O92(H2O)4} (β-Nb32) (ca. 2.0 × 2.0 nm in dimensions). β-Nb32 is constructed from four {Nb6O19} clusters and a circular 8-ring {Nb8O40(H2O)4} formed by alternate corner-sharing of four NbO6 octahedra and four NbO5(H2O) octahedra. In β-Nb32, each {Nb6O19} cluster is capped by a NbO6 octahedron from the 8-ring, and meanwhile, every two adjacent clusters are bridged by a NbO5(H2O) octahedron from the 8-ring. Notably, β-Nb32 is the fourth type of PONb macrocycle, which is isostructural with the known 32-nuclearity PONb macrocycle α-Nb32.22 As illustrated in Fig. S6,† the main difference between them is that the four {Nb6O19} clusters in β-Nb32 are located on the same side of a circular Nb–O 8-ring plane and the whole β-Nb32 exhibits a square macrocycle with C4v-symmetry, while the four {Nb6O19} clusters in α-Nb32 are located symmetrically on both sides of an elliptic Nb–O 8-ring plane and whole α-Nb32 shows a rectangular macrocycle with C2h-symmetry.

Templated by square β-Nb32, Cu12-B also represents a square metallacycle with D4h-symmetry built from the alternative connection of four pairs of [Cu(en)2]2+ complexes and four Cu2+ cations via eight tz linkers (Fig. 2a and S7b†), in which the [Cu(en)2]2+ complexes and Cu2+ cations are located at the corners and midpoints of the edges of the square Cu12-B ring, respectively. By the coordination of Cu2+ ions with terminal oxo atoms of β-Nb32, Cu12-B is sandwiched by two Cs-symmetry-related β-Nb32 macrocycles, resulting in a ring-on-ring-on-ring-shaped trilayer composite macrocycle 3. In 3, all Cu2+ ions of [Cu(en)2]2+ complexes have a square pyramidal geometry defined by one oxo atom from one β-Nb32 and four N atoms from two tz linkers and one en ligand, while each of the four Cu2+ cations is in an octahedral geometry completed by four oxo atoms from two β-Nb32 and two N atoms from two tz linkers.

Based on structures 1–3, it can be found that the substituents on the azole ring have a dramatic effect on the resulting macrocyclic structure. In addition to the use of different azole pro-ligands, we have also explored different chelating organoamine ligands. For example, the use of the enMe ligand allows us to get another interesting trilayer composite macrocycle 4 isolated as the Na/K/Cu-complex salt Na4K[Cu(enMe)2]7[4]·solvent. As shown in Fig. 2b, 4 is composed of two Ci-symmetry-related triangular Nb24 macrocycles sandwiching a new 12-Cu-containing metallacycle {Cu12(OH)6(atz)6(enMe)6} (Cu12-C) with S6-symmetry. Macrocycle Nb24, firstly reported by Nyman,21 is formed by three {Nb6O19} clusters held together by a Nb–O 6-ring {Nb6O27(H2O)3}. Cu12-C shows a circular ring made up of six S6-symmetry-related hydroxy-bridged binuclear [Cu2(OH)(enMe)]3+ complexes linked together by six tritopic atz ligands. Both Cu2+ ions in [Cu2(OH)(enMe)]3+ display a 5-coordinate square pyramid geometry but different coordination environments. One is coordinated to one μ2-OH and two N atoms from two atz linkers, leaving two coordination sites occupied by two oxo atoms from two Nb24 macrocycles, while another is surrounded by one μ2-OH, one oxo atom from one Nb24 macrocycle, and three N atoms from one atz linker and one enMe ligand.

It is interesting to firstly point out that the known Nb24, α-Nb32, and newly observed β-Nb32 and Nb48 reveal the existence of a class of molecular PONb macrocycles with the general formula {[Nb8O23(H2O)]n}, in which Nb24, α-Nb32/β-Nb32, and Nb48 correspond to n = 3, 4, and 6, respectively. The discovery of new members of {[Nb8O23(H2O)]n} with more fascinating structural features (e.g., larger sizes and higher nuclearities) and properties is to be expected.

In 1–4, all Cu–N, Cu–O, Cu–H2O, NbO, Nb–O, and Nb–H2O bond lengths are in the ranges of 1.896–2.210 Å, 1.918–2.279 Å, 2.051–2.088 Å, 1.687–1.834 Å, 1.841–2.456 Å, and 2.428–2.469 Å, respectively. The oxidation states of all Nb and Cu atoms in 1–4 were confirmed as +5 and +2 by bond valence sum calculations,23 respectively. Solids 1/2/3/4 are all insoluble in water and their phase purities were confirmed by PXRD (Fig. 3a–d). We also investigated their pH stabilities using PXRD. The results indicate that, compound 2 is unstable in either acidic or basic aqueous solutions, and 1/3/4 are stable over a wide pH range of 2–11, 3–13, and 3–13, respectively. The photoluminescence properties of compounds 1/2/3/4 are displayed in Fig. S8.† Under 205 nm UV light excitation at 300 K, all samples exhibit a broad emission band in the range of 370–420 nm, which is attributed to the O → Nb charge transfer. Considering their chemical stabilities, solids 1/3/4 are employed for further sorption and photocatalytic measurements.

Calculations reveal that the solvent-accessible volumes per unit cell of 1/3/4 are 16 000 Å3, 10 364 Å3, and 22 120 Å3, respectively, and occupy 13.9, 12.5, and 10.2% of the crystal volumes. Subsequent N2 and water sorption experiments were undertaken to study their porosities. The N2 adsorption isotherm at 77 K revealed no uptake, whereas the water sorption isotherm at room temperature (298 K) proved that they have permanent microporosity. As shown in Fig. 4a, the water absorbed amounts of compounds 1/3/4 increased on increasing the relative pressure, and all data afford a type I isotherm characteristic of a microporous solid. At P/P0 = 0.75, compounds-1/3/4 show 194 cm3 g−1 (15.6 wt%), 240 cm3 g−1 (19.3 wt%), and 207 cm3 g−1 (16.5 wt%) water vapor uptake values, respectively, which are higher than those of most of the known porous POM materials (ESI Table S1†).

Considering that niobium oxides are promising photocatalysts for H2 evolution, the visible-light-driven photocatalytic H2 evolution activities of compounds-1/3/4 were evaluated under heterogeneous conditions using a [Ir(ppy)2(dtbbpy)]+/TEOA/CH3CN/DMF/H2O system explored firstly by Hill et al.,24 in which compounds 1/3/4, [Ir(ppy)2(dtbbpy)][PF6], and TEOA were used as a catalyst, a photosensitizer, and a sacrificial electron donor, respectively. Additionally, the CH3CN/DMF/H2O solution was used to dissolve the photosensitizer. The solutions were irradiated at 278 K with a 300 W Xe-light source (λ > 420 nm). Experiments revealed that no H2 was detected when the reactions were conducted in the dark. However, under Xe lamp irradiation, the H2 evolution increased with time and maximum values of 142.6 μmol, 215.0 μmol, and 228.4 μmol were achieved for 50 mg samples of compounds 1/3/4 after 12 h illumination (Fig. 4b), respectively, corresponding to TON values of 57.5, 60.0, and 59.0. The photocatalytic activities of compounds 1/3/4 compare favorably with that of the starting PONb precursor K7HNb6O19, giving a H2 release amount of 21.6 μmol under the same experimental conditions. Furthermore, the H2 evolution amount is negligible in the absence of any one of the compounds 1/3/4, [Ir(ppy)2(dtbbpy)][PF6], and TEOA. Additionally, the H2 evolution amount is also negligible by replacing compounds 1/3/4 with Cu(Ac)2 or CuCl2 as catalysts under the same experimental conditions. The results indicate that these novel composite materials are active for visible-light-driven photocatalytic H2 evolution. Finally, the PXRD patterns after the H2O sorption and photocatalytic experiments were consistent with those of the initial samples (Fig. S15–S17†), showing that the structures of compounds 1/3/4 can remain intact throughout the H2O sorption and photocatalytic processes.

In summary, we have demonstrated that it is possible to unify metallacycles (with different sizes, nuclearities, configurations, and components) and PONb macrocycles (ranging from trimers and tetramers to hexamers) in the same molecule to form diverse inorganic–organic hybrid discrete and extended composite structures with a unique ring-on-ring-shaped configuration despite the fact that metallacycles (metal–organic rings) and PONb macrocycles (metal–oxide rings) are different types of materials made by different synthetic methods. Such molecular-level combination and crystallographically well-defined molecules have not been known before in any other type of POM material, and they connect two so far separated research areas of PONb chemistry and metallacycle chemistry. These novel materials can exhibit high chemical stability, water vapor adsorption capacity, and effective activity for visible-light-driven photocatalytic H2 evolution. Additionally, the results demonstrate that the combination of PONbs with 3d metal ions, azoles, and chelating organoamines is a potentially general and versatile synthetic route for the creation of novel and diverse inorganic–organic hybrid composite materials.
