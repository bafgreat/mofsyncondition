1. Introduction

Nowadays, people are paying more attention to quality of life and environmental issues. Regrettably, problems from environmental pollution have brought about an endless detriment to the balance of nature and are becoming abnormally grim, which causes numerous potential threats to human beings and sustainable development. Therefore, seeking highly efficient materials for detecting harmful substances has become one of the hottest topics worldwide. Among all kinds of pollution, small organic molecules are ubiquitous and widely used in industry, agriculture and daily life. However, many small organic molecules are highly toxic and affect physiological and pathophysiological functions within the human body, most of which participate in the regulation of vascular homeostasis and the central nervous system.1 Thus, it is of high importance to develop fast and facile methods for detecting those small molecules. During the last few decades, considerable effort has been devoted to developing porous luminescent metal–organic frameworks (LMOFs) as chemical sensors due to their optical and porous properties.2 Up to now, numerous luminescent MOF sensors have been utilized to detect specific target substances, covering metal ions3 (such as Cu2+, Al3+, Cd2+, and Fe3+), anions4 (such as PO43− and Cr2O72−), small organic molecules5 (such as DMF, acetone, and explosives), temperature variation,6etc. Among such MOF-supported sensors, lanthanide metal–organic frameworks (Ln-MOFs) have emerged as important multifunctional luminescent materials benefiting from their unique luminescence properties, such as high luminescence quantum yields, long-lived and characteristically sharp line emissions, and large Stokes shifts, etc.7 The combination of these intrinsic luminescence features together with the inherent porosity of MOFs provides an effective platform for chemical sensing with easy operation, fast response and high sensitivity.8,9

One of the basic challenges in creating Ln-MOFs with good luminescence properties comes from selecting appropriate ligands to act as “antenna” to effectively transfer the energy to the luminescent centers.10 In the past few decades, several chromophoric antenna ligands, especially the β-diketonate11 and carboxylate ligands,12 have been developed in an effort to construct luminescent Ln-MOFs. As is well known, aromatic multi-carboxylate ligands are potential organic linkers in the architecture of Ln-MOFs because lanthanide ions have high affinity for the oxygen atoms from carboxylate groups. Recently, many Ln-MOFs constructed from multi-carboxylate ligands have been synthesized and their potential applications in chemical sensors have been subsequently studied.13–15

Based on the above-mentioned considerations, we designed and synthesized a novel rigid multi-carboxylate ligand, 5,5′-(9-oxo-9H-fluorene-2,7-diyl)diisophthalic acid (H4ofdp), aiming to construct novel microporous Ln-MOFs as luminescent sensors. The presence of two elongated isophthalate units in the H4ofdp are beneficial for building high dimensional microporous frameworks. As expected, we successfully constructed four novel Ln-MOFs containing metallacyclodimeric secondary building units, [Me2NH2]2[(Ln)2(ofdp)2(DMF)(H2O)]·7H2O·DMF, using a solvothermal method based on the reaction of Ln(NO3)3·6H2O and H4ofdp. Notably, the compounds are extremely stable in an aqueous system and can resist acids or bases over a wide pH range from 3 to 11. Luminescence measurements reveal that the materials can be utilized as potential sensors for the excellent luminescent sensing of acetone molecules.

2. Experimental section

2.1 Materials and methods

All relevant chemicals and solvents were reagent-grade quality and used in the synthetic processes as commercially purchased without any further purification. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns were collected using a Rigaku MiniFlex 600 diffractometer using Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5406 Å). Elemental analyses (C, H and N) were performed on a German Elementary Vario EL III instrument. Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were recorded in the temperature range of 30–900 °C, with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 under a flowing nitrogen atmosphere on a Netzsch Model STA 449C instrument. Emission and excitation spectra in the solid state were investigated on a Horiba Jobin-Yvon Fluorolog-3 spectrofluorometer. Time-resolved fluorescence (transient decays) was carried out on an Edinburgh FLS920 spectrophotometer analyzer with both a pulse and continuous (450 W) xenon lamp. The overall photoluminescence quantum yields were obtained on a calibrated integrating sphere coated with barium sulfate at room temperature.

2.2 Synthesis of tetraethyl 5,5′-(9-oxo-9H-fluorene-2,7-diyl)di-isophthalate (1) (Scheme 1)

2,7-Dibromo-9H-fluoren-9-one (1.35 g, 0.004 mol), diethyl 5-(4,4,5,5-tetramethyl-1,3,2-dioxaborolan-2-yl)-1,3-benzene-dicarboxylate16 (3.062 g, 0.0088 mol), cesium carbonate (8.07 g, 0.025 mol) and tetrakis(triphenylphosphine)palladium (0.2 g, 0.0002 mol) were added to a 250 mL schlenk flask charged with a stir bar. The flask was pumped under vacuum and refilled with N2 three times, and then 150 mL of degassed 1,4-dioxane solvent was transferred to the system and the solution was refluxed for 48 h under a nitrogen atmosphere. After the reaction mixture was cooled to room temperature, the organic solvent was evaporated, and the solid was washed with water. The aqueous layer was then extracted with dichloromethane (3 × 50 mL), and the combined organic layers were dried over magnesium sulfate and filtered. After removing the dichloromethane, the crude product was purified by column chromatography on silica gel with ethyl acetate/hexane (v/v = 1 : 5) to give 1 as a pale yellow solid (1.81 g, 0.003 mol, 73% yield based on 2,7-dibromo-9H-fluoren-9-one). 1HNMR (400 MHz, CDCl3): δ 1.48 (t, 12H), 4.48 (q, 8H), 7.73 (d, 2H), 7.88 (d, 2H), 8.04 (s, 2H), 8.51 (s, 4H), 8.72 (s, 2H).

2.3 Synthesis of 5,5′-(9-oxo-9H-fluorene-2,7-diyl)diisophthalic acid (Scheme 1)

1 (1.8 g, 0.003 mol) was dissolved in 10 mL of THF, to which 10 mL of 10 M NaOH aqueous solution was added. The mixture was stirred under reflux for 10 hours, before the organic solvent was removed using a rotary evaporator. The aqueous phase was acidified with 6 M HCl to yield yellow precipitate of H4ofdp, which was filtered, washed with water, and dried under vacuum. Yield: 1.37 g, 90%. 1HNMR (400 MHz, DMSO-d6): δ, 7.22 (d, 4H), 7.81 (d, 4H), 8.38 (d, 4H), 8.45 (s, 2H), 13.38 (s, 4H).

2.4 Synthesis of [Me2NH2]2[(Ln)2(ofdp)2(DMF)(H2O)]·7H2O·DMF (compounds 1–4)

A mixture of Ln(NO3)3·6H2O (45 mg, 0.10 mmol), H4ofdp (25 mg, 0.05 mmol) in DMF (N,N-dimethylformamide) (5 mL) and H2O (5 mL) solvent were sealed in an approximately 23 mL Teflon cup under autogenous pressure and heated at 140 °C for three days under acidic conditions. After the mixture was slowly cooled to room temperature at a rate of 5 °C h−1, light yellow crystals were obtained, then washed with DMF and ethanol, and dried in air. The yields were ca. 66%, 68%, 63% and 61% for compounds 1–4 based on the ligand, respectively.

2.5 Single-crystal X-ray crystallography

All the crystal structural data of compounds 1–4 were collected on a SuperNova diffractometer equipped with multilayer mirror Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5418 Å) using a ω scan. All the structures of compounds 1–4 were solved using direct methods and the full-matrix least-squares refinement on F2 were finished using SHELX-97.17 All non-hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic thermal refinement, except some disordered water molecules. Hydrogen atoms from aromatic carbon atoms were generated geometrically, while those on the water molecules could not be determined. It should be stated that PLATON/SQUEEZE18 was used to obtain solvent-free diffraction of compounds 1–4, because the solvent molecules were highly disordered and could not be modeled properly. Structures were then refined again using the data generated. The final chemical formulas were obtained from the refined data combined with the elemental analysis and thermogravimetric analysis data. The CCDC numbers for compounds 1–4 are CCDC 1516440, 1873262, 1873261 and 1516443, respectively. Crystal data and structure refinements for compounds 1–4 are summarized in Table 1. Selected bond lengths and angles are given in Table S2,† and are available in CIF files in the ESI.†

2.6 Stability investigation

Aqueous solutions with different pH values (ranging from 3 to 11) were prepared. Then, 30 mg of as-synthesized samples were soaked in each solution (10 mL) for 48 h. After that, all the samples were centrifuged, washed several times and dried in the air before the sample was subjected to PXRD measurement for pH stability.

2.7 Luminescence measurements

To examine the properties of 1 to sense small molecules, activated 1a with open Eu3+ sites (activated as-synthesized 1 by thermal activation of as-synthesized 1 at 145 °C under vacuum for 24 hours) was prepared for sensing small molecules. Then, 5 mg 1a was immersed in 5 mL different organic solutions, including H2O, acetonitrile, dimethyl sulfoxide, ethanol, methanol, n-propane, i-propane, n-butanol, i-butanol, tetrahydrofuran, DMF and acetone, to prepare various stable suspensions through ultrasound for two and a half hours. The luminescence properties of these suspensions were characterized and compared.

3. Results and discussion

3.1 Structure description

Considering the feature that compounds 1–4 are isomorphous, exhibiting similar coordination environments, compound 2 was selected to be discussed in detail as a representative. Crystal structure analysis data reveal that compound 2 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P. Two crystallographically independent Gd3+ ions, two completely deprotonated [ofdp]4− ligands and one coordinated DMF molecule and one coordinated water molecule compose the asymmetric unit of compound 2, where two free [Me2NH2]+ ions exist for charge balance. As illustrated in Fig. 1a, Gd1 is nine-coordinated, consisting of six oxygen atoms from three chelating bidentate carboxylate groups, two oxygen atoms from two μ2-bridging carboxylate groups and one oxygen atom from one coordinated water molecule, while Gd2 is eight-coordinated, which consists of four oxygen atoms from two chelating bidentate carboxylate groups, three oxygen atoms from three μ2-bridging carboxylate groups and one oxygen atom from one coordinated DMF molecule. In compound 2, the coordination modes of the two ligands are different: the first one is a μ5-bridging mode, where two carboxylate groups adopt a chelating bidentate mode and the other two carboxylate groups adopt a μ2-bridging bidentate mode and a monodentate mode, respectively (MODE I, Fig. S1†); the second one is another kind of μ5-bridging mode, constructed from three carboxylate groups adopting chelating bidentate modes, leaving a carboxylate group adopting a μ2-bridging bidentate mode (MODE II, Fig. S1†). Self-assembly of two Gd3+ ions and two [ofdp]4− ligands yields a systematic metallacyclodimeric secondary building unit (SBU), [Gd2(ofdp)2] (Fig. 1a). Bridging the Gd atom and its corresponding symmetry generated one via two monodentate carboxylate groups, and adjacent SBUs constitute a unique interlocking 1D chain (an infinite double-loop chain) architecture, in which the Gd⋯Gd separation is 5.473 Å (Fig. 1b). The adjacent 1D chains are further aggregated into a 2D layer network by chelating bidentate carboxylic groups of the ligands, as Fig. S2† shows. Then, these 2D layers are cross-ligated to construct a 3D skeleton through the linkers (Fig. 1c). The aromatic moieties between the layers up and down in each chain are almost planar and array in a sliding mode. Strong π⋯π interactions are produced between such aromatic rings with the centroid–centroid distances being 3.615, 3.764, 3.584 and 3.725 Å, respectively (Fig. S3†). The non-interpenetrated 3D framework contains a 1D tetragonal channel along the c axis (Fig. 1d and S4†), having an effective aperture size of 3.86 × 4.6 Å2. About 34.9% solvent accessible volume was estimated using PLATON software.

From the viewpoints of topology, if the metal center is taken as 8-connected and two ligands in different coordinated modes are taken as two distinct kinds of 4-connected node, the 3D coordination structure of compound 2 can be considered as a simplified binodal 4,8-connected network with (4-c)2(8-c) stoichiometry, the point symbol of which is {412·612·84} {46}2, as depicted in Fig. 2.

3.2 Framework stability

Stability is an extremely important key for LMOF sensors in practical applications, where LMOFs have to bear various complicated systems. Therefore, the stability of 1–4, including thermal stability and chemical stability, was measured. The thermal stability of compounds 1–4 in the temperature range of 30–900 °C under a N2 atmosphere is depicted in Fig. S6.† The TGA curve of compound 2 was selected to be described in detail since compounds 1–4 are isomorphous and have similar thermal behavior. As illustrated by the TGA curve, compound 2 undergoes two steps of mass loss: the first step (found, 7.51%) at 30–145 °C corresponds to the loss of seven lattice water molecules (theoretical, 7.39%); the second step (found, 10.16%; theoretical, 9.63%), in the range of 200–450 °C, is attributed to the liberation of one coordinated water molecule, one coordinated DMF molecule and one lattice DMF molecule, after which decomposition occurs in the remaining framework.

Additionally, chemical stability was also validated. The fresh sample was exposed to aqueous solutions of pH = 3–11 for 48 hours, followed by PXRD analysis. As presented in Fig. 3, the compound can withstand acidic/basic conditions and retain the integrity of the framework in a wide pH range (pH = 3–11), revealing the abnormal chemical stability of 2.

3.3 Room-temperature luminescence properties

In order to determine whether the H4ofdp ligand is an excellent antenna chromophore for sensitizing the lanthanide ions, especially Eu3+ and Tb3+ ions, the luminescence spectra of 1, 3 and the free ligand in the solid state at 298 K were recorded. As shown in Fig. S7† (black line), under excitation at 340 nm, the emission spectrum of the ligand H4ofdp displays a broad band at 375–550 nm with a maximum at 427 nm, attributed to π → π* transitions. Broad bands at 250–450 nm are observed in the excitation spectra of 1 and 3 (dotted lines, Fig. 4a and b), which may be ascribed to the n → π* or π → π* transitions of ligand H4ofdp. Besides, four sharp characteristic peaks of Eu3+ ions centered at about 362 nm, 382 nm, 394 nm and 417 nm are detected in the excitation spectra of 1 (Fig. 4a), resulting from the series of transitions for Eu3+ ions between the 7F0,1 and the 5G6, 5H4, 5L6 and 5D3 energy levels.19

The emission spectra of 1 and 3 (solid lines, Fig. 4a and b) display the characteristic red and green luminescence of Eu3+ and Tb3+ ions, which exhibit typical lines at 580, 593, 614, 655 and 699 nm for 1 and at 489, 543, 584 and 622 nm for 3, attributed to the 5D0 → 7FJ (J = 0–4) transitions of Eu3+ ions and 5D4 → 7FJ (J = 6–3) transitions of Tb3+ ions, respectively. No broad emission bands for the ligand in the compounds are observed, implying that both Eu3+ and Tb3+ ions can be sensitized by the ligand H4ofdp efficiently. The intensities of the 5D0 → 7F2 transitions (Eu3+) and 5D4 → 7F5 transitions (Tb3+), the most intense characteristic peaks in the emission spectra of the corresponding compounds, were monitored to estimate the time-resolved luminescence lifetimes (τobs). The observed transient decay curves of 1 and 3 are well-fitted with mono-exponential functions at 298 K, giving values of 0.680 ms for Eu3+ ions in 1 and 1.076 ms for Tb3+ ions in 3 (Fig. S8†). The luminescence quantum yields of compounds 1 and 3 were also investigated, the values of which are 8.79% and 48.69%, respectively.

Efficient energy transfer from the triplet state of the ligand to the emitting levels of the central Ln3+ ions is the main factor for developing highly luminescent lanthanide compounds.19 In order to illuminate the mechanism of energy transfer in 1 and 3, it is necessary to establish the relevant ligand-centered triplet state T1 of H4ofdp, which is calculated from the shortest wavelength emission edge of the corresponding phosphorescence spectrum of compound 2 ([Me2NH2]2[(Gd)2(ofdp)2(DMF)(H2O)]·7H2O·DMF) at 77 K. Because the lowest excited state of the Gd3+ ion is so high, the energy transfer could not occur from the ligand to the Gd3+ ion. Hence, the energy levels of Gd3+ ions have no significant influence on the energy levels of the ligand-centered triplet state.19 As expected, the overlap between the low-temperature emission spectrum of 2 and the corresponding room-temperature emission spectrum of the ligand was found, making the ligand an efficient antennae and allowing for the ligand-to-metal energy transfer (Fig. S7†). The triplet state energy level T1 of the ligand was calculated from the phosphorescence spectrum of compound 2 with a value of 22 988 cm−1 (435 nm), which is obviously higher than the lowest excited states of Eu3+ (5D0: 17 267 cm−1) and Tb3+ (5D4: 20 500 cm−1). Based on Latva's empirical rule,19,20 an optimal ligand-to-metal energy transfer process needs (ΔE = T1 − 5DJ) 2500–4000 cm−1 for Eu3+ and 2500–4500 cm−1 for Tb3+. The energy gaps between the experimental triplet state energy level T1 of the ligand and central Ln3+ ions were calculated subsequently: ΔE(T1 − 5D0) for Eu3+ is 5721 cm−1, and ΔE(T1 − 5D4) for Tb3+ is 2488 cm−1. This result reveals that, compared with 1, Tb3+ ions in compound 3 can be sensitized by the ligand more effectively with a more suitable energy gap. This gives a reasonable theoretical explanation of the relatively higher observed overall quantum yield of 3 than 1.

3.4 Sensing of small molecules

The microporous structural feature of the compounds inspires us to further explore their potential for sensing various small molecules. As a representative, activated 1a with open Eu3+ sites was immersed into several solvents for small organic molecules, including H2O, acetonitrile, dimethyl sulfoxide, ethanol, methanol, n-propane, i-propane, n-butanol, i-butanol, tetrahydrofuran, DMF and acetone, to prepare stable suspensions for the fluorescence sensing measurements. As displayed in Fig. 5, the fluorescence intensity of the various suspensions mainly relies on the corresponding solvents. Specifically, differing from other solvents, acetone shows an extraordinary quenching effect on the emission intensity of 1a. This result reveals that 1a can be selected as a promising chemical sensor for detecting acetone molecules by a turn-off effect. Further studies were examined in detail. The suspension of 1a dispersed in i-propane solvent, the fluorescence intensity of which lies in middle of the twelve solvents, was selected as a standard suspension. Then, the emission intensity at 614 nm was monitored with the gradual addition of acetone (Fig. 6). Obviously, the quenching effect apparently enhances along with increasing content of acetone in the emulsion, and finally achieved a maximum extent at an acetone content of 400 μL, which is comparable to recently reported luminescent sensors based on Ln-MOFs for acetone molecules (Table S1†),15a,21,22b,23b indicating the potential capacity of 1a for sensitively detecting acetone molecules.

Although the mechanism for such fluorescence sensing of small solvent molecules based on a turn-off effect is still not very clear at this stage, size effects and the binding interaction between the open luminescent metal sites and guest solvent molecules should definitely play a significant role.22,23 The energy adsorbed by the ligand [ofdp]4−, which should be transferred to the resonant energy level of the central Eu3+ ions, is consumed partially by the entering acetone solvent molecules. It is suggested that the weakly coordinated i-propane molecules on the Eu3+ sites are replaced by acetone molecules, which results in luminescence quenching. This result reveals that Ln-MOFs are promising chemical sensors for air-pollution molecules. Most significantly, compound 1a is considered to be an excellent candidate for a luminescent probe for detecting acetone molecules sensitively.

4. Conclusions

In summary, we present the design and synthesis of an extended rigid bent ligand with di-isophthalate moieties (H4ofdp), and then four novel microporous lanthanide metal–organic frameworks generated from the ligand were successfully designed, synthesized and characterized. It is worth noting that the Ln-MOFs possess outstanding chemical stability in acidic or basic aqueous solutions over a wide pH range of 3–11, which is a precondition of Ln-MOFs applied as promising chemical sensors in complicated aqueous systems. Luminescence spectra of the Eu3+ and Tb3+ compounds at room temperature indicate that the ligand H4ofdp is a promising potential light sensitizer for lanthanide ions in view of the well-matched relationship between the triplet energy levels of the ligand and the emitting levels of Eu3+/Tb3+ centers. More impressively, the further exploration of the luminescence properties, with compound 1 selected as a representative, reveals that compound 1 exhibits a highly sensitive fluorescence response to acetone molecules based on the remarkable luminescence quenching effect, and may be applied as a sensitive fluorescent sensor for acetone molecules.
