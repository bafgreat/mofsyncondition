Introduction

During the past decade, considerable efforts have been devoted to construct new metal–organic coordination polymers (MOCPs), not only owing to their enormous variety of intriguing structural topologies but also because of their great potential applications as microporous, magnetic, and non-linear optical and fluorescent materials.1,2 An effective and facile approach for the synthesis of such complexes is still the self-assembly of well-designed multi-dentate organic ligand and d-block (or f-block) metal ions or metal clusters under hydrothermal (solvothermal or ionothermal) conditions.3 The selection of multifunctional organic ligands containing appropriate coordination sites linked by a spacer with specific positional orientation is especially crucial to the construction of desirable frameworks.4 Thus, much attention has been paid to the design and synthesis of various multifunctional bridging ligands.

In recent years, the increasing interest has been concentrated on the rigid N-heterocyclic multi-carboxylate ligands due to their diversities in coordination modes and versatile conformations according to the restrictions imposed by the coordination geometry of the metal ion.5–7Imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid (H3IDC) has been widely investigated owing to its excellent performance in the construction of porous and helical complexes.8 Currently, a series of analogue ligands of H3IDC were synthesized by introducing different substituents, such as alkyl (methyl, ethylic and propyl), phenyl, hydroxymethyl and carboxyl groups, on the two-position of the imidazole ring. Many coordination polymers with interesting structures based on these ligands have been synthesized.92-(pyridin-4-yl)-1H-imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid (H3PIDC), as a multidentate rigid ligand, possesses multiple coordinate sites involving one pyridin N atom, two imidazole N atoms and four carboxylate O atoms, and the H3IDC group and pyridin ring can rotate around the C–C bond to meet the different coordination requirements of metal ions in the assembly process. A series of complexes constructed from the H3PIDC ligand have been achieved successfully by Liet al. and Jing et al.10

Herein, we focus our attention on the coordination polymers built by H3PIDC in the presence of different metal centers and investigated the influence of the co-ligands on their structures. As is well known, the presence of functional secondary ligands can significantly produce multiple effects on the core ligand in addition to the metal coordination sphere, which ultimately govern the overall structures of the mixed-ligand complexes.11 In this report, we present the syntheses and crystal structures of seven co-ligand-regulated complexes with various dimensions from 2D to 3D, formulated as [Cd2(HPIDC)2(bipy)]n (1), {[Cd3(HPIDC)2(btc)(H2O)4]·2H2O}n (2), {[Cd2(HPIDC)2(bdc)1/2(H2O)Cl]·H2O}n (3), {[Cd2(PIDC)(Ac)(H2O)]·H2O}n (4), [Zn2(HPIDC)(ox)(H2O)2]n (5), {[Eu(HPIDC)(ox)1/2H2O]·2H2O}n (6) and {[Tb(HPIDC)(ox)1/2H2O]·2H2O}n (7). Furthermore, the powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) data, thermal stabilities, and photoluminescent properties of these complexes have also been investigated.

Experimental

Materials and physical measurements

All chemicals were purchased commercially and used without further purification. The H3PIDC ligand was prepared by the method reported in the literature.12 Elemental analyses (C, H, and N) were performed on a Perkin-Elmer 2400 CHN elemental analyzer. IR spectra (Fig. S4–S9, ESI†) were recorded in the range 4000–400 cm−1 on a Mattson Alpha-Centauri spectrometer using KBr pellets. Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) of the samples were performed using a Perkin-Elmer TG-7 analyzer heated from room temperature to 1000 °C under nitrogen. PXRD patterns were recorded on a Siemens D5005 diffractometer with Cu-Kα (λ = 1.5418 Å) radiation in the range of 3–50°. Solid-state fluorescent spectra were taken on a Cary Eclipse spectrofluorometer.

X-ray crystallography

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction data for 1–7 were recorded on a Bruker Apex CCD II area-detectordiffractometer with graphite-monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71069 Å) at 293 K. Absorption corrections were applied using multi-scan techniques. Their structures were solved by Direct Method of SHELXS-97 and refined by full-matrix least-square techniques using the SHELXL-97 program.13 Metal atoms in each complex were located from the E-maps and non-hydrogen atoms were located in successive difference Fourier syntheses and refined with anisotropic thermal parameters on F2. The hydrogen atoms of the organic ligands were generated theoretically onto the specific atoms and refined isotropically. The H atoms of the host water molecules in 2, 3, 6 and 7, the H atoms of coordination water molecules in 6 and the H atoms of carboxyl groups in 2, 3 and 5 could not be located, but were included in the formula. Results and discussion

Syntheses

The complexes 1–7 were generated by hydrothermal reaction of H3PIDC ligand and metal ions (ZnII, CdII, EuIII and TbIII) with the help of assistant ligands, and the synthetic strategy of 1–7 was presented in Scheme 1. Hydrothermal synthesis has been proven to be a useful synthetic technique for the preparation of MOCPs. The high temperature and the pressure during the reaction process can dramatically enhance the ligand solubility and the reactivity of reactants. The reaction variables (metal ions, pH value, temperature, starting materials, metal–ligand stoichiometry and time of reaction, etc.) may influence the reaction process and the final products. In this work, we adopted d10 metals (Cd(NO3)2, Cd(Ac)2, CdCl2 and Zn(NO3)2) and lanthanide metals (Eu(NO3)3 and Tb(NO3)3) as the metal sources to produce complexes 1–7 in the reaction, respectively. In order to explore the influence of co-ligands on the final structure of complexes, different organic secondary ligands including 2,2′-bipy, H4btc, H2bdc, piperazine and H2ox were introduced into the reaction system. When the N-containing assistant ligands (2,2′-bipy and piperazine) were introduced into the reaction system, the 2D layer-like complex 1 and 3D framework 4 were successfully obtained. Notably, the stoichiometric piperazine species in the preparation of 4 only serves as the deprotonation reagent and a competing ligand, rather than acting as a co-ligand. Complexes 2 and 3 with the same metal salt (CdCl2) but different assistant ligand (H4btc for 2 and H2bdc for 3) were obtained at the same reaction temperature. Complexes 5–7 were obtained by using the same M(NO3)n–H3PIDC–H2ox system, but at different temperatures (150 °C for 5, 160 °C for 6 and 7). Complexes 1–7 are air-stable and insoluble in water and common organic solvents, such as chloroform, toluene, acetonitrile, DMF, methanol, and methanol, and ethanol.

Crystal structure of 1

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that complex 1 presents a two-dimensional (2D) layer-like polymeric structure which crystallizes in the space groupPca21. There are two unique CdIIcations (Cd1 and Cd2), two HPIDC2− anions and one bipyligand in the asymmetric unit. As shown in Fig. 1a, both Cd1 and Cd2 locate in two types of six-coordinate octahedral environments. The coordinate geometry of Cd1 is completed by two O atoms (O1; O5A) and four N atoms (N1; N3C; N4A; N6B) from three individual HPIDC2− anions. The coordination sphere of Cd2 formed by two O donors (O4; O8) and two N atoms (N2; N5) from two HPIDC2− anions as well as two N atoms (N7; N8) from one bipyligand. The distances of Cd–O range from 2.348(4) to 2.403(4) Å and the Cd–N range from 2.271(4) to 2.384(4) Å, respectively. Each doubly deprotonated HPIDC2− anion adopts μ3–kN,O: kN′,O′: kN′′ coordination mode (Scheme 2a) bridging three CdII ions. Firstly, the HPIDC2−ligand links two metal ions by means of two carboxylic O atoms and two N atoms of imidazole ring generating left- and right-handed single helices with a pitch of 13.325 Å along the c-axis (Fig. 1b). The single helices processing opposite chirality arranged alternately and connected with each other by Npyridine–Cd bonds result in a 2D sheet along the ac plane. If each metal center is considered as a four-connecting node and each HPIDC2− is regarded as a three-connecting node, the 2D sheet can be regarded as a unique (3,4)-cennected topology with the Schläfli topological symbol of (4·62)(42·62·82) (Fig. 1c). In general, the introduction of organic chelating co-ligands is capable of “passivating” metal sites and may induce lower-dimensional complexes. In 1, the bipy molecules locate on the outside of the monolayer in a bidentate-chelating fashion connecting metal ions and block the formation of a 3D covalent framework. While, a 3D supramolecular network is finally formed by linking these layers through the weak π⋯π stacking interactions between bipy rings from adjacent layers, with a plane-to-plane distance of 3.419 Å and centroid–centroid distance of 4.325 Å (Fig. 1d and Fig. S1, ESI†).

Crystal structure of 2

When the multidentate secondary ligand1,2,4,5-benzenetetracarboxylic acid (H4btc) was introduced into the assembled system, a distinct 2D network was produced. Complex 2 crystallizes in the space groupP and the coordination environment around CdII ions is shown in Fig. 2a. The Cd1 ion lies in a distorted octahedral geometry, in which the basal plane is completed by two N and two O atoms from two separated HPIDC2−ligands (Cd1–O4, 2.292(4) Å; Cd1–O4A, 2.292(4) Å; Cd1–N2, 2.336(4) Å; Cd1–N2A, 2.336(4) Å), and the apical positions are occupied by two coordination water molecules (Cd1–O2W, 2.322(5) Å; Cd1–O2WA, 2.322(5) Å). The Cd2 ion with octahedral sphere is surrounded by three O atoms (Cd2–O5B, 2.409(3) Å; Cd2–O8, 2.239(3) Å; Cd2–O7C, 2.245(3) Å) of three btc4−ligands, one O atom and one N atom (Cd2–O1, 2.351(3) Å; Cd2–N1, 2.337(4) Å) from one HPIDC2−ligand as well as one water molecule (Cd2–O1W, 2.330(4) Å). In complex 2, each btc4−ligand adopts a central-symmetry coordination mode in monodentate and bidentate-chelating fashions linking two CdII ions. The HPIDC2− anion only adopts μ2–kN,O: kN′,O′ coordination mode connecting two CdII centers in a bis-N,O-chelating fashion (Scheme 2b). Two adjacent Cd2 ions were linked by two carboxylate groups of two individual btc4− anions forming one ring-shaped di-metal Cd2 unit with the Cd⋯Cd distance of 4.193 Å. The neighboring di-metal Cd2 units were bridged by the btc4−ligands and the [Cd1(HPIDC)2] groups built by two HPIDC ligands sharing a Cd1 ion only resulting in a 2D network (Fig. 2b). The 3D covalent framework cannot be obtained because the stronger π⋯π interaction between benzene rings of btc4−ligands and pyridine rings of HPIDC2−ligands makes the Npyridine atoms point to the saturated metal sites of the adjacent layer. The 2D network is a (4,4) net if the di-metal Cd2 units are regarded as a 4-connected node. The 2D nets are packing along the a direction in abcabc mode (Fig. 2c). Moreover, the hydrogen-bonding interaction (O1W–H1A⋯O6, 1.844 Å; O2W–H2B⋯O3W, 2.067 Å) between adjacent layers stabilized the 3D supramolecular structure (Fig. S2, ESI†).

Crystal structure of 3

When 1,4-benzenedicarboxylic acid (H2bdc) was used instead of H4btc, a 3D framework was obtained. The asymmetric unit of 3 includes two CdII ions, one HIPDC2−ligand, a half of bdc2− anion residing on an inversion centre, one μ3–Cl atom, one coordination water molecule and one lattice water molecule. As illustrated in Fig. 3a, the Cd1 is coordinated by two O atoms and two N atoms (Cd1–O1, 2.314(3) Å; Cd1–O6A, 2.362(3) Å; Cd1–N1, 2.313(3) Å; Cd1–N3C, 2.279(3) Å) from three HPIDC2− anions and two μ3–Cl atoms (Cd1–Cl1, 2.6059(16) Å; Cd1–Cl1B, 2.6941(13) Å) furnishing a distorted octahedral coordination geometry. Cd2 adopts a distorted pentagonal bipyramidal coordination sphere consisting of one O atom and one N atom (Cd2–O4, 2.386(3) Å; Cd2–N2, 2.284(3) Å) from one HPIDC2− anion, three O atoms (Cd2–O5, 2.290(3) Å; Cd2–O6, 2.3667(3) Å; Cd2–O5D, 2.518(3) Å) from two distinct bdc2−ligands, a μ3–Cl atom and one water molecule (Cd2–Cl1E, 2.7428(15) Å; Cd2–O1W, 2.224(3) Å). In complex 3, each bdc2− anion links to six CdII ions in chelating-bidentate and bridging-bidentate fashions with the dihedral angle of 35.37° between the carboxylate group and the benzene ring. The HPIDC2− anion only adopts the same μ3–bridging mode as in 1 connecting three CdII ions. The imidazole ring and pyridine ring with the dihedral angle being 54.48° is slightly larger than that found in 1 (47.94°). The adjacent CdII ions were linked together by carboxylate groups of bdc2− anions and the μ3–Cl atoms resulting in the formation of a Cd–(CO2)–Cl chain along the b axis (Fig. 3b). The infinite rod-shaped Cd–(CO2)–Cl chain can be regarded as a secondary building unit (SBU), where the neighbouring CdII ions are separated by 3.907 and 4.123 Å. These SBUs are sustained by rigid benzene rings of bdc2−ligands and HPIDC2−ligands forming a 3D framework (Fig. 3c). The distance of neighbouring Cd–(CO2)–Cl chains may suit HPIDC2−ligand to adopt a T-shaped configuration linking these SBUs. Furthermore, the stronger π⋯π interaction between benzene rings of bdc2−ligands and imidazole rings of HPIDC2−ligands further favor such a configuration of the HPIDC2−ligand. A better understanding of the structure of complex 3 can be achieved via topological considerations where the HPIDC2−ligand is regarded as a three-connected node and two types of CdII sites are considered as topologically 4-connected nodes (Fig. 3d). Therefore, the overall 3D network can be simplified to a trinodal (3,4)-connected 3D net with an unprecedented topology of (4·6·8)(53·6·82)(4·5·6·82·10) (Fig. 3e).

Crystal structure of 4

The 3D complex 4 is constructed by H3PIDC and acetic acid (HAc) ligands, and crystallizes in a monoclinic space groupP21/n. Fig. 4a illustrated the coordination environment of two unique CdII ions. Cd1 is ligated by three O atoms, two N atoms from three PIDC3− anions and one O atom from one Ac− anion forming a distorted octahedral coordination sphere. Cd2 has a similar coordination environment to Cd1, which is surrounded by three O atoms and one N atom from two PIDC3− anions, one O atom from one Ac− anion and water molecule. The bond lengths of Cd–L (O, N) are in the range of 2.213(4)–2.532(4) Å. In complex 4, each H3PIDC is all deprotonated to PIDC3− and adopts a complicated μ5–kN,O: kO,O′: kO,O′: kO′′,O′′′: kN′,O′′′: kN′′ mode (Scheme 2c) bridging five CdII ions in bis-N,O- and O,O-bidentate as well as N-monodentate fashions, in which one carboxylate group and imidazole ring have a dihedral angle of 24.86° and the dihedral angle between the pyridine ring and imidazole ring is 53.68°. Similar to 3, a rod-shaped SBU [Cd(COO)]n (Fig. 4c) in 4 also is observed, and it is constructed from the carboxylate groups of PIDC3− ligands linking the neighboring CdII ions, in which the two unique CdII ions are separated by 4.922(9) Å. These rod-shaped SBUs are further connected with each other through pyridine and imidazole groups of PIDC3−ligands leading to a 3D coordination framework along b axis (Fig. 4c). The Ac− anion as a linker connecting two adjacent [Cd(COO)]n SBUs in a bi-monodentate fashion further stabilize the whole framework. The existence of Ac− may be beneficial to the deprotonation of H3PIDC owing to its suitable size and smaller steric hindrance. So, the PIDC3− ligand link more metal ions by making use of all seven coordination atoms in 4. Topologically, PIDC3−ligands can be regarded as five-connected nodes and the metal sites can be considered as three- and four-connected nodes, respectively (Fig. 4d). So, the overall framework of compound 3 can be described as a trinodal (3,4,5)-connected (410·65)(410·63·82) net (Fig. 4e).

Crystal structure of 5

Complex 5 is obtained by using H2ox as the co-ligand and crystallizes in the monoclinic space groupC2/c with one crystallographically independent ZnII ion in the crystal lattice, as shown in Fig. 5a. Each ZnII ion was six-coordinated by two O atoms and one N atoms (Zn1–O1, 2.066(4) Å; Zn1–O1C, 2.216(4) Å; Zn1–N1, 2.078(4) Å) from two individual HPIDC2− anions which lie about a twofold axis, two O atoms (Zn1–O3, 2.013(4) Å; Zn1–O4A, 2.171(4) Å; Zn1–O1W, 2.055(4) Å) from one ox2− anion locating on an inversion centre and one terminal water molecule giving a distorted octahedral coordination geometry. Complex 5 displays a 3D network built from HPIDC2− and ox2− anions linking metal centers. Two adjacent ZnII ions were connected by two μ2 carboxylate O atoms of two HPIDC2−ligands forming a binuclear unit, and the separation of Zn⋯Zn is 3.246 Å. The HPIDC2−ligands adopt μ2–kN,O: kN′,O′: kNO′: kO′ (Scheme 2d) coordination modes bridging the binuclear units to result in a 1D [Zn2(HPIDC)]n chain (Fig. 5b). It is noteworthy that the atoms of the chain except the distorted pyridine rings are co-planar. The adjacent chains are connected to each other by the ox2− linkers in a bis-bidentate fashion to further extend a 3D framework (Fig. 5c).

The distance of the two metal ions linked by oxalic acid is about 5.369 Å close to the length (6.355 Å) of two metal ions bridged by the HIDC group of HPIDC2−ligand. Such space may hinder the pyridine group of HPIDC2−ligand further participating in coordination. From the viewpoint of topology, the overall framework of complex 5 can be described to be a CdSO4 net if each binuclear ZnII unit was considered as a 4-connected node (Fig. 5d).

Crystal structure of 6 and 7

Complexes 6 and 7 possess 3D frameworks and crystallize in the monoclinic space groupP21/c. Because they are isomorphous, only the structure of 6 is described in detail. In the asymmetric unit of 6, there exist one EuIII ion, one HPIDC2− anion, a half of ox2− anion siting on an inversion centre, one coordination water molecule and two lattice water molecules. Each eight-coordinate EuIII ion displays a distorted bicapped trigonal-prismatic coordination environment, defined by one N atom (Eu1–N2A, 2.567(4) Å) and four O atoms (Eu1–O2, 2.308(4) Å; Eu1–O3, 2.362(3) Å; Eu1–O1A, 2.391(3) Å; Eu1–O4B, 2.401(3) Å) from three HPIDC2− anions, three O atoms (Eu1–O5, 2.433(4) Å; Eu1–O6C, 2.443(3) Å) from one ox2− anion and one water molecule (Eu1–O1W, 2.415(4) Å) (Fig. 6a). In complex 6, the partially deprotonated HPIDC2− anion adopts a μ3–kN,O: kO′,O′′: kO′′,O′′′: kO′′′ mode (Scheme 2e) linking three EuIII centers using two carboxylic groups and one Nimidazole atom. The EuIII ions were connected by the HPIDC2−ligands to form a 2D layer formulated as [Eu(HPIDC)]n (Fig. S3, ESI†). The adjacent layers were further pillared by ox2−ligands in chelating-bidentate fashion into a 3D framework (Fig. 6b). The distance between two EuIII linked by the ox2− anion is 6.253 Å. Similar to 5, the distance of metals linked by the ox2−ligand may restrict the coordination tendency of core ligand HPIDC2−. The topology analyzed method was also employed to evaluate the whole framework of complex 6. When a binuclear EuIII ring [Eu2(COO)2] built by two carboxylic groups linking two EuIII ions is viewed to be a six-connected node (Fig. 6c) and the two kinds of ligands be considered as the linkers, the 3D network of 6 can be simplified to be a classical α-Po net (Fig. 6d).

Thermal analyses and PXRD studies

The phase purity of bulk materials for 1–7 was confirmed by comparison of their powder X-ray diffraction patterns (PXRD) with those calculated from single-crystal X-ray diffraction studies (Fig. S11–S17, ESI†). Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were carried out to examine the thermal stability of these complexes (Fig. 7). The experiments were performed on samples consisting of numerous single crystals of 1–7 under N2 atmosphere with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. As shown in Fig. S4,† the TGA curve for 1 reveals that the whole framework is stable up to 265 °C. Complex 2 loses the coordination and lattice-water molecules from room temperature to 250 °C (obsd 7.5%, calcd 7.9%). With further heating, gradual weight loss occurs, which is due to the departure of HPIDC2− and btc4− anions. The weight loss of 3, corresponding to the release of one lattice water molecule, is observed from 50 to 94 °C (obsd 2.6%, calcd 2.9%), and the weight loss corresponding to the release of the one coordinated water molecule is observed from 243 to 297 °C (obsd 3.5%, calcd 2.9%). The anhydrous framework of 3 maintains stability up to 350 °C. For 4, the first weight loss of 4.2% occurred in the temperature range of 50–230 °C, due to the release of coordinated and lattice water molecules (calcd 4.5%), and the decomposition of the residue occurs at 385 °C. For 5, the loss of two coordination water molecules is observed in the range 205–230 °C (obsd 7.6%, calcd 7.4%), and the decomposition of the network occurs at ca. 284 °C. It should be noted that the weight of 1–5 at 1000 °C are nearly close to zero, which can be ascribed to the sublimation of newly formed MO (M = Zn, Cd) and/or MCl2.14 Owing to the structural similarity of 6 and 7, compound 7 was selected to study their thermal stability. It can be seen from the TGA curve of 7 that the weight loss of 11.03% from 45 to 225 °C corresponds to the removal of the coordination and lattice water molecules (calcd 11.07%). The second weight loss of 50.37% between 360 and 980 °C is attributable to the loss of ox2− and HPIDC2−ligands. The remaining weight (38.60%) indicated that the final product was Tb2O3 (calcd 37.47%). The TG curve of 6 is similar to that of 7, where the first weight loss of two guest water and one coordinated water molecule is 10.5% for 6 (calcd 11.23%), respectively.

Luminescent properties

The photoluminescent properties of complexes 1–7 and the free H3PIDC ligand have been investigated in the solid state at room temperature, as depicted in Fig. 8. Intense luminescence emission bands with the peaks were observed at 364/453 nm (λex = 264 nm) for 1, 460 nm (λex = 380 nm) for 2, 429 nm (λex = 372 nm) for 3, 420 nm (λex = 367 nm) for 4, and 440/500 nm (λex = 266 nm) for 5, respectively. The main emission peak of the H3PIDC ligand is at 470 nm (λex = 282 nm), which may be attributed to the π*→π transition. According to the reported literature, the free 2,2-bipy, H4btc, H2bdc and H2ox exhibit fluorescent emission bands at 390 and 414 nm (λex = 318 nm),15 393 nm (λex = 347 nm),16 342 nm (λex = 308 nm),17 and 370 (λex = 340 nm),18 respectively. It should be pointed out that the emissions of complexes 1–5 are neither metal-to-ligand charge transfer (MLCT) nor ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT) in nature since the ZnII and CdII ions are difficult to oxidize or to reduce due to their d10 configuration, which are mainly based on the luminescence of ligands.19 By comparing the locations and profiles of their excitation/emission peaks with the corresponding complexes 1–5, we can presume that the auxiliary H4btc, H2bdc and H2ox ligands have little contribution to the fluorescent emissions of 1–5. The emission peaks in the range of ca. 360–500 nm for 1–5 should originate from the intraligand π→π* transitions mainly through the H3PIDC and bipyligands, namely ligand-to-ligand charge transfer (LLCT).20 Different delocalization environments may be an important factor on influencing different luminescent behaviors (red-shift or blue-shift) of complexes 1–5. The different dihedral angles between the imidazole rings and pyridyl rings in these complexes may lead to different π-electron systems. In addition, the introduction of an auxiliary conjugate system such as 2,2′-bipy can effectively promote the emission peaks shift towards the short wavelength direction. Excitation of the as-synthesized solid of 6 at 273 nm reveals the characteristic transition of the EuIII ion (Fig. 8b). They are attributed to the 5D0 → 7FJ (J = 1, 2, 3, 4) transition, i.e., 591 nm (5D0 → 7F1), 615 nm (5D0 → 7F2), 653 nm (5D0 → 7F3), and 700 nm (5D0 → 7F4). The most intense transition is 5D0 → 7F2, which implies a red emission light of 6. The high (5D0 → 7F2)/(5D0 → 7F1) intensity ratio of 5.0 for 6 confirms the absence of centrosymmetry in the EuIII site, which agrees with the result of X-ray single-crystal diffraction. For 7, the emission spectrum is illustrated in Fig. 8c, as all the transitions to the ground-state manifold from the emitting 5D4 level are observed when excited at 285 nm. It exhibits the characteristic transition of 5D4 → 7FJ (J = 3–6) of the TbIII ion. Two intense emission bands at 490 and a sharp line emission at 545 nm, which correspond to 5D4 → 7F6 and 5D4 → 7F5 transition manifold of TbIII, are observed, which gives an intense green luminescence, while the weaker emission bands at 586 and 622 nm originate from the 5D4 → 7F4 and 5D4 → 7F3 transition.

Conclusions

In conclusion, we have successfully constructed seven novel ZnII/CdII- and EuIII/TbIII-containing coordination polymers based on the 2-(pyridin-4-yl)-1H-imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid (H3PIDC) ligand and different co-ligands, such as 2,2′-bipy for 1, H4btc for 2, H2bdc for 3, HAc for 4, and H2ox for 5–7. It has been proven that the H3PIDC is a good candidate for construction of coordination polymers with diverse structures. By careful inspection of these structures, it is found that the coordination abilities of organic secondary ligands and the coordination geometries of the central metals have a significant influence on the resulting structures of the coordination polymers. The luminescent properties of compounds 1–7 indicate that they may be potential candidates for luminescent materials.
