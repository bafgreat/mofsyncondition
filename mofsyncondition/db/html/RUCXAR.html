Introduction

The depletion of fossil fuel sources and the prominence of environmental pollution have triggered the development of clean energy conversion and storage technology.1,2 Electrocatalytic water splitting is regarded as one of the most promising solutions to the recent energy crisis, which consists of two half-reactions, namely, the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) at the cathode and the oxygen evolution reaction (OER) at the anode.3–7 As we all know, the OER process often proceeds by a multi-step four-electron coupling (4OH− → 2H2O + 4e− + O2) with sluggish kinetics in alkaline media,7–9 and needs to overcome a high energy barrier of 237 kJ mol−1. To date, favourable electrocatalysts such as Ru- and Ir-based compounds are considered the most effective electrocatalyst in the OER.10–15 However, Ru- and Ir-based electrocatalysts are poor choices for large-scale commercial applications due to their expensive cost and undesirable stability. It is important to develop a non-noble metal OER electrocatalyst with high earth abundance, low cost and non-toxicity. Among the possible alternatives, the first-row transition metals (e.g. Fe,16,17 Co,18–23 and Ni24,25) have attracted considerable interest owing to their earth-abundance and superior OER performance.26

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), a type of periodic coordination polymer composed of metal ions and organic ligands that self-assembles, have potential in catalytic application related fields and so on.8,27–34 The abundant metal centers in MOFs can provide potential active sites, while the flexibility and large surface area in the framework not only make the open pores designable but also aids in mass and electron transport, which is necessary for OER electrocatalysis. Moreover, the unsaturated coordination metal centers in MOFs could improve the utilization of the active center, which benefit the nucleophilic attacks of OH− in the OER process. In addition, metal centers could be doped with similar atoms, forming isomorphic MOFs containing mixed-metal sites to regulate the local electronic structure, which results in enhanced OER performance through the synergistic effect.35–41 For example, Chen and co-workers adopted modular and stepwise synthetic strategies, in which the paddle-wheel cobalt carboxylate cluster was immobilized in a distinct FeIII dicarboxylate framework, resulting in striking OER catalytic activities.42 Fe3-Co2@GC has been demonstrated to possess a low overpotential of 283 mV at 10 mA cm−2 and a Tafel slope of 43 mV dec−1 in alkaline solutions (pH = 13). Xu et al. designed a series of CoxFe1−x-MOF-74 through in situ one-pot syntheses that had significantly enhanced OER activity in the mixed-site phase due to obtaining more open metal sites and an electron-rich environment.43,44 Despite these developments, it remains a challenge to explore excellent OER electrocatalysts in the tens of thousands of MOFs. In our previous studies on MOFs,45,46 Co3(OH)2(btca)2 (Co3-btca, H2btca = benzotriazole-5-carboxylic acid) possess a 1D porous structure and an unsaturated coordination CoII site (Scheme 1),47,48 which is expected to be an OER electrocatalyst. In this work, with consideration to a similar radius and valence, FeII and NiII were doped with CoII sites (M3-btca), forming a bimetallic and/or trimetallic M3-btca to elevate the OER catalytic activity via regulating the local electronic structure. Five isostructural mixed-site frameworks with formulas Co3−xFex-btca (Co2.74Fe0.26-btca, Co2.77Fe0.23-btca and Co2.79Fe0.21-btca) and Co3−x−yFexNiy-btca (Co1.25Fe0.83Ni0.92-btca, and Co2.36Fe0.19Ni0.45-btca) were obtained during the optimizing process (Scheme 1), and all of the frameworks could maintain good crystallinity in a KOH solution (pH = 14) for at least 8 h. Detailed studies revealed that Co2.36Fe0.19Ni0.45-btca possesses the best OER performances with a low overpotential of 292 mV at 10 mA cm−2, a small Tafel slope of 72.6 mV dec−1, and high stability up to 74 h.

Experimental

Syntheses

All starting materials were purchased at commercial reagent grade. Benzotriazole-5-carboxylic acid (97%, H2btca) was purchased from Maya Reagent Co Ltd.

Preparation of [Co3(OH)2(btca)2]·3.7H2O (denoted as Co3-btca)

The synthesis method is similar to previous literature with only slight optimization.47 The mixture of Co(NO3)2·6H2O (0.6 mmol, 175 mg), H2btca (0.2 mmol, 33 mg), DMF (2 mL) and H2O (2 mL) was added into a 15 mL Teflon-lined stainless reactor and stirred for about 30 min. The solution was placed in high temperature conditions of 140 °C for 4 days. Subsequently, after cooling to room temperature, purple crystals were gained in 40% yield.

Preparation of [(Co3−xFex (OH)2(btca)2]·3.7H2O (denoted as Co3−xFex-btca)

The bimetallic Co3−xFex-btca were synthesized with a similar process, only replacing Co(NO3)2·6H2O with the mixture of Co(NO3)2·6H2O and FeSO4·7H2O with the ratio of 0.6 : 0.2 mmol, 0.5 : 0.3 mmol, and 0.7 : 0.1 mol in a DMF : H2O 3 : 2 mL solution under a reaction temperature of 160 °C for Co2.74Fe0.26-btca, Co2.77Fe0.23-btca, and Co2.79Fe0.21-btca, respectively.

Preparation of [(Co3−x−yFexNiyOH)2(btca)2]·3.7H2O (denoted as Co3−x−yFexNiy-btca)

The trimetallic Co3−x−yFexNiy-btca were synthesized similar with Co3−xFex-btca, replacing Co(NO3)2·6H2O with the mixture of Co(NO3)2·6H2O, FeSO4·7H2O, and NiCl2·6H2O with the ratio of 0.3 : 0.1 : 0.2 mmol and 0.3 : 0.2 : 0.1 mmol for Co1.25Fe0.83Ni0.92-btca, and Co2.36Fe0.19Ni0.45-btca, respectively.

Characterization

The diffraction data of Co3-btca and Co2.74Fe0.26-btca were obtained by an Agilent Gemini Eos diffractometer at 293 K using Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). Co2.36Fe0.19Ni0.45-btca was examined by an Agilent Gemini Eos diffractometer at 293 K using Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.54184 Å). Structures of as-synthesized samples were solved by the direct method and refined with the full-matrix least-squares technique on F2 by the SHELXTL-2016 software package.49 All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. Hydrogen atoms were placed geometrically. The PLATON SQUEEZE treatment was applied because all guest molecules are extremely disordered and cannot be modelled. Detailed structure determination parameters and crystallographic data are given in Table S2.†

X-ray powder diffraction (PXRD) was carried out by Ultima IV-185 diffractometer. The morphology of the samples was observed by scanning electron microscopy (JEOL-JSM-7001F) at an acceleration voltage of 200 kV. A Thermo Scientific K-Alpha X-ray photoelectron spectrometer (XPS) was used to characterize the chemical compositions and valence of the samples. Determination of the elemental content was confirmed with a PerkinElmer Optima8000 Plasma Emission Spectrometer (ICP-AES). Infrared spectra were measured in the range of 400–4000 cm−1 by Tianjin Gangdong Technology HW-10 Fourier transforms Infrared Spectroscopy (FT-IR). Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were tested in an air atmosphere under heating to 800 °C using Shanghai yinnuo 1000B thermogravimetric analysis. All electrochemical tests were carried out on a Shanghai Chenhua 660 Electrochemical Workstation (CHI 660E).

Electrochemical measurements

Preparation of working electrode

The as-synthesized catalysts (10 mg) and acetylene black (3 mg) were mixed into 1 mL solvent (H2O/EtOH = 4 : 1, 80 μL Nafion) to form a uniform ink. Nickel foam (NF, 1 cm × 1 cm) was used as the conductive substrate and ultrasonically treated with acetone, 3 M hydrochloric acid, deionized water and EtOH before use. After that, the as-prepared ink was dripped onto the clean and dry NF and then dried at room temperature completely. The catalyst mass loading was about 3 mg cm−2.

Oxygen evolution reaction (OER) test

The electrochemical measurements were carried out in a standard three-electrode system in 1 M KOH (pH = 14) electrolyte. The Ag/AgCl electrode and carbon rod were used as the reference electrode and counter electrode, respectively. Prior to testing, the flow was kept in the N2 atmosphere for at least half an hour to exclude interference from other gases. The linear scan voltammetry (LSV) was tested with a scan rate of 5 mV s−1. Under the 0.892–0.992 V vs. RHE voltage window, the cyclic voltammogram (CV) was tested at the speed of 10–120 mV s−1 to calculated the double-layer capacitance (Cdl). The electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) was performed in the frequency ranges of 0.01 to 106 Hz. All electrode potentials were converted to the standard hydrogen electrode (RHE): E(RHE) = E(Ag/AgCl) + (0.197 + 0.059 pH). All the electro-chemical tests were performed without iR compensation.

Results and discussion

Crystal structure

The structural details of Co3-btca are shown in Fig. S1.† The Co1 ion has a six coordinate geometry and the Co2 site has an unsaturated square-pyramidal coordination geometry (Fig. S1b†). The unsaturated coordination of Co2 enables it to act as an active site, which aids the nucleophilic attack of –OH groups in the OER process. The CoII ions are bridged by μ3-OH groups to form a Co3(OH)2 chain (Fig. S1a†), and adjacent chains are further linked by μ5-btca ligands to furnish a 3D MOF with 1D channels (Fig. S1c†). The porous framework of Co3-btca could ensure mass transfer occurs in the OER catalytic process.

For the bimetallic Co3−xFex-btca and trimetallic Co3−x−yFexNiy-btca, the cell parameters of the bulk crystals were determined by single crystal diffraction and the results show they are isostructural. Furthermore, the X-ray powder diffraction (PXRD) patterns show that the peaks of all the as-synthesized samples were consistent with the simulated spectrum (Fig. 1a), indicating that the as-synthesized samples keep the same structure. To further confirm the isomorphism, bulk crystals of Co2.79Fe0.21-btca and Co2.36Fe0.19Ni0.45-btca were selected to gather crystal data. The corresponding crystallographic data and selected bond lengths of the three compounds are presented in Tables S1 and S2.† The Co2–O/N distances of unsaturated square-pyramidal Co2 for Co3-btca are in the range of 2.019(4)–2.176(5) Å. The corresponding distances in Co2.79Fe0.21-btca and Co2.36Fe0.19Ni0.45-btca tend to be smaller, indicating that the doping was successful. Interestingly, all the six MOFs maintain good crystallinity after immersing in 1 M KOH for at least 8 h at room temperature. The PXRD curves are shown in Fig. 1b, in which some changes occurred, especially around 20° and 28° of Co2.36Fe0.19Ni0.45-btca and Co2.79Fe0.21-btca before and after 1 M KOH impregnation for 8 h. This phenomenon may be caused by the following reasons: (1) the solvent molecules could enter the pores during the immersion, causing a slight deformation of the main body skeleton, and eventually leading to shift of the peak position; (2) water molecules or hydroxide may occupy the unsaturated coordination sites of Co atoms, causing minor changes in the skeleton, which is common in MOFs;50 (3) during immersion in the KOH solution, the crystal surface in direct contact with KOH may collapse to form a new phase. Additionally, we have tried to collect single crystal data after immersion, but no useful data could be acquired because the crystal quality was not good enough. Moreover, Co3-btca have been shown to be a breathing framework in our previous study and related references,48,50 which indirectly indicates that H2O or hydroxide in the aqueous solution will occupy the coordination unsaturated point.

Micromorphology and chemical composition

The morphology of the as-synthesized samples presents bulk crystals and the photos are viewed with an optical microscope. As shown in Fig. 2a, Co3-btca are purple crystals with the smallest size. The crystal colors of Co3−xFex-btca and Co3−x−yFexNiy-btca became darker, and the corresponding size tended to increase in which the size of Co2.36Fe0.19Ni0.45-btca is the largest. The elemental mapping analysis of Co2.36Fe0.19Ni0.45-btca also demonstrated the presence of Co, Fe, Ni, C, N and O elements, and all the elements were uniformly distributed throughout the whole structure (Fig. 2g). Inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-AES) was used to analyse the concentration of the metal elements (Table S3†). The analysis results revealed that the mole ratios of Co : Fe : Ni in the bimetallic and trimetallic phase were not equal to the original composition ratio. The phenomenon may be caused by the poor coordination ability of Fe2+ and Ni2+, which leads to some of them remaining in solution.37

X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) measurements were used to characterize the chemical composition and valence state of Co, Ni, and Fe for selected samples Co3-btca, Co2.79Fe0.21-btca and Co2.36Fe0.19Ni0.45-btca. The survey spectra are presented in Fig. S6.† From the high-resolution spectrum of Co 2p for Co3-btca, the peaks at around 797.3 eV and 781.5 eV can be attributed to Co 2p1/2, and Co 2p3/2, respectively, as well as the two satellite peaks at 802.8 and 785.9 eV (Fig. 3a), which suggest the +2 oxidation state of Co. The Co 2p spectra of Co2.79Fe0.21-btca and Co2.36Fe0.19Ni0.45-btca reflected that the peaks clearly shift to a more positive binding energy, which is similar with the trend observed in CoFeBO nanoparticles,51,52 indicating that the electronic structure can be regulated by doping with Fe and/or Ni. The more positive binding energy of the Co centers, especially for undersaturated sites, indicates that they were more susceptible to nucleophilic attack by OH− groups. For Co2.79Fe0.21-btca, the peaks of Fe 2p1/2 and Fe 2p3/2 appear at around 724.1 and 710.1 eV (Fig. 3b), suggesting the presence of Fe2+. The minor peaks at 712.2 eV can be assigned as Fe3+, due to the spontaneous oxidation of Fe2+ during the synthesis and measurement. The peaks of Fe 2p1/2 in Co2.36Fe0.19Ni0.45-btca were similar with those of Co2.79Fe0.21-btca (Fig. 3c). The Ni 2p spectra of Co2.36Fe0.19Ni0.45-btca displayed two main peaks (856.4 eV and 873.9 eV) and two satellite peaks (861.9 eV, 880.2 eV), indicating that Ni2+ ions were present (Fig. 3d).

Oxygen evolution activity

The standard three-electrode system was used to test the OER electrocatalytic activity with 1 M KOH solution as the electrolyte. For comparison, commercial IrO2 was tested as the catalyst under the same conditions. From the LSV results, it was found that the overpotential and current density of the three bimetallic Co3−xFex-btca have obvious advantages compared with the pure Co sites in Co3-btca (Fig. 4a). The overpotentials at a current density of 10 mA cm−2 (η10) derived from the LSV are 384 mV, 359 mV, 351 mV, and 349 mV, for Co3-btca, Co2.74Fe0.26-btca, Co2.77Fe0.23-btca, Co2.79Fe0.21-btca, respectively (Fig. 4b). The decreasing trends of η10 indicated that the doping of Fe reduces the overpotential. The Tafel slope of Co2.79Fe0.21-btca was the lowest at 81.5 mV dec−1 among the four catalysts (Fig. 4c).

Although the OER performance of bimetallic Co3−xFex-btca was extended, they have still not achieved the ideal qualifications. Therefore, it was desirable to optimize the OER performance by introducing a third metal component to further regulate the local electronic structure. The optimized trimetallic Co3−x−yFexNiy-btca were characterized by PXRD (Fig. S3†), IR (Fig. S4†), XPS and ICP-AES (Table S3†). As expected, the electrochemical activity of the trimetallic Co3−x−yFexNiy-btca was improved apparently. The η10 value of Co1.25Fe0.83Ni0.92-btca, Co2.36Fe0.19Ni0.45-btca was 306 mV and 292 mV, respectively (Fig. 5b). The Tafel slope of Co2.36Fe0.19Ni0.45-btca was not only smaller (72.6 mV dec−1) than that of Co1.25Fe0.83Ni0.92-btca, but also lower than that of the bimetallic electrocatalyst Co3−xFex-btca, indicating that Co1.25Fe0.83Ni0.92-btca possessed higher reaction kinetics (Fig. 5c). In order to further study the reason for the enhancement of performance of the trimetallic electrocatalyst, CV was performed and the electrochemical double-layer capacitance (Cdl) of the electrocatalyst was measured (Fig. 5d). The Cdl value of Co2.36Fe0.19Ni0.45-btca showed a certain attenuate tendency, which suggested Co3−x−yFexNiy-btca has a high number of local active centers at the solid–liquid interface when used as an OER electrocatalyst.53 To further discuss the catalytic kinetics, the electrochemical impedance spectroscopy was measured (Fig. S9†). According to the fitting equivalent circuit diagram, the internal resistances of Co2.36Fe0.19Ni0.45-btca could be calculated as 5.66 Ω (Fig. S10†), which is close to the value of NNU-23,39 demonstrating the fast charge transfer rate. The stability of Co2.36Fe0.19Ni0.45-btca was tested, and a 90% current density can be reserved after 74 h of continuous tests, which showed the high stability of the catalyst during the long-term OER process (Fig. 5e). To further reveal the possible origin of the enhanced OER activity, PXRD patterns and XPS analysis were recorded after the first 100 cycles (33 h) and 200 cycles (74 h) on a glassy carbon electrode (ϕ = 3 mm), respectively. As seen in Fig. S12,† the PXRD patterns suffered from obvious variation, which included not only the original peaks of the MOFs but several new peaks. The new peaks could be assigned to α-Ni(OH)2/α-Co(OH)2 and/or NiOOH,54,55 which is consistent with the high-resolution XPS spectra of Co 2p, Ni 2p and Fe 2p after cycling, as presented in Fig. S13–S15.† Compared to the original Co2.36Fe0.19Ni0.45-btca, the peaks of Co 2p tend to shift to lower binding energies after 100 and 200 cycles due to the collapse of the framework, which leads to independent Ni–O bonds, increased local electron density, and the formation of Co(OH)2 finally.52,54 Moreover, the peaks at 781.9 and 797.6 eV should be assigned to Co3+ after 200 cycles. The XPS spectra of Ni 2p shows a similar tendency, indicating that the structure may evolve from MOFs to Ni(OH)2 and NiOOH. In the Fe 2p XPS spectra, the peaks of Fe2+ exhibit no obvious shifts but the peak area of Fe3+ (713.0 eV) increases, indicating that Fe2+ was oxidized to Fe3+ during the oxygen evolution reaction. Additionally, the satellite peak of Fe3+ is clearly observable. The enhanced OER performance of trimetallic Co3−x−yFexNiy-btca may be attributed to the synergistic effect of Co, Fe and Ni in the unsaturated coordinated metal center, which were beneficial to the nucleophilic attack of OH− generating adsorption intermediates, and led to the formation of α-Ni(OH)2/α-Co(OH)2 and/or NiOOH finally.

Conclusions

In summary, five isostructural bimetallic and trimetallic frameworks based on Co3-btca containing a 1D channel and unsaturated coordinated metal centers were obtained by Fe and/or Ni doping. The OER performances of the series of isostructural frameworks showed that the optimized Co2.36Fe0.19Ni0.45-btca possesses the lowest overpotential of 292 mV at 10 mA cm−2 and a small Tafel slope of 72.6 mV dec−1. The enhanced catalytic performance could be attributed to the synergistic effect between unsaturated coordinated Co, Fe and Ni sites, which benefit the nucleophilic attack of OH− forming adsorption intermediates.
