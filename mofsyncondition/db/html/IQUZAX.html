1 Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have been intensively studied because of their interesting architectures and potential applications as functional materials in gas storage, catalysis, separation, magnetism, and sensing.1 Lanthanide-based metal–organic frameworks (LnOFs) have attracted great attention because of their 4fn electronic structure, high coordination number, and variable coordination geometries. These features led to a range of topological structures as well as magnetic, optical, and electronic properties.2 In particular, Ln3+ ions show unique luminescence properties, such as long luminescence lifetimes and high quantum yields. Hence, LnOFs materials have gained increased attention for a range of potential applications, such as luminescent probes in biomedical assays, light-emitting diodes (LEDs), and luminescence sensors for chemical species.3 LnOFs act as highly sensitive and simple chemical sensors, which do not require extensive devices and can be detected by the naked eye. For example, Eu3+ and Tb3+ complexes show a characteristic red emission (5D0 → 7FJ (J = 0–4) transitions) and a green emission (5D4 → 7FJ (J = 6, 5, 4, 3) transitions), respectively. The luminescence color change response of the Eu3+ and Tb3+ complexes can be clearly distinguished by the naked eye under UV light.4 Considering the satisfactory photophysical properties of Ln3+ ions, scholars have used several LnOFs for sensing anions, cations, and small molecules.4,5 The sensing principle is based on a fluorescence quenching or enhancement mechanism. Studies indicated that MOFs are promising luminescence sensors, which are important in biological and environmental systems.1c,6 Thus, luminescent MOFs are of great interest for studies on sensing of ions, gases, and solvents.4–7

MOFs are designed and synthesized using a combination of organic linkers and metal cations or clusters as secondary building units (SBU) to form unique structures. Different organic linkers and inorganic metal ions can tune the structures and properties of these frameworks.1f,8 Naphthalenedisulfonate (Nds)-based ligands are used to form various porous MOFs.9 The Nds is an appropriate binding ligand because of its structural rigidity, multiple coordination sites, and various binding modes, which impose conformational rigidity and prevent energy loss through bond vibration and electron transfer. The π-conjugated system on the ligands can considerably affect the luminescence properties of complexes. Nds ligands are suitable for constructing MOFs with good luminescence properties. In this work, using positional isomers of Nds as ligands, seven new complexes were synthesized, namely, {[Ln(1,5-Nds)0.5(ox)(phen)(H2O)]·H2O}n (Ln = Eu (1), La (2), and Sm (3); 1,5-Nds = 1,5-naphthalenedisulfonate; ox = oxalate; and phen = 1,10-phenanthroline), [La(2,6-Nds)(ox)0.5(phen)2(H2O)]n (4), and [Ln(2,6-Nds)0.5(ox)(phen)(H2O)]n (Ln = Eu (5), Gd (6), and Tb (7); 2,6-Nds = 2,6-naphthalenedisulfonate). The system features the combination of Nds and incorporated ox as bridging ligands as well as phen as an ancillary co-ligand because it can enhance the luminescence intensity of the complexes as an assistant antenna group. Although 1–3 and 5–7 have similar compositions, they exhibit remarkable diverse structural features, which are induced by the sulfonate groups in different positions. Here, we reported the synthesis, structure, and luminescence properties of these fabricated LnOFs. Notably, the pillared-layer microporous framework 1 shows a higher luminescence intensity than the other complexes and is thus used for sensing organic solvents and metal cations. The luminescence sensing of 1 under various solvents and metal cations is proposed potential sensing application.

2 Experimental

2.1 Materials and physical measurements

Ln(NO3)3·6H2O (Ln = Eu, La, Sm, and Tb) were prepared by mixing the corresponding oxides of the metals with nitric acid. Other reagents were commercially available and were used without further purification. Elemental analyses (C, H, and N) were carried out on an Elementar Vario EL analyzer. Infrared (IR) spectra were recorded on a Bruker Tensor37 spectrophotometer using KBr pellets from 400 to 4000 cm−1. Experimental powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) (Fig. S1a and b†) was carried out on a PANalytical X'Pert PRO MPD diffractometer with CuKα radiation (λ = 1.5406 Å), with a scan speed of 2° min−1 and a step size of 0.02° in 2θ. The temperature-dependent powder X-ray diffraction patterns (Fig. S1c and d†) are recorded on a Bruker D8 Discover with CuKα radiation. A field-emission (λ = 1.5406 Å, continuous, 40 kV, 40 mA, increment = 0.02°) study was performed at 30 °C, 130 °C, 210 °C, 350 °C, 400 °C, 480 °C for 1 and 30 °C, 200 °C, 300 °C, 400 °C, 460 °C, 500 °C for 5, respectively. The excitation and emission spectra of the solid and liquid samples were recorded on an FL7000 fluorescence spectrophotometer (Japan, Hitachi Co.) at room temperature. The emission quantum yields were measured at room temperature using a Quantum Yield Measurement System Fluorolog®-3 (HORIBA Company) with a 450 W Xe lamp coupled to a monochromator for wavelength discrimination, an integrating sphere as the sample chamber, and an analyzer R928P for signal detection. The lifetimes were recorded on an FLS920 Steady State & Time-resolved Fluorescence Spectrometer (Edinburgh Instrument) at room temperature. Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were carried out on a HCT-2 thermal analyzer at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 from room temperature to 800 °C in air. Inductively coupled plasma (ICP) spectroscopy was performed on an Agilent 7500Ce spectrometer. UV-vis spectroscopic studies were performed with a 2550 UV-visible spectrophotometer (Shimadzu, Japan). Nitrogen sorption isotherms were measured at 77 K using a Micromeritics ASAP 2020 HD88 analyser.

3 Results and discussion

3.1 Crystal structures

Structure of [Ln(1,5-Nds)0.5(ox)(phen)(H2O)]·H2O [Ln = Eu (1), La (2), and Sm (3)]

X-Ray analysis reveals that isostructural complexes 1–3 crystallize in the P space group and give pillared-layer 3D microporous frameworks. Herein only the structure of 1 will be discussed in detail. The asymmetric unit of 1 contains one Eu3+ ion, half of a 1,5-Nds anion, one ox ion, one phen ligand, one coordination water and one free water molecule. The Eu3+ ion is nine-coordinated in a [EuO7N2] tri-capped trigonal prismatic geometry and is coordinated to two O (O6, O8) atoms from two 1,5-Nds, two N (N1, N2) atoms from one phen in chelating mode, four O (O4, O2, O3, O1) atoms from two ox, and one O (O5) atom from the water molecule (Fig. 1a). The Eu–O and Eu–N bond lengths are in the range of 2.405(5)–2.528(6) Å and 2.596(6)–2.667(7) Å, respectively. The ox ligand in μ2:η1η1/μ2:η1η1 mode can coordinate to the Eu3+ ions. Ox and SO3 alternately connect to Eu3+ ions to form a honeycomb-like layer (63-hcb layer) with Eu⋯Eu distances of 6.306 and 6.247 Å, running parallel to the ac crystal plane. 1,5-Nds ligands, as pillars, link the adjacent layers in bis(bidentate-bridging) mode to build a 3D pillared-layer porous structure, with an Eu⋯Eu distance of 10.658 Å between adjacent layers (Fig. 1b). The space-filling model of 1 shows square channels down the a axis and a porous honeycomb structure down the b and c axes (Fig. 1c), where water molecules reside (Fig. S2a†). The void volume of complex 1 without free water molecules is 3.5% per unit volume, as determined by PLATON.11 To examine the existence of porosity, N2 gas sorption was performed. Sample 1 was activated at a temperature of 90 °C under vacuum for gas sorption studies. The N2 adsorption–desorption isotherms are shown in Fig. S3.† A Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface area of 9.0444 m2 g−1 was obtained. The non-coordinated O atoms of 1,5-Nds are located inside the channels and build hydrogen bonds with free water molecules [O7 [−x, 2 − y, 1 − z]⋯H1WA–O1W, with an O⋯O distance of 2.896 Å, and O1 [−x, 2 − y, 1 − z]⋯H1WB–O1W, with an O⋯O distance of 2.819 Å]. A helix-like chain structure is found along the b axis and consists of six Eu3+ ions, four ox ligands, and two 1,5-Nds units per turn, with a screw pitch of 21.3054 Å (Fig. S2b†). Topological analysis12 reveals that the 3D skeleton can be viewed as a uninodal net with a point symbol of {36·48·56·6} (Fig. S2c†).

Structure of [La(2,6-Nds)(ox)0.5(phen)2(H2O)]n (4)

Compound 4 crystallized in the triclinic, P space group. Compound 4 features a 2D framework, with the asymmetric unit comprising one La3+ ion, one 2,6-Nds ligand, half of an ox ligand, two phen ligands and one coordination water molecule. The La3+ center binds to two O (O1 and O4) atoms from two 2,6-Nds ligands, two O (O7 and O8A) atoms from ox, four N (N1, N2, N3, and N4) atoms from two phen molecules and one water molecule (O9) in a distorted [LaO5N4] tri-capped trigonal prism (Fig. 2a). The La–O and La–N bond lengths are in the range of 2.461(3)–2.579(3) Å and 2.698(3)–2.754(4) Å, respectively. The two terminal phen molecules are not parallel with a dihedral angle of 86.54°. The 2,6-Nds ligand adopts a bis(monodentate) mode to link the La3+ centers, leading to the formation of a La–2,6-Nds zigzag chain, with a La⋯La⋯La angle of 95.23°. The ox ligand in μ2:η1η1/μ2:η1η1 coordination mode connects the neighboring chains to form the (63) grid-like 2D layer structure parallel to the ac crystal plane (Fig. 2b), where the separation distances between La⋯La and ox and 2,6-Nds ligands are 6.436(4) and 14.923(2) Å, respectively. A hydrogen bond [C6–H6⋯O8, 3.251 Å, 135.28°] between phen and ox is present between layers and contributes to a two-fold interpenetrating 3D supramolecular architecture (Fig. 2c). The space-filling model of the 3D architecture shows square porous channels down the b axis (Fig. S4†).

Structure of [Ln(2,6-Nds)0.5(ox)(phen)(H2O)]n [Ln = Eu (5), Gd (6), and Tb (7)]

Complexes 5–7 are isostructural, possessing 2D network structures and crystallized in the monoclinic space group P21/c. As a representative example, only the structure of 5 will be discussed in detail. The asymmetric unit of 5 contains one Eu3+ ion, half of a 2,6-Nds anion, one ox ion, one phen group, and one coordination water molecule. The Eu3+ ion is eight coordinated in a [EuO6N2] distorted square-antiprism geometry formed by one O (O3) atom from a 2,6-Nds ligand, four O (O5, O6, O4 and O7) atoms from two ox ligands, two N (N1 and N2) atoms from a phen molecule and one water molecule (O8) (Fig. 3a). The Eu–O bond distances vary from 2.369(6) to 2.464(6) Å; the Eu–N bond lengths are 2.581(7) and 2.561(7) Å. The 2,6-Nds and ox ligands adopt bis(monodentate) and bis(bidentate-bridging) coordination modes, respectively, as seen in 4. The ox ligands link the Eu3+ ions to form Eu–ox zigzag chains with an Eu⋯Eu⋯Eu angle of 94.698°. The chains are conjoined through 2,6-Nds moieties into the (63) grid-like layer running parallel to the ac crystal plane (Fig. 3b), which differs from the (63) structure of 4. The distances between Eu⋯Eu and ox and 2,6-Nds are 6.084 and 11.759 Å, respectively. The O atoms from ox and 2,6-Nds form hydrogen bonds with hydrogen atoms on the phen ligand [O7⋯H5–C5, 3.276 Å; O1⋯H10–C10, 3.061 Å], thereby forming a 3D supramolecular structure. Two helix chains were formed by the hydrogen bonds (Fig. S5†).

3.2 Luminescence properties

The luminescence properties of complexes 1–7 in the solid state were investigated at room temperature. Complexes 2, 4 and 6 exhibit a ligand-centered broad band emission at 378 nm with a shoulder at 393 nm for 2 (λex = 276 nm), 374 nm with a shoulder at 392 nm for 4 (λex = 325 nm), and 447 nm for 6 (λex = 374 nm) characterized by a π* → π transition. These findings are in accordance with the absence of an f–f transition for La3+ and Gd3+ ions (Fig. S6†). For complex 3, the emission spectrum (λex = 381 nm) presents narrow bands at 560, 597, and 644 nm, which correspond to the 4G5/2 → 6HJ (J = 5/2, 7/2, and 9/2) transitions of Sm3+ ions, as well as a broad band centered at 465 nm, which is due to the ligands (Fig. 4). The quantum yield (QY) is 1.25%, and the luminescence lifetime (τ) is 0.0141 ms (Fig. S7a†). The excitation spectrum of complex 7 monitored at 545 nm, which corresponds to an intense 5D4 → 7F5 transition of the Tb3+ ion, exhibits a broad band between 200 and 400 nm with a maximum peak at 271 nm, which is due to ligand excitation. Excited at the maximum excitation wavelength of 271 nm, complex 7 exhibits characteristic narrow emission peaks at 489, 545, 585, and 614 nm, which correspond to the 5D4 → 7FJ (J = 6–3) transitions of the Tb3+ ion (Fig. 5). The maximum peak at 545 nm (5D4 → 7F5) shows green light under UV light. The fluorescence quantum yield is 30.54% and the lifetime value is 0.2135 ms (Fig. S7b†). The broad emission band due to ligands in 7 is not observed, indicating that the ligand-to-Tb3+ energy transfer is efficient.

The excitation spectrum of 1 was recorded at 618 nm, which corresponds to the intense 5D0 → 7F2 transition of the Eu3+ ion. The spectrum comprises broad absorption and narrow bands. The strong broad band in the 200–390 nm region (centered at 358 nm) can be attributed to the π → π* transition of the ligands. The low-intensity narrow bands in the 350–450 nm region arise from the f–f transitions of Eu3+ ions, from 7F0 to the excited states: 5G6 (386 nm), 5L6 (395 nm), 5D3 (417 nm), 5D2 (465 nm), and 5D1 (534 nm). Therefore, luminescence sensitization from ligand excitation is efficient. Under maximum excitation at 358 nm, complex 1 shows characteristic narrow emission peaks at 581, 594, 618, 652, and 694 nm, which correspond to the 5D0 → 7FJ (J = 0–4) transitions of the Eu3+ ion (Fig. 6). The emission at 618 nm (5D0 → 7F2) is the most prominent, and complex 1 emits red light under UV light. The luminescence properties of 5 were also investigated. Similar excitation and emission spectra were obtained under wavelengths of 613 nm and 329 nm, respectively (Fig. S8†). The fluorescence quantum yield is 38.91% for 1 and 28.29% for 5. The luminescence decay fits well with a monoexponential curve (Fig. S7c and d†), with a lifetime of 0.7871 ms for 1 and 0.6008 ms for 5.

3.3 Framework stability of complexes 1 and 5

The powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns (Fig. S1a and b†) of the as synthesized samples 1 and 5 fit well with the simulated patterns; hence, these compounds are in pure phase. The thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) data (Fig. 7) of 1 show that weight loss occurs in two steps. A minor weight loss of approximately 5.6% occurs at 146–202 °C because of the removal of free and coordinated water molecules (calcd.: 6.0%). No weight loss is observed up to 401 °C. With further heating, a major weight loss occurs because of the decomposition of organic ligands. The TGA data of 5 show that coordinated water molecules (found: 3.76%; calcd.: 3.8%) are released at 230–264 °C, and the dehydrated sample is stable up to 383 °C. Upon further heating, organic ligands in 5 were decomposed. The PXRD pattern of 1 obtained by vacuum drying is consistent with that of 1 in the solid state; hence, the basic 3D framework of 1 after vacuum drying treatment is retained. The luminescence lifetime of sample 1 by vacuum drying increases to 0.8986 ms (Fig. S7e†), which is 14.2% higher than the initial solid 1. Thus, the luminescence of sample 1 is improved by vacuum drying as a result of loss of guest water molecules.

The temperature-dependent powder X-ray diffraction patterns (PXRD) for the complexes 1 (Fig. S1c†) and 5 (Fig. S1d†) were collected. For 1, the patterns at 130, 210, 350 and 400 °C are very similar to that at room-temperature (30 °C). However, the PXRD pattern at 480 °C is obviously different. So, the PXRD patterns indicate that after the removal of water molecules, the structural skeleton remains unchanged. The TGA and PXRD data show that the structural skeleton remains stable up to 400 °C. For 5, the pattern at 200 °C is very similar to that at room-temperature (30 °C), while the patterns at 300 and 400 °C are similar. The small changes between PXRD patterns at 300 and 200 °C are due to the removal of coordinated water molecules. The PXRD pattern at 460 °C is clearly different, showing that the structural skeleton collapsed because of the decomposition of organic ligands.

3.4 Discussion

Two series of LnOFs were hydrothermally synthesized by using Nds and ox as mixed-building blocks as well as Ln3+ ions with the phen ligand. Although these compounds exhibit similar compositions, they significantly differ in terms of structure because of differences in the following: positions of the sulfonate groups in the Nds connector and coordination environments of metal centers and Nds bridging modes. The 2,6-Nds-based linker yields 2D (63) network structures of complexes 5–7. However, the 1,5-Nds-based linker can pillar into a high dimensionality pillared-layer microporous motif of complexes 1–3, affording one-dimensional channels and creating a void volume occupied by solvent water molecules. In addition, a distinct geometric motif is present and recognized as the [LnO7N2] tri-capped trigonal prismatic geometry for 1–3, [LaO5N4] tri-capped trigonal prisms for 4, and [LnO6N2] square-antiprism coordination geometry for 5–7. The 1,5-Nds ligand adopts a bis(bidentate-bridging) coordination mode in 1–3, whereas the 2,6-Nds ligand adopts a bis(monodentate) coordination mode in 5–7. These results indicate that the diverse structural topologies of these complexes mainly depend on the positional isomeric characteristic of the Nds ligands; the different dispositions of the sulfonate groups of the ligands considerably influence the structure of final products. The different structures of complexes 4–7 may be attributed to the larger radius and higher coordination number of the La3+ cation than those of the other Ln3+ cations. Comparison of the average distances (Table S1†) of Ln–O, Ln–N, and Ln⋯Ln for complexes 1–3, 4, and 5–7, respectively, shows that the corresponding distances decrease with decreasing ionic radius of the Ln3+ ions in the order La3+ > Sm3+ > Eu3+ and La3+ > Eu3+ > Gd3+ > Tb3+. This finding is due to lanthanide contraction along the lanthanide series.

Complex 1 is dehydrated at 146 °C because of the loss of lattice water molecules, and complex 5 is dehydrated at 230 °C because of the loss of coordination water molecules. The structural skeleton of 1 and 5 is stable up to 401 °C and 383 °C, respectively. Comparison of luminescence between 1 and 5 shows that the maximum excitation wavelength (358 nm) for 1 is longer than that for 5 (329 nm); however, analysis of luminescence data shows that the quantum yield increases by 37.5%, and the luminescence lifetime increases by 30.0%. Thus, 1,5-Nds is an effective sensitizer for the Eu3+ ion, resulting in an increased sensitization efficiency of the ion. The quantitative relationship between these parameters and the structure of the complexes remains unclear.13 The results highlight that 1,5-Nds is the most suitable ligand for construction of Ln3+ complexes with a novel structure and satisfactory luminescence properties. The maximum excitation wavelength for 1 (358 nm, which is longer than 340 nm) is an evident advantage for luminescence applications, since a longer excitation wavelength means lower energy and is less harmful to biological tissues.

3.5 Luminescence sensing for small molecules

The luminescence of MOF materials as chemical sensors is sensitive to some guest molecules or ions.6a Complex 1 possesses the microporous pillared-layer framework and strong luminescence at a maximum excitation wavelength longer than 340 nm. In addition, non-coordinating sulfonate oxygen atoms and aqua ligands are located inside the channels of the framework, which are potential Lewis basic sites for sensing metal ions. Thus, complex 1 was chosen for luminescence sensing analysis. Sensing was primarily focused on small organic molecules. Suspensions of sample 1 dispersed in different solvents were prepared. These solvents included acetone, isopropanol, ethyl acetate, tetrahydrofuran (THF), acetonitrile, methanol, N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF), H2O, methanal, aniline, trichloromethane (THMs), triethylamine, dimethylsulfoxide (DMSO), dichloromethane (DMC), xylene, and benzaldehyde. The luminescence properties of these suspensions were determined at an excitation wavelength of 358 nm (Fig. 8a). The emission of these suspensions strongly depends on the type of solvents and the luminescence color changes from red-pink to blue (Fig. 8b). When complex 1 is dispersed in acetone, isopropanol, and ethyl acetate, they show the highest intensity 5D0 → 7F2 emission from the Eu3+ center and fall within the red region. THF, acetonitrile, methanol, DMF, and H2O as solvents give a moderate intensity 5D0 → 7F2 emission from the Eu3+. However, when complex 1 was dispersed in THMS, triethylamine, DMSO, and DMC, the luminescence spectra of the suspensions and the solid-state of 1 remarkably differed. The suspensions show a very low 5D0 → 7F2 transition emission from the Eu3+, with the occurrence of a new broad emission band from 350 to 550 nm. The broad emission band is attributed to organic ligands. Furthermore, the broad emission band is stronger than that of the Eu3+. Especially, complex 1 immersed in THMS and triethylamine shows a strong blue light emission with CIE coordinates (0.157, 0.086) and (0.159, 0.122), respectively. Notably, in the case of benzaldehyde, emission is not observed and the red luminescence color of 1 disappeared, which indicates that benzaldehyde exhibits a strong quenching effect on the luminescence of Eu3+. Benzaldehyde can completely quench the luminescence of Eu3+, which indicates that complex 1 is a promising luminescent material for sensing of the pollutant benzaldehyde. The effect of the benzaldehyde concentration on luminescence sensing of complex 1 in isopropanol was also investigated (Fig. 8c). In the presence of 200, 300, and 400 ppm benzaldehyde, the emission intensity decreased gradually and the quenching efficiencies are 62%, 88%, and 90%, respectively (the quenching efficiency is defined as (I0 − I)/I0 × 100%, where I0 and I are the luminescence intensities of 1 before and after the addition of benzaldehyde, respectively.4c) The red color luminescence of Eu3+ could not be detected by the naked eye under 365 nm UV-light. These results suggest the possibility of using complex 1 for detection of benzaldehyde pollution. The PXRD pattern (Fig. S1a†) of 1 immersed in benzaldehyde is very similar to that of 1 in the solid state. The slight difference between PXRD patterns may be due to the benzaldehyde attached onto the surface of the framework 1, suggesting that the basic framework of 1 is retained and is stable in the benzaldehyde.4e The possible quenching mechanism is an energy transformation from ligands in 1 to the electron-withdrawing benzaldehyde molecule; this phenomenon reduces the ligand-to-Eu3+ energy transformation, leading to the quenching effect on the luminescence intensity of Eu3+.4e,7a,b Complex 1 was separated from benzaldehyde/isopropanol by filtration and washing with isopropanol (5 mL) three times; the red color of Eu3+ luminescence then appears under 365 nm UV-light. When sample 1 was re-immersed in benzaldehyde (600 ppm)/isopropanol (5 mL), the red luminescence color disappeared. The color tuning from red to colorless can be distinguished by the naked eye under 365 nm UV-light, showing the optical “turn-off” sensing behavior for a benzaldehyde molecule. Thus, the recyclable performance of complex 1 was investigated (Fig. S9†). Complex 1 was recycled and reused three times. The luminescence intensity of 1 does not decrease compared with that in the initial state. Hence, this compound possesses high stability for repeated usage for benzaldehyde detection. Benzaldehyde confers serious pollution in the environment; as such, a convenient and highly efficient detection technology can be developed based on MOFs.

3.6 Luminescence sensing for inorganic ions

Crystals of 1 immersed in an aqueous solution can retain its characteristic luminescence. We further investigated the sensing ability of complex 1 for metal ions in an aqueous solution. Complex 1 was dispersed in an aqueous solution containing the same concentration (10−2 mol L−1) of M(NO3)n (M = Ba2+, Mg2+, K+, Ni2+, Pb2+, Zn2+, Cr3+, Cu2+, Co2+, Cd2+, Ag+, and Fe3+) to form Mn+@1 suspensions; luminescence experiments were further performed at an excitation wavelength of 358 nm (Fig. 9a). Different cations show varied effects on the luminescence of complex 1. Ba2+ and Mg2+ ions slightly increase the luminescence intensity. Other cations can quench the luminescence, but their quenching effects differed. In the presence of these metal ions, the luminescence intensity of the 5D0 → 7F2 transition for the Eu3+ ion shows different degrees of decrease. An interesting phenomenon is that upon addition of Cr3+ or Cd2+ to aqueous solutions of 1, the suspensions show a blue light emission due to the occurrence of strong ligand-emission bands centered at 418 and 406 nm. Notably, the Fe3+ ion totally quenches the luminescence of complex 1. Thus, complex 1 can be used for luminescence sensing of Fe3+ ions in an aqueous solution. The influence of the Fe3+ concentration on the luminescence of 1 was also examined. The emission intensity at 618 nm of Fe3+@1 gradually decreases with increasing concentration from 10−6 to 10−2 mol L−1 (Fig. 9b). In the addition of Fe3+ concentrations of 5 × 10−4 and 10−3 mol L−1, the quenching efficiency reaches 96.92% and 98.04%, respectively. However, a linear relationship cannot be obtained between the Fe3+ concentration and the corresponding quenching effect (Fig. S10†). A fluorescence lifetime of 0.6876 ms for 1 in an aqueous solution is significantly reduced to 4.228 μs (Fig. S7f†) in the presence of 10−3 mol L−1 Fe3+; hence, the luminescence of complex 1 is almost completely quenched by the Fe3+ ion.

A complex 1 sample was immersed in four random aqueous solutions containing 10−2 mol L−1 Ba2+, Pb2+, Cr3+, and Cd2+ to further study the highly selective luminescence sensing of complex 1 toward the Fe3+ ion in an aqueous solution, and the corresponding emissions were analysed (Fig. S11†). When Fe3+ ions (10−2 mol L−1) were added to these solutions, the Eu3+ luminescence was completely quenched (Fig. 10), indicating that the other metals do not interfere with the detection of the Fe3+ ion. These results show that complex 1 can still selectively sense Fe3+ ions in the presence of these other metal ions in an aqueous solution by luminescence quenching, thereby confirming that complex 1 has high selectivity for detection of Fe3+ ions.

3.7 Possible mechanism for luminescence quenching by Fe3+ ions

It is well-known that the luminescence of Ln3+ complexes is the result of the ligand absorption energy and efficient energy transfer to Ln3+ ions. Luminescence quenching by Fe3+ ions is speculated to be influenced by Fe3+ ions on the ligand-to-Eu3+ energy transfer. The possible luminescence sensing mechanism for Fe3+ was discussed. The PXRD pattern (Fig. S1a†) of Fe3+@1 is very similar to the pattern of the as-synthesized 1 and the calculated pattern from the crystal data of 1. The slight difference between PXRD patterns may be due to the Fe3+ ion attached onto the surface of the framework 1. As such, the basic framework structure of 1 remains unchanged4g and Fe3+ does not affect the first coordination sphere geometry. The N2 gas sorption experiment of complex 1 shows a lower adsorption amount with a Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface area of 9.0444 m2 g−1. Therefore, the Fe3+ ion may attach onto the surface and/or pores of the framework. First, photographs before and after immersion in Fe3+ differ, as observed by the naked eye (Fig. 9c). This difference in color response demonstrates that the Fe3+ ions were absorbed on the surface of complex 1. The ICP result of the solid-state Fe3+@1 clearly indicates that the Fe3+ ion is attached to crystal 1, and the ratio of Fe3+ to Eu3+ is 2 : 7 in H2O. Furthermore, the lifetime value for the solid-state Fe3+@1 is 8.355 μs (Fig. S7g†), which is significantly decreased compared with that of 1 in the solid state (0.7871 ms). Furthermore, the adsorption of Fe3+ ions was further confirmed from the UV-vis absorption spectrum (Fig. S12†). The UV-Vis absorption spectrum for the aqueous Fe3+ solution shows strong absorption at about 296 nm, which may affect the efficiency of the absorption of the ligand and the ligand-to-Eu3+ energy transfer; this phenomenon could lead to the luminescence quenching of Eu3+. In addition, compared with other ions, Fe3+ with a half filled d-electron spherical distribution has the most number of unpaired electrons, which can effectively quench luminescence.4f,7c Moreover, the possible interaction between Fe3+ ions and Lewis basic sites within the micropores of the framework 1 results in the luminescence quenching.4g,h

3.8 Solvent effect on luminescence sensing for inorganic ions

As mentioned above, the luminescence of complex 1 depends on the type of solvents. So, luminescence sensing for metal ions was further investigated using solvents, such as isopropanol, acetone, and ethyl acetate because of the high luminescence intensity of complex 1 in these solvents. As shown in Fig. 11, metal ions Mg2+, Zn2+, Ni2+, Ba2+, Co2+, Cd2+, Cr3+, Ag+, and Cu2+ show varied degrees of luminescence quenching effects on 1, whereas Fe3+ shows a total quenching effect on the luminescence of 1. The sensitivity of the luminescence quenching of complex 1 toward Fe3+ in isopropanol was examined by varying the concentration of the Fe3+ ion ranging from 10−6 to 10−2 mol L−1. When the concentration of Fe3+ is 5 × 10−3 mol L−1, the quenching efficiency is 98.82%. The result demonstrates that complex 1 has high selectivity for Fe3+ in an isopropanol solution. However, K+ and Pb2+ can drastically enhance the luminescence intensity of 1. The characteristic emission at 618 nm corresponding to the 5D0 → 7F2 transition of the Eu3+ ion increases by approximately 55% compared with that of 1 immersed in an isopropanol solution without K+ or Pb2+ ions. This finding suggests the high efficiency of the energy transfer from the ligands to the Eu3+ ion in the presence of K+ or Pb2+ ions. This is different from the result of 1 immersed in an aqueous solution. We explored the possibility of complex 1 for luminescence sensing of Fe3+ ions by using acetone as a solvent (Fig. S13†). The compound shows selectivity for sensing Fe3+, and the emission of 1 is almost completely quenched in the presence of Fe3+. K+, Ba2+, and Pb2+ ions can enhance the emission of Eu3+ by 104–131%. However, the effect of metal ions on the emission behavior of complex 1 in ethyl acetate differs from that in aqueous/isopropanol solutions (Fig. S14†). K+, Ag+, Ba2+, and Pb2+ ions enhance the luminescence of 1. Mg2+, Cd2+, Cr3+, Ni2+, and Co2+ ions quench the luminescence of 1. Fe3+, Zn2+, and Cu2+ ions show a total quenching effect on the luminescence of 1, which indicates that Zn2+ and Cu2+ ions have interference for sensing of Fe3+ ions. These results indicate that the luminescence sensing of complex 1 for metal ions is related to the solvents used. So, choosing the appropriate solvent can increase the selectivity of sensing Fe3+ or the addition of the appropriate metal ions can greatly improve the luminescence intensity. It should be pointed out that a non-radiative energy transfer to nearby O–H oscillators of polar water molecules results in a low luminescence intensity.3a However, complex 1 exhibits highly selective sensing for Fe3+ ions in an aqueous solution through the luminescence quenching effect. It is noteworthy that one of the important advantages of complex 1 as a luminescence sensor is that this complex can be excited with a longer excitation wavelength (358 nm) compared with previously reported luminescent lanthanide sensors,4,5 which is significantly important for applications.

4 Conclusions

Distinct 2D and 3D Ln3+-coordination frameworks were synthesized by using two positional isomers of naphthalenedisulfonate ligands together with ox and phen ligands, namely, {[Ln(1,5-Nds)0.5(ox)(phen)(H2O)]·H2O}n (Ln = Eu (1), La (2), Sm(3)), [La(2,6-Nds)(ox)0.5(phen)2(H2O)]n (4) and [Ln(2,6-Nds)0.5(ox)(phen)(H2O)]n (Ln = Eu (5), Gd (6), and Tb(7)). Notably, the 1,5-Nds-based linker can pillar into a high dimensionality pillared-layer microporous motif. The pillared-layer microporous Eu3+-framework 1 exhibits good framework stability and strong luminescence. Luminescence studies reveal that the framework 1 exhibits solvent-dependent luminescence properties and luminescence sensing for metal ions. Especially, the framework displays a highly sensitive sensing function with respect to benzaldehyde and Fe3+ under a longer excitation wavelength, which is an evident advantage for luminescence applications and provides important information for the future application of this luminescent compound.
