Abstract

N-acylhydrazones of salicylaldehyde is a well known class of ligands bearing O,N,O tridentate chelate coordination site. But up to now no structurally characterized Ag+ complexes with O,N,O coordinated N-acylhydrazones of salicylaldehyde were known. Therefore three Ag+ coordination compounds with functionally substituted N-acylhydrazones of salicylaldehyde were synthesized and characterized by X-ray diffraction, IR spectroscopy and thermogravimetric studies. In solutions the complexes were studied by 1H NMR and UV–Vis spectroscopy.

The influence of the presence and location of additional nitrogen donor atoms in N-acylhydrazones of salicylaldehyde on their coordination modes was investigated. Ligand N’-salicylidenephenylacetohydrazide (contains no additional donor atoms) coordinates to Ag+ in classical O,N,O tridentate chelate manner. Ligand N′-salicylidene-3-pyridinecarbohydrazide (contains β-N additional donor atom in acyl moiety) involves mixed O,N,O + β-N coordination. Whereas N’-salicylidene-4-pyridinecarbohydrazide (contains γ-N additional donor atom) coordinates to Ag+ in monodentate manner only via γ-N atom of heterocyclic substituent.

All the complexes provide high coordination numbers of Ag+ and irregular shapes of coordination polyhedra which are not consistent with classical concepts of Ag+ coordination chemistry. However all the complexes are not thermally stable and easily undergo solvolysis upon dissolution.

1. Introduction

Nowadays, a problem of microbial resistance to current antimicrobial agents, particularly antibiotics, is very urgent. Therefore, there is a need in novel antimicrobial compounds developing with different from current antibiotics mechanism of action. Special attention in this field is deserved to silver and its compounds which antimicrobial properties have been known from ancient times [1], [2]. Despite the fact that exact mechanisms of Ag+ antimicrobial activity are still not completely understood, silver is known to kill pathogens in a variety of ways by deactivating metabolic pathways and genetic machinery and by weakening bacterial cell walls. Such multiple modes of activity make the Ag+ a broad-spectrum antimicrobial agent and reduce the possibility of resistant organisms developing [3], [4]. Up to now the complex of Ag+ with sulfadiazine antibiotic (silver sulfadiazine [5], [6]) is still the most widely used to treat and prevent the development of bacterial infections in skin burns and wounds [7], [8], [9].

High interest to chemistry of hydrazones is determined by their pronounced biological activity. Hydrazone derivatives are known to possess high spectrum of physiological effects as well as antibacterial, antifungal and antimycobacterial activities [10]. Also functionally substituted hydrazones such as N-acylhydrazones of salicylaldehyde are also well known ligands (Fig. 1). They provide fixed tridentate chelate O,N,O coordination mode for a wide variety of metal ions with formation of bis- [11], [12] and ever tris- (with lanthanides) [13], [14] coordination compounds and also complexes with a M:L ratio of 1:1 in which vacant coordination sites of a metal ion can be occupied by secondary anionic or neutral ligand as well as solvent molecules [11], [15], [16], [17], [18].

The special feature of this type of ligands is their ability to amido-imidol tautomerism. N-acylhydrazones of salicylaldehyde can coordinate as mono- (a) or dianion (b) and also in molecular form [19], [20] (c) depending on synthesis conditions (Fig. 1).

Search in Cambridge Structural Database for 2015 year [21] showed 867 structures of different transition metals complexes with O,N,O coordinated N-acylhydrazones of salicylaldehyde. But the same search for Ag+ complexes showed that there are no silver complexes involving the O,N,O coordination site of the ligands in complex formation. So synthesis and investigation of Ag+ complex compounds involving N-acylhydrazones of salicylaldehyde as ligands is interesting from chemical point of view. Also such study may be interesting for medicine due to possible synergism of the pronounced antibacterial properties of Ag+ and acyl hydrazones.

The present work provides synthesis, X-ray diffraction analysis, spectral characterization and thermal stability analysis of Ag+ complexes with a row of N-acylhydrazones of salicylaldehyde derivatives.

2. Experimental

2.1. Materials and measurement

Initial salt AgNO3 (99%), HNO3 (65%), N2H4 (50–60%), ethyl phenylacetate (99%), ethyl nicotinate (99%), ethyl isonicotinate (98%) and salicyaldehyde (98%) were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich and used without further purification. Solvents C2H5OH and diethyl ether were purchased from local company and purified by standart methods [22]. Attenuated total internal reflection Fourier transform infrared (ATR-FT-IR) spectra were recorded on a Thermo Nicolet Nexus 4700 FT-IR spectrometer in a range of 4000–500 cm−1. 1H NMR spectra were measured on a Bruker Avance DRX-500 spectrometer (500.00 MHz) in DMSO-d6 and CD3CN solution using TMS as internal standard. UV–Vis spectra of methanol solutions of ligands and their complexes were obtained on a Specord M40 spectrophotometer in a range of 48000–11000 cm−1 using a 2 mm quartz cell. Thermal stability of samples was studied using a derivatograph Q-1500D (Paulik, Paulik & Erdey, MOM, Hungary) with computer data registration. TGs were recorded in the temperature range from 20 to 1000 °C. Samples with mass 50+–1 mg were heated at 10 °C/min in air.

2.2. Synthesis of ligands H2L1−3 and complexes I-III

The ligands N′-salicylidenephenylacetohydrazide (H2L1), N′-salicylidene-3-pyridinecarbohydrazide (H2L2) and N′-salicylidene-4-pyridinecarbohydrazide (H2L3) were prepared by condensation of corresponding carbonic acids hydrazides with salicylaldehyde in boiling ethanol. The carbonic acids hydrazides were prepared by hydrazinolysis of corresponding ethyl esters [23], [24]. Synthesized organic molecules were identified using 1H NMR spectroscopy (see 1H NMR data in section 3.4).

It should be noted that the complex III is already known [25], but in the present paper we provide a slightly improved synthesis with much higher yield.

2.2.1. Synthesis of [Ag(H2L1)2]NO3 (I)

A finely ground portion of AgNO3 (42.5 mg, 0.25 mmol) was dissolved in 20 ml of C2H5OH. To formed solution was added dropwise H2L1 solution (127 mg, 0.5 mmol in 10 ml of С2H5OH) under constant stirring. Obtained colorless solution was stirred carefully and left for crystallization in the dark by slow solvent evaporation. In 3 days white thin needle-shaped crystals formed. The crystals were filtered off, washed with cold С2H5OH and diethyl ether. Yield 135 mg (80%). Calculated for C30H27AgN5O7, %: C, 53.19; H, 4.02; N, 10.34. Found, %: C, 52.92; H, 3.85; N, 10.28.

2.2.2. Synthesis of ([Ag2(H2L2)2](NO3)2·H2O)n (II)

To solution of 85 mg (0.5 mmol) AgNO3 in 5 ml H2O acidified with 1 ml of 1 M HNO3, was added dropwise under constant stirring solution of H2L2 (120 mg, 0.5 mmol in 20 ml of C2H5OH). In few minutes gel-like precipitate formed. Resulted yellowish mixture was left for crystallization in the dark. In 3 days all the precipitate transformed into small yellowish prism-shaped crystals. The crystals were filtered off, washed with cold С2H5OH and diethyl ether. Yield 169 mg (81%) Calculated for C26H24Ag2N8O11, %: C, 37.16; H, 2.88; N, 13.34. Found, %: C, 36.94; H, 2.5; N, 13.22.

2.2.3. Improved synthesis of [Ag(H2L3)2H2O] (III)

To solution of 42.5 mg (0.25 mmol) AgNO3 in 5 ml H2O acidified with 1 ml of 1 M HNO3, was added dropwise under constant stirring solution of H2L3 (120 mg, 0.5 mmol in 20 ml of C2H5OH). Resulted yellowish solution was left for crystallization. Formation of light-yellow crystal druses started in 1–2 h. The solution was left for 1 day in the dark. Then the crystals were filtered off, washed with cold С2H5OH and diethyl ether. Yield 138 mg (83%). Calculated for C26H24AgN7O8, %: C, 46.58; H, 3.61; N, 14.63. Found, %: C, 46.33; H, 3.3; N, 14.39.

2.3. Single crystal X-ray diffraction

Crystal structures of complexes I–III were determined by single crystal X-ray diffraction. The measurements were performed on Bruker SMART APEX2 diffractometer (Mo Kα radiation, graphite monochromator, λ = 0.71073 Å). The data were collected using ω- and φ-scans and integrated with SAINT program [26]. The obtained intensities were corrected for Lorentz, polarization and absorption using multi-scan technique in SADABS program [27]. All structures were solved by direct methods and refined by full matrix least-square method against F2 using SHELXTL program package [28]. Positions of the hydrogen atoms were located from difference Fourier maps and refined using a riding model with Uiso(H) = nUeq (n = 1.5 for methyl groups and water molecule and 1.2 for other H atoms).

Crystallographic data for all structures have been deposited in the Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre. CCDC 1524004-1524006 contain the supplementary crystallographic data for complex compounds I-III. Copies of the data can be obtained free of charge by an application to the Director, CCDC, 12 Union Road, Cambridge CB2 1EZ, UK (Fax: +44 1223 336 033; E-Mail: deposite@ccdc.cam.ac.uk; internet: http://www.ccdc.ac.uk). 3. Results and discussion

Complexes I-III were synthesized according to the scheme (Fig. 2). It should be noted that Ag+ is a relatively strong oxidant and heating of all mixtures should be avoided to prevent formation of Ag black. In case of ligands HL2,3 the formation of Ag black occurs even without heating and leads to very poor yields of resulting complex compounds (∼5%) [25]. So a little amount of diluted HNO3 was used to acidify the reaction mixture and prevent reduction of Ag+ because in acidic pH the Ag+ is much weaker oxidant. Such a little improvement leads to much higher yields (up to 83%). But excess of the acid should be avoided because it can cause hydrolysis of the hydrazones [29].

It should be noted that in all complexes I-III the ligand molecules are coordinated in molecular form without deprotonation of phenoxyl OH even if synthesis was done at neutral pH. The ligand HL1 derived from hydrazide of phenylacetic acid does not contain any additional donor atoms except the standart for acyl hydrazones of salicylaldehyde O,N,O-coordination site. Therefore in complex I the ligand is coordinated only via O,N,O atoms with formation of bisligand (M:L = 1:2) complex. The ligand HL2 derived from hydrazide of nicotinic acid contains except O,N,O site also additional β-N atom in acyl moiety. This β-N atom also participates in coordination with formation of polymeric complex II. The ligand HL3 derived from hydrazide of nicotinic acid differs from HL2 only by location of N-atom in γ-position of pyridine ring. But such a little difference leads to drastically different coordination behavior of the ligand and therefore the structure of complex III. In the complex III two molecules of the ligand are coordinated only via heterocyclic γ-NPy nitrogen atoms. Search in Cambridge Structural Database [21] showed that such type of ligands (N′-salicylidene-4-pyridinecarbohydrazide derivatives) bearing γ-N donor atom form complexes mainly with O,N,O or both O,N,O + γ-NPy coordination. But few complexes were found in which the ligands are coordinated in monodentate manner only via γ-NPy, CCDC identifiers: PUHNEM, RIHQEG, RUGXEX, VAKBOA, MIQXOA, HOKSAD [25], [30], [31], [32], [33], [34]. It should be noted that such monodentate coordination is not typical for N’-salicylidene-3-pyridinecarbohydrazide derivatives bearing β-N atom. Such a great difference in coordination behavior between N’-salicylidene-4-pyridinecarbohydrazide (HL3) and N′-salicylidene-3-pyridinecarbohydrazide (HL2) may be caused by strong negative mesomeric effect of γ-pyridine substitute in the HL3. This effect lowers electron density on donor atoms of salycylhydrazone moiety that makes coordination via the γ-NPy more favorable than via the O,N,O site. Moreover such monodentate coordination only via the γ-NPy atoms creates the least steric hindrance between two ligand molecules. This fact together with preferable linear structure for Ag+ coordination polyhedra favors formation of linear complex III. Changing of metal to ligand ratio from M:L = 1:2 to even M:L = 4:1 did not lead to formation of any complexes with O,N,O coordinated ligand, in all cases the complex III formed. According to the literature [32] formation of complex with the ligand coordination also via the O,N,O site can be driven by increasing of pH of reaction mixture. But in case of AgNO3 addition of any base to the reaction mixture immediately leads to quick reduction of Ag+ with formation of Ag black.

3.1. X-ray diffraction analysis

Complex I crystallizes in chiral space group C2 with two complex molecules per unit cell, named A and B (Fig. 3). The molecules are centrosymmetric, atoms Ag(1А) and Ag(1В) are located in inversion centers, both “A” and “B” complex molecules have identical structure. Selected bonds and angles are shown in Table 2 in comparison to the free HL1 ligand [23]. The coordination polyhedra of both Ag+ ions can be characterized as trigonally distorted octahedrons, formed by O(1A), O(2A) oxygen atoms of phenoxyle rings, N(1A), N(1B) atoms of azomethine groups and O(2A), O(2B) oxygen atoms of hydrazone moieties. Adjacent angles around Ag+ ions are in range of 67.7–168.1° and 68.2–162.2° for Ag(1A) and Ag(1B) respectively (Table 1).

The OH groups of phenoxyl moieties of both ligands are coordinated without deprotonation, therefore two outer-sphere nitrate counter ions are also present in crystal structure. The N(2)C(8), O(2)C(8) bond lengths (Table 2) confirm amide tautomeric form of coordinated ligand molecules [35]. Both ligand molecules are coordinated in a typical for N-acylhydrazones of salicylaldehyde O,N,O tridentate chelate manner with formation of five and six membered metallacycles. Five membered metallacycles Ag1/O2/C8/N2/N1 are almost planar with mean deviation from plane of 0.0443 Å (molecule A) and 0.0255 Å (molecule B). Whereas six membered ones Ag1/N1/C7/C6/C1/O1 are significantly deformed, mean deviation from plane is 0.1091 Å and 0.1982 Å for molecule “A” and “B” respectively. Dihedral angles between planes of 5-membered and 6-membered metallacycles are 8.6° and 12.0° for molecules A and B respectively. For comparison, a similar Cu2+ complex (CCDC identifier: UKEVEL01) with a related ligand forms much more planar metallacycles and therefore more regular coordination polyhedron (mean deviation from plane of 0.0149 and 0.0581 Å for 5- and 6-membered respectively, dihedral ring between mentioned planes is 6.6°) [18].

In crystal structure molecules of complex I form layers parallel to the (010) crystallographic plane due to intermolecular NH⋯O hydrogen bonds between hydrazone NH groups and outersphere nitrate ions and via numerous CH … π interaction between aromatic systems of ligand molecules (Fig. 4a, Table 3). The layers are bonded into stacks along [010] direction due to double OH⋯O hydrogen bonds between nondeprotonated OH groups of phenoxyl moieties and C=O groups of the nearest complex molecules (Fig. 4b).

The main feature of complex II is its polymeric structure with presence of two differently coordinated Ag+ ions (Fig. 5). Selected bonds and angles values for complex II are given in Table 4 in comparison to the free HL2 ligand [24]. Coordination environment of the Ag(1) atom is formed by O(1), N(1), O(2) atoms of the first tridentate chelate coordinated ligand molecule and γ-N(6)Py heterocyclic nitrogen atom of the second neighboring ligand molecule. The Ag(1) has O(1),N(1),O(2)+N(6)Py coordination environment. The Ag(2) atom has a similar coordination sphere but also provides an additional coordinational interaction with C(3) atom of phenoxyle ring of the third ligand molecule resulting in {O(3),N(4),O(4)+ γ-N(3)Py + C(3)} coordination environment. The N(3), N(6) and C(3) atoms act as bridges providing formation of 2D-polymeric layers (Fig. 6). The coordination polyhedra of Ag(1) and Ag(2) have irregular shapes with adjacent angles value in a range of 157.82–69.78° and 143.49–68.98° respectively. Such a strong distortion makes impossible their assignment to any typical coordination polyhedron shape.

The N(2)C(8), O(2)C(8), N(5)C(24), O(4)C(21) bond values (Table 4) and the presence of protons on O(1), O(3) atoms of phenoxyl moieties indicate coordination of both ligand molecules as amide tautomers in molecular form. Therefore two nitrate counter ions occupy an outer sphere of the coordination compound.

In crystal structure the complex II form 2-D coordination layers parallel to a (001) crystallographic plane. The layers contain cavities along [100] crystallographic axis occupied by one of nitrate ions which is disordered over two positions (Fig. 7). The layers are connected by branched network of OH⋯O, NH⋯O hydrogen bonds between nondeprotonated phenoxyl OH groups, NH of hydrazone moiety, water molecule and outersphere nitrate ion (Fig. 7, Table 5).

In the complex compound III two ligand molecules are coordinated in atypical for acyl hydrazones of salicylaldehyde monodentate manner only via γ-N atoms of isonicotinic moieties (Fig. 8). Tridentate O,N,O coordination sites of ligands do not take part in coordination. The main feature of complexes I-III is high coordination numbers of Ag+ ions and unusual forms of their coordination polyhedra. According to classical knowledge, linear, triangular and rarely tetrahedral coordination polyhedra are the most common for Ag+ ions [38]. But in case of polydentate complexonates and especially macrocyclic ligands the formation of complexes with Ag+ coordination number 5 [39], [40], 6 [41], [42] and even 9 [43] is also possible. In the present study the complexes provide coordination numbers of 6 (complex I), 4 + 5 (complex II) and 3 (complex III). But coordination polyhedra of all synthesized complex compounds cannot be directly related to any of typical octahedral, square-pyramidal, tetrahedral or triangular shapes. On the one hand such an uncommon deformation of coordination polyhedra can be caused by too large covalent radius of Ag+ (rAg = 1.45 Å). For comparison rCu = 1.32 Å [44]. Larger radius together with a lesser contribution of ion-dipole interaction in case of a singly charged Ag+ in comparison to double charged Cu2+ lead to higher values of Ag+ coordination bonds. According to the literature [35] the CuN bond length values in complexes with Shiff bases are in range of 1.954–2.01 Å, and the AgN bonds are about 2.4 Å. Because of such a long coordination bonds the Ag+ just cannot fit the O,N,O coordination site of the ligand in contrast to Cu2+ which forms much more regular shapes of coordination polyhedra in complexes with this type of ligands [18]. On the other hand, such irregular shapes of the coordination polyhedra can be a result of predominance of electrostatic interactions in the AgO bonding. Electrostatic interactions in contrast to orbital ones do not possess specific orientation and saturation that leads to higher coordination numbers and irregular polyhedra shapes. The best evidence of this phenomenon is complex III in which the Ag+ ion possess N,N,O coordination environment. In the complex III two “medium” N(6), N(3) donor atoms of isonicotine moieties form classical linear Ag+ polyhedron with NAgN angle of 172.08(9)°. But the Ag(1)O(8) bond (oxygen is “hard” Lewis acid) adopts almost orthogonal orientation to the NAgN line with formation of unusual T-shaped polyhedron (Fig. 8, Table 6).

Another interesting feature of structures of the complexes is a tendency of Ag+ ions to formation of coordination interactions with C atoms of aromatic rings (complex II, III). Such Ag+-π coordination interactions are very typical for Ag+, for example AgNO3 is noticeably soluble in benzene due to formation of [Ag(C6H6)3]+ complex [45].

3.2. Infrared spectroscopy

Infrared spectra of complexes I-III were obtained utilizing attenuated total internal reflection Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (ATR-FT-IR). Because all attempts to obtain IR spectra of the (I-III) in KBr pellets failed due to reaction between the KBr and the complexes with formation of AgBr.

The high-frequency region of spectra of all ligands and complexes I-III contains valence vibrations of OH and NH groups as wide overlapping bands (Fig. 10, Table 8). This fact clearly indicates that in all complexes the ligands are coordinated in nondeprotonated molecular form. The spectrum of complex II contains weak and very wide absorption band (AB) at 3400 cm−1 which correspond to ν(OH) of cocrystallized water molecules. In contrast to this the spectrum of complex III contains relatively narrow AB of water ν(OH) at 3409 cm−1 that clearly distinguishes the coordinated water molecule.

In a low-frequency region of IR spectra of complexes I-III the most characteristic are valence vibrations of C=O group (amide I) which undergo significant shifts upon complex formation. Valence vibrations of the conjugated azomethine C=N (amide II) group usually lie about 1620–1580 cm−1 and cannot be clearly identified due to their possible overlapping with deformation vibrations δ(NH) and skeletal vibrations of 1,3-substituted aromatic rings. In spectra of ligands HL1 and HL3 the ν(C=O) are present as a single intensive bands (Fig. 10, Table 8). But in the ligand HL2 there are two bands at 1646 and 1659 cm−1 which both most likely correspond to ν(C=O). It was shown in literature that 1H NMR spectrum of ligand HL2 contains a double set of signals due to equilibrium mixture of two isomers in solution [23]. But according to the present data, two ν(C=O) bands in the IR spectrum may evidence that HL2 exist as two isomers even in solid state. However in the corresponding complex II the ligand coordinated as single isomer which is confirmed by the presence of only one clear ν(C=O) absorption band. In complexes I and II the ν(C=O) bands are shifted to a low-frequency region (Table 8, Fig. 10). In contrast to this the ν(C=O) absorption band in complex III undergo significant high-frequency shift for +19 cm−1 even despite the fact that this group is not involved in coordination. But the C=O group is conjugated with isonicotynic moiety of ligand HL3. So lowering of electron density on the β-N atom due to coordination pulls off electron density from the C=O group and leads to high frequency shift of ν(C=O) absorption band in the complex III. However according to obtained X-ray diffraction data, formation of the complexes does not cause any significant influence at bond lengths in coordinated ligand molecules (Table 2, Table 4, Table 6).

The ABs in a region of 1620–1450 cm−1 corresponds to skeletal vibrations of aromatic rings. The very wide strong absorption band in a region of 1400–1200 cm−1 with maximum of about 1300 cm−1 in spectra of all the complexes corresponds to valence vibrations of outersphere NO3− ions [24], [46].

Thermal stability of the complexes I-III was studied using TG/DTG/DTA techniques. The TG/DTG/DTA curves of complexes I-III are displayed in Figs. S1–S3 of the supplementary data. The main decomposition data are tabulated in Table 9.

The total decomposition of the complexes can be divided into two main steps: lattice or coordination water loss (only for complexes II and III) and destruction of the ligand part. According to crystal structure analysis, the complex II contains cocrystallized water molecules while complex III contains coordinated ones. However TG curves of both complexes reveal almost equal temperature of water release for both complexes II and III. This might indicate that coordinated water molecules in complex III are weakly bonded to Ag+ ions. The second main step of decomposition starts in a range of 150–203° that is quite low (Table 9). Such a low thermal stability of complexes I-III can be explained by the presence of Ag+ and NO3− ions in complexes which act as oxidizers. Like gunpowder, mixtures of oxidizers and reductants (ligands) are not very stable while heated. After heating up to 1000 °C only Ag metal remained in a solid residue as very small shiny metal balls.

As we can see the chemical shifts in resulting 1H NMR spectra of complexes I-III in DMSO-d6 are almost identical with 1H NMR spectra of free ligands. The maximum shift is observed for C1013H protons of pyridine ring of complex III. Also the NMR spectra of the complexes showed the same ratio of isomers in solution as the free ligands. These facts clearly evidence that use of high donor solvents such as DMSO-d6 leads to complete solvolysis of the complexes with total substitution of ligands for solvent molecules. However, the presence of weak shifts of pyridine protons in complexes II and III together with significant broadening of C12H, C13H (II) and especially C1112H (III) signals (Fig. 11) may indicate participation of pyridine nitrogens in fast ligand exchange with Ag+ in solution.

Almost the same results were obtained by using CD3CN as solvent, except complex II which is insoluble in CD3CN.

3.5. UV–vis spectroscopy

Because of complete solvolysis of complexes I-III in DMSO and CH3CN we tried to use less donor solvents to study the complexes in solution. Unfortunately the complexes are insoluble in common solvents such as ethanol, water or chloroform but slowly dissolve in warm methanol that allowed us to use UV–Vis spectroscopy. UV–Vis spectra of methanol solutions of ligands HL1−3 were obtained in 5·10−4 M concentrations. The spectra of complex compounds were measured in concentrations 2.5·10−4 M for bisligand complexes I, III and 5·10−4 M for M:L = 1:1 complex II (Fig. 12). Different complex concentrations are needed to make resulting molar concentration of ligands in solution equal for all experiments.

There are no d-d transitions for d10 electronic configuration of Ag+ but it might be possible to observe complex formation by shift of absorption bands corresponding to π-π*, p-π*, n-π* transitions in the ligands molecules. However analysis of obtained UV–Vis spectra showed absolute identity of the spectra of the complex compounds and corresponding ligands with zero shifts of absorption bands. This fact indicates total dissociation of the complexes upon dissolution in CH3OH. The only difference between UV–Vis spectra of the ligands and their complexes is stronger absorbance in a high-frequency region of spectra of the complexes due to absorbance of the NO3− ion (Fig. 12).

The data obtained by 1H NMR and UV–Vis spectroscopy showed that complexes I-III are highly unstable in solutions and readily undergo substitution of ligands for solvent molecules. Unfortunately the complexes are insoluble in noncoordinating solvents such as CCl4, CHCl3 etc.

4. Conclusions

Three coordination compounds I-III involving N-acylhydrazones of salicylaldehyde derivatives were synthesized and characterized by single-crystal X-ray diffraction and IR spectroscopy. Compounds I and II are the first structurally characterized Ag+ complexes involving O,N,O-coordinated N-acylhydrazones of salicylaldehyde. The compound III is already known [25]. But in the present study a slightly improved synthesis method of complex compounds was proposed. Such a little improvement allowed to avoid formation of Ag black and led to much higher yields (83% for complex III in comparison to ∼5% from literature [25]). In all complexes I-III the ligand molecules are coordinated in molecular amide tautomeric form without deprotonation of phenoxyl OH even if synthesis was done at neutral pH.

It was found that the coordination mode of ligands strongly depends on the presence of additional donor atoms in the ligands molecules:

–

Ligand HL1 derived from hydrazide of phenylacetic acid does not contain any additional donor atoms and provides classical tridentate chelate O,N,O coordination (complex I).

–

Ligand HL2 derived from hydrazide of nicotinic acid contains one additional nitrogen donor atom in β-position of the acyl moiety and provides mixed (O,N,O + β-N) coordination mode with formation of polymeric complex II.

–

Ligand HL3 bearing isonicotine moiety (γ-N additional donor atom) coordinates only via heterocyclic γ-N atom of acyl moiety. The standart O,N,O coordination site of the ligand is not involved in coordination to Ag+ (complex III).

All complexes I-III provide high coordination numbers of Ag+ and irregular shapes of polyhedra which are not consistent with classical concepts of Ag+ coordination chemistry.

Complexes I-III possess low thermal stability that is caused by the presence of relatively strong Ag+ and NO3− oxidizers in the complex molecules.

It should be noted that all complexes are unstable in DMSO and acetonitrile solution and readily undergo solvolysis upon disolution that is confirmed by 1H NMR spectroscopy. Even in much less donor solvent CH3OH complexes undergo complete solvolysis that is confirmed by UV–Vis spectroscopy.

  • Tetranuclear Zn(II) complexes with ditopic picolinohydrazone ligands: Synthesis, crystal structure, spectroscopic studies, and Hirschfeld surface analysis

    2022, Journal of Molecular Structure

    Two N- and O-donor hydrazone ligands, (E)-N'-(1-(4‑hydroxy-6-methyl-2-oxo-2H-pyran-3-yl)ethylidene)picolinohydrazide (H2L1) and (E)-N'-(4-oxo-4-phenylbutan-2-ylidene)picolinohydrazide (H2L2), were synthesized by the reaction of picolinohydrazide and dehydroacetic acid or benzolyacetone in methanol solvent. The ligands were characterized by spectroscopic methods and were employed in preparing Zn(II) complexes. Two new tetranuclear Zn(II) complexes, [Zn4(L)2(µ-OAc)4(CH3OH)2] (1) and [Zn4(L2)2(µ-OAc)4(CH3OH)2] (2), were synthesized by the reaction of Zn(OAc)2·H2O with H2L1 and H2L2 in methanol, respectively. These complexes were also characterized by elemental analysis, spectroscopic methods, and single crystal X-ray analysis. Structural studies indicated both 1 and 2 are acetato-bridged tetranuclear Zn(II) complexes and in both of them, the picolinohydrazone ligands are coordinated to the metal ions as both bridging and chelating ligand to fill the coordination environments of Zn(II) ions. In compound 1 four Zn(II) ions have an almost linear arrangement, while in compound 2 they show square arrangement. This matter can be attributed to the steric and electronic difference of the beta-diketone moiety in the structure of ligand. Intermolecular interactions in compounds 1 and 2 were investigated by Hirschfeld surface analysis.

  • Molecular structure and catalytic activity of Fe(III) coordination compound with ONO-donor hydrazone ligand in the oxidation of cyclooctene by H<inf>2</inf>O<inf>2</inf>

    2022, Journal of Molecular Structure

    A new mononuclear Fe(III) hydrazone Schiff base coordination compound, [Fe(HL)Cl2]·CH3OH (1), was synthesized and characterized by spectroscopic methods and elemental analyses. The compound was obtained by the reactions of FeCl3·6H2O and ONO-donor hydrazone Schiff base ligand (H2L = (E)-3-hydroxy-N'-((Z)-4-hydroxy-4-phenylbut-3-en-2-ylidene)-2-naphthohydrazide) in methanol. The structure of compound 1 was also determined by single crystal X-ray diffraction studies. The catalytic activity of compound 1 was investigated in oxidation of cyclooctene by hydrogen peroxide as a green oxidant and the effects of some influencing parameters like temperature, solvent and the ratio of the reagents were investigated. The results of studies indicated these parameters have considerable effect on the catalytic activity of compound 1. The interaction of compound 1 and H2O2 was studied by UV-Vis spectroscopy and the results showed new catalytically active species form by their interactions which act as intermediates in oxidation process of substrate.

  • Synthesis, spectroscopic characterization (FT-IR, PL), DFT calculations and antibacterial activity of silver(I) nitrate complex with nicotinaldehyde

    2021, Inorganic Chemistry Communications

    The [Ag(3-Py-CHO)2NO3] complex, where (3-Py-CHO); Nicotinaldehyde (3-pyridinecarboxaldehyde), was synthesized and characterized by means of elemental analysis, various spectroscopic techniques (FT-IR, 1H NMR, PL) and DFT calculations. Besides, the silver metal, Ag(I), was coordinated with two organic ligand molecules in the complex. The optimized molecular geometry, vibrational frequencies, electronic properties, such as frontier molecular orbitals energies as well as the molecular electrostatic potential (MEP) were employed by utilizing the DFT theory. Calculated results and experimental data for the IR, 1H NMR and PL spectra indicated a solid inter-related consistency. Antimicrobial studies have been conducted with the ligand and silver(I) complex against various gram +ve, gram −ve bacterial species. Antibacterial activity of the ligand and its Ag(I) complex were tested againsts elected bacteria by minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) and discdiffusion method. The Ag(I) complex reflected good activity in the range of 15.6–125 μg /mL against all bacteria tested, while the ligand how better activity in the range of 15.6–62.5 μg/ mL as MIC values. The complex indicated zone of inhibition values in the range of 10–30 mm (100 µg /mL). The results pointed out that the Ag(I) complex and ligand were more effective against bacteria.

  • New Cu(II) and VO(II)-O,N,O-aroylhydrazone complexes: Biological evaluation, catalytic performance, ctDNA interaction, DFT, pharmacophore, and docking simulation

    2021, Journal of Molecular Liquids

    Polar mononuclear Cu(II) (CuSSL·H2O) and VO(II) (VOSSL·0.5H2O) complexes were prepared employing 4-hydroxy-3-((2-(4-methoxybenzoyl)hydrazineylidene)methyl)benzene sodium sulfonate ligand (H2SSL) under green conditions. The ligand and its complexes were characterized by various spectroscopic (1H & 13C NMR, IR, UV–Visible, and Mass) and analytical tools (elemental analysis (EA), thermogravimetric analysis (TGA), conductivity, and magnetic moments). The ionic sodium sulfonate group enhanced the catalytic potential of the two complexes, as green catalysts, in the epoxidation of 1,2-cyclohexene using H2O2 under homogeneous reaction conditions. Interestingly, complete epoxidation (100% conversion) of 1,2-cyclohexene was observed using water as an environmentally friendly solvent. The high valent ion (V4+) in the VO(II)-catalyst offered better catalytic efficiency than that of the low valent ion (Cu2+) in the Cu(II) catalyst. The new compounds' biological activity was assessed using different microbes (Staphylococcus aureus, Serratia Marcescence, Escherichia coli, Candida albicans, Aspergillus flavus, and Trichophyton rubrum), as well as various cancer cell lines (HepG2, MCF-7, and HCT-116). Additionally, their antioxidant potential was also examined by DPPH radical scavenging and SOD assays. Moreover, the activity of the new reagents was assessed towards ctDNA-interaction by spectrophotometry and viscosity measurements. Both M(II)-complexes offered higher antimicrobial, anticancer, and antioxidant potential compared to that of their free ligand. Conformational and in-silico studies were carried out to support the newly synthesized complexes' catalytic and biological properties.

  • Synthesis and characterization for pharmaceutical models from Co(II), Ni(II) and Cu(II)-thiophene complexes; apoptosis, various theoretical studies and pharmacophore modeling

    2021, Journal of Molecular Liquids

    New Co(II), Ni(II) and Cu(II) complexes were prepared from thiophene derivative and then characterized to elucidate their chemical formulae. IR-spectral data suggested a monobasic tridentate binding mode for the ligand towards the metal ions within mono-nuclear complexes. Ligand filed transitions as well as magnetic susceptibility, orient strongly for square-planer geometry with Ni(II) and Cu(II) complexes, while octahedral geometry for Co(II) complex. Mass spectroscopy and TGA were performed for complexes to assess on their molecular formulae and the molecular ion peak is attributing to dehydrating complex (M+-nH2O). TEM, EDX and XRD were carried out to indicate morphology, crystallinity and chemical composition of tested complexes. The crystal data estimated, reflect nanometer sizes of studied complexes. DFT method was utilized to obtain optimized structures under 6-31G and LANL2DZ basis sets. Hirshfeld surface properties were estimated for 3D crystal models of complexes, to put view about the contact strength within crystal packing. 2D-fingerprint plots for elemental contribution, clarify the effective contribution of O and H atoms in surface contact between crystals. Cu (II) complex showed greatest potent cytotoxic profile against MCF-7, HepG2 and PC-3 carcinoma cell lines, by IC50s 2.2, 2.6 and 2.1 μg, respectively. High killing rate for tumor cells was observed with an early apoptotic pathway under treatment with all compounds. Also, Cu(II) complex stimulates necrosis killing effect on prostate (PC-3) and breast (MCF-7) cancer cells. Ligand-based pharmacophore methodology, was performed to indicate the most suitable contact sites in compounds towards 1z8l & 3rcd proteins. The search hits several compounds reach 3,732,214 hits and a closer 3D-fingerprint drug model was obtained. MOE docking was performed for most compounds to explain all interaction features through such simulation process. Best docking scores were recorded with HL-3rcd, Co(II)complex-1z8l, Co(II) complex-3rcd and Cu(II) complex-3rcd by values of −60,628, −6.1447, −6.055 and −6.0626, respectively. Amino acid residues that contributing in allosteric binding were clearly categorized. Finally in-silico approach confirms the superiority of Co(II)-L, Cu(II)-L and free thiophene derivative in controlling human cancer cells, which agree with in vitro results.

  • Biocompatibility and mechanical properties evaluation of chitosan films containing an N-acylhydrazonic derivative

    2020, European Journal of Pharmaceutical Sciences

    The N-acylhydrazone subunit is considered a privileged structure in medicinal chemistry for its importance in pharmaceutical research. Also, alternative methods to deliver these molecules have a great pharmaceutical interest. Therefore, the objective of this work was to encapsulate JR19, an N-acyl hydrazone subunit, into chitosan films and evaluate several properties relevant for transdermal delivery, including biocompatibility using in vitro tests. CHI + JR19 film demonstrates greater strength, flexibility, water absorption capacity, low contact angle and higher surface roughness when compared to CHI. Agar diffusion and 3-(4,5-dimethyl)-2,5-diphenyl tetrazolium bromide (MTT) assay show the absence of cytotoxicity and the higher cell viability for CHI + JR19 films. Therefore, the addition of JR19 in the system positively influenced mechanical properties and granted better compatibility with biological environments, showing the potential to treat skin inflammation.
