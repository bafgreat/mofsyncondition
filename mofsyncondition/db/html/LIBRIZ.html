Abstract

Six novel 2D metal–quinolone complexes, namely [Cd(cfH)(bpdc)]H2O (1), [M(norfH)(bpdc)]H2O (M=Cd (2) and Mn (3)), [Mn2(cfH)(odpa)(H2O)3]0.5H2O (4), [Co2(norfH)(bpta)(μ2-H2O)(H2O)2]H2O (5) and [Co3(saraH)2(Hbpta)2(H2O)4]9H2O (6) (cfH=ciprofloxacin, norfH=norfloxacin, saraH=sarafloxacin, bpdc=4,4′-biphenyldicarboxylate, odpa=4,4′-oxydiphthalate, bpta=3,3′,4,4′-biphenyltetracarboxylate) have been synthesized and characterized. Compounds 1–3 consist of 2D arm-shaped layers based on the 1D {M(COO)}nn+ chains. Compounds 4 and 5 display 2D structures based on tetranuclear manganese or cobalt clusters with (3,6)-connected kgd topology. Compound 6 exhibits a 2D bilayer structure, which represents the first example of metal–quinolone complexes with 2D bilayer structure. By inspection of the structures of 1–6, it is believed that the long aromatic polycarboxylate ligands are important for the formation of 2D metal–quinolone complexes. The magnetic properties of compounds 3–6 was studied, indicating the existence of antiferromagnetic interactions. Furthermore, the luminescent properties of compounds 1–2 are discussed.

1. Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) are a burgeoning field in the last two decades, not only stems from their tremendous potential applications in areas such as catalysis, molecular adsorption, magnetism, nonlinear optics, and molecular sensing, but also from their novel topologies and intriguing structural diversities [1], [2], [3]. Aided by the rapid growth of network-based crystal engineering, a large number of attractive networks with various structural motifs have been designed and extensively discussed in comprehensive reviews by Yaghi, Kitagawa, Rao, Chen, and their co-workers [4]. On the other hand, many organic drugs, which possess modified pharmacological and toxicological properties administered in the form of metallic complexes [5], have the potential to act as ligands and the resulting metal–drug complexes are particularly important both in coordination chemistry and biochemistry [6], [7], [8], [9], [10], however, the study of metal–drug complexes is still in its early stages, thus representing a great challenge in current synthetic chemistry and coordination chemistry.

Quinolones represent a large group of synthetic antibacterial agents widely used in clinical practice and are characterized by a broad-spectrum antibacterial activity. Ciprofloxacin (cfH), norfloxacin (norfH) and sarafloxacin (saraH) (Schemes 1 and S1) are typical members of this group and are used for the treatment of certain diseases [7], [8], [9], [11], [12]. The mechanisms of action of the quinolone antibacterial drugs are either their inhibition of the supercoiling of DNA catalyzed by the enzyme DNA gyrase or their interaction with the DNA molecule via a metal complex intermediate [6], [7], [8], [9]. Recently new theoretical–experimental studies on the activity of quinolones and their metal complexes have supported the hypothesis that the mechanism of action of quinolones could be mediated by a metal ion [7], [9], [10]. It is obvious that the metal ions may play a very important role in the mechanism of action of the quinolone antibacterial agents. However, the structurally characterized metal–quinolone complexes, especially for high-dimensional extended complexes [6], [8], to our knowledge, are still rare, as evidenced in a review by Iztok Turel [7]. Only a few two-dimensional (2D) metal–quinolone complexes have been reported up to now [6], [8]. The possible reason is that the steric demands of the bulky quinolone ligands will increase the steric hindrance at the metal center, which may restrain spatial extension of the skeleton and thus prevent the formation of a 2D structure. Therefore, the construction of 2D metal–quinolone complexes is still a great challenge for synthetic chemists, and much work is still necessary to enrich and develop this branch.

Ongoing research in our laboratory has been directed toward the design and construction of high-dimensional extended metal–quinolone complexes [8] with the aim of enriching the structural chemistry of metal–quinolone complexes and preparing new metal–drug complexes with particular functions. As an extension of our previous work [8], for our synthetic strategy we chose the long aromatic polycarboxylates as bridging ligands in an attempt to link metal–quinolone fragments into 2D metal–quinolone complexes. In our experiment, the long aromatic polycarboxylates and quinolones are simultaneously introduced based on the following considerations: (i) on the one hand, multidentate carboxylates are known to be essential in chelating metal ions and locking their position into metal clusters [4a], and it should be feasible to link discrete clusters by polycarboxylate bridges to give an extended network containing highly connected nodes. On the other hand, the use of carboxylate-bridged metal clusters as second building units (SBUs) to build 2D structures is relatively mature [13], [14]. Therefore, the topologies of resulting structures can be predicted and designed [15]. (ii) Coordination polymers constructed from the mixed quinolones and aromatic polycarboxylate ligands are still very rare, which may be attributed, at least in part, to the fact that two carboxyl-containing ligands would yield more negative charges and make the charge balance difficult [8], [8]. Because poor solubility of the ligands, and the mixing of metal salt and quinolone solution usually results in a precipitation, making it difficult to grow single crystals of complexes, we employed hydrothermal technique to put the designed strategy in practice and successfully synthesized a series of novel 2D metal–quinolone complexes, namely [Cd(cfH)(bpdc)]H2O (1), [M(norfH)(bpdc)]H2O (M=Cd (2) and Mn (3)), [Mn2(cfH)(odpa)(H2O)3]0.5H2O (4), [Co2(norfH)(bpta)(μ2-H2O)(H2O)2]H2O (5) and [Co3(saraH)2(Hbpta)2(H2O)4]9H2O (6) (bpdc=4,4′-biphenyldicarboxylate, odpa=4,4′-oxydiphthalate, bpta=3,3′,4,4′-biphenyltetracarboxylate). The syntheses, crystal structures and physical properties of these compounds will be represented and discussed in this paper. This work may provide new structural information that will aid in understanding the mechanisms of action of the quinolone antibacterial agents.

2. Experimental section

2.1. Materials and methods

All chemicals were commercially purchased and used without further purification. The elemental analyses (C, H and N) were carried out with a Perkin-Elmer 2400 CHN Elemental Analyzer. Cd, Mn and Co were determined by a tps-7000 Plasma-Spec(I) inductively coupled plasma atomic emission spectrometer (ICP-AES). IR spectra were recorded in the range 400–4000 cm−1 on a Bio-Rad FTS-185 FT/IR Spectrophotometer using KBr pellets. TG analyses were performed on a NETZSCH STA 449C instrument in flowing N2 with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. Excitation and emission spectra were performed on an F-4500 fluorescence spectrophotometer equipped with a 150 W xenon lamp as the excitation source. XRPD were recorded on a XD-3 diffractometer using CuKα (λ=1.5406 Å) radiation at room temperature. Variable-temperature magnetic susceptibility data were obtained on a SQUID magnetometer (Quantum Design, MPMS-7) in the temperature range of 2–300 K with an applied field of 1 kOe.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Description of crystal structures

3.1.1. Crystal structures of 1–3

Compounds 2 (Fig. S1) and 3 (Fig. S2) are isostructural, and the structures of 1 and 2 are close to being isostructural, so only the structure of 1 will be discussed in detail. In the crystal structure of 1, the asymmetric unit contains one Cd atom, one cfH ligand, one bpdc ligand, and one lattice H2O molecule (Fig. 1). Each Cd atom is coordinated by two oxygen atoms (Cd–O 2.2221(17)–2.2339(15) Å) from one cfH ligand and four oxygen atoms (Cd–O 2.2569(19)–2.5367(19) Å) from three bpdc ligands, showing a distorted octahedron geometry. The bpdc ligand adopts a tetradentate chelating and bridging coordination mode; one carboxylate group chelates with one CdII ion bidentately, while the other adopts a bidentate bridging coordination mode connecting two other metal ions (Scheme S3a). Based on this connection mode, the CdII ions are bridged by μ2-carboxylate groups of bpdc ligands to generate a 1D infinite {Cd(COO)}nn+ chain along the c axis and adjacent 1D chains are further connected by the biphenyl groups of bpdc ligands to give rise to a 2D framework (Fig. 2). The cfH groups, grafted on the 2D network, resemble open arms that protrude from both sides of the sheet. From the topological point of view, this 2D network can be regarded as a three-connected net with (63) topology, in which the CdII ions and bpdc ligands act as three-connected nodes, respectively (Fig. S3). In the packing arrangement of 1, the adjacent 2D layers are extended by strong hydrogen bonding interactions (N1⋯O1 2.778 Å, N1 ⋯OW1 2.75 Å, OW1⋯O6 2.788 Å and OW1⋯O5 2.761 Å) and aromatic ππ stacking interactions of cfH groups (separation 3.3–3.5 Å) into a 3D supramolecular network (Fig. 2b).

3.1.2. Crystal structure of 4

When rigid aromatic dicarboxylate (bpdc) was replaced by V-shaped flexible tetracarboxylate (odpa), structurally different 2D arm-shaped layers were formed in 4. There are two crystallographically independent Mn atoms in the asymmetric unit of 4 (Fig. 3). Both Mn atoms (Mn1 and Mn2) are six-coordinate and exhibit distorted octahedron geometries. The Mn1 center is coordinated by two oxygen atoms (Mn–O 2.105(4)–2.196(3) Å) from one cfH ligand, three oxygen atoms (Mn–O 2.078(3)–2.198(3) Å) from three carboxylate groups of three odpa ligands, and one aqua ligand (Mn–OW 2.208(3) Å). Whereas the Mn2 center is coordinated by four oxygen atoms (Mn–O 2.134(4)–2.225(3) Å) from four carboxylate groups of three odpa ligands, and two aqua ligands (Mn–OW 2.093(3)–2.346(4) Å). The four carboxylate groups of the odpa ligand exhibit three kinds of coordination modes with the Mn atoms, as shown in Scheme S3b. That is, two of them adopt a monodentate mode, the third carboxylate group displays a bidentate bridging coordination mode, while the last carboxylate group exhibits tridentate chelating/bridging mode coordination mode connecting with three metal ions. Based on these connection modes, four Mn atoms are bridged by a pair of odpa μ2-carboxylate ends and a pair of odpa μ3-carboxylate ends to give a tetranuclear manganese unit (Fig. S4a) that is further extended by odpa ligands into a 2D layer (Fig. 4a). Similar to compound 1, the bulky cfH groups, acting as one-end coordinated ligands, graft at two sides of the 2D layer (Fig. 4a). To understand the 2D structure of 4, the network can be simplified as a (3,6)-connected 2D kgd (kagome dual) net (also called CdCl2 or CdI2 net) with (43)2(46·66·83) topology (Fig. 4b). In this simplification, the odpa ligands act as the three-connected nodes and the tetranuclear manganese clusters function as the six-connected nodes (Fig. S5); the ratio of the number of three-connected to six-connected nodes is 2:1. In the packing arrangement of 4, the adjacent 2D layers are further extended by means of NH⋯O, OH⋯O and CH⋯O hydrogen bonds (N1⋯O11 2.778 Å, OW3⋯O1 2.614 Å and C10···OW3 3.271 Å) into a 3D supramolecular network (Fig. S6).

3.1.3. Crystal structure of 5

When flexible tetracarboxylate (odpa) was replaced by rigid tetracarboxylate (bpta), structurally analogical 2D arm-shaped layer was formed in 5. There are two crystallographically independent Co atoms in the asymmetric unit of 5 (Fig. 5). Both Co atoms (Co1 and Co2) are six-coordinate and exhibit slightly distorted octahedron geometries. The Co1 center is coordinated by one oxygen atoms (Co–O 2.057(2) Å) from one norfH ligand, two oxygen atoms (Co–O 2×2.083(2) Å) from two carboxylate groups of two bpta ligands, one bridging water molecule (Co–OW2 2.152(3) Å), and two aqua ligands (Co–OW 2.027(3)–2.115 Å). Whereas the Co2 center is coordinated by three oxygen atoms (Co–O 2.012(2)–2.140(2) Å) from two norfH ligands, two oxygen atoms (Co–O 2.045(2)–2.092(2) Å) from two carboxylate groups of two bpta ligands, and one bridging water molecule (Co–OW2 2.142(3) Å). The norfH ligand in 5 acts as a tetradentate ligand connecting three Co atoms; the carboxylate group adopts a tridentate bridging mode, while the carbonyl exhibits monodentate mode (Scheme S2c). To our knowledge, this coordination mode of norfH ligand has not been observed in other metal–quinolone complexes. Four carboxylate groups of bpta ligand exhibit three kinds of coordination modes, that is, the bidentate bridging, noncoordinated and monodentate modes (Scheme S3c). Based on these connection modes, four Co atoms are connected by two μ3-carboxylate groups of norfH ligands to form a tetranuclear cobalt core, which is further joined by two carbonyl oxygen atoms, two bridging oxygen atoms from two bpta ligands, two bridging water molecules, four monodentated oxygen atoms from four bpta ligands, and four aqua ligands to generate a tetrametallic cluster [Co4(μ3-CO2)2(Ocarbonyl)2(μ2-O)2(μ2-OH2)2(O)4(H2O)4] as a structurally directing secondary building unit (SBU) (Fig. S4b), which is different from the tetranuclear manganese unit in 4. Each SBU is further connected by six bpta ligands to generate an unusual 2D layer framework (Fig. 6a). To understand the 2D structure of 5, the network can be simplified as a (3,6)-connected 2D kgd net with (43)2(46·66·83) topology (Fig. 6b). Similar in 4, in this simplification, the bpta ligands act as the three-connected nodes and the tetranuclear cobalt clusters function as the six-connected nodes. In the packing arrangement of 5, the adjacent 2D layers are further extended by means of a hydrogen bonding involving the protonated nitrogen atom (N1) and a uncoordinated carboxyl oxygen atom (O10) from dangling bpta ligand (N1⋯O10 2.752 Å) into a 3D supramolecular network (Fig. S7b). Interestingly, when these hydrogen bonds are taken into account, the resulting structure displays a (4,8)-connected 3D net symbolized as (45·6)2(412·612·84) (Fig. S7c), in which the bpta ligands act as the four-connected nodes and the tetranuclear cobalt clusters function as the eight-connected nodes (Fig. S7a).

3.1.4. Crystal structure of 6

When norfH was replaced by saraH ligand, structurally different 2D bilayer frame was formed in 6. In the crystal structure of 6, the asymmetric unit contains one and a half Co atoms (Fig. 7). The Co1 center is coordinated in a ratherish distorted octahedron geometry to four oxygen atoms (Co–O 2.024(3)–2.081(3) Å) from two saraH ligands, and to two oxygen atom (Co–O 2×2.165(3) Å) from two terminal water molecules. Whereas the Co2 center is ligated by four oxygen atoms (Co–O 2.006(4)–2.250(3) Å) from two bpta ligands, one carboxylate oxygen atoms (Co–O 2.003(3) Å) from one saraH ligands, and one aqua ligand (Co–OW 2.108(4) Å) to complete a distorted octahedron geometry. The saraH ligand in 6 acts as a tridentate bridging ligand connecting two Co atoms; the carboxylate group adopts a bidentate bridging mode, while the carbonyl exhibits monodentate mode (Scheme S2d). To our knowledge, this coordination mode of saraH ligand has not been observed in other metal–quinolone complexes. The bpta ligand acts as a tetradentate bridging ligand; one carboxylate group adopts a bidentate chelating mode, one carboxylate group is noncoordinated, while the remaining two adopt a monodentate coordination mode (Scheme S3d). Based on this connection mode, the Co2 centers are connected by saraH and bpta ligands to form a [Co(saraH)(Hbpta)] unit, which is extended by bridging Co1 atoms into a 2D bilayer (Fig. 8). In contrast to the variety of structures, such as rectangular grids, brick walls, herringbones, ladders, rings, boxes, diamondoids, and honeycombs, bilayer motif is a new motif and relatively scarce [13], [17]. Moreover, the metal–quinolone complex with 2D bilayer structure, to the best of our knowledge, has not been reported yet. In the packing arrangement of 6, the adjacent 2D bilayers are further extended by aromatic ππ stacking interactions of 4-fluorophenyl groups of saraH ligand (separation 3.4–3.5 Å) into a 3D supramolecular network (Fig. S8).

Through comparison of the structures of 1–6, it was found that the steric geometries of the long aromatic polycarboxylates and quinolones may have a very significant effect on the formation and dimension of the resulting structure. As demonstrated by a comparison of 1–3 with 4–5, the difference of their structures are mainly caused by using different types of polycarboxylates ligands (bpdc, odpa and bpta). Looking at the entire study, we can sense that the polycarboxylate ligands can take different coordination modes and conformation geometries to meet the coordination nature of the metal cations, thus having a great influence on the formation of the final structure. Furthermore, by inspection of the structures of 1–6, it is believed that the long aromatic polycarboxylate ligands are important for the construction of 2D metal–quinolone complexes. Due to the steric hindrance of the bulky quinolone ligands, the distance between the carboxylic groups and the length of polycarboxylate ligands are essential for the generation of high dimensional structure. On the other hand, the comparison of compounds 5 with 6 also shows that the substituting groups in the 1-position of quinolones (Scheme 1) may play a role in determining the final structure of metal–quinolone complex, although the minor structural change of quinolones have no effect on the framework formation of compounds 1–2. This observation indicates that the structures of metal–quinolone complexes may be tuned systematically by simple modification of the substituting groups of quinolones, and thus the exploitation of such species can lead to the possibility of predicting the structure of the metal–quinolone complex obtained.

In addition, the reaction pH value also plays a crucial role in tuning the dimension of the metal–quinolone complexes, as evidenced by the comparison of compounds 1–6 with four 3D metal–quinolone complexes reported by us very recently [8c,d]. According to the review by Turel [7], the quinolone molecules with the piperazinyl group can exist in four possible forms: an acidic cation QH2+, a neutral nonionized species QH, an intermediate zwitterion QH± and a basic anion Q−, depending on the pH of the solution. In a neutral or weakly acidic solution, the N atom of piperazinyl group is protonated and fails to coordinate to the metal ion, and therefore the bulky quinolone can act as a one-end coordinated ligand, which plays a “passive” role by occupying coordination sites on the metal centers and providing steric constraints, thus reducing the dimension of the metal–quinolone complex formed (compounds 1–6) [8b,e,f,g]. While in a basic solution, the piperazinyl group and the carboxyl group both connect the metal centers and thus the quinolone could equally well be used as a long bridging ligand, which may provide great possibilities for the formation of 3D metal–quinolone complexes [8c,d].

3.2. Thermal stability analyses

In order to examine the thermal stability of these compounds, thermal gravimetric (TG) analyses were carried out. The TG curve of 1 exhibits three weight loss stages (Fig. S9a). The first weight loss is 2.82% in the temperature range of 32–205 °C, which corresponds to the loss of the non-coordinated water molecule (calcd. 2.56%). The second weight loss 47.34% from 280 to 590 °C assigns to the decomposition of cfH ligands (calcd. 47.30%). The last weight loss starts from 590 °C and continues up to 1000 °C, which arises from the release bpdc ligand. The total weight loss of 73.39% is much less than the calculated value of 81.71% if the final product is assumed to be CdO; hence, the decomposing process is not complete due to the use of N2 atmosphere. In the DSC curve of 1, the endothermic peak at 161 °C corresponds to the release of lattice water molecules. The exothermic peaks at 323, 357 and 540 °C are related to the release of cfH ligands; and 670 and 965 °C are attributed to the decomposition of the bpdc ligands. The TG curves of compounds 2 (Fig. S9b) and 3 (Fig. S9c) exhibit similar weight loss stages to 1. For 2, the total weight loss (74.21%) is much less than the calculated value (81.39%); for 3, the total weight loss (77.23%) is much less than the calculated value (88.77%). The decomposing process is not complete due to the use of N2 atmosphere.

The TG curve of 4 is shown in Fig. S9d. The first weight loss is 0.84% in the temperature range of 35–139 °C, which corresponds to the loss of the non-coordinated water molecule (calcd. 1.06%). The second weight loss (3.10%) from 139 to 205 °C can be assigned to the loss of two coordinated water molecules (calcd. 4.25%). As the existence of strong hydrogen bond (OW3⋯O1), the third coordinated water molecule (OW3) did not lost at this step. The last weight loss starts from 355 °C and continues up to 1000 °C, which can be assigned to lost the third coordinated water and burn the organic groups. The total weight loss of 79.47% is less than the calculated value of 83.22% if the final product is assumed to be MnO; hence, the decomposing process is not complete due to the use of N2 atmosphere. In the DSC curve of 4, the endothermic peak at 184 °C corresponds to the release of two coordinated water molecules; the exothermic peaks at 404 and 462 °C are attributed to the decomposition of the cfH and odpa ligands.

The TG curve of 5 exhibits three stages of weight loss (Fig. S9e). The first weight loss of 8.62% lies in the temperature range of 35–240 °C, corresponding to the release of the non-coordinated and coordinated water molecules (calcd 9.41%). The second weight loss is 47.60% at 240–560 °C, and the third step is 14.72% from 560 to 680 °C, all assigned to the decomposition of norfH and bpta ligands (calcd 62.77%). The residue is CoCO3. The exothermic peaks at 197, 399 and 550 °C are attributed to the decomposition of the compound.

In order to check the phase purity of these compounds, the X-ray powder diffraction (XRPD) patterns were checked at room temperature. As shown in Figs. S10–S15, the peak positions of simulated and experimental XRPD patterns are in good agreement with each other, indicating the phase purity of the products. The differences in intensity may be due to the preferred orientation of the crystalline powder samples.

3.4. Photoluminescence properties

Taking into account the excellent luminescent properties of d10 metal complexes, the luminescence of 1 and 2 were investigated. It can be observed that intense emissions occur at 421 nm (Fig. 9a, λex=362 nm) for 1, and 404 nm (Fig. 9b, λex=347 nm) for 2. To understand the nature of the emission band, the photoluminescence property of cfH was analyzed. It was found that one weak emission at 437 nm could be observed for free cfH ligand (Fig. S16). In addition, recent studies have shown that the solid H2bpdc and norfH ligands exhibit weak emissions at 404 nm and 440 nm, respectively [6], [18]. Therefore, the emissions of 1 and 2 may be assigned to the intraligand fluorescent emission. The enhancement of luminescence may be attributed to ligand chelation to the metal center, which effectively increases the rigidity of the ligand and reduces the loss of energy by radiationless decay [18]. These observations indicate that compounds 1 and 2 may be excellent candidates for potential photoactive materials.

3.5. Magnetic properties

The magnetic properties of 3–6 were investigated over the temperature range 2.0–300.0 K (Fig. 10). For 3, the χMT value at 300 K is 4.203 cm3 K mol−1 (5.799μB), slightly smaller than the expected value (4.375 cm3 K mol−1, 5.916μB) of one isolated spin-only MnII ions (S=5/2, g=2.0; Fig. 10a). As T is lowered, χMT decreases continuously to a value of 0.382 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K. The magnetic behavior of 3 indicates a dominant antiferromagnetic interaction between the MnII ions in the structures. Based on the structure analysis of 3, the magnetic behavior of 3 can be explained by the magnetic exchange coupling through the carboxylate bridge. The fit of the curve for 1/χM versus T plot of 3 to the Curie–Weiss law gives a good result in the temperature range of 8–300 K with C=4.325 cm3 K mol−1 and θ=−8.410 K.

For 4, the χMT value at 300 K is 8.886 cm3 K mol−1 (8.431μB), which is slightly higher than the expected value (8.750 cm3 K mol−1, 8.367μB) of two isolated spin-only MnII ions (S=5/2, g=2.0; Fig. 10b). The χMT value of 4 remains almost constant from 300 K to 183 K, and then decreases on further cooling, reaching a value of 0.654 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K. The magnetic behavior 4 indicates a dominant antiferromagnetic interaction between the MnII ions in the structures. Based on the structure analysis of 4, the magnetic behavior of 4 can be explained by the magnetic exchange coupling within the tetranuclear manganese cluster. The fit of the curve for 1/χM versus T plot of 4 to the Curie–Weiss law gives a good result in the temperature range of 9–300 K with C=9.439 cm3 K mol−1 and θ=−13.290 K.

For 5, the χMT value of 5.903 cm3 K mol−1 (6.872 μB) at 300 K is much higher than the spin-only value of 3.75 cm K mol−1 (5.477μB) expected for two isolated octahedral CoII ions (S=3/2, g=2.0; Fig. 10c), which can be ascribed to the strong orbital contribution to the magnetic moment of CoII centers [19]. As T is lowered, χMT decreases continuously to a value of 1.567 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K. The magnetic behavior indicates a dominant antiferromagnetic interaction between the CoII ions in the structures. Because the tetranuclear cobalt clusters are interconnected by large bpta ligands, the overall antiferromagnetic interaction should be attributed to the magnetic exchange coupling within the tetranuclear cobalt cluster. The fit of the curve for 1/χM versus T plot of 5 to the Curie–Weiss law gives a good result in the temperature range of 10–300 K with C=6.057 cm3 K mol−1 and θ=−9.074 K. The dominant antiferromagnetic interactions are probably responsible for the negative θ value, although spin–orbit coupling effect of CoII ions may contribute to the negative θ value as well.

For 6, the χMT value of 10.311 cm3 K mol−1 (9.082μB) at 300 K is also much higher than the spin-only value of 5.625 cm3 K mol−1 (6.708μB) for three isolated octahedral CoII ions (S=3/2, taking g=2.0; Fig. 10d), which can be ascribed to the strong orbital contribution to the magnetic moment of CoII [19]. As T is lowered, χMT continuously decreases and reaches a local minimum of 7.556 cm3 K mol−1 at about 15 K, and then increases to a value 7.762 cm3 K mol−1 at 10 K, before dropping quickly to 7.181 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K. The magnetic behavior of 6 is unusual and interesting, indicative of a strong antiferromagnetic interaction admixture with a very weak ferromagnetic interaction [20]. The fit of the curve for 1/χM versus T plot of 6 to the Curie–Weiss law gives a good result in the temperature range of 4–300 K with C=10.516 cm3 K mol−1 and θ=−7.442 K.

4. Conclusions

In summary, we have successfully prepared a series of novel 2D metal–drug complexes by self-assembly of quinolone antibacterial agents with metal salts in the presence of long aromatic polycarboxylate ligands under hydrothermal conditions. The successful isolation of these species not only enriches the realm of 2D metal–quinolone complexes, but also provides new structural information that may help to understand the mechanisms of action of the quinolone antibacterial drugs. This work further demonstrates that the introduction of polycarboxylate ligands into metal–quinolone systems is a promising strategy to synthesize high-dimensional metal–quinolone complexes, thus opening interesting perspectives in the study of metal–drug complexes with particular functions.

    Pt(II) complex cis-[Pt(PEA)(OH2)2] X2, C-2 (where, PEA = 2-Pyridylethylamine and X  = ClO4− or NO3–) was synthesized by hydrolysis of cis-[Pt(PEA)Cl2] C-1. Glutathione (GSH) and DL-penicilamine (DL-pen) substituted complexes cis-[Pt(PEA)(GSH)],C-3 and cis-[Pt(PEA)DL-pen)]X C-4 were synthesized and characterized by spectroscopic methods. Kinetic studies were traced on complex C-2 with the thiols, GSH and DL-pen. Pt(II)-Sulfur adduct formation mechanisms of the substituted products C-3 and C-4 were established from the kinetic investigation. At pH 4.0, C-2 - thiols interactions follow two consecutive steps: the first step is dependent, and the second is independent of [thiol]. The association equilibrium constant (KE), substitution rate constants for both steps (k1 & k2), and activation parameters (ΔH‡ and ΔS‡) have been assessed to propose the mechanism. Agarose gel electrophoresis mobilization pattern of DNA with complexes was performed to visualize the interaction nature. CT-DNA and BSA binding activities of the complexes have been executed by electronic, fluorescence spectroscopy, and viscometric titration methods. Evaluation of thermodynamic parameters (ΔH0, ΔS0, and ΔG0) from BSA binding constants was executed to propose the driving forces of interaction between these species. A molecular docking study was performed to evaluate the binding mode of complexes with BDNA strands. Anticancer activity of the complexes C-1 to C-4 was explored on both A549 and HEp-2 cell lines, compared with approved anticancer drugs cisplatin, carboplatin, and oxaliplatin. All these complexes were tested by NBT assay on normal cell line skeletal muscle cells (L6 myotubes) to observe the adverse effects compared to recognized anticancer medications. The ultimate aim is to explore the role of anticancer agents on cell death mechanism, which has been performed by flow-cytometer on HEp-2 cell lines.

  • Interaction of bioessential metal ions with quinolone antibiotics: Structural features and biological evaluation

    2021, Inorganica Chimica Acta

    Bioessential metal complexes with quinolone antibiotics as ligands attract wide attention in bioinorganic chemistry due to their greater biological potential that may lead to the synthesis of novel coordination compounds with enhanced pharmacological activity. In this review structural features of copper(II), zinc(II) and cobalt(II)-quinolone complexes are presented and discussed in detail. Antimicrobial and antitumor activity as well as the binding affinity of selected coordination compounds to calf-thymus DNA, bovine and human serum albumins are also investigated. Further perspectives related to the choice of the metal ion, quinolone ligand and various co-ligands are also discussed to clarify and predict the main factors that contribute to biological activity of the bioessential metal complexes.

  • Synthesis and characterization of Pd(II) antitumor complex, DFT calculation and DNA/BSA binding insight through the combined experimental and theoretical aspects

    2021, Journal of Molecular Structure

    A novel palladium(II) complex, [Pd(bpy)(acac)]NO3 (where bpy and acac are 2,2′-bipyridine and acetylacetonato), has been synthesized and fully characterized through 1H NMR, UV-Vis and FT-IR spectra, conductivity measurement and elemental analysis. Geometry optimization using DFT calculation demonstrated that a square-planar geometry is built around Pd(II) atom. The MEP and FMO analysis were also applied. Partition coefficient evaluation was conducted to determine the lipophilicity of the compound which follows the order complex > cisplatin. The antitumor activity of the complex has been evaluated on K562 cancer cells by MTT method, which exhibited an appropriate activity in compare to cisplatin. The interaction of [Pd(bpy)(acac)]NO3 towards biomolecules (CT-DNA and BSA) was investigated through in-detail fluorescence, UV–Vis and other techniques. The findings of fluorescence titration demonstrated that the Pd(II) complex binds to CT-DNA via hydrophobic and with BSA through van der Waals forces and hydrogen bond. UV–Vis experiment revealed a reduction in the absorption spectra of biomolecules in presence of the complex. The binding affinity was further endorsed for CT-DNA utilizing viscosity measurement and gel electrophoresis and for BSA via CD spectroscopy and FRET calculation. In addition, molecular docking supports the findings of experiments and indicates that the Pd(II) complex appears to be situated at DNA intercalation position and BSA Sudlow's site I. All results have shown that this compound may be a useful potential antitumor candidate for further medical purposes.

  • DNA/protein interactions, cell cycle arrest and apoptosis study of potent cytotoxic Pt(II) complexes with reduced side-effects

    2017, Journal of Molecular Liquids

    A series of platinum(II) complexes (C1–C6), incorporating an intercalating substituted benzimidazole (2-aminomethylbenzimidazole) and varied leaving groups such as chloride, chelating carboxylates, and thiols, was synthesized to achieve more potent and specific metallodrugs. Spectroscopic titrations, viscosity and electrophoresis measurements revealed that the complexes bound to DNA via an intercalative mode. The complexes were also found to have high affinity towards serum albumin, BSA. Cell viability experiments against three human cancer cell lines (A549, MCF-7, MDA-MB-231) in vitro indicated that the antitumor activities were comparable with cisplatin and its successors, with the greatest efficiency towards A549. Interestingly, complexes C1–C4 were 1.3–1.6 times more potent than cisplatin towards MDA-MB-231 cells. The complexes generate lower degrees of oxidative stress and are almost non-toxic to normal L6 myotubes, indicative of selective toxicity for tumor cells over normal cells. Further studies with A549 cell line revealed that the inhibition of cancer cell proliferation by the complexes is brought about by a combination of cell cycle arrest at the G2/M phase and induction of a caspase-mediated apoptosis.
