Introduction

The study of molecular magnetic materials has attracted much attention and seen great progress in recent decades from both applied and fundamental points of view.1,2 Recently, the design and controlled assembly of metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) or coordination polymers (CPs) has been experiencing great growth.3,4 The great structural diversity in MOFs or CPs has provided many low- and high-dimensional magnetic systems that can serve as models to understand magneto-structural correlations or as materials with useful magnetic properties. To obtain novel structures, much effort has been devoted to modifying the building blocks and controlling the assembled motifs via selection of different organic ligands. However, controllable synthesis of MOFs or CPs is still a great challenge because many factors such as the metal-to-ligand ratio, the pH value, the reaction procedure, the temperature and the solvent, can impose strong influences on the final structures.5

Carboxylate compounds are among the most frequently used ligands for the study of coordination materials. On the one hand, the carboxylate group is a versatile bridge that can link two or more metal ions in various modes and can mediate magnetic exchange of different nature, so carboxylate ligands have been fruitfully used in the field of molecular magnetic materials.6,7 On the other hands, multicarboxylate ligands, especially aromatic ones, have been the most important organic building blocks for MOFs. The widely studied examples of dicarboxylates are 1,4-benzenedicarboxylate (bdc) and biphenyl-4,4′-dicarboxylate (bpdc), which has led to a variety of MOFs with diverse topologies and interesting properties.8 Recently, to functionalize MOFs for specific applications such as gas adsorption and catalysis, there is a growing interest in introducing a non-coordinative functional group into aromatic multicarboxylate ligands. In this context, some MOFs with modified bpdc ligands, such as those bearing –CN, –CHO, –NH2, –X (X = Br, I) functionalities, have been designed and studied in recent years.9 We have been interested in aromatic dicarboxylate ligands bearing the nitro group,10 including 2,2′-dinitrobiphenyl-4,4′-dicarboxylate (dnbpdc). During the study, we obtained some MOFs in which paramagnetic centers are aggregated by short bridges, which are interesting for magnetic studies. Here we present the hydrothermal syntheses, structures and magnetic properties of a series of copper(ii) compounds derived from H2dnbpdc, [Cu2(dnbpdc)(OH)2]n (1), [Cu(Hdnbpdc)2(bipy)]n·2nH2O (2), [Cu2(dnbpdc)2(bipy)(H2O)2]n·2nH2O (3) and [Cu3(dnbpdc)2(bipy)(OH)2(H2O)]n·nH2O (4), where bipy = 4,4′-bipyridyl.

Experimental

Materials and physical measurements

The reagents were obtained from commercial sources. The ligand2,2′-dinitrobiphenyl-4,4′-dicarboxylic acid (H2dnbpdc) was prepared according to the literature.11

Elemental analyses (EA) were determined on an Elementar Vario ELIII analyzer. The FT-IR spectra were recorded in the range 500–4000 cm−1 using KBr pellets on a Nicolet NEXUS 670 spectrophotometer. Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were carried out on a Mettler Toledo TGA/SDTA851 instrument under flowing air at a heating rate of 5 °C min−1. The powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) was recorded on a Rigaku D/Max-2500 diffractometer at 35 kV, 25 mA for a Cu-target tube and a graphite monochromator. Simulation of the PXRD patterns was carried out using the Mercury program available free of charge via the Internet at http://www.iucr.org. Temperature-dependent and field-dependent magnetic measurements were carried out on a Quantum Design SQUID MPMS-5 magnetometer. Diamagnetic corrections were made with Pascal's constants.

Synthesis

[Cu2(dnbpdc)(OH)2]n (1)

A mixture of H2dnbpdc (0.10 mmol, 0.033 g) and Cu(NO3)2 (0.10 mmol, 0.024 g) in water (8 mL), to which a drop of 0.1 M NaOH aqueous solution was added, was stirred for 30 min in air. The resulting suspension (pH ∼ 6) was transferred into a 23 mL Teflon-lined autoclave and heated at 180 °C for 24 h. After cooling to room temperature, pale-blue prism-shaped crystals of 1, contaminated by some yellow floccules that were rinsed out with DMF, were collected for X-ray diffraction and other measurements. The yield was 46% based on Cu. Elemental analysis calc. for C14H8N2O10Cu2 (M = 491.32): C, 34.23; H, 1.64; N, 5.70. Found: C, 34.14; H, 1.86; N, 6.09%. IR (KBr, cm−1): 3599w, 3090w, 2924w, 2854w, 1612sh, 1583s, 1529s, 1479m, 1410s, 1346s, 1263w, 1136w, 1095w, 992m, 926w, 829w, 783w, 760w, 725w.

[Cu(Hdnbpdc)2(bipy)]n·2nH2O (2)

A mixture of H2dnbpdc (0.10 mmol, 0.033 g), Cu(NO3)2 (0.10 mmol, 0.024 g) and bipy (0.05 mmol, 0.008 g) in water (4 mL), to which a drop of 0.1 M NaOH aqueous solution was added, was stirred for 30 min in air. The resulting suspension (pH ∼ 6) was heated in a 23 mL Teflon-lined autoclave at 180 °C for 24 h. After cooling to room temperature, blue-purple prism crystals of 2 were obtained. Yield, 62% based on H2dnbpdc. Elemental analysis calc. for C38H26N6O18Cu (M = 918.19): C, 49.70; H, 2.85; N, 9.15. Found: C, 50.04; H, 2.86; N, 9.19%. IR (KBr, cm−1): 3442br, 3061w, 2924w, 2854w, 1709m, 1614s, 1533s, 1484w, 1409s, 1381s, 1350s, 1260sh, 1225s, 1153w, 1127w, 1096w, 1071w, 1044w, 1008w, 925w, 897w, 852w, 821m, 777m, 697m.

[Cu2(dnbpdc)2(bipy)(H2O)2]n·2nH2O (3)

H2dnbpdc (0.10 mmol, 0.033 g) and Cu(NO3)2 (0.1 mmol, 0.024 g) were mixed in water (8 mL). After adding a drop of 0.1 M NaOH aqueous solution, the mixture was stirred for 30 min at room temperature, and then bipy (0.05 mmol, 0.008 g) was added. The blue suspension (pH ∼ 6) was stirred further for 2 h, transferred into a 23 mL Teflon-lined autoclave and heated at 180 °C for 24 h. After cooling to room temperature, blue block crystals were collected. Yield, 51% based on Cu. Elemental analysis calc. for C38H28N6O20Cu2 (M = 1015.74): C, 44.93; H, 2.78; N, 8.27. Found: C, 45.15; H, 2.84; N, 8.49%. IR (KBr, cm−1): 3556sh, 3404br, 3084w, 2924w, 2854w, 1616s, 1589sh, 1524vs, 1480m, 1411m, 1390m, 1344s, 1248w, 1218w, 1164w, 1118w, 1090w, 1069w, 1050w, 926w, 862w, 826m, 783m, 763m, 726m.

[Cu3(dnbpdc)2(bipy)(OH)2(H2O)]n·nH2O (4)

A drop of 0.1 M NaOH aqueous solution was added into the mixture of H2dnbpdc (0.1 mmol, 0.033 g), Cu(NO3)2 (0.15 mmol, 0.036 g), and bipy (0.05 mmol, 0.016 g) in water (8 mL). The resulting cyan suspension (pH ∼ 6) was stirred for 2 h, and then heated in a 23 mL Teflon-lined autoclave at 180 °C for 24 h. After cooling to room temperature slowly, green rod-shaped crystals of 4 were collected. Yield, 44% based on Cu. Elemental analysis calc. for C38H26N6O20Cu3 (M = 1077.27): C, 42.36; H, 2.43; N, 7.80. Found: C, 42.82; H, 2.92; N, 7.93%. Crystal structure analysis

Diffraction intensity data were collected at 293 K on a Bruker APEX II diffractometer equipped with a CCD area detector and graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). Empirical absorption corrections were applied using the SADABS program.12 The structures were solved by the direct method and refined by the full-matrix least-squares method on F2, with all non-hydrogen atoms refined with anisotropic thermal parameters.13 Some of the nitro groups in 1, 2, and 4 were refined with rotating disorder and distance restraints. All the hydrogen atoms attached to carbon atoms were placed in calculated positions and refined using the riding model, and the hydrogen atoms attached to oxygen atoms were located from the difference maps. Results and discussion

Syntheses and general characterization

Compounds 1–4 were synthesized by hydrothermal reactions with good reproducibility. The products have been characterized by single-crystal X-ray crystallography, IR, EA, PXRD, and TGA techniques (see Fig. S1 and S2†).

The reactions are compared in Scheme 1. In all cases, a small amount of NaOH was added to help the deprotonation of H2dnbpdc. 1 was synthesized from H2dnbpdc and Cu(NO3)2, and 2–4 were all prepared in the presence of bipy as co-ligand. The major differences between the synthetic conditions of 2–4 are the metal-to-ligand ratio and the amount of solvent. For 2 and 3, the ratios are the same. The use of a smaller amount of water led to 2, which contains the half deprotonated and singly charged ligand Hdnbpdc−. Doubling the amount of water led to 3, in which the ligand is fully deprotonated and doubly charged (dnbpdc2−). The difference may be explained by the assumption that a less concentrated solution promotes the dissolution and dissociation of the H2dnbpdc acid. A quick comparison between 3 and 4 indicates that the product from the less concentrated system is sensitive to the metal-to-ligand ratio: increasing the metal content helps the dnbpdc ligand bind more metal ions. This is consistent with the structural observations that each dnbpdc binds two CuII ions in 3, compared to three or four CuII ions in 4 (vide infra).

The FT-IR spectra of 1–4 all show medium or strong bands in the 1583–1616 and 1381–1410 cm−1 regions, characteristic of the asymmetric and symmetric vibrations of the deprotonated carboxylate groups. They also show strong absorptions in the 1524–1533 and 1344–1350 cm−1 regions due to the asymmetric and symmetric vibrations of the nitro groups. For 2, the presence of the medium absorption at 1709 cm−1, attributable to the CO vibration of the COOH group, clearly supports the partial deprotonation of the dicarboxylic ligand in 2. In addition, 1 and 4 exhibit a weak but relatively sharp band at 3600 and 3641 cm−1, respectively, assignable to the hydroxo ligand.

The TGA plots of compounds 1–4 are given in Fig. S2 (see ESI†). Compound 1 shows no appreciable weight loss until about 300 °C, consistent with the absence of any solvent molecules in the structure. Compounds 2–4 all release their water molecules before 200 °C, the observed (calculated) weight losses being 3.2 (3.9), 6.9 (7.1), and 2.9 (3.3) wt%, respectively.

Crystal structures

X-ray crystallography revealed that the asymmetric unit of compound 1 consists of two CuIIcations, a dnbpdc ligand and two hydroxo anions. As shown in Fig. 1, both CuII ions are four-coordinated in the distorted square planar geometry by two hydroxo and two carboxylate groups. The Cu–O distances range from 1.867(2) to 1.969(2) Å, with the Cu–Ohydroxo bonds being slightly shorter than the Cu–Ocarboxylate ones. The ligand is fully deprotonated and uses its two carboxylate groups in the syn–syn bridging mode to connect four CuII ions (Scheme 2a, omitting the weak interactions). As expected, the steric hindrance of the 2- and 2′-nitro substituents forces the ligand into a highly twisted conformation: the two benzene rings rotate around the C–C bond between them to give a large dihedral angle of 53.3(1)°.

The compound exhibits a 2D coordination network parallel to the ab plane, in which identical [Cu(OH)(COO)]n chains are interlinked through the dinitrobiphenyl backbones of the dnbpdc ligands (Fig. 2a). The chain, which propagates along the b direction, contains two independent and alternating sets of (μ-OH)(μ-COO) bridges. The Cu1⋯Cu2 distances spanned by the mixed double bridges are alternately 3.3687(5) and 3.4216(5) Å, and the Cu–O–Cu angles for the hydroxo bridges are 126.1(1)° and 132.19(15)°, respectively.

The coordination sheets are stacked in a complementary fashion so that each copper ion from one sheet is approached by an oxygen atom from the neighboring sheets. The distances, 2.643(3) Å between Cu1 and a nitro oxygen atom (O9, 0.5 − x, 0.5 − y, −z) and 2.920(3) Å between Cu2 and a carboxylate oxygen atom (O1, 0.5 − x, 0.5 + y, 0.5 − z), may indicate weak coordinative interactions. It may be said that the sheets are associated into a 3D network through these weak interactions (Fig. 2b). Taking the interactions into account, the dndpc ligand may be said to adopts an unusual μ6,η5 coordination mode involving not only all carboxylate oxygens but also a nitro oxygen (Scheme 2a). Accordingly, the coordination geometry around CuII can be said to be square pyramidal with significant axial elongation.

Compound 2 exhibits a 2D coordination network with mixed Hdnbpdc and bipyligands (Fig. 3 and 4). The Hdnbpdc ligand is half deprotonated with both COO− and COOH groups. The unique CuII ion resides on an inversion center and assumes a octahedral geometry with significant axial elongation. The equatorial plane is defined by two COO− and two pyridyl groups, with Cu–O = 1.941(2) Å and Cu–N = 2.011(2) Å. Two COOH groups from different Hdnbpdc ligands are axially coordinated via their hydroxyl oxygen atoms, with significantly longer Cu–O distances (2.726(3) Å). Thus, the Hdnbpdc−ligand may be said to adopt a bis(monodentate) coordination mode (μ2,η2, Scheme 2b). The dihedral angle between the two benzene rings is 76.7(1)°, defining a highly twisted conformation.

As shown in Fig. 4a, the CuII centers are linked by double Hdnbpdc−ligands to form 1D chains along [101] directions, with a long Cu⋯Cu distance of 13.820(5) Å. The chain is reinforced by strong hydrogen bonding between the COOH and COO groups around the same metal center. The hydrogen bond involves the weakly coordinated hydroxyl group (O4–H4) from COOH and the uncoordinated oxygen (O2, 1 + x, y, 1 + z) from COO, with a short O⋯O distance of 2.564(3) Å and a large O–H⋯O angle of 167(4)°. The adjacent [Cu(Hdnbpdc)2]n chains are pillared by the rigid bipyligands to give a 2D grid-like sheet along the (11) plane. The Cu⋯Cu distance spanned by bipy is 11.100(4) Å. The sheets are stacked in parallel in the structure.

Compound 3 also exhibits a 2D network, but it is based on a dimeric Cu2(H2O)2 unit. As shown in Fig. 5, the unique CuII ion adopts the five-coordinated square pyramidal (SP) geometry, with a little distortion towards trigonal bipyramidal (TBP), as is indicated by the very small distortion parameter τ = 0.064 (τ = 0 for ideal SP and 1 for ideal TBP).14 The basal positions are occupied by a water molecule (O5), two carboxylate oxygen atoms (O1 and O4A), and a pyridylnitrogen atom (N1), with the Cu–O/N distances in the range from 1.910(3) Å to1.992(3) Å. The apical position is occupied by another water molecule (O5B) with a significantly longer Cu–O length (2.439(3) Å). Two equivalent water molecules (O5 and O5B) serve as double bridges to link CuII ions into a [Cu2(H2O)2]4+ dinuclear unit, in which the Cu⋯Cu distance is 3.325(1) Å and the Cu–O–Cu angle is 97.3(1)°. The fully deprotonated dnbpdc2−ligand adopts a bis(monodentate) mode (μ2,η2Scheme 2c), and the dihedral angle between the two benzene rings is 78.21(15)°. The bis(aqua)-bridged dinuclear unit is linked by the dnbpdc2− and bipyligands in two different directions to give a grid-like 2D sheets along the ab plane (Fig. 6).

The adjacent sheets are stacked in the ABAB offsetting fashion to minimise the void volume. A guest water molecule (O6) included in the inter-sheet aperture is hydrogen bonded to a bridging water molecule (O5) and an uncoordinated carboxylate oxygen atom (O3) from different sheets. The inter-sheet hydrogen bond combines with the intra-sheet one between O5 (water) and O3 to form a 12-membered cyclic motif in the chair conformation [graph set symbol R64(12)] (Fig. S3 and Table S1†).

Compound 4 exhibits a complicated 3D coordination framework based on infinite [Cu(OH)(COO)]n chains. The asymmetric unit contains three crystallographically distinct CuII ions, which are all five-coordinated but with somewhat different geometry (Fig. 7). The geometry for Cu1 is SP, with a small distortion toward TBP (τ = 0.16).14 The basal donors include two carboxylate oxygens (O2 and O5) and two hydroxo anions (O9 and O10), while the apex is furnished by a water molecule (O19). The apical bond length [2.422(5) Å] is obviously longer than the basal ones [1.871(3)–1.962(3) Å]. Cu2 may also be said to be in an SP environment, but the distortion towards TBP is significant (τ = 0.31). The short basal bonds [1.894(3)–2.020(3) Å] involve two carboxylate oxygens (O1 and O4C), a hydroxo anion (O9) and a pyridylnitrogen (N2B), and the long apical bond [2.326(3) Å] is formed by a third carboxylate oxygen (O3A). For Cu3, the τ parameter (0.57) is even larger, and the geometry is better described as distorted TBP. The TBP equatorial positions are defined by two carboxylate oxygens (O6 and O7E, Cu–O = 2.129(3) and 1.974(3) Å, respectively) and a hydroxo anion (O10D, Cu–O = 2.142(3) Å), and the axial positions are occupied by a pyridylnitrogen (N1, Cu–N = 1.999(3) Å) and another hydroxo (O10, Cu–O = 1.933(2) Å). Note that the equatorial distances are on average longer than the axial ones.

There are two kinds of hydroxo bridges in 4: one is μ2-OH (O9) between Cu1 and Cu2 and the other is μ3-OH (O10) among Cu1, Cu3 and Cu3′ (Fig. 8). The hydroxo bridges combine with the carboxylate bridges to link the CuII ions into a complicated chain, which propagates along the a direction and has an overall composition of [Cu(OH)(COO)]n. The smallest repeating unit of the chain is a quasi-linear trinuclear motif, in which Cu1 is connected to Cu2 by the mixed (μ2-OH)(syn, syn-COO) bridges on one side and to Cu3 by (μ3-OH)(syn, syn-COO) bridges on the opposite side. The Cu⋯Cu distances within the trinuclear motif are 3.376(2) Å and 3.343(2) Å, and the corresponding Cu–O–Cu angles are 127.38(15)° and 120.3(1)°. The adjacent trinuclear units along the chain are related by inversion centers, and they are linked by double μ3-OH bridges between two Cu3 atoms and by double syn–anti carboxylate bridges between two Cu2 atoms. The μ3-OH bridge also serves to link Cu1 and Cu3′ from different units. The Cu⋯Cu distances are 3.080(1) Å for Cu3(μ3-OH)2Cu3′ (Cu3–O–Cu3′ = 98.1(1)°), 3.426(2) Å for Cu1(μ3-OH)Cu3′ (Cu1–O–Cu3′ = 114.8(1)°), and 4.248(2) Å for Cu2(COO)2Cu2′.

The deprotonated dnbpdc2−ligands in 4 display two different coordination modes (Scheme 2d and e), namely, μ4,η4 (two μ-1,3-carboxylates, one in the syn–syn mode, one in the syn–anti mode) and μ3,η3 (a monodentate carboxylate binding Cu3 and a syn–syn μ-1,3-carboxylate binding Cu1 and another Cu3). The dihedral angles between benzene rings for the two modes are 74.0(1) and 66.9(1)°, respectively.

The adjacent [Cu(OH)(COO)]n chains are linked into a complicated 3D framework by the long dnbpdc2− and the bipyligands. To gain a better understanding, the structure may be described as pillared layers. The bipyligands, which connect Cu2 and Cu3 from different chains, serve to link the chains into 2D layers parallel to the ab plane (Fig. 9a), and the dinitrobiphenyl backbones of the dnbpdc2−ligands serve as relatively inclined pillars between the layers (Fig. 9b).

Magnetic properties

The temperature dependence of the magnetic susceptibilities (χM) for compounds 1, 3 and 4 in the temperature range 2–300 K were recorded at 5 kOe and shown as χMT–T and χM−1–T plots in Fig. 10. For compound 1, the χMT value at 300 K is 0.76 emu K mol−1, which lies in the range expected for two CuII ions. Upon cooling, the value decreases monotonically, and the data above 110 K follow the Curie–Weiss law with C = 1.06 emu K mol−1 and θ = −111.7 K. The χMT versus T behavior and the negative Weiss constant indicate antiferromagnetic coupling between the CuII ions. From the magnetic point of view, 1 can be treated as an quasi-1D system with significant magnetic interactions along the [Cu(μ-OH)(μ-COO)]n chain and negligible interactions between the chains. Since there are two alternating sets of intra-chain (OH)(COO) bridges that have different structural parameters, the 1D magnetic system can be described by the spin Hamiltonian H = −JΣiS2iS2i+1 − αJΣiS2iS2i+1. For an alternating antiferromagnetic chain (J < 0 and 0 ≤ α < 1) with S = 1/2, it has been demonstrated that there is an energy gap between the singlet ground state and the first excited state.1b Consequently, upon cooling the magnetic susceptibility is expected to climb over a broad maximum at certain temperature and then tend to zero when T approaches zero. For 1, the minimum of χM−1 (or the maximum of χM) at 70 K (Fig. 10a) is typical of strong antiferromagnetic coupling between CuII ions. The final decrease of χM−1 (or the increase of χM) below 24 K cannot be intrinsic to the singlet ground state but can be due to the presence of paramagnetic impurity,1b which is also indicated by the slowed decrease of χMT below 25 K. The magnetic contribution from the impurity may be obscured at high temperature, but it can become dominant at low temperature when the main phase is in a singlet ground state. Therefore, we have used the expression χM = χchain(1 − ρ) + χpiρ to fit the experimental data over the temperature range 2–300 K, where ρ is the molar fraction of the paramagnetic impurity. For χchain (magnetic susceptibility of the alternating antiferromagnetic chain), a numerical expression with the J, α, and g as variable parameters has been proposed by Hatfield et al.1b,15 The susceptibility of the paramagnetic impurity was assumed to be χpi = Ng2β2S(S + 1)/(3kT). The best fit gave J = −103.6 cm−1, α = 0.33, g = 2.17, and ρ = 0.037.

Previous experimental and computational studies on CuII systems with mixed (OR)(COO) bridges (OR = OH or alkoxo) have demonstrated that the magnetic exchange is sensitive to the Cu–O–Cu angle. It is usually antiferromagnetic and the magnitude decreases with decreasing Cu–O–Cu angle; the coupling can become ferromagnetic in cases where Cu–O–Cu is lower than about 110°.16 The effects have been explained by invoking the concept of orbital countercomplementarity of the two dissimilar bridges.1b,17 For compound 1, we can assign J = −103.6 cm−1 to the bridge with Cu–O–Cu = 132.15(15)° and αJ = −34.2 cm−1 to the bridge with Cu–O–Cu = 126.15(15)°. Both J and αJ values fall within the range for similar systems.

For compound 3, the room-temperature χMT value is 0.87 emu K mol−1, which lies in the usual range expected for two CuII ions. Upon cooling, the χMT product decreases first slowly and then rapidly, and χM increases to a maximum at 6 K and then drops rapidly. The data above 20 K can be fitted to the Curie–Weiss law with C = 0.89 emu K mol−1 and θ = −6.6 K. These thermal behaviours indicate weak antiferromagnetic coupling. The compound is based on double aqua-bridged dimers separated by long dnbpdc and bipy linkers. Accordingly, magnetic analysis were carried out using the well-known Bleaney–Bowers equation18 for CuII dimers. The best fit gave J = −7.0 cm−1 and g = 2.11. The rather weak antiferromagnetic interaction is consistent with the disposition mode of the aqua bridges between CuII ions. The CuII ion in the square pyramidal geometry has a dx2−y2-type magnetic orbital, which mainly interacts with basal ligands. The aqua bridge occupies a basal position of one CuII ion and the apical position of another CuII ion. The basal–apical disposition allows a small overlap between the magnetic orbitals of the two CuII ions, and hence weak interactions are operative. The number of CuII complexes with aqua bridges (single or double) is rather limited. In the known examples, the aqua bridges usually take the basal–apical (or equatorial–axial, for octahedral CuII) or apical–apical (or axial–axial) disposition, mediating negligible to weak interactions, ferromagnetic or antiferromagnetic.19

For compound 4, the χMT value per formula at 300 K is about 1.24 emu K mol−1, which is in the normal range expected for three CuII ions. Upon cooling, the χMT value decreases monotonically and the data above 140 K follow the Curie–Weiss law with C = 1.45 emu K mol−1 and θ = −48.3 K. These features suggest overall antiferromagnetic interactions between the CuII ions. The low-temperature behaviour of this compound is somewhat similar to that of 1, which may indicate the presence of some paramagnetic impurity. Neglecting the interactions between the [Cu(OH)(COO)]n chains, which are separated by the long bipy or biphenyl spacers, five interaction parameters are needed to account for the five different bridging moieties between the neighbouring CuII ions within the chain: Cu1(COO)(OH)Cu2, Cu1(COO)(OH)Cu3, Cu1(OH)Cu3′, Cu3(OH)2Cu3′, and Cu2(COO)2Cu2′. An exact magnetic solution to the complicated 1D five-J system is impossible. Considering that the first two moieties involve only short Cu–O bonds [Obasal (SP) and Oaxial (TBP)] and that the other bridges each involve long Cu–O bonds [Oapical (SP) or Oequatorial (TBP)], and also considering that the magnetic orbitals in the SP and TBP geometry are basally (dx2−y2) and axially (dz2) directed, respectively, we had attempted a simple trimer-based model for magnetic fit: the stronger interactions through the (COO)(OH) bridges define a linear trinuclear 2-J magnetic motif, while the weaker interactions through the other bridges could be treated as secondary effects and averaged into intermolecular interactions in the molecular-field approximation.1b However, the model failed to reproduce the observed thermal magnetic data with reasonable parameters, perhaps due to oversimplification.

Conclusions

We have described the hydrothermal synthesis, structures and magnetic properties of four CuII coordination polymers derived from 2,2′-dinitrobiphenyl-4,4′-dicarboxylic acid (H2dnbpdc). Compound 1 exhibits a 2D coordination network in which 1D [Cu(μ-OH)(μ-COO)]n chains are linked by dnbpdc2−. Magnetic measurement shows that the CuII ions are antiferromagnetically coupled through the mixed hydroxo and syn–syn carboxylate bridges. In the presence of bipy as co-ligand, three different compounds were isolated, dependent upon the metal-to-ligand ratio and the concentration of reactants. In compound 2, the mononuclear CuII centers are linked into 2D grid-like layers by Hdnbpdc− and bipyligands. Compound 3 also consists of 2D grid-like coordination layers, which however are based on the bis(aqua)-bridged dimeric nodes with dnbpdc2− and bipy as linkers. Magnetic studies show that the magnetic coupling through the double aqua bridges is weak and antiferromagnetic. In compound 4, the CuII ions are connected by a mixture of μ3-OH, μ2-OH and μ-COO bridges to give infinite chains, which are cross-linked into a 3D framework by dnbpdc2− and bipy. Magnetic studies reveal overall antiferromagnetic interactions through the mixed bridging networks. This work illustrates the great potential of obtaining diverse magnetic systems through an approach that is currently intensively applied for metal–organic frameworks.
