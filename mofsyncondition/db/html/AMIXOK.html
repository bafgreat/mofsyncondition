Network dimensionality and ligand flexibility in lanthanide terephthalate hydrates

Abstract

Various lanthanide open framework materials incorporating the terephthalate (TP) entity were prepared using hydrothermal synthesis methods at a moderate temperature of 170 °C. The compounds Nd2(TP)3(H2O)4(1), Er2(TP)3(H2O)4(2), Yb2(TP)3(H2O)2(3), Yb2(TP)3(H2O)6(4), and Yb2(TP)3(H2O)8·2H2O (5), were characterized by single crystal structural analysis and FT-IR spectroscopy. While compounds 1 and 2 have been reported before on the basis of powder X-ray diffraction, the structural characterization of any ytterbium terephthalate species is unprecedented. Compounds 1–5 crystallize in triclinic settings with space group P-1. The compounds are compared with their previously reported Er and Tb-counterparts and the reduction of the dimensionality of the resulting networks from 3D over 2D to 1D with increasing level of hydration is discussed. Compounds 1, 2, and 3 with the lowest water content assemble in three-dimensional network lattices. Compounds 4 and 5, however, form 2D layered systems and 1D rod like chains, respectively, which are held together by hydrogen bonds originating from coordinating H2O. The crystal lattices of the 3D networks experience higher levels of tension as can be seen by increasing out-of-plane torsion with regard to the terephthalate carboxylate groups. Moreover, there seems to be a correlation between the level of strain on the aromatic ligands and the reduction of the number of carboxylate oxygen atoms that are part of the coordination polyhedra.

1. Introduction

Investigations into Metal Organic Frameworks (MOFs) have shown that three-dimensional (3D) porous systems can be obtained that have the ability to hold small atoms, ions, or molecules [1], [2], [3], [4], [5], [6], [7], [8]. These materials have received considerable attention over recent years because of their potential implications in gas storage [9], [10], [11], drug delivery [12], [13], [14], electronic [15], magnetic [16], [17], sensing [18], [19], and catalytic [20], [21] systems. The various organic building units that are integrated into MOF materials are generally equipped with high levels of linking capabilities, and comprise carboxylates as well as other functional groups, which allow these open framework materials to assemble in three-dimensional lattice structures [22], [23], [24], [25]. MOFs have become increasingly popular throughout recent years for their various degrees of porosity, while providing fairly rigid 3D lattices [9], [23], [26]. This allows for the inclusion of various guest entities and their subsequent removal without altering the structural integrity of the frameworks. More recently, numerous researchers have begun to extend the investigation of MOFs from the transition metals to the f-elements. This has become an interesting part of MOF science, as the f-elements exhibit considerably larger coordination spheres with coordination numbers between 8 and 10 [27], [28], [29], [30], [31]. Including a variety of lanthanide and actinide elements in the design of open frameworks may be relevant for the sequestering and subsequent storage of nuclear waste materials [32], [33], [34].

In this work we investigate the structural properties of various lanthanide open frameworks involving the planar, rigid terephthalate unit (TP) with various levels of hydration. We compare the structures of the compounds we obtained with some similar compounds that have been reported in the literature. This work illustrates how the crystal lattice in lanthanide terephthalate systems counteracts increasing tensions induced by decreasing ionic radii (for the most heavy lanthanides) and by decreasing levels of hydration in conjunction with increased dimensionality.

The terephthalate ligand represents a relatively rigid arene system, in which eight sp2 hybridized carbon atoms (1 benzene ring with 2 carboxylate groups in para position) form a conjugated π-system. Daiguebonne and co-workers synthesized an extended series of lanthanide terephthalates tetra-hydrate compounds (Ln2(TP)3(H2O)4) with Ln = Ce–Tm (except Pm). They subsequently structurally characterized these compounds based on powder X-ray diffraction and found that all of these are isostructural, crystallizing as 3D networks in a triclinic setting with the space group P-1. Their plot of the unit cell parameters vs. the ionic radii along that series shows a steady slight decrease in unit cell volume and no unusual fluctuations among any of the cell parameters. Using powder X-ray diffraction, these researchers established that, while all water molecules in these compounds are coordinated to the metal center, they can nevertheless be dehydrated by heating to temperatures of 125 °C and above. They point out that the lighter Ln2(TP)3(H2O)4 (Ln = Ce–Eu) do not undergo any change in their crystal structures upon dehydration. The heavier counterparts (Ln = Tb–Tm) however, do experience a change in the crystal structure and form a new 3D compound crystallizing in a different triclinic P-1 system. All anhydrous compounds bind water reversibly when exposed to moisture, restoring the initial structure [35]. In particular, the structures of a number of erbium-terephthalates with various degrees of hydration have received the attention of the Daiguebonne team [36].

We obtained single crystal X-ray structural data for a small number of lanthanide terephthalates with varying degrees of hydration, namely Nd2(TP)3(H2O)4 (1), Er2(TP)3(H2O)4 (2), Yb2(TP)3(H2O)2 (3), Yb2(TP)3(H2O)6 (4), and Yb2(TP)3(H2O)8·2H2O (5), as well as two mixed ligand complexes, Yb4(TP)5(SO4)(H2O)161 and Nd2(TP)(Glut)2(H2O)36 Footnote1 (Glut = glutaric acid anion). In addition to the powder XRD data presented by Daiguebonne and co-workers the single crystal X-ray structure of Tb2(TP)3(H2O)4 has been analyzed and described by Reinecke et al. [23].

Due to the rigidity of the terephthalate ligand, the resulting frameworks show very constrained arrangements of the aromatic spacer units which suffer varying degrees of out-of-plane distortion with regard to the carboxylate groups. This allows the carboxylate groups to be part of the coordination spheres and to absorb tensions as they increase towards the heavier lanthanide complexes with smaller ionic radii while retaining the same structure as their lighter lanthanide counterparts.

2.3. X-ray structure determination

Intensity data for crystals of compounds 1, 2, and 5 were collected at 296 K on a Bruker P4 single-crystal X-ray diffractometer equipped with charge-coupled-device (CCD) detector and a graphite monochromatized Mo Kα X-ray source (λ = 0.71073 Å). Data were collected using Φ and ω scans to 2θ > 60° and corrected for absorption using the multi-scan method using SADABS [37]. Structures were solved by direct methods using SHELXS [38] and refined on F2 using SHELXL [38]. Hydrogen atoms were visible in difference maps, but were either placed in idealized positions, or were refined with appropriate restraints where needed.

Crystals of compounds 3 and 4 were mounted on Mitegen 20 micron micromesh mounts using a small amount of mineral oil. The data were collected on a Bruker Smart APEX charge-coupled-device (CCD) diffractometer with an ApexII software upgrade, with a KRYO-FLEX liquid nitrogen vapor cooling device. Data were collected at 100 K. The instrument is equipped with a graphite monochromatized Mo Kα X-ray source (λ = 0.71073 Å) with MonoCap X-ray source optics. Hemispheres or spheres of data were collected using ω scans. Data collection and initial indexing and cell refinement were handled using the APEX II software [39]. Frame integration, including Lorentz-polarization corrections, and final cell parameter calculations were carried out using the SAINT+ software as implemented in Apex II. The data were corrected for absorption using the SADABS program. The structures were solved either by direct methods or Patterson methods and were refined by full matrix least squares against F2 with all reflections using the SHELXTL V6.14 package [38], [40]. Non hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. CH hydrogen atom positions were idealized, water H atom positions were refined with an OH distance restraint of 0.84(2) Å and (where necessary) with an H⋯H distance of 1.35(2) Å. All other H atoms were treated based on the data quality (refined with appropriate restraints where possible, placed in calculated positions where not). For all five structures hydrogen isotropic displacement parameters were constrained to be 1.5 (water) or 1.2 times (all others) that of the Ueq value of the carrier atom. Additional details of hydrogen atom treatment and software versions may be found in the supplementary cif files. Figures displaying structural motifs were created using CrystalMaker® 1.3.5 for Windows XP and CrystalMaker® 8.3 for Mac.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Synthesis

The treatment of Nd2O3, Er2O3, and Yb2O3 with terephthalic acid or a mixture of terephthalic acid and glutaric acid under hydrothermal conditions at 170 °C resulted in the formation of water insoluble products that formed millimeter-sized crystals, microcrystalline powders, amorphous solids, or a mixture of these. The crystal forms ranged from loose, thin, long individual needles to large rectangular blocks. All products contain coordinated water molecules, while one of them also accommodates interstitial water molecules. The FT-IR spectra exhibit the strong bands one expects to see for the symmetric and asymmetric vibrations of terephthalate (1700–1470 and 1460–1220 cm−1) and also for the hosted water molecules (3300–3600 cm−1). The IR spectra of 1 and 2, which are isostructural, look basically the same. The IR spectra of all Yb2(TP)3(H2O)x compounds (3–5) do also look similar. Based on the FT-IR spectra, the elemental analysis data, and the fact that some samples contained crystals of different compounds we assume that we did not obtain pure phases of these compounds.

3.2. Solid state structures

Nd2(TP)3(H2O)4 (1) and Er2(TP)3(H2O)4 (2), which are isostructural with identical structural motifs, crystallize in the triclinic crystal system with space group P-1. Our results are in good agreement with previous reports about Er2(TP)3(H2O)4 [35], [36], [41], [42], [43], Tb2(TP)3(H2O)4 [23], [44], and Ln2(TP)3 with various degrees of hydration [35], [36], [42]. The structure of Er2(TP)3(H2O)4, has been reported on the basis of powder X-ray diffraction data [35], while the Tb-counterpart remains the only structure so far that has been analyzed via single crystal X-ray diffraction [23]. To the best of our knowledge, we are presenting the single crystal X-ray structures of Nd2(TP)3(H2O)4 (1) and Er2(TP)3(H2O)4 (2) here for the first time. 1 and 2 are isostructural with Tb2(TP)3(H2O)4 [23], [44] forming 3D extended lattices, in which the Ln3+-cations are the centers of LnO8 coordination polyhedra. Six of the oxygen atoms are provided by six terephthalate anions which coordinate in mono-dentate, μ1-fashion to the Ln-central atom. The carboxylate groups bridge adjacent Ln3+ cations into crystallographic directions “a” and “c”. The resulting 2D LnO networks in the “ac” plane are extended into an overall 3D extended lattice via terephthalate anions tying these layers together along the “b” axis. The remaining two oxygen atoms of the LnO8 coordination polyhedra stem from coordinating water molecules. Representing the structural motifs of 1 and 2, Fig. 1 depicts the view onto the “bc” plane in 1.

In Fig. 2 we showcase the slightly twisted view of this network along the “c” direction, which demonstrates that these frameworks accommodate thin, infinite channels between the aromatic rings towards that direction. The aromatic rings are aligned in a nearly parallel manner, while they are slightly contorted towards each other. Thus, these materials exhibit micro porosity but can only hold smallest guest molecules, such as water. In purely inorganic open frameworks a rearrangement of the crystal lattice or some sort of adjustments of unit cell parameters takes place commonly between the lighter lanthanide complexes and their heavier counterparts due to the decreasing ionic radius of the Ln3+ cations with increasing atomic number. We recently asserted that the likelihood of such changes in the crystal lattice within the series of similar lanthanide compounds rises with increased levels of rigidity [45], [46].

The fact that compounds 1 and 2 are isostructural and that the unit cell parameters show no inconsistencies between the Ce and the Tm-counterparts, which are all isostructural [35], suggests that due to the larger organic linking units the space reduction with decreasing ionic Ln3+ radius in MOFs has less drastic effects on the crystal lattice and rather allows these structures to arrange in an isotypic manner along extended series of lanthanide terephthalate tetra-hydrate compounds. However, among the choices of organic spacer units the terephthalic acid entity appears to be of high rigidity, which limits the flexibility within the lanthanide terephthalate structures. This can be recognized in Fig. 1, where the positioning of the carboxylate oxygen atoms in the coordination polyhedron places a certain degree of torque onto the aromatic ring systems leaving them somewhat contorted towards each other. Due to the sp2 hybridization throughout the entire terephthalate system, this places a substantial amount of strain on these spacer units, which confer the lanthanide terephthalates with very limited flexibility with respect to their framework topology. For instance, the out-of-plane torsion of the carboxylate groups in the terephthalate anions in 1 varies between 6.1° and 25.5°. In Tb2(TP)3(H2O)4 the corresponding angles are somewhat more intense and range between 6.0° and 28.0° [23]. Finally, the Er-counterpart (2) experiences the highest level of torsion as the spacer units compensate for the decreased space availability resulting from the smaller Er3+ radius (1.004 Å vs. 1.04 Å for Tb and 1.109 Å for Nd) [47]. Consequently, the out-of-plane torsion angles of the terephthalate carboxylate groups in the Er compound (2) lie between 6.6° and 30.6°.

The structural description of any ytterbium terephthalate is unprecedented. We were also unsuccessful in synthesizing an Yb-counterpart isostructural to 1, 2 or any of the reported Ln2(TP)3(H2O)4 derivatives with Ln being thulium or lighter. Instead, we obtained and structurally characterized compounds 3, 4, and 5. The Yb compound with a structure closest related to that of 1 and 2 is Yb2(TP)3(H2O)2 (3). This compound assembles in a 3D open network, while 4 forms 2D layers that are connected by hydrogen bonds and 5 consists of 1D chains that interact via hydrogen bonds. All three compounds have the commonality that at least one oxygen atom of the terephthalate carboxylate groups remains uncoordinated. This can be attributed to the highly rigid terephthalate anions in concert with the limited space availability, as Yb3+ has the smallest ionic radius (0.985 Å) [47] among the lanthanide compounds presented in this manuscript. Yb4(TP)5(SO4)(H2O)16, obtained from the reaction of ytterbium sulfate with sodium terephthalate, is the only ytterbium based compound that provides sufficient flexibility for all carboxylate oxygen atoms to be part of the coordination sphere. This can be attributed to the sulfate entities that provide somewhat more rotational freedom due to the tetrahedral sulfur atom. We will provide a more detailed discussion of the two mixed ligand complexes, Yb4(TP)5(SO4)(H2O)16 and Nd2(TP)(Glut)2(H2O)36, in subsequent publications on mixed ligand Ln–TP complexes.

When discussing the structural properties of compound 3 it should be noted that the number of crystallographically distinct metal and terephthalate units is doubled when compared to the other four structures 1, 2, 4, and 5 and it thus exhibits two crystallographically different ytterbium ions. The terephthalate units are arranged in a nearly perpendicular manner to each other, while the coordination numbers of the two Yb atoms are limited to seven and six, respectively. Compound 3 does not feature any channels of significant size as part of its framework, but instead the perpendicular arrangement of the aromatic spacer units furnishes a rigid framework with individual rectangular chambers of considerable void space. Fig. 3 depicts the 3D framework of 3 seen along the “a” axis, where it becomes apparent that this lattice is segmented into two different types of layers within the “ab” plane, which are arranged in alternating fashion along the “c” direction. Furthermore, they elongate infinitely in the “b” direction. For simplicity we assigned the abbreviations Seg1 and Seg2 to these segments in Fig. 3.

Each layer within the “ab” plane features chains of aromatic terephthalate units and Yb-O coordination polyhedra that stretch along the “b” axis. A series of aromatic rings is displayed almost parallel to the “bc” plane, while others are arranging nearly perpendicularly to this plane. The alignment of parallel and perpendicular terephthalate systems alternates in layers along the “a” axis in both segments, while they are off phase as the segments align in the “c” direction (Fig. 4). While the terephthalate anions in the Ln2(TP)3(H2O)4 frameworks align in an approximately parallel manner to the “bc” plane, their carboxylic acid groups suffer fewer degrees of distortion as compared to 3. In general, the eight-coordinate Ln-ligand polyhedra, observed for Ln2(TP)3(H2O)4, in conjunction with the organic spacers seem to exhibit a sufficient degree of space availability and flexibility within these frameworks, so that all carboxylate oxygen atoms coordinate to the lanthanide metal centers. This sort of arrangement results in out-of-plane torsion angles for the carboxylate groups to range between 6.6° and 30.6° in 1, Tb2(TP)3(H2O)4 [23], and 2. In 3, however, the rigidity seems to be increased as a result of the lower coordination numbers and the smaller ionic radius in the Yb3+ cations.

The combination of both effects apparently increases the overall tension on the aromatic systems, and thereby, causes the carboxylate groups to twist further out of the conjugate π-systems’ planes. The torsion angles for the carboxylic acid groups in 3 lie between 10.6° and 38.1°. This compound suffers the highest degree of out-of-plane torsion for its terephthalate anions of all compounds discussed herein. The arrangement of terephthalate anions in Seg1 prevents one of the carboxylate oxygen atoms from coordinating to the nearest metal center. We colored these oxygen atoms purple in Fig. 3, Fig. 4 for the reader to identify the uncoordinated carboxylate oxygen atoms more easily.2 In Fig. 4a we showcase the first kind of segments (Seg1) that extend throughout the “bc” plane. The coordination number in Seg1 appears to be 7, incorporating two water molecules and five carboxylate oxygen atoms. As a result, one carboxylate oxygen atom remains uncoordinated in these segments. These oxygen atoms receive two hydrogen bonds from adjacent water molecules. In Fig. 4b we depict the second kind of segments (Seg2) that alternate with Seg1 along the “c” axis. In Seg2 the coordination number is lowered to 6. All terephthalate oxygen atoms are coordinated to the Yb-metal centers, while water molecules are excluded from the YbO6 coordination polyhedra in Seg2. As a result of the more constricted arrangement of terephthalate anions in Seg2, out-of-plane torsion angles (15.3–38.0°) exceed the numbers observed for Seg1 (11.6–25.8°).

In contrast, compound 4 (Yb2(TP)3(H2O)6) exhibits the coordination number 8. The resulting YbO8 polyhedra comprise three water molecules and five carboxylate oxygen atoms. 4 is isostructural to the Er-counterpart that was previously described [36], [48]. All unit cell parameters are slightly reduced as compared with the Er-analogue but otherwise agree very well. Four terephthalate entities are linked to one Yb-central atom. One of these coordinates in a bi-dentate fashion with both oxygen atoms linking to this central atom. Another one only coordinates via one oxygen atom, while the second oxygen atom remains uncoordinated. The two remaining terephthalate units coordinate in a mono-dentate fashion with only one oxygen atom to that Yb3+ cation. The other oxygen atom of the respective carboxylate group connects to an adjacent Yb-central atom in the “b” direction. Thus, while all four terephthalate anions link Yb3+ cations in the “c” direction, two of them also function as bridging units between adjacent Yb-central atoms. Fig. 5a illustrates the view onto the “bc” plane, in which terephthalate anions act as linking units between Yb-central atoms along the diagonal of this plane. Two carboxylate oxygen atoms of one type belonging to two equivalent terephthalate anions remain uncoordinated (purple), while receiving hydrogen bonds from water molecules that are coordinated to the closest metal centers. The out-of-plane torsions of the carboxylate groups range between 2.0° and 33.4°. Thus, they are slightly less intense than those observed for the 3D network in 3.

The corresponding torsion angles in the Er-counterpart lie between 5.6° and 20.2°, which shows that the differences between Ln2(TP)3(H2O)6, with Ln = Er and Yb, are more significant with the more constricted arrangement of ligands at a higher dimensionality with fewer coordinating water molecules as compared to the numbers for 5 and its Er-counterpart as we will point out below. Apparently the amount of tension placed on the terephthalate entities in this compound fits well in-between 3 and 5, which is in reconciliation with 4 expanding as a 2D framework in form of zig/zag layers. These individual layers are tied together via hydrogen bonds, originating from water molecules. In Fig. 5b we show how these layers form an interwoven network of metal centers and terephthalate entities (water molecules not shown). Fig. 6 presents how one type of chains of terephthalate anions linking the Yb-central atoms stretches along the diagonal of the “ac” plane in one layer, while a second type of chains runs across the “ac” plane in the direction of the other diagonal.

The Yb-metal centers in compound 5 (Yb2(TP)3(H2O)8·2H2O) form 8-coordinate polyhedra that incorporate four water molecules and four carboxylate oxygen atoms. 5 is isostructural to the Er-counterpart that was reported previously [36]. The unit cell parameters are in good agreement with those of the Er-analogue and show slightly shorter cell lengths. Compound 5 extends with infinite one-dimensional chains along the “c” direction and is the only ytterbium terephthalate of the series that displays interstitial water molecules. In the 1D chains four Yb-metal centers are linked by three terephthalate anions that are arranged in two different orientations. Within the “b” direction, coordinating water molecules connect to uncoordinated carboxylate oxygen atoms via hydrogen bonds, resulting in 2D layers within the “bc” plane. Individual layers are further tied together via hydrogen bonds along the “a” axis. In Fig. 7 we present this framework with view onto the “ac” plane. A total of four carboxylate oxygen atoms (purple and magenta) remain uncoordinated to Yb-metal centers and they are the recipients of hydrogen bonds from coordinating water molecules. Two of these are crystallographically identical oxygen atoms belonging to two carboxylate groups of one terephthalate unit (purple), while the other two are of a second type and belong to two other but equivalent terephthalate anions (magenta). The alignment of the terephthalate building blocks is less crowded, resulting in a less rigid arrangement of aromatic ring systems with fewer tensions placed on the terephthalate anions than in compounds 1, 2, and 3. This “more relaxed” form of arrangement is achieved through the restriction of extended linkage to one dimension and the larger number of uncoordinated carboxylate oxygen atoms (purple and magenta). Consequently, the carboxylate groups suffer a significantly less out-of-plane distortion with regard to the conjugated π-system. One type of terephthalate system has a maximum torsion angle of 19.5°, while the other type experiences only minor torsions between 1.1° and 5.8°.

These numbers are very similar to the Er-counterpart, ranging between 2.3° and 5.0° as well as 17.9° and 20.8° [36]. The difference in torsion angles between these two analogues is negligible, which can be attributed to the generous arrangements of the terephthalate entities in these 1D frameworks. In Fig. 8 we portray the one-dimensional chains in the “ab” plane after deleting the water molecules. Fig. 8a demonstrates that adjacent chains assemble to interwoven two dimensional networks within the “bc” plane that are held together via hydrogen bonds stemming from water molecules (not shown). Fig. 8b displays one of the infinite chains after deletion of water molecules, demonstrating the truly one dimensional nature of 5 and its Er-analogue [36].

Each chain is composed of two sub-chains of Yb-central atoms that are linked by terephthalate anions along the “c” axis.

The aromatic rings of these two chains are oriented in parallel fashion to each other. Furthermore, each of these parallel terephthalate anions coordinates in bi-dentate fashion to one of the two Yb3+ ions, while coordinating in mono-dentate fashion to the second Yb3+ ion, leaving one carboxylate oxygen atom uncoordinated (magenta) for each of these two equivalent terephthalate entities. In each link these two chains are connected via a third terephthalate unit. These aromatic entities are oriented perpendicularly towards the two parallel terephthalate anions, while aligning roughly along the “bc” diagonal. The diagonally linking terephthalate anions coordinate in exclusively mono-dentate fashion to the Yb-central atoms, leaving two carboxylate oxygen atoms (one on each side) uncoordinated (purple). These aromatic systems experience the lowest degree of tension, and thereby, exhibit the least out-of-plane torsion, not exceeding 19.5°. The terephthalate entities in the Er counterpart show quite similar torsion angles with a maximum angle of 20.8° for one type of terephthalate anion (slightly larger than in 5) and 5.0° for the second type (slightly smaller than in 5) [36].

It seems that in all Yb2(TP)3(H2O)x structures that we synthesized, as well as in the related Ln compounds already known, the tension buildup on the aromatic π-systems and the rigid nature of the terephthalate spacing units, is counteracted via; (1) varying levels of out-of-plane torsion; (2) one or more carboxylate oxygen atoms remaining uncoordinated; (3) addition of water molecules to the coordination sphere; and (4) variation of the dimensionality of the framework lattices. In fact, the most similar structure as compared to 1 and 2 can be recognized in Yb2(TP)3(H2O)2 (3), forming a 3D network with two water molecules as part of the coordination sphere in Seg1 and excluding one carboxylate oxygen atom from the YbO7 coordination polyhedron. At the same time all terephthalate oxygen atoms are part of the coordination sphere of the YbO6 polyhedron in Seg2, with no coordinating water molecules present. Even with the uncoordinated carboxylate oxygen atom the tension on the aromatic spacer entities is more intense than for 1 and 2 as well as for 4 and 5. Thus, this results in the greatest out-of-plane torsion observed for any compounds presented in this manuscript. As a matter of fact, since the coordination sphere in Seg2 appears significantly more constricted than in Seg1, out of torsion angles are significantly larger in Seg2 (15.3–38.0°) as compared to Seg1 (11.6–25.8°). Yb2(TP)3(H2O)6 (4) shows a more relaxed arrangement of the aromatic ligands as the structure is reduced to a 2D network, in which also one carboxylate oxygen atom is excluded from the YbO8 coordination polyhedron that incorporates three water molecules. Out-of-plane torsion of carboxylate groups is less intense than in 3 and slightly increased as compared to 2. Its Er-counterpart shows, however, significantly smaller torsion angles [36]. We attribute this to the constrained arrangement of terephthalate units in these two compounds that do not experience as much rotational freedom as compared to the terephthalate anions in the 1D networks. Yb2(TP)3(H2O)8·2H2O (5) suffers the least strain on its terephthalate linking units, as it arranges in 1D chains, and excludes altogether three carboxylate oxygen atoms from the YbO8 coordination polyhedron (2 crystallographically identical oxygen atoms on one TP unit and 1 oxygen atom on another TP unit), while accommodating four water molecules. The level of torsion experienced by the carboxylate groups in this compound is the lowest among complexes 1–5. The torsion angles in its Er-counterpart deviate only slightly and can be neglected. The hybrid compound, Yb4(TP)5(SO4)(H2O)16, integrates both, inorganic and organic ligand systems and assembles as a three-dimensional network with less out-of-plane torsions as observed for complexes 1–5. This compound will be further described elsewhere.

4. Conclusions

We applied hydrothermal synthesis methods to produce an array of lanthanide open frameworks incorporating terephthalate ligand systems. The chosen reaction temperature of 170 °C was appropriate for the production of X-ray quality single crystals. Comparing the structural properties of these compounds allows for an interesting insight into the topologies of the resulting extended 3D, 2D, and 1D-frameworks. We demonstrated that materials with rigid spacer units, such as terephthalate anions, seem to alleviate tension buildup induced by reduced ionic radii more efficiently than purely inorganic lanthanide mixed ligand frameworks of high rigidity. Comparing the structural properties of our compounds with some related compounds in the literature suggests that this mechanism is likely based on the ability of aromatic spacer systems to undergo significant out-of-plane distortion without complete disruption of the conjugate π-system. We observed out-of-plane distortions up to 38° for the most obstructed frameworks such as Yb2(TP)3(H2O)2 (3) with 38.0°, and Yb2(TP)3(H2O)6 (4) with 33.4°. For the terephthalate system in 3 with out-of-plane torsions of 38.0° for both carbonyl groups one would certainly expect that the conjugation is limited to the arene ring only. The π-conjugation can be viewed as disrupted beyond torsion angles of 35–40° [50]. Moreover, the level of hydration in concert with the dimensionality of these networks seems to govern the number of uncoordinated carboxylate oxygen atoms, and ultimately the degree of out-of-plane distortion of carboxylate groups.

Frameworks that assemble exclusively from Ln3+-cations and terephthalate units exhibit quite constrained topologies of low symmetry and with limited porosity. Beyond the structures incorporating exclusively terephthalate entities we expect that it is possible to obtain open frameworks with higher levels of porosity and higher symmetry as we will demonstrate in a future discussion.

Our current efforts focus on the design of novel ligand systems that integrate the flexibility of spacer units like the glutarate anion and the rigidity of entities similar to the terephthalate building blocks simultaneously. To achieve this goal we are developing synthetic pathways for the creation of bisphenol derivatives that incorporate butanoic and pentanoic acid groups as attachments to the bisphenol oxygen atoms. We anticipate that various lanthanide cations will combine with these ligand systems under the formation of novel MO materials.
