Unusual coordination of a chloride ion to six copper ions: Synthesis and crystal structure of copper(II) complexes with 4-(4-hydroxyphenyl)-1,2,4-triazole

Author links open overlay panel
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.poly.2006.11.051Get rights and content

Abstract

Novel copper(II) complexes, molecular [Cu6(μ6-Cl)(μ3-OH)2(μ-L)6Cl9(H2O)3] · 3H2O (1) and polymeric [Cu(μ-L)(μ-OH)(H2O)2]Cl (2) (L = 4-(4-hydroxyphenyl)-1,2,4-triazole), have been prepared and characterized by X-ray structural analysis. Compound 1 appears to be an unusual example of a chloride ion with six equal Cu–Cl distances of 2.8397(3) Å. It has also been characterized by X-ray powder diffraction and magnetic measurements. Both complexes have distorted octahedral configurations of copper ions; the coordination cores are CuN2Cl2O2 or CuN2Cl3O (1) and CuN2O4 (2).

Graphical abstract

Novel copper(II) complexes with substituted 1,2,4-triazole, molecular and polymeric, have been prepared and characterized. The molecular complex shows interesting coordination of Cl− to six copper ions with all six Cu–Cl bonds equal to 2.8397(3) Å. It shows mainly antiferromagnetic exchange interactions between the Cu2+ centers.

1. Introduction

1,2,4-Triazoles are widely used ligands in coordination chemistry. They can be coordinated to a metal ion in different modes, bidentate-bridging or monodentate, depending on the position and the nature of the substituent at the triazole ring [1]. If the N1 and N2 positions of the triazole ring are not substituted they can coordinate metal ions forming bridges (the so-called N1,N2 coordination mode). This mode leads to polynuclear complexes that often demonstrate magnetic exchange interactions [2], [3], [4]. The coordination chemistry of 1,2,4-triazole and its N4 derivatives is of strong interest because they are found to form complexes with iron(II) that show the spin crossover phenomenon 1A1 ⇔ 5T2 [5], [6], [7], [8] associated with cooperative interactions between the metal centers [6]. The spin transition is usually accompanied by thermochromism (colour change pink ⇔ white). This phenomenon can be used for various applications, attracting additional attention to these complexes [9]. Moreover, 1,2,4-triazoles can be used in bioinorganic chemistry [10], [11], [12] as precursors for photographic materials [13] and as radioprotective compounds [14]. The coordination chemistry of OH-substituted derivatives of 1,2,4-triazoles has been poorly investigated. Recently we have reported the synthesis and study of coordination compounds of Co(II), Ni(II) and Cu(II) with 1-(4-hydroxyphenyl)-1H-1,2,4-triazole [15]. These complexes are mononuclear due to monodentate coordination of the ligand. The present contribution deals with the synthesis, X-ray structure and magnetic properties of new copper(II) coordination compounds with 4-(4-hydroxyphenyl)-1,2,4-triazole.

2. Results and discussion

We have found that the reaction of copper(II) chloride with 4-(4-hydroxyphenyl)-1,2,4-triazole (L, C8H7N3O, Scheme 1) in EtOH solution with a Cu:L molar ratio of 5:1 leads to formation of two crystalline complexes [Cu6(μ6-Cl)(μ3-OH)2(μ-L)6Cl9(H2O)3] · 3H2O (1) and [Cu(μ-L)(μ-OH)(H2O)2]Cl (2). Complex 1 is the main product of the reaction:6Cu2++6L+12Cl-+8H2O→[Cu6(μ6-Cl)(μ3-OH)2(μ-L)6Cl9(H2O)3]·3H2O+2HCl

Despite a 1:1 metal-to-ligand stoichiometry in 1 the five times excess of CuCl2 is strongly recommended to avoid precipitating the low soluble ligand.

We found conditions when complex 1 can be prepared pure in high yield, and investigated its magnetic properties. Complex 1 is stable in the ambient air and is readily soluble in water and water–organic mixtures, but poorly soluble in organic solvents (ethanol, MeCN, 2-propanol, CHCl3) and their mixtures. Unfortunately, our attempts to prepare a phase of the individual compound 2 failed.

2.1. Crystal structures

In both compounds the L molecules are coordinated to copper ions by N1 and N2 atoms in a bidentate-bridging manner. However, the structures of 1 and 2 are different due to the difference in the coordination mode of the hydroxo-group and the role of chloride anion. In compound 1 there are three types of bridging ligands. Three bidentate-bridging 4-(4-hydroxyphenyl)-1,2,4-triazole molecules are coordinated to three copper ions to form Cu3L3 triangle. The OH-group caps the Cu3 triangle, and finally two Cu3L3(OH) triangles are joined to one another in quite an unusual way, by the μ6-Cl ligand (Fig. 1) to form a hexanuclear complex. The distance between Cu3 planes is 4.21 Å. The coordination polyhedron of the μ6-Cl ion is trigonal antiprism. The resulting complex molecule lies on the fixed point of the 3¯ axis of the R3¯ space group. Each copper ion has two coordination positions, equatorial and apical, available for terminal ligands. All six equatorial positions are occupied by chloride ions, whereas the apical ones are statistically occupied by three water molecules and three chloride ions. The statistical distribution of H2O and Cl over the six apical positions is imposed by relatively high crystallographic symmetry. It only became possible to distinguish the positions of Cl (Cu–Clapical, 2.7774(19) Å) and H2O (Cu–Oapical, 2.466(7) Å) and to locate H atoms at O of the H2O molecules due to the low temperature at the diffraction measurements. Thus, the coordination sphere of Cu2+ consists of 2N + μ3-O(H) + Cl + μ6-Cl + (Cl or H2O). The coordination polyhedron of the copper ion is a distorted octahedron elongated towards μ6-Cl and (Cl/H2O)apical positions (Fig. 1).

According to the statistical distribution of H2O and Cl− over six apical positions, four geometrical and optical isomers of molecule 1 (Scheme 2, view along 3¯ axis.) may theoretically coexist, but cannot be recognized in the crystal by using X-ray diffraction data.

The most symmetrical isomer, I, is shown in Fig. 1, where all “upper” apical positions are occupied by three water molecules and the “lower” ones by chloride ions. It is achiral because of C3v point symmetry. Two isomers with lower symmetry correspond to the combination of 2H2O + Cl− on one Cu3 triangle and H2O + 2Cl− on the other. One of them, II, possesses a mirror plane and therefore is achiral too, while the last, III, is asymmetrical and exists as the pair of chiral forms, IIIa and IIIb. However, the nature of the 6Cu–Cl bonding is unclear, we could even expect free rotation of one Cu3L3(OH) triangle with respect to the other. The free rotation might occur, for example, in solution, if the molecule 1 does not dissociate. In this case only two isomers, I and II, would exist. The existence of an eclipsed conformation in this case might also be possible.

The main bond distances are given in Fig. 1 caption. The μ6-Cl-ligand is equidistant from six copper ions. Relatively large Cu–(μ6-Cl) distances of 2.8397(3) Å indicate that the two Cu3 units are weakly joined together. According to the Cambridge Structural Database (release 5.27) [16] the only one copper(II) complex containing μ6-Cl is known, [Cu6(μ6-Cl)(μ3-OMe)2(μ-pz)9] (3; pz – pyrazolate anion) [17]. In 3, in contrast to 1, two Cu3 triangles are joined not only by a chloride anion but also by three pyrazolate ligands; all are coordinated to Cu in a bidentate-bridging mode. The resulting coordination environment of the μ6-Cl− is trigonal prism, in contrast to trigonal antiprism in 1. The presence of additional bridging pyrazolate ligands resulted in a shortening of the Cu–Cl bonds to 2.6032(13)–2.623(2) Å. In 3 the chloride ion is “caged” into the molecule while in 1 it binds two parts of the molecule together. Note that in all possible isomers of 1 two Cu3 triangles are not equivalent but all six (μ6-Cl)–Cu bonds are of the same length. We carefully checked the residual density around the chloride ion and its a.d.p. ellipsoid shape, and came to the conclusion that it is definitely ordered. It could be an argument pro covalent and not electrostatic Cu–Cl bonding.

In the crystal packing, the molecules of 1 are involved in extended network of intermolecular hydrogen bonds between terminal OH-groups of the 4-(4-hydroxyphenyl)-1,2,4-triazole ligand, apical water molecules coordinated to copper and disordered solvent water molecules.

In compound 2 the hydroxo groups together with L join copper ions into infinite cationic {[Cu(μ-L)(μ-OH)(H2O)2]+}∞ polymeric chains (Fig. 2) running along the b axis. The chloride anion is in the outer sphere. The coordination environment of Cu2+ ions is accomplished by two water molecules and consists of 2N + 2μ-O(H) + 2 elongated O(H2), thus the coordination polyhedron of the copper ion is a distorted octahedron, as in 1. There is weak hydrogen bonding between the chloride counter anion and the hydroxo group of L, Cl ⋯ O(H), 3.01 Å.

There are about 20 compounds containing the fragment [Cu(μ-L′)(μ-OH)], where L′ is a substituted N-containing heterocycle, coordinated through the N–N bond in a bidentate bridging manner similarly to 2. All but three of them are molecular, while three chain structures achieve a polymeric motif through neither NO3 group [18], coordinated to the copper ion, nor through the oxygen atom of an OH-group, being additionally coordinated to one more copper ion [19], [20] with an elongated contact Cu–O of about 2.7–2.8 Å. However, most similar to 2, the compounds contain a cyclic tetranuclear anion {Cu4(μ-OH)2Cl4(μ-pz)4}2− (4 [21]) and an octanuclear molecule {Cu(μ-OH)(μ-Me2pz)}8 (5 [22], [23]). The copper ions in 4, 5 and 2 are joined through two bridging ligands, N-containing heterocycle and OH-groups, resulting in cyclic species in 4, 5 and polymeric chains in 2. The Cu ⋯ Cu distances in 2 are longer by ∼0.1 Å and can be compared only with those in the Cu–O(H) ⋯ Cu fragment in 4, where one of the copper ions weakly coordinates the OH group (Table 1). However, a rather long Cu ⋯ Cu distance leads to Cu–O(H) and Cu–N bonds also becoming longer. Besides, the coordination environment of the copper ions, being distorted octahedral in 2, also differs from that in 4 and 5. In 4 there is no additional coordination to the copper ion, the coordination polyhedra of all copper ions are distorted tetrahedra, while in 5 there are copper ions in tetrahedral and in trigonal prismatic environments.

The experimental powder diffraction pattern of 1 (Fig. 3) is in good agreement with that calculated for compound 1. Some differences between intensity profiles may be accounted for by the texture of the sample. The presence of peaks referring to any additional phase (e.g. phase 2) is not observed. This fact clearly indicates that the bulk product synthesized corresponds to phase 1 only.

2.2. Magnetic properties

The dependence of the magnetic moment (μeff) on temperature of 1 is shown in Fig. 4. On decreasing the temperature from 300 K, the μeff value decreases continuously down to a plateau near 80 K. Further decrease of the temperature causes a decrease of μeff to 1.75 β at 2 K. Such a behavior points to mainly antiferromagnetic exchange interactions in complex 1. The structural data shown in Fig. 1 were used to choose a theoretical model for a description of the dependence observed. Thus, we chose the most adequate model for the description of the magnetic properties, consisting of two triangles joined by a μ6-Cl ion. We considered the model of the exchange cluster consisting of two weakly interacting triangles. The model is valid because the distances between copper(II) ions of different triangles bonded through μ6-Cl ion are 5.73 Å and are much larger than the distances between copper(II) ions within the same triangle. The model assumes that the value of the exchange interaction parameter J between Cu(II) ions inside triangles is much larger than that of both inter-cluster exchange interactions for the same molecule and other molecules (zJ′).

The theoretical analysis of the magnetic behavior was carried out in cluster approximation with the isotropic spin-Hamiltonian (H^=-2J∑iS^i·S^J) [25]. As the result of optimization the following parameters for the theoretic curve (solid line in Fig. 4) were found: gCu = 2.10(5), J = −51.4(3) cm−1, zJ′ = −2.3(2) cm−1.

A comparison of magnetic properties of complexes 1 and 3 shows that the values of the exchange interaction parameters between Cu(II) ions inside copper triangles for these complexes are comparable (−51.4(3) cm−1 in complex 1 (this paper) and −66.4(1) cm−1 in complex 3 [17]). However, inter-cluster exchange interactions differ considerably: zJ′ = −2.3(2) cm−1 (1) and J2 = −17.1(1) cm−1 (3). This is expected to be due to structural reasons. The Cu3 units in complex 3 are bonded more strongly than in complex 1 due to the three bridging pyrazolates between them. In 1 only the μ6-Cl ion bridges similar Cu3 units.

3. Conclusions

Thus, the reaction between copper(II) chloride and 4-(4-hydroxyphenyl)-1,2,4-triazole leads to the formation of two crystalline complexes, hexanuclear 1 and polymeric 2. The 1,2,4-triazole ring coordinates in a bidentate-bridging mode. One of complexes, 1, with an unusual molecular structure is obtained as the bulk product. Two Cu3 triangles appear to be joined together by six unsupported Cu–Cl bonds of length 2.8397(3) Ǻ. We think that this crystal structure could be of importance for structural chemistry because it elucidates some aspects of weak chemical bonding between metal and ligand atoms. Traditional structural chemistry treats long interatomic contacts as a sort of non-bonding interaction (specific, electrostatic, etc.). In our opinion, we found an example of bonding that lies somewhere between a “normal” covalent bond and specific non-valent bonding. The argument for some covalent nature to this bonding is that the chloride ion symmetrically binds non-equivalent Cu3 units. The argument for the specific non-bonding nature is that the magnetic exchange between those units is quite small. However, the nature of this bonding is still open. We hope that quantum chemical calculations that we plan in the future will help.

4. Experimental

4.1. General

For the synthesis, we used CuCl2 · 3H2O of “p” grade and 4-(4-hydroxyphenyl)-1,2,4-triazole was prepared by the procedure in [24], Tm.p. = 288–290 °C (Tm.p. = 285–286 °C [24]).

4.2. Synthesis of [Cu6(μ6-Cl)(μ3-OH)2(μ-L)6Cl9(H2O)3] · 3H2O (1)

A hot solution of L (1 mmol) in 20 ml of ethanol was slowly added with stirring to a hot solution of CuCl2 · 2H2O (5 mmol) in 5 ml of water. A green precipitate was formed after a short time (ca. 1 min). After stirring for 30 min the precipitate was filtered off, washed with small amount of ethanol and dried in ambient air. Yield 85%.

Anal. Calcd for C48H56N18Cl10O14Cu6: C, 31.2; H, 3.1; N, 13.7; Cu, 20.7. Found: C, 31.2; H, 3.0; N, 13.3; Cu, 20.6.

Green crystals of 1 suitable for X-ray analysis were obtained by slow evaporation of an ethanol solution of the compound over two weeks.

With a greater excess of copper chloride against L, both green crystals of 1 and blue crystals of the complex 2 suitable for X-ray analysis were formed from a solution after leaving in air for two weeks. Unfortunately, we did not succeed in isolating the individual phase of compound 2 without impurities of compound 1.

4.3. Physical measurements

The copper content was determined by complexonometric titration, the samples being decomposed in a 1:1 mixture of concentrated H2SO4 and HClO4. The CHN analyses were performed with a Carlo-Erba analyzer using standard procedures.

4.4. Magnetic measurements

Magnetic susceptibility measurements of polycrystalline samples of the complex were performed on a SQUID magnetometer MPMS-5s (Quantum Design) over the temperature range 2–300 K. The magnitudes of the magnetic field were 5 kOe. Dependence between magnetic susceptibility and intensity of the magnetic field was not found. The effective magnetic moment μeff was calculated by the formula μeff=3kNβ2·χT1/2≈(8χT)1/2, where χ is the paramagnetic susceptibility corrected for diamagnetism and is equal to −870 × 10−6 cm3/mol, N is Avogadro’s number, k is the Boltzsmann constant and β is Bohr’s magneton.

4.5. X-ray crystallographic study

Crystallographic data and details of single-crystal diffraction experiments are given in Table 2. X-ray intensity data were collected on a Bruker X8Apex CCD diffractometer at 90 K (1) and room temperature (2) using standard techniques (ω- and ϕ-scans of narrow frames) and corrected for absorption effects [26]. The structures were solved by direct methods and refined by full-matrix least-squares on F2 using the shelxtl program set (version 6.22) [27]. All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. Hydrogen atoms at OH-groups and H2O molecules were refined without geometrical constraints and with thermal parameters related to those of the oxygen atoms. Hydrogen atoms at carbon atoms were refined in calculated positions. In structure 2 the hydrogen atoms of the water molecule coordinated to copper were not located. The solvent water molecule in 1 appeared to be statistically disordered over two symmetrically related positions and refined with s.o.f. = 0.5.
