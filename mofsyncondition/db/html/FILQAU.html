Introduction

Metal–organic-frameworks (MOFs) are a very promising class of materials for their wide applications in the areas of optics, catalysis, magnetism, gas adsorption and electrophysics because of their unique structures and properties.1 With regards to lanthanide metal–organic-frameworks (LnMOF), they have attracted increasing attention in tunable luminescent materials,2 upconversion emission,3 time-resolved microscopy4 and luminescent sensors1e,5 due to the “antenna effect” of the ligand.6 Lanthanide complexes have been explored as luminescent sensor materials to sense many chemical species, such as CO32−, HSO4−, OH−, H+, Ag+, Zn2+, Cu2+, Mg2+, halide ions, acetone, explosives, proteins and antibiotics, and also have important applications in environmental and biological applications.3d,7 Though many species could be detected by different materials and some species can be quantified, to the best of our knowledge, detecting species with MOFs by a handy UV banknote lamp has not been reported. Since the UV banknote lamp is convenient to transport, it is very appropriate to perform outdoors. Furthermore, the miniaturization of detection instruments is necessary for real usage.

Hg2+ is among the most toxic metal ions, which is harmful to humans and it can cause many diseases.8 Thus, the sensing of Hg2+ in the environment and biology systems is very important. The present detecting methods seem to be time-consuming and detection at a wild field scene is not easy to perform.9

As the insoluble micro-sized MOF materials are usually not homogeneous in solutions,10 there is a growing number of research on methods to prepare MOF thin films or coatings, composites and nanoparticles.10 Until recently, MOF films could be made by several ways, such as MOFs at metal/SAM interfaces (SAM = self-assembled monolayers);11 MOFs at oxide interfaces;12 MOFs at polymer interfaces;13 MOFs at carbon interfaces;14 MOFs at biopolymer interfaces;15 MOFs at MOF interfaces;16 MOFs at liquid crystal interfaces.17 But these MOF films are almost always based on transition metals and cannot satisfy all the applications in industry or sensing usage. So, a new strategy for making mechanically robust MOF thin films should be devoted to satisfying more applications. Moreover, LnMOFs films are seldom documented.

In our previous studies, some luminescent lanthanide complexes and their sensing properties have been reported.7b,18 Recently, we paid particular attention to LnMOF films based on the ligand 2,2′-biquinoline-4,4′-dicarboxylate (bqdc2−), as bqdc2− has an intense absorption band in the near-UV region (about 20 000 M−1 cm−1) and has a good antenna effect for Eu3+.19 Since the “hard acid” of Ln3+ is not affinitive to the “soft base” N atom, when bqdc2− reacts with Ln3+, the pyridyl N atoms in bqdc2− are always uncoordinated.20 These preserved pyridyl N sites could interact with other metal ions and lead to luminescence intensity changes of Ln3+.21

Inspired by the aforementioned considerations and our previous work on lanthanide complexes,7b,18,22 herein, a new highly luminescent porous LnMOF {[Eu2(bqdc)3(H2O)(DMF)3]·0.5DMF·H2O}n (1) was synthesized. 1 was characterized by single-crystal diffraction, powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD), fourier transform infrared (FT-IR), thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) and elemental analysis (EA). Based on 1, a new strategy for fabricating a novel durable thin film of 1 was developed. The strategy is a convenient manner of using electrodeposition in combination with subsequent solvothermal synthesis and this technique is feasible for most metal coordination polymers. The film of 1 exhibits a highly selective response to Hg2+ and could be used for sensing Hg2+ conveniently. The sensor is a device prototype for Hg2+ detection. Novel MOF devices for the easy detection of various ions or small molecules can be obtained by modifying this device prototype. Studies on the diffusion of Hg2+ into 1 have provided direct and quantitative information on the performance of MOF materials. The fluorescent lanthanide film was first used to monitor the permeation of ions into the MOF films.

Experimental section

Materials and methods

All the chemicals were commercially available and used without further purification. Luminescence spectra were recorded using a RF-5301PC spectrofluorophotometer at room temperature. The fluorescence quantum yield was determined by a FLSP920 combined fluorescence lifetime and steady state spectrometer with a 450 W Xe lamp and integrating sphere at room temperature. Powder X-ray diffractionspectra were obtained by a D-MAX 2200VPC diffractometer with Cu–Kα radiation. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was performed under flowing nitrogen on a Netzsch/TG209 instrument at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. The morphologies of the samples on ITO supports were evaluated with a Quanta 400F thermal field emission environmental scanning electron microscope. The morphology of the powdered sample was characterized with a JSM-6330F field emission scanning electron microscope. Single crystal X-ray data were collected on a Bruker SMART 1000 CCD diffractometer with a Mo–Kα (λ = 0.71073 Å) graphite monochromator at T = 173(2) K. The single crystal structure was refined by full-matrix least-squares methods with the SHELXL-97 module.23 Anisotropic thermal parameters were applied to all non-hydrogen atoms. Hydrogen atoms were generated geometrically.

Preparation of single crystals of 1

A solution of EuCl3·6H2O (0.0183 g, 0.05 mmol) in 5 mL of DMF was allowed to diffuse slowly into a sodium silicate gel (pH = 6, adjusted by concentrated HCl) containing Na2bqdc (0.0310 g, 0.08 mmol) in a 15 mL test tube. After three months, single crystals of 1 were obtained in the gel. Anal. calcd (%): C, 52.18; H, 3.634. Found (%): C, 52.21; H, 3.632. FT-IR (KBr pellet, cm−1): 3390 (s), 1649 (s), 1610 (s), 1537 (s), 1431 (s), 1387 (s), 1281 (w), 1257 (w), 1207 (w), 1153 (w), 1072 (w), 1026 (w), 908 (w), 885 (w), 812 (m), 773 (m), 754 (m), 667 (m), 544 (w), 467 (w).

Preparation of nanocrystals of 1

1 mL of H2O and 9 mL of DMF was added to Na2bqdc (0.0388 g, 0.1 mmol), resulting in a clear solution. The solution was added dropwise to a mixture of EuCl3·6H2O (0.0256 g, 0.07 mmol), 9 mL of DMF and 1 mL of H2O under stirring in 3 h. The white powder of 1 was collected by centrifugation and then washed with DMF (3 × 3 mL) and dried at 80 °C for one day.

Electrodeposition of the Eu(OH)3 coating

The Eu(OH)3 coatings were fabricated according to the literature with minor modifications.24 The coatings were electrodeposited onto ITO glass slices (0.7 × 2 cm2) galvanostatically (current density, 2 mA cm−2) by a constant potential rectifier. The deposition process was carried out in a two-electrode cell (volume, 25 mL) containing a 0.05 M Eu(NO3)3 aqueous solution. ITO slices were used as the working electrode (cathode) and a 5 mm diameter columniform graphite rod was employed as the anode. After 10 minutes of electrolysis, a white Eu(OH)3 coating grew on the ITO electrode.

Preparation of the film of 1

Na2bqdc (0.023 g, 0.06 mmol) was dispersed in 6 mL of DMF, 3 drops of 0.5 M HCl was added, resulting in a clear solution. To a 6 mL DMF solution of EuCl3·6H2O (0.015 g, 0.04 mmol), the Na2bqdc solution was added. After that, a Eu(OH)3 coated ITO slice was placed vertically into the solution. The solution and the ITO slice were sealed in a 50 mL Teflon-lined stainless-steel autoclave and heated at 80 °C for 3 days. After cooling, a white coating of 1 grew on the Eu(OH)3 coating. The resulting film was rinsed with DMF and then immersed in a mixed solvent of DMF–H2O (v/v = 1 : 1) before being used to detect Hg2+.

Results and discussion

Single crystal X-ray analysis revealed that 1 features a 2D network of {[Eu2(bqdc)3(H2O)(DMF)3]·0.5DMF·H2O}n (Fig. 1). It belongs to the triclinic space group P (no. 2), a = 11.122(1), b = 11.938(1), c = 14.184(2) Å, α = 74.869(2), β = 76.480(2), γ = 73.859(2)°, V = 1719.8(4) Å3 and the structure is different from the structure reported by Zhang.25 The difference may due to the various synthesis methods. 1 is constructed by dinuclear second building units (SBUs). Each SBU consists of two equivalent Eu3+ (Fig. 2) and each Eu3+ is coordinated by nine oxygen atoms to result in a EuO9 unit, where one O atom comes from coordinated water, one O atom comes from coordinated DMF and the other seven O atoms are from carboxylic oxygen. The nine O aroms around Eu3+ are arranged in a distorted tri-capped triangular prism (Fig. 3). The ligands have two coordination modes of μ4-η1-η1-η1-η1 and μ3-η2-η1-η1-η1 (Fig. 4). Eu3+ are bridged by these two types of bqdc2− to construct a 2D layer. The 2D layers are packed together by van der Waals forces and aromatic π–π interactions to consolidate into a 3D structure. The stacking of the layers results in channels along the c axis between adjacent layers (Fig. S1, ESI†). Selected bond lengths and angles are listed in Table S1 in the ESI.†

PXRD proves that the as-synthesized nanocrystal's peaks are identical to the single crystal X-ray data of 1 (Fig. 5a and b). The broad reflections and low reflection intensities of the pattern suggest the presence of crystals with small sizes.24b

Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA)

The TGA results (Fig. 6) show 1 loses a lattice water, half a crystalline DMF, a coordinated H2O and a terminal DMF (8.96% weight loss, calculated: 8.98%) from 20 to 201 °C.

Between 201 and 413 °C, one coordinated DMF is removed with a weight loss of 4.51% (calculated: 4.50%). Further heating leads to the loss of the last coordinated DMF and the network starts to decompose. The TGA analysis result indicates 1 is a thermostable material, which is comparable to two other recently reported highly thermostable MOFs.26

Luminescence of 1

The maximal excitation wavelength of a 1.33 mg mL−11 DMF emulsion is at 368 nm. When excited at 368 nm, the emission bands centred at 581, 594, 621, 653, 700 nm can be seen, which correspond to 5D0 → 7Fn (n = 0 → 4) transitions of Eu3+, respectively (Fig. 7).27 Among them, the symmetric forbidden emission band of 5D0 → 7F0 around 581 nm is almost invisible, the medium-strong emission around 594 nm is attributed to the magnetic dipolar 5D0 → 7F1 transition and the strongest emission band around 621 nm is ascribed to the electric-dipolar 5D0 → 7F2 transition. Moreover, the weak emission peaks at 653 nm (5D0 → 7F3) and 700 nm (5D0 → 7F4) also correspond to magnetic dipole transitions. The electric-dipolar 5D0 → 7F2 transition is hypersensitive to the coordination environment of Eu3+, while the magnetic dipolar 5D0 → 7F1 transition is fairly insensitive to it. In this study, the intensity of the 5D0 → 7F2 transition is similar to 5D0 → 7F1, indicating that the Eu3+ is in a centro-symmetric coordination environment, which is in good agreement with the result of the single-crystal structure. The fluorescence quantum yield of 1 is as high as 19%. The emission of 1 is very strong for the intense absorption of the bqdc2−ligand in the near-UV region.

Fabrication of the sensing materials

The SEM image of nanocrystals of 1 (Fig. 8) looks like micaceous leaves and the diameter of the “leaf” is 200–400 nm with a thickness of 10–30 nm. To examine the application of 1 in sensing metal ions, nanocrystals of 1 were dispersed in DMF which contained various metal ions. This is similar to the method reported by Chen.28 After 24 h, the luminescence of 1nanocrystal emulsion was measured (Fig. S2, ESI†). It demonstrated that 1 had a highly selective response to Hg2+. To the best of our knowledge, the sensitivity of the emulsion of 1 to Hg2+ is the highest in LnMOF sensors. This may due to the fact that Hg2+ diffuses into the channels of the framework of 1 and coordinates to the pyridyl atom on bqdc2−. The coordinated Hg2+ interfered with the energy transfer from the antenna of bqdc2− to Eu3+, resulting in the quenching of the Eu3+ luminescence. An analysis of the emissions of H2bqdc and its complexes with Hg2+, Cd2+ and Zn2+ found that the emission intensity of the complex formed by bqdc and Hg2+ is much lower than that of bqdc and its complexes with Cd2+ and Zn2+ (Fig. S3, ESI†). This means Hg2+ interferes with the excited state of the bqdc ligand, which may cut down the energy transferring from bqdc to Eu3+ in 1 and then quench luminescence of Eu3+. The minor difference between the emission spectra of bqdc and its complexes with Cd2+ and Zn2+ may come from the emission of these complexes themselves.

When repeating the experiments for detecting Hg2+ with the above emulsion method, it was found that the reproducibility was unsatisfactory. This might be due to the bad dispersity and/or the stability of the powder solution. In order to get closer to real usage, we took an interest in preparing LnMOF films, which is a kind of facile sensor.10 At the beginning, the film of 1 was deposited directly onto the ITO glass with a hydrothermal method and PXRD proved the film was complex 1 (Fig. 5c). The detecting effect for Hg2+ was very good and the reproducibility had been improved. Unfortunately, the film easily dropped off the ITO glass during usage and the surface of the film of 1 on ITO was jagged.

Though crystals of 1 can't adhere to common substrates tightly, the ligand may combine with Ln3+ of a Ln(OH)3 coating and the Ln(OH)3 coating could bind to ITO tightly.24a So, 1 was devised to deposit onto the Ln(OH)3 coating. Nearly six months' efforts were addressed: we got the mechanically robust thin film of 1 successfully. The preparation of the film of 1 consists of two steps. Firstly, a Eu(OH)3 coating was deposited onto an ITO slice by electrolysis of a Eu(NO3)3 solution.24 The Eu(OH)3 film would not drop off in water over several weeks, indicating that the film combined with the ITO glass very firmly. An SEM image shows the film is uniform (Fig. 9a). The leaf-like crystals of Eu(OH)3 grow on the support to form a well-packed coating with a porous surface morphology. The diameter of the “leaf” is 1–2 μm with a thickness of 10–40 nm. The thickness of the Eu(OH)3 layer is about 3 μm (Fig. 9b). PXRD confirmed the composition of this film is Eu(OH)3 (Fig. S4, ESI†). Secondly, the film of 1 was fabricated on the Eu(OH)3 coating by a solvothermal synthesis at 80 °C for 3 days. The porous surface of the Eu(OH)3 coating facilitates nucleation and strengthens the adhesion of crystals of 1. By this strong interaction, crystals of 1 anchor onto the Eu(OH)3 coating tightly to form a film of 1 which is also stable in water over several weeks. The pillar-like and block-like crystals of 1 grow on the surface of the Eu(OH)3 to form another well-packed coating (Fig. 10). The size of the crystal of 1 is 0.5–5 μm. The thickness of the coating of 1 is about 10 μm. A PXRD experiment proved that composition of the film is identical to complex 1 (Fig. 5d). When the film of 1 was immersed in water solutions with pH from 3 to 9, the PXRD patterns of the film of 1 match well with the pattern calculated from the data of the single-crystal of 1 (Fig. S5, ESI†), which shows the film of 1 has good pH stability. Since electrodeposition is a facile method for the preparation of metal oxide and hydroxide coatings, solvothermal synthesis is a powerful technique for assembling MOFs. By this two-step combined strategy, we have also prepared a number of mechanically robust, smooth and homogeneous LnMOF thin films with different metal ions or ligands (Fig. S6, ESI†).

The high-performance sensing for Hg2+ of the film of 1 was experimentally confirmed. After the film of 1 was immersed in a 1 × 10−3 M Hg2+ DMF solution for 4 hours, the strongest emission of Eu3+ (5D0 → 7F2, 621 nm) was almost quenched (Fig. S7, ESI†). The reproducibility is very good and the film did not drop off from the substrate. This indicates the film of 1 is more suitable to be used as a sensor than in the powder or emulsion form. However, Na+, K+, Mg2+, Ca2+, Zn2+ and Cd2+ at 1 × 10−3 M have no significant effect on the luminescent intensity of 1 (Fig. 11 and S7†).

Furthermore, the film of 1 immersed in Hg2+ for 4 h shows no luminescence under a handy UV banknote detector with excitation in daylight (Fig. 12) or under the lab UV lamp (Fig. S8, ESI†), but in a blank solution or in a 1 × 10−3 M Na+, K+, Mg2+, Ca2+, Zn2+ and Cd2+ solution, the red luminescence can be clearly seen when excited by a handy UV banknote detector. Since the difference in the luminescence before and after immersion in Hg2+ solutions can be easily discriminated by the naked eye, this film of 1 can be used as a simple sensor, coupled with a handy UV cash detector to detect Hg2+ conveniently.

In order to investigate the optimal exposure time of the film of 1 to Hg2+ and the possible relationship between amount of Hg2+ and number of binding sites within the framework of the film of 1, the time-dependent emission intensity of the film of 1 in the presence of various amounts of Hg2+ at 298 K was monitored (Fig. 13). When the film of 1 was immersed in Hg2+ (1 × 10−3 M, the number of binding sites are nearly 7 times as much as the amount of Hg2+) for 4 h, the luminescence almost disappeared. After the introduction of Hg2+ at 1 × 10−4 and 1 × 10−5 M (the number of binding sites are 70 and 700 times, respectively, as much as the amount of Hg2+), both the luminescence intensities of the film of 1 decreased as a function of time in the early 8 h and then reached stable states within 12 h. The emission intensities of the film of 1 reduced by 48% (the ratio of the number of binding sites to the amount of Hg2+ is 70) and 12% (the ratio of the number of binding sites to the amount of Hg2+ is 700) when the stable states were reached. The luminescence intensity did not change when the film of 1 was immersed in a contrast solvent (no metal ions added). The relation between the emission intensity of the film of 1 immersed in Hg2+ and the denary logarithm of the concentration of Hg2+ (namely log10[Hg2+]) presents a good linearity (Fig. 14). It is possible to measure the concentration of Hg2+ in the range of 1 × 10−5 to 1 × 10−3 M by comparison of the emission intensity.

After being immersed in the solutions containing various metal ions, the film of 1 would not peel off the support. The PXRD pattern of 1 is retained after it is exposed to Hg2+ (Fig. 5e). From the above results, it was found that the performance of the film of 1 for Hg2+ sensing is superior to that of the emulsion of 1 or the powder of 1 in practicability and data reproducibility. As shown in Fig. S9, ESI†, the detection limit of this sensor to Hg2+ is 1 × 10−8 M based on a signal-to-noise ratio of 3 : 1. This detection limit is as low as the maximum permitted level of Hg2+ in drinking water (10 nM) regulated by the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency.5h This sensor is suitable for real-world detection. Further efforts to devise this kind of film to detect metal ions at lower concentrations and for other chemical species is in progress.

Diffusion kinetics study

The diffusion kinetics of Hg2+ from solution into the film of 1 was studied at 298 and 333 K by following the time evolution of the luminescence intensity of 1 (see part S6 in the ESI† for details).29 The diffusion kinetics of Hg2+ can be represented by a simple model: (1) where I0, It and I∞ are respectively the luminescence intensities of the film of 1 at time 0 (namely at the time when the film was just immersed in the solution containing Hg2+), at time t and at the end of the diffusion process, l is the thickness of the film, D is the diffusion coefficient. The diffusion coefficient was calculated according to Crank's method and the exponential fit of all the experimental data of the diffusion process.30 The values of D obtained by the two methods are similar, which shows the approach presented here is feasible (Table S2, ESI†). The order of magnitude for the diffusion coefficient in this study is consistent with literature values, namely 10−15 to 10−23 m2 s−1, for metal ions into microporous materials. From the values of D at 298 and 333 K, the diffusion activation energy (Ea) of Hg2+ in the film of 1 was calculated using the Arrhenius equation. The values of Ea are 7.08 (calculated from the values of D measured by Crank's method30) and 4.29 kJ mol−1 (calculated from the values of D measured by the method presented in this work).

Conclusions

In conclusion, a highly luminescent LnMOF, 1, was designed and synthesized. It was characterised by single crystal X-ray diffraction, FT-IR, TGA and EA. A new strategy for depositing a mechanically robust MOF thin film was developed by electrodeposition in combination with subsequent solvothermal synthesis. With this strategy, a highly selective sensor consisting of a film of 1 was fabricated. Furthermore, the change in the photoluminescence intensity of this sensor before and after exposure to Hg2+ is distinguishable even with the naked eye when illuminated by a handy UV banknote detector. Such a thin film is a promising sensor device prototype for Hg2+ as it can perform well in scenes where precision equipment is absent. The diffusion kinetics of Hg2+ from solution into the film of 1 was also studied.
