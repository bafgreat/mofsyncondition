Introduction

The crystal engineering of supramolecular architectures based on metal and mixed-organic building blocks has been rapidly expanding in recent years, owing to their intriguing variety of architectures and potential applications in luminescence, gas absorption, catalysis, and magnetism.1 The combination of different ligands may result in greater tenability of structural frameworks than that present with single ligands.2 A careful selection of the metal cations, the nature of anions and connecting ligands, and the reaction conditions are key for the rational design of structures with specific chemical and physical properties.3 Among those mentioned above, the selection of anions is extremely important because changing anions can control and adjust the topologies of coordination frameworks.4 For example, it has been shown that the flexible bridging ligand 1,1′-(1,4-butanediyl)bis(imidazole-2-phenyl) and Zn(ii) ion gives a one-dimensional double-stranded chain structure if reacted with 1,2-benzenedicarboxylic acid; however, a 3-fold interpenetrating diamondoid network is produced if reacted with fumaric acid.5 It is clear that the structures of the anions play an important role in the formation of these architectures. So far, although the bis(benzimidazole) ligands have been widely studied as bridging ligands in the construction of metal–organic architectures,6 systematic investigations on the influence of organic anions such as carboxylate anions on their complex structures have been lacking.

In order to investigate the effect of the organic anions and the metal cations on their complex structures, we selected ten structurally different organic acids, Zn(ii) and Ag(i) ions, and the ligand bbmi as the reactants. Here, we describe the successful syntheses of eleven novel compounds: [Zn(L1)2(bbmi)] (1), [Zn(HL2)2(bbmi)] (2), [Zn0.5(L3)(bbmi)0.5] (3), [Zn0.5(L4)(bbmi)0.5(H2O)] (4), [Zn(L5)2(bbmi)] (5), [Zn(L6)(bbmi)] (6), [Zn(L7)(bbmi)0.5(H2O)] (7), [Zn(L8)(bbmi)0.5(H2O)] (8), [Ag2(L6)(bbmi)(H2O)] (9), [Ag(L9)0.5(bbmi)0.5]·2H2O (10) and [Ag2.5(L10)(bbmi)2]·0.5(OH)·0.5(EtOH)·1.5H2O (11) (bbmi = 1,1′-(1,4-butanediyl)bis(2-methylbenzimidazole), HL1 = benzoic acid, H2L2 = salicylic acid, HL3 = p-nitrobenzoic acid, HL4 = nicotinic acid, HL5 = 3,5-dinitrobenzoic acid, H2L6 = o-phthalic acid, H2L7 = m-phthalic acid, H2L8 = fumaric acid, H2L9 = p-phthalic acid and H2L10 = 5-aminoisophthalic acid). The thermal stabilities of 4 and 7–11 and the luminescence properties of 1, 3–5 and 7–11 in the solid state are also discussed.

Experimental

Materials and methods

All reagents and solvents for syntheses were purchased from commercial sources and used as received. The ligand bbmi was synthesized according to a previously reported method.6hElemental analysis was conducted on a Perkin–Elmer 240C elemental analyzer. The FT-IR spectra were recorded from KBr pellets in the range 4000–400 cm−1 on a Mattson Alpha-Centauri spectrometer. The phase purities of the bulk samples were identified by X-ray powder diffraction on a Siemens D5005 diffractometer. The photoluminescent properties were measured on a FLSP920 Edinburgh Fluorescence Spectrometer. TGA was performed on a Perkin–Elmer TG-7 analyzer heated from 25 to 600 °C under nitrogen.

X-ray crystallography

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction data for compounds 1–11 were recorded on an Oxford Diffraction Gemini R CCD with graphite-monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at 293 K. Absorption corrections were applied using multiscan techniques. All the structures were solved by Direct Method of SHELXS-97 and refined by full-matrix least-squares techniques using the SHELXL-97 program.7Non-hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic temperature parameters (when no disorder was present). The disordered L5 anions in compound 5 were refined using isotropic O, C and N atoms split over two sites, with a total occupancy of 1. The hydrogen atoms of the organic ligands were refined as rigid groups. Some of the water H atoms in compound 11 could not be positioned reliably and were omitted from the final model. All other H atoms of water molecules were located from a difference Fourier map. Although the water H atoms in compound 4 were located from a difference Fourier map, their coordinates are not very reliable. In compound 6, the Flack parameter of 0.62(3) may come from a racemic twin, which represents the percentage of twin in the current structure. Results and discussion

Structure of [Zn(L1)2(bbmi)] (1)

In the structure of 1, the Zn(ii) ion lies on a twofold axis and is coordinated by two N atoms from two bbmi ligands (Zn–N 2.285(4) Å) and four carboxylate O atoms from two H2L1 anions (Zn–O 2.328(4)–2.408(4) Å), showing a distorted octahedral coordination geometry (Fig. 1a). The Zn–N and Zn–O bond lengths are all within the normal ranges.1a The bbmi ligand lies about an inversion center and adopts a trans-conformation and serves as a bidentate bridging ligand. The four methylene groups are arranged in a zigzag shape. Zn(ii) ions are bridged by the bbmi ligands to form a 1D chain, with the distance between adjacent Zn(ii) ions being 14.551 Å. The chelating L1 anions are located on both sides of the chain. The adjacent chains are further connected through face-to-face π–π interactions between bbmi ligands (with centroid-to-centroid distances of 3.86 Å and face-to-face distances of 3.48 Å),8 resulting in a 2D supramolecular layer (Fig. 1b).

Structure of [Zn(HL2)2(bbmi)] (2)

Compounds 1 and 2 are isomorphous. In 2, the Zn(ii) ion lies on a twofold axis and the bbmi ligand lies about an inversion center (Fig. 2a). The hydroxyl group of the HL2 anion does not participate in coordination, but is involved in an intramolecular hydrogen-bonding interaction (O3–H3⋯O2 = 2.543(8) Å). In 2, the Zn(ii)⋯Zn(ii) distance separated by trans-conformation bbmi ligand (14.456 Å) is slightly shorter than that in 1 due to the effect of the organic HL2 anion. The adjacent chains are also connected through face-to-face π–π interactions between bbmi ligands (with centroid-to-centroid distances of 4.046 Å and face-to-face distances of 3.481 Å), yielding a 2D supramolecular layer. A remarkable feature of 2 is the presence of edge-to-face C–H⋯π interactions between the HL2phenyl rings of adjacent chains (d(C–H⋯π) = 3.08 Å and ∠C–H⋯π = 158°).9 The C–H⋯π interactions lead the 2D supramolecular arrays to form a 3D supramolecular structure along the a-axis (Fig. 2b).

Structure of [Zn0.5(L3)(bbmi)0.5] (3)

In 3, the Zn(ii) ion lies on a twofold axis and the bbmi ligand lies about another independent twofold axis. As shown in Fig. 3a, the Zn(ii) ion is four-coordinated by two N atoms (Zn–N 2.022(2) Å) from bbmi ligands and two carboxylate O atoms (Zn–O 1.926(2) Å) from L3 anions. Zn(ii) ions are connected by bbmi ligands to form a chain with the Zn⋯Zn distance being 12.786 Å. In the chain, the bbmi ligand adopts a cis-conformation, and the four methylene groups arrange in a Greek letter “Ω” shape. The L3 anion coordinates to the Zn(ii) center in a monodentate mode, whereas the nitryl group is not involved in coordination.10 The adjacent chains are connected by π–π interactions between the L3 anions (with centroid-to-centroid distances of 3.99 Å and face-to-face distances of 3.70 Å) to generate a 2D supramolecular layer (Fig. 3b).

Structure of [Zn0.5(L4)(bbmi)0.5(H2O)] (4)

As shown in Fig. 4a, the central Zn(ii) ion, lying on an inversion center, is six-coordinated by two N atoms from two bbmi ligands, four O atoms from two L4 anions and two water molecules in a distorted octahedron coordination geometry. Zn(ii) ions are linked by the trans-form bbmi ligands to form a 1D infinite chain, with the methylene groups resembling the letter “S”. The bbmi ligand lies about another independent inversion center. The Zn⋯Zn distance separated by bbmi is 12.857 Å. There also exist π–π stacking interactions between the bbmi ligands of neighboring layers (with centroid-to-centroid distances of 3.98 Å and face-to-face distances of 3.60 Å), which extends the 1D chain into a 2D supramolecular layer (Fig. 4b).

So far, several Zn–L4 compounds have been reported previously.11,12 The reported [Zn(L4)2] (A) was obtained by reaction of Zn(ClO4)2·6H2O and 3-cyanopyridine under hydrothermal conditions. In (A), the Zn(ii) center coordinates to four oxygen atoms of two carboxylate groups and two pyridylnitrogen atoms to form an infinite 2D square grid.11 The reported [Zn(L4)2]·MeOH·2H2O (B) was synthesized by diffusion of the methanolic solution of Zn(NO3)2·6H2O into a methanolic solution of HL4 under ambient conditions. The Zn(ii) ions in B are also six-coordinated by four oxygen atoms of two carboxylate groups and two pyridylnitrogen atoms, and the structure is a 3D network with 42·84 topology. The systematic products with guest aromatics, [Zn(L4)2]·naphthalene (B′) and [Zn(L4)2]·2-nitrobenzene (B′′) contain 44 networks as in compound A.12 Three coordination donor atoms from L4 anions, two oxygen atoms and one nitrogen atom in compounds A, B, B′ and B′′ coordinate to Zn(ii) ions, respectively; while in 4, only one oxygen atom coordinates to the Zn(ii) ion.

Structure of [Zn(L5)2(bbmi)] (5)

The asymmetric unit of compound 5 has one Zn(ii) ion and two L5 anions in general positions and two independent half bbmi ligands lying about inversion centers. One of the L5ligands is equally disordered over two orientations. As illustrated in Fig. 5a, each Zn(ii) ion in 5 is six-coordinated by two N atoms and four carboxylate O atoms. The carboxylate of L5 anion exhibits a bidentate chelating coordination mode. Both of two crystallographically unique bbmi ligands in 5 display trans conformations, with the alkyl (–CH2–)4 spacer adopting “zigzag” and “S” conformations, respectively. Zn(ii) ions are linked by these alternatively arranged bbmi ligands to generate a chain with Zn⋯Zn separations of 14.452 and 12.497 Å, respectively (Fig. 5b). The neighboring chains are involved in π–π interactions among the L5 anions, with centroid-to-centroid distances of 3.88 Å and face-to-face distances of 3.53 Å. As a result, these chains are linked by the π–π interactions to yield a 2D supramolecular assembly (Fig. 5c).

Structure of [Zn(L6)(bbmi)] (6)

As shown in Fig. 6a, the Zn(ii) ion is six-coordinated by four carboxylate O atoms from two L6 anions (Zn–O 2.247(6)–2.525(5) Å) and two N atoms from two bbmi ligands (Zn–N 2.306(6)–2.322(7) Å). Two carboxylate groups of the L6 anion display bidentate chelating modes. The bbmi ligand adopts a trans conformation and the (–CH2–)4 spacer adopts a “zigzag” conformation. Zn(ii) ions are bridged by L6 anions and bbmi ligands to give a 3D diamond network with separations of the Zn⋯Zn distances of 6.405 and 14.171 Å, respectively (Fig. 6b). There exist face-to-face π–π interactions between the imidazolyl ring and the phenyl ring of adjacent bbmi ligands (with centroid-to-centroid distances and face-to-face distances of 3.66 and 3.61 Å, respectively), which further stabilize the whole structure.

Structure of [Zn(L7)(bbmi)0.5(H2O)] (7)

In the structure of 7, the asymmetric unit has one Zn(ii) ion and one L7 anion in general positions and half a bbmi ligand lying about an inversion center. Each Zn(ii) cation is five-coordinated by one N atom from the bbmi ligand and four O atoms, three of which are from different L7 anions and one from a water molecule (Fig. 7a). The two carboxylate groups of the L7ligand exhibit different coordination modes: one bridges two Zn(ii) ions in a bidentate bridging mode, while the other connects one Zn(ii) ion in a monodentate mode. A pair of Zn(ii) ions are bridged by four carboxylate O atoms from two L7 anions to give a dinuclear [Zn2(CO2)2] unit with a Zn⋯Zn distance of 4.340 Å. The [Zn2(CO2)2] units are linked by trans bbmi ligands to form a 1D chain structure (Fig. 7b). These chains are further connected by L7 anions to form a 2D network (Fig. 7c). If the [Zn2(CO2)2] unit can be defined as a four-connected node, L7 anions and bbmi ligands are considered as connectors, the 2D framework can be simplified as a (4,4) topology (Fig. 7d). There exist intramolecular π–π interactions between bbmi ligands and L7 anions (with centroid-to-centroid distances of 3.90 Å and face-to-face distances of 3.76 Å).

Structure of [Zn(L8)(bbmi)0.5(H2O)] (8)

As shown in Fig. 8a, the asymmetric unit has one Zn(ii) ion, one L8 anion and one water molecule in general positions and a half bbmi ligand lying about an inversion center. The Zn(ii) center is five-coordinated by one N atom from the bbmi ligand (Zn–N 2.246(6) Å) and four O atoms, three of which are from three L8 anions and one from a water molecule (Zn–O 2.299(5)–2.389(5) Å). The two carboxylate groups of L8 exhibit monodentate and bidentate coordination modes, respectively. Zn(ii) ions are bridged by two L8 anions to give a Zn2O2 dimer, in which the Zn–Zn distance is 3.784 Å. These Zn2O2 dimers are bridged by L8 anions to generate a 2D square-grid with rhombic windows (Fig. 8b). Furthermore, the 2D layers are linked into a 3D network by the trans bbmi ligands. If the Zn2O2 dimers can be defined as six-connected nodes, and both L8 and bbmi ligands are considered as connectors, then the overall topology of 8 is α-Po (Fig. 8c). There also exist face-to-face π–π interactions between the imidazolyl rings of adjacent bbmi ligands (with centroid-to-centroid and face-to-face distances of 4.00 and 3.46 Å, respectively).

Structure of [Ag2(L6)(bbmi)(H2O)] (9)

When the Ag(i) ion was utilized to take the place of the Zn(ii) ion of 6, a (4,4) network was formed in the structure of 9. There are two crystallographically unique Ag(i) ions in the structure (Fig. 9a). Ag1 is coordinated by one N atom (Ag–N 2.176(2) Å) from the bbmi ligand, two O atoms from two L6 anions (Ag–O 2.291(2)–2.3333(18) Å) and one Ag(i) ion (Ag–Ag 2.8921(4) Å), showing a distorted tetrahedral coordination geometry. The Ag2 ion is five-coordinated by one N atom from the bbmi ligand (Ag–N 2.195(2) Å), one Ag(i) ion and three O atoms, two of which are from two L6 anions and one from a water molecule (Ag–O 2.399(2)–2.500(2) Å). Two L6 anions connect four Ag(i) ions in the chelating modes to form a [(Ag1)2(Ag2)2(L6)2] unit (Fig. 9b). These [(Ag1)2(Ag2)2(L6)2] units are joined by bbmi ligands into a 2D layer network. The neighboring layers are linked by the π–π interactions between the bbmi imidazolyl rings (with centroid-to-centroid distances of 3.45 Å and face-to-face distances of 3.42 Å) to yield a 3D supramolecular framework.

Structure of [Ag(L9)0.5(bbmi)0.5]·2H2O (10)

The asymmetric unit of compound 10 has half a bbmi ligand and half a L9 anion each lying about independent inversion centers. As shown in Fig. 10a, the Ag(i) center is two-coordinated, having an approximately linear geometry, containing one N atom from the bbmi ligand and one O atom from L9 anion (N1–Ag1–O2 173.66(11)°). The Ag–O and Ag–N distances are 2.173(2) and 2.118(3) Å, respectively. The bbmi ligand adopts a trans-conformation with the separation of the Ag⋯Ag distance of 12.867 Å. Ag(i) ions are bridged by L8 and bbmi ligands alternatively to generate a zigzag chain (Fig. 10b). When intermolecular Ag⋯Ag weak interactions (Ag⋯Ag = 3.506 Å) and π–π interactions between bbmi and L8ligands (with centroid-to-centroid distances of 3.82 Å and face-to face distances of 3.76 Å) are taken into account,13 the chains can be assembled into a 2D supramolecular network.

Structure of [Ag2.5(L10)(bbmi)2]·0.5(OH)·0.5(EtOH)·1.5H2O (11)

As illustrated in Fig. 11a, the structure of 11 contains three kinds of unique Ag(i) ions, one L10 anion, two kinds of unique bbmi ligands, one half-occupancy OH−, one half-occupancy ethanol molecule, two half-occupancy water molecules and two quarter-occupancy water molecules. Three unique Ag(i) ions in compound 11 exhibit three different coordination geometries. Ag1 lies on an inversion center, and is coordinated by two N atoms from two bbmi ligands. Ag2 is coordinated by two N atoms from two bbmi ligands and two O atoms from one L10 anion, showing a highly distorted tetrahedral geometry. Ag3 shows an approximately linear geometry, surrounded by one N atom from a bbmi ligand and one O atom from the L10 anion with the N8–Ag3–O2#2 angle being 167.62(18)°. The positive charge of [Ag2.5(L10)(bbmi)2]0.5+ species is balanced by the half-occupancy OH−group.14 The two carboxylate groups of the L10 anion exhibit bidentate and monodentate fashions, respectively. The two crystallographically unique bbmi ligands display cis conformations, and adopt “zigzag” and Arabic number “3” configurations, respectively. As shown in Fig. 11b, each Ag1 ion is linked by two zigzag bbmi ligands to form the rung of the ladder. Each Ag3 ion is connected by one L10 anion and one “3”-shaped bbmi ligand to form the side of the ladder. Furthermore, the rungs and the sides are linked by the Ag2 ions to furnish the ladder-like structure of compound 11. The adjacent ladders are linked by π–π interactions of bbmi ligands to yield a 2D supramolecular assembly (with centroid-to-centroid distances of 4.20 Å and face-to-face distances of 3.42 Å). Furthermore, the Ag1⋯Ag3 weak interactions (3.455 Å) contribute to the stabilization of the 2D structure (Fig. 11c). The void volume of 11 is 460.7 Å3 per unit cell, which is 15.6% of the total crystal volume.15

Roles of the carboxylate anions and bis(benzimidazole) ligand in the formation of the frameworks

As shown above, the simultaneous use of the flexible bbmi ligand and carboxylate anions affords a diversity of 1D, 2D and 3D frameworks. The connectivities of the Zn(ii) containing compounds 1–8 are strongly related to the carboxylate anions. Each of the L1, HL2, L3, L4 and L5 anions contains one carboxylate group, and coordinates to only one metal ion acting as a terminal ligand, and the related complexes (1–5) show 1D chain structures. In these complexes, each carboxylate anion only connects one metal in a monodentate mode. Each of the L6, L7 and L8 anions contain two carboxylate groups. Complexes 6–8 display 2D and 3D polymeric structures. In these complexes, both dicarboxylate and bbmi act as the bridging ligands. It is clear that increasing the number of carboxylate groups can result in a higher dimensionality.

It should be pointed out that the positions of the carboxylate groups may also affect the construction of the complex framework. Although both L6 and L7 act as dicarboxylate anions with the benzene rings, the positions of the carboxylate groups are different (o-phthalic acid and m-phthalic acid, respectively). In 6, the Zn(ii) ion adopts a distorted octahedral coordination geometry, and the 1D –Zn–bbmi– chains are connected by L6 anions to give a 3D diamond network. While in 7, the Zn(ii) ion shows a distorted trigonal bipyramidal coordination geometry, and the dinuclear [Zn2(CO2)2] units are linked by bbmi ligands and L7 anions to form a 2D network. In addition, the lengths of the spacers between the two terminal carboxylate groups can also influence the construction of the resulting frameworks. Take compounds [Zn(L8)(bbmi)0.5(H2O)] 8 and reported [Zn(L11)(bbmi)0.5] 12 (L11 = adipate dianion)6l for example: in 8, the L8 anion contains a rigid –CHCH– spacer, while in 12 the L11 anion possesses a flexible long –CH2–CH2–CH2–CH2– spacer. The Zn2O2 dimeric units in 8 are bridged by bbmi ligands and L8 anions to give α-Po topology. In 12, two Zn(ii) ions are linked by two L11 anions to form a double chain, and these chains are further connected by bbmi ligands to form a 2D layer.

Besides the carboxylate anions, the bis(benzimidazole) ligands also play a significant role in the resultant structures. The phenyl groups of bbmi ligands in compounds 1–11 display different orientations. In 3 and 11, the bbmi ligands show cis-conformations, while in 1, 2 and 4–10, they display trans-conformations (Fig. 12). Obviously, the different conformations of the bbmi ligands resulted in diversified face-to-face π–π stacking and edge-to-face C–H⋯π stacking, which further influences the final supramolecular structures of the complexes.

Thermal analysis

In order to characterize the compounds more fully in terms of thermal stability, the thermal behaviors of compounds 4 and 7–11 were studied by TGA. The experiments were performed on samples consisting of numerous single crystals under a N2 atmosphere with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. The TGA curves for compounds 4 and 7–11 are shown in Fig. S1, ESI.†

For compound 4, a rapid weight loss of the coordination water (5.00%, calcd 5.43%) was observed from 82 to 120 °C. For 7, the weight loss from room temperature to 346 °C corresponds to the loss of the coordination water molecule (obsd 4.44%, calcd 4.43%). The removal of the organic components occurs in the range of 348 to 540 °C. The TGA curve of 8 shows a weight loss of 4.99% from 65–170 °C from the loss of the coordination water molecule (calcd 5.05%), and the framework collapsed at 247 °C. The first weight loss, corresponding to the water molecules, is observed from 109 to 192 °C (obsd 2.89%, calcd 2.51%) for 9 and from room temperature to 148 °C (obsd 13.32%, calcd 9.35%) for 10. The weight loss of 11 in the range of room temperature to 137 °C can be assigned to the departure of guest waters, free OH− and half ethanol molecules (obsd 7.92%, calcd 5.13%). The organic components begin to decompose at 215 °C.

Luminescent properties

Solid-state luminescence studies were carried out for compounds 1–11, the bbmi ligand and all sodium carboxylates (Fig. 13, Fig. S2, ESI† and Table 2). No clear photoluminescence was observed for compounds 2 and 6 at ambient temperature. The free bbmi ligand displays luminescence with an emission maximum at 371 nm (λex = 295 nm), which can be attributed to the π → π* transitions.3b The emissions of NaL1, NaL3, NaL4, NaL5, Na2L6, Na2L7, Na2L8, Na2L9 and Na2L10 can be assigned to the n → π* transition as previously reported.16

The emission spectra of the Zn(ii) compounds 1, 3, 4, 7 and 8 consist of a main peak at 378 nm (λex = 342 nm) for 1, 425 nm (λex = 380 nm) for 3, 462 nm (λex = 370 nm) for 4, 409 nm (λex = 362 nm) for 7 and 465 nm (λex = 346 nm) for 8, respectively. Compound 5 consists of a main peak at 472 nm and a shoulder at 407 nm (λex = 362 nm). These emissions are neither metal-to-ligand charge transfer (MLCT) nor ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT) in nature, since the Zn(ii) ions are difficult to oxidize or to reduce because of the d10 configuration. These emissions are mainly based on the luminescence of ligands.17 The emission peaks of 378 nm for 1, 407 nm for 5 and 409 nm for 7 can be ascribed to π → π* transitions of neutral ligands because similar peaks (λem = 371 nm) also appear for the free bbmi ligands. The red-shifts of compounds compared to bbmi ligands probably result from the fact that the coordination of bbmi to Zn(ii) increases the conformational rigidity of the ligand and thus reduces the non-radiative energy loss.3c The peaks of 425 nm for 3, 462 nm for 4, 472 nm for 5 and 465 nm for 8 may be assigned to the intra-ligand fluorescent emissions of the carboxylate anions due to their resemblance to NaL3 (424 nm), NaL4 (broad emission peak with maxima at 408 and 530 nm), NaL5 (468 nm) and Na2L8 (441 nm), respectively. The weak red-shift of the emission peaks with respect to the free sodium carboxylates may be ascribed to the fact that their coordination to Zn(ii) ions decreases the n → π* energy gap.14 Briefly, the emissions of these compounds should originate from the bbmi ligand, the carboxylate anions, or both. The red shifts of 3, 5 and 7 compared with 1 may originate from the obvious decrease of the electron density of the ligands affected by electron-withdrawing nitryl or carboxylate groups and is in line with the energy of π* orbital. This low energy shift phenomenon influenced by the coexistent groups has also been reported in other metal–organic systems.18

The Ag(i) compounds (9–11) exhibit emissions at about 428 nm (λex = 317 nm) for 9, 504 and 374 nm (λex = 332 nm) for 10 and 515 and 376 nm (λex = 325 nm) for 11. The peak at 428 nm for 9 exhibits a red-shift with respect to the Na2L6 (406 nm), which may be assigned to the intra-ligand fluorescent emission of the carboxylate anions. The peaks at 374 nm for 10 and 376 nm for 11 can be ascribed to π → π* transitions of bbmi ligands. With reference to the literature, the peaks at 504 nm for 10 and 515 nm for 11 respectively are probably assigned to metal-to-ligand charge-transfer (MLCT) transitions.13 The spectra of 10 and 11 are significantly different from that of 9. This may be due to the presence of Ag⋯Ag interactions. The presence of π–π interactions may also contribute to such differences in luminescence.19

Conclusion

In summary, using various carboxylate ligands and the flexible bbmi ligand under hydrothermal and ambient conditions, we isolated eleven novel Zn(ii) and Ag(i) coordination complexes with different frameworks. Compounds 1–5, containing monocarboxylates, display 1D chain structures, and the chains are further linked by π–π and C–H⋯π interactions to form high dimensional supramolecular structures. Compounds 6–9, with dicarboxylates, show 2D or 3D structures. Ag(i) containing compounds 10 and 11 containing dicarboxylates display 1D structures, Ag⋯Ag and π–π interactions connecting them into 2D supramolecular assemblies. The varieties of the structures indicate that carboxylates and metal cations play important roles in the construction of compounds. In addition, the methylene groups of bbmi ligand display different conformations in compounds 1–11, which also affects the formation of the final architectures. The fluorescence emissions show that the complexes may be good candidates for photoactive materials.
