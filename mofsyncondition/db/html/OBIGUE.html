Cl-VOCs are ubiquitous in the environment, released in a large scale by various industrial activities.1 Many Cl-VOCs are toxic to humans, some are even suspected to be carcinogenic, and in general terms, they are classified as pollutants. Hence, materials and methods which allow for the removal or separation of such chemicals from the environment are highly desired, particularly for air remediation. Current methods are based on incineration, oxidation and reduction techniques,2 all destructive, which do not allow for the recovery of the organic compounds. Novel solutions are thus needed in order to achieve the reversible and selective adsorption of Cl-VOCs and their controlled release.

Materials capable of selective adsorption of small molecules are of considerable importance in areas as diverse as nanotechnology,3 analytical and separation science,4 but also in fundamental studies.5 In this regard, coordination polymers (CPs) and metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have attracted increasing attention due to their structural and chemical features derived from a combination of organic ligands and metal centres.6 The possibility to fine-tune the organic ligands by modern synthetic methods, i.e., by using different functional groups, spacers, etc., and to choose among many different metal ions allows the synthesis of CPs with voids or channels able to selectively adsorb different molecules such as CH4, CO2 and H2.7 However, despite the vast literature on the subject, involving mainly 2D and 3D CPs, data available on the adsorption of larger, chlorinated volatile organic molecules remain scarce, especially for 1D CPs.8

Here, we report on the synthesis of microcrystalline isostructural 1D CPs, 1–3Pwd, using the rod-like ligand L9a and Zn halide salts (ZnX2, X = Cl, Br and I) which are obtained in the gram scale, quantitatively and in short crystallization times (Scheme 1). Most importantly, these materials are able to trap Cl-VOC vapours at room temperature and at ambient pressure. Among several tested Cl-VOCs, 1–3Pwd show the ability to trap trichloromethane (TCM), 1,1,2-trichloroethylene (TCE), dichloromethane (DCM) and dichloroethane (DCE), with DCE and TCE being the most efficiently adsorbed species. By fine-tuning the channel dimensions using different halides, the amount and the selectivity pattern of the adsorbed species are different, with the best selectivity for DCE over TCE found for X = I. Selectivity was tested also by exposing 1–3Pwd to a mixture of the four Cl-VOCs. A rationale for such selectivity of 1–3Pwd is proposed based on a detailed analysis on the structural and dynamic features of 1–3·TCMSC and 1–3·MeCNSC and on their solvent free single-crystal (SC) counterparts 1a–3aSC (Fig. 1b and c).

Gram-scale quantities of 1–3Pwd are obtained by adding ZnX2 (X = Cl, Br, I) dissolved in MeOH to a vigorously stirred suspension of L in MeCN at room temperature (see ESI†).10 After 5 minutes of stirring, the solid was filtered, dried and collected.11 X-ray powder diffraction (XRPD) analyses of 1–3Pwd showed that the material is crystalline and differs from L and from the 1D CP 1·TCMSC, previously reported (see the ESI†). A final proof of the actual structural features of microcrystalline 1–3Pwd was given by a comparison between their XRPD patterns with the simulated XRPD patterns calculated from single crystals of 1–3·MeCNSC.9b The agreement between the experimental XRPD of 1–3Pwd and the simulated XRPD from single crystal data of 1–3·MeCNSC corroborates the exchange of TCM by MeCN molecules (see the ESI†). This confirms that the bulk microcrystalline 1–3Pwd correspond to the structure obtained by single crystal X-ray diffraction (SC-XRD). Additionally, the presence of MeCN and the absence of TCM in 1–3Pwd were confirmed by 1H-NMR (see Fig. S23 ESI†).

CPs 1–3Pwd were tested for adsorption by exposing them to vapours of one among selected Cl-VOCs (such as TCM, DCM, TCE, DCE, chlorobenzene, 1-chlorohexane, chlorocyclohexane, 2-chloro-butane) in a closed vessel for 24 h, then left exposed to open air under ambient conditions and sampled at different time intervals by 1H-NMR. Among the chlorinated VOCs, the three CPs showed the capability to adsorb TCM, DCM, TCE and DCE, but not 1-chlorohexane, chlorocyclohexane or 2-chlorobutane. In Fig. 2, plots of the amount of trapped TCM (R is calculated as the molar ratio between solvent molecules and L-ZnX2 units) over up to ca. 1900 hours are shown. Clearly, 1–3Pwd can initially adsorb TCM with the R factor between 0.3 and 0.4, but also they are able to keep the guest within the solid framework for long periods of time.12 Similar plots can be obtained for DCE, TCM and DCM (see the ESI†).

We want to emphasize here that adsorption inside the material channels was investigated by 1H-NMR and X-ray diffraction, and it has been considered as occurring only if both techniques gave positive indications of guest inclusion. Indeed, the combination of the data from the two techniques allows one to confidently evaluate the internal adsorption capability of the studied materials and rule out the hypothesis of external/surface adsorption. SC-XRD tests assess the actual capability of the solvent to sterically fit inside the material channels and, at the same time, of the materials to adsorb the solvents from the atmosphere. Complementary information can be instead obtained from the 1H-NMR analyses which provide quantitative information on the adsorbed species. Regarding the X-ray analyses, they were carried out using single crystals. Large single crystals of 1–3·TCMSC were obtained by the triple layer crystallization method and exposed for 24 h to vapours of the selected solvents (Fig. 1b). No indication of the inclusion of 1-chlorohexane, chlorocyclohexane or 2-chloro-butane was obtained after several attempts (24 h exposure). As to the adsorption of the other solvent molecules, single crystals of 1·TCMSC, 1·DCMSC and 1·DCESC were described in previous work.9a Instead 1·TCESC was obtained by SC-to-SC transformation by exposing 1·TCMSC to TCE vapours for 24 h (Fig. 1c). The X-ray structures obtained for 1·YSC (with Y = DCM, TCM, DCE and TCE) demonstrate that each of the four solvents can be entrapped into the material pores under heterogeneous conditions. As to the NMR screening, adsorption tests were considered positive if the solvent was detected by 1H-NMR after 5 h of equilibration under ambient conditions. In the case of TCE, DCM, TCM and DCE, both X-ray and 1H-NMR analyses confirmed the adsorption. Notably, the fact that, in most cases, the amount of solvent is relatively abundant (R > 0.2) and steady over a long period of time (up to ca. 1900 h), can be considered alone as a strong indication of internal adsorption (Fig. 2 and S24†).

The inclusion of Cl-VOCs was also monitored by ex situ XRPD on 1–3Pwd microcrystals after their exposure to TCM, DCM, TCE or DCE vapours, showing anisotropic peak shifts due to lattice structural variations (Fig. S19†).13

In order to consider a more realistic scenario, for example that of a mixture of different chlorinated solvents generated in an industrial process, we tested the adsorption selectivity of the three materials by exposing 1–3Pwd to a mixture of the four chlorinated VOCs in a closed vessel for 24 h.14 The outcome of the above experiment is shown in Fig. 3, where the materials, left exposed to open air, under ambient conditions after the initial exposure time, are investigated by 1H-NMR about their solvent content at different time intervals. It appears evident that the three materials all show a preference for the adsorption of DCE (△) and TCE (○) over DCM and TCM. However, both the R factors and the selectivity factor, S are quite different and dependent on the halide identity.

More detailedly, the amount of trapped TCE and DCE increases when going from 1Pwd to 3Pwd (I > Br > Cl), reaching an RDCE of ca. 0.65 in 3Pwd (compared to R in the range of 0.2–0.25 for both 1Pwd and 2Pwd at ca. 150 h), but most interestingly, also the S factor improves in 3Pwd. Indeed, the selectivity between DCE and TCE is almost negligible in 1Pwd and 2Pwd, but increases to S = RDCE/RTCEca. 3.5 for 3Pwd. To the best of our knowledge, adsorbent materials that can be tuned so effectively are rare.15

The trapped chlorinated solvents can be extracted upon heating the samples for 5–10 h at 80 °C. Importantly, the thermal treatment keeps the coordination polymers 1–3Pwd intact in terms of metal–organic framework, as demonstrated by XRPD analyses on 1–3Pwd and on 1–3·TCMSC. Such thermal stability represents an important feature also in view of the opportunity to test their dynamic behaviour upon heating (vide infra).

Additional vapour adsorption data were also obtained by IR-spectroscopy. Fig. 4 shows the ATR FT-IR spectra in the 2000–550 cm−1 region of the as synthesized163Pwd (Fig. 4a) and after its exposure to each of the four solvent vapours (Fig. 4b–e). In all cases, new adsorption peaks appear whose frequency is dependent on the identity of the solvent vapours to which the sample is exposed. The black arrows in Fig. 4 indicate the most evident new peaks for each sample which we believe are corresponding to the inclusion species. Small shifts (2–5 cm−1) of the CP framework signals, depending on the identity of the vapour included, are also observed.

Despite structural similarity, 1–3Pwd can display striking differences in their solid-state behavior.17 In the present case, the observed selectivity factors (Fig. 3) are clearly dependent on the identity of the halide ions coordinated to the Zn centres. Thus, in order to gain insights into this phenomenon, we embarked on a more detailed X-ray analysis on the structural and dynamic features of the single crystals of the three CPs (Fig. 1b and c). We started from the structures of 1–3·TCMSC, obtained at 100 K. They are isostructural, crystallizing in the orthorhombic system and in the Pnna space group. The Zn–N coordination bond forms 1D polymer chains that are not interpenetrated and stack in such a way that 1D channels including TCM guest molecules expand along the crystallographic a-axis. In all the structures, the bulky cyclohexane moiety is important as it prevents π–π stacking of the flat aromatic region of L. The interior of the channels has two different sides: one constituted of iminic bonds and cyclohexyl ring moieties (side A), and the other (side B) facing aromatic rings and Zn metal centres. These point their C-Hs and Zn-coordinated halides towards the centre of the channels (Fig. 5a). The different halogen identities are clearly reflected on the size and shape of the channels that change when going from chloride to bromide and iodide. To better describe the shape of the channels, the dA and dB distances have been defined.18 As shown in Fig. 5, the channels in 1·TCMSC (dA: 7.469(6) Å; dB: 8.904(2) Å) and 2·TCMSC (dA: 7.713(1) Å; dB: 8.711(1) Å) are elongated as dB > dA, while the channel in 3·TCMSC (dA: 8.55(2) Å; dB: 8.355(1) Å) has similar dB and dA.

As regards channel volumes, they account for the 23.8%, 23.0% and 23.0% of the total unit cell volume in 1–3·TCMSC, respectively.19 The halide identity effect is also evident by looking at the vdW contacts between the TCM guest and the iminic nitrogen. The dN⋯X distances are 3.12(2) Å and 3.23(1) Å for X = Cl and Br respectively, while for the iodide case dN⋯I = 3.77(1). Regarding the geometric features involving the Zn-L unit, small variations in the N–Zn–N angles (Cl = 98.7(1)°; Br = 99.7(2)° and I = 99.9(3)°) and the pyridine–benzene torsion angles (Cl = 138.2(5)°; Br = 137.9(9)° and I = 140.9(7)°) are present. Comparison could also be made among the structural data available for the L-ZnCl2 system with the four different guest molecules. Channel volumes for 1·DCMSC, 1·DCESC and 1·TCESC are 23.6%, 24.2% and 23.5%, respectively, while dA and dB distances are dA: 7.426(6) Å, dB: 8.899(1) Å for 1·DCMSC, dA: 7.829(7) Å, dB: 8.714(2) Å, for 1·DCESC and dA: 7.529(7) Å, dB 8.891(2) Å, for 1·TCESC. Unfortunately, relevant guest solvent–framework interactions in the 1·TCESC case cannot be properly identified due to pronounced guest disorder.

The initial state of the adsorbent materials might have a crucial effect on the adsorption capabilities. Thus, having in mind that the adsorption experiments on 1–3Pwd were carried out on samples containing MeCN and under room temperature conditions, we analysed single crystals of 1–3·MeCNSC at 300 K (Fig. 1c). This also would give additional indications of how the non-chlorinated solvent guest molecule affects the CP structure in its single crystal phase.

Interestingly, the voids corresponding to the included MeCN account for 24.8%, 23.3% and 22.4% of the total unit cell volume, which points to a decreasing trend of accessible void space when going from chloride to bromide and iodide.20 Notably, this trend does fairly match with that found in the single crystal containing TCM recorded at 100 K and also indicates that the materials are dynamic and structurally guest dependent. Recalling the better adsorption performances of 3Pwd in the 1–3Pwd series, it appears that the smaller voids allow for the better inclusion of DCE and TCE as observed by 1H-NMR analysis. It is well known that in some CPs the larger size of the channels is not a condition to trap more efficiently a given guest molecule.21 Not only that, but also the polarizability of the Zn-bound halide which is found at the surface of the pore can have a role. Among the three X species (X = Cl, Br and I), iodine is the most polarizable element and the arising dispersion forces that can be established with solvent guests, important in several previously reported systems,22 could be stronger in that case and give a rationale for the observed behaviour. Another important aspect to take into account is the shape of the channels (shown in Fig. 6) which could have an impact on their trapping capability.23 Indeed, the dA and dB parameters show that the channel with less marked differences between dA and dB corresponds also to the X = I case, 3·MeCNSC,24 which displays also a wavier structure (Fig. 6d–f).

Finally, as said, these three species might also display different dynamic behaviours. Thus, we performed in situ variable-temperature single crystal X-ray diffraction experiments on the evacuated CPs (Fig. 1c). In order to do that, single crystals of 1–3·TCMSC were emptied of TCM molecules under reduced pressure at 80 °C thus obtaining 1–3aSC, which were analysed by in situ SC-XRD at different temperatures.25 The plots of unit cell lattice parameters (a, b, c axes and V) as a function of temperature in the 100–400 K range are shown in Fig. 7. In particular, the a-axis increases for 1aSC, while for 2aSC and 3aSC it decreases. Such a decrease is observed for the b-lattice parameter in all CPs. The c-lattice parameter, instead, shows an increase, in all cases. As for the unit cell volume variation, clearly there is a gradual increase on going from 1aSC to 2aSC and 3aSC, showing up to ca. 250 Å3 expansion (ca. 8% v/v) in the 100–400 K range. This value is quite remarkable in terms of %, also considering the anisotropicity of the transition.25b

We believe that the observed different dynamic behaviour surely reflects – although in a non-trivial way – on the guest inclusion capabilities and selectivity observed.

In conclusion, we reported on a family of isostructural 1D CPs 1–3Pwd which show selective adsorption of Cl-VOC vapours, which can be produced, fast and quantitatively, as microcrystalline powders in the gram-scale. Their adsorption behaviour has been studied by a combination of 1H-NMR and X-ray diffraction techniques. It has been found that DCE and TCE molecules were most effectively trapped in 1–3Pwd and that the arising selectivity patterns were based on the halide identity, with 3Pwd behaving the best in terms of adsorption R and selectivity S factors. Structural details of single crystals related to the microcrystalline CPs (1–3·TCMSC and 1–3·MeCNSC and 1·(Y)SC with Y = DCE, DCM and TCE) were also analysed in order to establish which structural elements could be responsible for the selectivity observed. On the other hand, in situ SC-XRD experiments also revealed interesting dynamic behaviour of solvent guest evacuated single crystals (1a–3aSC) dependent as well on the halide identity. As the separation of closely related organic molecules is challenging, and especially desirable in several industrial processes, the superior adsorption performance of microcrystalline 3Pwd renders it suitable for the development of selective adsorbent materials in the remediation of polluted atmospheres.
