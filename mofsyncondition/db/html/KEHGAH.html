Introduction

The rigid aliphatic skeleton of adamantane, the smallest “nanodiamond” molecule,1 provides enormous potential for molecular design, in particular as a platform supporting geometrically proper multiple binding directions for generation of extended macromolecules2 for nanotechnology,3 dendrimers,4 and polymers.5 Special interest in adamantane derivatives arises from their utility as polydentate molecular building blocks for crystal design. Particularly, the elegant hydrogen bonded frameworks of 1,3,5,7-adamantanetetracarboxylic acid6 and 1,3,5,7-tetrahydroxyadamantane7 themselves were of paramount significance for the developing approaches of crystal engineering, recognition of close topological relations between organic and inorganic structures, and stimulating further efforts in the field.8 By substitution at the four available bridge-head positions of adamantane, a number of polydentate carboxylate,9 phosphonate,10 sulfonate,11 and phenolate12 tectons have been developed so far and examined for the synthesis of coordination9–13 and hydrogen bonded6,7,12polymers.

In this context, new prospects for the synthesis of metal–organic framework (MOF) materials may be delineated while combining geometric and functional features of the “nanodiamond platform” and the fascinating structural potential of common heteroaryl N-donors, such as pyrazoles,14triazoles,15,16tetrazoles,16,17etc. The ability of 1,2-diazoleligands to sustain short bridges between the metal ions is especially beneficial and this may be applicable for multiconnected MOFs incorporating coordinatively unsaturated polynuclear clusters,15,18,19 unusual metal–oxide MOFs,20 materials with non-trivial magnetic properties, and spin cross-over systems.21 For these applications, the role of adamantane spacers is crucial for providing an independent origin of the framework connectivity and also for very desirable variations in the ligands functionality, shape and charge (for pyrazole and tetrazole species) leading to the formation of larger coordination-bonded clusters and a general increase of network connectedness, as may be anticipated for the unique libraries of angular 1,3-disubstituted, trigonal 1,3,5-trisubstituted and tetrahedral 1,3,5,7-tetrasubstituted adamantanes. Following this methodology, we have developed a series of 1,2,4-triazolyl functionalized adamantane and 1-azaadamantane tectons (Scheme 1) and introduce them as illustrative polydentate building blocks for supramolecular synthesis.

Results and discussion

Preparation of the organic ligands

Synthesis of the ligands by stepwise functionalization of adamantane was performed with the aim of preparing corresponding amines, which readily undergo an acid-catalyzed condensation with N,N-dimethylformamide azine and yield the desired 1,2,4-triazol-4-yl derivatives (tr2ad, tr3ad, tr4ad). Although the first step was also common, as is illustrated by selective bromination (Scheme 2; See the ESI† for the full synthetic details), the preparative sequences were determined by a specific reactivity of di-, tri- and tetrasubstituted adamantanes. Unlike the 1,3-dibromo derivative, tri- and tetrabromides were inert in the direct Ritter amination, while the previously reported photochemical Ritter amination towards tetraamino derivative is cost-ineffective and laborious.22 Therefore, a longer way, including Hoffmann degradation of the amides, was employed. The 1,3,5,7-tetracarboxylate itself is available through photochemical cyanation,23 while preparation of the 1,3,5-triacid by Koch–Haaf carbonylation required more drastic conditions. Looking for a synthetic alternative for the hardly accessible trisubstituted adamantanes, we have developed a closely related 1-aza system (tr3ada), which is readily available by direct assembly of the aza-cage frame starting with 1,3,5-trinitrobenzene (Scheme 3).

Structure of the coordination polymers

In the metal complexes, the adamantaneligands exhibit various coordination modes that are based upon partial or full utilization of the triazole-N functionality (Table 1). Most of the typically sophisticated metal–triazole coordination motifs, such as double and triple triazole bridges, heteroligand triazole/hydroxo and halogenido bridges, different bi- and trinuclear clusters could be anticipated for these versatile systems and employed as special kinds of the supramolecular synthons. The latter could predetermine an extremely high connectivity of the frameworks, as revealed by the UCl3-like net assembled with [Cd2(μ-tr)3(tr)6] nine-connected clusters. The crystal chemistry of a simpler bitopic ligandtr2ad is more sensitive to the specific coordination preferences imposed by the central atoms (Cd, Ag) and the dimensionality of the resulting arrays is variable. At the same time, tripodal ligandstr3ad and tr3ada are especially suited for generation of 2D layered structures derived from characteristic topological prototypes, such as hexa- and trigonal plane nets (Table 1). The tetrahedral tr4ad could be applicable for 3D frameworks following the inherent symmetry of the molecular building blocks.

Complexes of the bitopic ligandtr2ad

Cadmium complexes [Cd2{tr2ad}4(H2O)4](CdBr4)2·2H2O (1) and [Cd{tr2ad}4{CdI3}2]·4H2O (2) represent the simplest structural function of the bitopic tr2adligand as a link between two metal ions. Both the compounds include cationic metal–organic and anionic halocadmate portions and feature “charge separation” typical for cadmium halogenides. In the first case, the Cd1 ions adopt cis-octahedral coordination [CdN4O2] completed with two aqua ligands (Table 2) and are linked by organic tectons into a square grid network (Fig. 1). The rectangular meshes of the net are appreciably distorted, with four unequal internodal distances in the range 8.99–12.37 Å, causing corrugation of the net and generation of specific pockets. The latter are shape-complementary with charge-compensating [CdBr4]2− anions (Cd–Br 2.5595(14)–2.6145(14) Å), which are held inside the pockets by a set of weak OH⋯Br hydrogen bonds (O⋯Br 3.32–3.49 Å).

The iodide complex (2) is a 1D coordination polymer based upon connection of metal ions by double tr2ad bridges (Fig. 2). Thus the angular structure of the ligand promotes formation of [Cd2{tr2ad}2] squares sharing Cd-vertices. The octahedral environment of the Cd1 ions (Cd–N 2.305(2)–2.403(2) Å) is completed by additional tr2adligands, which are terminal in the topological sense but are linked to the anionic [CdI3]− moieties, similarly to the structure of the cadmium iodide complex with N-(3-pyridylmethyl)benzotriazole.24

The high potential of the angular tr2ad linker could be utilized when exploiting specific coordination properties of the triazole sites such as the short-distance N1,N2-bridge, especially in the compounds with soft silver(i) ions.25 Thus, in the complex [Ag2{tr2ad}2](NO3)2·2H2O (3) the ligand is integrated into the overall structure as a tetradentate building block and the 3D cationic framework originated from a combination of such blocks and four-coordinate metal ions, in a 1 : 1 proportion. The result is a binodal four-connected {42·84} net related to the PtS structure (“pts” topology).26 In this way, chains of the metal ions (Ag⋯Ag 3.87, 4.14 Å), along the a axis, are linked in two directions and produce a distorted tetragonal framework with channels hosting counter anions and solvate water molecules (the framework itself occupies only 52.4% of the crystal volume).27 This array is related to the structure of [Ag(tr2eth)]ClO4 (tr2eth = 1,2-bis(1,2,4-triazol-4-yl)ethane),28 while a specific adamantane spacer is important for the shape of the channels. A significant distortion of the framework leads to adamantane groups from the opposite “walls” being close together and to the occurrence of hydrophobic Ad⋯Ad interactions, which divide the channel into two separate “chambers” (Fig. 3b). This effect could be even more significant for the ligands with multiple adamantane spacers, e.g.1,1′-biadamantane,29 and may be considered as a special design tool. The coordination around silver ions is distorted tetrahedral, with two short (2.185(5)–2.194(5) Å) and two longer (2.407(5)–2.529(4) Å) bonds with triazole-N donors (Table 3).

Complexes of tripodal ligandstr3ad and tr3ada

Unlike the simpler bitopic connector tr2ad, the family of trifunctional ligands features a higher number of binding sites per molecule and presumably could constitute an independent three-connected node for the framework. Thus the simple motif of complex 2 involving six-coordinate metal ions [M(tr)6] could be transformed into a network with the participation of an additional triazole link.

Such an illustrative evolution of the structure was relevant for a very characteristic series of Cd complexes with trifunctional ligands. The compounds [Cd{tr3ad}2]X·nH2O (4: X = 2NO3−, n = 6; 5: X = CdCl42−, n = 3) and [Cd{tr3ada}2]CdI4·3H2O (7) are isomorphous and possess 2D structures of CdI2 or Mg(OH)2 type (Kagomé dual net, {43}2{46·66·83}, three-letter notation “kgd”), with Cd ions and tris-triazole ligands representing six- and three-connected nodes, respectively (Fig. 4). Since the “kgd” net involves plane 6-coordination of the nodes, it is very uncommon for coordination polymers,30 and only a specific configuration of three triazole “arms” at the adamantane platform makes it possible to assemble the net with octahedral metal ions. The Cd–N distances are actually uniform for all the compounds (2.337(3)–2.360(4) Å, Table 2), while the interlayer separations (defined as 1/6 of the c axis) are sensitive to the volume of the non-coordinating anions and increase from 8.84 Å (X = 2NO3−) to 9.54 Å for X = CdI42−.

Higher “concentration” of the triazole-N donor sites, as may be compared with a bitopic ligandtr2ad, is also beneficial for the generation of multiple acceptor centers in the form of polynuclear clusters (Scheme 4), which contribute to the progressive increase of the connectivity, complexity and versatility of the frameworks.

The structure of [Cu3(OH)2{tr3ada}2(H2O)4](ClO4)4 (8) reveals how the same topological result, the CdI2-like net, was feasible with participation of complex polynuclear clusters (Fig. 5). Within the trimetallic core, the central Cu1 ion is linked with two inversion-related outer Cu2 ions by means of the combined μ-OH/μ-tr “double bridge”31 (Cu1⋯Cu2 3.38; Cu–O 1.879(2) and 1.898(2) Å; the axial positions are occupied by distal oxygen atoms of ClO4− and aqua ligands, Table 4). The additional monodentate tr-groups, two at each of the Cu2 ions, determine a six-fold connectivity for a single trinuclear node. Similarly to the above group of compounds 4, 5 and 7, the organic ligands also act as three-connected nodes. Potential versatility for the structural functions of the tripod is also illustrated by the presence of three different triazole groups (Fig. 5b): short-distance 1,2-azole bridge (B); monodentate group (C) and monodentate ligand and acceptor of strong hydrogen bond (A).

An even more complex 2D framework connectivity was provided by the structure of the hydroxo sulfate [{Cu3(OH)}2·(SO4)5(H2O)3{tr3ad}3]·25H2O (6). Synergism of μ3-hydroxo and triazole bridges led to generation of tricopper units18,32,33 capped with tridentate sulfate anions and accommodating three terminal sulfates or monodentate triazoles at the equatorial plane (Scheme 4A and B; Table 5). Thus, two different trinuclear units, cationic [Cu3(μ3-OH){μ3-SO4}(μ-tr)3(tr)3]3+ (which itself has a molecular precedent in the compound with simple 4-amino-1,2,4-triazole)34 and anionic [Cu3(μ3-OH){μ3-SO4}(μ-tr)3(SO4)3]3− are present in 1 : 1 proportions. In the latter case the Cu–O(μ3) bonds are slightly elongated, while Cu–N(μ2-tr) bonds are shortened (Table 5).

Every organic ligand exhibits the pentadentate coordination with two μ-triazole groups integrated into the adjacent tricopper secondary building units (SBUs) and one triazole as a single N-donor. Considering only the first type of the tr groups, the metal–organic subtopology could be simplified to a hexagonal plane net with alternating cationic and anionic SBUs as topologically equivalent three-connected nodes (Fig. 6a), while monodentate tr-groups provide connections between pairs of the layers yielding a new 3D two-periodic three- and six-connected trinodal net (Fig. 6b), with a Schläfli point symbol {4·62}3{43·69·83}{63}. The net could be described as derived from a simple planar trigonal network, where every non-adjacent six-connected vertex is split into two symmetric trigonal vertices, which deflect above and below the network plane. The resulting double layers are separated by 12.13 Å and this packing provides sufficient space for the housing of a number of solvate water molecules (Fig. 7). As revealed by thermo-XRPD patterns, desolvation of the complex occurs at 150°, with loss of crystallinity.

For the compounds [Cu2{tr3ada}2(H2O)3](SO4)2·7H2O (9) and [Cd2{tr3ada}3]Cl4·28H2O (10), the local metal/triazole motifs are similar. They exist as similar binuclear clusters with a triple triazole bridge19,35 between two metal ions (Scheme 4D and E), while the total linkage of the cluster depends on the number of additional monodentate triazoles or terminal aqua ligands and increases from 6 (typical 4 + 2 coordination of Cu ions) to 9 (octahedral Cd ions).

In the first case, the combination of three μ-tr and three singly bonded tr groups is achieved with participation of two non-equivalent ligands as 3-connected nodes, which adopt tetra-(μ-tr + 2tr) and pentadentate (2μ-tr + tr) coordination for linking of three adjacent binuclear clusters (6-connected nodes). Although the overall 3D topology is a new trinodal 3,3,6-coordinated net (with a Schläfli point symbol {4·63·811}{4·82}{63}), it is closely related to the 2D “kgd” net in the above structures. Five out of six links of the binuclear cluster are situated in a plane, while the remaining one, with singly coordinating tr group, is found between the adjacent planes down the c axis and it extends the array in a third dimension. Thus only one of the links of the “kgd” net is broken and replaced by the interlayer bond, as it depicted in Fig. 8.

The resulting framework is open, with 30.5% of the solvent accessible area27 providing space for counter anions and a number of water molecules. The azaadamantane N-atoms contribute to the hydrophilic character of the framework voids and they are functional as acceptors of hydrogen bonds (N⋯O 2.92 Å; ∠OH⋯N 163°) with the solvate water. The compound is stable up to 155 °C, after which the diffraction pattern disappears indicating loss of the crystallinity.

The very elegant framework of [Cd2{tr3ada}3]Cl4·28H2O (10) demonstrates a further structural possibility of the system. The binuclear SBU is closely related to that of the example above, while substitution of three terminal water molecules for the singly coordinated triazole groups increases the connectivity of the node up to nine, with almost uniform Cd–N distances (2.313(5)–2.358(5) Å, Table 2). The organic ligands are tetradentate (one bridging and two singly coordinating tr groups), they link three of the binuclear units and provide three-connected nodes of the framework. In this way, a very salient and uncommon 3D binodal 9- and 3-coordinated net is formed with topology Schläfli symbol {412·615·89}{43}3 (three-letter notation “gfy”; structural type UCl3).36,37

The array may also be viewed as a chain of Cd ions along the c axis, linked in two different fashions: by triple 1,2-triazole bridges at 4.22 Å (binuclear SBU) and by triple 1,1′-bridges at 10.18 Å (Fig. 9a). The latter fragment generates small hydrophobic cages and entraps non-coordinating chloride ions using a set of six symmetry equivalent CH⋯Cl hydrogen bonds (C⋯Cl 3.47 Å, ∠CH⋯Cl 178°).38 The entire structure contains large hexagonal channels along the c axis (Fig. 9b) populated with disordered chloride anions and water molecules. The total guest accessible area of the framework is as large as 2097 Å3 per unit cell, or 48.8% of the crystal volume.26 The 1-azaadamantane N-atoms, as potential acceptors of strong hydrogen bonding,39 are directed towards the channel center. Similarly to complex 9, these sites may be important for the functionalization of the crystal cages for specific interactions with hydrophilic guest species. The structure of the complex remains intact up to 130 °C and the further desolvation processes at 130° and 200° are indicated by the PXRD patterns. Disintegration of the framework structure and loss of crystallinity occur in the 200–330° range.

Thus, the inherent molecular symmetry and shape of the tripodal ligand has a clear significance for the resulting architecture and this demonstrates new synthetic prospects for the “kgd” layers and MOFs incorporating trigonal [Cu3(μ3-OH)]18,32 and [M2(μ-tr)3] clusters.

Complexes of the tetrahedral ligandtr4ad

The tetrafunctional tecton tr4ad has valuable and unique potential for crystal design, especially for the bridging of polynuclear clusters. However, in the examined compounds 11–13 the connectivity of the ligands is lower, providing formation of simpler architectures. In part this may be attributed to too high a “concentration” of the binding sites (eight triazole-N donor atoms per molecular unit). Specific shape-complementary close packings of the large tetrakis-arylsubstituted molecules, similarly to the structure of tetraphenyladamantane,40 could be also important.

The complexes [Ag{tr4ad}]NO3·3.5H2O (11) and [Cu{tr4ad}(H2O)](ClO4)2·3H2O (12) demonstrate a similar topological result achieved with the metal ions in very different environments. In the first case, a monodentate coordination of every triazole group and tetrahedral [AgN4] geometry at the silver ions (Ag–N 2.255(4)–2.365(5) Å, Table 3) leads to the assembly of a 3D framework. Both Ag ions and the organic ligands constitute the tetrahedral nodes in a 1 : 1 proportion, and the latter are topologically equivalent. This yields a uninodal {42·63·8} net, usually related to the SrAl2 structure (“sra” topology).41,42 A single cationic framework occupies only 24.7% of the crystal volume,27 and the entire structure originates in interpenetration of two equivalent frameworks (Fig. 10). That is a class IIa interpenetration, Z = 2, with a full symmetry element .42,43

In the case of the copper(ii) complex 12, the tr4adligand is bound in a similar fashion, while the central atom adopts 4 + 1 coordination with four triazole-N donors in the basal plane (Cu–N 1.997(5)–2.014(5) Å) and a distal apical aqua ligand (Cu–O 2.202(8) Å) (Fig. 11). The entire topology is the same “sra” net as in the above structure 11. However, the geometry at the Cu1 node and the presence of the additional aqua ligand explains the increased proper volume of the framework, which now occupies 61.9% of the crystal volume27 and therefore the interpenetration disappears. The remaining crystal space is occupied by ClO4− anions and solvate water molecules.

Partial utilization of the available N-donor functionality was also observed for the complex [Cd3Cl6{tr4ad}2]·9H2O (13), in which the two tr groups are N1,N2-bidentate (Cd–N 2.315(5), 2.384(5) Å), one is monodentate (Cd–N 2.317(6) Å) and the fourth one is left uncoordinated. The structure features the formation of a neutral trinuclear cluster [Cd3(μ-Cl)2(μ-tr)4Cl4(tr)2] (Scheme 4F), in which the cadmium ions are linked by triple bridges of two triazoles and one chloride (Fig. 12) and two additional monodentate tr groups at the outer Cd ions increase connectivity of the cluster up to six. Since the organic ligands link three adjacent clusters, the resulting 3D topology could be simplified as a 3- and 6-coordinated binodal rutile net with a Schläfli symbol {4·62}2{42·610·83} (three-letter notation “rtl”).44 Such complementary action of the chloride and triazole bridges45 may be applicable for the further modification of M/tr motifs, similarly to the hydroxo and triazole cobridges, which are characteristic of the Cu compounds.

Conclusions

Our study is important for developing innovative strategies towards modular assembly of MOF solids with utilization of polyvalent building blocks whose inherent, designed at the molecular level, configurations could provide desired features for the topology and functionality of the lattices. In particular, the heteroaryl substituted adamantanes are examples of realistic chemical systems, combining such inputs as rigid structure and polyvalency of the molecular framework. They are especially suited for the generation of trigonal layered structures and open 3D frameworks with the nodes augmented by incorporation of polynuclear metal ion clusters. Our synthetic protocols suggest a convenient way to make families of bridge-head N-functionalized adamantanes, such as the 1,2,4-triazole species, that may find wider applications in supramolecular synthesis. Due to the specific functionality and chemical availability, the 1-azaadamantane scaffold could be viewed as an especially promising find, facilitating access to a series of rigidized tripodal molecular building blocks.

Experimental

Synthesis of the ligands

All chemicals were of reagent grade and used as received without further purification. 1,3-Diaminoadamantane,46 1,3,5-trihydroxyadamantane47 and 1,3,5,7-adamantanetetracarboxylic acid23 were synthesized by literature methods. Preparation methods for the key intermediate materials, which are of general interest for chemistry of functionalized adamantanes (1-azaadamantanes), are presented below.

Synthesis of 1,3,5-adamantanetricarboxylic acid

5.00 g (27.2 mmol) of 1,3,5-trihydroxyadamantane was added in small portions to 220 mL of pre-cooled (0 °C) 15% oleum (d = 1.87 g cm−3) with intensive stirring. The resulting light yellow solution was heated up to 50 °C (water bath) and 40 mL of 100% HCOOH was added dropwise under intensive stirring with increasing speed over a period of 2 h (Caution: the evolution of CO gas during addition of the first drops is rapid, a free outlet should be available). After the addition, the mixture was cooled to room temperature and stirred overnight. The solution was poured onto 1.5 kg of crushed ice and allowed to stand for 5 h, then the colorless crystalline precipitate was filtered off. It was repeatedly washed with water and dried at 100 °C. The yield was 4.51 g, 62% (72% for the synthesis using 1.00 g scale of 1,3,5-trihydroxyadamantane). 1H NMR (dmso-d6), δ: 1.70 (6H, d, CH2), 1.81 (6H, dd, CH2), 2.17 (1H, m, CH).

Synthesis of 1,3,5-adamantanetricarboxamide

A mixture of 3.50 g (11.2 mmol) of 1,3,5-adamantanetricarboxylic acid, 20 mL of SOCl2 and one drop of DMF was gently refluxed for 2 h under argon until a clear solution formed. Then the excess SOCl2 was removed under heating in a weak argon stream. The pale yellow solid of the chloranhydride was quickly ground in a mortar and added (within 20 min of being ground) to pre-cooled 100 mL of 25% aqueous ammonia with intensive stirring (0 °C). The solution with the colorless precipitate was concentrated under reduced pressure at 50 °C to a volume of 20 mL, the product was filtered and washed with water. The yield was 2.93 g (85%). 1H NMR (dmso-d6), δ: 1.63 (6H, d, CH2), 1.71 (6H, dd, CH2), 2.14 (1H, m, CH), 6.78, 7.03 (6H, s, NH2).

Synthesis of 1,3,5-triaminoadamantane

To a solution of 2.72 g NaOH in 25 mL of water, 0.69 mL (13.6 mmol) of bromine was added over a period of 10 min with efficient stirring at 0 °C. Solid 1,3,5-adamantanetricarboxamide (1.00 g, 3.77 mmol) was added at once, the mixture was stirred for 1 h at room temperature and for 1 h at 50–60 °C. Then the mixture was acidified with HCl to pH = 2 and refluxed for a short time. The cooled solution was made strongly alkaline with 10% NaOH and evaporated to dryness under reduced pressure. The residue was sublimed at 140 °C (0.03 Torr) yielding 0.45 g (66%) of pure colorless triamine. 1H NMR (dmso-d6), δ: 1.18 (6H, s, CH2), 1.24 (6H, d, CH2), 2.05 (1H, m, CH).

Synthesis of 1,3,5,7-adamantanetetracarboxamide

1.56 g (5.00 mmol) of the 1,3,5,7-adamantanetetracarboxylic acid was gently refluxed in a mixture of 10 mL of SOCl2 with one drop of DMF with stirring for 3 h (dissolution of the starting material was observed within 1.5 h). The excess SOCl2 was removed under heating in an argon stream, while pale yellow solid chloranhydride remained in the flask. The residue was taken up in 30 mL of dry toluene and poured at once into 25 mL of cold 25% aqueous ammonia. The precipitation of the product began immediately; the mixture was stirred for 30 min at room temperature and then for 30 min at reflux. The fine powdery deposit was filtered off, washed four times with water and two times with methanol and dried at 100 °C (yield: 1.41 g, 92%). 1H NMR (dmso-d6), δ: 2.39 (12H, s, CH2), 3.5 (8H, s, NH2).

Synthesis of 1,3,5,7-tetraaminoadamantane

0.46 mL (9.0 mmol) of bromine was added dropwise to a solution of 2.00 g (50 mmol) NaOH in 16 mL water at 0 °C with stirring. After 5 min the 1,3,5,7-adamantanetetracarboxamide (0.615 g, 2 mmol) was added in one portion to the light yellow solution. The solid dissolved within 10 min, after which the solution was heated at 60 °C for 1 h. Then the solution was acidified to pH = 1 using HCl and was boiled for a short time. The cooled solution was basified using 10% NaOH solution to pH = 11 and evaporated to dryness. The slightly brownish residue was divided between 15 mL of saturated K2CO3 solution and 10 mL of 2-propanol. The aqueous layer was further extracted with 4 × 10 mL of 2-propanol, the combined organic extracts were dried over K2CO3, filtered carefully washing the solid residues and evaporated to dryness. Sublimation at 130–170 °C (0.01 Torr) yields 0.199 g of the pure product. An additional 0.063 g portion of crude amine could be obtained by further sublimation at 170–200 °C. The combined yield was 0.273 g (70%). 1H NMR (dmso-d6), δ: 1.09 (12H, s, CH2).

Synthesis of 1,3,5-trinitro-1,3,5-tris(hydroxymethyl)-cyclohexane (mixture of isomers)

30.0 g NaBH4 was dissolved in a pre-cooled (−10 °C) mixture of 500 mL methanol and 500 mL water and a solution of 40.0 g 1,3,5-trinitrobenzene in 240 mL THF was added dropwise for 45 min under efficient stirring and cooling (−4 to −7 °C). Then a solution of 50.0 g tartaric acid in 400 mL of cold water was added, the mixture was stirred for an additional 20 min followed by addition of 40.0 g solid K2CO3 (to pH = 8). 350 mL of 38% formaldehyde solution was added over a period of 30 min under efficient stirring, which led to dissolution of the initially precipitated oil of 1,3,5-trinitrocyclohexane. The stirring was continued for 24 h at room temperature. The colorless deposit of the product was filtered, it was thoroughly washed with water and methanol and dried in air. Yield: 50.36 g (87%).

Synthesis of 3,5,7-trinitro-1-azaadamantane

54.9 g of the above trimethylolcyclohexane (finely powdered) was suspended in 5.5 L of cold water (5 °C), then 29.5 mL of 25% ammonia and 60 mL water were added dropwise over a period of 2 h and the stirring was continued at room temperature for 12 days, while a dark-brown color developed. Some concentrated HCl was added dropwise to adjust the pH to 5 and the mixture was filtered through a 2 cm layer of macerated paper. The product (which was mixed with some of the macerated paper) was dried and finely powdered in a mortar, after which it was extracted with four 0.7 L portions of boiling chloroform. The combined extract was refluxed for 15 min with 20 g charcoal, then it was filtered through a 2 cm layer of Al2O3 and evaporated under reduced pressure to a volume of 100 mL. Colorless crystals of the product were filtered, washed with chloroform and purified by recrystallization from acetone. The yield was 23.2 g (48%). 1H NMR (dmso-d6), δ: 2.91 (6H, dd, CCH2), 3.39 (6H, s, NCH2).

Synthesis of 3,5,7-triamino-1-azaadamantane

2.80 g (10.3 mmol) of 3,5,7-trinitro-1-azaadamantane was stirred in 45 mL of 2-propanol at 50 °C, 0.3 mL of RANEY® Ni suspension in 2-propanol was added and a solution of 6.0 mL of N2H4·H2O in 10 mL 2-propanol was added dropwise over a period of 14 h at 50–55 °C. Two additional portions of RANEY® Ni suspension (0.3 mL) were added after 4 h and 8 h of the reaction. The mixture was cooled to room temperature, filtered and evaporated under reduced pressure. The semi-solid residue was mixed with Na2SO4 and sublimed at 130–140 °C/0.1 Torr yielding 1.60 g (86%) of the pure colorless product. 1H NMR (dmso-d6), δ: 1.26 (6H, m, CCH2), 2.43 (6H, s, NCH2).

The triazoleligands were prepared in an acid-catalyzed condensation from the corresponding amines and N,N-dimethylformamide azine48 in yields of 50–70%. In the case of 1,3-diaminoadamantane, the synthesis may be carried out in boiling xylene.46 These conditions were not satisfactory for tri- and tetrasubstituted adamantanes due to the poor solubility of partially derivatized intermediates and formation of inseparable mixtures. However, the reactions proceed smoothly in refluxing N,N-dimethylacetamide (DMA) following the general protocol: a mixture of 1 eq of the amine, 2–3 eqs of DMF azine and 6–7.5 mol% of p-toluenesulfonic acid monohydrate was heated in DMA (the amount of DMA was 10–25 times that of the other components, by total weight of the components) for 24–48 h until the evolution of NHMe2 gas ceased. After removal of the solvent under reduced pressure the residue was thoroughly triturated with CH3CN yielding the crystalline product.

Synthesis of 1,3-bis(1,2,4-triazol-4-yl)adamantane (tr2ad)

A solution of 1,3-diaminoadamantane (1.66 g, 10 mmol), DMF azine (4.27 g, 30 mmol) and TsOH·H2O (0.30 g) in 30 mL of DMA was gently refluxed for 20 h (external bath temperature 180 °C). The solvent was evaporated under a reduced pressure at 50–60 °C and the solid residue was triturated with 20 mL of CH3CN, yielding a colorless powdery precipitate. The product was filtered off, washed thoroughly with acetonitrile and dried in air at 80 °C. The yield of pure crystalline triazole was 2.24 g (83%). Preparation of the coordination compounds

In view of the poor solubility of the organic ligands, especially of the tri- and tetrafunctional adamantanes, all the metal complexes were prepared under hydrothermal conditions as follows. A mixture of the components and distilled water was placed into a 20 mL Teflon vessel and heated in a steel autoclave to the temperature range of 160–180 °C for 24–40 h. Cooling of the reaction mixture to room temperature at a rate of 2–2.5 °C h−1 yields the crystalline products. In many cases the behaviour of the trifunctional ligandstr3ad and tr3ada was different, which could be attributed to an appreciably better solubility of the latter. Actually only one case illustrates formation of isotypic complexes (4, 5 and 7), while the structures of products formed by these tripodal ligands, CdCl2 and CuSO4 are not uniform.

Measurements

NMR spectra were recorded with a Bruker 400 MHz spectrometer. The X-ray powder diffraction was carried out on a Stoe STADI P using Cu-Kα radiation and a linear position sensitive detector. The temperature dependent X-ray measurements were carried out using the same device with a high temperature attachment and an image plate detector system.

Single crystal X-ray crystallography

Crystallographic measurements were made at 213 K using a Stoe Image Plate Diffraction System (for 3, 7–9 and 12, numerical absorption correction using X-RED and X-SHAPE)49 and at 100 K using a Bruker APEXII CCD area-detectordiffractometer for 1, 2, 4–6, 10, 11, 13 (φ scans) with graphite-monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). The data were corrected for Lorentz-polarization effects and for the effects of absorption (multi-scan method). The structures were solved by direct methods and refined in anisotropic approximation using SHELXS-97 and SHELXL-9750,51 (Tables 6 and 7). All CH-hydrogen atoms were added geometrically, with Uiso = 1.2Ueq (parent C-atom).

Crystals of 1 emulate an orthorhombic cell (Rint = 0.105) yet no solution was found in any of the possible orthorhombic space groups. The structure was solved based on a monoclinic cell (P21/n, β ≈ 90°) and refined as pseudomerohedral twin with nearly equal contributions of two domains (0.56 and 0.44). Crystals of 3 exhibit non-merohedral twinning. The data frames were indexed and integrated as a two-domain system using RECIPE/TWIN facilities implemented in the Stoe IPDS software.52 The data were scaled and merged yielding a single dataset of about 84% completeness due to a partial overlap of the reflections, which correspond to the different domains of the crystal.

For structures 4, 5 and 7, the non-coordinating NO3−, CdCl42− and CdI42− anions are equally disordered over two symmetry-related positions overlapping with the solvate water molecules. These atoms were refined anisotropically and only for 4 a set of restraints in the geometry and thermal values of the nitrate anions was applied. Disorder of non-coordinating NO3− anions in 1 (two equal contributions of 0.5) and 11 (0.6 and 0.4), ClO4− anions in 12 (0.7 and 0.3) and coordinating SO42− anions in 6 was refined with restrained geometry (±0.01 Å). For the disordered ClO4− anions in 8 (0.8, 0.2), the major contribution was freely refined, while atoms of the minor component were left isotropic and bond distances and isotropic thermal values were restrained. In the structure of 10, the non-coordinating chloride anions and solvate water molecules populating large hexagonal channels are totally disordered. This electron density was successfully modelled using a Squeeze routine implemented in Platon.27 The same approach was applied for modelling of 10 badly disordered solvate water molecules in the structure 6. In this case only 7 out of 16 located solvate molecules were refined anisotropically, but the hydrogen atoms were not added due to the above disorder of SO42− hydrogen bond acceptors. Disorder of some of the solvate water molecules was also resolved for the structures of 10, 12 and 13. For 1–3, 5, and 7–9 all the OH–hydrogen atoms were located and then constrained with d(O–H) = 0.85 Å and Uiso = 1.5Ueq (parent O-atom).

Graphical visualization of the structures was made using the program Diamond 2.1e,53 and the topological analysis was performed using TOPOS 4.0.54
