Introduction

In recent years, great interest has been focused on the rapidly expanding field of metal–organic coordination polymers.1 It is well known that aromatic carboxylic acids exhibit a variety of bridging modes and coordination fashions and have been widely used to construct coordination polymers with specific network topologies and interesting properties.2 In the rational design and synthesis of metal–organic frameworks, rigid polycarboxylates, for example benzenepolycarboxylates and pyridinepolycarboxylates, have been extensively employed to produce various extended structures.3 Nevertheless, only a few coordination polymers based on ligands with flexible conformations have hitherto been reported.4 This is probably due to the flexibility of the ligand backbones, which makes it more difficult to predict and control their orientation in the final coordination networks. Different from benzene-hexacarboxylic acid, 1,2,3,4,5,6-cyclohexanehexacarboxylate (H6L) possesses diverse conformations due to its single-bonded cyclohexane skeleton in combination with equatorial or axial orientations of the carboxyl groups, leading to abundant coordination behaviors. However, H6L has been less studied as a building block in the construction of metal–organic frameworks, and the reported research has been mainly restricted to those with fully deprotonated L6− forms as ligands.5 Many investigations have been focused on the flexible 1,4- or 1,3-cyclohexanedicarboxylic acids in view of their cis- and trans-conformations.6 H6L, which is characterized by multiple binding sites and pH-dependent coordination modes and shows versatility through its flexible conformations, has attracted our great interest in studying its conformational transformations and use in constructing metal–organic frameworks.7 H6L adopts the all-cis (Scheme 1, LI) conformation with multiple hydrogen bonds, which can be converted in situ to the trans-form or cis, trans-mixed forms under different hydrothermal conditions (Scheme 1).8 Tong and coworkers have observed two predominant conformations, LI at room temperature and LII under hydrothermal conditions.7d Theoretical calculations have shown that the all-cis LI stereoisomer is the second less stable conformation.9 Although, only the all-cisligand is offered by commercial sources, the Tong group has obtained various isomers of the coordination complexes, the most important conformation is the all-trans LII one.7d,7e,9 It is necessary to breakdown and rearrange some C–H bonds when using the all-cisligand to achieve typical conformations of the trans-one, energetic gap between different conformations could be granted using high temperature synthesis, in particular, hydrothermal conditions. This approach, although fruitful to trap the different isomers, lacks the controlled conditions which can lead to a more systematic investigation. In preparation of this manuscript, several papers appeared in the literature.5,10 In all published papers, the partially or fully deprotonated cyclohexanehexacarboxylate acts as the ligand coordinating to a metal ion. There is still no precedent for cyclohexanehexacarboxylate acting as a counter ion without any coordination.

We have been interested in the design and synthesis of coordination polymers showing an unusual topology structure.3e,11 In this paper, we report the synthesis, crystal structure and properties of nine novel crystals by using different alkalis and under different reaction conditions to elucidate the delicate synthetic condition–structure–properties relationship.

Experimental section

All chemicals for syntheses are analytical grade and used as purchased without further purification. Elemental analyses for carbon, hydrogen and nitrogen were carried out on a Vario EL III elemental analyzer. Infrared spectra were recorded on a Nicolet A370FT-IR spectrometer using KBr pellets in the 400–4000 cm−1 region. TGA experiments were performed on a Shimadzu DT-20B thermogravimetric analyzer from 20 to 800 °C at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 in nitrogen. The fluorescence spectra were measured on a Shimadzu RF-5301 spectrophotometer. Powder X-ray diffraction patterns were recorded on DX-2700 X-ray diffractometer (Dandong Haoyuan company) with graphite monochromatic CuKα (1.54056 Å) radiation at 40 kV/250 Ma.

Synthesis of {[Zn3(η9-LI)(η2-H2O)1(η1-H2O)7]·(H2O)5}3n (1)

2 ml aqueous solution of ZnSO4·7H2O (0.15 mmol, 57.5 mg) was slowly added to 2 ml aqueous solution containing H6L (0.05 mmol, 17.4 mg) and NaOH (0.1 mmol). The mixture was ultrasonicated for 10 min. The resulting solution was filtered and the filtrate was allowed to stand at room temperature. After five weeks well-shaped colorless rhombic single crystals were obtained with yield 58.3% based on Zn. FT-IR (KBr, cm−1): 3427(w), 1537(vs), 1398(m), 1321(m), 1165(w), 1041(w), 1003(w), 894(m), 853(w), 817(w), 696(w), 514(w). Anal. calcd (%) for C12H32O25Zn3: C, 18.64; H, 4.14. Found: C, 19.14; H, 4.12.

X-ray crystallography

The well-shaped single crystals of 1–9 were selected for X-ray diffraction study. Data were collected on a Bruker SMART CCD diffractometer with graphite monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71703 Å) by using the phi and omega scan method at 293(2) K. The structures were solved by direct method with SHELXS-97 program12 and refined by full-matrix least-squares on F2 with SHELXL-97 program.13 All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically and hydrogen atoms were located and included at their calculated positions. Results and discussion

Description of the crystal structure

{[Zn3(η9-LI)(η2-H2O)1(η1-H2O)7]·(H2O)5}3n (1) and {[Co3(η9-LI)(η2-H2O)1(η1-H2O)7]·(H2O)5}3n (2)

X-ray crystallography reveals that complex 1 and 2 are isomorphous which crystallized in the P21/n space group. There are three crystallographically independent metal ions in complexes 1 and 2. All the carboxylate groups are deprotonated, which coincides with the absence of ∼1700 cm−1 in the IR spectrum. There are three M(ii) (M = Co, Zn), one L6−, eight coordinated water molecules and five lattice water molecules in the asymmetric unit. All M(ii) ions are in an octahedron environment (Fig. 1a). Three Co centers have similar {CoO6} distorted octahedral coordination spheres. Co1 coordinated by two carboxylate oxygen atoms (O7 and O11) from two L6−ligands, two bridging water molecules (O15 and O15′), and two monodentate water molecules (O13 and O14). The Co2 is coordinated to three carboxylate oxygen (O6, O8 and O9) and three monodentate water molecules (O16, O17 and O18). While Co3 coordinates to four carboxylate oxygens (O1 and O12 from one ligand and O4 and O5 from another ligand), two monodentate water molecules (O19 and O20). Co–O(carboxylate) distances are in the ranges of 2.051(4) (Co3–O4#3) to 2.106(4) (Co1–O7#1) Å. Co–O(water) distances are longer at 2.073(4) (Co1–O13) to 2.156(5) (Co2–O18) Å. The smallest bond angle is O(15)–Co(1)–O(15)#1 at 80.74(14)°. All other angles are close to ∼90° or 180° except bridging O15 related O(13)–Co(1)–O(15) 166.61(15)°. We have proposed a parameter σ to quantify how distorted an octahedral geometry can be,3e where σ = [amin + amax − 180]/90, amin and amax mean the min and max angles in an octahedral. For an ideal octahedral complex, σ is 1, or 100% octahedron. For a distorted octahedral complex, σ is expected to be less than 1. One can simply compare the relative distortions by comparing σ values of different six-coordinated species. The σ around Co1, Co2 and Co3 in complex 2 are 0.89, 0.92 and 0.91 respectively, which indicate that Co1 distorted higher than Co2 and Co3.

The fully deprotonated L6− exhibits a chair configuration. The six carboxylate groups arrange alternatively in a,e fashion (Scheme 1, LI). Three carboxylates (C1, C4 and C5 containing) coordinate to the metal ion in cis-trans 1,3-bridge fashion. All other carboxylates are monodentate. Each L6− provides nine oxygen atoms coordinated to the metal ion. Fig. 1b is the simplified packing diagram from the a-axis. The complex has the largest pore of dimension 9 × 12 Å channel as shown in (Fig. 1c). PLATON calculation shows that the effective volume for the inclusion is about 397.2 Å3 and 403.4 Å3 per unit cell, comprising 16.0% and 16.4% of crystal volume in complexes 1 and 2, respectively.14 The complex contains a considerable amount of water molecules as indicated in the formulae. It is the hydrogen bondings that bind these water molecules together. As shown in Fig. 1d, there are water channels from the ac plane. The water channel is linked via O19, O21, O22, O24 and O25 where O25 located in the water-channel center. The repeat unit of the hydrogen-bond channel has a distance of 14.301(6) Å. All water molecules are located in the channel except O12. In these water molecules, O21 and O25 are fully saturated by hydrogen bonds. O18 forms hydrogen bonds with O21 and O13, but may also have some weaker interaction with O16 and O23 as the O⋯O distances are shorter than 3.0 Å. The average O⋯O distances range from 2.692(7) Å to 2.871(9) Å along the chain, maintaining strong water–MOF interactions as well.15 All the bond lengths and angles in 2 are comparable to those in 1. The Zn–O is about 0.01 Å longer than the corresponding Co–O distances as shown in Table s1 in the ESI.†

Topological analysis indicates that L6− in 1 and 2 are 5-connected nodes, while metal ions are 3-connected nodes as shown in Fig. S1a in the ESI.† Complex 1 and 2 can be represented as a 3,5-connected network with Schlafli notation of {32;4}{3;63;86}.

{[Cu5(η8-HLI)2·10H2O]·(H2O)4}3n (3)

A single-crystal X-ray diffraction study revealed that complex 3 recrystallizes in the space group C2/c. There are three crystallographically independent Cu atoms and one HL5−ligand. Cu1 is obviously in an enlongated octahedron environment. Cu1 located in the symmetry center coordinated to two O1 from two HL5−ligand and four coordinated water molecules to form an octahedral environment (Fig. 2a). Cu1–O3W has the longest distances at 2.404(2) Å. All other Cu1–O distances are 1.94–1.96 Å. Cu2 coordinates to four carboxylate oxygen atoms from three HL5−ligands (O5 from one ligand, O9 from another one, O2 and O3 from the third one) and one water molecule to form a square pyramidal environment (τ = 0.12). O9 coordinates to Cu2 at 2.536(2) Å apically, which is much longer than the other Cu2–O distances of 1.91–1.96 Å, but still obviously shorter than the sum of the van der Waals radii of Cu and O at ∼3.2 Å. Cu3 coordinates to O10 and O11 from the adjacent carboxylate of a ligand, O12 from the other ligand and two water molecules to form a square-pyramidal environment (τ = 0.18). It is O5w that coordinates to Cu3 apically at distances of 2.342(2) Å. The apical O–Cu distance around Cu3 is shorter than that around Cu2. However, all other distances around Cu3 are slightly longer than that around Cu2. The ligand is partially protonated. Except the protonated carboxylate (C10), all other carboxylate are deprotonated and coordinated to Cu(ii) ions. Of the five coordinating carboxylate, three of them are in 1, 3-bridging fashion (O1, O2, O9, O10, O11 and O12), the other two are monodentate carboxylates. Thus the HL5−ligand forms 8 coordination bonds. The cyclohexane ring adopts a chair conformation. Similar to that in complex 2, six carboxylates in HL5− arranges in all-cis fashion (Scheme 1, LI). There are ten coordinating water molecules and four cocrystallized water molecules in the complex. There is a proton that binds to carboxylate O7, which is in agreement with the IR spectrum with an absorption peak above 1700 cm−1. Except the protonated carboxylate (O8C10O7), which is on the e position (perpendicular to the central cyclohexane least plane), all other carboxylates are deprotonated and coordinated to Cu(ii). Each HL5−ligand connects one Cu1, three Cu2 and two Cu3 atoms through its five carboxylate groups. The HL5− bridges through Cu ions into a 3D porous polymer. In Fig. 2b, the cyclohexane moiety was represented as a pentagon. The red sticks represent C1–O1–Cu1–O1–C1 bonds. From these figures, complex 3 is a porous coordination framework. It has the largest pore in the c-axis as indicated in Fig. 2c and 2d. Calculations using PLATON show that the effective volume for the inclusion is about 415.9 Å3 per unit cell. The coordinated water and lattice water donate hydrogen bonds with oxygen atoms from HL5− and water (Fig. 2e). Topological analysis indicate that metal ion and ligand are in a (3,6)-connected network with Schafli symbol of {43}{45;67;83} as shown in Fig. S1b in the ESI.†

{[Ni3(η12-LII)(η1-H2O)6]·1.5H2O}3n (4)

Complex 4 is the highest symmetry compound in all of the cyclohexanehexacarboxylate complexes. The asymmetric unit of 4 contains 1/3 of a ligand L6− and one Ni(ii) (Fig. 3a). Its composition and structure are similar to complex [Co3L(H2O)6]n reported by Wang et al.7e The synthesis procedure is somewhat different from Wang's method. We added 4-aminopyridine rather than 2,2′-dipyridyl. Complex 4 is almost identical to [Co3L(H2O)6]n except for the cocrystallized water molecules. In 4, carboxylate O1, O2, O3 and O4 are all coordinate to the metal ion. Two water molecules coordinate to the metal ion axially. In the framework of 4, L6− adopt all-trans configuration (Scheme 1, LII). All Ni–O distances are in the range between 2.044(4) and 2.081(4) Å, which is obviously shorter than Co–O distances (2.080(4) ∼2.139(4) Å) in [Co3L(H2O)6]n.7e There exists 1.5 water molecules per Ni3(η12-LII) unit, which is consistent with the EA analysis. However, the TG curve does not show these extra 1.5 water molecules possibly due to them being unstable at room temperature (Fig S10, in the ESI†). There are three kinds of water guest molecules in the asymmetric unit, fully occupied O1W and O2W and 1/2 site occupancy O3W. The water cluster can be described as a chair like hexamer (formed by six O2Ws) and a planar like hexamer (formed by six O1Ws). The planar and chair-shaped cyclic water hexamers are shown in Fig. 3b. The planar water hexamer is much stronger as it has shorter O⋯O distances (2.856(2) Å) than that in the chair-shaped water hexamer (3.086(2) Å). Different from Wang's complex in which the O⋯O distances are 2.834(2) Å and 3.101(2) Å within chair and planar water hexamers, respectively.7e The half occupied cocrystallized water molecule (O3w) in 4 links planar and chair-shaped water hexamers (Fig. 3b). The special distance of O3W⋯O2W, O3W⋯O1W or O3W⋯O3 is shorter than the shortest OW⋯OW distance (2.318(4) Å) reported by Mak et al.16 due to half-occupancy of O3w. The IR absorption for the water cluster in the present supramolecular complex occurs at 3335 cm−1 and 3470 cm−1 which is consistent to the reported data.17 The observed differences in the distances of O⋯O molecular should be explained by the differences in the matrices in which the cluster is formed. The other O⋯O distances (range from 2.790 to 2.842 Å) has some characteristics similar to those observed in liquid water (2.854 Å)18 or ice (2.740–2.760 Å).19 Each ligand binds to nine Ni atoms. Three of the Ni atoms bind to the ligandvia O2 and O3 to form seven membered rings. Each ligand provides all its twelve carboxylate oxygen atoms to form twelve coordination bonds. All twelve carboxylate oxygen atoms are coordinated to the metal ion in complex 4. This is one of the largest coordination numbers the ligand can form until now.5,8,20 PLATON calculations show that the effective void volume in 4 is about 74.7 Å3 per unit cell, comprising 2.9% of the crystal volume, which is much less than that in complexes 1, 2 and 3. From the viewpoint of network topology, complex 4 can be simplified as a 3,9-connected 9-noted network, the Schlafli symbol for this uniform network is {42;6}3{46;621;89} (Fig. S1c, ESI†).

{[Zn(η3-H4LI)(4,4′-bipy)(η1-H2O)]·(H2O)2}n (5)

The structure of 5 is shown in Fig. 4. In complex 5, four protons bind to four carboxylate oxygen atoms. Each Zn(ii) atom displays a distorted octahedral coordination geometry {ZnN2O4}, coordinated by three oxygen atoms from two deprotonated carboxylate and one oxygen from the water molecule and two 4,4′-bpy nitrogen atoms. C1 carboxylate chelates to Zn(ii) at Zn–O distances of 2.287(2) and 2.308(2) Å respectively. C11 carboxylate coordinates to Zn(ii) in a monodentate fashion (via O9) at 2.048(2) Å, which is significantly shorter than chelating carboxylate oxygen atoms. Both N atoms of 4,4′-bpy coordinate to Zn(ii), Zn–N distances are 2.147(3) and 2.159(3) Å respectively. There is a water molecule that also coordinates to the metal ion at 2.057(2) Å. The largest bond angle is N(1)–Zn(1)–N(2)#2 (177.18(10)°). The smallest bond angle is O(1)–Zn(1)–O(2) [56.78(8)°] (σ = 0.60). H4L2− adopts an all-cis configuration (Scheme 1, LI). This complex is a 2D square grid. 4,4′-bpy links Zn(ii) from the b-axis with Zn⋯Zn distances of 11.34 Å. While H4L2− links Zn(ii) from the a-axis with Zn⋯Zn distances of 10.29 Å. From the a-axis, the grid packs alternatively as shown in Fig. 4c. The three protonated carboxylates arrange up and down in two adjacent 2D coordination grids.

{[Zn2(η4-H3LI)(1,10-phen)3·(η1-NO3)]·H2O}1n (6)

Complex 6 is a 1D chain in the space group C2/c. The asymmetric unit of 6 contains two Zn atoms, one H3L3−ligand, three 1,10-phenanthrolineligands, one nitrate anion and one water molecule (Fig. 5a). Thus Zn1 can be best described in a triangle bipyramidal coordination environment {ZnN2O3}. Zn1 center is coordinated by two N atoms from 1,10-phenanthroline molecule at 2.091(3) and 2.157(3) Å respectively. One oxygen atom from NO3− coordinate to Zn1 at 2.058(4) Å , and two oxygen atoms from two H3L3−ligands (e position) at 1.985(2) and 2.024(2) Å. Thus Zn2 can be described as octahedron coordination geometry {ZnN4O2}, coordinated by four N atoms from two 1,10-phenanthroline molecules at distances of 2.072(3) to 2.160(3) Å. Two carboxylate oxygen atoms from H3L3−ligand chelate to Zn(ii) at 2.037(2) (O10) and 2.402(2) (O9) Å, respectively. Complex 6 is a 1D coordination polymer as shown in Fig. 5b. Zn1 located in the coordination chain, while Zn2 acts as side arm. The Zn1⋯Zn1 distances in the chain is 10.4123(6) Å. There is π–π interaction between the neighboring 1,10-phenanthrolineligands as their distances are ∼3.49 Å from b-axis (Fig. S2a and Fig. S2b, ESI†).

{[Cd2(η4-H4LI)2(2,2′-bipy)2(η1-H2O)2]·(2,2′-bipy)·(H2O)3}1n (7)

Similar to that in 6, {[Cd2(η4-H4LI)2(2,2′-bipy)2(η1-H2O)2]·(2,2′-bipy)·(H2O)3}1n (7) is a 1D chain but crystallized in monoclinic P21/c space group. The asymmetric unit of 7 contains two Cd atoms, two H4L2−ligand, two 2,2′-bipyridineligands. There is one water molecule that coordinates to Cd ion. Thus each Cd is in a seven coordinated environment {CdN2O5}. Each Cd coordinates to one 2,2′-bipyridine, two 1,3-chelate carboxylates and one water molecule (Fig. 6a). The Cd–N distances are ∼2.32 Å. While the Cd–O (carboxylate) distances varies from the shortest Cd2–O13 at 2.271(7) Å to the longest Cd2–O14 at 2.774(7) Å. Cd–O(W) distances are 2.269(8) and 2.300(9) Å respectively. Both H4L2− adopt all-cis configuration as shown in Fig. 6a. It is worth to note that it is the two deprotonated carboxylate, in the e-position, that chelate to the metal ion. The four protons form intra- or intermolecular hydrogen bonding with adjacent carboxylate oxygen atoms. The Cd1⋯Cd2 distance is 10.242(3) Å. The two adjacent H4L2− arrange opposite as shown in Fig. 6b. From the b-axis, the 1D coordination polymers arranges up and down alternatively. Interestingly, there are non-coordinate 2,2′-bipyridines in the complex. All the 2.2′-bpy are parallel to each other as shown in Fig. S3.† The distance between adjacent 2,2′-bipyridines is ∼3.5 Å, which indicates that there are π–π interactions between adjacent bipyridine molecules.

{[Co1.5(η3-H3LI)(η1-4,4′-bipy)3(η1-H2O)3]·6H2O}1n (8)

Crystal 8 is also a 1D coordination polymer crystallizes in triclinic, P group. Asymmetric unit contains 1.5 Co(ii) ions, one H3L3−ligand, three monodentate 4,4′- bipyridine and three water molecules as shown in Fig. 7a. Co2 is located in the symmetric center. Both Co(ii) coordinate to two pyridinenitrogen, two carboxylate oxygen and two water molecules to form an octahedron coordination environment {CoN2O4}. The Co–N has the longest distances varying from Co(2)–N(5) 2.153(3) to Co(1)–N(1) 2.171(3) Å. Co–O distances vary from Co(2)–O(6) 2.070(2) to Co(1)–O(10)#1 2.103(2) Å. Co–O (water) from the shortest Co(1)–O(1W) 2.069(2) to the longest Co(1)–O(2W) 2.134(2) Å. H3L3− acts as a tridentate ligand binding to 3 Co(ii). All deprotonated carboxylates are in the e-position and coordinate to Co(ii) monodentately (Fig. 7b). The N3N4-contain bipyridine rings which are almost coplanar (dihedral 5.86°), while two other 4,4′-bipyridine, both have a dihedral of ∼24°. All the three carboxylate protons form intramolecular hydrogen bondings with the coordinating carboxylate oxygen atoms as indicated in Fig. 7c. As seen from Fig. S4a,† the 4,4′-bipyridine moieties are close to each other. However, only the coplanar N3N4-contain 4,4′-bipyridine have π–π interaction with adjacent coordination polymer as shown in Fig S4b.† The nearest N3 and N4 distance is 3.515 Å. The N4-containing pyridine is also parallel with N5-containing pyridine from other chains and has central to edge distances of 3.561 Å.

[Mn(1,10-phen)2(H2O)2]·(η-H4LI)·(H2O)5 (9)

Complex 9 is a mononuclear 1,10-phenanthroline complex crystallized in monoclinic, P21/c group. As shown in Fig. 8, Mn(ii) is coordinated to four nitrogen atoms from two 1,10-phenanthroline and two water oxygen atoms. The H4L2− acts as a counter ion to charge balance. The Mn–N distances ranges from 2.228(4) to 3.303(4) Å, which is obviously longer than Mn–O distances at 2.128(4) and 2.138(3) Å. It seems that Mn(ii) ions prefer O coordination. As we know that the deprotonated cyclohexanehexacarboxylate has a stronger coordinate ability, it can substitute chelate oxalate under hydrothermal conditions.10b However, carboxylate oxygen does not participate in the coordination in 9. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first example that deprotonated cyclohexanehexacarboxylate acts as a counter ion in the presence of a transition metal ion. The free H4L2− is in all-cis configuration (LI). From b-axis, the cation complex and anion H4L2− packs alternatively. The H4L2− links together via hydrogen bonding, while the cation moiety stacks via π–π interactions as indicated in the ESI, Fig. S5.† The centroid to edge distance between adjacent 1,10-phenanthrolines is 3.583(7) Å, which indicates a moderate π–π interaction.

Structural comparison and discussion

As shown in Scheme 1, the cyclohexane moiety in the ligand is always in a chair conformation. However, the proton and carboxylate arranges differently to form a different conformation. In all-cis conformation LI, three protons and three carboxylates arrange up and down relative to the least square plane of the cyclohexane (Scheme 1). The other three carboxylates are essentially coplanar with the least plane. While in all-trans LII, all the six carboxylates are essentially coplanar with the least square. The coplanar carboxylate is in the e-position, while the perpendicular one is in the a-position.

In the nine new complexes, the ligand deprotonate varies from H4L2− in 5, 7 and 9, to H3L3− in 6 and 8, HL5− in 3 and fully deprotonated L6− in 1, 2 and 4. As a whole, deprotonation tends to increase the ligand coordination number. The coordination numbers of H4L2− in 5, 7 and 9 synthesized at room temperature, where ligands adopt all-cis conformation LI, are 3, 4 and 0. H3L3− in 6 and 8 also adopt all-cis configuration LI, the coordination numbers are 4 and 3 respectively. HL5− in 3 also in configuration LI, its coordination number increased to 8. Fully deprotonated L6− in 1, 2 and 4 adopt conformation LI or LII according to the synthetic temperature. 1 and 2 are synthesized at room temperature, ligands adopt all-cis configuration LI, while hydrothermal synthesized 4 takes all-trans configuration LII. Interestingly, the coordination ability of the carboxylate differs according to the position of the carboxylate. In all the complexes, it is the e-position carboxylate that coordinates to the metal ion. The non-coordinated carboxylates are always in the a-position. The complex dimensional depends on the deprotonation of H6L as well as the second building block. Highly deprotonated H6L without a second building block tend to form 3D coordination polymers (1, 2, 3 and 4). Partially deprotonated H6L with a bridging 4,4′-bipyridine form 2D coordination polymers (5), while highly protonated H6L and chelating ligands can only form 1D coordination polymers (6, 7 and 8). The structural data are summarized in Table 2. Zheng et al. have summarized the coordination mode of the ligand H6L.5 Most of the reported complexes are polydentate and the coordination mode arranged from η5μ11 to η16μ11, the coordination modes of our complexes 1 or 2, 5, 6, 7 and 8 are new coordination modes (η9μ6, η3μ2, η4μ3, η4μ2, η3μ3) that are not reported in the literature.5Scheme 2 shows the new coordination modes of cyclohexanehexacarboxylate in this article.

IR spectroscopy

The IR spectra of complexes 1–9 offer some valuable information about the metal–ligand bonding and the coordination modes of the carboxylates, shown in Fig. S6 and Fig. S7.† Amongst the two strong absorptions at 1543 and 1399 cm−1 of complex 1, 1540 and 1404 cm−1 of complex 2, the bands at about 1543 cm−1 are attributed to νas(COO−) of the monodentate carboxylate, and the bands at 1404 cm−1 may correspond to the νas(COO−) of the bridging bidentate carboxylate groups. The strong bands at 1231 and 1168 cm−1 in complexes 1 and 2 are assigned to C–O stretching vibrations.20 In complex 3 the absorbance at 1732 cm−1 are attributed to the vibration of protonated carboxyl groups (COOH), indicating that the carboxyl groups are partially protonated. In complex 4 the strong absorbance at 1556 and 1407 cm−1 are also attributed to νas(COO−) and bridging bidentate carboxylate groups. In complexes 1, 2, 4 there are no bands in the region of 1690–1730 cm−1 indicating complete deprotonation of the carboxylic groups,21 being in agreement with their crystal structures. In complexes 5–8, the strong absorption around 1730 cm−1 expected for the vibration of protonated carboxyl groups (COOH), indicates that the carboxyl groups are partially protonated like in complex 3. The strong absorption at 1610, 1634 , 1597and 1594 cm−1 respectively in complexes 5, 6, 7 and 8 are assigned to the νas (CO) stretching vibration of the carboxylic group, and this significantly shifts to lower wave numbers of the bands of carboxylic groups showing the coordination of the carboxylate oxygen atoms to metal ions. The asymmetric vibration and symmetric vibration of the nitrate group are observed at 1300 cm−1 in complex 6 which is somewhat like the reported data.11i In complex 7, the peak of 764 cm−1 is the characteristic absorption of 2,2′-bipy, respectively. The peaks of 851 and 726 cm−1 in complex 6 and 851 and 727 cm−1 in complex 9 are the characteristic absorption of 1,10-phenanthroline.

Thermogravimetric analysis

The TG/DSC analyses were carried out to examine the thermal stabilities as well as the purity of the complexes 2–7 and 9. The TG/DSC curves are provided in Fig. S8 to Fig. S14.† The TG/DSC curve of complex 2 shows water loss in the temperature range 50–350 °C. The weight loss of 29.4% from room temperature to 350 °C corresponds to the release of eight coordinated water and four lattice water (calc. 31.8%). We think that one lattice water molecule has already been lost before the TGA analysis. One cocrystallized water molecule was lost below 85 °C, and another three cocrystallized water molecules and three weakly coordinated water molecules lost under 132 °C. From 132 to 172 °C, three other coordinated water molecules were lost. The remaining bidentate water molecule and another coordinated water molecule were lost from 172 to 350 °C. The framework starts to decompose on approaching 350 °C. The remaining weight of 30.6% (calc. 29.9%) corresponds to the percentage of the CoO. But CoO is not stable in air at 460 °C,22 so the mass is increased to 33.0% (calc.33.1%) and the Co is oxidized into Co2O3 at temperature 700 °C. In contrast to the complex 2, complex 3 is less stable as it has a lower decomposition temperature which persisted up to about 200 °C and then the mother frameworks began to collapse. The complex 3 lost 5.1% of its weight from room temperature to 120 °C corresponding to four cocrystallized water molecules (5.7%). From 120 to 290 °C, it further lost 23.3%, corresponding to ten coordinated water molecules and 2.5 CO2 molecules (23.1%). A residue of CuO remained (found 40.2%, calc. 39.6%) at 430 °C. The complex 4 is more stable than complexes 1, 2 and 3 which shows the weight loss of 8.07% (calc.8.26%) and 8.00% (calc.8.26%) at 280 °C and 370 °C corresponding to the six coordinated water molecules, respectively. Then lost the ligand L at 395 °C, and the remains 34.68% at last is the weight of three NiO (calc. 34.30%). For complex 5, a weight loss of 9.64% and 5.12% was observed in the temperature range of 25–200 °C, which corresponded to the loss of two coordinated and one uncoordinated water molecules (calc. 10.42% and 5.21%). The remaining weight of 10.41% (calc.11.48%) corresponds to the percentage of the ZnO. The TG/DSC curve of complex 6 shows the weight loss of 1.9% (calc. 1.64%) of one lattice water from 90–260 °C, the weight lost of 23.1% (calc. 22.08%) corresponding to the loss of one 1,10-phenanthroline and dehydrate five H2O molecules from the protonated ligand (23.0%). The remaining weight of 32.5% at 800 °C corresponds to the percentage of two ZnO (calc. 29.7%). For complex 7, water loss in the temperature range 70–209 °C, the weight loss of 6.0% (calc.6.1%) corresponds to the release of three lattice and two coordinated water molecules. The weight loss of 48.8% (calc.47.2%) between 210 °C and 335 °C corresponding to the removal of two L ligands. The remaining weight of 18.2% (calc.17.4%) corresponds to the percentage of the CdO. The TG/DSC curve of complex 9 shows the weight loss of 12.79% (calc. 12.18%) which corresponds to the loss of five coordinated water molecules and one lattice water molecule from 50–165 °C. On further heating, the material loses ligands continuously. The weight lost of 39.50% corresponds to the release of two 1,10-phenanthroline molecules (calcd 40.60%). All these TGA diagrams are in good agreement with the X-ray crystal structural data. Phase purities were also proved by a crystal density test in CH2Cl2 : BrCH2CH2CH2Br11h mixtures and elemental analysis.

Fluorescent spectra

Although many cyclohexanehexacarboxylate complexes have been reported and there are some magnetic complexes, few fluorescence data is available yet. We have examined the emission spectra of Zn(ii) complexes 1, 5, 6 and Cd(ii) complex 7 since coordination polymers containing zinc and cadmium exhibited excellent photoluminescent properties.6i,23 The emission spectra of complexes 1 and 5–7 were measured in the solid state at room temperature (Fig. S15†). Complex 1 exhibits an emission peak at 468 nm with the excitation at 432 nm. Upon the irradiation at 358 nm for complex 5, it exhibits an emission peak at 443 nm. The main emission of complexes 1 and 5 are between 450–480 nm which is the blue region, suggesting that they may potentially be useful for blue fluorescent materials.24 Upon excitation at 350 nm, complex 6 shows fluorescent emission maxima at 389 and 413 nm, respectively. Complex 7 exhibits obviously different luminescent behavior which shows a fluorescent emission maximum at 385 nm when excited at 360 nm. Owing to their d10 configurations, Zn(ii) and Cd(ii) are difficult to oxidize or reduce, the emissions here are neither metal-to-ligand charge transfer nor ligand-to-metal charge transfer . The excitation wavelength and the emission peak is very close such as excitation at 350 nm and the emission is at 389 nm for complex 6 as shown in Table 3. According to the close emission energy, we attributed it to the intraligand transition of cyclohexanehexacarboxylate which is modified by metal coordination.

Reversible de-/rehydration behaviours in complex 2

Complex 2 is a porous coordination polymer with lower crystal density and ∼9 × 12 Å void channel in the ac plane. PXRD patterns at different temperatures indicate that 2 gradually lost crystallinity from 80 °C (Fig. 9). Drying 2 at different temperatures, the water molecules could be removed in a stepwise fashion, resulting in a series of dehydrated products. The X-ray powder pattern of 2 after removal of the water molecules will gradually lose crystallinity as the temperature increases from 30 °C to 80 °C or higher. To investigate whether the drying and rehydration were reversible, the samples dried at 100 °C were immersed in an aqueous solution for 24 h. The resulting rehydrated complex showed the identical PXRD pattern with their PXRD patterns of as-synthesized 2 (Fig. 9). These data indicate that 2 is a breathable porous coordination polymer. At 100 °C or below, 2 breathes reversibly.

Conclusion

Nine new complexes assembled from flexible 1,2,3,4,5,6-cyclohexanehexacarboxylate (H6L) with Zn2+, Cu2+ , Co2+ , Ni2+ and Mn2+ ions and different alkalis in aqueous solution at different temperatures. Their dimensional varies from 0D to 3D. Coordination numbers of H6L vary from 0 to 12. Flexible H6L adopts all-cis LI conformation at room temperature and all-trans LII configuration under hydrothermal conditions. Interestingly, the e-position carboxylate (coplanar to cyclohexane least-square) tends to coordinate to the metal ion rather than the a-position (perpendicular to cyclohexane least-square) carboxylate. The coordination number of fully deprotonated and mono-protonated H6L varies from 8 to 12, and form 3D coordination polymers. Higher protonated ligand prefers to form 0–2D complexes. Complex 2 exhibits 3,5-connected with a {32;4}{3;63;86} network in the Schlafli notation. 3 is a 3,6-connected {43}{45;67;83} network. 4 is a 3,9-connected 9-noted network. Moreover, complex 2 is new porous coordination polymer which was capable of absorbing water molecules reversibly under 100 °C.
