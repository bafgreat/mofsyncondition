Introduction

Recently, a variety of metal phosphonates have been synthesized not only due to their structural diversity and intriguing molecular topologies but also for their potential applications in many fields such as catalysis, sorption, ion exchange, nonlinear optics, magnetism, and so on.1–3 However, there exist difficulties in acquiring their precise molecular structures and determining their properties due to the poor crystallinity of the metal phosphonates. So compared with the widely reported coordination compounds based on carboxylate or N-donor ligands, a relatively small number of metal phosphonates have been reported so far.4,5 To tune the crystallinity of the metal phosphonates, an effective strategy can be used: modification of phosphonic acidligands with functional groups such as carboxyl,6hydroxyl,7 sulfonic groups,8amino,9 and pyridyl10–12 that can improve the water-solubility of the final complexes, which is also a promising way for tuning the crystallinity and structural diversity of metal phosphonates. Results from documents indicate that the introduction of carboxylate groups is an effective method for the syntheses of metal phosphonates with interesting structures, since the carboxylate groups can provide varieties of coordination modes and increase the hydrophilicity of the ligands.13,14 For example, the reported compound [Zn3(O3PCH2CO2)2(O3PCH2CO2H)(NH3CH2CH2NH3)(BTC)] (BTC = 1,3,5-benzene-tricarboxylate) shows an open-framework structure with dangling carboxylate groups in 24-ring channels,15 however, the compound Zn2[(S)-O3PCH2NHC4H7CO2]2 displays a homochiral structure with helical channels.16

Taking inspiration from the aforementioned points, we introduced carboxylate groups into the backbone of the phosphonic acidligand and synthesized a new ligand2′-carboxybiphenyl-4-ylmethylphosphonic acid (H3L) (Scheme 1). In this work, seven compounds based on H3L, namely, [Cu(HL)(H2O)] (1), [Cu5(L)2(OH)4]·H2O (2), [Zn3(L)2(H2O)] (3), [Zn3(L)2(phen)2(H2O)2] (4), [Cd3(L)2(H2O)2] (5), [Cd2(L)(OH)(H2O)1.5] (6) and [Cd(HL)(phen)2]·2.5H2O (7), where phen = 1,10-phenanthroline, have been synthesized under hydrothermal conditions. All the compounds have been characterized by elemental analyses and X-ray crystallography. The structures and topological analyses of these compounds, as well as the influence of the ligands, metal ions, and pH values on the structures of the compounds have been discussed. The photoluminescent properties of compounds 3–7 have also been investigated.

Experimental

Materials and methods

H3L was prepared by the reported procedures.17 Other reagents were obtained commercially and used as received. The C, H, and N elemental analyses were conducted on a Carlo Erba 1106 elemental analyzer. The FT-IR spectra were recorded from KBr pellets in the range 4000–400 cm−1 on a Mattson Alpha-Centauri spectrometer. TGA were performed on a Perkin-Elmer TG-7 analyzer heated from room temperature to 800 °C under nitrogen gas. The solid-state emission/excitation spectra were recorded on a Perkin-Elmer FLS-920 spectrometer at room temperature. PXRD patterns of the samples were collected on a Rigaku Dmax 2000 X-ray diffractometer with graphite monochromatized Cu-Kα radiation (λ = 0.154 nm) and 2θ ranging from 5 to 50°.

X-ray crystallography

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction data for 1–7 were recorded on an Oxford Diffraction Gemini R Ultra diffractometer with graphite-monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at 293 K. Absorption corrections were carried out using a multi-scan technique. All the structures were solved by Direct Method of SHELXS-9718 and refined by full-matrix least-squares techniques using the SHELXL-9719 program. Non-hydrogen atoms of the compounds were refined with anisotropic temperature parameters. All hydrogen atoms on carbon atoms were generated geometrically and refined using a riding model with d(C–H) = 0.93 Å, Uiso = 1.2Ueq(C) for aromatic and d(C–H) = 0.97 Å, Uiso = 1.2Ueq(C) for CH2 atoms. The hydrogen atoms of phosphonate group for 7 and carboxylate group for 1 were generated geometrically and refined using a riding model with d(O–H) = 0.86 Å, Uiso = 1.5Ueq(O). The hydrogen atoms of water molecule for 5 were located from difference Fourier maps and refined as riding atoms with d(O–H) = 0.849–0.850 Å and Uiso = 1.5Ueq(O). Other hydrogen atoms of water molecules and hydroxyl groups were not included in the model. O1W for 1, and O2W and O5 for 6 were refined as disordered atoms using O atoms split over two sites with a total occupancy of 1. Results and discussion

Structure of 2′-carboxybiphenyl-4-ylmethylphosphonic acid (H3L)

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that L crystallizes in the space group P and exhibits a discrete structure (Fig. 1a). As shown in Fig. 1b, the discrete structure of H33L is extended by O–H⋯O hydrogen-bonding interactions to form a 2D supramolecular layer (Table S1b†).

Structure of [Cu(HL)(H2O)] (1)

The asymmetric unit of 1 contains one Cu(ii) ion, one HL ligand and one water molecule. As shown in Fig. 2a, Cu1 is five-coordinated by four oxygen atoms from four different HL anions, and one water oxygen atom in a trigonal bipyramid coordination geometry. The Cu–O bond lengths vary from 1.929(3) to 2.007(14) Å. The phosphonate group adopts a μ3-η1:η1:η1 coordination mode, while the carboxylate group takes a μ1-η1:η0 coordination fashion. In this way, the HL anions link the Cu(ii) ions to form a 2D layer (Fig. 2b and 2c). In addition, there are intramolecular O–H⋯O hydrogen bonds between O4 of the carboxylate group and O1 of the phosphonate groups, which further consolidate the whole structure of 1 (Table S2b and Fig. S1†).

Topologically, if the Cu(ii) ions and the HL anions are considered as 4-connected nodes, the 2D layer can be simplified as a 4-connected net with the (42·64) topology (Fig. 2d).

Structure of [Cu5(L)2(OH)4]·H2O (2)

The asymmetric unit of 2 contains three kinds of Cu(ii) ions, one L anion, two hydroxyl anions, and half a water molecule. As shown in Fig. 3a, Cu1 is six-coordinated by four oxygen atoms from four different L anions, and two hydroxyl oxygen atoms in a distorted octahedral sphere. Cu2 is five-coordinated in a trigonal bipyramid coordination environment, defined by five oxygen atoms from three distinct L anions and two hydroxyl anions. Cu3 is four-coordinated in a square-planar environment, completed by four oxygen atoms from two different L anions and two hydroxyl anions. The Cu–O bond distances are in the range of 1.882(5)–2.393(5) Å. Two μ3-O7 atoms link one Cu3, two Cu1, and two Cu2 atoms to form a pentanuclear unit [Cu5(μ3-O7)2]. The phosphonate group displays a μ5-η1:η2:η2 coordination mode, while the carboxylate group shows a μ3-η1:η2 coordination mode. In this manner, the L anions and the O6 atoms connect the pentanuclear units to yield a 2D layer (Fig. 3b and 3c).

Topologically, if the L anions are considered as 3-connected nodes, the pentanuclear units are regarded as 10-connected nodes, and the O6 atoms are considered as linkers, the 2D layer can be considered as a (3,10)-connected net with the (32·6)(38·48·512·69·74·84) topology (Fig. 3d).

Structure of [Zn3(L)2(H2O)] (3)

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that the asymmetric unit of 3 contains three Zn(ii) ions, two L anions and one water molecule. As shown in Fig. 4a, Zn1, Zn2 and Zn3 are four-coordinated in tetrahedral coordination spheres, but their coordination environments are entirely different. Zn1 and Zn3 are coordinated by four oxygen atoms from four different L anions, whereas Zn2 is coordinated by four oxygen atoms from three L anions and one water molecule. The Zn–O bond lengths range from 1.866(3) to 2.014(3) Å. The two phosphonate groups exhibit μ3-η1:η1:η1 and μ4-η1:η1:η2 coordination modes, while the two carboxylate groups show μ2-η1:η1 coordination fashions. In this way, Zn1, Zn2, and Zn3 are connected by the phosphonate and carboxylate groups to generate a trinuclear unit, which are further linked by the L anions to form a 2D layer (Fig. 4b and 4c).

Topologically, if the L anions are reduced to 3-connected nodes, and the trinuclear units are reduced to 6-connected nodes, the 2D layer can be simplified as a (3,6)-connected net with the (42·6)2(47·66·82) topology (Fig. 4d).

Structure of [Zn3(L)2(phen)2(H2O)2] (4)

The asymmetric unit of 4 consists of three Zn(ii) ions, two L anions, two phen ligands, and two water molecules. As shown in Fig. 5a, Zn1 and Zn3 are five-coordinated in trigonal bipyramid coordination geometries, and Zn2 is four-coordinated in a tetrahedral coordination environment. Zn1 is coordinated by two nitrogen atoms from two phen ligands and three oxygen atoms from two individual L anions and one water molecule. Zn2 is coordinated by four oxygen atoms from four distinct L anions. Zn3 is coordinated by two nitrogen atoms from one phen ligand and three oxygen atoms from two different L anions and one water molecule. The Zn–N bond distances are in the range of 2.119(3)–2.191(3) Å, and the Zn–O bond distances are in the range of 1.872(3)–2.231(3) Å. The two phosphonate groups exhibit μ3-η1:η1:η1 coordination modes, while the two carboxylate groups display μ1-η1:η0 coordination modes. In this way, Zn1 and Zn2 are bridged by two phosphonate groups to form a binuclear unit. The L anions connect Zn3 and the binuclear units to form a 3D framework (Fig. 5b), which is further strengthened by the phen ligands (Fig. 5c).

From a topological viewpoint, if the L anions are regarded as 3-connected nodes, the binuclear units are considered as 4-connected nodes, and Zn3 are reduced to linkers, the whole framework can be described as a (3,4)-connected net with the (123)(42·6)(42·6·123) topology (Fig. 5d).

Structure of [Cd3(L)2(H2O)2] (5)

Compound 5 crystallizes in the space groupPbca and exhibits a 2D layer structure. The asymmetric unit of 5 contains two kinds of Cd(ii) ions, one kind of L anion, and one water molecule. As shown in Fig. 6a, both Cd1 and Cd2 are six-coordinated in distorted octahedral spheres, but their coordination environments are entirely different. Cd1 is coordinated by six oxygen atoms from four individual L anions and two water molecules. Cd2 is coordinated by six oxygen atoms from four different L anions. The Cd–O bond lengths range from 2.205(2) to 2.473(3) Å. The phosphonate group exhibits a μ5-η1:η2:η2 coordination mode, while the carboxylate group shows a μ1-η1:η1 coordination mode. In this fashion, the L anions link the Cd(ii) ions to generate a 2D layer (Fig. 6b and 6c). The 2D layer is further stabilized by the intramolecular O–H⋯O hydrogen bonds between the carboxylate oxygen atoms and the water molecules (Table S6b† and Fig. 6d).

Topologically, if Cd1 and Cd2 are considered as 4-connected nodes, and the L anions are viewed as 6-connected nodes, the whole structure can be simplified as a (4,6)-connected network with the (44·62)(46)(410·65) topology (Fig. 6e).

Structure of [Cd2(L)(OH)(H2O)1.5] (6)

The asymmetric unit of 6 consists of two Cd(ii) ions, one L anion, one and a half water molecules, and one hydroxyl anion. As shown in Fig. 7a, Cd1 and Cd2 show similar octahedral coordination geometries, but their coordination environments are entirely different. Cd1 is six-coordinated by six oxygen atoms from four distinct L anions and one hydroxyl anion, while Cd2 is six-coordinated by six oxygen atoms from two individual L anions, two hydroxyl anions and two water molecules. The Cd–O bond distances vary from 2.088(10) to 2.539(12) Å. A pair of hydroxyl anions link two Cd1 and two Cd2 to form a tetranuclear unit [Cd4(OH)2]2+. The phosphonate group exhibits a μ5-η1:η2:η2 coordination mode, while the carboxylate group shows a μ1-η1:η1 coordination mode. In this way, the L anions connect the tetranuclear units to generate a 2D layer (Fig. 7b and 7c).

From the topological view, if the L anions can be regarded as 3-connected nodes, and the tetranuclear units can be reduced to 6-connected nodes, the 2D layer can be simplified as a (3,6)-connected network with the (42·6)(46·66·83) topology (Fig. 7d).

Structure of [Cd(HL)(phen)2]·3H2O (7)

Compound 7 crystallizes in the P space group and displays a discrete structure. The asymmetric unit of 7 contains one Cd(ii) ion, one HL anion, two phen ligands, and three water molecules. As shown in Fig. 8a, Cd1 is six-coordinated by two oxygen atoms from two distinct L anions, and four nitrogen atoms from two phen ligands in a distorted octahedral coordination environment. The Cd–N bond distances range from 2.342(4) to 2.455(4) Å and the Cd–O bond distances range from 2.173(3) to 2.324(3) Å. The phosphonate group exhibits a μ1-η0:η0:η1 coordination mode, while the carboxylate group shows a μ1-η0:η1 coordination mode. In this way, the L anion link the Cd(ii) ions to form a closed loop, which is further consolidated by the intramolecular hydrogen bonds between O3 and O5 of the HL anions (Fig. 8b).

Coordination modes of the L anions in compounds 1–7

From the structure descriptions, we found that the phosphonate and carboxylate groups of the L anions exhibit a variety of coordination modes. There are five kinds of coordination modes of the phosphonate groups and four types of coordination modes of the carboxylate groups (Scheme 2). The phosphonate groups show μ3-η1:η1:η1 coordination modes in 1 and 4, μ5-η1:η2:η2 modes in 2 and 6, μ5-η2:η2:η2 mode in 5, and μ1-η0:η0:η1 mode in 7. Whereas, in 3, one of the phosphonate groups adopts a μ3-η1:η1:η1 mode and the other one displays a μ4-η1:η1:η2 mode. The carboxylate groups exhibit μ1-η0:η1 coordination modes in 1, 4 and 7, μ1-η1:η1 modes in 5 and 6, μ2-η1:η1 mode in 3, and μ3-η1:η2 mode in 2. Clearly, the diverse coordination modes of the phosphonate and carboxylate groups resulted in the structural differences of compounds 1–7.

Effects of the central metals on the structures

The metal centers play important roles in the formation of 1–7. The varieties in coordination numbers of the central metals are the main reason for the complex structure discrepancies. Because the radius of the Cd(ii) ions are larger than those of the Zn(ii) and Cu(ii) ions, the coordination numbers (n) of the Cd(ii) ions in 5, 6 and 7 (n = 6) are larger than those of the Zn(ii) ions in 3 and 4 (n = 4 and 5) and the Cu(ii) ions in 1 and 2 (n = 4, 5 and 6). As a result, the structural differences between these compounds may be attributed to the differences in coordination numbers of the metal ions.

Effects of the phen ligands on the structures

The differences in the structures of compounds 3 and 4, as well as 5 and 7 indicate that the phen ligands have remarkable effects on the formation of the final structures. Compound [Zn3(L)2(H2O)] (3) exhibits a 2D layer structure. However, when the phen ligand was introduced into the reaction system of 3, a 3D framework of [Zn3(L)2(phen)2(H2O)2] (4) was obtained. Similarly, compound [Cd3(L)2(H2O)2] (5) has a 2D layer structure, when the phen ligand was introduced into the reaction system of 5, a 0D loop of Cd(HL)(phen)2]·3H2O (7) was isolated.

PXRD patterns and thermal analyses

In order to confirm whether the crystal structures are truly representative of the bulk materials, the PXRD patterns were recorded for compounds 1–7. The experimental PXRD patterns were comparable to the corresponding simulated ones, indicating that the synthesized bulk materials and the measured single crystals are the same (Fig. S2†).

To estimate the stability of the coordination architectures of compounds 1–7, thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were carried out. The experiments were carried out under N2 atmosphere with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1, as shown in Fig. S3.† The weight losses corresponding to the release of water molecules are observed in the range of 30–227 °C for compound 1 (obsd 4.3%, calcd 4.8%), 30–261 °C for compound 2 (obsd 2.9%, calcd 1.8%), 345–471 °C for compound 3 (obsd 2.8%, calcd 2.3%), 30–236 °C for compound 4 (obsd 5.0%, calcd 3.1%), 184–246 °C for compound 5 (obsd 4.2%, calcd 3.8%), 30–232 °C for compound 6 (obsd 4.8%, calcd 4.8%), and 30–127 °C for compound 7 (obsd 6.4%, calcd 5.6%). The departure of the organic components started at 254 °C for compound 1, 261 °C for compound 2, 471 °C for compound 3, 330 °C for compound 4, 397 °C for compound 5, 232 °C for compound 6, and 283 °C for compound 7, respectively.

Luminescent properties

Luminescent properties of compounds with d10 metal centers have attracted much attention due to their potential applications in many fields, such as photochemistry, chemical sensors, and electroluminescent display.20 Therefore, in this work, the photoluminescent properties of H3L and compounds 3–7 have been investigated in the solid state at room temperature. The emission and excitation peaks are shown in Fig. 9.

The emission peaks of the free H3L and the phen ligand are at 428 and 409 nm,21 respectively, which are probably caused by π*–n or π*–π transition.22 Compounds 3–7 show emission peaks at 422, 485, 424, 409 and 453 nm, respectively. The emission peaks of compounds 3, 5 and 6 are similar to those of the free H3L and the phen ligands. Thus, the emission bands of these compounds can probably be assigned to the intraligand fluorescent emissions.23 For compounds 4 and 7, the emission peaks are highly red-shifted with respect to the free H3L and phen ligands. Because the Zn(ii) ions and Cd(ii) ions are difficult to oxidize or to reduce, the emissions of these compounds are neither MLCT nor LMCT.24 The emissions of 4 and 7 can probably be attributed to a mixture of characteristics of intraligand and ligand-to-ligand charge transition (LLCT), as reported for other d10 metal complexes with N-donor ligands.25

Conclusion

In conclusion, seven new metal phosphonates based on 2′-carboxybiphenyl-4-ylmethylphosphonic acid (H3L) have been prepared under hydrothermal conditions. These compounds display interesting 0D, 2D and 3D structures. The structural diversities of the final products imply that the coordination modes of the L anions, the coordination geometries of the central metals, and the N-donor ligands have significant effects on the resultant structures of the compounds. The photoluminescent behaviors of compounds 3–7 indicate that they may be good candidates for potential luminescent materials.
