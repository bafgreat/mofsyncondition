Introduction

Hybrid organic–inorganic compounds are of considerable interest due to the broad fundamental and functional properties of these materials.1–8 The driving force of this intense research activity is mainly originating from the very large spectrum of organic molecules or ions and of inorganic entities that are prone to build stable crystal structures. Carboxylic or phosphonic acids and amines, together with divalent, trivalent or tetravalent transition metal cations are mainly investigated.9–11 If amine and fluoride systems are considered, the architectures depend on the reaction conditions: nature of the solvent, concentrations of the reactants, time and temperature;12–14 moreover, the state of the organic molecule, protonated, neutral or deprotonated, is a function of the pH of the starting medium.15 The ammonium cations lie in the cavities of the inorganic networks16–19 while nitrogen atoms of neutral or deprotonated amines belong to the metal coordination spheres. Generally, the global ratio F/X (X = ligand atom) is small; as found in numerous compounds of the literature.20–22 The aim of this work was to find fluoride rich phases with dense subnetworks of metal cations and to improve the thermal stability. Previous experiments with 1,2,4-triazole amine (C2H2N3H, Htaz) and several M2+–M3+ couples had shown that dense cationic subnetworks could be obtained with this amine.7,23 With the Zn2+–Al3+ couple, it was found that ZnAlF5(Htaz) adopts the MIL(53) “breathing” topology but is not porous.23,24 With M2+–Fe3+ couples (paramagnetic M2+ cations), the resulting weberite type (M1−x2+Mx3+)M3+F5(Htaz)1−x(taz)x phases had interesting magnetic properties above liquid nitrogen temperature25 and a thermal stability up to 300 °C under humid air atmosphere.7 The coordination versatility and the ability of divalent cations to nitrogen bonding are demonstrated.26,27 It was previously shown that tetravalent metals exhibit a tendency to adopt the octahedral coordination,28 together with extended connectivity.17 The Zn2+–Ti4+ couple, for which the prerequisites for the appearance of ionic exchange, optical or catalytic properties are satisfied, was then chosen. Therefore, the ZnF2–TiF4–triazole system was investigated in hydro(solvo)thermal conditions.

In this paper, two new fluoride rich phases, structurally related, are described: trans-Zn3TiF7(H2O)2(taz)3·3H2O and cis-Zn3TiF7(H2O)2(taz)3·C2H5OH. Their architectures are built up from identical inorganic moieties and only differ from the connection mode, cis or trans, of the TiF6 octahedra in isoformular ∞[Zn3TiN9(H2O)2F7] chains; cis connection involves two neighboring fluoride anions of the TiF6 octahedron while these fluoride anions are opposite for a trans connection. Both phases undergo very similar thermal evolutions on heating, leading to anhydrous phases after desolvation and/or dehydration; however, rehydration occurs for the anhydrous cis-phase only. All transformations were investigated by X-ray powder thermodiffraction, thermogravimetric analyses and infrared spectroscopy. The structures and the relative proportions of all the starting, intermediate and anhydrous phases were determined from Rietveld refinements of the diffraction patterns. 1H and 19F MAS NMR experiments were carried on the trans and cis hydrated and anhydrous phases, and NMR parameters were calculated. All along the thermal paths, it is remarkable that the symmetry of the structures is left unchanged, even during the last condensation step.

Results and discussion

The synthesis of trans-Zn3TiF7(H2O)2(taz)3·3H2O (1) and cis-Zn3TiF7(H2O)2(taz)3·C2H5OH (4) by solvothermal method is found to be mainly dependent on the ethanol/water ratio. The samples are obtained in 100 mL autoclaves with the following weights (g) of ZnF2: 0.517 and 0.206, TiF4: 0.619 and 0.248, and Htaz: 0.349 and 0.276, for 1 and 4, respectively and 10 mL of water for 1 and 3.74 mL of 15% aqueous HF and 20 mL of ethanol for 4.

The structures of 1 and 4 were determined by single crystal X-ray diffraction in the Cm and Pnma space groups, respectively (Fig. 1a, b, Fig. S1 and S2†).

On heating 1 and 4, two intermediate desolvated phases Zn3TiF7(H2O)2(taz)3 (2 and 6) and two anhydrous Zn3TiF7(taz)3 (3 and 7) phases are evidenced. The anhydrous cis form rehydrates at room temperature in air to give cis-Zn3TiF7(H2O)2(taz)3·nH2O (5) (n ≈ 0.5) that further leads on heating to 6′ and 7′. All powders are white. The cell parameters of 6′ and 7′ are close but significantly different from that of 6 and 7. The anhydrous trans form 3 is stable in air at room temperature. The structures of 2, 3, 6, 7 were ab initio determined from X-ray powder diffraction data and Rietveld refinements in the Cm and Pnma space groups (Fig. 1c and d). Crystal data and the results of the refinements are reported in Table 1. The atomic positions are collected in Tables S1–S5, S21 and S22.† The relations between the monoclinic cell parameters of 1 and the orthorhombic cell parameters of 4 are: c1 ≈ ½a4, b1 ≈ b4, a1 ≈ c4. The transformation of the monoclinic cells of 1 to 3 is: a3 = a1, b3 = −b1, c3 = −½a1 − c1. The volumes associated to one formulae unit are very similar for 1, 2, 4, 5 and 6, 484.9(1) Å3, 482.4(1) Å3, 491.0(1) Å3, 487.9(1) Å3 and 485.6(1) Å3 respectively, and, as expected, smaller than the parent phases for anhydrous 3 (428.6(1) Å3) and 7 (441.9(1) Å3). A detailed comparison of these variations is discussed later.

Single crystal X-ray structures of Zn3TiF7(H2O)2(taz)3·S (S = 3H2O (1) or C2H5OH (4))

A structure solution for 1 was only found in noncentrosymmetric Cm space group excluding C2 and centrosymmetric C2/m, while the structure of 4 was determined in centrosymmetric Pnma. The noncentrosymmetric Pn21a space group was tested for 4 but the results were not improved, even with a racemic twinning (R = 0.0394 for 167 refined parameters and 2560 (hkl) in Pnma; R = 0.0422 for 298 refined parameters and 4851 (hkl) in Pn21a). Both structures 1 and 4 contain two crystallographically inequivalent Zn2+ sites, denoted Zn(1) and Zn(2), and one Ti4+ site; two organic linkers are not symmetrically equivalent (Table S1†). A partial site occupancy (0.5) is attributed to terminal O atom of the ethanol molecule in 4; it is indicative of a disorder of ethanol molecules over two mirror symmetry related positions. In 1 and 4, very similar trimers exist: they consist of one ZnN3(H2O)2F octahedron and two ZnN3F2 trigonal bipyramids sharing a μ3-F central atom (Fig. 2a and b). The resulting Zn3N9(H2O)2F3 trimers are then linked along b by TiF6 octahedra. In the resulting chains, the connection mode of the TiF6 octahedra is trans in 1 and cis in 4. The trimers are also linked by taz− anions (deprotonated amines) in order to build the 3D network (Table S1†). At the opposite from 4, all the orientations of Zn3N9(H2O)2F3 trimers are strictly identical in 1.

In the ZnN3(H2O)2F octahedra, Zn–N distances are in the vicinity of 2.1 Å for both structures and Zn(1)–OH2 distances, 2.131(2) Å for 1 and 2.121(7) Å for 4 are shorter than the Zn(1)–F(1) distances, 2.150(3) Å for 1 and 2.187(4) Å for 4 (Table S2†). Such distances are similar to that found in the ZnN2(H2O)2F(OH) octahedra of Zn(tpt)2/3(SiF6)(H2O)2·MeOH (Zn–N, 2.074(1) Å; Zn–OH2, 2.143(1) Å and Zn–F, 2.215(1) Å).29 In the ZnN3F2 trigonal bipyramids of 1 and 4, the equatorial positions are occupied by three nitrogen atoms from three taz− linkers while two fluorine atoms lie in the axial positions. The Zn(2)–N distances, [1.976(3)–2.006(2)] Å for 1 and [1.988(4)–2.006(3)] Å for 4 are close to that observed in Zn(bpz)2(H2O)3·3Hpta.30 The axial Zn(2)–F(1) distances with the μ3-F central atom, 2.059(1) Å for 1 and 2.083(2) Å for 4, are comparable with the distances within Zn(ii) trigonal bipyramids.31–33 The longest axial Zn(2)–F(2) distances, 2.287(2) Å for 1 and 2.384(2) Å for 4, are found for fluorine atoms that are connected to TiF6 octahedra; they are consistent with the literature values.

Three water molecules and one ethanol molecule are located in the cavities of the structures 1 and 4, respectively, while two water molecules are linked to Zn(1) cations. All water molecules are hydrogen bonded to the nearest fluorine atoms of TiF6 octahedra (Fig. 3a and b). The O⋯F and O⋯O distances lie in the range [2.72–2.92] Å and [2.68–2.85] Å in 1, [2.55–2.80] Å and [2.52–2.79] Å in 2, respectively. In 4, the hydrogen atom of ethanol hydroxyl group exchanges one strong hydrogen bond with one fluorine atom of TiF6 octahedra (dO⋯F = 2.55 Å). Both water molecules of Zn(1) polyhedra are also hydrogen bonded to one fluorine atom of the TiF6 octahedra and to the oxygen atom of ethanol; two of these hydrogen bonds are strong (dO⋯F = 2.55 Å and dO⋯O = 2.52 Å).

Thermal analyses of Zn3TiF7(H2O)2(taz)3·S with S = 3H2O (1) C2H5OH (4), nH2O (5)

X-ray powder thermodiffraction, thermogravimetric analyses and infrared spectroscopy were used to detect the evolutions of 1 (Fig. 4) and 4 (Fig. 5) between room temperature and 600–800 °C. A phase transformation scheme is proposed in Fig. 6. The framework of trans-Zn3TiF7(H2O)2(taz)3·3H2O (1) remains stable until ≈80 °C but a continuous loss of water molecules is noted. It is shown that these water molecules originate from the cavities and that the intermediate compound trans-Zn3TiF7(H2O)2(taz)3 (2) exists. The further loss of two zinc bonded water molecules implies the transformation of the framework to trans-Zn3TiF7(taz)3 (3).

The corresponding observed and calculated total weight loss values for five water molecules (13.3% and 13.4%) are very close (Fig. 4a). The dehydrated phase 3 is stable from 130 °C to 280 °C (Fig. 4c). Further endothermic events take place between 320 °C and 600 °C with a weight loss of 38.2%. This value is fairly well explained by the formation of ZnO and Zn2TiO4 due to hydrolysis by water impurities in the flowing gas at high temperature (theoretical, 38.3%).

The evolution of the cell parameters of 1, 2 and 3 with the temperature is given in Fig. 7. A small increase and a further decrease of the a parameter occurs below 120 °C; the b parameter undergoes a continuous decrease until 80 °C, related to the loss of water molecules in the cavities, while the c parameter is almost constant. Above 90 °C, the anhydrous trans-Zn3TiF7(taz)3 (3) appears and exhibits a smaller c parameter but the a and b parameters are left almost unchanged. The temperature evolution of the relative proportions of 1 (or 2) and 3 is indicated in Fig. 8.

The thermogravimetric curve of cis-Zn3TiF7(H2O)2(taz)3·C2H5OH (4) (Fig. 5a) is very similar to that of 1 (Fig. 4a). It is explained by the loss of ethanol and water to give cis-Zn3TiF7(taz)3 (7). However, the weight loss observed below 240 °C, 10.9%, is significantly smaller that the calculated weight loss, 12.4% (Fig. 5a). This discrepancy is explained by the presence of two impurities: ZnF2 and one unknown phase. The quantities of these phases are small, as shown by the 340 °C spectrum of the thermodiffraction experiment. At 340 °C, all the anhydrous cis-Zn3TiF7(taz)3 (7) is decomposed and the resulting solid is amorphous; then, the intensity of the impurity lines can be modelized. The amorphous solid is further hydrolysed and recrystallises to give ZnO and Zn2TiO4 (Fig. 5c).

In the experimental conditions of the thermodiffraction chamber, it is shown that the loss of ethanol to give cis-Zn3TiF7(H2O)2(taz)3 (6) concerns only a part of the crystallites, 35 wt% at the maximum. This phase 6 is stable over a narrow temperature range, from 70 °C to 120 °C, while the starting phase 4 disappears only at 200 °C. The relative proportions of 4, 6, 7 are given in Fig. 8. It can be assumed that the loss of ethanol is restricted to the external parts of the crystallites and that the resulting layer creates a barrier for the further diffusion of ethanol molecules located in the crystallite core.

The loss of ethanol of 4 to give 6 implies a large increase of the a parameter and a large decrease of b, while c is almost constant (Fig. 5c). It can be noted that the volume of the isoformular cis and trans dihydrates 6 and 2, respectively, are very similar. The loss of cation bonded water molecules of 6 to give the anhydrous phase cis-Zn3TiF7(taz)3 (7) ends at 130 °C. 4, 6, and 7 coexist at 110 °C and 120 °C while 4 and 7 coexist between 110 °C and 200 °C.

Rehydration of the anhydrous cis-phase 7 occurs in ambient conditions and gives cis-Zn3TiF7(H2O)2(taz)3·nH2O (5) (n ≈ 0.5, thermodiffraction experiment, n ≈ 1.0 thermogravimetry experiment) (Fig. S3†). On heating, the loss of water molecules in the cavities of 5 is continuous and ends at 90 °C to give cis-Zn3TiF7(H2O)2(taz)3 (6′), stable up to 110 °C. A small and continuous decrease of the b parameter and of the volume (V/Z) can be noted; it is reminiscent of the b and (V/Z) variations of 1. The dehydration of 6′, leading to 7′, is fast, between 110 °C and 120 °C. The cell volumes of 7′ and 7 become similar above 140 °C while their a and b cell parameters differ slightly up to 270 °C. These subtle differences cannot be explained by the structural features that are determined from the high temperature X-ray patterns.

Above 300 °C, the anhydrous phases 7 or 7′ are further hydrolysed to give ZnO and Zn2TiO4; the corresponding experimental and theoretical weight losses are in good agreement (exp. 38.0%, th. 38.8% for 4, exp. 39.0%, th. 40.5% for 5).

In summary, the evolutions of the cell parameters for the transformations of 1 to 2, of 4 to 6 or 5 to 6′ are small; the volumes of one formulae unit vary slightly: −2.5 Å3 (−0.53%) for 1 to 2, −5.4 Å3 (−1.1%) for 4 to 6, −2.3 Å3 (−0.56%) for 5 to 6′. At the opposite, the variations of the cell volumes V/Z for the transformations to the anhydrous phases are large: −53.8 Å3 (−11.2%) for 2 to 3, −43.7 Å3 (−9.0%) for 6 to 7 and −46 Å3 (−9.5%) for 6′ to 7′. Logically, the volumes of the cis- or trans-Zn3TiF7(H2O)2(taz)3 phases are intermediate between the volumes of the solvated and anhydrous compounds; moreover, the values of the b parameters of trans-phases are always larger than that of the corresponding cis-phases (Fig. 7) at the odd from the values of a and c parameters.

On heating below 200 °C, the infrared spectra of 1 (Fig. 4b) and 4 (Fig. 5b) indicate the disappearance of υO–H vibration lines correlated to the loss of water and ethanol molecules. During the transformation of 1 to 3, the intensity of υC–H lines remains constant: all CH groups of triazole ligands are left unchanged. On heating cis-Zn3TiF7(H2O)2(taz)3·C2H5OH (4), υC–H line intensity decreases and reaches a plateau; this evolution is consistent with the loss of ethanol molecules that lie in the cavities of 4.

The thermal evolution of the infrared spectra of 5 is not very conclusive (Fig. S3†). The variations of the υO–H or βO–H lines intensity are not well correlated with the expected evolution of the formulations: it can be assumed that rehydration of 6′ and of the anhydrous phase 7′ is fast and variably occurs during the transfer of the samples to the spectrometer.

X-ray powder structures of trans-Zn3TiF7(H2O)2(taz)3 (2) and trans-Zn3TiF7(taz)3 (3) derived from 1, of cis-Zn3TiF7(H2O)2(taz)3·0.5H2O (5), cis-Zn3TiF7(H2O)2(taz)3 (6) and (6′), and cis-Zn3TiF7(taz)3 (7) and (7′) derived from 4

All transformations to intermediate and anhydrous phases maintain the Cm and Pnma space groups of the parent phases 1 (for 2 and 3) and 4 (for 5, 6, 6′, 7, 7′) (Fig. 9a–e).

The structure determinations show that the Zn3N9(H2O)2F3 trimers, previously found in 1 and in 4, are left unchanged in 2, 5, and 6 (or 6′): free water or C2H5OH solvent molecules in the channels are only removed at ≈100 °C. In 5, the content of water molecules in the cavities, inaccurately determined by X-ray diffraction, should preferably take into account the results of thermogravimetric experiments (Fig. S3a†). The structures determinations of anhydrous 3 and 7 (or 7′) show that all zinc cations are five-fold coordinated and that three ZnN3F2 trigonal bipyramids share a common fluorine atom to give Zn3N9F4 trimers (Fig. 10a and b). The trimers are further connected along b to TiF6 octahedra that adopt the trans or cis connection modes already found in 1 and 4, respectively, and along [102] in 3 or along [001] in 7. The 1D dimensionality of the inorganic part of 1 and 4 transforms to a 2D dimensionality in 3 and 7. As already mentioned, the subtle differences between 6 and 6′ or 7 and 7′ cannot be explained.

Discussion about the structural transformations

All phases can be also considered as [010] ∞[TiF6Zn2N6F] inorganic chains that are grafted by ZnN3(H2O)2F octahedra in 1, 2, 4, 5 and 6 (or 6′) or by ZnN3F2 polyhedra in anhydrous 3 and 7 (or 7′). These [010] chains are further connected by the organic ligands along [100] and [001] in 1, 2, 4, 5, 6 and 7 and along [100] and [−102] in 3. The mean planes of the trimers are parallel to (100) for 1 and 2 (Fig. 2a), (−201) for 3 (Fig. 1c) and (001) for 4 and all phases derived from 4 (Fig. 1b, d and 2b). In anhydrous 3 and 7, one fluorine atom of one adjacent TiF6 octahedron enters the coordination sphere of grafted Zn2+ polyhedron. Inorganic ∞[Zn3TiN9F7] layers are formed in the (−201) planes for 3 (Fig. 1c and 10a) and (100) planes for 7 (Fig. 1d and 10b). Then, the mean plane of the trimer lies in the (−201) layer plane in trans-Zn3TiF7(taz)3 (3) (Fig. 10a), while it is approximately perpendicular to the (100) layer plane in cis-Zn3TiF7(taz)3 (7) (Fig. 10b). The condensation reaction leading to 7 is permitted by a small displacement of fluoride anions, correlated to the removal of coordinated water molecules in 4 after the loss of ethanol molecules. This process is illustrated as an arrow in the lower part of Fig. 1b. A relative shift of successive (001) layers explains the transformation of 1 to trans-Zn3TiF7(taz)3 (3) if it is assumed that Fe–N bonds are not broken. Larger atomic displacements are required during this condensation reaction (arrow in Fig. 1a). This feature probably explains that anhydrous trans-Zn3TiF7(taz)3 (3) is stable and does not rehydrate easily in ambient conditions.

1H and 19F NMR spectroscopy and DFT calculations

Partial atomic occupancies of O atom of ethanol and of water molecules in the structures of phases 4 and 5 respectively prevent DFT calculations. So, they were performed for phase 4, on an ordered structural model (4o) determined in the non-centrosymmetric Pn21a space group and, for phase 5 on a structure (5p) obtained by filling one of the two water sites.

Optimization effects on structural models are presented and discussed in ESI (Tables S1–S5†). Despite several high atomic displacement values, atomic environments remain alike. For fluorine atoms, interatomic distances in ES and APO structures are close, except some F–H ones (Tables S6–S10†).

1H and 19F solid-state MAS NMR spectra were recorded for phases 1, 3, 4 and 7 (Fig. 11 and 12). Calculated 1H σiso and 19F σiso parameters are gathered in Tables S11 and S12† respectively. Individual spectrum reconstructions and resulting NMR data are given in ESI (Fig. S4–S15 and Tables S13–S20†). NMR parameters relevant for the following discussions are gathered in Tables 2 and 3.

All studied structures present protons from taz and, as expected, all 1H NMR spectra show a contribution towards 8–9 ppm (Fig. 11), typical of HC δiso values in taz amine (9.4 ppm18 and 7.7 ppm34). In the hydrated phases 1 and 4, protons from water molecules are encountered and assigned to the broad 1H NMR lines around 4 ppm. This NMR line being also present on the 1H NMR spectrum of phase 7, we assume that a rehydration of this phase is already in progress (Fig. 11). As the delay from synthesis to NMR experiment was short (less than 5 min), the compound actually studied is either phase 5 or more likely an intermediate between phases 7 and 5. In contrast, the comparison between the NMR spectra of phases 1 and 3 shows that phase 3 is dehydrated. Phase 4 is the only one that contains ethanol molecules. The additional NMR line around 1 ppm shown by the 1H NMR spectrum of 4 is then attributed to the protons of CH3 of ethanol molecules (Fig. 11).

A significant decrease in the width of 1H NMR lines is observed when the spinning frequency increases from 44 kHz to 64 kHz, except for phase 3 (Fig. S4†). This indicates strong 1H–1H and 19F–1H dipolar couplings in the (re)hydrated phases 1, 4 and 7, and lower ones in the dehydrated phase 3. Due to very short relaxation times, relative intensities for water protons are lower than expected (Table 2).

The structure of phase 3 has three H sites of equal multiplicity, and the main signal of the corresponding NMR spectrum is reconstructed with three NMR lines (Fig. S6†), but their relative intensities differ from the expected 1 : 1 : 1 ratio (Table S14†). This indicates some variations of the local environments around protons, certainly due to slight positional disorders most likely in the organic part.

Calculated 1H σiso values range from 22.0 to 26.3 ppm for protons in Zn bonded water molecules and from 22.2 to 29.8 ppm for protons of water molecules in cavities (Table 2 and Table S11†). Distinct 1H σiso,cal ranges (and consequently 1H δiso ranges) for these two environments are therefore excluded. For the protons in taz amine, the 1H σiso,cal values are slightly higher for the trans phases 1 and 3 (21.4 ppm) than for the cis phases 4o, 5p and 7 (20.6 to 20.9 ppm). Consistently, 1H δiso,exp values (8.7 and 8.9 ppm) for trans phases are slightly lower than for cis phases (9.1 and 9.4 ppm).

In order to compare 1H δiso,exp values with 1H σiso,cal values, weighted averages were calculated, based on proton environments (amine, water or ethanol) for σiso,cal values and on the shape of the contributions for δiso,exp values (Table 2, Tables S11 and S13–S16†). For phase 7, the σiso,cal values obtained with the structural model 5p are used, as the calculated values for protons in amine are very close (20.7 and 20.9 ppm) in both phases, and only phase 5p presents protons in water molecules. Satisfying correlations between 1H δiso,exp and 1H σiso,cal values are obtained (Fig. S9†).

In the structures of the four phases studied by NMR, three fluorine environments are encountered: F bridging three Zn atoms (FZn3) or one Ti and one Zn atoms (FTiZn), and terminal F atoms from TiF6 octahedra (FTi). The 19F NMR spectra of trans phases 1 and 3 have roughly the same pattern, with one contribution below −150 ppm and one other contribution above −50 ppm (Fig. 12). The latter is much wider than the former and is also very sensitive to spinning speed. The 19F NMR spectra of cis phases 4 and 7 also show these two previous contributions, but the ones at 79–80 ppm are thinner. For phase 7, an additional 19F NMR line is observed at −18.2 ppm. Both 19F NMR spectra of the cis phases 4 and 7, also present a small NMR line at −204.4 ppm, indicating the presence of ZnF2 as an impurity.35 The 19F NMR lines between −170 and −190 ppm, close to that of ZnF2 (−204.7 ppm), are attributed to the F atoms bridging three Zn atoms (Table 3). In all structures, only one crystallographic site is attributed to these F atoms. However, for phases 1, 3 and 7, at least two 19F NMR lines are needed to correctly account for the shape of this contribution (Tables S17–S20†), indicating slight variations of the local environments around these F atoms. This is certainly due to slight positional disorders most probably in the organic parts.

The remaining 19F NMR lines are assigned to the remaining (four to six) F sites, corresponding to FTiZn and FTi environments. The 19F experimental chemical shift ranges are consistent with the 19F δiso values for terminal F atoms in TiF6 octahedra (from 71 to 102 ppm).18,36,37 DFT calculations on APO structures show that, for these F sites, the 19F σiso,cal range is very wide, from 110 to 330 ppm depending on whether the phase is hydrated or not (Table S12†). However, the 19F experimental δiso ranges are significantly lower, from 5 to 50 ppm for each phase (Tables S17–S20†). This could indicate an averaging due to the movement of fluorine atoms jumping from one site to another within the same TiF6 octahedron. This is also consistent with the high sensitivity to spinning speed (and the related temperature variation generated by frictions) shown by these 19F NMR lines (Fig. S10†). This assumption of motions is strengthened by the experimental δcsa values for these F atoms which are significantly lower (Tables S17–S20†) than the calculated ones (100 to 400 ppm, Table S12†). Similar phenomenon was previously observed for the terminal F atoms in K16[Mo3O4F9]2[TiF6]3·2H2O,36 and [Cu(bpy)2(H2O)2]2[TiF6]2·3H2O.37

As already done for 1H, weighted averages were calculated based on fluorine environments (bridging three Zn atoms or not) for σiso,cal and on the position of the 19F NMR line (below −150 ppm or above −50 ppm) for δiso,exp values (Table 3, Tables S12 and S17–S20†). For phase 7, the 19F σiso values calculated with structures 5p and 7 show differences of more than 20 ppm, so these values were excluded from the linear regression. A good correlation between 19F δiso,exp and 19F σiso,cal values is obtained (Fig. S15†). For phase 7, 19F σiso,cal values are better calculated from structure 5p for F atoms bridging three Zn atoms but from structure 7 for others F sites. This reinforces the assumption that the compound studied by NMR is a rehydrated phase, intermediate between phases 7 and 5.

Overall, this NMR study supports the crystalline structures but the lack of resolution on the NMR spectra, the motions and the positional disorders impact the accuracy of measurements and hinder the comparison with calculated parameters.

Experimental

The starting materials were ZnF2 (99%, Alpha Aesar), TiF4 (98%, Alpha Aesar), 1,2,4-triazole (Htaz) (99.9%, Alpha Aesar), 15% hydrofluoric acid solution (prepared from 40% HF, Riedel De Haen). The ZnF2/TiF4-Htaz-HFaq.-EtOH system was investigated for a constant ratio [Zn2+]/[Ti4+] = 1 in solvothermal conditions at 160 °C under microwave heating for 1 h and natural cooling. The solid products were washed with ethanol and dried at room temperature and ambient air.

Thermal analysis

Thermogravimetric experiments were performed with a SETARAM TGA 92 thermoanalyzer under a humid air flow and a heating rate of 5 °C min−1 from room temperature to 800 °C.

Thermodiffraction patterns were collected with a PANalytical X'Pert Pro diffractometer (CuKα radiation) equipped with an Anton Paar XFK 900 high temperature furnace. The powder was heated in air atmosphere from room temperature to 180 °C at a heating rate of 3 °C min−1 separated by 10 min intervals. XRD data were collected in the θ range 2.5–50° or 2.5–30°.

Infrared spectroscopy

Infrared spectra were collected on a FT-IR ALPHA Bruker Optik spectrometer (single reflection diamond) over the range 400–4000 cm−1.

X-ray single crystal and powder diffraction

Single crystals were selected under a polarizing optical microscope and mounted on a MicroMount needle (MiTeGen). X-ray intensity data were collected on a Bruker APEX II Quazar diffractometer (4-circle Kappa goniometer, IμS microfocus source (Mo Kα), CCD detector) at 296 K for 1 and 4. The structures were determined by direct methods with SHELXS-86 and SHELXL-2013 programs included in the WINGX package.38–40 The final refinements, performed on the basis of F2, included the anisotropic thermal parameters of all non-hydrogen atoms. The conditions of crystal and powder data collections appear in Table 1.

The structures of 2, 3, 5, 6, 6′, 7 and 7′ were determined from X-ray powder data collected on a MDP-PRO diffractometer (PANalytical). The structure refinements were performed by the Rietveld method using the Fullprof software.41 Hydrogen atoms for the ligand molecules were geometrically fixed. It is worth noting that several distance restraints were used. Isotropic displacement parameters were allowed to vary successively for metal cations (Zn(1), Zn(2), Ti) and for fluorine atoms, N and C atoms together. Tentative site occupancy refinements for cations confirmed that both independent Zn sites (4b, 2a) are fully occupied, as well as the Ti site (2a), with no cross substitution. The refinement of the cell parameters of minor phases is difficult; the values were fixed from the closest temperature at which the phase proportion is significant; these inaccurate cell values are not displayed in Fig. 7.

Crystallographic data (excluding structure factors) for the structures have been deposited with the Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre as supplementary publication no. NMR spectroscopy

1H and 19F solid-state MAS NMR experiments were performed on a Bruker Avance III spectrometer operating at 7.0 T (1H and 19F Larmor frequencies of 300.1 and 282.4 MHz, respectively), using a 1.3 mm CP-MAS probe head. The 1H and 19F MAS spectra were recorded using a Hahn echo sequence with an interpulse delay equal to one rotor period. For 19F, the 90° pulse length was set to 1.25 μs and the recycle delay was set to 300 s for phases 1 and 3, and to 10 s for phases 4 and 7. For 1H, the 90° pulse length was set to 1.25 μs and the recycle delay was set to 180 s for phases 1 and 3, and to 10 s and 2 s for phases 4 and 7, respectively. 1H and 19F spectra are referenced to TMS and CFCl3, respectively. All spectra were fitted by using the DMFit software.42

DFT calculations

Chemical shielding tensor calculations were performed using the GIPAW43,44 approach implemented in CASTEP code.45,46 The Perdew–Burke–Ernzerhof (PBE) functional47 was employed in the generalized gradient approximation (GGA) for the exchange correlation energy. “On-the-fly” ultrasoft pseudopotentials provided in CASTEP were used with core radii of 0.8, 1.4, 1.5, 1.3, 1.4, 1.8 and 2.0 a. u. for H, C, N, O, F, Ti and Zn respectively, and with 1s valence orbitals for H; 2s and 2p valence orbitals for C, N, O and F; 3s, 3p, 4s and 3d valence orbitals for Ti and 4s and 3d valence orbitals for Zn. The wave functions were expanded on a plane-wave basis set with a kinetic energy cutoff of 700 eV. The total energy was converged up to change below 1 × 10−8 eV. The Brillouin zone was sampled using a Monkhorst–Pack grid spacing of 0.04 Å−1 (corresponding to a k-point mesh of 4 × 4 × 4 for phases 1 and 3, 2 × 3 × 3 for phases 4 and 5 and 2 × 4 × 4 for phase 7. Geometry optimized (APO) structures were obtained by minimizing the residual forces (|F|max below 10 meV Å−1) for all atoms, using the Broyden–fletcher–Goldfarb–Shanno (BFGS) method48 and keeping symmetry constraints and fixing cell parameters to experimentally measured values. Ordered structural models were optimized for phase 4 and phase 5. For 4, the non-centrosymmetric Pn21a space group was used to give 4o (the general 8d position of Pnma for 4 is replaced by two general 4a positions of Pn21a: x,y,z and −x,−y,−z in 4o) (Table S3†). The structure 5p was deduced from phase 5 by filling one of the two water sites only. Optimization effects are discussed in ESI (Tables S1–S5†).

NMR parameters were calculated for experimental (ES) and geometry optimized (APO) structures. The isotropic chemical shielding value is defined as σiso = (σxx + σyy + σzz)/3, σii being the principal components of the shielding tensor defined in the sequence |σzz − σiso| ≥ |σxx − σiso| ≥ |σyy − σiso|. The isotropic chemical shift is defined as δiso ≈ −σiso + σref.

Conclusions

Two hydrated or solvated hybrid organic–inorganic fluorides are evidenced from the reaction of zinc and titanium fluorides with 1,2,4-triazole in hydro(solvo)-thermal conditions at 160 °C. At room temperature, both structures are based on the same building trimers of two ZnN3F2 and one ZnN3(H2O)2F entities sharing a μ3-F central atom. The Zn3N9(H2O)2F3 trimers are connected by TiF6 units to give cis- or trans-chains ∞[Zn3TiN9(H2O)2F7]. Triazolate (C2H2N3)− anions link the chains to build the 3D network Zn3TiF7(H2O)2(taz)3. Water or ethanol molecules are inserted in the cavities of the cis or trans phases, respectively. On heating at ≈110 °C, these water or ethanol molecules are first removed from the structures; however, the removal of ethanol out of the cis-phase concerns only 35% weight per cent of the crystallites at the maximum. This part of the crystallites transforms rapidly to the anhydrous cis-Zn3TiF7(taz)3 at 120 °C while the remaining 65% transform directly to the anhydrous phase until 180 °C. This effect is probably due to the formation of a protective layer (shell) on the crystallites that limits the diffusion of ethanol out of the bulk (core) of crystallites. The final elimination of zinc bonded water molecules results in a condensation reaction that implies the connection of neighbouring chains by fluoride anions of TiF6 octahedra. All structural transformations leave the overall features of the 3D networks unchanged and, consequently, the symmetry of all cis or trans phases is maintained.

1H solid state NMR experiments confirm the water loss of phase 3 and indicate that phase 7 rehydration is effective. Moreover, the results of 1H and 19F NMR point to the existence of both positional disorder and motion of fluorine atoms within TiF6 octahedra.

All the features of these cis- or trans-Zn3TiF7(taz)3·phases show that their zeolitic, exchange, catalytic or gas adsorption properties must be investigated.
