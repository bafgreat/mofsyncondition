Introduction

Supramolecular interactions have pivotal roles in the formation of molecular aggregates or molecular assemblies in various fields, starting from covalent-organic materials to inorganic–organic hybrid materials or in the formation of macromolecular biological entities.1–7 Supramolecular interactions are associated with the concept of coordination bonds between metal centres and organic ligands (e.g., crown ether).8 Over the last few decades, the role of supramolecular interactions has been studied widely in terms of the design and synthesis of organic crystalline materials.9–11 However, utilisation of these weak chemical forces in the design and synthesis of inorganic–organic hybrid materials or coordination polymers (CPs) has not been explored.

In recent years, CPs have attracted immense attention because of their applications in gas storage/separation,12–16 magnetism,17–20 drug delivery,21–23 catalysis24–27 and conductivity.28–31 In general, CPs contain metal ion centres or metal clusters linked with organic ligands. One of the interesting features of CPs is the possibility to obtain an infinite number of structural arrangements with intriguing architectures and topologies by changing the metal ions and varying the lengths and functionalities of the organic ligands. However, one-dimensional (1D) CPs have attained special interest owing to their potential applications in electrical conductivity, magnetism and optical properties.32

Recently, we developed a series of CPs using linear dicarboxylate along with monodentate N-donor ligands. We observed that the monodentate N-donor ligands could attach in the third direction, which can undergo weak interactions to form supramolecular architectures. The importance of supramolecular interactions in terms of crystal engineering and their physical importance have been revealed.33–35 In a different approach, to obtain a deeper insight into these observations, we employed monodentate benzoate ligands (Scheme 1) instead of widely used dicarboxylate ligands. Here, we report the syntheses, crystal structures and electrical conductivity properties of two new Zn(ii)-based 1D CPs, [Zn6(bpd)3(p-clba)6(μ3-OH)4]·(p-clba)2·(CH3OH), (1) and [Zn6(bpd)3(p-brba)6(μ3-OH)4]·(p-brba)2·(CH3OH), (2) (bpd = 1,4-bis(4-pyridyl)-2,3-diaza-1,3-butadiene, H2p-clba = para-chlorobenzoic acid and H2p-brba = para-bromobenzoic acid) containing {Zn6} clusters. We noticed that both compounds had similar structures with [Zn6(μ3-OH)4]8+ secondary building units (SBUs) connected by bpd ligands to form 1D chains. Simultaneously, hydrogen bonding and π⋯π and halogen bonding interactions resulted in the formation of supramolecular assemblies in the crystal architectures. Interestingly, these two compounds showed substantive conductivity and behaved as Schottky barrier diodes (SBDs). Here, the effect on charge transport in Schottky diodes was studied by altering interchain interactions as a result of the modification of para-substituents on the benzoate ligands. In addition, mostly toxic Cd(ii) compounds have been employed for the fabrication of electronic devices for their interesting photophysical and photochemical properties.28,29,36–39 However, here we used relatively non-toxic Zn(ii) compounds for device fabrication.

Experimental section

Materials and physical method

All chemicals purchased were of reagent grade and used without further purification. Elemental analysis (carbon, hydrogen and nitrogen) was done on a PerkinElmer 240C elemental analyzer. Infrared spectra in KBr (4500–500 cm−1) were recorded using a PerkinElmer Fourier transform-infrared (FT-IR) spectrometer (RX1). Solid-state electronic absorption spectra were recorded on a PerkinElmer UV/vis spectrophotometer (LAMBDA 35). A PerkinElmer Pyris Diamond TG/DTA system was used during thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) at 30–800 °C under a nitrogen atmosphere at 12 °C min−1. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) data were collected on a Bruker D8 Advance X-ray diffractometer using Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.548 Å) generated at 40 kV and 40 mA. To check the purity of the sample, the PXRD spectrum was recorded in a 2θ range of 5–50.

Synthesis of compound 1 and 2

Synthesis of compound 1

A solution of bpd (0.042 g, 0.2 mmol) in MeOH (2 mL) was slowly and carefully layered on a solution of Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (0.06 g, 0.2 mmol) in H2O (2 mL) using 2 mL of a 1 : 1 (v/v) buffer solution of MeOH and H2O followed by layering of H2p-clba (0.031 g, 0.2 mmol) neutralised with Et3N (0.021 g, 0.2 mmol) in 2 mL of EtOH. Yellow block crystals of [Zn6(bpd)3(p-clba)6(μ3-OH)4]·(p-clba)2·(CH3OH), 1 were obtained after 3 days (0.055 g, yield = 70%). Elemental analysis (%) calcd for C93H69Cl8N12O21Zn6: C 47.16, H 2.91, N 7.09; found: C 47.65, H 2.67, N 7.12. IR (KBr pellet, cm−1): 1592 νas(COO−), 1413 νsys(COO−).

Synthesis of compound 2

Compound 2 was synthesised by a similar procedure as adopted for 1 except that H2p-brba (0.040 g, 0.2 mmol) was employed. Yellow block crystals of [Zn6(bpd)3(p-brba)6(μ3-OH)4]·(p-brba)2·(CH3OH), 2 were obtained after 3 days (0.063 g, yield = 70%). Elemental analysis (%) calcd for C93H66Br8N12O21Zn6: C 41.04, H 2.43, N 6.18; found: C 41.04, H 2.44, N 6.17. IR (KBr pellet, cm−1): 1592 νas(COO−), 1405 νsys(COO−).

General X-ray crystallography

Single-crystal XRD data of compound 1 were recorded on a SuperNova, AtlasS2 diffractometer using monochromatic Cu-Kα radiation (λ = 1.54184 Å). The molecular structure was solved with the ShelXT structure solution program40 and by using Olex2 as the graphical interface.41 However, a single crystal of 2 was used for data collection using a Bruker SMART APEX II diffractometer equipped with graphite-monochromated MoKα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) and the structure was solved using ShelXT 2014/4.40 Non-hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic thermal parameters. Hydrogen atoms were placed in their geometrically idealised positions and constrained to “ride” on their parent atoms. The crystallographic data for 1 and 2 are summarised in Table S1, and selected bond lengths and bond angles are given in Tables S2 and S3 (ESI†).

Fabrication and characterisation of the device

We undertook an electrical study of compound 1- and 2-based Schottky diodes. At first, the synthesised materials were dispersed in dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) solvent at 4 mg mL−1 and sonicated to make a stable dispersive medium. Simultaneously, indium tin oxide (ITO)-coated glass substrate was cleaned with acetone, ethanol and distilled water in sequence through sonication and dried. After that, the stable dispersion was spin-coated onto the clean ITO-coated substrate at 1000 rpm for 2 min using a SCU 2700 spin-coating unit. The film thickness was ∼1 μm according to a surface profiler. Then, aluminium (Al) metal was deposited onto the film using a shadow mask with the help of vacuum coating unit 12A4D (HINDHIVAC) at 10−6 Torr. The effective area of the film was maintained as 7.065 × 10−6 m−2.

To characterise the electrical property of the devices, current–voltage (I–V) data were recorded with the help of a Keithley 2635B source meter using a two-probe method. All preparations and characterisations were done at room temperature.

Electrical characterisation

To study I–V characteristics, two devices of Al/compound 1/ITO and Al/compound 2/ITO were fabricated. A bias voltage varying from −1 V to +1 V was applied to the devices at room temperature under dark and light conditions.

Results and discussion

Structural descriptions of [Zn6(bpd)3(p-clba)6(μ3-OH)4]·(p-clba)2·(CH3OH), (1) and [Zn6(bpd)3(p-brba)6(μ3-OH)4]·(p-brba)2·(CH3OH), (2)

Single-crystal X-ray crystallography revealed that 1 and 2 were isostructural. Compound 1 crystallised in the triclinic space group P with Z = 1. Three independent Zn(ii) ions were present in the asymmetric unit, in which Zn1 had a trigonal bipyramidal (TBP) structure, Zn2 had an octahedral geometry and Zn3 had tetrahedral geometry (Fig. 1). The asymmetric unit also contained a lattice methanol molecule and a p-clba ion. One oxygen atom from a 4-clba anion and two μ3-OH groups defined the equatorial plane, and one nitrogen atom from a bpd ligand and one μ3-OH group were at axial positions in the TBP geometry of the Zn1 centre. Three oxygen atoms from three p-clba anions and a μ3-OH group adopted a distorted equatorial plane, whereas one nitrogen atom from a bpd ligand and one μ3-OH group were in axial positions in the octahedral Zn2 centre. A bpd ligand, two oxygen atoms from two p-clba anions and a μ3-OH group were bonded to Zn3 to form a tetrahedral geometry. Furthermore, six Zn(ii) ions were held tightly by two pairs of μ3-OH groups to form a chair-like hexagonal [Zn6(μ3-OH)4]8+ SBU (Fig. S1, ESI†). The intermetallic Zn⋯Zn distances separated by the μ3-OH groups spanned in the range 3.105–3.481 Å. Hexanuclear Zn(ii) clusters were linked by bpd ligands to form 1D chains. The pyridyl rings of bpd ligands in the chain underwent extensive face-to-face π⋯π interactions with the centroid–centroid distances of 3.680–3.900 Å (Fig. 2). In addition, π⋯π and hydrogen bonding interactions between lattice 4-clba anion and SBU were noted (Fig. S2, ESI†). The 1D chains also underwent halogen bonding C–H⋯Cl interactions (H⋯Cl = 2.786 Å) to form 2D supramolecular networks (Fig. 3).

Compound 2 was isotypical with 1 and crystallized in the triclinic space group P with Z = 1. The topology of the CP was exactly the same as 1 and dictated by the connectivity of bpd ligands from the repeating chair-like hexagonal [Zn6(μ3-OH)4]8+ SBU. The intermetallic Zn⋯Zn distances separated by the μ3-OH groups spanned in the range 3.095–3.477 Å. The centroid–centroid distances of pyridyl rings involved in π⋯π interactions in the 1D chain were 3.686–3.857 Å. The 1D chains also formed 2D supramolecular architectures by C–H⋯Br interactions (H⋯Br = 2.808 Å). However, the distance between the centres of the {Zn6} clusters in adjacent chains increased from 14.125 Å in 1 to 14.322 Å in 2 (Fig. S3, ESI†).

Optical characterisation

We have recorded UV-vis absorbance (Fig. 4a) data to derive the optical band gap energy of 1 and 2 using Tauc's equation.42 (αhν)n = c(hν − Eg) (1) where α is the absorption coefficient, Eg is the optical band gap energy, h is Planck's constant, ν is frequency, c is a constant and n is the order of energy transition. The value of n is considered to be 2 and 1/2 for the possible allowed direct and indirect optical transitions, respectively.42 The band gap energy of compound 1 was derived to be 2.71 eV and 2.59 eV for direct and indirect transitions, respectively, whereas, for compound 2, it was measured to be 2.85 eV and 2.73 eV (Fig. 4b and c).

Electrical characterisation

An absorbance edge in the visible range and the band gap energy in the semiconductor region indicated the possibility of charge-transport properties of the synthesised compounds. To obtain insight into these charge-transport phenomena, we undertook electrical characterisations of compound 1- and 2-based metal–semiconductor (MS) junction devices. We applied a bias voltage of ±1 V and measured the current density (J = current/area) of the ITO/compound 1 or 2/Al-configured MS diode at room temperature (Fig. 5a). The characteristic J–V curves showed non-linear rectifying behaviour, indicating the Schottky diode nature of the materials (Fig. 5a). However, Schottky diode behaviour was improved for compound 1 as compared with 2. In addition, the conductivity of 1 in the low-voltage region (corresponding to Ohmic behaviour) was also better than that for 2. The conductivity of 1- and 2-based devices was measured to be 3.29 × 10−4 S m−1 and 2.22 × 10−4 S m−1, respectively. Notably, conductivity was enhanced with respect to the corresponding compound synthesised from the isomeric 3-bpd ligand.36 However, the higher conductivity of compound 1 could be attributed to the decrease in the metal-to-metal distance between adjacent chains.31

To obtain more information of carrier transport through the MS interface, we plotted the ln J versus ln V for the forward bias voltage regime (Fig. 5d). Using the slope of the ln J versus ln V plot, we explored the governing mechanism of carrier conduction by the Schottky interface formed in the fabricated device. Charge transport followed Ohmic conduction if the slope value was ∼1. TGA and PXRD

To check the thermal stability of the compounds, TGA were done under a N2 atmosphere at 30–800 °C. Both compounds followed a similar pattern of decomposition (Fig. S5, ESI†). Compound 1 had an initial weight loss up to 150 °C corresponding to a lattice methanol molecule and then decomposed above 200 °C. Loss of one lattice methanol for compound 2 was observed at 50–150 °C. However, compound 2 was more stable and decomposed above 250 °C. The final residues were assumed to be ZnO (ca. 20.54% for 1 and 17.87% for 2), which corresponded to the experimental weight of residues (19.22% for 1 and 16.55% for 2). PXRD was carried out at room temperature with freshly prepared samples. The PXRD patterns of compound 1 and 2 were in good agreement with those simulated from single-crystal data, thereby indicating the phase purity of the samples (Fig. S6 and S7, ESI†).

Conclusions

We have synthesised two novel 1D CPs based on para-substituted benzoate ligands together with N-donor ligands by slow diffusion. The 1D chains underwent halogen bonding C–H⋯X (X = Cl and Br) interactions to form 2D supramolecular networks. These two compounds showed high electrical conductivity and exhibited a Schottky diode nature. However, electrical conductivity and charge-transport properties were improved for the device fabricated with 1. Alteration of para-substituents of benzoate ligands had effects on interchain interactions, which impacted upon electrical conductivity. Compound 1 with a shorter interchain distance had higher conductivity and better applicability in device fabrication as compared with 2. These data suggested that electrical conductivity and Schottky nature could be modified by altering the interchain interactions as a result of modification of ligand functionalities.
