Short Communication
Novel luminescent heterobimetallic Ln–Cu(I) 3D coordination polymers based on 5-(4-pyridyl) isophthalic acid as heteroleptic ligand. Synthesis and structural characterization

Author links open overlay panel
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.inoche.2015.10.036Get rights and content

Highlights

•

Six novel, isomorphic, Ln-Cu(I) coordination polymers based on an N/O mixed ligand were synthesized.

•

First association of above lanthanides with Cu(I) in heterobimetallic 3D coordination polymers is reported

•

Upon excitation compounds 3–5 show remarkable photoluminescence of warm white, cool white and light blue

•

Color of the light emitted depends on the nature of the lanthanide ion, a key factor for advanced applications of 3–5.

Abstract

Six novel photoluminescent Ln–Cu(I) heterobimetallic 3D coordination polymers, [LnCu (pyip2 −)2(H2O)]n (1–6) (where Ln is Pr, Nd, Sm, Eu, Gd, Ho, respectively, and H2pyip = 5-(4-pyridyl) isophthalic acid) have been synthesized in a concise and reproducible manner under hydrothermal conditions. All of these substances were fully characterized by appropriate spectroscopic methods and elemental analysis. Structural determinations revealed that 1–6 are isomorphic and display 3D frameworks. Luminescent properties of the obtained compounds have been studied in detail revealing light emissions of warm white, cool white and light blue for 3, 4 and 5, respectively. The effectiveness of association of a rare earth with a transition metal in producing valuable luminescent materials for practical applications is demonstrated.

In recent years, considerable effort has been devoted to the design and synthesis of 3d–4f coordination polymers not only due to their appealing potential applications in modern areas of materials science such as photoluminescence and magnetism, probes in biosciences, and molecular adsorption but also to their spectacular architectures and diverse topologies [1], [2]. However, regiospecificity issues frequently arise in obtaining 3d–4f coordination polymers; that is because of competition between the transition metal ions and rare earth ions for coordination to the same ligand. Therefore, the synthesis of this type of hybrid materials and the investigation of their application profiles are real challenges for today's chemistry. The classical hard–soft acid base theory provides a basis for synthesis of 3d–4f coordination polymers [3]. Yet, we should take into account that rare earth ions belong to hard acids and prefer O- versus N-donors, whereas most transition metal ions are soft acids, or borderline acids, with a strong tendency to bind to N-donors [4]. Therefore, the idea of using N/O mixed ligands appears to be a promising approach for synthesizing heterobimetallic 3d–4f coordination polymers. Based on these premises, we selected 5-(4-pyridyl) isophthalic acid (H2pyip) as the most appropriate N/O mixed bifunctional, chelating ligand and structure-directing vector [5]. It was anticipated that heterobimetallic systems will be easily formed as the pyridyl motif provides N atoms to readily coordinate with transition metal ions, while the carboxylate groups combine preferentially with rare earth ions thus allowing the chemoselective synthesis of coordination polymers with diversified structures.

The H2pyip ligand has been reported previously [6] in either lanthanide complexes or transition-metal complexes. Surprisingly, studies on 3d–4f coordination polymers using this type of ligand are scarce. As a part of our on-going work on metal coordination complexes, in this paper we describe for the first time the convenient synthesis and detailed structural characterization of family novel heterobimetallic 3D coordination polymers using a three-component concept: (i) a selected array of lanthanides as photoactive sites in view of luminescent materials, (ii) a transition metal (Cu(I)) with propensity to coordinate to both N and O sites, and (iii) H2pyip as a heteroleptic organic ligand showing excellent ability for creating interstitial links between different metal ions. Lanthanides were chosen for their emission properties, long luminescence lifetime and easily identifiable emission lines [7], [8], [9]. The coordination mode of H2pyip ligand is discussed. In addition, we investigate the photoluminescent properties of the newly synthesized complexes.

After optimizing reaction conditions, we successfully obtained the unparalleled heteroleptic coordination polymers [LnCu(pyip2 −)2(H2O)]n (1–6). Compounds 1–6 were prepared by self-assembling on reacting a rare earth oxide (Pr6O11, for 1; Nd2O3, for 2; Sm2O3, for 3; Eu2O3, for 4; Gd2O3, for 5; Ho2O3, for 6) with 5-(4-pyridyl)isophthalic acid and CuI, under hydrothermal conditions. The solution of these components in 10 mL distilled water was kept at 180 °C for 6 days under autogenous pressure. Single crystal X-ray analysis revealed that compounds 1–6 are isomorphic, crystallize in the orthorhombic system, space group Pbca, and show a three-nodal topological network. The crystallographic data are listed in Table S1. Since 1–6 are isostructural, only the structure of 4 is described here in detail. The asymmetric unit of 4 contains one Eu3 + ion, one Cu+ ion, two pyip2 − ligands and one coordinated water molecule (Fig. 1). The Eu3 + central ion is in an eight coordination mode having a trigonal dodecahedral configuration with the coordination sites occupied by seven carboxylate oxygen atoms (O1, O2, O4A, O5, O6, O7A, O8A) from two different pyip2 − ligands and O9 from the coordinated water molecule. The Eu–O bond distances range from 2.290(3) to 2.505(3) Å and the bond angles of O–Eu–O are in the range of 52.49(9)–156.70(10)°, corroborating with those in other Ln carboxylate complexes from previous studies [10] (Table S2). Cu+ ions exhibit tetrahedral configuration and are four-coordinated to two nitrogen atoms (N1, N2A) and two carboxylate oxygen atoms (O3A, O5A) from two different pyip2 − ligands. The Cu–N bond distances are 1.932(4) Å and 1.953(4) Å and their bond angle is 128.66(17)°. The Cu–O bond distance is 2.025(4) Å. The N–Cu–O bond angles are 112.23(18)° and 107.17(18)°, in agreement with those observed for the penta-/hexa-coordinated Cu+ ions in carboxylate complexes [11].

Ligand pyip2 − in 4 shows two types of coordination modes (Scheme. S1). The first one adopts a (κ1-κ2-μ2)-(κ1-κ1-μ2)-κ1-μ5 mode to bridge three Eu3 + ions and two Cu+ ions in O-bridge, O,O′-bidentate, bis-O-monodentate, N-monodentate forms. The other one adopts the (κ1-κ1-μ1)-(κ1-κ1-μ2)-κ1-μ4 mode to bridge two Eu3 + ions and two Cu+ ions in O,O′-chelating, bis-O-monodentate, N-monodentate forms. The connection between ligands with (κ1-κ1-μ1)-(κ1-κ1-μ2)-κ1-μ4 mode and Eu3 + ions gives rise to a 2D Eu-ligand layer in ab plane (Fig. 2). The adjacent layers are linked together by means of pyip2 − ligands with (κ1-κ2-μ2)-(κ1-κ1-μ2)-κ1-μ5 mode to further connect Cu+ ions thus leading to a 3D framework as shown in Fig. 3.

In order to clearly understand the crystal structure of 4 using the topological approach to simplify the structure, we consider each CuEu2 trinuclear unit (Fig. S1.) as seven-connected and the two pyip2 − ligands as three-connected and four-connected, respectively. Thus, the 3D framework can be described as a three-nodal {4.62} {43.63} {44.612.85} topological network (Fig. 3).

The experimental powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns of 1–6 are consistent with those simulated from the corresponding single crystal structures, demonstrating the phase purity, as shown in Fig. S2. Most trivalent lanthanide ions show relatively narrow emission bands at a fixed wavelength originating from internal 4fn–4fn transitions, while Cu+ (3d10) commonly exhibits wide emission bands at unfixed wavelength due to d–d transitions [12]. The luminescent properties obtained when combining lanthanide and Cu+ ions into a single complex cannot be easily predicted because the metal ions which dominate the emission intensity cannot be adjudged simply. The photoluminescent properties of complexes 3–5 in the solid state were investigated at room temperature to find new behavioral patterns. The excited spectrum of compound 5 and the emission spectra of compounds 3–5 irradiated at 330 nm are presented in Fig. S3 and Fig. 4, Fig. 5, Fig. 6, respectively.

The emission spectra of complex 3 exhibit a very wide band ranging from 400 to 750 nm which indicates that the d–d transitions of Cu+ ions and the π*–π transition of the ligands are dominant throughout the emissions [12]. Of course, the characteristic emission peaks of the Sm3 + ions can also be found at the wavelength of 649 nm, attributed to the transition from 4G5/2 to 6H9/2. The other characteristic peaks of Sm3 + were overlapped by the wide emission band from d–d transitions. The CIE coordinates of (x, y) (0.38, 0.38) and the color temperature of 4100 K suggest that the emission of compound 3 is warm white light, close to that of an incandescent lamp. For complex 4, the emission spectra present a very wide band, between 400 and 600 nm, indicating that the d–d transitions of Cu+ ions and the π*–π transition of the ligands prevail during the emissions. The characteristic emission peaks 616 nm of the Eu3 + ions were observed clearly, which can be attributed to the 5D0 → 7F2 transition of Eu3 +. The CIE coordinates of (x, y) (0.24, 0.24) and the color temperature of 18,000 K suggest that the emission of compound 4 is cool white light, which is similar to blue sky. For 5, the emission spectra present three peaks at 423, 443 and 483 nm (between 400 and 550 nm), indicating that the emission mainly originated from d–d transitions of Cu+ ions and the π*–π transition of the ligands. The CIE coordinates of (x, y) (0.21, 0.21) and the color temperature of 30,000 K suggest that the emission light of compound 5 is blue.

According to the principle of the three primary colors, to implement a white light emission also needs introduction of some red light emitting ions on the basis of compound 5. When the luminously inert ion Gd3 + in the blue light emitting compound 5 was replaced by the red light ion Eu3 +, the CIE coordinates obviously red-shifted. Because the low energy transfer efficiency from the excited state of ligand to Eu3 + ions leads to less red emission, the red-shift is small. When Sm3 + ion takes the place of Gd3 + ion, the synergistic effect of Sm3 + and Cu+ bring about more red light emission, so that the CIE coordinates move to the yellow zone. This strategy can be used to modulate emission light colors of this type of Ln–Cu(I) complexes via changing the kind of the lanthanide ion [12], [13]. In on-going research we aim at changing the content of the different lanthanide ions in Ln–Cu(I) complexes in order to adjust the emission light colors.

Figs. S4–S6 in SI shows the decay profile for compounds 3–5 upon excitation at 330 nm with a pulsed nitrogen laser, respectively. The decay profiles could be fitted with three exponentials A + B1*exp.(− t/τ1) + B2*exp.(− t/τ2) + B3*exp.(− t/τ3), where A, B1, B2 and B3 are the parameters obtained from the curve fitting, and τ1, τ2 and τ3 represent the lifetime. The values of τ1, τ2 and τ3 (and their relative contribution to the total luminescent output) are obtained as follows: τ1 = 4.73 (20.3%), τ2 = 20.51 (28.8%) and τ3 = 105.16 μs (20.9%) for 3, τ1 = 4.12 (15.0%), τ2 = 18.64 (61.30%) and τ3 = 95.23 μs (23.7%) for 4, and τ1 = 2.56 (12.8%), τ1 = 15.49 (63.5%) and τ1 = 103.45 μs (23.7%) for 5.

In conclusion, a family of six new heterobimetallic coordination polymers has been readily and reliably synthesized taking advantage of an unprecedented combination of a lanthanide with Cu(I) and using the 5-(4-pyridyl) isophthalic acid as heteroleptic ligand. The role of this multifunctional organic ligand is crucial for creating the 3D structure of these frameworks. Thorough examination of the photoluminescent properties of compounds 3–5 evidenced warm white, cool white and light blue emissions upon excitation. Importantly, it was shown that playing on the nature of the lanthanide ion in the Ln–Cu(I) complex enabled color tuning of the light emission following the excitation process. This result indicates that these functional materials are amenable to applications ranging from materials science to lighting devices and probes for biomedical assays.
