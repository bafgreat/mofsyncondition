Introduction

Mechanochemistry is a rapid and environmentally benign method used for the synthesis of various materials, ranging from small organic molecules to supramolecular complexes.1–5 Typically, minimal (if any) solvent is needed, greatly reducing environmental impact and processing costs. The sub-stoichiometric amounts of solvent used in liquid-assisted grinding (LAG) typically accelerate the conversion and improve the crystallinity of the resulting polycrystalline powder products.6–8 As an alternative synthetic approach, mechanochemistry provides opportunities to target new materials and non-equilibrium compounds unattainable in solution.9

Coordination polymers (CPs), including metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), are functional materials consisting of metal nodes bridged by functionalized organic linkers.10 The interesting topologies of these materials and their physiochemical properties such as porosity, crystallinity, thermal stability, and luminescence make them promising candidates for various applications in electronics, purification, catalysis, and biomedicine.11,12 The crystal structures and properties of these materials differ depending on the type of metal, functional organic linker, and synthesis conditions.

CPs based on transition metals are intensively studied.13,14 In contrast, studies of CPs based on alkaline-earth metals (AEMs) are still limited.15–17 AEMs possess high charge densities leading to strong interactions of functional linkers like carboxylate anions.12 Such materials are envisioned for applications in such areas as gas separation, catalysis, and molecule or ion recognition.18–22 Furthermore, the abundance and biocompatibility of magnesium and calcium metals render the respective CPs ideal for biological applications.

CPs are typically synthesized under solvothermal conditions.23 For example, Murugavel et al. prepared AEM CPs based on anthranilic acid (oABAH) using metal chlorides as the metal sources.24 Arlin et al. published AEMs-CPs based on ortho- and para-aminobenzoic acids.25 Blair et al., Coles et al., and Ruschewitz et al. described fluorinated CPs based on AEMs.26–28 Côté and Shimizu, Duan et al., Williams et al., Falcão et al., and Huang et al. demonstrated the influence of AEM cation sizes, showing that increasing the cation radii leads to the formation of CPs with higher dimensionalities.19,29–32 Only recently, mechanochemistry was used for the preparation of CPs.6,33,34 We herein report an alternative, ‘green’ route to obtain Ca-, Sr-, and Ba-CPs from oABAH via ball milling. This follows our previous work, which demonstrated the preparation of Ca-, Sr-, and Ba-CPs from benzene-dicarboxylic acids and their fluorinated analogs.35–42 The AEM-CPs obtained in this work are systematically investigated by different analytical methods.

Results and discussion

Mechanochemical synthesis

Compounds 1–3 were synthesized by milling anthranilic acid (oABAH), together with Ca-, Sr-, and Ba-hydroxide in a molar ratio of 2 : 1.

{[Ca(oABA)2(H2O)3]}n (1) was obtained by milling oABAH and Ca(OH)2. A small amount of water (130 μl) was added to the powder mixture to improve the crystallinity of the final product and reduce the milling time to 1 h. In the absence of water, a less crystalline product was formed and a milling time of 4 h was necessary to complete the reaction. The powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns of the resulting products are compared in Fig. S1a.†

{[Sr(oABA)2(H2O)2]·H2O}n (2) was formed by milling oABAH and Sr(OH)2·8H2O for 1 h. Reactions carried out using the anhydrous analog, Sr(OH)2, as reactant were considerably slower (milling time = 4 h) and led to a somewhat different product than 2. This product was also observed when LAG conditions were used with Sr(OH)2 using water as a solvent, although milling time decreased (milling time = 1 h). However, the PXRD patterns for these reactions are similar to that of 2, particularly with respect to their dominant reflections (Fig. S1b†). This suggests that all Sr-based products are identical.

Milling oABAH and Ba(OH)2·8H2O led to the formation of {[Ba(oABA)2(H2O)]}n (3). The same compound 3 was also obtained in 1 h when Ba(OH)2 was used as a reagent. This was observed under both neat and LAG conditions, with water as a solvent. The two latter products, however, exhibit lower crystallinity (Fig. S1c†).

These findings are in line with our previous work on the mechanochemical syntheses of Ca-, Sr-, and Ba-CPs from benzene-dicarboxylic acids (H2BDC) and their fluorinated analogs (H2BDC-F4). The highest crystallinity of Ca-based CPs was achieved by LAG (water as solvent) of an organic precursor and Ca(OH)2.35,37,40,41 The most crystalline products of Sr-and Ba-CPs (organic linker H2BDC-F4) were instead obtained from Sr- and Ba-hydroxide octahydrates, rather than by adding (external) water to the powder mixture.37–39,41,42 For the fluorine-free CPs based on H2BDCs, the variations of water content in Sr- and Ba-hydroxide samples influence the crystallinity and the coordination systems of the final products.35,39,41,42

Crystal structures

The crystal structures of 1, 2, and 3 were successfully refined by the Rietveld method. The results of the refinements are shown in Fig. S2.† The crystallographic data are given in Table S1.† The resulting crystal structures coincide with those reported by Murugavel et al., which were synthesized by solvothermal methods.24

{[Ca(oABA)2(H2O)3]}n (1)

The Ca-based compound crystallizes in the orthorhombic space group Pbcn.24 The asymmetric unit consists of a Ca2+ ion and a water molecule; both positioned on a two-fold axis. The asymmetric unit is completed by one organic linker and two additional water molecules. The CaO7 polyhedron comprises four carboxylate-oxygen ligands from four organic linkers and three terminal oxygen atoms from water molecules (Fig. 1a, left). The organic linkers bridge the Ca2+ ions via the carboxylate oxygen atoms (O1, O2) to form 1D-polymeric chains (Fig. 1a, right). An extended hydrogen bond network (HBN) forms the supramolecular structure of 1. The Ca2+⋯Ca2+ distance of 4.706(1) Å is elongated due to the separation between the CaO7 polyhedra via the organic linkers. The inter-chain Ca2+⋯Ca2+ distance of 14.2 Å is equal to the half-length of the crystallographic b-axis.

{[Sr(oABA)2(H2O)2]·H2O}n (2)

Sr-Anthranilate trihydrate crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2.24 The asymmetric unit consists of one Sr2+ ion, two organic linkers, and three water molecules. The Sr2+ ions have a coordination number of nine (Fig. 1b, left) higher than the smaller, hepta-coordinated Ca2+ ions in (1). The coordination environment of Sr2+ comprises five carboxylate oxygen atoms (from four organic ligands), one amino group, one coordinating water molecule (O7w), and two half bridging water molecules (O5w, O6w). The Sr2+ ions are arranged in 1D-polymeric chains, formed by direct inorganic connections through the carboxylate oxygen atoms (O1, O2) and the two bridging water molecules (O5w, O6w) in addition to the indirect connections via the amino (N2) group. The co-crystallized water molecules (O8w) stabilize the supramolecular structure by connecting the neighboring 1D polymeric chains in an extensive HBN. This leads to the overall supramolecular structure observed in this material (Fig. 1b, right). The intermetallic distance within the 1D polymeric chain is 3.917(4) Å. The inter-chain Sr2+⋯Sr2+ separation is 7.832(9) Å.

{[Ba(oABA)2(H2O)]}n (3)

The Ba-based CP crystallizes in the orthorhombic space group Pbcn.24 The asymmetric unit consists of one Ba2+ ion, two organic linkers, and one water molecule. In the crystal structure of 3, the Ba2+ ion has a coordination number of nine, similar to the Sr-compound 2. However, the local coordination environment varies significantly between 2 and 3. The nine-fold coordination of Ba2+ ion comprises six carboxylate-oxygen atoms, one amino group, and one water molecule (Fig. 1c, left). Unlike the 1D-polymeric chains in 1 and 2, the polymeric chains in 3 extend to a 2D-network. The Ba2+ ions are bridged by two carboxylate oxygen atoms (O1, O3), water-oxygen atom (O5w), and amino group (N1) to form 1D-polymeric chains. Ba2+ ions from two neighboring chains are bridged via the carboxylate oxygen ligand (O2) to extend the motif to 2D-polymeric chains. A HBN is formed by interactions of water molecules, carboxylate oxygen, and amino groups, and it is limited to the 2D-polymeric chains. Layers of 2D-polymeric chains form the final crystal structure of 3 (Fig. 1c, right). The intermetallic distance is 3.329(1) Å within a 1D polymeric chain, and 6.084(1) Å between the bridged 1D-chains (i.e., within the 2D network). The shortest interlayer distance between to Ba2+ ions amounts to 14.369(5).

Influence of metal cation sizes

The differences in the coordination environments, the content of crystal water, and reaction modes of the oABA linkers in the crystal structures of 1, 2, and 3 are determined by the variation in cation sizes of the AEMs Ca, Sr, and Ba. The larger Sr2+ and Ba2+ ions exhibit higher coordination numbers (CN = 9) compared to the smaller Ca2+ ion (CN = 7). The local coordination sites of the two larger cations are filled by amino group nitrogen atoms of organic ligands (Fig. 1b and c). Moreover, the metal-bound water molecules act as bridging ligands (in Sr- and Ba-CPs) rather than only capping ligands (Ca-CP).

One type of oABA2− organic linker is identified in the crystal structure of 1 (Fig. 1a). One carboxylate oxygen ligand (O1 or O2) bounds to a single Ca2+ ion, with an average Ca–O(carboxylate) bond lengths of 2.34 Å. Each of the neighboring CaO7 polyhedra forms an eight-membered ring (Ca2O4C2) consisting of two Ca ions, four carboxylate-oxygen atoms, and two carboxyl carbon atoms. The nitrogen-amino atom is not directly coordinated to the Ca2+ ions. In the crystal structure of 1, the amino group interacts with the carboxylate oxygen atoms and coordinating water molecules via hydrogen bonding interactions.

Two types of oABA2− anions are found in the crystal structure of 2 (Fig. 1b) differing in their interaction geometry. oABA2− (I) chelates one Sr2+ ion via two carboxylate oxygen atoms (O1, O2) and bridges the chelated metal to two other Sr2+ ions. The bridging mode of the carboxylate group allows the amino group in oABA2− (I) to coordinate to nearby bridged Sr2+ ion. The second type of ligand, oABA2− (II) is monodentate, interacting through one carboxylate-oxygen atom (O3). The average Sr–O(carboxylate) bond length is 2.61 Å. The Sr–N(amino) bond length is 3.069(2) Å. The C–N bond distance 1.437(2) Å of the metal-bound amino group is larger than the C–N bond distance 1.396(1) Å of the non-coordinating nitrogen-amino atoms.

Two types of oABA2− ligands are found in 3 (Fig. 1c). oABA2− (I) coordinates to three Ba2+ ions via the carboxylate oxygen ligand (O1) that bridges two Ba2+ ions and the amino group (N1) which coordinates to one Ba2+ ion. oABA2− (I) ions further bridge the 1D-inorganic chains via the carboxylate-oxygen atom (O2) and extending the connection into 2D-polymeric chains. oABA2− (II) chelates one Ba2+ ion via the carboxylate group (O3, O4) and bridges another one via O3. The average Ba–O(carboxyl) bond length is 2.79 Å. The average MII–O(carboxyl) distances increases from 2.34, 2.61, to 2.79 Å as cation size increases from Ca2+, Sr2+, to Ba2+ in 1, 2, and 3, respectively.

The numbers and nature of crystallized water molecules vary with cation sizes. The smaller Ca2+ ions have three metal-bound water molecules which are not involved in further interactions. The three water molecules in 2 embrace one water molecule bound to Sr2+ ion in terminus, two water molecules bridge Sr2+ ions, and a co-crystallized water molecule. The sole water molecule in 3 bridges Ba2+ ions within the 1D polymeric chain. In the three crystal structures, the MII–O(water) distances are longer than MII–O(carboxyl) bonds. The MII–O bonds tend to have longer distances as the cation sizes increase. The average MII–O(water) bond lengths are 2.46, 2.66, and 2.86 Å in the crystal structures 1, 2, and 3, respectively.

Based on the influence of cation sizes on the bonding modes of the organic linker and the role of crystal water, the crystal structures of 1, 2, and 3 exhibit different organic–inorganic connectivities (Fig. 1, left). Compound 1 exhibits pure organic connectivity of I0O1 in which the CaO7 polyhedra are separated by the bridging carboxylate groups. Each neighboring SrO8N or BaO8N polyhedron within the 1D-polymeric chains in 2 or 3 exhibit inorganic connectivity via two carboxylate-oxygen anions and one water-oxygen atom. In the structure of 3, the polymeric chains are further bridged via the organic linkers and form 2D-pattern. For that, the polyhedron connectivity in 2 and 3 are described as I1O0 and I1O1, correspondingly (ImOn terminology by Cheetham et al. is used, where m = inorganic connections and n = organic bridges).43

Wiesbrock et al. reported a crystal structure of Mg-anthranilate octahydrate {[(Mg(H2O)6)(oABA)2(H2O)2]}n which consists of a hexahydrate Mg2+ ion, two anthranilate anions, and two co-crystallized water molecules.44 Contrarily to Ca2+, Sr2+, and Ba2+ ions in the structures of 1, 2, and 3, respectively, the local coordination sphere of the Mg2+ ion does not include anthranilate anions.

The influence of cation size on the CNs, organic–inorganic connectivity, and dimensionality in CPs based on AEMs and N-containing organic ligands have been studied by other authors. For example, in the study of Falcão et al. the CN, M–O–M inorganic connectivity, and dimensionality of the CPs Mg-, Ca-, Sr-, and Ba-thiazolothiazoledicarboxylates increase upon increasing cation size (Mg2+ < Ca2+ < Sr2+ < Ba2+ ions). With increasing ion radii, the CN increases as 6, 7, 8, and 10 for Mg2+ < Ca2+ < Sr2+ < Ba2+, respectively. Following the same cation ordering, the inorganic connectivity increases as 0, 1, 2, 3, and dimensionality increases as 0, 1, 1, and 2.32 Huang et al. reported crystal structures based on Mg-, Ca-, Sr-, and Ba-ions and 1-carboxymethylpyridinium-4-carboxylate that vary in dimensionality from 0 (Mg-CP), 2D (Ca- and Sr-CPs), to undulated 2D (Ba-CP).19

The local coordination environments around the Ca2+ and Sr2+ ions in the refined crystal structures of 1 and 2, respectively, were investigated by EXAFS measurements (Fig. S3†). The simulated data and the measured spectra are consistent within the first coordination sphere of the respective metal and the refined structures. The M–O bond lengths in 1 and 2 (in addition to Sr–N in 2), scattering paths, and fit parameters are given in Table S2.†

Characterization

oABAH exhibits a polymorphic transition (from phase I to phase III) depending on the conditions of crystallization.45–49oABAH (Aldrich, 98%) is identical to the phase I crystal structure published by Brown et al.46 in which the asymmetric unit contains one neutral and one zwitterionic molecule. The two non-equivalent molecules are connected by hydrogen bonds.46,47 The structural properties of the organic linker are confirmed by MAS NMR (Fig. 2, black spectra) and ATR-IR measurements (Fig. S4,† black spectrum). The 1H MAS NMR spectrum depicts a broad signal at δ = 7.1 ppm corresponding to hydrogen bonds. At least nine carbon positions could be resolved in the 1H → 13C CP MAS NMR spectrum in the chemical shift range of 150–117 ppm. The ATR-IR spectrum exhibits two bands around 3318 cm−1 and 3237 cm−1 that correspond to the stretching vibrations of ν (N–H) and ν (O–H).

The measured MAS NMR spectra of compounds 1, 2, and 3 are consistent with the determined crystal structures. Fig. 2a shows the typical 1H MAS NMR spectra of compounds 1 (red spectrum), 2 (blue spectrum), and 3 (green spectrum), with protons located on benzene rings and amino groups showing superimposed resonances. Milling removes the strong hydrogen bridged network of oABAH, visible by the proton signal of the acid at approximately 18 ppm (black spectrum). Due to the superposition of the individual signals, the overall 1H MAS NMR spectra are broad and hard to resolve. The lower intensity in the green spectrum compared to red and blue spectra is due to the lower content of crystal-embedded water molecules in 3 (one water molecule) compared to compounds 1 and 2 (three crystal water molecules).

The 1H → 13C CP MAS NMR spectrum of 1 (Fig. 2b, red spectrum) depicts one signal, corresponding to one type of carboxylate carbon atom at δ = 173 ppm. This is consistent with the refined crystal structure. The signals observed at 149 and 122 ppm are assigned to the aromatic carbon atoms bound to the carboxyl and amino groups. The other four carbon (C–H) types known from the crystal structure are assigned as δ = 134, 132, 117, and 116 ppm. The variations in the intensities stem from the fact that some of the aromatic C–H bonds are involved in intra- and inter-molecular hydrogen bonds with carboxylate-oxygen atoms, water-oxygen atoms, and amino-nitrogen atoms. The narrow and well-defined signals are an indication of the high crystallinity of the Ca-containing sample.

In the 1H → 13C CP MAS NMR spectrum of 2 (Fig. 2b, blue spectrum), the two carboxylate carbon atoms from the two types of organic linkers are assigned to δ = 176 and 174 ppm. The two signals at 123 and 121 ppm are assigned to the two carbon atoms bound to amino-nitrogen atoms (C–NH2). The signal at 149 ppm corresponds to the aromatic carbon atoms connected to carboxyl groups and are free of protons (C(ring)–COO2−). The signals corresponding to the aromatic carbon atoms C–H are seen at δ = 132, 120, 118, 116 ppm. The other C–H signals known from the crystal structure are present within the broad signal at 134 ppm.

The 1H → 13C CP MAS NMR spectrum of 3 (Fig. 2b, green spectrum) exhibits one signal corresponding to the carboxylate carbon atoms. The two carboxylate carbon atoms from the two types of organic linkers are not observed as both carbon types are quite similar in their bonding modes. The proton-free carbon atoms bound to the carboxyl groups are assigned to the signal at 150 ppm. The two unique C–NH2 carbon atoms, known from the crystal structure, are resolved at 120 and 119 ppm. The C–H positions in the aromatic rings are assigned to the signal at 133 (broad signal with a shoulder at 134 ppm), 132, 118, and 116 ppm.

The vibrational bands belonging to the stretching vibration of the amine groups in the ATR-IR spectrum of anthranilic acid (Fig. S4,† black spectrum) are masked by the strong vibrational bands above 3000 cm−1 in the red, blue, and green spectra. These are attributed to the presence of different types of water molecules, and the formation of HBNs along the crystal structures of 1–3.50 The vibrational bands assigned to water bending appear at 1643 cm−1 (red spectrum), 1662 cm−1 (blue spectrum), and 1700 cm−1 (green spectrum). The broad band around 3000 to 3400 cm−1 are assigned to the presence of HBN within the crystal lattices. The OH stretching of water is assigned at 3418 cm−1 (compounds 1 and 2) and 3440 cm−1 (compound 3). The OH stretching at 3693 and 3612 cm−1 indicate the presence of co-crystallized water in 2. The vibrational bands assigned to the carboxylate stretching appear at 1530 cm−1 for asymmetric stretching (νas) and 1393 cm−1 for symmetric stretching (νs) and at 1512 cm−1(νas) and 1380 cm−1(νs) in compound 3. The separation (Δa–s) between asymmetric and symmetric stretching of carboxylate groups are about 137 cm−1 (compounds 1 and 2) and 132 cm−1 (compound 3) which are attributed to the multi-dentate mode of carboxylate groups in the crystal structures of 1–3.51,52

The formation of the hydrated compounds of 1, 2, and 3 are confirmed by the results of the elemental analysis (Table S3†) and thermal analysis (Fig. S5†). The thermoanalytical investigations indicate the liberation of three water molecules in the samples of compounds 1 and 2 and one water molecule for compound 3. The release of the crystallized water is completed by 175 °C for 1 (measured: −14.5%, calc. 14.7% for 3H2O molecules), 150 °C for 2 (measured: −12.8%, calc. 13% for 3H2O molecules), and 245 °C for 3 (measured: −4.1%, calc. 4.2% for 1H2O molecule). The water-free frameworks of the three compounds are stable up to 280–300 °C.

Compounds 1, 2, and 3 transform into new phases upon dehydration, as indicated by comparison of the PXRD patterns (Fig. 3). The PXRD pattern of the dehydrated sample 3 shows remnants of the hydrated phase. This is confirmed by the Bragg reflections that match the PXRD pattern of the hydrated sample (not all are visible in the depicted figure). The reflections at 2θ = 6.1° and 12.3° correspond to the formation of a new phase. The separation between the two phases was difficult due to the high degree of peak overlap in the data.

The hydrated samples of 1, 2, and 3 exhibit very small surface areas that decrease for the dehydrated samples (Table S2†). The controlled hydration of the dehydrated samples 1, 2, and 3 (annealed at 200 °C) indicate chemical sorption for all samples (Fig. 4), and formation of new hydrated phases as indicated by PXRD patterns (Fig. 3, red patterns). For Ba-CP (3), the dehydrated–hydrated transition is nearly reversible (Fig. 3c). Attempts at solving the crystal structures of the dehydrated CPs and sample 2 after controlled hydration are in progress. For more investigations, dynamic vapor sorption (DVS) experiments were performed for the Ca-CP. The sorption isotherms of n-octane over sample 1 as-synthesized and after thermal post-treatment (annealed at 200 °C) are depicted in Fig. S6.† The dehydrated sample exhibits poor sorption properties compared to the hydrated sample. The SEM images (Fig. S7†) depict similar morphologies for 1 and 3 with variation in crystallite sizes. Sample 2 contains plates-like structures.

Summary

In summary, we described the mechanochemical synthesis, structural diversities, and phase transformation of coordination polymers based on alkaline earth metals and anthranilic acid. Compounds 1, 2, and 3 were synthesized by milling Ca-, Sr-, or Ba-hydroxide samples and anthranilic acid, respectively. High crystalline compounds are achieved by adding a small amount of water to Ca(OH)2 to compensate for the absence of water required for building the trihydrate CP 1. The metal hydroxide octahydrates Sr(OH)2·8H2O and Ba(OH)2·8H2O were used as inorganic precursors for synthesizing Sr-, and Ba-based CPs, respectively. Here, the highest crystallinity is achieved using the present internal water source (crystal water from the crystal structure of octahydrate metal hydroxide samples) rather adding a small amount of water to Sr(OH)2 or Ba(OH)2 samples.

The crystal structures of CPs 1, 2, and 3 were refined via the Rietveld method. Interestingly, these compounds maintain their structural integrities known through signal crystals after solvothermal treatments.

Coordination number, connectivity in polyhedra, and the dimensionality of crystal structures are determined by cation sizes, as the cation sizes become larger as CN increases from 7 (in Ca-CP) to 9 (Sr- and Ba-CP) and with the connectivity of I0O1, I1O0, to I1O1, respectively. Crystal water in CPs based on the heavier metals (Sr and Ba) is involved in the bridging systems rather than only capping ligands (Ca-CP). These compounds are thermally stable and exhibit small surface areas. Compounds 1–3 transform into new dehydrated phases upon thermal post-treatments which can be a reversible phase transition process (Ba-CP).
