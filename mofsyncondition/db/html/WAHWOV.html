Introduction

Copper(i) halide complexes are a subject of continuing interest and research efforts due to their structural diversity,1 photochemical or photophysical properties2–4 and possible applications as luminescence-based sensors, OLED materials, sensitisers in solar energy conversion and biological imaging agents.5 The complexes of general formula CunXnLm (where X = Cl, Br or I; and L = N, P or S-bound organic ligand) are a rich family of discrete or extended polymeric structures. Combination of CuX with monodentate neutral ligands usually leads to a wide variety of cluster compounds including simple rhomboid dimers, cubane, stepped or open cubane tetramers and more complex metal-halide aggregates.1,6–9 A prominent example is the pyridine (py) adduct with CuI creating a cubane tetramer Cu4I4(py)4 extensively explored due to its unusual luminescence properties.2,10,11 On the other hand, copper(i) halide coordination polymers may be solely based on metal-halide frameworks or a combination of metal-halide substructures with bridging ligand linkers. The polymers may assume various one-, two- or in some cases three-dimensional architectures. Thus, 1D aggregates consisting of CuX systems connected by mono- or bidentate ligands may form zigzag chains, double chains, helical chains or ribbon motifs.1 2D undulating layers and 3D CuX aggregates are sporadically encountered.1,12 The structure of the CuX aggregates is strongly influenced by the nature, size and shape of organic ligands and to some extent by the conditions of the crystallization process.

Although aromatic nitrogen donor-ligands predominate but in recent years sulfur-containing compounds have played an increasingly important role in the construction of organic–inorganic hybrid coordination polymers.13–17 This kind of ligand containing sulfur “soft” donor atoms has a strong affinity to “soft” copper(i) Lewis acids. Thioamides being an important and easily accessible class of organosulfur compounds are particularly attractive as ligands in metal complexes because of their relevance in biological systems, and interesting chemical, structural and spectroscopic properties.13,18–20 Their coordination chemistry with Cu(i) and Ag(i) ions and luminescence properties have captured considerable interest in recent years.21–31

We focused our attention on dithiodianthranilides {dibenzo[b,f][1,5]diazocine-6,12(5H,11H)-dithiones} mdta and bdta bearing two thiocarbonyl donors accessible for interaction with metal centers (Chart 1). N-Alkylation of thioamide groups not only improves the solubility of these compounds in organic solvents but also excludes possible contribution from a thiol tautomer (–NH–C(S)– –NC(–SH)–). Although dithionolactams mdta and bdta are devoid of stereogenic centers, they adopt chiral boat conformations and may exist in two enantiomeric forms. The enantiomers are exchangeable due to a slow boat-to-boat ring inversion (Scheme 1). Since the barrier to ring inversion in these compounds is sufficiently high (ΔG is of 36.5 kcal mol−1) we have been able to resolve mdta to configurationally stable enantiomers.32,33

This work reports the synthesis of CuX complexes with dithiodianthranilides mdta and bdta ditopic ligands, characterization of their crystal structures with the aim of studying the role of ligand stereochemistry in the self-assembly of metal-halide aggregates. Thus in the case of mdta we were able to compare the structures of the complexes obtained with the racemic as well as the optically active (homochiral) ligand. In the case of the optically active coordination polymers we recorded their circular dichroism (CD) spectra in the solid state and in solution. We also prepared the AgI complex with mdta for comparison with its CuI counterpart that illustrated structural and spectroscopic analogies between copper(i) and silver(i) halide complexes.34 Luminescent d10 polynuclear metal complexes have recently undergone a great development due to their potential applications as optoelectronic materials.2–5,35 Particularly a short metal–metal distance is crucial for their emission properties.2 Thus we measured solid state luminescence spectra of the obtained coordination polymers.

Results and discussion

Comparison of crystal structures

The crystal structures of the complexes 1–10 and the ligand bdta were determined by single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis and their crystallographic data are collected in Table S1.† The self-assembly reaction of the dithiodianthranilides mdta or bdta with copper(i) halides resulted in polymeric complexes where polynuclear CuX clusters were linked together by the ditopic bridging ligands into 1D chains (Scheme 2a and b) or 2D network structures (Scheme 2c). The structures formed by the racemic ligand mdta were completely different from those created by the homochiral (R)-(−)-mdta. Topological description of the dimensionality of hybrid organic–inorganic frameworks was proposed by Cheetham et al. based on the connectivity of inorganic and organic parts using the symbol InOm, where n and m represent the connectivity of inorganic and organic parts, respectively and n + m ≤ 3.36 Symmetry of subperiodic structures was described in terms of rod groups or layer groups.37 Thus the double stranded chains I0O1 (Scheme 2a) with centrosymmetric inorganic clusters Cu4X4 (X = Br, I) or Ag4I4 (1, 3, 9, and 10) connected by enantiomeric pairs of the ditopic ligands belong to the rod group p (R2). Similarly, the chain I0O1 in 8 with the Cu4I4 cluster part of the C2 symmetry and inversion center between the pair of enantiomeric linkers belongs to the rod group p12/c1 (R7). In contrast, homochiral (R)-(−)-mdta creates a single strand chain I0O1 involving Cu5I5 clusters linked by single enantiomers of mdta as in 2 (Scheme 2b), the rod group p1 (R1) or the layers I0O2 with a square (4,4) network, if clusters shrink to point nodes, composed of Cu3X3 or Cu3X4 clusters and homochiral ligand molecules as in 4–7 (Scheme 2c).

The bulkiness of the N-substituent in the ligand molecules seems to be an important factor influencing the structure and availability of the complexes; for example CuCl easily forms complexes with mdta but it does not react with bdta. Probably in this case, bulky N-benzyl groups pointing outside the dithiolactam ring in bdta units make close contact between the 2D layers impossible and thus dense packing in the solid state as it occurs in the crystals of 4–7. Also the geometries of the Cu4I4 tetramers are influenced by the N-substituent, comparing the case of 1 and 8 showing the Ci and C2 cluster symmetry, respectively (Fig. 1a and b).

The reaction of CuI with racemic mdta afforded monoclinic crystals with the space group P21/n of the complex [Cu2I2(mdta)]n (1). Analogously, the reaction of CuBr with mdta gave the complex [Cu2Br2(mdta)]n (3) (Fig. S3†), isostructural with 1. The structure of 1, shown in Fig. 2 and S1,† is a double stranded chain polymer with a centrosymmetric tetranuclear [Cu4I4(mdta)2] unit as a building-block element. The chains extend in the [010] direction (b axis). The centrosymmetric Cu4I4 tetramers assume a ‘stair-step’ (stepped cubane) conformation with the inner pair of four-coordinate Cu2 atoms and the outer pair of three-coordinate Cu1 atoms (Fig. 1a). This geometry leads to a short Cu⋯Cu contact of 2.8192(7) Å between inner and outer Cu atoms, Cu1–Cu2 (x, y + 1, z), which is close to the sum of the van der Waals radii of Cu atoms (2.80 Å), implying the existence of weak Cu⋯Cu interactions. The Cu⋯Cu distance in 3 is 2.8202(8) Å for Cu1–Cu2 (x, y − 1, z). The stepped cubane Cu4X4 core has been found in several discrete 0D CuX adducts1 but there are only two examples of this core incorporated in polymeric chain structures involving bidentate ligands.38,39 Each pair of enantiomeric mdta ligand molecules is related by center of inversion. They assume a boat conformation which is close to that found in the crystals of uncomplexed compounds.33 The Cu–S bond lengths are 2.2357(10) for Cu1–S1 and 2.2881(10) Å for Cu2–S2.

The polymeric complex 2, with the formula [Cu5I5{(R)-(−)-mdta}(MeCN)3]n, obtained by the reaction of CuI with the homochiral (R)-(−)-mdta crystallizes in the orthorhombic space group P212121. The structure of 2, shown in Fig. 3 and S2,† reveals infinite single stranded chains running parallel to the a axis and composed of solvated pentanuclear Cu5I5 clusters connected by optically active (R)-(−)-mdta ligand molecules. In the Cu5I5 core four Cu atoms (without Cu4) create a severely distorted cubane framework bridged by four iodide atoms (without I4) with an inserted supernumerary Cu2–I4 unit on one of the cubane faces (Fig. 1e). All five Cu atoms in the cluster adopt the tetrahedral 4-coordinated geometry and are bridged by one μ2-I (I4), three μ3-I (I1, I2 and I5) and one μ4-I (I3) atom, two copper Cu1 and Cu5 atoms coordinate the S atoms in (R)-(−)-mdta ligands whereas the remaining three are coordinated to three MeCN ligands. In this cluster the Cu⋯Cu distances of 2.7219(11) for Cu3–Cu5 (x + 1, y, z), 2.7219(11) for Cu2–Cu4 and 2.7396(11) Å Cu1–Cu5 (x + 1, y, z) are slightly shorter than those observed in compound 1. Two analogous pentanuclear Cu5I5 clusters embedded in coordination polymers have been so far reported.40,41

The complexation of CuBr with the optically active ligand (R)-(−)-mdta yielded [Cu3Br3{(R)-(−)-mdta}2(MeCN)]n (4) crystallizing as the MeCN solvate in the monoclinic space group P21. The structure of complex 4, shown in Fig. 4 and S4,† is again very different from that of the racemic analogue 3. This reveals a 2D network parallel to the (1 0 −1) crystal plane, formed from 36-membered rings composed of atoms belonging to four mdta molecules bridging four Cu3(μ3-Br)(μ-Br)2 clusters being nodes of the net (Fig. 1c). The clusters are nearly planar ladder structures and each one is bridged by two mdta ligands above and two below the trinuclear plane. They are constituted by two tetrahedrally and one trigonally co-ordinated Cu(i) atoms, one triple-bridging, and two double-bridging bromine atoms. The Cu⋯Cu distances within the Cu3(μ3-Br)(μ-Br)2 framework are 2.802(5) for Cu1–Cu3 and 2.916(4) Å for Cu2–Cu3 (−x, y + 1/2, −z). This kind of a ‘three-runged ladder’ structure has been found in some CuX complexes with 2,5-bis[(diphenylphosphino)methyl]thiophene42 and 2,3-dimethylpyrazine.43

The reaction of CuCl with the racemic mdta afforded the layer complex [Cu3Cl3(mdta)2(MeCN)2]n (5) crystallizing as a conglomerate which results in its self-resolution into enantiomeric chiral crystals. Thus the single crystal structure of complex 6 obtained with optically active (R)-(−)-mdta is identical with that of 5. The structure of 5 (Fig. S5†) and 6 closely resembles that of 4, the only difference is that the monomeric unit complex [Cu3Cl3 (mdta)2] is solvated by two molecules of MeCN and the Cu⋯Cu distances within the Cu3(μ3-Cl)(μ-Cl)2 frame of 2.7927(15) for Cu2–Cu3 and 2.8312(15) Å for Cu1–Cu2 are slightly shorter than those in 4. Nevertheless, in both cases the solvent is present in the second coordination sphere and placed between the inorganic–organic hybrid layers. It is noteworthy that obtaining noncentrosymmetric materials from achiral or racemic building blocks is of great significance in materials chemistry due to their potential applications in nonlinear optics (NLO).44

The reaction of CuCl2 with (R)-(−)-mdta produced the mixed-valence complex [Cu3Cl4{(R)-(−)-mdta}2(MeCN)]n (7) due to partial reduction of CuCl2 by the thiocarbonyl ligand.45 Complex 7 crystallizes as the interlamellar MeCN solvate in the monoclinic space group P21 and its structure (Fig. S6†) closely resembles that of 6. The nearly planar Cu3(μ3-Cl)(μ-Cl)2(η1-Cl) cluster composed of two tetrahedrally co-ordinated Cu(i) atoms and one square-planar tetracoordinated Cu(ii) atom (Fig. 1d) is unprecedented in the vast majority of rich CuX clusters. Topologically, structures 5, 6 and 7 are very similar, with the main difference being in the node composition (Cu3Cl3 in 5 and 6versus Cu3Cl4 in 7). The Cu⋯Cu non-bonding distances within this frame of 3.4271(9) for Cu1–Cu3 and 3.4881(9) Å for Cu2–Cu3 are significantly longer than those in 4 and 6.

The bdta ligand adopts a boat conformation in the solid state with the geometry closely corresponding to that of mdta (Fig. S10†). However, more bulky N-substituents modify its coordination behaviour with copper(i) halides. Thus, the polymeric complex [Cu2I2(bdta)(MeCN)]n (8) crystallizing as a MeCN solvate in the monoclinic space group I2/a as a 1D double stranded chain. Although its crystal structure (Fig. 5 and S7†), in some respect, resembles that of 1 the Cu4(μ3-I)2(μ-I)2 core tetramers bridged by two enantiomeric pairs of bdta ligands assume a ‘displaced stair-step’ geometry of the C2 symmetry (Fig. 1b). This core tetramer located on the two-fold symmetry axis is composed of two tetrahedrally co-ordinated and two trigonally co-ordinated Cu(i) atoms, two triple-bridging, and two double-bridging iodine atoms. The Cu⋯Cu non-bonding distance within this frame of 2.6427(6) Å for Cu1–Cu3 is significantly shorter than the sum of the van der Waals radii of Cu atoms. This kind of ‘displaced stair-step’ core structure is unprecedented among CuX clusters, however it has been reported as a fragment of some extended CuX aggregates.46,47

The structure of the polymeric complex [Cu2Br2(bdta)]n (9) crystallizing in the space group P closely resembles that of 1 (Scheme 2a and Fig. S8†). The centrosymmetric Cu4Br4 tetramers assuming a ‘stair-step’ geometry connected by pairs of enantiomeric bdta molecules create a double stranded chain running along the a axis. The Cu⋯Cu non-bonding distance within the Cu4Br4 core frame is 2.9053(5) Å for Cu1–Cu2 (x + 1, y, z).

The polymeric complex [Ag2I2(mdta)]n (10) crystallizing in the monoclinic space group P21/n resulted from the reaction of AgI with racemic mdta is isostructural with 1. Tetrameric Ag4I4 ‘stair-step’ clusters connected by enantiomeric pairs of mdta molecules create a double stranded chain running along the b axis (Fig. S9†). The shortest Ag⋯Ag distance within this frame is 3.1479(3) Å which is shorter than the sum of the van der Waals radii of Ag atoms (3.44 Å).

Spectral characterization

The significant IR bands of the complexes 1–9 are listed in the Experimental section. The diagnostic peaks due to the thioamide function in free mdta and bdta ligands occurring at 1474 and 1301 cm−1 have little CS content and originate mainly from CN vibrations whereas the peaks at 1097 and 771 cm−1 are mainly due to the ν(CS) vibrations.48 There is a little change in the position of first two bands upon complexation whereas the ν(CS) peaks shift to a slightly lower energy region due to the coordination of thione sulphur to the metal center, but the shifts are not significant. In the case of the acetonitrile solvates 2 and 4–8 the ν(CN) band near 2270 cm−1 was observed.

The electronic absorption spectra of the free mdta and bdta ligands taken in non-polar solvent show a relatively weak band at 393 nm that shifts by 15 nm to the blue upon changing the solvent to methanol. It has been assigned to the forbidden thioamide n–π* electronic transition.33 Much stronger absorption bands at 285 and 230 nm are only slightly dependent on the solvent changes and correspond to the allowed π–π* and σC–S–π* excitations, respectively.33 The solid state absorption spectra of the free ligands measured in KBr discs do not differ significantly from those taken in solution and show a weak absorption at 388 nm and a stronger one near 285 nm (Fig. 6).

The CD spectra of the free ligand (R)-(−)-mdta measured in solution and in the solid state (KBr disc) are quite similar and reveal a negative Cotton effect at 390 nm or at 402 nm in solution or in the solid state, respectively, which reflects the helicity of the thiobenzamide chromophore twisted in the M sense [the SC–CC angles are −54.5(2) and −59.7(2)°].33 The UV-vis absorption spectra of the complexes 1, 3, 5, 8 and 10 taken in acetonitrile solution closely resemble those of the free mdta and bdta ligands measured in the same solvent. Similarly the circular dichroism (CD) curves of 2, 4, 6 and 7 in MeCN (Fig. S11†) almost exactly match that of the (R)-(−)-mdta ligand.33 This is apparently due to the instability of the polymeric complexes in acetonitrile solution that results in their dissociation to starting materials.29 In contrast, the solid state absorption spectra (diffuse reflectance) (Fig. 6) revealed some differences between the complexes and free ligands: the long-wavelength absorptions of the copper(i) complexes occur at slightly lower energies and they are shifted slightly above 400 nm, they are also significantly broader with the shape suggesting contribution of at least two electronic transitions. Indeed, the CD spectra of the optically active complexes 2, 4 and 6 measured in KBr discs (Fig. 6) reveal two oppositely signed Cotton effects in this region: a negative one at 450 nm and a positive one near 370 nm. The same CD sign corresponding to the long-wavelength absorption of the free ligand (R)-(−)-mdta and the complexes 2, 4, and 6 may suggest a contribution from the ligand centered (LC) n–π* electronic transition, and ligand-to-metal (LMCT) or metal-to-ligand (MLCT) charge transfer transitions to the lowest energy absorption of the complexes. Of these neither the LC and MLCT would appear likely since the LC in mdta and bdta occur at slightly higher energies whereas the MLCTs are important in Cu(i) complexes of easily reducible ligands.49 Furthermore, the lowest energy absorption of the silver complex 10 occurs at slightly shorter wavelengths than in the our Cu(i) complexes. Thus according to the above considerations the LMCT remains as the most likely assignment. The nature of the positive CD band at shorter wavelengths of the complexes is less clear. Since its position shifts from 392 nm through 371 nm to 347 nm in the CuI, CuBr and CuCl complexes 2, 4 and 6, respectively, it may suggest some contribution from the halide-to-metal charge transfer (XMCT).2

Luminescence features

The photoluminescence properties of the free ligands mdta and bdta as well as the Cu(i) complexes 1, 3, 5 and 8 were examined in acetonitrile solution at room temperature. They show very similar blue fluorescence with the maximum at 410 nm upon irradiation at 325 nm with the intensity rapidly increasing during irradiation (Fig. S14†), which is apparently due to rapid photolysis of ligands in solution. Furthermore, almost the same shape of the emission curves exhibited by the free ligands and the complexes again points to dissociation of the last ones to the starting materials in acetonitrile solution. Thus we measured the luminescence spectra of the complexes in the solid state. At room temperature only the bdta complexes 8, 9 and the mdta complex 5 revealed weak emission centred near 620 nm and 595 nm, respectively (Fig. 7).

Cooling of the samples down to 10 K caused a significant increase of emission of all complexes due to the suppression of nonradiative processes. The mdta complexes 2 and 4 as well as the bdta complexes 8 and 9 exhibit almost identical emission spectra centered at 600 and 615 nm, respectively. This can indicate negligible changes in the excited state energy when bromine is replaced by iodine, and a slight decrease of energy when methyl is replaced by the benzyl group. A strong hypsochromic shift of the emission of silver(i) complex 10 to 540 nm evidences that the metal core has a key influence on the electronic transitions in the discussed complexes.

Time-dependent luminescence spectroscopy investigations revealed that at room temperature emission of the complexes 5, 8, and 9 decays with an average lifetime of 0.7–6 μs (Fig. 8, Table S2†). Under cooling, these values for all investigated complexes increase remarkably reaching 17–84 μs. Such relatively long luminescence indicates that triplet states are involved in the emission process, which can be either phosphorescence from the lowest triplet state or delayed fluorescence from the lowest excited singlet state. Both these processes can be efficient in copper and silver complexes due to high spin–orbit coupling enabling enhanced intersystem crossing between T1 and S1, S0 states. However, delayed fluorescence is usually a thermally activated process and its efficiency decreases with cooling, which is not the case for all investigated complexes. Moreover, luminescence spectral shape and maxima are not affected by temperature or delay time: at RT and 10 K, spectra in both the nanosecond and microsecond time domain remain very similar. We thus conclude that all complexes exhibit phosphorescence from the lowest triplet state, whilst fluorescence from the S1 state cannot be detected due to a very high ISC rate and probably low radiative rate.

Experimental section

General materials and techniques

All reagents and solvents were purchased from commercial sources which were of reagent grade. The ligands mdta and bdta were obtained in excellent yield by thionation of corresponding dianthranilides with Lawesson's reagent.33,50 The synthesis and resolution of mdta to enantiomers has been described earlier.33 Dibenzyldibenzo[b,f][1,5]diazocine-6,12(5H,11H)-dione was obtained according to a literature procedure.51 CuX complexes were prepared by treatment of the ligands with excess copper(i) halides in boiling MeCN and the single crystals were obtained by cooling the solution to room temperature or by slow evaporation of the solvent.

1H and 13C NMR spectra were obtained with a Varian Unity Plus spectrometer at 500 and 125 MHz, respectively. The deuterated solvents were used as an internal lock for 1H and 13C NMR. FTIR spectra were recorded with a Bruker IFS66 spectrometer.

Absorption spectra were recorded on a UV-1900 Shimadzu spectrophotometer. Steady-state fluorescence spectra were recorded on a Cary Eclipse spectrofluorometer (Varian Inc., Australia) using right-angle “L-shaped” geometry for solutions and front face geometry for crystals. Fluorescence spectra were corrected for instrumental sensitivity. Time-dependent luminescence investigations were conducted using a C4334-01 streak camera (Hamamatsu, Japan) and a YAG:Nd laser PL 2143A/SS type and a parametric optical generator PG 401/SH as an excitation source. Low-temperature experiments were carried out in a helium-cooled cryostat.

The solid state CD spectra were taken in freshly prepared KBr disks and recorded with a JASCO J-715 dichrograph. A mixture of 1–1.5 mg of the sample and 100 mg of dried KBr was ground and formed into a disk of 0.5 mm thickness and radius of 15 mm. The disk was rotated around the optical axis and the CD recordings were made for several positions in order to check the reproducibility of the spectra.

X-ray structural analysis

Diffraction reflection intensity data were collected on an IPDS 2T dual-beam diffractometer (STOE & Cie GmbH, Darmstadt, Germany) at 120.0(2) K with MoKα radiation of a microfocus X-ray source (GeniX 3D Mo High Flux, Xenocs, Sassenage, 50 kV, 1.0 mA, λ = 0.71069 Å). The investigated crystal was thermostated in a nitrogen stream at 120 K using a CryoStream-800 device (Oxford CryoSystem, UK) during the entire experiment. Data collection and data reduction were controlled using the X-Area 1.75 program (STOE). The absorption correction was performed on the integrated reflections by a combination of frame scaling, reflection scaling and a spherical absorption correction. The structures were solved by intrinsic phasing methods (ShelXT) and refined anisotropically using the program package OLEX2 (ref. 52) and SHELX-2015. The positions of the CH hydrogen atoms were calculated geometrically and taken into account with isotropic temperature factors and refined in the usual, default riding model. The structure of 5 was refined as a non-merohedral twin with contribution of two domains of 0.778(5) and 0.222(5). Severely disordered MeCN molecules were found in the structure of 8 and were removed from the electron density map (and the CIF file) using a numerical procedure (“Use solvent mask” in OLEX2). The program found two ca. 495 Å3 voids with 78 electrons inside each, which is equivalent to ca. 7 molecules of MeCN per unit cell (Z = 8 in this space group). No absorption correction was applied to the structure of ligand bdta due to low absorption (m = 0.26 mm−1).

Conclusions

In conclusion, the self-assembly of chiral dithiodianthranilides with copper(i) halides afforded polymeric complexes where polynuclear CuX clusters were linked together by the ditopic ligands into hybrid organic–inorganic 1D chains or 2D network structures. X-ray crystallography revealed that the structures formed by the racemic ligands were completely different from those created by the homochiral ones. Homochiral ligands achieve optimum interactions with the inorganic part either by reconstruction of CuX clusters (Cu5X5 or Cu3X3 instead of Cu4X4) or/and increasing the dimensionality of the hybrid network. Furthermore, the polymeric CuCl complex obtained from the racemic ligand crystallized as a conglomerate that led to its self-resolution into enantiomeric chiral crystals. Such a simple method creating noncentrosymmetric materials from racemic building blocks might be of some significance in materials chemistry. The complexes easily dissociate to starting materials in acetonitrile solution as revealed by absorption, CD and photoluminescence spectra taken in this solvent. Thus we performed their spectral characterization in the solid state. The solid state CD spectra appeared to be useful in assigning the lowest energy excited states: they revealed contribution of two electronic transitions to the diffuse lowest energy absorption band. In addition the observed long wavelength Cotton effect sign reflected the helicity of the thiobenzamide chromophore in the ligand unit. According to the steady-state and time-resolved photoluminescence investigations at room temperature and 10 K, emission of the complexes originates from the triplet state. The phosphorescence of the investigated copper(i) complexes is centered at 600–620 nm whereas the silver(i) complex phosphoresces at 520 nm.
