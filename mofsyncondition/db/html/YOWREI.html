1. Introduction

The quest for functional materials has emerged with considerable new urgency over the last two decades due to the worldwide efforts towards the design and synthesis of novel organic-inorganic compounds. Those which are crystalline and based on traditional coordination chemistry are commonly known as coordination polymers, with the relationship between the properties of the metal centres and bridging organic ligands being determinant in the attained dimensionality (i.e., 1D, 2D or 3D) and properties.1 Besides the peculiar structural architectures found almost on a daily basis, the symbiotic presence in the same material of organic and inorganic components has led to the development of new types of materials, many of which have potential industrial applications2 in gas storage and separation,3,4 guest exchange,3,5 sensors based on optical or magnetic properties,6 and catalysis.7

Our research group has been investigating the use of rare-earth elements (RE) as the metallic nodes in the construction of multi-dimensional coordination polymers with carboxylate- and/or phosphonate-based organic linkers,8–10 with the final objective of isolating novel photoluminescent materials.11 Indeed, the use of chelating groups with two or more phosphonate groups has recently resulted in the isolation of a number of structures by other groups, having highly flexible and/or aromatic-based organic moieties.12–14 Nevertheless, it still remains a challenge to discover the finer structural details of this type of compounds because they are usually isolated as poorly crystalline materials.15 In this sense, we have recently proposed a strategy combining X-ray diffraction, solid-state NMR and photoluminescence to unveil their structure.10 Based on the work by Lin14 (on chiral Lewis acid catalysts in the cyanosilylation of aldehydes, ring opening of meso-carboxylic anhydride, and Diels–Alder reactions), Seri16,17 (on the cyclodehydration of the monosaccharide glucose into 5-hydroxymethyl-2-furaldehyde in water) and Snejko18 (on the use of heterogeneous recyclable Lewis-acid catalysts), we envisaged that our photoluminescent phosphonate-based materials, in particular those having protonated phosphonic acid groups,9,10 could be used as effective heterogeneous catalysts. For example, the acid-catalysed cyclodehydration of xylose in the liquid phase gives furfural, which is nowadays a platform product of significant commercial interest used in the production of a wide range of important non-petroleum-derived chemicals.19 Several industrial processes employ traditional mineral acids as catalysts but these are toxic, corrosive and homogeneous. The type of layered crystalline materials we have been investigating9,10,20 presents a number of advantages in catalysis: on the one hand, they are insoluble in all common solvents, which allows easier separation of reactants/products, catalyst recycling, handling and storage; on the other, they are usually thermally stable at least up to 300 °C, which permits their regeneration using conventional calcination procedures, commonly employed for zeolitic materials.

In this manuscript we report the synthesis, detailed structural characterisation, catalytic activity and photophysical properties of a series of layered RE networks formulated as [RE(H2cmp)(H2O)] [where H5cmp stands for (carboxymethyl)iminodi(methylphosphonic acid); RE3+ = Y3+ (1), La3+ (2), Pr3+ (3), Nd3+ (4), Sm3+ (5), Eu3+ (6), Gd3+ (7), Tb3+ (8), Dy3+ (9), Ho3+ (10) and Er3+ (11)]. The structural details of these compounds were unveiled using synchrotron radiation (micro-crystal and powder) diffraction data in close combination with data derived from solid-state NMR studies and also from photophysical measurements. The catalytic performance of all materials (and a detailed study for 1) in the cyclodehydration of xylose to furfural was investigated. The photoluminescent properties of the pure Eu3+ material (6) and a diluted sample, [(Gd0.95Eu0.05)(H2cmp)(H2O)] (12), were also studied at room and low temperature. To the best of our knowledge these layered materials constitute the first examples of photoluminescent and catalytic rare-earth-based coordination polymers.

2. Experimental

2.1. General instrumentation

Elemental analyses for carbon, hydrogen and nitrogen were performed on an Exeter Analytical CE-440 Elemental Analyser at the Department of Chemistry of the University of Cambridge. Samples were combusted under an oxygen atmosphere at 975 °C for 1 minute, with helium used as the purge gas.

Attenuated total reflection Fourier transform infrared (ATR-FT-IR) spectra (range 4000-100 cm−1) were measured on a Matson 7000 FT-IR spectrometer equipped with a Specac Golden Gate Mk II ATR Accessory having a diamond top-plate and KRS-5 focusing lenses. FT-Ramanspectra (range 4000–100 cm−1) were recorded on a Bruker RFS 100 spectrometer with a Nd:YAG coherent laser (λ = 1064 nm).

Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were carried out using a Shimadzu TGA 50, from room temperature to ca. 700 °C, with a heating rate of 5 °C/min, under a continuous nitrogen stream with a flow rate of 20 cm3/min.

Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images were collected using either Hitachi S-4100 or Hitachi SU-70 field emission gun tungsten filament instruments working typically at 15 or 25 kV, respectively.

2.2. Synthesis

Mixtures containing the rare-earth (RE) chloride salt (RECl3·nH2O, where RE3+ = Y3+, La3+, Pr3+, Nd3+, Sm3+, Gd3+, Eu3+, Tb3+, Dy3+, Ho3+ or Er3+) and N-(carboxymethyl)iminodi(methylphosphonic acid) [H5cmp, (H2O3PCH2)2NCH2CO2H, ≥ 98%, Fluka] were prepared in distilled water (typical molar ratios for RE : H5cmp : H2O of 1 : 1 : 650), and stirred thoroughly for 120 min at ambient conditions. The obtained homogeneous suspension was transferred to a Teflon-lined Parr Instruments autoclave and placed inside a MMM Venticell oven (Confort model with minicomputer-controlled temperature programme) to react at 180 °C for 48 hours. Reaction vessels were allowed to cool slowly to room temperature (while inside the oven) before opening. The mixed-lanthanide [(Gd0.95Eu0.05)(H2cmp)(H2O)] (12) material was prepared by an analogous experimental procedure using the appropriate lanthanide molar ratio (19 Gd : 1 Eu). [RE(H2cmp)(H2O)] materials were recovered by vacuum filtration mainly as microcrystalline powders, washed with abundant amounts of distilled water and dried at ambient conditions for at least two days.

2.3. Synchrotron micro-crystal X-ray diffraction

Very thin fragments of [La(H2cmp)(H2O)] (2) were examined under an optical microscope and mounted on Hampton Research CryoLoops using PARATONE oil.21 Several fragments were inspected under the synchrotron radiation beam at the Swiss-Norwegian BM01a beamline (ESRF, Grenoble, France) until the collected diffraction patterns resembled that of a typical single crystal and for which the collected frames could also be unequivocally indexed. A complete micro-crystal data set was collected at 100(2) K for the best diffracting crystal on a KUMA-KM6 goniometer equipped with an Oxford Cryosystems cryostream, monitored remotely using the software interface Cryopad,22 and using a highly monochromatic synchrotron radiation with wavelength fixed at λ = 0.71013 Å. Images were processed using the software package CRYSALIS.23 Data were not corrected for absorption. The (micro-)single-crystal structure of [La(H2cmp)(H2O)], hereafter designated as 2s, was solved using the direct methods implemented in SHELXS-97,24,25 which allowed the immediate location of the majority of the atoms composing the framework backbone. All the remaining non-hydrogen atoms were directly located from difference Fourier maps calculated from successive full-matrix least squares refinement cycles on F2 using SHELXL-97.25,26 Due to the overall poor quality of the crystal, only the lanthanum and phosphorus sites could be successfully refined using anisotropic displacement parameters, with all remaining non-hydrogen atoms being instead refined using independent isotropic displacement parameters.

Hydrogen atoms were located at their idealised positions using appropriate HFIX instructions in SHELXL (23 for –CH2–, 13 for the –NH moiety and 147 for the protonated P–OH group), and included in the structural model for subsequent refinement cycles using a typical riding-motion approximation with isotropic thermal displacements parameters (Uiso) fixed at 1.2 × Ueq of the atom to which they are attached.‡

2.4. Synchrotronpowder X-ray diffraction

Powder synchrotron X-ray diffraction patterns with a high signal-to-noise ratio for the bulk materials of 2, 3, 4, 8 and 11, suitable for Rietveld refinements, were collected at ambient temperature (298K) at the multi-purpose powder station assembled at the Swiss-Norwegian BM01a beam line (bending magnet source) at the ESRF. Finely powdered samples were loaded into Hilgenberg borosilicate glass capillaries (ca. 0.4 mm in diameter) which were slowly spun 180° (in φ scanning mode) during data collection to improve powder averaging. Two-dimensional powder data were collected using a MAR345 image plate detector and then integrated using the software Fit2D.27 The monochromatic wavelength (λ = 0.71013 Å) and sample-to-detector distance (d of about 150 mm, giving a usable 2θ range of about 1–48° for the one-dimensional pattern) were calibrated from an individual run using the standard LaB6.

Prior to the use of the collected data, all powder patterns could be readily indexed with DICVOL04,28 thus unequivocally confirming the assumed structural similarities with compound 2s. Rietveld structural refinements29 using as starting premise the atomic coordinates of the structural model of 2s were performed with FullProf.2k.30 Background was corrected using fixed points throughout the entire angular range determined by the linear interpolation between consecutive (and manually selected) breakpoints in the powder pattern. Gaussian profile functions, along with two asymmetry correction parameters, were selected to generate the line shapes of the simulated diffraction peaks. The angular dependence of the full-width-at-half-maximum (FWHM) of individual reflections was also taken into account by employing a Caglioti function correction.31

Overall refinements for all structures were performed in consecutive stages to avoid instability and divergence. Zero shift, scale factor, parameters related to peak shape and unit cell parameters were consecutively added as fully refineable variables upon previous full convergence of the remaining parameters to their optimal values. Fractional atomic coordinates for all non-hydrogen atoms were ultimately allowed to refine in conjunction with weighted soft distance constraints solely applied to the H2cmp3− residue (N–C, C–C, C–H, P–O and O⋯O bond lengths and internuclear distances, respectively). This approach has the advantage of ensuring a final chemically feasible geometry for this organic moiety while it allows the rare-earth environment to refine freely. A common refineable isotropic parameter was used for all non-hydrogen atoms. No correction was made for absorption effects. After full convergence of the model the hydrogen atoms associated with the –CH2–, –NH and –POH moieties were added to the structure using a similar strategy to that employed for 2s. The final Rietveld refinements were performed with the fractional atomic coordinates of the hydrogen atoms fixed (in order to avoid divergence), ultimately converging to the profile and reliability factors summarized in Table 1 (for 2), and in Fig. S2, S3, S7 and S10 (see ESI‡) for structures 3, 4, 8 and 11, respectively.

To unequivocally confirming the unit cell metrics of the bulk material (not shown) powder X-ray data were collected at the high-resolution powder synchrotron ID31 beam line at the ESRF. Data were collected at 100K and the monochromatic wavelength was fixed at 0.29509(2) Å to prevent radiation damage. Powdered samples were loaded into Hilgenberg borosilicate glass capillaries (1.0 mm of diameter), which were rotated at 3 kHz during data collection to improve powder averaging over the individual crystallites, thus avoiding textural effects (e.g., preferential orientation). Acquisition was performed in a continuous-scanning mode (ca. 2° 2θ min−1 in order to eliminate the dead time of a conventional step scan) over the angular range of 0–20° 2θ, with accumulation times increasing with the scattering angle. The counts of eight detectors (covering roughly 8° 2θ) were rebinned and normalised to give the equivalent step scans suitable for further structural analyses.

2.8. Catalysis

Batch catalytic experiments were performed under nitrogen in a tubular glass micro-reactor equipped with a valve for gas purging. In a typical procedure, D-xylose (30 mg), powdered catalyst (20 mg) and a solvent mixture comprising H2O (0.3 mL) and toluene (0.7 mL) were poured into the reactor. The reaction mixtures were heated with a thermostatically controlled oil bath at 170 °C and stirred magnetically at 600 rpm. The influence of the stirring rate on the reaction rate becomes negligible over 600 rpm. Zero time was taken to be the instant the micro-reactor was immersed in the oil bath. In the recycling experiments with [Y(H2cmp)(H2O)] (1), the solid was separated from the liquid phase by centrifugation and regenerated either by thermal treatment at 280 °C for 3 h (method T; heating rate 1°C min−1) or by multiple washing procedures (method W; the centrifuged solid was washed with methanol, ethanol/water (50% v/v) and dried at 60 °C overnight).

3. Results and discussion

3.1. General considerations

As-synthesised materials belonging to the [RE(H2cmp)(H2O)] family [where H5cmp stands for (carboxymethyl)iminodi(methylphosphonic acid); RE3+ = Y3+ (1), La3+ (2), Pr3+ (3), Nd3+ (4), Sm3+ (5), Eu3+ (6), Gd3+ (7), Tb3+ (8), Dy3+ (9), Ho3+ (10) and Er3+ (11)] were systematically isolated as micro-crystalline powders (see Fig. S14 to S23 in the ESI‡) which prevented a priori a straightforward elucidation of their structural details using conventional X-ray diffraction techniques. Mao and collaborators34 reported very recently two layered compounds belonging to the same system: [La(H2cmp)(H2O)] and [La(H4cmp)(H3cmp)(H2O)]·2H2O. Even though the structures reported in this manuscript are similar to the former material (structural details and comparisons are provided in the following sections), the unit cell parameters and overall symmetry are undoubtedly different. Indeed, precise unit cell parameters and symmetry for compounds 1 to 11 have been unequivocally obtained from synchrotron (micro-)single-crystal X-ray diffraction in tandem with high-resolution synchrotron powder X-ray data (for the analysis of the bulk solids – data not shown). All members of this series of compounds are, thus, structurally identical (see Rietveld plots provided in the ESI‡). The unit cell parameters (a = 9.9649(13) Å; b = 10.5833(15) Å; c = 10.5288(15) Å) and space group (Pca21) of the lanthanum compound described by Mao et al. do not describe the collected powder patterns for 1 to 11. Indeed, at 100K and at room temperature the collected powder patterns exhibit significantly more reflections than those expected considering the metrics reported by Mao et al. Hence, this material constitutes a striking example of supramolecular polymorphism among coordination polymers.

All materials exhibit identical crystal habit, being essentially composed of small and very thin plates or plate-like aggregates. Rietveld refinements using conventional powder X-ray diffraction data allowed the refinement of the G1 parameter of the March's modified function for preferential orientation along the [002] vector, which was found between 0.468(1) and 0.962(2) (see ESI‡). These values are typical of plate-like crystals, in good agreement with the observed morphology. A comparison of the observed crystal habits with a simulation using the traditional Bravais–Friedel–Donnay–Harker (BFDH)35 method (Fig. 2 left) clearly shows that the addition of small building subunits (such as lanthanide centres and bridging organic ligands) are much faster processes than the formation of a new layer along the c-axis. This leads to much larger (002) faces and very thin crystals (in fact, nano-sized for most cases, see Fig. 2 right). Since the Brønsted acid sites which are important for the catalytic studies are uniformly distributed on top of the (002) layer, we infer that this crystal habit favours the observed catalytic features of the materials.

3.2. Crystal structure description

Due to the isostructural features of all materials belonging to the [RE(H2cmp)(H2O)] family, in this section we shall only discuss the structural details of the (micro-)single-crystal determination of the lanthanum-based compound (2s), with the analogous geometrical values for the powder determination of the bulk solid (2) being tabulated side-by-side for comparative purposes.

The crystal structure calls for a single La3+ metal centre, one bridging and chelating trianionic organic ligand (H2cmp3−) and one coordinated water molecule (see Fig. 3a). La3+ is eight-coordinated, {LaO8}, to two carboxylate oxygen atoms of the same H2cmp3−ligand [O(1) and O(2)] in a typical syn,syn-chelating mode, to five phosphonate oxygen atoms belonging to four symmetry-related H2cmp3− anionic ligands [O(4), O(5), O(6), O(7) and O(8)], and to one water molecule [O(1W)]. The overall coordination geometry resembles a highly distorted dodecahedron with the La–O bond lengths ranging between 2.38(2) Å and 2.70(2) Å (Table 2), and the O–La–O polyhedral angles being in the 48.6(8)–149.9(8)° range (Table 3). These values are comparable with those found in the polymorphic compound reported by Mao and collaborators:34 2.360(9)–2.728(8) Å and 48.0(3)–153.8(3)° for the bond lengths and angles, respectively. In contrast to other layered materials having the same organic ligand, [Ln(H2cmp)]·xH2O (x < 1; Ln3+ = Nd3+ and Eu3+),20 or [Ln(H3NMP)]·1.5(H2O) [Ln3+ = La3+, Pr3+, Nd3+, Sm3+ or Eu3+; H3NMP3− is a residue of nitrilotris(methylenephosphonic acid)],10 a water molecule is included in the first coordination sphere in 2s. Indeed, the presence of this coordinated water has an important impact on the photoluminescence properties of the Eu3+-based material, [Eu(H2cmp)(H2O)] (6), particularly the lifetime (see details in the photoluminescence section).

The location of the two protonation hydrogen atoms in the H2cmp3− anionic ligand could only be established by using in tandem several characterisation techniques, in particular high-resolution synchrotron (micro-)single-crystal diffraction data, vibrational (FT-IR and FT-Raman) and solid-state NMR spectroscopies. While the C(1) carboxylate and the P(2) phosphonate groups are fully deprotonated, giving –CO2− and –PO32−, the terminal oxygen of P(1) is protonated (–PO3H−). Moreover, the H2cmp3−ligand is present in a typical zwitterionic form as a consequence of the protonation of the central uncoordinated amine functional group [N(1)], which is a recurrent behaviour among amino-phosphonate based organic ligands, as revealed by systematic searches in the literature and in the Cambridge Structural Database.36 These features of the H2cmp3−ligand are similar to those reported for its supramolecular polymorph34 and in the related layered materials recently reported by us.20 The crystallographically independent anionic H2cmp3− anion behaves as a heptadentate ligand due to the coordination to five symmetry-related La3+ metal centres, showing the high connectivity recently described for related flexible poly-phosphonate based ligands.10,12,20,34 H2cmp3− establishes three single unidentate interactions involving the two phosphonate groups with three symmetry-related La3+ metal centres (mode I; Fig. S11 in the ESI‡), and two chelating bidentate interactions with the other two optical centres, one involving the carboxylate group (mode II; Fig. S11‡) and the other involving the two distinct phosphonate groups (mode III; Fig. S11‡).

The structural flexibility allied to the high connectivity (bridging and chelating) of the H2cmp3− anionic ligand originates a 1D tape running parallel to the a-axis of the unit cell (Fig. 3). When viewed along the [001] direction of the unit cell this tape exhibits the La3+ metal centres arranged in a linear fashion (Fig. 3b); on the other hand, when view viewed along the [010] direction of the unit cell the La3+ centres are distributed in a zig-zag fashion (Fig. 3c). The smallest intra-tape La⋯La distance is 6.1995(4) Å. Inter-tape connections along the [010] direction are assured by the bridging phosphonate groups, in particular via the P(1)–O(4) and P(2)–O(7) terminal groups (mode I; Fig. S11 in the ESI‡), leading to the formation of a neutral two-dimensional (2D) network, ∞2[La(H2cmp)(H2O)], extended in the ab plane of the unit cell. The La⋯La intermetallic distance across neighbouring tapes is 5.650(4) Å and 6.9824(4) Å (Fig. 4a).

The structural robustness within the ∞2[La(H2cmp)(H2O)] layers is further assured by cooperative N–H⋯O hydrogen bonds involving the central protonated amine group and neighbouring oxygen atoms (dashed green lines; Table 4, Fig. 4b and 5, and Fig. S12 in the ESI‡). In fact, N(1) is involved in trifurcated hydrogen bonds in which the protonating hydrogen could be donated to three neighbouring oxygen atoms belonging to the carboxylate [O(1)], the P(2) phosphonate groups [O(6)] and the coordinated water molecule [O(1W)] (Table 4). The close packing of individual ∞2[La(H2cmp)(H2O)] layers along the [001] direction of the unit cell is mediated by strong and highly directional inter-layer O–H⋯O hydrogen bonds (dashed blue lines; Table 4 and Fig. 5): the hydroxyl of the protonated phosphonate group, P(1)O(3)–H(3), donates the hydrogen to the adjacent O(2) oxygen atom of the carboxylate group in the adjacent layer (Table 4).

3.3. Topological studies

The mathematical reduction of the layered [RE(H2cmp)(H2O)] networks to central nodes (i.e., the rare-earth centres) and bridging rods (H2cmp3− residues which establish physical links between adjacent nodal points)37 reveals the presence of 12-connected uninodal plane nets with total Schäfli symbol of 330.434.52 (Fig. S13 in the ESI‡), for which the inter-nodal distances vary between ca. 5.66 and 10.45 Å. This result further confirms the identical connectivity with the supramolecular polymorphic lanthanum structure reported by Mao and collaborators,34 which has the same topology (unveiled using the software package TOPOS),38 even though the overall symmetry and cell metrics are markedly different in the two compounds. Nevertheless, this connectivity is, to the best of our knowledge, completely unprecedented. Indeed, even though the EPINET39 contains over one thousand 2D tilings having 3- and 4-ring circuits, no plane net has been so far enumerated also containing 5-ring circuits.

This topological feature seems to be a trend for layered materials having residues of H5cmp: on the one hand, the [Ln(H2cmp)]·xH2O layered materials recently described by us20 are 8-connected uninodal nets with Schäfli symbol of 312.414.52; on the other, the second material reported by Mao and collaborators34 and having pendant protonated carboxylic acid groups, [La(H4cmp)(H3cmp)(H2O)]·2H2O, is also a 12-connected uninodal net with Schäfli symbol of 333.432.5.

3.4. Solid-state NMR

Fig. 6 shows the 31P, 13C and 1H solid-state NMR spectra of materials 2 (spectra b, d, f and g) and 5 (spectra a, c and e), which contain La3+ and Sm3+ metal centres, respectively. Although the crystal structure of 5 calls for two crystallographically distinct phosphorus sites, the 31P MAS spectrum (Fig. 6a) shows only a single peak. There are, however, two resonances on the 31P MAS spectrum of 2 (Fig. 6b), and because both compounds are isostructural (see Fig. 1 and Fig. S4 in the ESI‡), the single peak observed in Fig. 6a must be due to the overlap of two distinct phosphorus resonances [P(1) and P(2)]. In addition, the 13C CPMAS spectra of 5 (Fig. 6c) and 2 (Fig. 6d) clearly attest the purity of the samples due to the presence of four 13C resonances in each spectra, in agreement with the presence of a unique H2cmp3−ligand in the asymmetric unit. It is worth mentioning that the 13C doublets at ca. 52.7 and 57 ppm (Fig. 6d) correspond to the two 13C signals [C(3) and C(4); Fig. 3] adjacent to the phosphorus nuclei. The splitting is caused by the JCP coupling interaction. The 13C spectrum of 5 has resonances in the same chemical shift region as 2. Although the 13C resonances in the 40–70 ppm region overlap, the observation of a unique carboxylate resonance at ca. 174.7 ppm is further evidence of sample purity. 1H MAS NMR experiments confirm the presence of an acidic 1H environment, assigned to P–OH groups, which is translated in a deshielded 1H resonance appearing around 12.3–12.8 ppm in samples 5 (Fig. 6e) and 2 (Fig. 6f). To improve the resolution of the 1H NMR spectra we have used high-resolution 1H CRAMPS, by implementing the wPMLG technique.33 This technique removes the strong 1H–1H dipolar couplings, thus enhancing the 1H resolution. Below 10 ppm the high-resolution 1H NMR spectrum of 2 (Fig. 6g) shows four distinct resonances: one at ca. 8 ppm and three in the 1–5.5 ppm region. The resonances at ca. 8 and 5 ppm are assigned to +N–H and H2Oprotons, while the remaining peaks are attributed to the distinct aliphatic CH2protons.

3.5. Photoluminescence

The excitation spectrum of [Eu(H2cmp)(H2O)] (6) recorded at room temperature (Fig. 7a) displays a series of sharp lines assigned to the 7F0,1 → 5D4–0, 5L6, 5G2–6, 5H3–7 and 5F1–5 Eu3+intra-4f6 transitions. In the high energy region, the spectrum exhibits an additional broad band peaking at 254 nm and attributed to oxygen-to-Eu3+ charge transfer (CT). At 12 K an extra broad band appears in the UV region (Fig. 7b) which can be attributed to the ligands excited levels. This band was isolated by monitoring the emission at 410 nm from the ligands (see below) of the compound (Fig. 7c), and does not appear at room temperature suggesting that this excitation path is thermally deactivated.

The sharp lines in the emission spectra (Fig. 8) are mainly assigned to transitions between the first excited non-degenerate 5D0 state and the 7F0–4 levels of the fundamental Eu3+ septet. The presence of one line for the non-degenerated 5D0 → 7F0 transition, together with the local-field splitting of the 7F1,2 levels into three and five Stark components, respectively (Fig. 8), and the predominance of the electric-dipole 5D0 → 7F2 transition, suggests the presence of a single Eu3+ environment in a low symmetry local site, in good agreement with the crystallographic evidence. The very low intensity of the 5D1 → 7F0–4 lines (Fig. 8) demonstrates an efficient non-radiative relaxation from higher excited states, such as 5D1, to the 5D0 level. The 5D0 decay curves (Fig. S24 in the ESI‡) are well fitted by single-exponential functions, independently of the monitored emission, giving a lifetime of 0.86 ± 0.01 ms at room-temperature and 0.89 ± 0.01 ms at 12 K, again supporting the presence of a single Eu3+ site. With the excitation performed on the ligand levels (maximum at ca. 325 nm) there is an extra broad emission in the UV/blue region (maximum at ca. 390 nm) due to emission from the organic ligand (Fig. 9). The Eu3+ levels are observed as self-absorption transitions. The decay curve of this emission is well fitted by a single-exponential function (Fig. 9) giving a lifetime of 727 ± 12 ms, in accordance with the existence of a crystallographically independent organic ligand in the structure (as also revealed by the crystallographic and solid-state NMR studies). The long lifetime suggests the persistence of the light emission of the organic ligand (phosphorescence).

The excitation spectrum of [(Gd0.95Eu0.05)(H2cmp)(H2O)] (12) at room temperature, monitored at the main 5D0 → 7F2 line (616.5 nm), displays the Eu3+ lines and the characteristic excitation lines of Gd3+ ions (Fig. S25‡). This is clear evidence for a Gd3+-to-Eu3+ energy transfer. Furthermore, the Gd3+-to-Eu3+ energy transfer is also detected in the emission of [(Gd0.95Eu0.05)(H2cmp)(H2O)], (Fig. S26 in the ESI‡). The time-resolved emission spectrum of 12 at 12 K excited in the Gd3+ (272 nm) levels shows the typical Eu3+ lines (present in the spectrum of the pure Eu3+ sample), the 6P5/2,7/2 → 8S7/2 transitions of Gd3+ ion and the broad band emission of the ligand (Fig. S26 in the ESI‡). The 5D0 decay curve of the Eu3+ diluted sample at room temperature (Fig. S27 in the ESI‡) gives the same lifetime (0.86 ± 0.01 ms) as found for 6, thus supporting the absence of a quenching effect of the Eu3+ concentration. This strongly indicates that the O–H oscillators associated with the coordinated water molecule play a decisive role in the non-radiative relaxation process of Eu3+ emission. The 6P7/2 decay curve of the Gd3+ emission (Fig. S27 in the ESI‡) is also fitted by a single-exponential function giving a lifetime of 1.14 ± 0.01 ms at room temperature (1.72 ± 0.01 ms at 12 K) in the range of the inorganic compound CsY1−xGdxP2O7,40 supporting once again the presence of a unique Ln3+ site and also the high structural versatility of this family of compounds, which may accommodate in the same network different lanthanide centres.

3.6. Catalysis

All synthesised materials were tested as catalysts in the cyclodehydration reaction of xylose to furfural, using a biphasic reactor system comprising an aqueous phase (where the reaction takes place) and an organic extracting phase in a 3:7 v/v ratio, operating at 443 K. The use of one-pot process for the reaction of xylose or fructose and the solvent extraction of the product, furfural or 5-hydroxymethyl-2-furaldehyde, leads to higher yields of the desired furan product than if only water is used as solvent.41,42 Without catalyst, the aqueous phase reaction gave a maximum of 5% furfural yield at 6 h; throughout the discussion the results have not been corrected for this non-catalytic contribution. In general, the materials formed a very finely dispersed phase in water, and gave 40–77% xylose conversion and 25–42% furfural yield at 4 h (Table 5). These results compare quite favourably with other solid acid catalysts used in the same reaction under similar conditions. For example, layered titanate, niobate and titanoniobates used as water-tolerant catalysts at 433 K gave 36–40% conversion and 12–22% furfural yield at 4 h.43 Composites comprising 12-tungstophosphoric acid immobilized in an ordered mesoporous silica gave 38–80% conversion and 22–43% yield after 4 h at 433 K (albeit with poor water stability and extensive metal leaching).44

A comparative study was carried out by performing the reaction of xylose in a homogeneous phase in the presence of the reaction precursors YCl3 (used in a molar amount equivalent to the number of moles of Y in the charged 1), or H5cmp, used in the same wt.% as in 1. These two homogeneous reactions were much faster (99–100% conversion at 4 h) than that observed for any of the materials, but selectivity was lower than that for 1. Thus, selectivities of 50–56% for YCl3 (at 80–100% conversion) and 74% for H5cmp (at 100% conversion) were observed, compared with 77–84% (at 80–100% conversion) for 1 (Fig. 10). The reaction is also faster when H2SO4 is used as catalyst (in a molar amount equivalent to half the number of moles of Y3+ in 1): 99% conversion was achieved at 4 h with 65% furfural selectivity.

The differences in the reaction rates for the [RE(H2cmp)(H2O)] materials may be due to an interplay of several factors, including acid–base and textural properties, crystallinity and particle size distribution. SEM images of all materials (see Fig. S15 to S24 in the ESI‡) show that the microcrystalline powders are quite heterogeneous with respect to the size and morphology of the aggregates of thin (width ≤ 500 nm) plate-like crystallites, which can influence the catalytic performance (e.g., if defect sites are active, there may be differences in the number of accessible active sites). The BET specific surface areas45 of the solids are quite low (e.g., SBET for compounds 1, 2 and 4 are 26, 4 and 13 m2 g−1, respectively) and the interlayer distances do not allow the xylose molecules to access the internal surface (accordingly, internal diffusion limitations can be neglected). The dehydration of carbohydrates can take place over Brønsted and Lewis acid sites.43,46 For the materials under study, the P–OH groups encountered on the top of the (002) crystal facet may account for the Brønsted acidity. On the other hand, under aqueous conditions, the solid materials may adsorb hydronium or hydroxyl ions, changing the concentrations of H+ and OH− in the bulk solutions,47 and water adsorbed on a solid acid may originate Brønsted sites from Lewis acid sites.48 The pH of aqueous suspensions of the as-synthesised [RE(H2cmp)(H2O)] materials is reduced to 2.2–3.5.49 These values are, for example, comparable to those reported for very acidic activated carbons prepared via treatment with concentrated HNO3 (pH = 2.4–3).45,50

Ishida and collaborators17 found relationships between the catalytic activity of Ln(III) chlorides (Ln series: La3+–Lu3+) in the dehydration of hexoses in water and the ionic radius of the Ln(III) ions (in hexahydrates); the highest reaction rates were observed for the smaller-sized, heavier lanthanides Er and Yb (supposedly less hydrated and more capable of stronger interactions with the hexose molecules). For the materials investigated in this work, conversion at 4 h varies between 40% for [Pr(H2cmp)(H2O)] (3) and 77% for [Nd(H2cmp)(H2O)] (4) (Table 5).

Fig. 10 shows the selectivity versus conversion profile for 1 (pH of aqueous suspension = 2.2). Selectivity increases to 84% as conversion reaches 83%, and then decreases slightly. Similar profiles have been reported for xylose-to-furfural conversion in the presence of homogeneous heteropolyacids and heterogeneous porous acid catalysts.42–44,51 Increasing selectivity with conversion can be attributed to a complex reaction mechanism, involving a series of elementary steps with formation of three water molecules per molecule of monosaccharide.19 During the reaction the colourless solution turned brown due to the formation of by-products, resulting mainly from consecutive condensation reactions between furfural and intermediates of the xylose-to-furfural conversion, which may be enhanced for longer residence times and account for the observed drop in selectivity for conversion greater than ca. 83%.19,42

The stability under the applied reaction conditions was investigated in more detail for [Y(H2cmp)(H2O)] (1) by reusing the solid in three consecutive 4 h runs. The compound was separated from the liquid phase by centrifugation and regenerated either by thermal treatment at 280 °C (method T) or by repeated washing with solvents (method W). The structural integrity of the material after regeneration was investigated using SEM (Fig. 11) and powder X-ray diffraction (not shown). As expected, the regeneration of 1 by thermal treatment leads to a reduction in overall crystallinity, but the morphological features of the recovered materials and the structural features remain intact (Fig. 11). When method W was applied, conversion and selectivity decreased in three consecutive runs by factors of 0.08 and 0.2, respectively (Fig. 12), possibly due to surface passivation by strongly adsorbed organic matter. The SBET52 of the recovered solid remained practically the same (26 and 30 m2 g−1 before and after catalysis, respectively) and no major differences in the powder X-ray diffraction patterns were observed for the used and recovered solids. For method T, no decrease in conversion was observed and selectivity increased between the first and second runs (Fig. 12). ICP-AES analysis of the recovered materials indicated no loss of phosphorus or yttrium, which is an important issue in the context of “green chemistry”. The recovered solid possessed slightly larger SBET (52 m2 g−1) than the original material. A comparison of the powder X-ray diffraction patterns of the original and used solids (not shown) revealed no major differences at low 2θ angles, suggesting that the layered structure was preserved and that the interlayer distance remained unchanged. At high angles, some minor differences were registered, most likely indicating slight changes in the atomic arrangements due to the thermal treatment. FT-IR studies showed that the coordinated water molecules were removed during thermal treatment. It is, thus, feasible to assume that this release may induce some conformational changes in the layer, and we are currently investigating the nature of these modifications. Nevertheless, these changes seem to have a positive effect on catalytic performance in recycling runs.

3.7. Vibrational spectroscopy and thermogravimetric studies

Vibrational spectroscopy (ATR-FT-IR and FT-Raman) studies clearly support the structural features unveiled by X-ray diffraction data.53 Fig. S29 and S30 in the ESI‡ depict selected regions in the spectra for the entire [RE(H2cmp)(H2O)] series. The bands mentioned in the following paragraphs concern the powdered 2 compound but are valid for all members of the series.

The different coordination modes of the H2cmp3− residues and other structural details are reflected in the spectra through a number of diagnostic bands in several spectral regions: 3600–3100 cm−1 (O–H stretching and N–H vibrational modes), 1700–1300 cm−1 (carboxylate groups—the corresponding Δ[νasym(–CO2−) − νsym(–CO2−)] values agree well with those expected for the syn,syn-chelating coordination mode)54 and 1210–900 cm−1 ranges (phosphonate groups). Individual assignments for the powdered 2 compound are summarised in Table 6.

It is worth mentioning that a comparison of the spectra of 1 and 11 reveals that the bands of the phosphonate groups are clearly shifted to lower wavenumbers, thus showing their sensitivity to the local coordination environments (in this case the variation of the ionic radius of the rare-earth elements used along the series).

Thermal treatment of [RE(H2cmp)(H2O)] compounds between room temperature and 800 °C under nitrogen atmosphere resulted in similar thermograms, as represented in Fig. S28 (see ESI‡). All compounds are thermally stable up to ca. 200 °C. After this temperature, the observed weight losses are attributed to the liberation of one water molecule immediately followed by the decomposition of the organic H2cmp3− residues. This latter weight loss is common to all compounds usually starting at about 300 °C, which ensures that the temperature selected for the regeneration of the catalysts (280 °C) does not destroy the materials. Even though the final residues could not be identified using powder X-ray diffraction, it is reasonable to assume that they are composed of a mixture of phosphate and oxides of the corresponding rare earth.

The weight loss attributed to the release of the coordinated water molecule occurs at a different temperature for each material: along the lanthanide series the temperature of liberation of the water is progressively lower, but identical for 1 (with Y3+) and 11 (with Er3+). We attribute this trend to the average d(O⋯O) distance of the possible O–H⋯O hydrogen bonding interactions in which the water molecules are engaged. Indeed, with the reduction in ionic radii along the series, the average d(O⋯O) distance increases slightly and, consequently, the overall interactions fixing the water molecule inside the layer become weaker. Hence, for 1 and 11 (Y3+ and Er3+ have almost identical ionic radii)55 the coordinated water molecule is released at the same but lower temperature than for the other lanthanides.

Concluding remarks

A series of layered [RE(H2cmp)(H2O)] materials [where RE3+ = Y3+, La3+, Pr3+, Nd3+, Sm3+, Eu3+, Gd3+, Tb3+, Dy3+, Ho3+ and Er3+], and the mixed-lanthanide [(Gd0.95Eu0.05)(H2cmp)(H2O)] material, have been successfully isolated from hydrothermal synthesis as phase-pure micro-crystalline compounds, and found to be supramolecular polymorphs of the compound recently reported by Mao and co-workers. Due to the small crystallite size and, most likely, the general occurrence of connection mistakes during self-assembly of the organic ligands and rare-earth centres, materials could not be isolated as large single crystals. Hence, only by using a combined study involving synchrotron radiation (micro-crystal and high-signal-to-noise powder diffraction), high-resolution solid-state NMR investigations (on 1H, 13C and 31P nuclei) and data derived from the photophysical measurements we were able to unequivocally unveil all structural details of this family of materials. Compounds were proved to be pure, isostructural and composed of ∞2[RE(H2cmp)(H2O)] layers placed in the ab plane of the unit cell, assembled from a single rare-earth cation and one H2cmp3− organic residue.

Morphological studies showed a preferential growth of the crystallites perpendicular to the c-axis which, on the one hand, accounts for the observed extra intensities in the powder patterns collected in our in-house Bragg–Brentano instrument and, on the other, suggests the existence of a large concentration of highly accessible terminal P–OH groups on the surface of the crystals. Hence, the usefulness of these materials as solid acid catalysts for the cyclodehydration of xylose to furfural was investigated, using an aqueous-organic biphasic reactor system at 443 K, which gave typical 40–77% xylose conversions and 25–42% furfural yield at 4 h. In the case of [Y(H2cmp)(H2O)] the maximum selectivity was 84% at 83% conversion in the first run. No decrease in conversion of xylose and furfural yield was observed in three consecutive runs (when the catalyst was regenerated thermally), and no phosphorus or yttrium leaching was detected by ICP-AES. The work presented clearly shows that these materials are potential candidates to be applied as water/organic-stable catalysts.

Photoluminescence studies revealed that the coordinated water molecule present in the first coordination sphere of the rare-earth cation plays a decisive role in the observed properties, acting as a highly efficient non-radiative channel. Efficient Gd3+-to-Eu3+ energy transfer was observed in the mixed-lanthanide [(Gd0.95Eu0.05)(H2cmp)(H2O)] material which strongly suggests that this family could also be used to engineer optical materials by adding different lanthanides (and corresponding ratios) to the starting reaction mixtures.

At the moment we are interested in developing these systems by the inclusion of an auxiliary ligand to expand the dimensionality into the three dimensions. Moreover, we also aim to significantly reduce the crystallite size to improve the catalytic performance of the materials and develop functional devices based on the photoluminescence properties.
