Two one-dimension copper(II) coordination polymers based on imine-based bidentate Schiff-base ligand: Synthesis, crystal structure and luminescent properties

Author links open overlay panel
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.saa.2011.08.073Get rights and content

Abstract

Two new one-dimension copper(II) coordination polymers [CuL2(NCS)2]n (1) and [CuL2(NO3)2]n (2) (L = (C5H4N)C(CH3)N–N(CH3)C(C5H4N)) have been synthesized and characterized by IR, elemental analysis, TG technique and X-ray crystallography. Each Cu(II) atom has a distorted octahedral N6 (1) or N4O2 (2) environment with four pyridyl N atoms from four ligands and two N atoms from two NCS− anions for polymer 1 or two O atoms from two NO3− anions for polymer 2, respectively. A pair of bis-monodentate bridging ligands links two Cu(II) centers to form one dimension chain structure containing bimetallic 22-membered macrometallacyclic rings. 1D chain is held together with its neighboring ones via C–H⋯S hydrogen bonds for 1 and C–H⋯O hydrogen bonds, C–H⋯π interactions for 2 to form a 3D supramolecular structure, respectively. The luminescent properties of the polymers 1 and 2 were investigated in the solid state at room temperature.

1. Introduction

In recent years, much interest is focused on the syntheses of coordination polymers with unusual structure and special functional properties such as optics, redox activity and magnetism owing to their potential as special functional materials [1], [2], [3]. The design of the bridging ligands remains a key element for assembling coordination polymers with fantastic topologies, due to even a small change in ligand backbone such as coordination sites and length or flexibility of spacer may lead to a remarkable diversity of architectures [4], [5], [6]. Among them, pyridine derivatives are excellent neutral bridging ligands such as 4,4′-bipyridine and 3,3′-bipyridine to generate the interesting architectures with metal ions [7], [8], [9]. Moreover, the coordination feature of neutral pyridine derivative ligands in coordination polymers is obviously influenced by the nature of different counteranions due to the electroneutrality principle of ligands [10], [11], [12]. Herein the chosen ligand L, (C5H4N)(CH3)CN–NC(CH3)(C5H4N) was prepared by mixing hydrazine hydrate with 3-acetylpyridine. It has been used to assemble some interesting coordination polymers with different metal ions, such as 1D ring-like chain structures [CdL2(NO3)2]n [15], [CoL2(NO3)2]n [15], [CdL2(NCS)2]n [12], [CoL2(NCS)2]n [16], [ZnL2(N3)2]n [17] and [ZnL2(NCS)2]n [17], 1D zigzag chain structures [Mn(H2O)4L]n(ClO4)2n·2nL [14a], [AgL(NO3)]n [14a], [HgLI2]n [13b], 2D sheet structures [Mn(N3)2L]n [14b] and [Hg2L)(μ-SCN)4]n[Hg2L(μ-SCN)4]2n [13a]. As a part of our continuing investigations on coordination polymers and their photoluminescent properties [11], [12], [18], in this paper we report the synthesis and X-ray single crystal structure of two new one-dimensional copper(II) coordination polymers [CuL2(NCS)2]n (1) and [CuL2(NO3)2]n (2). The two polymers exhibit fluorescent emission in solid state at room temperature.

2. Experimental

2.1. Physical measurements

Elemental analyses (C, H and N) were carried out on a Perkin-Elmer 240C analytical instrument. IR spectra were recorded in KBr pellets with a Nicolet 170 S spectrophotometer in the 4000–400 cm−1 region. The luminescent spectra were performed on a XFT-IR Hitachi F-7000 fluorescence spectrophotometer. The thermogravimetric analysis was carried out under nitrogen condition on a Perkin-Elmer-7 thermal analyzer at a heating rate of 10 °C/min from 25 to 1000 °C.

2.2. Materials

All the chemicals were reagent grade quality obtained from commercial sources and used without further purification. Ligand L (C5H4N)(CH3)CN–NC(CH3)(C5H4N) was synthesized by a previously reported procedure [15].

2.3. Preparation of the polymer 1

A 6 mL CH3CN solution of L (0.048 g, 0.2 mmol) was layered onto a solution of Cu(NO3)2·3H2O (0.156 g, 0.6 mmol) and KSCN (0.116 g, 1.2 mmol) in DMF (6 mL). The solution was left for several days at room temperature and dark green crystals 1 suitable for X-ray structure determination were obtained in 62% yield. Anal. calc. For C30H28CuN10S2 (%): C, 54.95; H, 4.31; N, 21.37. Found (%): C, 54.87; H, 4.25; N, 21.44. IR (KBr pellet, cm−1): 3420(w), 3081(w), 2088(vs), 1653(w), 1614(s), 1485(m), 1475(m), 1384(s), 1350(s), 1302(s), 1196(m), 1093(w),1051(m), 1040(m), 821(m), 785(w), 707(m), 696(m), 650(w), 629(w).

2.4. Preparation of the polymer 2

A 6 mL CH3CN solution of L (0.048 g, 0.2 mmol) was layered onto a solution of Cu(NO3)2·3H2O (0.145 g, 0.6 mmol) in H2O (10 mL). The solution was left for several days at room temperature and light blue block crystals 2 suitable for X-ray structure determination were obtained in 70% yield. Anal. calc. For C28H28CuN10O6 (%): C, 50.67; H, 4.26; N, 21.12; Found (%): C, 51.10; H, 4.33; N, 21.05. 2.5. Crystallographic data collection and refinement

The suitable samples of size 0.18 mm × 0.15 mm × 0.12 mm for 1 and 0.20 mm × 0.18 mm × 0.16 mm for 2 were chosen for the crystallographic study and then mounted on a BRUKER SMART APEX CCD diffractometer with ω and φ scan mode in the range of 2.35° < θ < 25.00° for 1 and 2.36° < θ < 27.72° for 2. All diffraction measurements were performed at room temperature using graphite monochromatized Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). A total of 7933 (2784 independent, Rint = 0.0187) reflections for 1 and 17,580 (3488 independent, Rint = 0.0320) reflections for 2 were measured. The structures were solved by direct methods and refined by full-matrix least-squares on F2 using SHELXL 97 program [19]. All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically by full-matrix least-squares techniques and all hydrogen atoms were geometrically fixed to allow riding on the parent atoms to which they are attached. CCDC reference number: 834557 (1), 834558 (2). Space group, lattice parameters and other relevant information are listed in Table 1. The relevant bond lengths and bond angles are listed in Table 2.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. IR spectra

The relatively weak absorption bands at around 3081 cm−1 for 1 and 3084 cm−1 for 2 are due to the C–H modes involving the aromatic ring hydrogen atoms. The C–H modes involving the aliphatic hydrogen atoms of ligand in polymers 1 and 2 reveal around 2920 cm−1 for 1 and 2958 cm−1 for 2. The absorption bands with variable intensity in the frequency range 1396–1578 cm−1 correspond to ring vibrations of the pyridyl rings of the ligand. The absorption at 1614 cm−1 for 1 and 1617 cm−1 for 2 is attributable to the ν(CN) vibration of the ligand. In the IR spectra of polymer 1, a sharp and strong ν(SCN) absorption band at 2088 cm−1 shows the coordination mode of NCS− anion, which agrees well with the relevant compounds [12], [16]. For polymer 2, the strong absorption bands at 1352 cm−1 and 1305 cm−1 are characteristic of NO3− anion. Their identities were finally confirmed by X-ray crystallography (see below).

3.2. Description of the crystal structure of polymers 1 and 2

The polymers 1 and 2 crystallize in the monoclinic space group P21/n. The structural feature of the polymers 1 and 2 in the solid state is that both of them form 1D ring-like chain coordination polymer. An ORTEP diagram of each polymer with the atomic numbering scheme and the coordination environment of Cu(II) atom is depicted in Fig. 1, Fig. 2, respectively. For 1, the asymmetric unit consists of a Cu(II) atom, one ligand L and one NCS−. The Cu(II) atom locates on a crystallographic inversion center, and is surrounded by four Npyridyl atoms from four different ligands and two N atoms from two NCS− to attain a distorted octahedral coordination geometry. The axial Cu(1)–N(1) bond distance is 2.380(4) Å and the average equatorial Cu(1)–N bond distance is 2.049 Å. For 2, the asymmetric unit consists of a Cu(II) atom, one ligand L one NO3−. The Cu(II) atom also locates on a crystallographic inversion center with four Npyridyl atoms from four ligands and two O atoms from two NO3− coordinate to it, and exhibits a distorted octahedral coordination geometry. The axial Cu(2)–O bond distance is 2.474(2) Å and the average equatorial Cu(2)–Npyridyl bond distance is 2.044 Å. These data reveal that the axial distances are significantly larger than the equatorial ones for two types of Cu(II) coordination groups. Therefore, each Cu(II) group for 1 and 2 shows the stronger Jahn–Teller effect. The bond angles of the adjacent donors around Cu(II) center are in the range from 87.20(13)° to 92.80(13)° for 1 and from 87.39(9)° to 92.61(9)° for 2, respectively. The NCS− group is almost linear with the N(1)–C(1)–S(1) bond angle being 177.7(4)° for 1.

While the metal centers are coordinated by two terminal pyridyl nitrogen atoms leading to a twist conformation of ligand with the torsion angle of 106.1° for 1 and 105.8° for 2 around C(6)N(2)–N(3)C(8) bonds, respectively. The angle between two pyridyl rings from each twist ligand is 80.6° for 1 and 84.3° for 2, respectively. These values are similar to those found in related complexes [12], [15], [16]. As shown in Fig. 3, in the solid state adjacent Cu atoms of polymers 1 and 2 are linked by a pair of ligands to form similar infinite polymeric chain containing bimetallic 22-membered macrometallacyclic rings with the Cu⋯Cu separation of 12.5 Å for 1 and 12.2 Å for 2, respectively. In addition, four types of C–H⋯N hydrogen bonds for 1 and two types of C–H⋯N hydrogen bonds and four types of C–H⋯O hydrogen bonds for 2 are found in the structure and play an important role in stabilizing the 1D structure (Table 3).

In polymer 1, each 1D chain is surrounded by four adjacent 1D chains with C–H⋯S hydrogen bonds to form the 3D network exhibiting a primitive cubic topology, in which each copper atom acts as a six-connected node. This kind of arrangement is similar to the relevant complexes [CdL2(NCS)2]n [12], [CoL2(NCS)2]n [16] and [ZnL2(NCS)2]n [17]. Furthermore, a single 3D framework shows combination with another identical one, giving a twofold interpenetrated structure of 1 (Fig. 4). The 1D chain of the polymer 2 was further interacted each other feathering 2D supramolecular network through two types of C–H⋯π interactions (Fig. S1). The C–H⋯π interactions between the C(7) atom and two pyridine rings [(C1–C4N1) and (C10–C14N4)] from adjacent chains are characterized by the C⋯Py separation and the C–H⋯Py angle of 3.75 Å, 104.8° and 3.90 Å, 133.8°, respectively, where Py is the center of the pyridine ring. Furthermore, the 2D layer was further interacted each other feathering a 3D supramolecular network through C–H⋯O hydrogen bonds interactions between the C(3) of ligands and O(3) of NO3− anions, similar as observed for [CdL2(NO3)2]n and [CoL2(NO3)2]n [15].

3.3. Thermal analysis

The thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) of 1 and 2 was carried out under nitrogen condition at a heating rate of 10 °C/min from 25 to 1000 °C. The TG curve of 1 indicates that the polymer is stable up to 238 °C at which temperature begins to decompose. The first step gives a loss of 68.23% in the range of 238–325 °C, corresponding to the release of two L (calculated 70.47%). Secondly, the two thiocyanate anions are lost in a continuous fashion after 325 °C. The decomposition of the framework 2 occurs at 226 °C. The weight loss from 226 to 463 °C corresponds to the release of two ligands and two nitrate anions. The weight loss of 86.42% is close to that calculated (88.31%) for the formula. The framework completely decomposes to generate the residue CuO (calculated, 11.98%; found, 13.21%).

3.4. Luminescence properties

It is interesting to note that metal ion play a key role in fluorescent emission of complexes. The luminescent properties of the polymers 1 and 2 were investigated in the solid state at room temperature, respectively. As shown in Fig. 5, upon excitation at 220 nm, polymers 1 and 2 have similar solid-state emission spectra, which show luminescent emission maximums at 337 nm (1) and 316 nm (2) except that the emission peak of 1 is weaker. The emission bands of 1 and 2 are comparable to that of the corresponding ligand [12], which suggests that the emissions can be ascribed to the intraligand π*–π transition, different to that of the reported d10 complex [CdL2(NCS)2]n [12].

4. Conclusion

In summary, we presented two new one-dimension copper(II) coordination polymers [CuL2(NCS)2]n (1) and [CuL2(NO3)2]n (2) (L = (C5H4N)(CH3)CN–NC(CH3)(C5H4N)). The structure of the two polymers has been established by single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis and also characterized by IR, elemental analysis, TG technique and luminescent properties. For polymers 1 and 2, adjacent Cu(II) atoms interconnect with a pair of bridging ligands to form one dimension chain structure containing bimetallic 22-membered macrometallacyclic rings. 1 forms a twofold interpenetrated three-dimensional supramolecular structure in the solid state through C–H⋯S hydrogen bonds. And C–H⋯π interactions and C–H⋯O hydrogen bonds are observed in three-dimensional supramolecular structure 2. The fluorescence spectra of polymers 1 and 2 showed similar solid-state emission peak at 337 nm (1) and 316 nm (2), respectively.
