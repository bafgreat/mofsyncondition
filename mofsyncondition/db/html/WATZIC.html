The preparation of chiral porous solids is of interest for chiral transformation.1 However, the design of crystalline materials displaying enantiomerically pure cavities or channels still remains a challenge. A possible strategy for the generation of such materials may be based on crystals composed of helical strands provided that the dimension of the helical space can be controlled and its handedness imposed. Several examples of helical metal–organic frameworks have been reported.2 Although the majority of infinite helical architectures described are composed of 1-D networks,3 examples of 2-D4 and 3-D helical networks have been also published.5

Using the molecular tectonics approach,6 coordination networks7 may be obtained by combining metallatectons, i.e. metal centres or metal complexes offering at least two free coordination sites oriented in a divergent manner, with organic coordinating tectons possessing at least two divergently oriented ligating sites. For the design of helical coordination networks, one may combine ZnSiF6, forming in the presence of organic tectons an infinite pillar by bridging consecutive zinc cations by SiF62−, anions through Zn–F bonds.8,9

Here, we report on the formation of enantiomerically pure 2-D helical coordination networks based on combinations of rigid enantiomerically pure bismonodentate tectons 1–4 (Scheme 1) with ZnSiF6.

The design of the enantiomerically pure chiral tectons 1–4 is based on rigid enantiomerically pure backbones 6 and 7 for 1 and 2 and 8 and 9 for 3 and 4 and two terminal pyridyl units as coordinating sites. The junction between the backbone and the interaction sites is ensured by an ester group (Scheme 1). The starting materials for synthesis of tectons 1–4 were enantiomerically pure diols6–9 (Scheme 1). Whereas compounds 6 and 7 are commercially available, diols8 and 9 were prepared using a reported procedure.10 All four tectons were obtained in 40–60% yield upon condensation at 25 °C of the diol with the hydrochloride salt of 5 in dry THF in the presence of Et3N for 1 and 2 or pyridine for 3 and 4 (see ESI†).

Crystallisation of tectons 1–4 in the presence of ZnSiF6 was attempted using two different solvent systems ((EtOH–CHCl3) or (EtOH–CH2ClCH2Cl, through a DMSO layer), see ESI†). Upon slow diffusion of an EtOH solution of ZnSiF6 into a CH2ClCH2Cl solution of tectons 1–4, a mixture (ca. 50/50 ratio) of X·ZnSiF6 (X = 1–4) and ZnSiF6 crystals was obtained. Owing to similar morphologies and colours between the two types of crystals containing either X·ZnSiF6 or ZnSiF6, the identification could only be made by X-ray diffraction. In order to circumvent this difficulty, the crystallisation was performed by slow diffusion of an EtOH solution of ZnSiF6 into a CHCl3 solution of tectons 1–3. The procedure, in addition to the desired crystals, produced an amorphous powder and the crystalline material was manually separated from the powder. For tecton 4, upon slow diffusion of an EtOH solution of ZnSiF6 into a CH2ClCH2Cl solution of tecton 4 through a DMSO layer, again both crystals and a powder were obtained. In the case of X·ZnSiF6 (X = 1 or 2), depending on the solvent mixture used, two different crystals are obtained, i.e. Orthorhombic in (EtOH–CHCl3) and Monoclinic in (EtOH–CH2ClCH2Cl). Independent of the solvent system used, for both 1·ZnSiF6 and 2·ZnSiF6, solvent molecules were found to be disordered.

Owing to the curvature and thus the distance between the two terminal monodentate coordinating sites of the enantiomerically pure tectons 1–4, their combination with the infinite pillar formed upon bridging of consecutive SiF62− anions by Zn(ii) cations adopting an Oh coordination geometry, one may envisage either 1D double strand helices (Fig. 1a–c) or a 2D architecture composed of single strand helices (Fig. 1d).

For the first category, depending on the orientation of tectons and the connectivity pattern, three possibilities may be envisaged (Fig. 1a–c). Indeed, considering the square base of the octahedron around Zn2+cations, for the four tectons connecting consecutive metal cations the two orientational possibilities are three up/one down (Fig. 1a) or two up/two down (Fig. 1b and c). For the latter, again two possibilities based on the relative cis (Fig. 1b) or trans (Fig. 1c) dispositions of tectons may be considered.

The structural investigation (see ESI†) by X-ray diffraction on crystals of X·ZnSiF6 (X = 1–4) obtained using the (EtOH–CH2ClCH2Cl) solvent system revealed the formation of analogous tubular 2D helical networks (Fig. 2). The connectivity pattern observed is of the type schematically presented in Fig. 1d. In all four cases, the crystal is composed of the organic tecton, Zn2+cations, SiF62− anions and solvent molecules (EtOH and CH2ClCH2Cl).

The formation of the 2D networks results from the interconnection of pillars resulting from the bridging of SiF62− anions by Zn(ii) through Zn–F bonds (dZn–F in the range of 2.04–2.11 Å) by organic tectons through Zn–N bonds (dZn–N in the range of ca. 2.13–2.19 Å). Within the linear pillar (FZnF angle of 180°), the coordination geometry around Si atoms is a distorted Oh with two sets of Si–F bonds (dSi–F in the range of 1.65–1.69 Å and 1.70–1.73 Å for F atoms located at the square base and two occupying the apical positions respectively).

The zinc cation, playing the role of a 6 connecting node, adopts also a slightly distorted Oh geometry with its coordination sphere composed of two F and four N atoms. The two F atoms are located at the apical positions and the remaining four sites are occupied by four pyridyl units belonging to four different tectons. Within the pillar, the distance between consecutive Zn2+cations varies between ca. 7.63 and 7.67 Å.

The chiral 2D architectures are enantiomerically pure and their handedness (P or M) is imposed by the stereochemistry of tectons 1–4 (R,R for 1 and 3 and S,S for 2 and 4). Thus, as expected, 1·ZnSiF6 (Fig. 2a, P helicity) and 2·ZnSiF6 (Fig. 2b, M helicity) as well as 3·ZnSiF6 (Fig. 2c, P helicity) and 4·ZnSiF6 (Fig. 2d, M helicity) are, respectively, isostructural and mirror images.

Within the 2D architecture, the consecutive pillars are slipped by ca. 3.80 Å with the shortest Zn–Zn distance in the 11.05–12.24 Å range (Fig. 2). The 2D networks in all four cases are tubular in nature and display enantiomerically pure helical channels along the pillar axis (Fig. 3). The dimension of channels (11.05 Å × 9.37 Å for X·ZnSiF6 (X = 1–2) and 12.24 Å × 8.33 Å for X·ZnSiF6 (X = 3–4)) is controlled by the organic tecton which imposes the inter-pillar distance.

The packing of consecutive 2D networks follows the concave–convex mosaic (Fig. 3). Owing to the size and shape of the organic tectons, two sets of shortest Zn–Zn distances between metal centres belonging to consecutive sheets are observed (14.91 Å for X·ZnSiF6 (X = 1 or 2) and 16.59 Å for X·ZnSiF6 (X = 3 or 4)). Although in the case of X·ZnSiF6 (X = 1 or 2), the solvent molecules could not be localised because of disorder, for both 3·ZnSiF6 and 4·ZnSiF6, EtOH molecules, interacting with the inorganic pillar through OH⋯F (dO–F = 2.716 Å) H bonds, are included within the channels, whereas CH2ClCH2Cl molecules are located in the empty space resulting from the packing of the 2D networks without any specific interactions with the framework.

The potential solvent accessible volumes, calculated using the Platon software,11 are 536.5 Å3, 548.1 Å3, 941.6 Å3 and 954.4 Å3, corresponding to 23.7%, 24.1%, 32.7% and 33.0% of the total unit cell volume for 1·ZnSiF6 (2259.4 Å3), 2·ZnSiF6 (2271.9 Å3), 3·ZnSiF6 (2878.9 Å3) and 4·ZnSiF6 (2892.4 Å3) respectively.

The homogeneity of the crystalline materials, obtained by manual separation of crystals and the powder in the mixture, was investigated by PXRD (Fig. 4). The match between the calculated and measured patterns indicated that all four crystalline materials were pure phases.

The thermal stability of all four crystalline materials was investigated by TGA measurements under a nitrogen atmosphere which revealed the loss of solvent molecules between 80 and 120 °C prior to decomposition of the sample, which appeared at ca. 200 °C for 1·ZnSiF6 and 2·ZnSiF6 and at 180 °C for 3·ZnSiF6 and 4·ZnSiF6.

In conclusion, chiral porous crystals with enantiomerically pure channels may be generated upon combining enantiomerically pure organic tectons with ZnSiF6. The above mentioned combination leads to the formation of tubular 2D helical networks which upon parallel packing generate porous crystals offering enantiomerically pure channels. The handedness of the helical channels is imposed by the stereochemistry of the organic tectons. Indeed, whereas the (R,R) tectons 1 and 3 lead to the formation of helical channels with P handedness, the (S,S) tectons 2 and 4 lead to helical arrangements with the opposite M orientation. Interestingly, the strategy used further allows control of the size of the helical channels by the length and curvature of the organic tecton and their orientation by the direction of the inorganic (ZnSiF6)n pillar. Currently, we explore the formation of other chiral porous crystals using other enantiomerically pure organic tectons with different lengths.
