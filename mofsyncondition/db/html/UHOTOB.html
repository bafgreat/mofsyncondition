Introduction

The chloranilic acid, 2,5-dichloro-3,6-dihydroxy-1,4-benzoquinone, belongs to a broad class of 3,6-dihydroxy-benzoquinones of a general formula H2C6X2O4 where X can be H or any other electron withdrawing or donating a substituent that modifies the electronic structure and properties of the system. The system is prone to realising all possible types of non-covalent interactions including coordination bonds, hydrogen bonds, π⋯π, halogen⋯halogen, and dipolar interactions. In the synthesis of metal complexes these molecules can produce various coordination modes generating finite and extended molecular assemblies (such as chains, ladders, grids, honeycomb nets) including different micropores (cavities, channels, interlayer space) in their structures.1 These topological characteristics can be used in the preparation of porous materials that are of interest for gas storage.2–4 The new concept of porous coordination polymers4 leads to construction of functionalised dynamic frameworks where pores can be compressed or expanded. One possibility to influence the dynamic properties of such networks can be by reversible rotation of aromatic rings controlled by their π interactions.4,5 The most sophisticated preparation of functional materials, such as porous coordination polymers, also involves the basic elements of crystal engineering.6

In addition to versatile topological assemblies generated by coordination bonds and other types of non-covalent interactions, the electronic properties of these molecules can direct their use for molecular magnets.7 The molecules H2C6X2O4 undergo two one-electron oxidation and two one-electron reduction processes generating species with charges from −4 to −1 where the species with −1 and −3 are radicals. This electronic property can be used in the synthesis of binuclear complexes of transition metals bridged by this type of ligand; such complexes can include various valence states of the metal and/or ligand sites resulting in different spin states.7

The molecules H2C6X2O4 can also be used for binary molecular components with organic bases, such as phenazine-chloranilic acid and phenazine-bromanilic acid to produce organic ferroelectrics where the π-conjugated molecules and dynamic proton (charge transfer complexes) play a crucial role.8,9

1,4-Benzoquinones are excellent systems to study hydrogen bonding10 due to their electronic properties and structural characteristics involving various combinations of donor and acceptor functions that can be accompanied by a proton transfer and combined by an electron transfer. Proton transfer coupled with a π–conjugated system significantly influences the electronic structure of the molecule and can be responsible for piezochromism.11

π-Interactions between aromatic rings have been intensively studied.12–15 The classical π⋯π interactions between aromatic rings will be favoured in the orientation defined as edge-to-face (T-shaped) and parallel rings with offset where the electrostatic interactions are attractive.12,14 For benzene rings, parallel stacks with no offset are energetically unfavourable due to strong electrostatic repulsions of delocalised π-electrons. The majority of intermolecular π⋯π contacts between aromatic six-membered rings found in the Cambridge Structural Database16 reveal centroid⋯centroid distances between 3.6 and 4.1 Å with an offset of about 1.8 Å. However, π⋯π interactions involving nonaromatic rings, such as quinoid rings, have been given little attention if any at all. The only example found in the CSD is the structure of barium chloranilate trihydrate17 with a short centroid⋯centroid distance of 3.262 Å. Recently, we prepared o-chloranilic acid trihydrate and solved its structure18 that reveals pronounced π-stacking with a centroid⋯centroid distance of 3.335 Å (no offset).

To study the hydrogen bonding and π-interactions of quinoid rings [including mono- (HCA−) and dianions (CA2−), Scheme 1] we prepared a series of alkali (hydrogen) chloranilato aqua-complexes. The influence of the hydrogen bonds and alkali ions on the polarization of stacked quinoid rings is discussed.

Results and discussion

The structures studied in the present work are: [LiHCAEtOH], [Na5HCA(CA)2(H2O)10], [KHCA(H2O)2], NH4HCA·2H2O, [RbHCA(H2O)2] and [CsHCA(H2O)2]. The alkali diaqua complexes of hydrogen chloranilic acid of [KHCA(H2O)2], [RbHCA(H2O)2] and [CsHCA(H2O)2], and the salt of NH4HCA·2H2O were prepared, but the chemically homologous aqua complexes of lithium and sodium were not obtained. The crystals of hydrogen chloranilates hydrates with small cations, Li+ and Na+ are not stable, whereas their analogues [LiHCAEtOH] and [Na5HCA(CA)2(H2O)10] were obtained instead. The crystal structure analysis of these compounds is focused on π-stacking of nonaromatic quinoid rings and modifications of the electronic properties of hydrogen chloranilate anion.

Hydrogen chloranilate anion and its symmetry

In the crystal structures examined, hydrogen chloranilate anion reveals C1 symmetry (located in a general position, Fig. 1). In the centrosymmetric crystal structures of [Na5HCA(CA)2(H2O)10], [KHCA(H2O)2], [RbHCA(H2O)2], and [CsHCA(H2O)2] the anions are related by inversion centres. In the noncentrosymmetric structures of [LiHCAEtOH] and NH4HCA·2H2O the anion centroids would coincide with the crystallographic inversion centres in their centrosymmetric analogues (Pnma and C2/c, respectively). The crystal structure of NH4HCA·2H2O was already solved in the space groupC2/c but it was misinterpreted as ammonium hydronium chloranilate with a rather elongated Zundel ion H5O2+ where unrealistic geometric parameters were observed.19 Our attempts to refine the structure in the space groupC2/c also yielded an electron density maximum of 0.5 e Å−3 located at an inversion centre halfway between two crystal water molecules. Obviously, this maximum is an artifact of erroneous space group symmetry. Refinement in the space groupCc yielded a realistic hydrogen bonding geometry (and lower R value); the displacement parameters values are also lower than in the centrosymmetric space group. Even more, in the crystal of NH4HCA·2H2O twinning simulating an inversion symmetry (the space groupC2/c) was observed. The proton disorder which would simulate Ci symmetry of a hydrogen chloranilate anion was not detected.

The values of the bond lengths in the hydrogen chloranilate anions in the structures studied (Table 1a) reveal a conjugated system over O3–C4–C5–C6–O4 (Fig. 1, the atom numbering) involving a delocalised electron. In the systems having deprotonated O4− with a localized electron the values of carbon–oxygen bonds are between 1.277(3) and 1.290(1) Å (e.g., 2-carboxypyridinium hydrogen chloranilate20 and phenazinium hydrogen chloranilate, respectively21) and the carbonyl bond lengths C1–O1 are in agreement with the hybridization [1.217(3) and 1.227(4)20; 1.226(1) and 1.231(2)21 Å]. In the system studied here C6–O4 is significantly shorter than in systems having O4− with a localized electron and it is comparable to C4–O3 bond lengths [from 1.237(4) to 1.255(1) and from 1.242(1) to 1.26(1) Å, respectively]. The geometric parameters of hydrogen chloranilate observed in this series agree with those for piperidinium hydrogen chloranilate22 and cytosinium hydrogen chloranilate monohydrate.23 The geometries of both chloranilate anions (Tables 1a and 1b) reproduce very well by second-order Møller–Plesset (MP2) calculations reported by Ishida and Kashino.10

Crystal packing and π-interactions

The crystal structures of NH4HCA·2H2O and [KHCA(H2O)2] display stacked hydrogen chloranilate anions with short π⋯π contacts [with the distances Cg⋯Cg of 3.229(2) and 3.334(6) Å, respectively, Table 2] parallel to the [001] direction. The parallel hydrogen chloranilate rings are stacked in a face-to-face fashion (no offset) (Table 2). The π⋯π anion stacks are generated by the cglide plane (Fig. 2) which connects two inequivalent parts into a hydrogen chloranilate anion of no molecular symmetry. The lengths of the c axes (Tables 2 and 4) are about two times the π⋯π contacts. The separation distance between the centroids of two stacked hydrogen chloranilates in [KHCA(H2O)2] of 3.229 (2) Å (Table 2) is the shortest value ever observed. The orientation of stacked quinoid rings is in favour of minimisation of the electrostatic repulsion of π-electrons and maximisation of the attraction of mostly antiparallel local dipoles (Fig. 3). The overlap of the negatively charged delocalised system O3–C4–C5–C6–O4 is very small, and electron-rich double bonds are sandwiched between electron-poor single bonds, minimising the electrostatic repulsion. The stacked arrays of hydrogen chloranilates in NH4HCA·2H2O and [KHCA(H2O)2] are similar (Fig. 4a and 4b) but dissimilar hydrogen bonding patterns generate an overall crystal packing of different symmetries (Fig. 5a and 5b; Fig. 6a and 6b) (space groupsCc and P21/c, respectively). In NH4HCA·2H2O the ammonium cation donates four protons to the hydrogen chloranilate anions forming a 2D-network (Fig. 5a and 6a; Table 3).

In the crystal structures of [RbHCA(H2O)2] and [CsHCA(H2O)2] the anions have C1 symmetry and the stacks are generated by crystallographic inversion centres located between the adjacent anions (Fig. 7). They display stacks of hydrogen chloranilate anions parallel to [010] but their separation distances are larger [ranging from 3.587(3) to 3.697(2) Å] than in ammonium and potassium homologues and the offset of the neighbouring quinoid rings is encountered: 1.625; 1.684 [RbHCA(H2O)2] and 1.626; 1.661 Å [CsHCA(H2O)2] (Table 2). These crystal structures display 3D-hydrogen-bonded networks (Fig. 8; Table 3).

In the structure of [Na5HCA(CA)2(H2O)10] there are stacks involving different anions in the sequence: (a) hydrogen chloranilates (related by inversion symmetry); (b) hydrogen chloranilates and chloranilates; (c) chloranilate dianions (related by inversion symmetry); and (d) between crystallographically independent chloranilate dianions (Table 2). Stacks of hydrogen chloranilates are composed of parallel rings but shifted (Fig. 9); the same feature characterises chloranilate stacks composed by the inversion symmetry related units. However, the mixed stacks of hydrogen chloranilate and chloranilate and also the stacks between the chloranilates of dianions crystallographically different are inclined for small angles (4.17 and 2.66°, respectively) and also shifted (Table 2). The layers of stacked anions parallel to the (11) plane are separated by hydrogen-bonded layers composed of water molecules and sodium cations (Fig. 9, Table 3). In the structure of [Na5HCA(CA)2(H2O)10] mono- and dianions of chloranilic acid are present, both coordinating sodium cations (the geometries of mono- and dianions of chloranilic acid are listed in Tables 1a and 1b, respectively). In the structure of [Na2(CA)(H2O)3] the chloranilate dianions are also stacked in the same fashion,24 however, the size of the unit cell is only 1/3 of [Na5HCA(CA)2(H2O)10] (due to stoichiometry).

Cation interactions and modifications of anion polarity

In [KHCA(H2O)2], the O and Cl atoms of hydrogen chloranilate anions and crystal water molecules coordinate the potassium cation in the shape of a tri-capped trigonal prism with the K+⋯O distance ranging from 2.693(2) to 2.919(2) Å and K+⋯Cl from 3.335(1) to 3.583(1) Å (details are provided as ESI†); among nine coordinated neighbours four are hydrogen chloranilates and the shortest K+⋯O distance involves O4 [2.693(2) Å is ∼0.2 Å shorter than the sum of the ionic radii]. The short K+⋯O4 distance suggests that this very interaction affects the electron distribution over the hydrogen chloranilate anion. The stacked columns of hydrogen chloranilates are linked into cation–anion layers parallel to the (011) plane (Fig. 5b) and two water molecules, coordinating K+, complete this hydrogen bonding network (Fig. 6b, Table 3).

The crystal structures of [RbHCA(H2O)2] and [CsHCA(H2O)2] are isomorphous taking into account non-hydrogen atoms (Table 4, Fig. 7 and 8) with the alkali ion coordinated by two water molecules, five oxygen atoms and a chlorine atom in the shape of distorted mono-capped pentagonal bipyramid (details are provided as ESI†). The shortest contact between the alkali metal ion and oxygen is observed towards O4 of hydrogen chloranilate. The difference in the unit cell volumes (Table 4) can be assigned to the size difference of Rb+ and Cs+ (1.61 and 1.74 Å, respectively). However, their isomorphous structures are entirely different to the structures of NH4HCA·2H2O and [KHCA(H2O)2].

Lithium hydrogen chloranilate, crystallized from anhydrous ethanol, forms a stable complex, [LiHCAEtOH]. Its crystal structure is composed of hydrogen-bonded hydrogen chloranilate anions and alcohol molecules generating chains parallel to the [001] direction in which Li+ is coordinated by five oxygen atoms from the hydrogen chloranilate anions and alcohol oxygen in a shape of a tetragonal pyramid generating a 2D hydrogen-bonded network in the (011) plane (Fig. 10, Table 3). There is no anion stacking via π⋯π interactions.

In this series the electron density of the chloranilate monoanion is affected by highly polar alkali cations; the hydrogen chloranilate oxygen and chlorine atoms coordinate the alkali cations. In the coordinations of Na+, K+, Rb+ and Cs+ the distances M+⋯O4 are significantly shorter than the sum of the ionic radii.

Conclusions

The structures of [RbHCA(H2O)2] and [CsHCA(H2O)2] are isomorphous. The structures of NH4HCA·2H2O and [KHCA(H2O)2] reveal two distinctive types of crystal packing due to a donor function of the ammonium cation, affecting the hydrogen bond network (Fig. 6 and Table 3). The highly pronounced π⋯π interactions of stacked hydrogen chloranilate anions with no shift of quinoid rings are observed in NH4HCA·2H2O and [KHCA(H2O)2]. However, in the structures of [RbHCA(H2O)2] and [CsHCA(H2O)2] with large cations, the quinoid rings are parallel but shifted (Table 2). The large cations such as Rb+ and Cs+ do not allow close π⋯π contacts between the hydrogen chloranilate anions whereas NH4+ generates more hydrogen bonds making the structure less densely packed where stacked hydrogen chloranilates have not been disturbed. In the structure of [KHCA(H2O)2] the medium sized K+ fits well into the channels between the closely stacked hydrogen chloranilates (Table 2, Fig. 4b). The crystal structure of the lithium analogue reveals no stacking. The crystal structures of Na+, K+, Rb+, Cs+, and NH4+ involve a 3D-hydrogen bond network with π-stacked anions. The observed differences in the crystal packing can be assigned to optimization of the cation size and the hydrogen bond network in order to achieve stable crystal lattices.

The crystal packing of the structures presented is primarily determined by pronounced π⋯π interactions and steric conditions; the molecules tend to organize via π⋯π interactions generating stacked arrays of hydrogen chloranilates which are influenced by attractive polar interactions of alkali cations and hydrogen bonds. The modified electron density of the quinoid rings by deprotonation, electron transfer, attached chlorine and oxygen atoms, and the presence of alkali cations significantly contributes to the dipolar interactions of stacked chloranilate anions. Thus, the stacked hydrogen chloranilates observed in this series strikingly differ from the stacks of unsubstituted aromatics but can be compared to stacked heterocyclic or substituted aromatic systems.

Experimental

Synthesis in the solution and crystallization

Chloranilic acid dihydrate was freshly prepared according to the procedure of Gräbe (1891).25 The alkali carbonates and aqueous ammonia of analytical grade (Kemika and Sigma-Aldrich) were used without further purification. Stoichiometric quantities of chloranilic acid and the appropriate base were dissolved in water and left in air at 291 K to evaporate. Lithium hydrogen chloranilate was prepared in dry ethanol and left to evaporate in dry atmosphere. The crystals suitable for data collections were selected under a stereomicroscope. The sample of [LiHCAEtOH] was homogenous, while the other samples were inhomogeneous; the crystals of hydrogen chloranilate, chloranilate, and chloranilic acid dihydrate were identified in crystallization beakers. In the preparation mixtures of each compound (K, Rb, Cs and NH4) the hydrogen chloranilate compound was the dominant species, whereas in a sample aimed to be a Na-homologue, different compounds were present. All attempts to prepare a hydrate of sodium hydrogen chloranilate were not successful. Instead, a mixture of chloranilic acid dihydrate26 and [Na5HCA(CA)2(H2O)10] was obtained. Chloranilic acid dihydrate was identified by its bright red colour; other species were identified by X-ray single-crystal diffraction (the unit cells were determined). In the series of alkali hydrogen chloranilates proton dissociation occurred in the solution during preparation.

Crystal structure determination

Data collections were performed on an Enraf Nonius CAD-4 diffractometer, using a graphite monochromated Cu Kα (1.54179 Å) radiation at room temperature [293(2) K] for [LiHCAEtOH], [KHCA(H2O)2], [RbHCA(H2O)2] and NH4HCA·2H2O. The WinGX standard procedure was applied for data reduction.27 Three standard reflections were measured every 120 min as intensity control. The absorption correction based on 8 ψ-scan reflections28 was performed for NH4HCA·2H2O. No absorption correction was applied for the structures of [LiHCAEtOH], [KHCA(H2O)2] and [RbHCA(H2O)2]. [CsHCA(H2O)2] and [Na5HCA(CA)2(H2O)10] were measured on a Nonius Kappa CCD diffractometer using a graphite monochromated Mo Kα (0.71073 Å) radiation at 150(2) K. Data reduction was performed using the programs DENZO and SCALEPACK.29 The absorption correction based on multi-scans was performed using the programs DENZO and SCALEPACK.29 The structures were solved by SHELXS9730 and refined with SHELXL97.30 The models were refined using the full matrix least squares refinement. The hydrogen atoms were located from difference Fourier maps and refined as mixed free and riding entities. The following restraints were used: for the hydroxyl moiety d(O–H) = 0.85(2) Å; for the water molecule d(O–H) = 0.95(2) Å, d(H–H) = 1.50(4) Å; for the ammonium iond(N–H) = 0.93(2) Å, d(H–H) = 1.50(4) Å. The structure of NH4HCA·2H2O was treated as an inversion twinning during refinement. The structures of Rb and Cs homologues are isomorphous at the level of non-hydrogen atoms. In the structure of the Cs-homologue it was not possible to locate unambiguously the hydrogen atoms of water molecules. The atomic scattering factors were those included in SHELXL97.30 Molecular geometry calculations were performed by PLATON,31 and the molecular graphics were prepared using ORTEP-3,32 and CCDC-Mercury.33 Crystallographic and refinement data for the structures reported in this paper are shown in Table 4.
