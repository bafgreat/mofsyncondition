Introduction

Massive emission of anthropogenic carbon dioxide (CO2) into the atmosphere has become a great environmental concern because it is the primary source of greenhouse gas causing climate change and other environmental problems.1–3 Separation of CO2 from methane (CH4) is also very crucial for upgradation and the treatment of biogas to improve the purity.4 In addition, pre-combustion CO2 capture is essential to separate it from hydrogen (H2) when it is produced by the reaction of a primary fuel with oxygen or air.5 Selective trapping of CO2 using carbon capture and sequestration (CCS) technologies plays a vital role in these issues.6,7 Presently, along with traditional CCS methods, metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), which are also known as porous coordination polymers (PCPs), have been rapidly emerging as a new type of functional material for selective CO2 adsorption due to the presence of highly ordered adjustable pore shape and size, large surface areas with controllable pore surface properties, and high thermal/solution stability.8–18 Selective CO2 adsorption by MOFs can be achieved by (a) control of the pore aperture,19–21 (b) the presence of open metal sites within the pore,22–27 (c) pore wall decoration by polar functional groups,28–31 or (d) by the immobilization of alkali metal ions within the pore.32–34 Nonetheless, it is difficult to achieve selectivity of CO2 over N2 and CH4 only by the fine control of the pore aperture as their kinetic diameters are very close to each other (CO2 = 3.30 Å, N2 = 3.65 Å, CH4 = 3.76 Å). In most cases the pore size and environment together help in achieving selectivity.35–42 Creation of coordinately unsaturated metal centers or open metal sites in the cavity of a MOF is one of the promising techniques for preparing selective CO2-adsorbing MOFs. However, it is indeed challenging to create open metal sites in mixed-ligand-based MOFs. In most cases organic ligands and coordinated solvent molecules make the metal centers coordinately saturated. Sometimes unsaturated metal centers are created by the removal of metal-coordinated volatile solvent molecules in a controlled manner without disturbing the framework structure. It can be possible by applying a vacuum, on controlled heating of the MOF or by applying both. Studies on the synthesis and characterization of MOFs were prolific in recent years, however, porous MOFs of mixed ligand systems for selective CO2 adsorption are still scanty in the literature.

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) or porous coordination polymers are widely attracting attention as functional materials for gas adsorption and storage,43–52 luminescent sensing,53–55 catalysis,56–58 magnetic properties,59–64 and many other applications, even as delivery tools of medicine and controlled release.65–69 Many groups including ours have reported the use of porous MOFs as heterogeneous catalysts in a variety of catalyzed organic reactions such as C–C, C–N, and C–O coupling reactions, aldol condensation, hydrogenation, epoxidation etc.56–58,70–79 Our present research focused on studies of adsorption and storage of small gas molecules in the porous MOF medium. We are particularly interested in designing MOFs with the ability of selective CO2 adsorption.

Herein, we report a mixed ligand 3D metal carboxylate framework compound, [Zn2(H2O)(nic)(pyrz)]n·nH2O (1), where H3pyrz is pyrazole-3,5-dicarboxylic acid and Hnic is isonicotinic acid (Chart 1). Compound 1 shows very selective CO2 adsorption with respect to small gas molecules like H2, N2, and CH4 at low partial pressure. To get deeper insight into the selective CO2 adsorption capability of the MOF with respect to small gas molecules we have undertaken Molecular Dynamics and Monte Carlo simulations vis-à-vis consideration of experimental results.

Experimental section

Materials

Zinc(ii) nitrate hexahydrate, pyrazole-3,5-dicarboxylic acid and 1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)ethane were purchased either from Sigma-Aldrich or from Merck (India) and were used as received without further purification.

Physical measurements

Elemental analysis was performed on a Vario-Micro V2.0.11 elemental (CHNSO) analyzer. Fourier transform infrared spectra of KBr pellets were measured using a PerkinElmer RX I FT-IR spectrometer. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) was performed with a Bruker D8 Advance X-ray powder diffractometer equipped with Cu-Kα radiation (λ = 1.54 Å). In situ variable temperature PXRD patterns were measured on the same instrument equipped with a high-temperature chamber (Anton Paar HTK 16N) and TCU 2000N temperature controller unit using Cu-Kα radiation. The sample was loaded on a Pt plate and heated at a rate of 5 °C min−1 under an N2 atmosphere. TG/DT analyses were performed on a PerkinElmer (SINGAPORE) Pyris Diamond TG/DTA unit. The heating rate was programmed at 5 °C min−1 with a protecting stream of N2 flowing at a rate of 150 mL min−1. The metal content of the sample was estimated on a PerkinElmer A Analyst 200 atomic absorption spectrometer (AAS).

Synthesis of [Zn2(H2O)(nic)(pyrz)]n·nH2O

An equimolar (0.5 mmol) amount of Zn(NO3)2·4H2O, pyrazole-3,5-dicarboxylic acid and 1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)ethane was mixed in milliQ water (8 mL) and stirred for 1 h and it was transferred into a 25 mL Teflon-lined autoclave and kept in an oven at 170 °C for 3 days. After slow cooling to room temperature over a period of 8 h, rod-shaped, light-yellow crystals were obtained (84% yield based on the metal ion). Elemental analysis (%) for [Zn2(H2O)(nic)(pyrz)]n·nH2O: C 29.89, H 2.05, N 9.51, O 28.96; found: C 29.9, H 2.0, N 9.5, O 28.9. Selected IR peaks (KBr disk, ν, cm−1): 1594, 1559 [νas (CO2−)], 1501 [νs (CO2−)], 1388, 1343 [νs (C–O)], and 3019–3505 s.br [νs (O–H)] (Fig. S1, ESI†).

X-ray crystallography

X-ray diffraction data for [Zn2(H2O)(nic)(pyrz)]n·nH2O (1) were collected at 180(2) K on a Bruker SMART APEX CCD X-ray single crystal diffractometer using graphite-monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). Determination of the integrated intensities and cell refinement were performed with the SAINT80 software package using a narrow-frame integration algorithm. An empirical absorption correction (SADABS)81 was applied. The structure was solved by direct methods and refined using the full-matrix least-squares technique against F2 with anisotropic displacement parameters for non-hydrogen atoms with the programs SHELXS2018 and SHELXL2018.82 Two water solvent molecules (O100 and O200) were found to be disordered, and their occupancies were fixed at 0.5 before final refinement. All the hydrogen atoms except the hydrogens at the disordered atoms were placed at calculated positions using suitable riding models with isotropic displacement parameters derived from their carrier atoms. The hydrogen atoms of the water molecules with half occupancy as well as the lattice water (O21) were not taken into account in the structure refinement. In the final difference Fourier maps there were no remarkable peaks except the ghost peaks surrounding the metal centers. A summary of the crystal data and relevant refinement parameters for compound [Zn2(H2O)(nic)(pyrz)]n·nH2O is given in Table S1 (ESI†).

Gas adsorption experiment

Gas sorption isotherms for N2 (77 K and 273 K), H2 (77 K and 273 K), CO2 (273 K and 298 K) and CH4 (273 K and 298 K) in the pressures range 0–1 bar were measured with an Autosorb iQ (Quantachrome Inc., USA) gas sorption system with an ultrahigh purity (99.999% purity) gas source. Samples were evacuated under a dynamic vacuum (10−3 Torr) at the desired temperature (100 °C). Gas adsorption experiments of the as-synthesized compound were carried out keeping only it under a dynamic vacuum (10−3 Torr) for 1 hour at room temperature without any further activation. For all isotherms, warm and cold free-space correction measurements were performed with ultrahigh purity He gas (99.999% purity). Prior to all the measurements, the samples (∼150 mg) were activated under a vacuum for 3 h at 90 °C.

Computational details

Molecular dynamics (MD) and Monte Carlo (MC) simulations of the monoclinic C2/c MOF crystal structure (cell formula of C88H72N24O64Zn16) were performed with the GULP and SORPTION program codes of Accelrys, Inc.83 The starting experimental crystal optimization, and MD and MC simulations were performed with the Dreiding force-field.84 It is a diagonal force-field with harmonic potential terms and a cosine-Fourier expansion torsion term. The umbrella functional form is used for inversions, which were defined according to the Wilson out-of-plane definition. The Lennard-Jones potential described the van der Waals interactions. Electrostatic interactions were characterized by atomic monopoles and a screened Coulombic term. An explicit Lennard-Jones 12-10 potential described hydrogen bonding. Materials Studio provides the QEq method for calculating approximate atomic point charges.85 The CO2, H2, N2, and CH4 molecules were treated with the same force field and charge method as the MOF structure. The potential parameters and charges used are reported in Table S2 (ESI†).

Four MD simulations of 10 ns with CO2, H2, N2, and CH4 molecules within the MOF structure were performed with the microcanonical ensemble, respectively. The Verlet leapfrog algorithm was used to integrate the equations of motion with a time step of 1 fs. The first 50 ps of the simulation time were spent in equilibrating the kinetic and potential energy distribution before the production phase. Fig. S2 (ESI†) shows the models, which were fully balanced after 1 ns of the simulation time.

The Metropolis Monte Carlo simulations86 were carried out with the grand canonical ensemble. In this ensemble, the fugacities of all components and the volume, as well as the temperature, are fixed. The MOF was in open contact with an infinite sorbate reservoir with a fixed temperature. In this method, the sorbate structure is treated as rigid and only rigid body translations and reorientations are incorporated. Sorbate molecules are allowed to flow in and out the framework to accommodate the fixed fugacity of the reservoir. A fixed pressure simulation closely mimics experimental conditions and allows one to calculate the average loading of a sorbate component at a given fugacity and temperature. The adsorption isotherm calculation is essentially performed by using a series of fixed pressure simulations with the grand canonical ensemble where the fugacity is steadily ramped up over the series. Simulations were carried out for a total of 106 cycles, in which the first 105 cycles were used for equilibration.

Results and discussion

Synthesis and characterization

The compound has been designed based on a pillar-layer technique where pyrazole-3,5-dicarboxylic acid has been chosen to achieve the metal-carboxylate layers and 1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)ethane as an N,N′-donor auxiliary ligand or spacer to extend the layers into a 3D porous framework. Pyrazole-3,5-dicarboxylic acid has multiple donor sites with both oxygen and nitrogen donor atoms and it also has versatile binding modes, which make it very useful to get a 2D layered network in the presence of another ligand having capability to connect 2D layers. But the problem of the use of a very long spacer is interpenetration, which makes the framework unsuitable for gas adsorption. In our case 1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)ethane oxidises to isonicotinate in the reaction medium. This conversion from 1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)ethane to isonicotinate helps to get a desired porous 3D framework without any interpenetration. The mere use of isonicotinic acid instead of 1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)ethane does not afford any stable framework compound.

[Zn2(H2O)(nic)(pyrz)]n·nH2O (1) crystallized in a monoclinic space group C2/c with Z = 8. Selected bond distances and angles are collated in Table S3 (ESI†). The asymmetric unit of [Zn2(H2O)(nic)(pyrz)]n·nH2O contains two Zn(ii) ions, one pyrazole-3,5-dicarboxylate linker (pyrz), one isonicotinate linker (nic), one coordinated water molecule and one crystalline water molecule. The ORTEP diagram is given in Fig. S3 (ESI†). Both metal centers (Zn1 and Zn2) of compound 1 are hexa-coordinated with distorted octahedral geometry (Fig. 1). The Zn1 center is surrounded by four carboxylato oxygen atoms, three (O1, O11a and O11b; a = x, −y, −1/2 + z; b = 1/2 − x, 1/2 + y, 1/2 − z) from three different pyrazole-3,5-dicarboxylate (pyrz) linkers and one (O12) from the isonicotinate (nic) linker, and the other two positions are occupied by two nitrogen atoms, N6 and N18 from the pyrz and nic linker, respectively. Whereas, the Zn2 center contains four carboxylato oxygen atoms (O1, O2, O10 and O13) linked in the same fashion as in the Zn1 center and one nitrogen atom (N5) from the pyrz linker along with a water molecule. Here two carboxylate groups of two different pyrz molecules and one carboxylate group of a nic molecule connect two Zn(ii) centers in μ1,3, μ1,1, and μ1,3 fashion, respectively, forming a dinuclear secondary building unit (SBU), and each dinuclear core is connected by the pyrazole-3,5-dicarboxylate to form 2D sheet arrangements (Fig. 2) along the crystallographic ac plane. Each 2D sheet is further pillared by the isonicotinate linker, resulting in 3D pillared networks (Fig. 3). The three-dimensional structure of compound 1 contains water filled channels parallel to the crystallographic b-axis. Coordinated water molecules are pointing towards the interior of the channels. Upon removal of the water molecules the framework features channels along the c-axis with dimensions of about 8.5 × 4.5 Å2 and along the b-axis with dimensions of about 9 × 7 Å2, with the open metal sites exposed to the pores. After the removal of the lattice water molecules the void space enhances from 15.9% to 30.2% with respect to the total crystal volume as estimated by PLATON and after further removal of coordinated water molecules the value improves to 34.1% with the open metal sites exposed to the pores.87 To understand the structure of compound 1 further, a topological analysis was performed by reducing the multidimensional structure to a node-and-linker net. Topological analysis by TOPOS reveals that the compound possesses a simple 3-nodal 2,4,6-c 3D net structure (Fig. 4) consisting of a nic linker as a two-coordinated node, a pyrz linker as a four-coordinated node and Zn dimers as a six-coordinated node (Fig. 5).88

Thermogravimetric and differential thermal analyses were carried out to study the stability of the framework. Thermal analysis of compound [Zn2(H2O)(nic)(pyrz)]n·nH2O (1) was performed using a powdered sample from room temperature to 800 °C under a nitrogen atmosphere (Fig. 6). Compound 1 showed a two stage mass loss. At the first step, upon heating a sharp mass loss of 7.8% from 28 to 68 °C corroborates with the theoretical value of 8.15% owing to loss of one lattice and one coordinated water molecule. The desolvated framework is then thermally stable up to 270 °C. On further heating the compound decomposes steadily. The corresponding DTA curve shows two endothermic peaks at ca. 55 and 490 °C for the loss of water molecules and for decomposition of the compound, respectively.

To confirm the phase purity of the bulk material of compound 1, powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) measurements were carried out. The PXRD patterns of the compound are shown in Fig. S6 (ESI†). X-ray powder diffraction patterns of compound 1 were simulated using its single crystal data. Comparison of the experimental and simulated PXRD patterns showed that all the major peaks of the experimental PXRD of compound 1 matched well with those of the simulated one, indicating its reasonable crystalline phase purity. TG analysis clearly indicates that upon heating compound 1 loses all water molecules to produce a dehydrated product which is stable up to 270 °C. For the PXRD measurement of the dehydrated variety, the required amount of the dehydrated product of compound 1 has been collected from the TG analyzer in several batches after heating up to 270 °C. Dehydration can also be performed by heating compound 1 at 100 °C under a vacuum (10−3 Torr) for 1 h (conditions used for activation for gas adsorption experiments). Dehydration of compound 1 was further confirmed by IR spectral analysis and elemental analysis (Fig. S1, ESI†). Anal. ca. for the dehydrated product of compound 1; C = 32.55, H = 1.24, N = 10.35, O = 23.65; found C = 32.58, H = 1.26, N = 10.31, O 23.69. The PXRD pattern of the calcined species is slightly shifted to a lower 2θ angle, indicating a slight elongation of the unit cell, but is largely unchanged, suggesting the robustness of the framework after dehydration. The PXRD patterns of the as-synthesized and dehydrated compound are retained upon prolonged storage (at least 6 months). No significant changes of the PXRD patterns of the as-synthesized and dehydrated compounds are observed upon exposure to water. Variable temperature PXRD patterns were also measured for compound 1 up to 250 °C (Fig. S4; ESI†). In situ variable temperature PXRD measurements also reveal that the framework structure of the compound is stable enough up to 250 °C. The framework structure is also retained after the gas adsorption study.

Gas sorption studies

[Zn2(H2O)(nic)(pyrz)]n·nH2O as well as its dehydrated analogue contain solvent accessible voids. Their pore sizes are bigger than the kinetic diameter of N2, CO2, CH4 and H2. Thus to evaluate the porosity and the adsorption capability of compound 1, we have carried out N2, H2, and CO2 adsorption measurements of the compound. Compound 1 was found to be very selective towards CO2 capture with respect to N2, CH4 and H2. Even the dehydrated compound adsorbs a very small amount of N2, CH4 and H2 at low partial pressure (∼1 bar) (Fig. 7 and Fig. S5, ESI†).

For post-combustion CO2 capture, the pressure of the flue gas (∼1 bar) and the low partial pressure of CO2 (0.15 bar) thereof lead to the lower-pressure region of the CO2 adsorption isotherm being the area of interest.89 CO2 adsorption isotherms for the as-synthesized (without any activation) and dehydrated compound were measured at 273 K and 298 K up to 1 bar pressure (Fig. 8). The CO2 adsorption of compound 1 shows type I isotherms. The gas uptake of the as-synthesized (without usual activation) and dehydrated compound was 5.6 and 11.7 wt% at 0.15 bar and 10.2 and 23.6 wt% at 1 bar respectively, at 273 K. At 298 K, the as-synthesized and dehydrated compound showed an uptake of 2.2 and 5.7 wt% at 0.15 bar and 5.3 and 12.2 wt% at 1 bar, respectively. The steepness of the slope in the low pressure part of the isotherms indicates strong adsorption. The presence of hysteresis in the adsorption–desorption isotherm also indicates the presence of strong interactions of CO2 with the surface of compound 1. The selective CO2 adsorption capacity of the activated compound with respect to N2, H2 and CH4 reported here was compared (Table 1) with other reported 3D-porous MOFs.90–104 The selective CO2 uptake capacity of the activated compound is higher than that observed in some of the other MOFs.90–98 There are a few recent reports in which the values are higher than that of compound 1.99–103 It is noteworthy that the Zn-MOF reported here is highly selective towards CO2 adsorption over all three small gas molecules (H2, N2, and CH4) at low partial pressure. High selectivity of CO2 by PCPs over multiple gas molecules is scanty in the literature.104,105 Stable three-dimensional magnesium(ii) and calcium(ii) PCPs synthesized using a neutral ligand are very selective in carbon dioxide adsorption and separation of small gas molecules.104 Preferential CO2 adsorption by a 2D Cu(ii) PCP has been achieved due to the strong interaction of CO2 with the pore wall decorated by the fluorinated anions.105 The selectivity of activated compound 1 was calculated using the ratio of the initial slopes in the Henry region of the CO2 and CH4 adsorption isotherms at 273 K. The selectivity of CO2 with respect to CH4 at 273 K is calculated to be 22.4 (Fig. S7, ESI†). The IAST selectivity also has been simulated on the basis of pure gas sorption data (Fig. S8, ESI†). The heat of adsorption calculated for the deactivated and dehydrated compound using the DFT model with respect to the CO2 isotherms of 298 K and 273 K was 23.40 kJ mol−1 and 50.57 kJ mol−1, respectively, at 0.84 mmol g−1 and 0.51 mmol g−1 loading (Fig. S6, ESI†). At higher loading these values fall slightly as the highest affinity sites are filled. The isosteric heat of adsorption for CO2 reported in this work is compared with the literature values (Table 2).100–106

Computational studies

A previous geometric optimization for each of the models studied (MOF-CO2, MOF-H2, MOF-N2 and MOF-CH4) was performed starting from the experimental crystal structure and including a CO2, H2, N2 or CH4 molecule. Fig. 9 shows the isonicotinic acid bridge disposition between the Zn layers in each crystal MOF channel (Fig. 9A–C). The red arrows in Fig. 9D show the possible diffusion ways of the gas molecule, through the 001 and 010 faces of the crystal. Later molecular dynamics simulations have been used to investigate the MOF modifications during a certain time. At that time, we observed the presence of rotations of the aromatic plane formed by the isonicotinic acid bridged between the Zn layers. In Table 3, a polar diagram of the changes undergone by each molecule bridge (A–D) is shown throughout the time of the simulation. Each dot represents the dihedral angle formed by the aromatic ring with respect to the layers of Zn to which they are attached. These rotations are performed synchronously with the rest of the bridge molecules found in the MOF. The arrangement of the four-bridge molecules averaged over the entire time of the MD can be seen schematically in Fig. 10B.

As can be seen in Table 3 and Fig. 10 the spatial arrangement of the four bridges per unit cell of the MOF is common and independent of the introduced gas. The synchronized rotations of the aromatic part of the organic bridges are able to form a hexagonal encapsulation within the Zn layers (Fig. 10B).

Starting from a representative snapshot of the full molecular dynamics, the calculation of the adsorption isotherm with grand canonical Monte Carlo simulations for the MOF-CO2, MOF-H2, MOF-N2 and MOF-CH4 systems was performed. This type of calculation allows characterizing the affinity of a specific sorbate component toward a framework and can be directly compared with experimental results. In our case and as shown in Fig. 11 great concordance has been found between the theoretical and experimental results. As can be observed, the MOF-CO2 system is the one that presents higher adsorption even at low pressures (∼10.7 molecule average loading per unit cell at 1 bar of pressure), with the MOF-H2 system (∼0.0 molecule loading per unit cell at 1 bar pressure) being the worst. The adsorption capacity between compounds is also correctly characterized, being the same as that found experimentally (MOF-CO2 > MOF-CH4 > MOF-N2 > MOF-H2).

These results are qualitatively similar to those found by MD, where a comparison of the MD shows that in the case of MOF-CO2, the CO2 is retained in the whole time of the simulation in the formed packaging. However, the molecules of H2, N2 and CH4 can move freely through the channel formed between layers of Zn without being retained. Differences between the MOF-H2 and MOF-N2 or MOF-CO2 MD models have also been observed. Just as the N2 and CH4 are only able to move freely through the formed zig-zag path (001 face of the MOF) (red arrows in Fig. 10B), the H2 molecule mostly moves through the face 001 but can also do so by the 010 face of the crystal due to its reduced size.

Conclusion

In essence, we prepared a new mixed ligand porous 3D metal carboxylate framework compound, [Zn2(H2O)(nic)(pyrz)]n·nH2O (H3pyrz = pyrazole-3,5-dicarboxylic acid, Hnic = isonicotinic acid). The compound was synthesized hydrothermally, and characterized by single crystal X-ray diffraction and other physicochemical methods. The as-synthesized compound has large solvent accessible void space (15.9%) which was enhanced to 34.1% upon removal of water molecules, leading to creation of open metal sites that are exposed to the pores. At 298 K, the as-synthesized and dehydrated compound showed an uptake of carbon-dioxide of 2.2 and 5.7 wt% at 0.15 bar and 5.3 and 12.2 wt% at 1 bar, respectively, which increased to 5.6 and 11.7 wt% at 0.15 bar and 10.2 and 23.6 wt% at 1 bar, respectively, upon cooling to 273 K. [Zn2(H2O)(nic)(pyrz)]n·nH2O and its dehydrated product are highly selective towards CO2 adsorption over all three small gas molecules viz. H2, N2 and CH4. Molecular dynamics and Monte Carlo simulations afforded a possible rationalization of the fact found in experiments.
