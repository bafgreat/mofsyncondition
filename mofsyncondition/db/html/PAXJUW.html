1. Introduction

Coordination polymers (CPs) continue to attract considerable interest because of their structural diversity and potential application in a variety of fields such as gas storage,1–3 separation,4 optics,5 catalysis6 and magnetochemistry.7 Most CPs are formed via coordination-driven self-assembly.8 Since the assembly processes are influenced by numerous factors, such as temperature, pH value and additional weak interactions, the final product is often difficult to predict and control. In the early 1990s, R. Robson9 reported examples of rationally designed CPs from post-self-assembly. According to this approach, further components may be added to an existing unit and this produces the aforementioned properties in a more controlled way. Following this strategy, the stepwise synthesis towards bimetallic CPs has been developed.

A variety of ditopic ligands, including Schiff bases,10 pyridinedicarboxylates,11,12 aminopolycarboxylic acids13 and N donor functionalized acetylacetonates,14–20 represent attractive constituents for building blocks which may be further crosslinked by additional cations. Among these ditopic ligands, N donor functionalized acetylacetonates have proved particularly successful. Our group has made use of these ligands because of their chelating nature and distinct Pearson hardnesses21 associated with the different coordination sites.

Depending on the valence state of the metal cation, the building blocks can be categorized into several classes with respect to connectivity (Scheme 1). Types II and III may be described as nodes of higher connectivity,18,22 whereas type I represents a simple linear spacer.17,23 We have reported earlier the type I square planar building block Pd(acacCN)2.23 The alternating arrangement of such neutral building units and Ag(i) cations resulted in cationic chains with obligatory counteranions. Here we report a labile building block based on Mg(ii) and 3-cyanoacetylacetone (HacacCN); it re-arranges its coordination sphere from type I to an anionic Mg(acacCN)3 type II upon crosslinking with Ag(i) cations, thus leading to overall neutral extended structures. As no counteranions outside the network are present, the solvent employed determines the topology of the resulting bimetallic networks. Scheme 2 summarizes the results of this contribution.

2. Results

2.1. Magnesium building block

The Mg building block was obtained by a reaction of the ligand HacacCN with magnesium methoxide. The product is strongly hygroscopic; when recrystallized from methanol, it takes up two molecules of water per cation. Our attempt to structurally characterize the resulting solid 1 at 100 K failed; cooling leads to a phase transition accompanied by fragmentation of the single crystal. The diffraction experiment on 1 was therefore conducted at room temperature; under these conditions the compound crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/c with the central magnesium cation located on a crystallographic two-fold axis. It adopts a slightly elongated octahedral geometry, with four coplanar oxygen atoms from two O,O′ chelating acacCN ligands in the equatorial and two water oxygen atoms in the apical positions (Fig. 1, left). Hydrogen bonds between the aqua ligands and nitrile groups link the neutral building units into 2D layers parallel to the ac plane (Fig. 1, right).

2.2. Bimetallic coordination polymers

After O coordination of the ditopic ligand, its dangling nitrile moieties may be crosslinked with Ag(i), leading to the bimetallic derivatives 2a–5. 2a–3b share the moiety formula Mg(acacCN)3Ag·solvent(s). These solids include uncoordinated and mostly volatile solvent molecules; single crystals and crystalline powder suffer from fast desolvation at the time scale of standard analytical methods such as powder diffraction and elemental analysis. Powder diffraction confirms that the desolvation products are crystalline but their structures do not match the desolvated framework; no permanent porosity has been encountered. Detailed information concerning each compound is provided in the Experimental part. In 4, the solvent methanol is coordinated to the Ag cation; desolvation is slightly less rapid. 5 differs significantly from the other solids with respect to the coordination environment of Mg(ii): its crystal structure contains ionic tris(ligand) type II (Scheme 1) as well as neutral bis(ligand) type I complexes.

2.2.1. Mg(acacCN)3Ag solvates

In compounds 2a–4, each Mg(ii) is coordinated by three ligands in octahedral geometry, with an intra-ligand O–Mg–O bite angle of ca. 85°, similar to the ligand geometry in 1 (Fig. 2). These solids are obtained by reactant diffusion; by changing the solvent, three different kinds of crystalline material have been obtained.

Diffusion of a Ag(i) salt in methanol into a solution of the Mg building block 1 in dichloromethane or acetone allowed the isolation of single crystals of 2a or 2b, respectively. Since 2a and 2b are isomorphous, only 2a is discussed in detail. 2a crystallizes in space group P21/c with Z = 4; all atoms reside in general positions. Apart from the common formula Mg(acacCN)3Ag, the asymmetric unit contains one molecule of dichloromethane. Each silver cation is coordinated by four nitrogen atoms of four acacCN ligands in a distorted tetrahedral geometry. A pair of silver cations, related by an inversion center, is bridged by two nitrogen atoms, thus forming a dinuclear aggregate (Fig. 3 left). A comparison of these Ag2 aggregates in 2a and 2b is shown in Table 1.

Each dinuclear silver unit connects six Mg(acacCN)3 anions and expands the structure to a three dimensional framework (Fig. 3, right, and Fig. 4, left). In the course of topological analysis of this framework, the Mg(ii) complexes may be perceived as 3-connected and the Ag2 units as 6-connected nodes. In order to ensure acceptable space filling, two of these frameworks interpenetrate (Fig. 4, right). The framework corresponds to a rtl-c24 network with the vertex symbol (4·62·62)(4·4·6·6·6·6·6·6·6·6.62·62).25

3a and 3b are crystallographically different but topologically related. Mg(acacCN)3 links to three Ag(i) via its peripheral nitrile groups; similarly, each silver cation is coordinated by three nitrogen atoms corresponding to a only slightly distorted triangle. In the resulting 2D extended layers, both cations can be viewed as topologically equivalent 3-connected nodes. This uninodal network corresponds to an hcb24 net with the vertex symbol 63 (Fig. 5).25 Two of these layers stack closely to a bimetallic double layer arrangement via Ag⋯Ag and Ag⋯N interactions. Contact distances in 3a and 3b have been compiled in Table 2, and Fig. 6 provides a visual comparison.

The comparison of the inter-layer contacts in Table 2 and Fig. 6 suggests that mainly Ag⋯Ag interactions drive the arrangement to double layers in 3a, whereas Ag⋯N interactions are more relevant in 3b. In both solids 3a and 3b, the double layers are further arranged in parallel stacks, with solvent molecules filling the interstitials (Fig. 7).

Compound 4 was obtained when the crosslinking reaction was performed in methanol or in a mixture of methanol and larger solvent molecules such as diethyl ether. The influence of the solvents on the structure of the solid reaction products will be discussed in the next subsection.

In contrast to the aforementioned bimetallic networks, 4 does not contain any free solvent molecules. The methanol molecule in 4 is part of the coordination sphere of the silver cation: Ag(i) is tetracoordinated by three nitrogen atoms from nitrile groups and one oxygen from methanol. In addition, a significantly longer and presumably weak Ag⋯O contact of 2.763(2) Å with an acetylacetonate oxygen atom occurs. In order to put this distance into a broader context, we performed a search in the CSD.26,27 Our search resulted in 1256 structures with five-coordinated Ag including a Ag–O bond; the latter spans a wide range of distances with a mean Ag–O distance of 2.467 Å. We suggest taking such rather long distances into account for the following reasons:

i: The 2.76 Å distance encountered in 4 is longer than the average but not rare: among the 1256 hits above, more than 100 show Ag–O distances longer than 2.7 Å. Ag–O surely corresponds to a heteropolar bond in which electrostatic forces dominate rather than orbital overlap. Such interactions are energetically favourable even at longer distances.

ii: The sum of the angles ∠N3–Ag1–O7, ∠O7–Ag1–N1 and ∠N1–Ag1–N3 (93.67(9)°, 142.56(8)° and 123.08(10)°) amounts to ca. 359°; Ag1, N3, O7 and N1 are therefore almost coplanar. Since the distance between Ag1 and N2 is also longer than the other Ag–N bonds, the coordination sphere about the five-coordinated silver cation can be understood as an elongated trigonal bipyramid with N3, O7, and N1 in the equatorial plane and N2 and O3 in apical positions (Fig. 8).

This long Ag–O interaction plays a key role in the topology of 4. When only the four shorter coordinative bonds to Ag(i) are taken into account, 4 corresponds to a 2D structure, namely a highly distorted fes24 network (Fig. 9). In this case, Mg and Ag centers can be reduced to equivalent 3 connected nodes. These 2D layers can, however, be further linked into the third dimension via the longer Ag–O interaction discussed above. In the latter case, both Mg and Ag cations can be perceived as 4-connected nodes. The resulting binodal (4,4) net corresponds to a pts24 net with the vertex symbol (4·4·82·82·88·88)(4·4·87·87·87·87) (Fig. 10).25,28

2.2.2. Solid with additional Mg(acacCN)2 linkers

As discussed in section 2.2.1, the labile Mg building block 1 preferably re-arranges and acts as a monoanionic tris-ligand complex when crosslinked with Ag(i). 5 may be regarded as a model for an intermediate state: in this solid, neutral Mg(acacCN)2 and monoanionic Mg(acacCN)3 building blocks coexist. This unexpected product was obtained from the bis-ligand complex 1 and Ag(i) cations in a 3 : 2 stoichiometry; the synthesis was conducted as reactant diffusion experiments in methanol and dimethylformamide as solvents.

5 crystallizes in the orthorhombic space group Pbca. Fig. 11 shows a displacement ellipsoid plot of an expanded asymmetric unit: Mg2 is located in a crystallographic center of inversion; similar to the situation in the mononuclear building block, the central cation is coordinated by two acacCN ligands in the equatorial plane and two methanol molecules in the apical positions of a slightly elongated tetragonal bipyramid. The other constituents reside in general positions: the Ag(i) cation binds to four nitrogen atoms of nitrile moieties, and Mg1 acts as the center of an anionic tris(ligand) complex in a slightly distorted octahedral geometry. An additional methanol and disordered DMF molecule remain uncoordinated. In order to understand the topology of 5, the tris(ligand) centers associated with Mg1 are treated as 3-connected and the Ag(i) cations as 4-connected nodes whereas the bis(ligand) center Mg2 and the ditopic ligands are considered linkers. The resulting binodal (3,4) net is shown in Fig. 12 and can be assigned the vertex symbol (109·1011·1013)(103·107·107·107·108·1010).25,28 Based on the calculation results from GTECS 3D,28 the cumulative sum of the first 10 shells of topological neighbours for Mg(ii) and Ag(i) amounts to 2179 (1, 3, 9, 21, 54, 108, 181, 273, 390, 502, 637) and 2259 (1, 4, 9, 24, 60, 114, 195, 292, 386, 519, 655), respectively. This network has not been included in the RCSR database,24 thus no abbreviated name can be assigned.

As we can see from Fig. 12, the smallest topological circuits are penetrated by rods from the same network, which suggests self-catenation of 5. This also causes the high topological density of this network, average td10 = 2219; according to the RCSR database,24 this is the highest topological density among the 10-ring based nets. The self-penetration of 5 derives from the requirement for close packing. Since the neutral building units greatly prolong the spacer length of this network, from 8 Å to 15 Å approximately, all the 10 cycles, involving this long spacer and a large angle ∠Ag⋯Ag⋯Mg of ca. 160°, are penetrated by two other similar rings (Fig. 13). In comparison with interpenetrated networks, self-penetrated solids are less common. Especially for binodal networks, only a few examples have been reported.29–34

2.3. Solvent effect

All bimetallic coordination polymers reported in this study are synthesized by reactant diffusion. Among the resulting solids, 2a–4 are obtained from the same reaction stoichiometry and show the same composition with respect to the constituents of the frameworks. Obviously, the solvents involved play a decisive role. Dissolution of the magnesium building block 1 and AgBF4 required the addition of methanol to each reactant diffusion experiment. The composition of 2a–4 (Scheme 2) suggests that methylene chloride, acetone and chloroform are the preferred cocrystallization partners rather than the ubiquitous solvent methanol. Two important solvent properties, molecular volume and dipole moment, are listed in Table 3; these trends allow the discussion of possible reasons for the observed preferences. An increasing volume of the less polar cosolvent leads to the change in the dimensionality of the products from the three-dimensional structure 2 to the two-dimensional structures 3a and 3b. Table 4 compiles the solvent accessible volume and space filling characteristics of the bimetallic solids characterized in the context of this work. Among the compounds sharing the same framework stoichiometry, 3a and 3b show the largest solvent accessible volume and the most pronounced difference between framework and overall packing indices. Solvents larger than benzene are not incorporated at all: synthesis of 4via reactant diffusion leads to a product in which the larger solvent partner, diethyl ether, does not show up.

Polarity is the second important aspect: moderately polar solvents such as acetone and dichloromethane obviously represent acceptable cocrystallization partners. In contrast, very polar solvents are not compatible with the framework stoichiometry of 2a–4: dimethylformamide as a solvent leads to the formation of 5, and with acetonitrile, no coordination polymers could be isolated at all. In order to understand better the preferred clathration of certain solvents, we performed two direct competition experiments; section 4.3 gives the experimental details. In a first test, the reactant diffusion between 1 and AgBF4 was performed in a mixture of all the potential cocrystallization partners, i.e. methylene chloride, acetone, chloroform, benzene and methanol. The crystalline solid thus obtained showed a unit cell very similar to that of the isomorphous compounds 2a and 2b, in agreement with our observation that methylene chloride and acetone are preferentially cocrystallized. For the solid formed in this first experiment, no solvent model with atomic resolution could be obtained, most likely due to the presence of small amounts of additional disordered solvent species – it remains unknown whether dichloromethane as in 2a or acetone as in 2b was the preferred solvent.

Fig. 14 depicts potential interactions between these two preferred solvent molecules and the framework. In 2a, dichloromethane fits the shape of the void very well: its chlorine atoms only weakly interact with the framework via Cl1⋯Ag1 and Cl2⋯O6. In the case of 2b, methanol is engaged in a single O7⋯Ag1 interaction with a presumably unfavourable angle ∠Ag1⋯O7–C20 of 122.5(3)°. Based on these isolated solvent–framework contacts, the less polar dichloromethane seems to be a more attractive cocrystallization partner than methanol. This idea is corroborated by the outcome of our second competition experiment involving only acetone and dichloromethane. The diffraction data collected on the resulting crystalline solid 2c confirmed that the solvent region contains both solvents. A disorder model with atomic resolution allowed site occupancy refinement and converged for a ratio of 79.8(3)% dichloromethane and 20.2(3)% acetone.

3. Conclusions and future work

The formation of our bimetallic coordination polymers relies on the rearrangement of a neutral bis(ligand) to a monoanionic tris(ligand) complex which is further crosslinked by Ag(i) cations during reactant diffusion. Interpenetration or solvent inclusion is required for achieving acceptable space filling of the resulting neutral frameworks. The topologies of the solvates depend on the co-crystallized small molecules; among the extended structures, we have identified a binodal 3D network with vertex symbol (109·1011·1013)(103·107·107·107·108·1010). To the best of our knowledge, this topology has not been encountered before. Competition experiments confirm that dichloromethane represents the preferred co-crystallization partner, in agreement with framework⋯solvent contacts deduced from individual crystal structures. When the very polar acetonitrile is used in reactant diffusion, no solid at all precipitates. Future work will focus on solvent exchange via the gas phase and on the phase transition in the neutral magnesium bis(ligand) building block.

Finally, we mention that we had observed an analogous transformation of a labile building block in the field of lanthanide coordination chemistry: a tris(ligand) Yb complex rearranged during the crosslinking reaction with Ag(i), leading to an overall neutral bimetallic framework of [AgYb(acacCN)4].19 The thermal decomposition product of this bimetallic compound, Ag nanoparticles in a metal oxide matrix, proved a promising catalyst for N2O decomposition.43 We will attempt to upscale the reactions reported here and investigate the decomposition products of the bimetallic polymers.

4. Experimental part

4.1. Materials and methods

The ditopic ligand HacacCN was prepared according to the method of Silvernail et al.44 Mg (99.8%, Fluka) and AgBF4 (98%, Aldrich) were used without further purification. IR spectra were recorded on a Nicolet Avatar 360 E.S.P. spectrometer in KBr windows for 1 and in Nujol mull for 2a–5. CHN microanalyses were carried out at the Institute of Organic Chemistry, RWTH Aachen University, using a Heraeus CHN-O-Rapid instrument. Powder diffraction experiments were performed at room temperature on flat samples with a Stoe & Cie STADI P diffractometer equipped with an image plate detector with a constant ω angle of 55° using germanium-monochromated Cu-Kα1 radiation (λ = 1.54051 Å). Upon isolation from the reaction mixture, the crystalline solids 2–5 underwent desolvation. The resulting desolvation products are crystalline but their structures do not match those established by the single crystal diffraction experiments. Our attempts to perform powder diffraction on freshly isolated samples protected by perfluoropolyallylether (PFPAE) gave satisfactory match between the experimental patterns and those calculated from the single crystal structure for 2a, 4 and 5 (Fig. S9, S13 and S14†). These solids are moderately stable; upon overnight exposure to air, they also release co-crystallized solvent and show different powder patterns (Fig. S9 and S14†). For crystalline powders of 2b, 3a and 3b, desolvation is very fast; the experimental powder patterns of these compounds always refer to desolvated products (Fig. S10, S11 and S12†).

4.2. Syntheses

4.2.1. Synthesis of Mg(acacCN)2(H2O)2 (1)

Magnesium methoxide was prepared by a reaction of Mg (23.9 mg, 1 mmol) with dry MeOH (10 mL) at room temperature; a standard Schlenk technique was used to exclude moisture and oxygen. This magnesium methoxide solution was combined with a solution of HacacCN (250 mg, 2 mmol) in dry MeOH and stirred for 3 h. The clear reaction mixture was left unperturbed for slow evaporation in contact with an ambient atmosphere. During crystallization, the bis(ligand) Mg complex took up two aqua ligands. Colorless crystals grew over a period of one week. Yield: 197.4 mg (1.28 mmol, 64%). Phase purity of the product was confirmed by powder diffraction of the bulk material (Fig. S8†). Anal. calcd for C12H16MgN2O6 (with coordinated water): C: 46.71, H: 5.23, N: 9.08. Found: C: 46.58, H: 5.07, N: 9.10. IR: ν(CN, cm−1) = 2190.

4.2.2. Synthesis of [Mg(acacCN)3Ag]·CH2Cl2 (2a) and [Mg(acacCN)3Ag]·C3H6O (2b)

In a test tube, the bis(ligand) Mg complex 1 (15 mg, 0.05 mmol) was dissolved in a mixture of MeOH/CH2Cl2 (1 mL/1 mL). An intermediate layer of 0.1 mL CH2Cl2 and 0.4 mL methanol was added, and a third layer of AgBF4 (9 mg, 0.05 mmol) dissolved in 1 mL methanol was placed on top. Colorless crystals were obtained after one day; single crystal X-ray diffraction characterized them as the 2-fold interpenetrated 3D network 2a. Yield: 11 mg (0.022 mmol, 66%). Microanalysis indicates that desolvation only occurs to a minor extent, but even this partial desolvation results in a significant change in the powder diffractogram (Fig. S9†). Anal. calcd for [Mg(acacCN)3Ag]·CH2Cl2: C19H20AgCl2MgN3O6: C: 38.71, H: 3.42, N: 7.13. Found: C: 38.88, H: 3.53, N: 7.03. IR: ν(CN, cm−1) = 2194, 2163.

Crystals of 2b were prepared in an analogous way using acetone instead of CH2Cl2. Yield: 10 mg (0.020 mmol, 60%). Microanalysis indicates partial loss of solvent. Anal. calcd for [Mg(acacCN)3Ag]·C3H6O: C21H24AgMgN3O7 (solvated compound as found by single crystal diffraction): C: 44.83, H: 4.30, N: 7.47; for Mg(acacCN)3Ag: C18H18AgMgN3O6 (framework after removing clathrated acetone): C: 42.85, H: 3.60, N: 8.33; for C19.5H21AgMgN3O6.5 (framework with 0.5 equivalent of acetone): C: 43.89, H: 3.97, N: 7.87. Found: C: 43.63, H: 4.14, N: 7.40. IR: ν(CN, cm−1) = 2198, 2163.

4.2.3. Synthesis of [Mg(acacCN)3Ag]·4CHCl3 (3a) and [Mg(acacCN)3Ag]·1.5C6H6·MeOH (3b)

In a test tube, the bis(ligand) Mg complex 1 (15 mg, 0.05 mmol) was dissolved in a mixture of MeOH/CHCl3 (1 mL/1 mL). An intermediate layer of 0.1 mL CHCl3 and 0.4 mL methanol was added, and a third layer of AgBF4 (9 mg, 0.05 mmol) dissolved in 1 mL methanol was placed on top. Colorless crystals were obtained after two days; single crystal X-ray diffraction characterized them as the 2D network 3a. Yield: 10 mg (0.020 mmol, 60%). Microanalysis indicates almost complete loss of cocrystallized chloroform. Anal. calcd for [Mg(acacCN)3Ag]·4CHCl3: C22H22AgCl12MgN3O6: C: 26.91, H: 2.26, N: 4.28; for Mg(acacCN)3Ag: C18H18AgMgN3O6 (framework after complete release of clathrated chloroform): C: 42.85, H: 3.60, N: 8.33; for C18.8H18.8AgCl2.4MgN3O6 (framework with 0.8 equivalent of chloroform): C: 37.63, H: 3.16, N: 7.00. Found: C: 37.92, H: 3.10, N: 7.03.

Crystals of 3b were prepared in an analogous way using benzene instead of CHCl3. Yield: 13 mg (0.026 mmol, 78%). Microanalysis indicates significant loss of cocrystallized solvent molecules. Anal. calcd for [Mg(acacCN)3Ag]·1.5C6H6·MeOH: C28H31AgMgN3O7: C: 51.44, H: 4.78, N: 6.43; for Mg(acacCN)3Ag: C18H18AgMgN3O6 (framework after complete release of clathrated benzene and methanol): C: 42.85, H: 3.60, N: 8.33; for C19.8H19.8AgMgN3O6 (framework with 0.3 equivalents of benzene): C: 45.04, H: 3.78, N: 7.96. Found: C: 44.90, H: 3.60, N: 7.46.

The powder patterns of 3a (Fig. S11†) and 3b (Fig. S12†) confirm low crystallinity and fast desolvation, even for freshly prepared samples. Both desolvation products show the same IR resonance at 2201 cm−1 associated with the CN vibration.

4.2.4. Synthesis of Mg(acacCN)3AgMeOH (4)

In a test tube, the bis(ligand) Mg complex 1 (15 mg, 0.05 mmol) was dissolved in 1 mL MeOH and a solution of AgBF4 (9 mg, 0.05 mmol) in 1 mL MeOH was carefully layered underneath with the help of a syringe. Colorless crystals were obtained after two days; single crystal X-ray diffraction characterized them as the 3D network 4. Yield: 9.9 mg (0.020 mmol, 60%). Anal. calcd for Mg(acacCN)3Ag: C18H18AgMgN3O6 (framework after desolvation): C: 42.85, H: 3.60, N: 8.33. Found: C: 43.33, H: 3.37, N: 8.64. IR: ν(CN, cm−1) = 2182.

4.2.5. Synthesis of [Mg(acacCN)3AgMg0.5(acacCN)MeOH]·MeOH·DMF (5)

In a test tube, the bis(ligand) Mg complex 1 (18 mg, 0.06 mmol) was dissolved in 0.5 mL DMF. AgBF4 (7 mg, 0.04 mmol) was dissolved in 1 mL methanol and layered on top. Colorless crystals were obtained after one week; single crystal X-ray diffraction characterized them as the 3D network 5. Yield: 10 mg (0.016 mmol, 40%). IR: ν(CN, cm−1) = 2202. Due to the hygroscopic nature of the desolvation product, no satisfactory elemental analysis for 5 could be obtained. However, powder diffraction of the bulk material could confirm the phase purity of this product (Fig. S14†).

4.3. Solvent competition experiments

A first solvent competition experiment included dichloromethane, acetone, chloroform, benzene, methanol and diethyl ether. In a test tube, the bis(ligand) Mg complex 1 (15 mg, 0.05 mmol) was dissolved in 1 mL MeOH; 0.2 mL CH2Cl2, 0.2 mL acetone, 0.2 mL CHCl3, 0.2 mL benzene and 0.2 mL Et2O were added. AgBF4 (9 mg, 0.05 mmol) was dissolved in 1 mL methanol and layered on top. Colorless crystals were obtained after two days. These crystals showed severe disorder in the solvent region, and the resolution of their diffraction pattern did not meet the usual standards. Their unit cell, however, corresponded to that of structure type 2, the solvate obtained from dichloromethane and acetone.

In order to decide whether dichloromethane and acetone were preferentially co-crystallized, a second competition experiment was limited to these two solvents. Colorless crystals were obtained after two days; single crystal X-ray diffraction characterized them as the 3D network 2c.

4.4. Crystallographic studies

The diffraction experiments were performed using a Bruker D8 goniometer equipped with an Incoatec microsource (Mo-Kα, λ = 0.71073 Å, multilayer optics) and an APEX CCD detector; the sample temperature was controlled with an accuracy of ca. 2 K using an Oxford Cryostream 700 instrument. Intensity data were integrated with SAINT+45 and corrected for absorption by multi-scan methods using the program SADABS.46 The crystal data and refinement results for the building block 1 and the bimetallic coordination polymers 2a–2c have been summarized in Table 5 and for the bimetallic compounds 3a–5 in Table 6.

The crystal structures were solved using Patterson or direct methods, and refinements were accomplished using full matrix least-squares procedures as implemented in SHELXL-13.47 All non-hydrogen atoms were assigned anisotropic displacement parameters; hydrogen atoms were placed in idealized positions and included as riding with constrained isotropic displacement parameters. In 1 and 3b–5, the hydrogen atoms of water and hydroxyl group were located as electron density maxima from the Fourier difference maps and included in the refinement with distance restraints.

Diffraction data were collected at 100 K except for 1. In order to avoid a drastic phase transition upon cooling, the diffraction experiment on 1 was conducted at 293 K. In 2c, substitutional disorder of dichloromethane and acetone was encountered. After assignment of the dichloromethane molecule, the geometry of a well-ordered acetone moiety from the CSD27 was used to fit the target residual electron density maxima, followed by rigid group refinement. A tentative site occupancy refinement converged for a ratio of dichloromethane : acetone = 79.8(3) : 20.2(3)%, with the sum of the site occupancies of both alternative solvents constrained to unity. In the final disorder model, dichloromethane was assigned a site occupancy of 0.8 and methanol a site occupancy of 0.2, the chlorine atoms associated with the dichloromethane molecule were assigned anisotropic displacement parameters, and the C atoms of both solvent variants and the O atom of the methanol molecule were assigned a common isotropic displacement parameter. For the case of 3a, a tentative SQUEEZE48 calculation suggested the presence of eight CHCl3 molecules per unit cell. Three of the four solvent molecules per asymmetric unit show positional disorder. A treatment as a disordered solvent region with the SQUEEZE48 resulted in an unsatisfactory weighting scheme and high unassigned residual electron density. A satisfactory structure model required two alternative orientations for each disordered chloroform molecule. The sum of their occupancies was constrained to unity, distance and angle restraints were used to ensure stable geometries, and anisotropic displacement parameters for alternative atom sites were refined with similarity restraints. In 5, N,N-dimethylformamide was disordered over two orientations with combined full site occupancy. Geometry and displacement similarity restraints were used during the refinement of the disorder model.
