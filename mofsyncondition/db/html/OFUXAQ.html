Introduction

The rational fabrication and development of synthetic methodologies toward the assembly of framework solids with topologies that can, to a certain level, be predicted from the structure of the molecular building blocks have gained remarkable success in the synthesis of MOF materials.1 A vast exploration of the synthesis and application of MOF materials has been done in the last few decades. Several approaches have been made to obtain novel and highly applicable materials in the fields of gas adsorption and storage,2 catalysis3 and magnetic4 material synthesis.

In the recent past, significant effort has been given to the synthesis and properties of molecule-based magnetic materials. New fascinating areas have been built up through the years after combined efforts of physicists and chemists facilitated the expansion of the basic understanding of these molecule-based magnetic materials.5,6 Perpetual interest in designing these materials can even allow the partial control of their magnetic properties. The simple idea to prepare molecule-based magnet requires the consideration of spin carriers and effective bridges. In the case of metal assembled systems, spin carriers are usually transition metal ions while bridging ligands connect the metal centers. Although some considerable success has been achieved in this field, it still demands further efforts to control and fabricate new structures.7 Usually, to find suitable magnetic MOFs a bottom-up approach of coupling the transition-metal ions with appropriate bridging ligands can be a simple strategy. For bridging, different ligands have been used; besides cyanide,8azide9 or N-heterocyclic compounds (pyrazole, imidazole, triazole, or tetrazole),10 carboxylates are of prime importance, because of their versatile bridging modes. Due to the modes of syn–syn, syn–anti and anti–anti, it is possible to tune up the effective magnetic moment and these systems help to understand the magneto-structural correlations and provide an effective advantage for further exploration of magnetic materials in academy and industry.11 In the present work we used a new hydroxy dicarboxylate ligand, tartronic acid (L), to construct the 2D layered MOFs. In the literature there are very limited results using this dicarboxylate ligand. Robson et al. constructed a 2D sheet structure with Zn11d and Goldberg and Koner reported a mixed carboxylate 2D coordination compound having La3+ as the central metal ion.11e Ablov et al. previously reported a trinuclear Cu2+ discrete compound constructed from the three tartronate ligands.11f The tartronate ligand is quite promising to produce a good magnetic coordination polymer with the μ2-O alkoxy bridging besides the syn–syn and syn–anti bridging modes, see Scheme 1.

In this paper we report two new layered metal carboxylates, [Co3(L)2(H2O)6]·2H2O (1) and [Ni3(L)2(H2O)6]·2H2O (2), and their magnetic behaviors. Temperature dependent magnetic susceptibility measurements showed the existence of antiferromagnetic short range correlations between Co(Ni) intra-chain metal centers. At low temperatures (TNCo = 11 K and TNNi = 16 K) a three-dimensional phase transition is suggested, showing that Co1–Co2 (Ni1–Ni2), interactions through carboxylate groups, should not be neglected in these systems. Both compounds have an antiferromagnetic ground state but a ferromagnetic component is observed for compound 2. Remarkably, three metamagnetic phase transitions are revealed below 8 K in compound 1. The magnetic phase diagram is given.

Results and discussion

Crystal structure of 1 and 2

X-ray single crystal structure determination revealed that compounds 1 and 2 possess similar structures. The compounds crystallized in the space group P21/c with Z = 4. The ORTEP diagrams of compounds 1 and 2 are given in Fig. 1 and S1.† Due to the different metal centers in the compounds, small changes were observed for cell parameters, bond distances and angles, and intermolecular hydrogen bonds (Tables 1 and S1–S3†). Here, only the structure of compound 1 is described. The basic unit of compound 1 (Fig. 1) contains two metal centers, Co1 and Co2 (with occupancy factor 0.5), both of which are in distorted octahedral geometry. The basal plane of the Co1 octahedron is formed by the two anti–anti oxygen atoms (O6, O9), one μ2-O bridging alkoxy oxygen atom (O3, 1 − x, −y, −z) and one water molecule (O1). The two apical positions are occupied by a water molecule (O2) and one μ2-O bridging alkoxy oxygen atom (O3, −1 + x, −1/2 − y, −1/2 + z). On the other hand, the basal plane of the Co2 octahedron is formed by the syn–syn bridging of the carboxylate anion (O7, O71#, O10, O101#; 1# = 1 − x, −y, −z) and the two apical positions are occupied by two water molecules (O11, O111#). The octahedra of Co1 centers are corner shared with each other forming a 1D chain parallel to the crystallographic b-axis. Connection among the 1D chains and the Co2 octahedra ultimately forms a layered metal–organic framework (Fig. 2). The layers of the compound are tightly held together through strong intermolecular O–H⋯O hydrogen bonds formed by the coordinated water molecules and the free water molecules existing in between the layers. The crystal packing in 1 is stabilized by intermolecular hydrogen bonds through O(1)–H(1A)⋯O(11) [1 − x, y − 1/2, −z + 1/2], O(1)–H(1B)⋯O(2) [−x + 2, −y, −z + 1], O(11)–H(11A)⋯O(10) [−x + 1, −y − 1, −z], O(11)–H(11B)⋯O(100) [x, y − 1, z], O(100)–H(10A)⋯O(7) [−x + 1, y + 1/2, −z + 1/2] and the structure finally goes to a 3D supramolecular network (Fig. 3). The intermolecular hydrogen bonds parameters are listed in Table S2.†

Magnetic behavior of 1

The magnetic susceptibility χ(= M/H) (FC and ZFC) of 1 and 2 as a function of temperature is presented in Fig. 4. The molar susceptibility of 1 (Fig. 4a) shows a monotonic increase upon cooling to ∼11 K, where a maximum is observed. The upturn indicates a magnetic phase transition occurring at this temperature (TNCo = 11 K). Considering that in 1D magnetic chains only short-range order is observed at finite temperatures, the presence of a 3D phase transition, as suggested by the magnetic susceptibility data, implies that other exchange interactions, rather than the intra-chain ones (represented by J1, see Fig. 5), might play a critical role in these compounds, i.e., Co1–Co2 (Ni1–Ni2) (represented by J2, in Fig. 5) interactions should work at low temperatures. In fact, the crystal structure described above and the schematic representation of relevant magnetic pathways (J1 and J2, presented in Fig. 5) indicate that the Co1–Co2 (Ni1–Ni2) atoms are bridged by a carboxylate group (Co2–O–C–O–Co1) and it is known that carboxylate bridges are efficient pathways for magnetic exchange interactions.11,10e

The inset of Fig. 4a depicts the reciprocal magnetic susceptibility (1/χ) as a function of temperature. A linear trend of the reciprocal magnetic susceptibility data is evident. In detail, above ca. 50 K, in the paramagnetic region, the Curie–Weiss law is strictly followed. By fitting the linear part of the 1/χ curve with 1/χ = (T − θp)/C, in the 150–320 K temperature range, the Curie–Weiss temperature (θp = −58 K) and Curie constant (C = 5.62 cm3 K mol−1) were obtained. The negative Curie–Weiss temperature indicates the presence of moderate antiferromagnetic interactions between the magnetic centers. Indeed, the χT curve (Fig. 6a) shows a downward curvature, typical of systems with antiferromagnetic correlations and/or non-negligible spin–orbit coupling. The χT (4.8 cm3 K mol−1 at 320 K) undergoes a gradual decrease down to 1.2 cm3 K mol−1 at 11 K and then rapidly decreases, reaching a minimum of 0.5 cm3 K mol−1 at 5 K, confirming the onset of long-range magnetic correlations. In both compounds, the intra-chain exchange pathway is mainly via Co1–O3–Co1 (Ni1–O3–Ni1) (i.e. via J1, see Fig. 5) bridges with angles between 125 and 127°. This type of intra-chain pathway can justify the observed antiferromagnetic correlations between magnetic centers.12 As mentioned, the decay of χT upon cooling may be explained by the existence of antiferromagnetic exchange interactions, but this phenomenon may also result from the spin–orbit coupling known to be active for CoII ions.13 In fact, the Curie constant (C = 5.62 cm3 K mol−1) obtained here for mononuclear Co is much higher than the expected one for the spin-only CoII in octahedral environment (3d7, S = 3/2 and g = 2, C = 1.87 cm3 K mol−1). The value obtained experimentally suggests an unquenched orbital moment contribution for CoII (theoretical expected value is C = 5.50 cm3 K mol−1).

Although the magnetic analysis for the CoII complexes is rather difficult due to its spin–orbit coupling, a noncritical scaling model is usually used to analyze the magnetic interactions between the CoII ions:14χT = A exp(E1/kT) + B exp(E2/kT), where A + B equals the Curie constant, and E1 and E2 stand for the energies corresponding to the spin–orbit coupling and antiferromagnetic exchange interaction, respectively. The best fit of the experimental data above 15 K (Fig. S2a†) gives A + B = 5.58 cm3 K mol−1, E1/k = 99.8 K, and E2/k = −11.3 K, corresponding to an exchange coupling constant of J = −22.6 K (assuming an Ising chain approximation). The obtained values confirm the antiferromagnetic exchange interaction between metal centers in compound 1, yielding also a spin–orbit coupling energy value that is consistent with those given in the literature.14a–c

To further characterize the magnetic behavior of 1, isothermal magnetization measurements at several temperatures, cycled up to 5.5 T, were performed. As depicted in Fig. 7a and S3,† where representative temperatures are shown, no evidence for magnetic saturation is present in the magnetic data and a step-like behavior is observed. It is clear that the well-defined magnetic fields trigger different magnetic states. The M versus H curve, at 5 K, exhibits a linear behavior up to Hc1 = 0.35 T, compatible with the antiferromagnetic ground state (State 1 – S1-AFI). Above this critical field the magnetization jumps to a plateau corresponding to a different magnetic order arrangement (S2). However, at Hc2 = 4.3 T a third state is reached (S3), followed by another one, S4, triggered by Hc3 = 4.7 T. From the differential plots (dM/dH) of the isothermal magnetization a magnetic phase diagram was constructed (Fig. 8). As depicted in Fig. 8, above TN the compound 1 is in the paramagnetic state, while at lower temperatures and low fields it is in the antiferromagnetic ground state, S1-AFI.

In order to understand the nature of S2 to S4 field induced states, the isothermal magnetization data were plotted as Arrott plots (H/M vs. M2) (Fig. 9). These plots are based on Landau theory of phase transitions and allow the extraction of zero-field magnetic properties from the high-field dependence. This is especially valuable in the case of magnetic field-induced transitions, such as those present in this work, since low-field magnetic characteristics cannot be directly accessed once the phase is not stable at zero field. Therefore, in the present work, this method was used to extract the temperature dependence of magnetic susceptibility of the field induced phases S2 and S4.

This approximation (see eqn (1)), which is usually valid in the vicinity of a magnetic phase transition, relies on a series expansion and the minimization of the Gibbs free energy yielding, in the case relevant to this work, (1) The A(T) and B can be directly extracted from the H/M vs. M2 plot by adjusting lines to the plot and thus extracting the slope and the intercept with the y-axis (A(T) = 1/χ) in a certain range of temperatures (5 K to 8 K for S2 and T > 8 K to for S4) and field (from 1.3 T to 3.2 T for S2 and from 3.8 to 5.3 T to S4). In this way, the reciprocal magnetic susceptibility of field induced phases, 1/χ(T) values, at different temperatures are determined by the intersection of the Arrott plot with the H/M axis. By plotting the extracted 1/χ values as a function of temperature the expected linear dependence is obtained. This linear dependence can be fitted using a Curie–Weiss law allowing the Curie–Weiss temperatures θp to be determined.

For the magnetic induced state S2, occurring at the lowest temperatures, the zero-field reciprocal susceptibility temperature dependence of 1/χS2 allowed a positive Curie–Weiss temperature (θpS2 ∼ 4 K) to be determined. This clearly hints at the antiferromagnetic/ferromagnetic-like nature of the metamagnetic phase transition. However, considering that our data are also compatible with a field induced canting of the spin structure (spin–flop-like transition), the S2 state has a ferromagnetic component in any case, thus is defined as S2-FM in the phase diagram. The values of Hc1 at which the magnetic transition occurs increase as the isothermal magnetization temperature is raised, while the Hc2 ones shift to lower fields, and they merge at around 1.7 T at ca. 8 K. With the presented data the nature of this intermediate state S3 cannot be disclosed. Nevertheless, using a similar approach as above, a negative Curie–Weiss temperature (θpS4 ∼ −16 K) was extracted for the S4 field induced state, indicating that above Hc3 the system is in an antiferromagnetic state, thus is named S4-AFII. With increasing temperature, this metamagnetic transition shifts to lower fields, disappearing at the ordering temperature. Comparing the magnetic parameters obtained for S1-AFI ground state and S4-AFII allows us to infer that these AF states are different in nature. S4-AFII presents weaker antiferromagnetic interactions (θpS1 > θpS4). To ascertain the nature of S2 and verify our suggestions for the S4-AFII state, additional measurements need be done at very high fields.

Magnetic behavior of 2

The molar susceptibility of 2 shows a monotonic increase on cooling to 20 K where a step increase is observed (Fig. 4b). Above this temperature the linear trend of the reciprocal magnetic susceptibility data is evident (inset of Fig. 4b), strictly following the Curie–Weiss law with θp = −66 K and C = 1.97 cm3 K mol−1. The negative Weiss temperature indicates the presence of dominant antiferromagnetic exchange interactions between the magnetic Ni centers, and similar to the 1, the spin–orbit coupling seems to also play an important role in this system. In fact the obtained Curie constant is between the limits of completely quenched and unquenched orbital contributions for NiII in octahedral environment (C = 0.98 and 3.91 cm3 K mol−1, respectively). Using a similar model as the one described before, the best fit of the experimental data above 25 K (Fig. S2b†) gives A + B = 1.90 cm3 K mol−1, E1/k = 99.0 K, and E2/k = −17.7 K, corresponding to an exchange coupling constant of J = −35.4 K. These values also confirm the antiferromagnetic exchange interaction between metal ions in 2, yielding a spin–orbit coupling energy value that is consistent with those given in the literature.14 In accordance with the antiferromagnetic interactions between the magnetic NiII centers, the χT (1.6 cm3 K mol−1 at 320 K) decreases upon cooling to 0.49 cm3 K mol−1 at 20 K (Fig. 6b). On further cooling a sudden increase to 1.4 cm3 K mol−1 and then a sharp decrease to 0.21 cm3 K mol−1 at 5 K occur. This effect could be due to intermolecular magnetic interactions, yielding a ferromagnetic contribution to the magnetization, or due to a small canting in the antiferromagnetic order. This abrupt rise in the χT curve indicates a phase transition with the onset of a ferromagnetic component, at ca. TNNi = 16 K, that is also obvious in the bifurcation between the ZFC and the FC curves. The existence of antiferromagnetic long range interactions with a small canting of the Ni spins can be confirmed by the isothermal magnetization data (Fig. 7b) where no evidence for saturation is observed at 5 K up to 5.5 T. Unlike in 1, no field-induced transition was observed.

The magnetic behavior of MOFs depends on many factors including the site symmetry of the magnetic ion, type of ligands and modes. As observed in compounds 1 and 2, CoII and NiII MOFs often present high temperature Curie–Weiss behavior, partially or completely unquenched orbital moment and a strong decrease in χT as the temperature decreases, due to dominant orbital contribution and/or antiferromagnetic exchange interactions.15–17 Nevertheless, as in compound 2, a considerable number of systems present a low temperature ferromagnetic component, usually attributed to spin canting or ferromagnetic-like interactions.10e,11a,16 More rarely, on the other hand, ferromagnetic interactions among the metal ions can be observed, e.g. in systems with distorted CoII (NiII) octahedra where the interactions are mediated by azide bridges.18–20

Like in compound 1, Co complexes based on the carboxylate polychlorotriphenylmethyl radical15a reveal moderate antiferromagnetic interactions. Similar behavior is found in 3D CoII MOF with hydroxyl-bridged metal Δ-chains,15c in a 2D Kagomé layer with various bridging modes16 and in triangular distorted magnetic lattices.17 Antiferromagnetic correlations are also observed in mixed azide and tetrazole bridged CoII/MnIIpolymers.10e However, as in 2, a ferromagnetic-like component is evident at low temperatures. The same features are found in [NH2(CH3)2][FeIIICoII(HCOO)6] framework11a and in a NiII based 2D Kagomé layer.16 In contrast to the previous examples,15a,b long-range order is attained in 1 and 2, probably as a result of the shorter and more efficient Co1–C–O–C–Co2 exchange pathways that, at low temperatures, compete with Co1–O–Co1 chains. The antiferromagnetic-like behavior exhibited by 1 and 2 is also present in isostructural stacked layers of CoII ions connected by carboxylato bridges.14a However, the magnitude of the antiferromagnetic interactions, extracted using the same noncritical scaling model14,21 (J = −2.06 K), is much weaker than the ones found in our compounds. On the other hand similar spin–orbit coupling of ESO/k = 56.6 K was estimated. These values are comparable to those found for the interactions through Co1II–O–P–O–Co2II bridges in metal carboxydiphosphonate22 and in [Co2(OA)(dpe)(H2O)2] 3D framework (dpe = 1,2-di(4-pyridyl)ethylene, ESO/k = 64.4 and J = −1.06 K).23 Similarly, in isostructural cobalt alkane mono- and di-carboxylate soaps ESO/k ∼ 100 K and J < 2 K were obtained.14c The stronger antiferromagnetic coupling found in 1 and 2, when compared to the previous examples,14a,c,22,23 should be related to the single Co1–O–Co1 (Ni1–O–Ni1) exchange pathway present in 1 and 2 that promotes more efficiently the magnetic interactions than the three-atom bridge.

The most spectacular effect presented in this work is the three-step field induced magnetic transitions observed in 1. Though field-induced phenomena can be found in some MOFs, such effects rather rarely occur.18,24,25 Transitions induced by a magnetic field can have very distinct origins. For example in the ligand-modified Mn4 dicubane single-molecule magnet, vertical steps in the hysteresis loops are assigned to quantum tunneling of magnetization.24

With a completely distinct nature, and similarly to 1, field-induced phenomena can be found in CoII triangular distorted magnetic lattices where a three-step transition from an antiferromagnetic ordering to a ferromagnetic state, spin competition and spin reorientation was reported.18 Similarly, in a monoclinic CoII framework with alternating corner- and edge-sharing Δ-ribbons25 a two-step field-induced magnetic transition is attributed to spin-competition and spin–flop transitions. A spin–flop transition is also reported in 3D CoII MOF with hydroxyl-bridged metal Δ-chains.16 Moreover, in a CoII based 3D structure with mixed bridges, azido chains, carboxylate and thiadiazole groups,19 metamagnetic behavior is ascribed to strong intra-chain ferromagnetic coupling and weak inter-chain antiferromagnetic interaction.

Magnetic transitions induced by the field are not exclusive of the CoII ion and also occur e.g. in MnII monoclinic Mn-pyz-Mn (pyz = pyrazine) chains26a and hydroxythiophenedicarboxylate MOF.26b These field-induced phenomena were attributed to spin–flop and paramagnetic transitions. Likewise, in a monoclinic 3-D MnII coordination polymer,27 magnetization multi-steps are associated to competing asymmetric antiferromagnetic couplings. FeII magnetic chains connected through bpe coligands (bpe = trans-1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)ethylene) show a two-step transition due to spin–flip, arising from large single ion anisotropy.28

The origin of the metamagnetic transition in compound 1 is still unclear, although, as in the last examples,19,27,28 it should be related to the competition between nearest-neighbor exchange coupling and the weaker but non-negligible second-neighbor one. Yet, in compound 2, with the same crystallographic symmetry and very similar pathways as 1, this effect is not observed. Thus the CoII single ion anisotropy should prevail here.

Conclusion

Two new layered compounds have been successfully prepared via traditional hydrothermal synthesis at moderate temperature. The structures of both compounds have been determined by single crystal X-ray diffraction data. The two compounds are isostructural. The metal centers in the compounds exist in both isolated octahedra and corner-sharing octahedral chains. The whole structure is secured by intermolecular hydrogen bonds. Magnetization measurements have shown that in both compounds the Curie–Weiss law is strictly followed at high temperatures. A strong spin–orbit coupling was found for 1, leading to an unquenched orbital moment. At low temperatures short range antiferromagnetic correlations between Co(Ni) metal ions (JCo = −22.6 K and JNi = −35.4 K) are evident, which is in accordance with the intrachain Co1–O3–Co1 exchange pathway (angles ca. 126°). Further decreasing temperature, a three dimensional phase transition was identified (TNCo = 11 K and TNNi = 16 K). An antiferromagnetic ground state was observed for 1, while a ferromagnetic component was found for 2, possibly due to spin canting. The presence of a 3D phase transition, as suggested by the magnetic data, indicated that another exchange interaction, rather than the intrachain ones, plays a critical role in these compounds. Moreover, for compound 1 the isothermal magnetization evidenced the existence of metamagnetic phase transitions below the Néel temperature. The magnetic phase diagram was constructed.

Experimental section

Synthesis

M(NO3)3·6H2O (M = Co or Ni), tartronic acid and NaOH were purchased from Aldrich and were used as received. Compounds 1 and 2 were prepared using the routine hydrothermal process. To obtain the desired product, M(NO3)2 hydrate (where M = Co or Ni), tartronic acid and NaOH were mixed in a 2 : 1 : 3 ratio and kept in a Teflon lined reaction bomb at 140 °C for 3 days under autogenous pressure. After cooling to room temperature, block shaped crystals with high yield were obtained. Yields were ca. 65% and 55% (based on metal) for 1 and 2, respectively. The crystals were collected by filtration, washed thoroughly with water and dried in ambient conditions. Elemental analyses and FTIR spectra of compounds 1 and 2 indicated the existence of tartronate ligand in both compounds. Elemental analyses gave C = 12.35, H = 3.75 and C = 12.55, H = 3.56% for compounds 1 and 2, respectively, which are in good agreement with the calculated ones based on the formulae obtained in the structure determination (C = 12.97, H = 3.24 and C = 12.99, H = 3.25% for compounds 1 and 2, respectively). FTIR spectra gave bands at 1617, 1558 [νas (CO2−)], 1403 [νs (CO2−)], 1306, 1195 [νs (C–O)], and 3600–3200 cm−1 s.br [ν (O–H)] for compound 1 and 1687, 1626, 1572 [νas (CO2−)], 1428 [νs (CO2−)], 1321, 1161 [νs (C–O)], and 3600–3200 cm−1 s.br [ν (O–H)] for compound 2, which are in good agreement with the existence of C–O, CO2 from the tartronate ligand and O–H2 groups. PXRD (Fig. S4†) indicates that both samples for magnetic studies are pure.

Measurements

Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) spectra of samples suspended in KBr pellets were measured on a Mattson 7000 FT-IR spectrometer in the range of 400–4000 cm−1 with resolution 2 cm−1. The samples for magnetic measurement were subjected to powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) study. The PXRD patterns were recorded at room temperature with an X'Pert MPD Philips diffractometer using Cu Kα radiation. The temperature dependent magnetic susceptibility χ (= M/H) measurements, performed between 5 and 340 K under a H = 50 Oe magnetic field, were done on samples using a commercial (Quantum Design) superconducting quantum interference device (SQUID) magnetometer. The measurements were performed using the zero field cooling (ZFC) and field cooling (FC) procedures. Isothermal magnetization between 5 and 20 K was acquired while sweeping the magnetic field from −5.5 up to 5.5 T.

X-Ray crystallographic studies of 1 and 2

Single crystal X-ray diffraction data for compounds 1 and 2 were collected at 293(2) K on a CCD Bruker APEX II X-ray diffractometer using graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). Determination of integrated intensities and cell refinement were performed with the SAINT29 software package using a narrow-frame integration algorithm. An empirical absorption correction30 (SADABS) was applied. Both structures were solved by direct methods and refined using the full-matrix least-squares technique against F2 with anisotropic displacement parameters for non-hydrogen atoms with the programs SHELXS97 and SHELXL97.31 The hydrogen atoms of the aliphatic moiety as well as in the water molecules were included on the basis of Fourier differential maps and refined isotropically. A summary of crystal data and relevant refinement parameters for compounds 1 and 2 is provided in Table 1. CCDC 890223 and 890224 contain the supplementary crystallographic data for this paper.
