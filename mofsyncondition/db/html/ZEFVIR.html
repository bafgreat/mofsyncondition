Introduction

The rational design and construction of novel functional metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) has drawn considerable attention in recent years1 due to their intriguing network topologies and exploitable applications in magnetism, luminescent materials and molecular adsorption.2 Much work has focused on the design of 3d–4f heterometallic MOFs. For the 3d magnetic transition metal ions such as Mn, Fe, Co, Ni and Cu, they can bind to N-donor and O-donor ligands and provide good opportunities for magnetic investigation.3 The 4f lanthanide ions possess high coordination numbers and variable coordination geometries and prefer to bind to hard O-donor ligands, which are good candidates to construct unusual network topologies.4 Therefore, many efforts have been devoted to the rational design and synthesis of 3d–4f heterometallic MOFs.5

In the construction of 3d–4f heterometallic MOFs, the selection of appropriate organic linkers is central to determine the structures of the resulting complexes. In fact, multidentate ligands containing both N- and O-donor atoms are usually employed in the construction of 3d–4f MOFs. For example, the study of the assembly of the pyridine carboxylate ligands (such as pyridine-2,5-dicarboxylic acid) with 3d and 4f ions was explored, and isolated a series of 3d–4f coordination polymers with intriguing structural motifs, magnetic and luminescent properties.6

In this paper, we chose an N-heterocyclic carboxylic acid, 1H-benzimidazole-5,6-dicarboxylic acid (H2L) as the target ligand, according to the following considerations:7 (a) it can act as a multidentate ligand with both N- and O-donor atoms; (b) the collaboration of the two adjacent carboxylate groups in H2L might connect metal magnetic centers serving as mediators of magnetic exchange; (c) the relatively large π-conjugated system in the benzimidazole ring might contribute much to form the π⋯π stacking interactions, and the N–H group on the imidazole ring can act as proton-donor to form hydrogen bonds, which also play important roles in the formation of coordination complexes. To date, many studies on H2L were focused on the design of homometallic coordination complexes which were all one-dimensional (1D) chain or two-dimensional (2D) layer structures.6 To the best of our knowledge, no examples of H2L have been reported in 3d–4f heterometallic coordination chemistry and three-dimensional (3D) MOFs. In addition, oxalic anions (C2O42−) as a short ligand has been proved to be a good bridge for magnetic exchange between metal centers.8

In our previous work, we reported six 3d or 4f homometallic coordination complexes of H2L ligand,7a inspired by the aforementioned considerations and in the continuing work, we chose H2L and oxalic acid as mixed ligands to prepare 3d–4f heterometallic coordination complexes. Fortunately, we successfully prepared three novel Co–Ln coordination complexes: [Co3Ln2(L)4(C2O4)2(H2O)2·6H2O]n (Ln = Pr(1), Nd(2) and Sm(3)). The thermal and magnetic properties have also been investigated.

Experimental

Materials and general procedures

Solvents and starting materials for synthesis were purchased commercially and used as received. Elemental analysis for C, H, and N was performed on a Perkin-Elmer 240 analyzer. The infrared (IR) spectra were recorded as KBr pellets on a Nicolet Magna-FT-IR 560 spectrometer in the 4000–400 cm−1 region. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) data were collected with a Rigaku D/max-RC diffractometer using a Cu target (λ = 1.54060 Å) operated at 50 kV and 180 mA. The thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was performed with a Shimadzu TGA-50H TGA analyzer in the range of 25–800 °C under a nitrogen flow at a heating rate of 5 °C min−1 for all measurements. Variable-temperature magnetic susceptibilities were measured on a Quantum Design MPMS-7 SQUID magnetometer.

X-ray crystallographic measurements for 1–3

Suitable single crystals of 1–3 were selected and mounted in air onto thin glass fibers. Accurate unit cell parameters were determined by a least-squares fit of 2θ values, and intensity data were measured on a Bruker Smart CCD with Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at 153 K. The intensities were corrected for Lorentz and polarization effects as well as for empirical absorption based on a multi-scan technique; all structures were solved by direct methods and refined by full-matrix least-squares fitting on F2 by SHELX-97.9 All non-hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic thermal parameters. Aromatic hydrogen atoms were assigned to calculated positions with isotropic thermal parameters, and hydrogen atoms of water molecules were found from the difference Fourier maps and refined using a riding model. Results and discussion

Crystal structure of [Co3Pr2(L)4(C2O4)2(H2O)2·6H2O]n (1)

Single crystal X-ray analysis reveals that 1, 2 and 3 are isostructural, hence, only the crystal structure of 1 is described in detail. Complex 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/c. The asymmetric unit of 1 consists of two independent Co(ii) ions, one independent Pr(iii) ion, two L2− anions, one C2O42− anion, one coordinated water molecule and three free water molecules. As shown in Fig. 1a, all the Co(ii) ions display six-coordinate environment with distorted octahedron geometry. Co1 is coordinated by four O atoms of two C2O42− anions (O1, O1A, O2 and O2A) and two N atoms of two L2− anions (N1 and N1A). Co2 is coordinated by two O atoms of one C2O42− anion (O3 and O4), two O atoms and one N atom of three L2− anions (O5, O9 and N3), and one O atom of one coordinated water molecule (O13). The Co–O/N bond lengths of 2.018(2)–2.186(3) Å (Table S1†) are in the normal range. From Table S1,† it can be seen that the Co–O/N bond lengths in 2 and 3 are similar to those in 1. For the Pr(iii) ion, it displays an eight-coordinate environment with distorted square anti-prism geometry (Fig. 1b). The Pr1 atom is coordinated by O6, O8, O11 and O10 atoms in a square plane, and coordinated by O7, O10, O12 and O11 atoms in another square plane. The Pr–O bond lengths of 2.347(2)–2.653(2) Å are in the normal range (Table S1†). The Ln–O distances slightly decrease from 1 to 3, as well as a showing a slight decrease in their unit cell volume (Table 1), which are consistent with the variation of the radius of lanthanide ions from Pr(iii) to Sm(iii).

In 1, the C2O42− anion can be viewed as a μ2 bridge (Scheme 1, I), which links two Co2 and one Co1 atoms together into a linear Co3 cluster (Fig. 2a), the Co1⋯Co2 distance is 5.349(1) Å and the angle of Co2⋯Co1⋯Co2A is 180°. The completely deprotonated L2− anions all connect two Co(ii) ions and three Pr(iii) ions and adopt two coordination modes (Scheme 1, II and III). For coordination mode II, the L2− anion coordinates to the Co1 atom with the N1 atom, to the Co2 atom with O5, and to three Pr1 atoms with O6, O7 and O8 atoms, both carboxyl groups show bridging coordinated mode. For coordination mode III, the L2− anion coordinates to two Co2 atoms with N3 and O9 atoms, and to three Pr1 atoms with O10, O11 and O12 atoms, of which the O10 and O11 atoms both act as μ2-O bridges, the two carboxyl groups show both bridging and chelating coordinated modes. Along the a direction, the Co3 clusters are linked by L2− anions into a 1D chain with parallelogram cavities, the cavities with dimensions of 5.35(2) × 7.90(1) Å are composed of two Co1 and two Co2 atoms bridged by two C2O42− and two L2− pillars (Fig. 2b). Along the b direction, the Pr atoms are connected by the carboxyl groups into a 1D {Ln–O–C}n rod-like structure with edge-sharing polyhedra (Fig. 2c and 2d), the adjacent Pr⋯Pr distance is 4.0864(7) Å and the Pr1–O10–Pr1 and Pr1–O11–Pr1 angles are 105.01(8) and 106.51(7)°, respectively. Finally, the {Ln–O–C}n rod-like structures are connected with the chains of Co3 clusters by the L2− anions to afford a 3D framework (Fig. 2e). The cavities of Co atoms bridged by C2O42− and two L2− pillars all pack into 1D nanotubular channels along the b direction (A in Fig. 2e), adjacent channels further pack parallel into channel layer in the ab plane, and the {Ln–O–C}n rod-like structures are located between two adjacent channel layers. The total solvent-accessible volume of the channels is 400.6 Å3, which is about 16.6% of unit cell volume (2413.0(8) Å3) as estimated by PLATON.10 The cavity in this complex is a larger one among the heterometallic 3d–4f coordination complexes reported to date (the largest one is about 34% of unit cell volume in the Sm–Zn polymer).11

In recent years, water structure has prompted extensive investigations because of its fundamental importance for human beings, and important role in many chemical and biological systems.12 To date, some discrete water clusters (H2O)n in various hosts have been reported.13 The water molecules can aggregate into water clusters with various topologies via hydrogen bonds depending on the shapes and sizes of channels or cavities.14 The interactions between the hosts and guest water molecules provide the key to understanding the behavior of water molecules.15 Upon further careful inspection of the structure of 1, in the 1D nanotubular channels there exists free water (O14, O15, O16) and coordinated water molecules (O13) which generate an eight-member (H2O)816 cluster via hydrogen bonds (Table S2†). The water cluster is comprised of an octahedral hexamer and two appended coordinated water molecules (O13), as shown in the Fig. 3. The average O⋯O distance in the water cluster is 2.966(7) Å, which is longer than that in ice water (2.77–2.84 Å) and liquid water (2.85 Å).17 The (H2O)8 water clusters occupy the 1D channels with parallel arrangement and are connected with the host framework by the hydrogen bonds of O13–H13B⋯O15 (2.852(6) Å, 158.7°), O14–H14A⋯O5 (2.852(5) Å, 176.4°) and O15–H15B⋯O2 (2.922(6) Å, 121.7°) (Table S2†). Furthermore, the N–H groups of the imidazolyl in the L2− anions also form hydrogen bonds: N2–H2⋯O1 (2.814(4 Å,145.8°), N4–H4⋯O3 (3.017(4) Å, 136.7°). All these hydrogen bond interactions also play important roles for stabilizing the network structure.

In addition, comparing the three structures with other 3d/4f coordination polymers with similar co-ligands, such as [Ni2L2(H2O)4·3H2O]n, [Tb(L)(HL)(H2O)·H2O]n and [Ln2L2(HL)2(H2O)2]n (Ln = Ho, Er, Lu),7a it can obviously be observed that complexes 1–3 have quite different structures from these complexes, which all display 2D structures. To our best knowledge, complexes 1–3 are the first examples of Co–Ln heterometallic complexes based on the H2L ligand.

IR spectra of 1–3

In the IR spectra of compounds 1–3 (Fig. S1†), a strong and broad band at 3393 cm−1, which associates with the O–H stretching vibrations, indicates the presence of water molecules. The characteristic bands of carboxyl groups are shown at 1623 cm−1 for asymmetric stretching and at 1533 and 1474 cm−1 for symmetric stretching. The respective values of (νasym(CO2) − νsym(CO2)) clearly suggest the presence of chelating (90 cm−1) and bridging coordination modes (149 cm−1) of the carboxylate groups.18 The absence of the expected absorption band at around 1700 cm−1 for the protonated carboxylic groups indicates that all carboxyl groups of the ligands in 1–3 have been deprotonated.17 In addition, the peaks at 1592 and 1488 cm−1, as well as those from 795 and 628 cm−1 can be attributed to the imidazolyl of the L2−ligand. This spectral information is consistent with the results of the single-crystal X-ray diffraction analyses.

Thermal analysis and powder X-ray diffraction analysis

Owing to the structural similarity of 1–3, complex 3 was selected to study their thermal stability. The TGA curve of 3 shows two major weight losses, as shown in Fig. S2.† The first weight loss of 8.4% from 25 °C to 177 °C corresponds to the loss of all free and coordinated water molecules (calcd for 8.9% for (H2O)8), and there is no weight loss from 177 to 321 °C. The second rapid weight loss above 321 °C was due to chemical decomposition. The TGA result is consistent with the single-crystal analyses.

The PXRD studies were performed on the crystal samples at room temperature and 180 °C. Comparing the PXRD patterns at 180 °C with those of the samples at room temperature, no collapse can be found for the frameworks after dehydration, as shown in Fig. S3.† The thermal stability from 177 °C to 321 °C and the PXRD studies both indicate that the three frameworks with permanent cavities could be utilized as potential porous materials.

Magnetic studies

Solid-state dc magnetic susceptibility measurements for 1–3 were performed in the temperature range of 2–300 K under a field of 1000 Oe, the χMT versus T and χM−1versus T curves are shown in Fig. 4 and Fig. S3,† respectively. At room temperature, the χMT of complexes 1, 2 and 3 are 11.37, 11.02 and 9.52 cm3 mol−1 K, respectively, which are higher than the expected value of three isolated Co(ii) ions (5.625 cm3 mol−1 K, S = 3/2 and g = 2.0) and the free-ion approximation for one isolated Ln(iii) ion (Pr(iii), 1.60 cm3 mol−1 K and g = 4/5; Nd(iii), 1.64 cm3 mol−1 K and g = 8/11; Sm(iii), 0.09 cm3 mol−1 K and g = 2/7). These higher χMT at room temperature can be attributed to the orbital contribution of the Co(ii) ions19 as also found in many other high spin Co complexes. Below room temperature, the χMT values of the three complexes all gradually decrease to the values of 2.02, 3.13 and 1.73 cm3 mol−1 K with cooling to 2 K, which are typical of antiferromagnetic behaviors.

For the three χM−1versus T curves, as shown in Fig. S4,† they display as straight lines between 20 K and 300 K for 1 and 2, 18 and 300 K for 3, which can be fitted by the Curie–Weiss law [χM = C/(T–θ)] giving the Curie constant values C = 12.62 cm3 mol−1 K and Weiss constant θ = −29.58 K for 1, C = 12.17 cm3 mol−1 K and θ = −31.37 K for 2, C = 10.74 cm3 mol−1 K and θ = −38.01 K for 3. The negative values of θ further indicate the presence of antiferromagnetic behaviors in 1–3.

Conclusion

In summary, we have successfully introduced both oxalic and benzimidazole-5,6-dicarboxylic acid (H2L) with 3d and 4f metal salts to result in three unique Co–Ln heterometallic complexes. In the three complexes, the Co(ii) ions are bridged by C2O42− anions to form linear Co3 clusters, the Ln(iii) ions are connected by carboxyl groups of L2− anions into 1D {Ln–O–C}n rod-like structures with edge-sharing polyhedra. The Co3 clusters and {Ln–O–C}n rod-like structures are linked by the L2− anions to form the 3D frameworks, which present 1D nanotubular channels occupied by (H2O)8 clusters. Magnetic studies of the three complexes all display antiferromagnetic behaviors. Such investigations demonstrate the selection of N/O-donor ligands (H2L) assisted by the short H2C2O4ligand is a useful strategy for the construction of 3d–4f heterometallic frameworks. It is believed that the preliminary results presented in this paper provide a promising access to the rational design and synthesis of 3d–4f heterometallic frameworks.
