Coordination polymers have attracted much attention not only due to their numerous potential applications in catalysis, separation, storage and optics but also because of their intriguing structures and diverse topologies.1 The coordination ability, geometry, and relative orientation of the donor group of the organic ligand, as well as the nature of the metal ions and counterions, play important roles in constructing the framework topologies.2 Most of the coordination polymers reported so far are of the homometallic type, whereas the heterometallic networks involving two or more different metal ions are rare, and the control of their structural dimensionality remains a challenge.3

The paddle-wheel motif comprising two metal centers bridged by four carboxylate or symmetry-related ligands has been investigated extensively, which can be used as a secondary building unit in the formation of 1D, 2D and 3D-coordination polymers.4 In nearly all cases, the paddle-wheel secondary building units were formed in situ.4a Most of the coordination polymers based on the multiple-bonded paddle-wheel motif reported so far show the spacer ligands in the axial positions of the paddlewheel motif (Fig. 1(a)). For example, the 1D structures of type [Mo2(O2CR)4(linker)]n (R = CH3 and CF3) comprise an infinite array of Mo2(O2CR)4 units bridged by various linkers.5,6 A 1D heteronuclear chain has been reported for [{Rh2(O2CMe)4}{Mo2(TiPB)2(nic)2}]∞, in which axial ligation of nic− to the Rh24− unit was shown.7 In the other type of bonding mode (Fig. 1(b)), the paddlewheel unit acts as both a donor and an acceptor, which can be seen in the 3D structure of [Mo2(TiPB)2(nic)2]n (TiPB = 2,4,6-triisopropylbenzoate, nic = 4-isonicotinate)7 and the 2D layer of {Mo2[μ-O2CC6H4-4-P(O)Ph2]4·4EtOH}n.8 The 3D quadruple-bonded metal–organic framework TUDMOF-1 (ref. 9a) formed via equatorial linkage of in situ formed paddlewheel units has been shown to be isostructural to [Cu3(BTC)2] (H3BTC = 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylic acid)9b and [Cr3(BTC)2].9c

The coordination chemistry of metal complexes containing formamidinate ligands has been investigated extensively during recent years.10 We have reported several trinuclear and tetranuclear Cu(i) and Cu(ii) complexes containing anions of N,N′-bis(pyrimidine-2-yl)formamidine (Hpmf).11 Obviously, this type of formamidinate ligand could be improved by having donor sites in different directions to construct coordination polymers with higher dimensionality. By taking into account the above-mentioned factors, we designed and synthesized the dimolybdenum paddlewheel complex [Mo2(4-pyf)4] (4-Hpyf = 4-pyridylformamidine)12 which can form an 8-connected node by the coordination of the dangling pyridyl nitrogen atoms to the other metal complexes, thus forming heteronuclear coordination polymers with higher dimensionalities, as shown in Fig. 1(c) and (d).

The reaction of [Mo2(O2CCH3)4] with the in situ prepared lithium salt Li(4-pyf) in THF gave [Mo2(4-pyf)4], 1.‡ The reactions of 1 with HgBr2 and HgI2 afforded {[Mo2(4-pyf)4(HgBr2)2]·(CH3OH)}n, 2, and {[Mo2(4-pyf)4(HgI2)2]·(CH3OH)}n, 3, respectively, whereas the reaction of 1 with HgCl2 gave [Mo2(4-pyf)4(HgCl2)3.6]n, 4. Complexes 2–4 are insoluble in most of the common solvents. Their structures were characterized by X-ray crystallography and the purity of 2–4 was checked using the powder XRD patterns (Fig. S1–S3).† Several molar ratios of HgX2 to 1 were used to prepare complexes 2–4. The crystals obtained from each experiment were rechecked by X-ray crystallography, showing the formation of the same compound but with different yields. The molar ratios that gave higher yields are 3 for complexes 2 and 3 and 6 for 4 (see experimental details in the ESI† and Table S1). In the structure of 4, the site for one of the HgCl2 units, the one involving Hg(3), is only 30% occupied, which was verified both by X-ray crystallography and elemental analysis. To further confirm the mercury content in the compound, we used SEM-EDS (Scanning Electron Microscopy-Energy Dispersive X-ray Spectrometry) to measure the molar ratio of Hg to Mo (Hg/Mo ratio) in the crystalline samples of 2–4. The result shows that the Hg/Mo ratios of 4 from three samples are 1.75, 1.76 and 1.82, which are similar to the ratio of 1.8 found crystallographically, indicating the existence of one HgCl2 position partially occupied (Fig. S4 and S5 and Table S2).† The absorption spectrum of 1 in CH2Cl2 shows the lowest energy band at 467 nm (ε = 4733 M−1 cm−1) (Fig. S6),† whereas those of 1–4 show the lowest energy bands in the solid state at 518, 554, 594 and 572 nm, respectively (Fig. S10),† which can be assigned to the δ → δ* transitions.10

The structures of 1–3 are shown in Fig. 2 and the coordination environments of the Mo(ii) ions in 1–4 are shown in Fig. S11–S14.† The dimolybdenum moieties of 1–4 are all coordinated by four 4-pyf− ligands, forming the paddle-wheel type structures with the Mo–N bond distances in the range of 2.1434(17)–2.1943(17) Å. The Mo–Mo bond distances in 1–4 are 2.0914(3), 2.0948(11), 2.0965(11) and 2.0990(18) Å, respectively, and the torsional angles looking down the Mo–Mo bonds are 4.0, 3.6, 3.5 and 1.6°, respectively. The small torsional angles indicate that the dimolybdenum units are supported by the quadruple bonds,10 and the coordination of the mercury(ii) halides to 1 hardly changes the bond strength of the quadruple bond. The Hg–N distances in 2–4 are in the range 2.268(10)–2.550(10) Å, which are lower than the upper limit of 2.75 Å for the Hg–N bond,13 and can be regarded as bonds.

In the isomorphous complexes 2 and 3, four mercury(ii) halide molecules are coordinated to the pyridyl nitrogen atoms of two trans 4-pyf− ligands [Hg–N = 2.372(6) and 2.425(7) Å for 2; 2.397(6) and 2.455(7) Å for 3], leaving the two other trans 4-pyf− ligands non-coordinated (Fig. 1(d)), resulting in a 2D double layer with 1D channels (Fig. 2(b)). Guest methanol molecules reside in the voids of 2 and 3 and the solvent-accessible void volumes calculated by the PLATON15 program are 380.5 and 406 Å3, occupying 14.0 and 14.4% of the unit cell volumes, respectively. Using TOPOS14 we can simplify both complexes 2 and 3 to their underlying topology by considering the dimolybdenum unit as a 4-connected node and the mercury(ii) halides as spacers; as a result, we get an ABAB square layer (sql) packing (left side of Fig. 3). In marked contrast to the 2D structures of 2 and 3, complex 4 forms a 3D coordination network, in which eight HgCl2 molecules involving four Hg(1) [Hg–N = 2.40(2) and 2.361(12) Å] and four Hg(2) [Hg–N = 2.268(10) and 2.550(10) Å] are coordinated to the pyridyl nitrogen atoms of the four 4-pyf− ligands of the [Mo2(4-pyf)4] unit (Fig. 1(c) and S9).† Due to the existence of the partially occupied positions for the HgCl2 units that involve Hg(3), two structural types can be evaluated. The inclusion of partially occupied molecules in the 3D framework generates a pentanuclear Hg5Cl10 chain, with the Hg–Cl distances in the range 2.279(16)–2.785(5) Å considered as bonds16 (Fig. 4(a) and (b)), resulting in a novel 3,6,8-connected trinodal net with the point symbol (32·4)2(34·42·63·74·82)(34·69·79·85·9) (Fig. 3). On the other hand, if Hg(3) is not considered, the underlying net becomes the binodal (4,8)-c 4,8T27 with the point symbol (32·53·6)(34·44·512·67·7), which is reported in the TTO database17 of TOPOS for a copper coordination network.18

In summary, the first 2D and 3D heteronuclear coordination networks based on the quadruple-bonded dimolybdenum units containing the formamidinate ligands have been successfully accomplished by the stepwise reactions of the paddlewheel unit 1 and mercury(ii) halides. The molar ratio of Mo and Hg in 4 derived from SEM-EDS matches quite well with that obtained from single crystal X-ray crystallography. The structural types of 2–4 are subject to the change of the halide anions. The bromide and iodide anions play the same role in the crystal structures, while the chloride anion is distinct, suggesting that the size of the halide anion is one of the structure-determining factors.
