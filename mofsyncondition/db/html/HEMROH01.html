Structural and biological studies of organotin(IV) derivatives with 2-mercapto-benzoic acid and 2-mercapto-4-methyl-pyrimidine

Abstract

Four organotin(IV) complexes with the heterocyclic thioamides 2-mercapto-4-methyl-pyrimidine hydrochloride (H2MPMT+Cl−, C5H7N2SCl) and 2-mercapto-benzoic acid (H2MBA, C7H6O2S), of the formulae [Ph3Sn(MPMT)] (1), [Me2Sn(MPMT)2] (2), {[(n-Bu)2Sn(MBA)]2}n (3), already reported in the literature, and {[Me2Sn(MBA)]2}n (4), with a different structure to the one already reported, have been used to study their influence upon the catalytic peroxidation of linoleic acid by the enzyme lipoxygenase (LOX). The IC50 values found lie in the range 20–30 μΜ. The crystal structure of 4 was determined by X-ray diffraction at room temperature (293(2) K). The structure of 4 is polymeric and it consists of {[Me2Sn(MBA)]2} dimeric building blocks in contrast to the complex of the same formula reported earlier which has a hexameric cyclic structure. Intermolecular Sn–O bonding interactions between these units in 4 lead to the formation of infinite chains with helical conformations. In addition the crystal structure of 3, previously reported with R = 9.9%, was now reduced to R = 6.1%. Comparison with the already reported hexanuclear macrocycle complexes {[(n-Bu)2Sn(MBA)]6} (5) and {[Me2Sn(MBA)]6 H2O} (6) to the helical polymeric 3 and 4 was made. DFT calculations performed for the hexameric complex 6 and helical polymeric complex 4 have shown that the energy (ΔΕ) difference between the two polymeric and hexanuclear conformers is only 1.65 Kcal/mol. Finally, the crystal structure of complex 2, previously solved in the orthorhombic Cmc21 space group, was now solved in the monoclinic P21/n space group.

1. Introduction

Organotin compounds have numerous applications and they are commercially used as agricultural biocides due to their anti-fungal properties [1], [2]. In the last few decades organotin(IV) compounds have been investigated for their anti-tumour activity [3], [4]. In the course of our work on the structure–activity relationship of organotin(IV) complexes [5], [6], [7], [8], [9], [10], [11] derived from heterocyclic thioamides such as 2-mercapto-pyrimidine, 2-mercapto-nicotinic acid, and 2-mercapto-benothiazole, we have shown that the antiproliferative activity of the complexes studied corresponded to the regularity of the inhibition of lipoxygenase (LOX) activity. LOX catalyzes the oxidation of arachidonic acid to leukotrienes, in an essential mechanism for cell life [12], [13]. This inhibition activity of tin(IV)–thioamide complexes is independent of the geometry or the number and type of the aryl and alkyl substituents at the tin(IV) ion, but is determined to a great extent by the nature of the ligand [5], [6], [7], [8], [9], [10], [11].

Yin et al. [14] had reported earlier the complexes [R2Sn(MBA)] (R = Me, n-Bu–, Ph–, and 3-Cl–Ph–CH2–), including the crystal structure of the n-Bu derivative {[(n-Bu)2Sn(MBA)]2}n (3) (R = 9.9%) which was helical polymeric. Ma et al. [15], on the other hand, reported the crystal structure of complexes {[(n-Bu)2Sn(MBA)]6} (5) and {[Me2Sn(MBA)]6 H2O} (6), with hexanuclear macrocycle “pseudo-cage” structures stabilized by intermolecular C–H···S weak hydrogen bonding interactions and π–π stacking interactions. Preparation of the named complexes included benzene in the former case (3) and ethanol in the latter case (5, 6) as solvents. EtONa was used in both preparation methods, followed by subsequent re-crystallization of the products obtained in methanol (3) and ethanol (5, 6). A nitrogen atmosphere was also used in the case of 5 and 6. Although, hydrogen bonding and stacking interactions stabilized both the macrocycle and polymeric structures, it would be of interest to know the difference for obtaining either structure, if possible. The complexes {[Ph3Sn(MPMT)]} (1) and {[Me2Sn(MPMT)]2} (2) were also prepared by Ma et al [16] by reacting 2-mercapto-4-methyl-pyrimidine hydrochloride with organtoin(IV) chlorides in benzene solution in the presence of EtONa. Crystals of complexes 1 and 2 were grown from ether and ether–dichloromethane solutions, respectively. All four complexes reported here were prepared again, with a slightly modified method, using a mixture of MeOH/H2O of 1:2 in order to investigate the possible solvent effect in their preparation. Different solvents were also used for their crystallization (see below).

In addition, the inhibitory activity of these four organotin(IV) complexes [14], [16] 1–4, on the catalytic oxidation of linoleic acid to hydroperoxylinoleic acid by the enzyme lipoxygenase LOX is also reported. Such an inhibition may be the cause of the anti-tumor action of tin complexes [12], [13] (Scheme 1).

2. Results and discussion

2.1. General aspects

The synthesis of complexes 1–4 were achieved with an alternative method to the one used previously for the synthesis of these complexes [14], [16]. Instead of using EtONa, a methanolic/water (1:2) solution of the reactants and KOH were used. Crystallizations were achieved by slow evaporation of methanol/acetonitrile (1:1) solutions for 1 and 2, and in the case of complexes 3 and 4 dichloromethane/methanol/toluene (3:1:1) solutions were used.

Both complexes 3 and 4 have a polymeric helical supramolecular structure. The structure of 3 was refined to R = 6.1%, compared to the previous R value of 9.9%. The crystal structure of complex 2 has a monoclinic P21 space group in contrast to the structure already published with an orthorhombic Cmc21 space group [16]. The crystal structure of 1 was found to be identical with the previous one [16].

2.2. 119mSn Mössbauer spectroscopy

The solid state 119mSn Mossbauer spectroscopic data of complexes 1, 3 and 4 are given in Table 1. The two symmetric Lorentzian doublets (50:50) observed for 3 and 4, confirmed the presence of two tin atoms in different chemical environments in the dimers [8], [10], which was also confirmed by the X-ray analysis (vide infra). The ΔEq values of complexes 3 and 4 lie between 2.95 and 3.32 mm s−1 in both complexes, indicating distorted trigonal bipyramidal to octahedral geometries. The two symmetric Lorentzian doublets (75:25) observed for 1 indicate the presence of two structural isomers. The ΔEq value of 1.48 mm s−1 corresponds to a complex with a tetrahedral arrangement (75%) while the ΔEq value of 2.53 mm s−1 to a complex with a trigonal bipyramidal geometry around tin (25%) [2].

The polymeric structure of 4 consists of dimeric building blocks with a distorted trigonal bi-pyramidal arrangement around Sn(IV). The ligand undergoes double deprotonation upon coordination. In the case of the [Me2Sn(MBA)]2 dimer, selected distances are Sn1A–S1A 2.4050(11), Sn1A–O21A 2.235(3) and Sn1A–O22D 2.203(3) Å in the first moiety and Sn1B–S1B 2.4197(10), Sn1B–O21B 2.267(3) and Sn1B–O22A 2.174(3) Å for the second moiety (Fig. 1). The corresponding bond distances in the [(n-Bu)2Sn(MBA)]2 dimer are Sn1A–S1A 2.4141(19), Sn1A–O22A 2.312(4) and Sn1A–O21B 2.191(5) Å for the first moiety and Sn1B–S1B 2.4155(18), Sn1B–O22B 2.1282(6) and Sn1B–O21C 2.216(4) Å for the second. These two units in both complexes are infinitely repeated to form the polymers 3 and 4. The Sn–O and Sn–S bond distances found in 3 and 4 are similar with the corresponding bond distances measured in the complexes 5 and 6 [15].

Selected angles around the Sn1A atom of the first moiety of the dimer [Me2Sn(MBA)]2 are O21A–Sn1A–O22D 171.59(9)° and C11A–Sn1A–C12A 129.89(16)°, while those around the Sn1B atom of the second moiety are O21B–Sn1B–O22A 158.02(10)° and S1B–Sn1B–C12B 125.45(13)°. The corresponding angles around the Sn1A atom of [(n-Bu)2Sn(MBA)]2 are O21B–Sn1A–O22A 177.22(17)° and C11A–Sn1A–C21A 130.35(3)° and those around Sn1B are O21C–Sn1B–O22B 164.35(16)° and C11B–Sn1B–C21B 129.93(3)°. According to Reedijk’s criteria [17] the geometry around a metal center is octahedral when the parameter τ = (β − α)/60 equals zero and when τ is one it is trigonal bipyramidal. In the case of the [Me2Sn(MBA)]2 dimer, the τ values are 0.70 and 0.54 for Sn1A and Sn1B, respectively, while in the case of [(n-Bu)2Sn(MBA)]2 the τ values are 0.78 and 0.57, thus justifying their description as distorted trigonal bipyramidal. The weak Sn···O contacts (Sn1A···O21D = 3.235 and Sn1B···O21A = 3.328 Å in 4 and Sn1A···O22B = 3.076 and Sn1B···O22C = 3.140 Å in 3) confirm the distortions of the geometry (Fig. 1). The intra-molecular hydrogen bonding interactions observed, C3A[H3A]···O22A = 2.719(5) Å in 4 and C13C[H13C]···O22A = 3.471(4) Å in 3, stabilize the structures in our case. π–π interactions were observed only in 4, stabilizing the supramolecular assembly (3.647 Å), (Fig. 2). It is noteworthy that no X–H···O hydrogen bonding interactions were found in the case of complexes 5 and 6, which have macrocyclic structures [15].

The torsional angles Sn–O···O′–Sn′ (namely Sn1B–O22A···O21A–Sn1A, Fig. 1) are −166.18° and 141.98° for 4 and −144.61° and 156.8° in the case of 3 (Sn1A–O22B···O22B–Sn1B). Thus, there is a difference of −24.2° in 4 and −12.2° in 3, resulting in the formation of helical structures. In contrast, the six such torsional angles in the case of 6 are −151.38°, −164.40°, −160.90° followed by 151.38°, 164.40° and 160.90°, compensating the differences and resulting in the formation of hexanuclear macrocycles [15]. Similarly for 5, the six torsional angles are 153.50°, −151.13°, 177.59° and −153.50°, 151.13°, −177.59°.

To study the influence of temperature and solvents in the preparation of complex 4 on its structure (helical polymeric or macrocyclic), XRPD spectra were recorded and compared (Fig. 3). The XRPD spectra of the product with the reaction carried out at 40 °C (Fig. 3A), 20 °C (Fig. 3B) and using EtOH as a recrystallization solvent (Fig. 3C) were extremely similar to the simulated spectrum (Fig. 3D) with the helical-polymer structure and were different from the simulated spectrum (Fig. 3E) of the macrocyclic product reported by Ma et al. [15].

The supramolecular assembly of compound 4 exhibits a helical pattern where in every two asymmetric units appreciable π–π stacking interactions between neighboring phenyl groups exist. The interplanar distance is 3.67 Å and the interplanar angle is 0°, implying completely parallel phenyl groups. The angle formed by the plane of the phenyl ring and the stack column axis is 19.89 °, characterizing this interaction as a parallel displaced non-covalent interaction [18]. It is likely that these π–π interactions play an important role in the stabilization of the molecular units. However this is not the case in the butyl analog 3 where the interaction between the neighboring phenyl groups is considered as point-to-face C–H···π, with the distance between the centroid of the ring and the nearest carbon atom being 3.99 Å and the H···centroid distance being 3.37 Å. On the other hand, the weak intermolecular C–H···S hydrogen bonds together with stronger C–H···O ones, may also play an important role in the supramolecular arrangements.

2.4. DFT calculations

In an attempt to better understand the formation of two structurally different complexes of the formulae {[Me2Sn(MBA)]2}n (4) and {[Me2Sn(MBA)]6 H2O} (6) we have also used computational DFT methods. Due to the requirement of lesser computational resources, only the methyl derivative has been examined here. Ignoring solid-state effects, symmetry plays a significant role to the computed outcome, since non-symmetric structures tend to converge to symmetric ones [19]. This fact led us to follow two different procedures: (i) to fully optimize all the geometrical parameters (ii) to impose symmetry constrains with the intention of improving the approximation of the solid state motif obtained from X-ray diffraction techniques. These constrains concern the dihedral angles of the six-membered rings formed by Sn, S, O, and two C atoms of each of the phenyl rings. As it is evident from the calculated data, although the standard LANL2DZ basis set is known to provide reliable results [20], the 3-21G∗∗ type basis set takes account of polarization functions on all atoms and consequently gives a more precise description of the tin-ligand interactions. The model with no constrains applied gave marginally better results than the one with the preset dihedral angles. The results showed that the energy difference between the two different structures, macrocycle [15] and helical [14], differ only slightly with ΔE = 1.65 Kcal/mol.

Fig. 4 shows the molecular diagrams of complexes 1 and 2.

The crystal structure of complex 2 has now been refined in the monoclinic P21 space group, with no significant differences in the bond distances and angles from those found previously [16]. Thus two sulfur and two nitrogen atoms from thiol ligands are coordinated to Sn(IV) forming a distorted octahedral arrangement around the metal center in case of 2. This coordination mode through N, S donor atoms of the ligand is similar to that observed previously in [Me2Sn(PMT)2] (HPMT = 2mercapto-pyrimidine) [21]. The presence of the methyl substituent, however, at the 5-position in the pyrimidine ring in the case of the 2-mercapto-5-methyl-pyrimidine ligand generates isomerism upon coordination to the tin atom. Thus in the case of 2 the ligand is coordinated through its adjacent and remote forms (Fig. 4B). The Sn–S bond distances are 2.490(3) and 2.496(2) Å, respectively in contrast to the 2.480(2) and 2.484(3) Å reported previously [16]. 2.5. Study of the peroxidation of linoleic acid by the enzyme of lipoxygenase (LOX) in the presence of complexes 1–4

The influence of the complexes 1–4 on the oxidation of linoleic acid by the enzyme LOX were studied over a wide concentration range. The degree of LOX activity (A, %) in the presence of each complex was calculated according to the method described previously [5], [6], [7], [8], [9], [10], [11]. The IC50 values determined are 23 (1), 30 (2), 30 (3) and 30 (4) μΜ, respectively, while no such activity was shown for cisplatin [5], [6], [7], [8], [9], [10], [11]. These values lie at the higher range to those found for other similar Sn(IV) complexes. For example, the IC50 values found for the organotin compounds tested towards LOX, were 26 and 14 μΜ for [(C6H5)2SnCl(HMNA)] and [(C6H5)3Sn(MNA)Sn(C6H5)3 (acetone)] (H2MNA = 2-thiobarbituric acid), respectively [9], 19, 16 and 21 μM for [(C6H5)3Sn(MBZT)], [(C6H5)3Sn(MBZO)], and [(C6H5)3Sn(CMBZT)] (MBZT = 2-mercapto-benzothiazole, MBZO = 2-mercapto-benzoxazole and CMBZT = 5-chloro-2-mercapto-benzothiazole), respectively [6], 10, 13 and 14 μM for [(C6H5)2Sn(CMBZT)2], [(n-C4H9)2Sn(CMBZT)2], and [(CH3)2Sn(CMBZT)2], respectively [6], 61.3, 26.2, 20.5 and 16.9 μΜ for [(CH3)2Sn(PMT)2], [(n-C4H9)2Sn(PMT)2], [(C6H5)2Sn(PMT)2], and [(C6H5)3Sn(PMT)] (PMT = 2mercapto-pyrimidine), respectively, [7] and 25 μΜ for [(n-Bu)3Sn(TBA)·H2O] (HTBA = 2-thiobarbituric acid) [5].

3. Conclusions

The antiproliferative activity of organotin(IV)-thioamide complexes were found to correspond to the regularity of inhibition of the lipoxygenase (LOX) activity [5], [6], [7], [8], [9], [10], [11]. This inhibition activity was also found to be independent of the geometry or the number and type of the aryl and alkyl substituents at the tin(IV) ion, but it is determined to a great extent by the ligand nature [5], [6], [7], [8], [9], [10], [11]. Therefore the IC50 values towards LOX activity found for complexes 1–4 (20–30 μΜ), which lie at the higher range of the corresponding ones for similar complexes, suggest mild or low antitumor activity [5], [6], [7], [8], [9], [10], [11].

On the other hand, slight modifications in the synthetic and crystallization conditions in the case of complexes 3 and 4 results in different conformations of the products, independent of the hydrogen bonding and π–π interactions [15]. The small ΔΕ = 1.65 Kcal/mol between the two conformations (helical or macrocycle) observed in the dialkyltin derivatives with 2-mercapto-benzoic acid may easily be overcome subject to slight solvent or temperature modifications, leading to macrocycles or polymers. Thus, there are inherent physicochemical reasons which lead to the particular packing characteristics of each structure. The self-assembly process of these type of complexes, seems to be fortuity dependent and not based on intermolecular C–H···S weak hydrogen bonds and π–π interactions. 4. Experimental

4.1. Materials and instruments

All solvents used were of reagent grade. 2-Mercapto-benzoic acid and the diorganotin dichlorides (Aldrich) were used with no further purification. Elemental analyses for C, H, N, and S were carried out with a Carlo Erba EA model 1108 elemental analyzer. The 119Sn Mössbauer spectra were collected at various sample temperatures (85–150 K), with a constant acceleration spectrometer equipped with CaSnO3 source kept RT. The calibration of the spectrometer was carried out with a 57Co source and Fe absorber at room temperature. The line widths were very close to the natural width (0.28 mm/s). The content of the tin samples was calculated to be approximately 4 mg Sn in a 2 cm2 area of sample holder. X-ray powder diffraction patterns, from powder derived from the crystals, were obtained using a Bruker AXS D8 Advance diffractometer in Bragg–Brentano geometry equipped with a Cu sealed-tube radiation source (λ = 1.54178 Å) and a secondary beam graphite monochromator.

4.2. Synthesis and crystallization of 1–4

A methanolic (3 cm−3) solution of (C6H5)3SnCl (0.385 g, 1 mmol, (1)) and (CH3)2SnCl2 (0.219 g, 1 mmol (2)) was added to a clear solution of 2-mercapto-4-methyl-pyrimidine (0.162 g, 1 mmol (1) and 0.326 g, 2 mmol (2)) in distilled water (8 cm−3) which was treated with KOH 1 N (2 cm−3, 2 mmol (1) and 4 cm−3, 4 mmol (2)). A white precipitate formed immediately, which was filtered off after stirring for 3 h, washed with 5 ml of cold distilled water and dried in vacuo over silica gel. Complexes 3 and 4 were prepared by a similar method. A suspension of 2-mercapto-benzoic acid (0.308 g, 2 mmol) in distilled water (8 cm−3) was treated with a solution of KOH 1 N (4 cm−3, 4 mmol), giving a clear solution. A methanolic (3 cm−3) solution of the diorganotin(IV) dichloride ((n-C4H9)2SnCl2 0.304 g, 1 mmol, (3) and (CH3)2SnCl2 0.219 g, 1 mmol (4)) was subsequently added. A white precipitate formed immediately, which was filtered off after stirring for 3 h, washed with 5 ml of cold distilled water and dried in vacuo over silica gel. The yields of the preparations are 60% for 1, 2, and 4 and 50% for 3. Crystals of complexes 1 and 2 suitable for X-ray analysis were formed by slow evaporation of methanol/acetonitrile (1:1) solutions while crystals of 1 and 2 suitable for X-ray analysis were formed by slow evaporation of dichloromethane/methanol/toluene (3:1:1) solutions.

4.3. Computational details

Using the crystallographic data reported above as a starting point, we performed geometry optimization at the gradient-corrected density functional theory (DFT) level by utilizing Becke’s three-parameter fit of the exact exchange correlation potential [22] in combination with the gradient-corrected functional of Lee et al. [23], (B3LYP), within the gaussian 03 software package [24]. For this study, the all-electron, split-valence 3-21G∗∗ basis set and the LANL2DZ double-zeta effective core potential basis set were utilized. Attempts to use a mixed basis set (LANL2DZ/6-31G∗∗) for increased accuracy were not successful as the optimization did not meet the convergence criterion. To characterize the nature of the stationary points, a harmonic vibrational calculation was carried out. No imaginary frequencies were computed.

4.4. X-ray structure determination

Data were collected by the ω scan technique in the range 2.6° < 2θ < 28.8° (1), 2.3° < 2θ < 29.0° (2), 2.8° < 2θ < 25.0° (3) and 2.6° < 2θ < 25.0° (4) on a KUMA KM4CCD four-circle diffractometer [25] with a CCD detector, using graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at room temperature. Cell parameters were determined by a least-squares fit [25]. All data were corrected for Lorentz-polarization effects and absorption [26].

The structure was solved with direct methods with SIR-92 [27] and refined by full-matrix least-squares procedures on F2 with shelxl97 [28]. In 4 all non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically, hydrogen atoms were located at calculated positions and refined as a ‘riding model’ with isotropic thermal parameters fixed at 1.2 times (1.3 for methyl groups) the Ueq’s of the appropriate carrier atom. In 3 the n-butyl chains were disordered over two positions. Weak constraints for the molecular geometry were applied; the disordered atoms were refined isotropically, all other non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically.
