Introduction

First row transition metals have been extensively used in the synthesis of paramagnetic polymetallic clusters with the primary aim of exhibiting large ground spin states (S). If coupled with appreciable magnetic anisotropy (negative zero-field parameter D), the cluster will possess a significant barrier to its magnetisation relaxation at low temperatures (DSz2 for integer spin, D(Sz2 − 1/4) for non-integer spin). This memory effect is readily observed by hysteresis loops and AC magnetisation experiments and has been named single-molecule magnetism.1 More recently, reports of single-molecule magnets (SMMs) and high-spin clusters linked by either ionic, covalent or extended H-bonding in the 1D (single chain magnets, SCM),2 2D3,4 and 3D5 are becoming more commonplace. The desire to synthesise such arrays stems from the desire to compare differences (if any) in the magnetism of high-spin/SMM clusters when isolated and when templated in ordered networks.6 The present work focuses on the FeIII (d5, S = 5/2, high-spin) and CoII (d7, S = 3/2, high spin, S = 1/2, low-spin) metal centres and their bonding with the potentially bifunctional diisopropanolamine (dipaH3) ligand (Fig. 1). The dipaH3ligand has three functional groups, all of which are known to bond to 1st row transition metals. It was proposed that this property, coupled with its flexible alkyl chain backbone may result in the dipaH3ligand bridging metal centres within and in-between clusters resulting in high spin networks described earlier.

Both FeIII and CoII have an appreciable number of unpaired electrons in their d shells, and come only second and third, respectively, to manganese as the most used transition metals in the field of single-molecule magnetism. Although antiferromagnetic interactions dominate between FeIII centres, it is still possible for polymetallic FeIII clusters to exhibit non-zero ground spin states through the spin frustration phenomenon. This occurs when competing antiferromagnetic interactions of different magnitudes force certain weaker pairwise Fe2 antiferromagnetic interactions to align their spins ferromagnetically, resulting in non-zero ground spin states and in some cases FeIII based single-molecule magnets.7 Because spin frustration depends on the relative magnitudes of the competing antiferromagnetic interactions (J) in FeIII clusters, the topologies of these systems governs whether spin frustration is observed at all.

Unlike polymetallic FeIII and MnII/III/IV clusters, there are relatively few CoII clusters with appreciable ground spin states.8 CoII-based single-molecule magnets are even rarer, with only two reported to date.9,10 This can be explained by considering that single CoII ions exhibit positive zero-field splitting parameters (D > 0), which when bridged together in high-spin polymetallic CoII architectures will usually project (although not always the case) an overall positive magnetic anisotropy (D) and reduces the likelihood of SMM behaviour.

We report in this work the synthesis, crystal structures and magnetic properties of two spin frustrated Fe6 clusters with S = 5 spin ground states using the diisopropanolamine (dipaH3) ligand. The ground spin state determinations are carried out using DC magnetic susceptibility measurements and AC variable field and temperature reduced magnetisation measurements. Least-square fitting of these data support spin-Hamiltonian parameters of S = 5 and D = 0 cm−1 in both 1 and 2. We also report the synthesis and magnetic properties of the first covalently bonded 1D polymer of mixed valence [CoII3CoIII(OH)(O2CC(Me)3)4(HO2CC(Me)3)2(dipaH)2] (3) tetramers. Additionally, the tetranuclear clusters in 3 are the first to exhibit a CoII3CoIII core, with all previously reported tetranuclear ‘butterfly’ mixed valence systems showing the CoII2CoIII2 oxidation state distribution.11 As proposed, the most interesting aspect that this work illustrates is the bonding flexibility of the dipaH3ligand within and between the clusters reported here. In clusters 1 and 2, the dipaH2− anion bridges FeIII centres in a μ2-manner via one of its alcohol arms, the second arm remaining free from bonding. In 3, however, the doubly deprotonated dipaH2− anion assumes dual roles. As observed in 1 and 2, the dipaH2− anions first role is bridging the CoII/III centres within the [CoII3CoIII] tetramers (this time in a μ3-fashion) via one of its two alcohol arms, however, unlike in 1 and 2, the second alcohol arm links the [CoII3CoIII] tetramers together, propagating the 1D polymeric chains in 3.

Experimental

Physical measurements

Elemental analyses (CHN) were carried out by Campbell Microanalytical laboratory, University of Otago, Dunedin, New Zealand. IR spectra were recorded on a Perkin-Elmer 1600 Series Spectrometer with the samples prepared as KBr discs.

Mössbauer spectra were recorded using a conventional constant-acceleration drive-system with a symmetrical wave-form. The radiation source was 57Co in Rh. Calibration was carried out with natural α-Fe foil. The source was always at room temperature and isomer shifts were given with respect to α-Fe at room temperature. Spectra of complexes examined in this work were fitted using Lorentzian lineshapes unless otherwise described.

Magnetic susceptibility

DC magnetic measurements were carried out on a Quantum Design MPMS 5 SQUID magnetometer calibrated by use of a standard palladium sample (Quantum Design) of accurately known magentisation or by use of magnetic calibrants such as CuSO4·5H2O. A powder sample dispersed in vaseline was contained in a calibrated gelatine capsule held at the centre of a drinking straw at the end of the sample rod in order to avoid torquing of the crystallites. Magnetisation isotherm measurements were made in fields between 0 and 5 T. AC susceptibilities were made using a Quantum Design PPMS instrument with an AC field of 2 Oe and frequencies varying over the range 10 to 1500 Hz, at temperatures between 1.75 and 4 K.

X-Ray crystallographic studies

Data were collected using a Nonius KappaCCD diffractometer with graphite monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). Integration was carried out by the program DENZO-SMN12 and data were corrected for Lorentz polarization effects and for absorption using the program SCALEPACK.12 Solutions were obtained by direct methods (SHELXS-97)13 followed by successive Fourier difference methods, and refined by full-matrix least squares on F2obs (SHELXL-97).13

In complexes 1 and 2 the hydrogens on the bridging μ2-OH− ions (O2–H2A) were restrained at 0.84(1) Å from the corresponding oxygen atoms. In both cases the uncoordinated arms of the dipaH2− anions exhibit disorder over two positions in the ratios 50 : 50 in 1 and 80 : 20 in 2. The hydrogen atoms of both the dipaH2− anions (consisting of one alcohol and one amine H atom) and -O2C(Me)3 anions in 2 were assigned to calculated positions while the methyl hydrogens were allowed to rotate about the C–C bonds. One MeCN molecule position (C42–C43–N2) was located in 1 and refined at 75% occupancy. Two water molecules (O15 and O16) occupy the same region at 25% occupancy. Absorption corrections to the data obtained in 1 were applied empirically using the SADABS program while in 2 numerical methods were employed using the XPREP program.

The contributions from the disordered surrounding solvent molecules in 1, 2 and 3 were subtracted using the SQUEEZE14 program. SQUEEZE calculated approximately 46 electrons per cavity in 1, resulting in the assignment of an extra four MeCN solvent molecules per cluster (two per cavity). In 2 approximately 54 electrons were calculated per cavity, leading to the assignment of one H2O and two MeCN molecules per cluster. In 3 approximately 44 electrons were calculated per cavity, resulting in two MeCN molecules per cluster unit.

In complex 3 the hydrogen atom of the μ3-OH− ion (H7) was restrained at 0.84(1) Å from oxygen atom (O7) and was further idealised using SADI. One of the dipaH2− anions in 3 showed disorder over two sites at one of the carbon atoms (C17) and was therefore split in a ratio of 0.69 : 0.31 (C17, C17′). As in 2, the dipaH2− and −O2C(Me)3protons were assigned to calculated positions. The crystal data for 1, 2 and 3 are summarised in Table 1.

Results and discussion

Reaction of the trinuclear complexes [Fe3O(O2CPh)6(H2O)3](NO3) and [FeIII3O(O2CC(Me)3)6(H2O)3](O2CC(Me)3)) with two equivalents of the dipaH3ligand in MeCN produce the hexanuclear [Fe6O2(OH)2(O2CPh)10(dipaH2)2]·5.5MeCN·1H2O (1) and [Fe6O2(OH)2(O2CC(Me)3)10(dipaH2)2]·2MeCN·3H2O (2) clusters, respectively. The presence of the dipaH3ligand seems to promote the dimerisation of the trinuclear starting complexes with no significant change in the geometry of the triangular {FeIII3O} moieties.

Structures of [Fe6O2(OH)2(O2CR)10(dipaH2)2] (R = Ph (1), C(Me)3 (2))

Clusters 1 and 2 crystallise in the monoclinic P21/n and the orthorhombic Pbca space groups, respectively, but can still be considered isostructural and so will be described here together. Both 1 and 2 exhibit crystallographically imposed inversion symmetry. Their cores comprise of two fused [FeIII(μ3-O)] trinuclear units linked by two bridges, each comprising one μ2-OH− ion (O2) and two μ2-carboxylate bridging ligands (R = Ph (1) and R = C(Me)3 (2)). The cores with respect to the Fe3IIIO units are near planar (the FeIII ions deviate between 0.048 and 0.067 Å from the mean plane); the μ3-O2− ions (O1 and s.e) deviating from the Fe3 plane by 0.2413 and 0.2063 Å, respectively (Fig. 2).

The two bridging μ2-OH ions lie above and below the plane of the molecule (Fig. 2), forming Fe–OH bond distances of 1.9436(17) (Fe2–O2) and 1.9829(19) (Fe3–O2) in 1 and 1.9556(19) (Fe2–O2) and 1.9751(19) (Fe3–O2) Å in 2. The Fe–OH–Fe angles in 1 and 2 are also comparable, producing angles of 122.98(10) and 122.59(10)°, respectively. In each cluster the two dipaH2−ligands bridge the Fe1 and Fe2 metal centres in a μ2-fashion via one of the alcohol arms (O11) while the other remains protonated, free from FeIII ligation and disordered over two sites (O14 and O14′, (Fig. 3)). The aminenitrogen atom is protonated (N1–H1) and bonds terminally to Fe1 with bond lengths of Fe1–N1 = 2.174(2) and Fe1–N1 = 2.162(3) Å in 1 and 2, respectively. The Fe–O and Fe–N bond lengths and Fe–O–Fe angles observed in 1 and 2 are comparable to those formed in previously reported S = 5 Fe6 clusters.18,19 Intra-cluster H-bonding occurs at the centre of the molecule between the μ2-OH− ions and the adjacent bridging carboxylate (O2(H2A)⋯O10′ = 2.795 (1) and 2.949 (2) Å, respectively, (Fig. 3). Selected bond lengths and angles for clusters 1 and 2 are listed in Table 2.

Packing in 1 and 2

The clusters in 1, pack in offset rows along the c axis with large cavities separating each row. Each of the four SQUEEZED cavities in each unit cell are occupied by two MeCN solvent molecules, resulting in an overall 5.5 MeCN solvent molecules per cluster (Fig. 4). The crystallographically located MeCN molecule (at 0.75 occupancy) in 1 weakly H-bonds to a disordered (O14 and O14′) and protonated dipaH2−alcohol arm situated on an adjacent cluster (Fig. 4). The CH3 group is in close proximity to the carboxylate oxygen atoms of the cluster. The position of this MeCN molecule is also occupied by two water molecules at 0.25 occupancy. These are H-bonded to each other and O15 H-bonds to the oxygen atoms of two bridging carboxylates (O15⋯O3 = 3.058 Å, O15⋯O13 = 2.992 Å). 2 packs in the familiar brickwork formation and although when observed along the a, b or c axis they appear to pack in linear rows (Fig. 5, (top)), the brickwork packing pattern is observed along the 1,0,−1 direction (Fig. 5, (bottom)). No inter-cluster H-bonding is apparent in 1 and 2.

DC and AC Magnetic susceptibility measurements

Solid state DC magnetic susceptibility measurements were carried out on powdered samples of 1 and 2 in fields of 2.0, 1.0 and 0.01 T over the temperature range 300–2 K. The powdered samples were contained in a vaseline mull in order to avoid torquing of crystallites. Fig. 6 shows the plots of effective magnetic moment μeff per Fe6vs. temperature for 1 (top) and 2 (bottom). In 1, the μeff values at 300 K of 8.61 μB (χmT = 9.26 cm3 mol−1 K) was lower than expected for the spin-only value (assuming g = 2.0) for six non-interacting FeIII centres (14.49 μB;χmT = 26.24 cm3 mol−1 K), indicating that antiferromagnetic interactions are occurring. As the temperature is decreased, μeff gradually increases to a maximum of ca. 10.66 μB at 28.0 K independent of the field applied, which is consistent with the spin-only μeff value for an S = 5 system (10.95 μB). μeff then drops sharply to a value of 8.08 μB at 2 K in the 1 T field. The μeff values are field dependent below 25 K (Fig. 6) because of Zeeman level (Ms) thermal depopulation effects, possibly combined with zero-field splitting effects (ZFS). Thus, in the 0.01 T field the μeff values plateau at ∼10.6 μB before a small decrease to 10.4 μB at 4.5 K, while the 2 T data shows a rapid decrease to 6.33 μB at 2 K. The magnetic data for 2 are very similar to those for 1, with the room temperature μeff value of 8.94 μB and the maximum (plateau) value below 50 K (10.24 μB (Fig. 6)).

AC susceptibility measurements on 1 and 2 were measured using frequencies of 10 to 1500 Hz and a field of 2 Oe, between 2 and 4 K, and showed a frequency independent Curie-like dependence of the in-phase component χM. The corresponding out-of-phase components χM data were also independent of frequency and close to zero in magnitude. These AC susceptibility data are indicative of no single-molecule magnetic behaviour being exhibited by these S = 5 clusters. This was also the case in the related Fe6 work of Christou et al.18

Magnetisation measurements on 1 and 2

In order to confirm the proposed S = 5 ground spin states observed in 1 and 2, magnetisation (M) measurements were measured on the same samples in a field range of 0–5 T at temperatures of 20, 10, 5.5, 4, 3 and 2 K. Assuming only the ground spin state is populated at very low temperatures, the magnetisation will saturate at a value of gS. In this case, the magnetisation of both samples 1 and 2 come close to saturation at 2 K and 5 T, with M values of 10.58 NμB (1) and 9.51 NμB (2), close to the expected 10 NμB. The data were fitted to a DSz2 Spin Hamiltonian using both positive and negative D values and calculating a full powder average of the magnetisation. Very good fits of all the isotherms were obtained for the parameter sets g = 2.12, D = 0.27 cm−1 (1) and g = 1.94, D = 0.25 cm−1 (2). The 2, 3 and 4 K data and fits are shown for 1 in Fig. 7. Those for 2 are similar. Use of negative D yielded much worse fits in the mid field region, particularly at 2 and 3 K. The values and sign of D are similar to those reported for the related S = 5 Fe6O2(OH)2(O2CBut)10(hep)2 (hep = 2-(2-hydroxyethyl)pyridine) (g = 1.96, D = 0.46 cm−1),18 Fe6O2(OH)2(O2CR)10(Me-hep)2 (Me-hep = 6-methyl-2-(hydroxyethyl)pyridine, R = C(CH3)3 and CH3)19b (g = 2.07, D = 0.27 cm−1) and Fe6O2(OH)2(O2CCH3)10(L)2 (L = 1,1-bis(N-methylimidazol-2-yl)-1-hydroxyethane19a (g = 1.94, D = 0.22 cm−1) clusters.

Rationale for the S = 5 ground spin state in 1 and 2

Clusters 1 and 2 add to a small family of isostructural S = 5 FeIII6 clusters sharing the same [Fe6(O)2(OH)2(O2CR)10(L)2]18,19 moieties. The relatively high spin S = 5 ground state observed in 1 and 2 can be attributed to competing antiferromagnetic interactions (or spin frustration). That is, even though antiferromagnetic interactions predominate in high spin polynuclear FeIII systems, in certain cases the relative differences in the strengths of these interactions lead to the inevitable ferromagnetic (parallel) alignment of spins between certain FeIII centres, leading to spin frustration induced non-zero ground spin states. Whether a cluster exhibits such phenomena is dependent on its topology (Fe–O bond lengths and Fe–O–Fe bond angles observed). A very recent publication by Christou et al.18 proposed that a small structural characteristic in these Fe6 clusters results in the S = 5 ground spin state. It was suggested that the common structural factor between all reported clusters was the relative dispositions of the short monatomic Fe⋯Fe interactions. In the case of clusters 1 and 2, these Fe2 pairwise interactions occur through the μ2-bridging oxygen (O11) from an alcohol arm of the dipaH2− anion, which is illustrated in Fig. 3 ((Fe(1)–O(11)–Fe(2)).

If these frustrated exchange pathways are situated trans to one another then an S = 5 spin ground state was achieved. This hypothesis was confirmed by the synthesis of two S = 5 trans isomer and two S = 0 cis isomer Fe6 clusters.18 Semiempirical calculations (ZILSH)20 and fitting of the magnetic susceptibility data were employed to rationalize the ground spin state in terms of the core topologies (trans vs. cis) present in their structures. The synthesis of the present trans isomer Fe6 clusters 1 and 2 (Fig. 3) and observation of their S = 5 ground spin states give support to the previously described cis vs.trans isomerism hypothesis.

Mössbauer spectra of 1 and 2

The 295 K and 77 K spectra, in zero applied field, of the benzoate cluster 1 are shown in Fig. 8, the spectra of the pivalate 2 being similar. Isomer shift, δ, and quadrupole splitting (ΔEQ) values are given in Table 3. The δ values are indicative of high-spin FeIII but with those of the inner doublet being smaller than normal at ∼0.2 mm s−1. This doublet is due to the peripheral Fe(1) and Fe(1′) atoms being in a FeNO5 environment while the outer doublet, with larger quadrupole splitting (∼0.9 mm s−1), is due to the Fe(2), Fe(3), Fe(2′), Fe(3′) atoms having FeO6 environments. The 77 K spectra of 1 and 2 are identical and have the unusual, broad lineshape shown, the origin of which is not known with certainty but probably involves relaxation effects. The present spectra are very similar to those reported by Hendrickson et al.19a for an imidazolyl-alcohol capped Fe6 cluster in which the capping ligand coordinates in a similar fashion to the dipaH2−ligand.

Synthesis of {[CoII3CoIII(OH)(O2CC(Me)3)4(HO2CC(Me)3)2(dipaH)2]·2MeCN}n (3)

The reaction of the dinuclear complex [CoII2(μ-H2O)(O2CC(Me)3)4(HO2CC(Me)3)4] with two equivalents of dipaH3 in MeCN affords {[CoII3CoIII(OH)(O2CC(Me)3)4(HO2CC(Me)3)2(dipaH)2]·2MeCN}n (3); a covalently bonded 1-D polymer of mixed valence [CoII3CoIII] tetramers. The doubly deprotonated dipaH2− anions not only bridge within the cluster (as observed for dipaH2− in 1 and 2) but also acts as the bridging ligand between the [CoII3CoIII] tetramers, propagating the 1-D chains in 3. The presence of the CoIII ion (Co3) in 3 can be attributed to the aerobic reaction conditions employed.

Structure of 3

The structure of 3 is best described as a covalently bonded polymeric chain of mixed valence [CoII3CoIII] tetranuclear clusters. Each Co4 core in 3 has the chair conformation of the common {M4O2} ‘butterfly’ motif, with one μ3-oxygen (O10) and one μ3-OH− ion (O7–H7) linking the ‘wing-tip’ cobalt centres (Co1 and Co3) to the ‘body’ cobalt ions (Co2 and Co4) (Fig. 9). Bond length analysis and BVS calculations21 strongly indicate three divalent (Co1, Co2 and Co4) and one trivalent centres (Co3). The Co2 centre is five coordinate, exhibiting distorted trigonal bipyramidal geometry while the remaining CoII centres (Co1, Co3 and Co4) are distorted octahedral in geometry (Fig. 10).

The μ3-OH− ion (O7–H7) bridges (assignment confirmed via BVS) Co centres Co1, Co2 and Co4 while the μ3 oxygen atom (O10) is donated by one of the alcohol arms of the doubly deprotonated dipaH2− ligand (linking Co2, Co3 and Co4). The aminenitrogen on this same dipaH2−ligand is terminally bonding to the Co3 ion (Co3–N1 = 1.971(4) Å) while the second alcohol (O9) arm twists away from the [Co4] cluster, instead acting as the bridge to the next [Co4] tetramer (Co1′–O9 = 2.127(3) Å) (Fig. 11). The second dipaH2−ligand bridges within the [Co4] cluster, the two alcohol arms (O8 and O11) bridging in a μ2-manner, linking Co centres Co2 and Co3 (via O8) and Co3 and Co4 (via O11), respectively, (Fig. 12). The aminenitrogen atom on the second dipaH2−ligand (N2) also bonds terminally to the Co3 centre (Co3–N2 = 1.962(4) Å). The presence of amineprotons was determined from (a) charge balance considerations and (b) the observed distorted tetrahedral geometries around the two amine centres. These hydrogen atoms were also confirmed crystallographically. The four deprotonated pivalate ligands bridge in the familiar μ2-mode while the two protonated pivalate ligands bond in a unidentate fashion. One of these protonated carboxylates (O1) strongly H-bonds with a nearby deprotonated pivalate ligand (O17) in the same [Co4] cluster (O1(H1)⋯O17 = 2.585 Å). Selected bond lengths and angles in 3 are listed in Table 4. H-bonding interactions also occur between adjacent [Co4] clusters, namely between the deprotonated alcohol arm of the chain propagating dipaH2−ligand (O9) to a protonated pivalate carboxylate (O15(H15)⋯O9 = 2.592 Å) on the adjacent cluster (Fig. 10).

Packing in 3

The chains in 3 pack parallel to one another, propagating along the 1,0,1 plane (Fig. 11). Each mean plane produced by the μ3 oxygen atoms (O7 (OH) and O10 (dipaH2−) and body centred Co ions (Co2–O10–Co4–O7) in each [Co4] clusters in 3 are approximately perpendicular (∼90°) to the planes of their adjacent clusters, alternating between these two configurations throughout the 1-D chain (Fig. 13).

Magnetism of 3

Plots of μeff, per Co4, vs. temperature are shown in Fig. 14 for three applied fields of 1, 0.1 and 0.01 T. The data are field independent with the μeff values decreasing, gradually, from 7.95 μB at 300 K to a shoulder of ∼3.6 μB at ∼4 K, then a more rapid decrease to reach 2.65 μB at 2 K (Fig. 14). The corresponding χvs. temperature plot is Curie–Weiss in nature between 300 and 4 K, then shows a sharp maximum at 2.5 K, indicative of antiferromagnetic coupling, possibly due to cluster–cluster coupling. The data are generally supportive of the CoII3CoIII formulation in which the CoII centres are high-spin d7 while the CoIII centre is low-spin d6. If the high-spin CoII's are assumed to have orbital degeneracy (4T1g states) and moments of 5 μB and the CoIII assumed to have zero moment, then an uncoupled tetranuclear cluster would have a moment of 8.66 μB. The observed value of 7.95 μB implies weak antiferromagnetic coupling is occurring, a feature further supported by the temperature variation of μeff. In general however, the μeff/T plot is reminiscent of that of uncoupled high-spin CoII octahedral species.

Isothermal plots of Mvs.H, between 2 and 20 K (Fig. 15), do not saturate and yield a value of 2.49 NμB when measured at 2 K and 5 T. This is much reduced from a S = 3/2 value, a feature also often noted in CoII clusters and networks22 and is ascribed to spin–orbit and weak antiferromagnetic coupling effects. The shoulder noted in the μeffvs. temperature plot at 3.6 μB suggests that the cluster has a S = 3/2 ground state, and thus the three CoII centres have competing interactions and consequent spin frustration. This seems reasonable in view of the cluster structure and the variation in bridging pathways between Co1, Co2 and Co4.

A useful model in rationalising the magnetic measurements observed in 3 can be obtained by including a small 2nd order Zeeman contribution to susceptibility (150 × 10−6 cm3 mol−1) for the CoIII (Co3) centre and treating 3 as a μ3-OH bridged trinuclear CoII3(μ3-OH) complex. One remarkably similar trinuclear complex is the previously reported [(py)5CoII3(μ3-OH)(O2CCH3)3(cat)] (cat is the catecholate dianion), by Coucouvanis et al.23 The pyramidal CoII3(μ3-OH) unit is asymmetric, exhibiting one long CoII⋯CoII edge and two shorter edges of approximately equal length. The asymmetry in [(py)5CoII3(μ3-OH)(O2CCH3)3(cat)] gives rise to two types of magnetic exchange interactions (J and J′) (Fig. 16) and therefore this catecholate complex was modelled using the Heisenberg–Van Vleck (−2JSi.Sj) approach24 for a trimer giving best-fit values of J = −1.88 cm−1, J′ = −1.32 cm−1 and g = 2.56.23

The proposed trinuclear model within 3 is also asymmetric, exhibiting one longer CoII⋯CoII edge (3.658(1) Å) and two shorter edges of approximately equal length (Co1⋯Co2 = 3.196(1) Å, Co2⋯Co4 = 3.207(1) Å). The Co–OH–Co angles in 3 are also similar to those observed in [(py)5CoII3(μ3-OH)(O2CCH3)3(cat)] (92.2(4) cf. 105.19(12)°, 92.6(4) cf. 105.50(12)° and 122.8(4) cf. 127.90(13)° in 3) (Fig. 16). Further similarities arise when comparing the μeffvs.T plots in 3 (Fig. 14) and [(py)5CoII3(μ3-OH)(O2CCH3)3(cat)] (μeff room temperature values of 7.95 μB (in (3)) and 8.5 μB in [(py)5CoII3(μ3-OH)(O2CCH3)3(cat)]). These striking similarities between the two systems gave us confidence in fitting the susceptibility data observed for 3 to a similar S = 3/2 trimer model. The data in the 300 to 30 K range could be fitted extremely well to three combinations of parameters, viz. (1) g = 2.50, J = −3.5 cm−1, J′ = −9.7 cm−1, S = 3/2; (2) g = 2.49, J = −7.0 cm−1, J′ = −2.4 cm−1, S = 3/2; (3) g = 2.50, J = −6.4 cm−1, J′ = −4.2 cm−1, S = 1/2. Fits (1) and (2) give a levelling off in calculated μeff values below ∼10 K of ∼4.3 μB because of the S = 3/2 ground state and thus do not reproduce the 30–2 K magnetic moment data. Fit (3), compatible with a S =1/2 ground state, gave very good agreement over the whole temperature range, even reproducing the inflection at ∼3.6 μB (Fig. 17). All three fits yielded energy levels separated from the ground level by a few cm−1. In principle, the magnetisation data of Fig. 15 should help resolve the ground state ambiguity by means of calculations of M in the absence and presence of zero field splitting (DSz2), for the S = 3/2 cases. This analysis assumes the ground state is isolated energetically, which is not the case as judged by the fits from the trimer exchange coupling model. Furthermore, the ‘laddering’ observed and calculated in zero-field split ground states by use of isofield Mvs. H/T plots (at 2 K), can be observed here using only the fit (3) parameters to generate M, and these do not include zero-field splitting. Use of fit (3) parameters to calculate the M isotherms of Fig. 15, gave linear like dependence on H, even at 3 and 2 K, which yielded M in H = 5 T fairly close to those observed, but the curvature of these isotherms was not reproduced. The fits were much better at 10 and 20 K, suggesting possible cluster–cluster interactions are occurring in the chains. Calculations of M, at 2 and 3 K, using the parameters of fits (1) and (2) gave very poor fits, with the calculated curves having the correct shape but being much bigger in size than the observed values. Since simple Brillouin curves for S = 1/2 and S = 3/2 underestimate and overestimate, respectively, the observed curves, it is possible that these spin states are equal in energy, as has been found in {MnIIMnIII2O} triangles.25

Conclusion

We have presented a new multi-functional ligand in cluster synthesis and have shown that the diisopropanolamine (dipaH3) ligand is able to dimerise two trinuclear complexes ([FeIII3O(O2CPh)6(H2O)](NO3) and [FeIII3O(O2CC(Me)3)6(H2O)3](O2CC(Me)3)) thus, producing two new Fe6 clusters exhibiting intermediate ground states of S = 5. Although antiferromagnetic interactions usually dominate in FeIII based clusters, in some cases the topology of these clusters can lead to non-zero (intermediate) ground spin states. This is due to the presence of dominating antiferromagnetic interactions within the cluster, forcing the weaker Fe2 pairwise interactions into aligning ferromagnetically. The topologies of 1 and 2 are isostructural to other previously reported S = 5 hexanuclear clusters sharing the same general formula: [FeIII6O2(OH)2(O2CR)10(L)2]. The S = 5 ground spin states of [FeIII6O2(OH)2(O2CPh)10(dipaH2)2]·5.5MeCN·1H2O (1) and [FeIII6O2(OH)2(O2CC(Me)3)10(dipaH2)2]·2MeCN·3H2O (2) were determined by solid state DC magnetic susceptibility measurements.

As well as dimerising trinuclear FeIII clusters into new magnetically interesting clusters, the synthesis of {[CoII3CoIII(OH)(O2CC(Me)3)4(HO2CC(Me)3)2(dipaH)2].2MeCN}n (3): a 1D polymer of mixed valence Co4 tetramers, illustrates the dual functionality of the dipaH3ligand perfectly. Here the dipaH3ligand not only dimerises the [CoII2(μ-H2O)(O2CC(Me)3)4(HO2CC(Me)3)4] starting material to form the {CoII3CoIII} tetramers in 3 but also brings about polymerisation of these individual tetramers via covalent bonding to form the 1-D polymeric chain.

Solid state DC magnetic susceptibilities measured on a powdered sample of 3, dispersed in vaseline, yield a number of possible fits and corresponding values of the CoII⋯CoIIJ values within a triangular moiety of the CoII3CoIII cluster. Unambiguous identification of the cluster ground state proved difficult to deduce from measurements of magnetisation isotherms obtained at 2 to 20 K. Weak cluster–cluster coupling within the 1-D chains in 3 might play a part.

One question which arises from these findings is why the dipaH2−ligand does not polymerize the FeIII6 clusters in 1 and 2. One explanation is that in order to polymerise these FeIII6 systems the extra −1 charge from the dipaH2− (level of deprotonation observed in 3) would only be possible with the aid of a counter cation which is not available in the synthesis of 1 and 2. However, work is currently underway in this area to test this hypothesis.

In this work we have shown the dipaH3ligand to be an excellent ligand not only in cluster synthesis but in the linking of these clusters into a novel 1-D system. Furthermore, recent interest in the linking of high-spin clusters and SMMs into 1-D, 2-D and 3-D arrays makes this diisopropanolamine (dipaH3) ligand extremely relevant at this moment in time. Work on MnII/III/IV/dipaH3 based clusters is currently underway and will be reported at a later date.
