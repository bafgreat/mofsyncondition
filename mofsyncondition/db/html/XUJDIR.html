Structural variations in terbium(III) complexes with 1,3-adamantanedicarboxylate and diverse co-ligands

Abstract

Terbium nitrate was reacted with 1,3-adamantanedicarboxylic acid (LH2) under solvo-hydrothermal conditions with either N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF) or N,N-dimethylacetamide (DMA) as organic solvents. Hydrolysation of the latter co-solvents resulted in the formation of formate or acetate ions, which are present as co-ligands in the 1D coordination polymer [Tb(L)(HCOO)(H2O)2] (1) and the 2D assembly [Tb(L)(CH3COO)(H2O)] (2). The increase in dimensionality in the latter arises from the higher connectivity provided by acetate versus formate, the L2− ligand being bis-chelating in both cases. The complex [Tb2(L)3(H2O)5][Tb2(L)3(H2O)4]·3H2O (3), another 1D species, crystallizes alongside crystals of 2. Further addition of cucurbit[6]uril (CB6), with DMF as co-solvent, gave the two complexes [Tb2(L)2(CB6)(H2O)6](NO3)2·6H2O (4) and [H2NMe2]2[Tb(L)(HCOO)2]2·CB6·3H2O (5). Complex 4 crystallizes as a 3D framework in which Tb(L)+ chains are connected by tetradentate CB6 molecules, while 5 unites a carboxylate-bridged anionic 2D planar assembly and layers of CB6 molecules with counter-cations held at both portals.

One- to three-dimensional assemblies are formed in terbium(III) complexes with 1,3-adamantanedicarboxylate obtained under solvo-hydrothermal conditions, these species including formate or acetate co-ligands formed in situ, or additional cucurbit[6]uril molecules.

1. Introduction

The use of hydrothermal methods for the synthesis of hybrid metal–organic polymeric species is widespread, in particular in the case of those based on trivalent lanthanide cations, which elicit much interest, mostly for their distinctive optical properties (for some recent reviews on lanthanide–organic species, see, for example [1]). However, an interesting modification of the synthesis conditions consists in using mixtures of water and organic solvents. In the course of an investigation of such solvo-hydrothermal methods directed at the synthesis of new uranyl–organic polymers or frameworks, (for an overview of uranyl–organic frameworks, see [2]) it appeared that the addition of different solvents (acetonitrile, tetrahydrofuran, methanol, N,N-dimethylformamide, N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone) as organic components often had a striking effect on the nature of the species formed [3]. Apart from the most obvious benefit of increasing the solubility of hydrophobic ligands, [3a] the organic solvent may itself be coordinating, such as N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone, [3b,c,e,f] which, somewhat counter-intuitively, does not necessarily lead to a dimensionality decrease. On the contrary, 3D frameworks could be obtained with this solvent/co-ligand in the case of polycarboxylic acids which are known to give lower dimensionality uranyl–organic polymers in its absence [3c,f]. The closely related solvent N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF) is also a potential co-ligand, but, as is well known [4], it readily undergoes hydrolysis under the conditions used, to give formate and dimethylammonium ions. This can be an asset, for example in the synthesis of complexes with cucurbituril ligands, in which the latter cations are included in the final species, while the formate anions are possible co-ligands [5]. Such a study of the solvo-hydrothermal synthesis of uranyl complexes was recently extended to 1,3-adamantanedicarboxylic acid (LH2) [6], a ligand for which few lanthanide complexes have been reported up to now, most of them including also 1,10-phenanthroline and crystallizing as 1D coordination polymers or as a discrete species in one case [7]. However, a 3D framework corresponding to the formula Ln2L3 (Ln=Y, Eu) was also described, which displays a porous structure [8]. All these complexes having been obtained under purely hydrothermal conditions, it appeared worthwhile to investigate the species formed under solvo-hydrothermal conditions. Three complexes were obtained when using either DMF or N,N-dimethylacetamide (DMA) as co-solvents, the crystal structures of which are described herein. Since dimethylammonium cations are formed in situ in both cases, cucurbit[6]uril (CB6) co-ligands, which have a strong affinity for these cations, have been added, resulting in two more complexes, one of them a 3D framework. Cucurbiturils are known as good complexants for lanthanide ions, [9] thus providing useful building blocks for integration in extended or supramolecular systems [10]. Carboxylates or ammoniocarboxylates have previously been used in association with cucurbiturils in order to build mixed species with lanthanide [9l,m,n,p] as well as uranyl cations [5a,c,11], and the present results further illustrate this approach.

2. Experimental section

2.1. Synthesis

Tb(NO3)3·5H2O and 1,3-adamantanedicarboxylic acid (LH2) were purchased from Aldrich, and cucurbit[6]uril pentahydrate was from Fluka. Elemental analyses were performed by MEDAC Ltd. at Chobham, UK.

[Tb(L)(HCOO)(H2O)2] (1): LH2 (11 mg, 0.05 mmol), Tb(NO3)3·5H2O (22 mg, 0.05 mmol), N,N-dimethylformamide (0.3 mL), and deionized water (0.7 mL) were placed in a 10 mL tightly closed glass vessel and heated at 140 °C under autogenous pressure, giving colourless crystals of complex 1 within two days (11 mg, 48% yield). Anal. calcd for C13H19O8Tb: C, 33.78; H, 4.14. Found: C, 33.21; H, 4.00%.

[Tb(L)(CH3COO)(H2O)] (2) and [Tb2(L)3(H2O)5][Tb2(L)3(H2O)4]·3H2O (3): LH2 (11 mg, 0.05 mmol), Tb(NO3)3·5H2O (22 mg, 0.05 mmol), N,N-dimethylacetamide (0.3 mL), and deionized water (0.7 mL) were placed in a 10 mL tightly closed glass vessel and heated at 140 °C under autogenous pressure, giving a mixture of colourless crystals of complexes 2 and 3 within three days.

[Tb2(L)2(CB6)(H2O)6](NO3)2·6H2O (4): LH2 (5 mg, 0.02 mmol), CB6·5H2O (11 mg, 0.01 mmol), Tb(NO3)3·5H2O (9 mg, 0.02 mmol), N,N-dimethylformamide (0.3 mL), and deionized water (0.7 mL) were placed in a 10 mL tightly closed glass vessel and heated at 140 °C under autogenous pressure, giving colourless crystals of complex 4 overnight (14 mg, 67% yield, includes some small crystals of 1). Anal. calcd for C60H88N26O38Tb2: C, 34.33; H, 4.22; N, 17.35. Found: C, 35.40; H, 3.96; N, 18.20%.

[H2NMe2]2[Tb(L)(HCOO)2]2·CB6·3H2O (5): The same experiment as that leading to complex 4 yields colourless crystals of complex 5 when the heating is prolonged for three weeks. Small crystals of complex 1 and crystals of complex 4 are obtained alongside those of 5.

2.2. Crystallography

The data were collected at 150(2) K on a Nonius Kappa-CCD area detector diffractometer [12] using graphite-monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ=0.71073 Å). The crystals were introduced into glass capillaries with a protective coating of Paratone-N oil (Hampton Research). The unit cell parameters were determined from 10 frames, then refined on all data. The data (combinations of φ- and ω-scans with a minimum redundancy of 4 for 90% of the reflections) were processed with HKL2000 [13]. Absorption effects were corrected empirically with the program SCALEPACK [13]. The structures were solved either by usual direct methods with SHELXS [14] or by intrinsic phasing with SHELXT, [15] expanded when necessary by subsequent difference Fourier synthesis and refined by full-matrix least-squares on F2 with SHELXL-97 [14]. All non-hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic displacement parameters. The hydrogen atoms bound to oxygen and nitrogen atoms were found on difference Fourier maps and the carbon-bound hydrogen atoms were introduced at calculated positions in all compounds. All hydrogen atoms were treated as riding atoms with an isotropic displacement parameter equal to 1.2 times that of the parent atom (1.5 for CH3). Special details are as follows:

Complex 1: The oxygen atoms of two carboxylate groups (O1, O2 and O9, O10) are disordered over two positions each, which were refined with occupancy parameters constrained to sum to unity and with restraints on displacement parameters.

Complex 2: Although many crystals have been tested, most were multiple, and it proved difficult to find one with a quality suitable for structure determination. The refinement is not very satisfying; in particular, several atoms have highly anisotropic displacement parameters (O1, C1, C6, C8), but only O1 could be separated into two components related by a mirror plane, so that the disorder present could only partially be taken into account.

Complex 3: ADDSYM (PLATON) [16] indicates possible cell halving along the c axis, but many (0 0 l) reflections with l odd display significant intensity and, further, the solution in the smaller cell is plagued by strong disorder around atom Tb2. Resolution of this disorder necessitates the larger cell, with Tb2 and Tb4 in different environments, in which refinement proceeds smoothly.

Complex 4: The highest residual electron density peak is located near atom O11 of the nitrate counter-ion, which had to be refined with restraints on displacement parameters. The disorder probably affecting this anion could not be resolved. Some voids in the lattice probably indicate the presence of other, unresolved water solvent molecules.

Complex 5: One solvent water molecule was given an occupancy factor of 0.5 in order to retain an acceptable displacement parameter.

Crystal data and structure refinement parameters are given in Table 1. The molecular plots were drawn with ORTEP-3 [17] and the polyhedral representations were prepared with VESTA [18]. The topological analyses were done with the program TOPOS [19]. CCDC-1029635 (for 1), -1029636 (for 2), -1029637 (for 3), 1029638 (for 4), and 1029639 (for 5) contain the supplementary crystallographic data for this paper. These data can be obtained free of charge from The Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre via www.ccdc.cam.ac.uk/data_request/cif.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Synthesis

All the present complexes were synthesized under solvo-hydrothermal conditions with either DMF or DMA as organic solvents. DMF is known to be readily hydrolyzed under the conditions used into dimethylammonium cations and formate anions. Similarly, DMA produces the same cations and acetate anions. Formate or acetate anions are found as co-ligands in complexes 1, 2 and 5, which can be viewed as an example of extremely simple, but highly predictable in situ ligand synthesis [20], the moderately bonding DMF or DMA molecules being converted into strongly coordinating carboxylate anions. A mixture of two species (complexes 2 and 3) was obtained in the case of DMA, with acetate ions being present in 2 only. Dimethylammonium, as well as other ammonium cations, have often been observed to interact with the carbonyl portals of cucurbiturils through ion–dipole and hydrogen bonding interactions, [5] and, as expected, they are present as counter-ions in complex 5. However, this complex was obtained when the heating of the solution was prolonged for several weeks, the crystals formed at first overnight being those of complex 4, devoid of ammonium cations (mixed with some crystals of complex 1 with no CB6 included, which proved that formate anions had already been generated at this stage). On the whole, the outcome of the reaction of terbium nitrate with LH2 appears to be very sensitive to the additional species present, with, moreover, mixtures of crystals obtained in several cases. Thus, it is a system well adapted to the investigation of the effect of diverse reactants, with the drawback that species of comparable stabilities often crystallize together, which may be favoured by the high coordination number of the metal cation and the several coordinating species present, some of them with multiple coordination sites.

3.2. Crystal structures

The complex obtained in water/DMF, [Tb(L)(HCOO)(H2O)2] (1), is represented in Fig. 1. The asymmetric unit contains two independent, but nearly identical moieties. The terbium cations are chelated by two carboxylate groups from two different L2− ligands, and are bound to two oxygen atoms from two bridging bidentate formate anions and two water molecules. The Tb–O bond lengths for chelating and bridging carboxylates are in the ranges 2.325(10)–2.453(5) Å [average 2.39(3) Å] and 2.294(7)–2.370(5) Å [average 2.34(3) Å], respectively, and are comparable to the average values from the Cambridge Structural Database (CSD, 2014 release, Version 5.35), [21] 2.46(6) and 2.35(6) Å. The Tb–O(water) bond lengths [2.362(5)–2.394(4) Å, average 2.378(11) Å] are unexceptional [average 2.41(5) Å from the CSD]. The two cations are thus eight-coordinated and in a square antiprismatic environment. Bridging by formate ions gives rise to linear chains directed along the a axis. Two such chains are linked by double L2− bridges connecting each pair of cations, thus giving a 1D polymer of nanotubular geometry, with however a very small internal space (~4×5 Å cross-section) and with the total point (Schläfli) symbol {4.84.12}{4}{8} (successive symbols for terbium atoms, L2− and formate ligands). The water ligands are directed toward the exterior of the chains and the packing brings the metal rows of four chains (two containing Tb1 and two Tb2, the members of each pair related by an inversion centre) into close contact so that intermolecular hydrogen bonds between the water molecules and carboxylate oxygen atoms (from both L2− and formate ligands) can be formed. A 3D network held by weak interactions is thus built, in which narrow channels lined by the adamantane skeletons of four chains run along the a axis (~5 Å in diameter). The Kitaigorodski packing index (KPI, estimated with PLATON [16]) of 0.71 indicates that no significant free space is present.

The two complexes [Tb(L)(CH3COO)(H2O)] (2) and [Tb2(L)3(H2O)5][Tb2(L)3(H2O)4]·3H2O (3) were crystallized from the same solution, in the presence of DMA as a co-solvent. Allowing for the replacement of formate by acetate, the formula of complex 2 differs only by one water molecule from that of 1, but the structures of these two compounds are completely different. The asymmetric unit in 2 contains half the formula unit, with the terbium atom located on the mirror plane perpendicular to the b axis (Wyckoff position 4c of space group Pnma), this mirror bisecting the L2− and acetate ligands (Fig. 2). The metal atom is chelated by two carboxylate groups from two L2− ligands and also by the acetate anion [it is worth noting that the difference in bonding mode between the bridging bidentate formate in 1 and the chelating acetate in 2 is in keeping with the general trend observed with all lanthanide cations since a search of the CSD gives, in the case of lanthanide formates, 56 occurrences of chelating and 96 of bridging bidentate coordination, while the corresponding numbers for acetates are 453 for chelating and 144 for bridging bidentate]. However, the acetate in 2 is also bridging since the cation is also bound to two oxygen atoms from two more acetate anions. One water molecule completes the nine-coordinated environment, which can be viewed as strongly distorted capped square antiprismatic with O2 in the capping position. The Tb–O bond lengths, in the range 2.368(8)–2.624(6) Å, are unexceptional, the increase with respect to those in 1 being due to the higher coordination number. Both the largest and smallest bond lengths involve the acetate ligand, the former in its chelating and the latter in its bridging coordination mode. The acetate anion thus connects three cations, instead of two for the formate in 1, while the coordination mode of L2− is unchanged and, as a result, an assembly of higher dimensionality is formed. Acetate-bridged chains of terbium cations run along the b axis, with edge-sharing coordination polyhedra, further linking of these chains by L2− ligands giving rise to the formation of a 2D assembly parallel to the bc plane, with the total point symbol {42.6.86.12}{42.6}{8} (for terbium atoms, acetate and L2− anions, respectively). In complex 1, the isolated nature of the terbium centres makes it possible for the two L2− anions attached to each to be located on the same side of the chain, thus enabling the formation of a closed, two-chains species. In contrast, the close approach of metal centres in 2, due to the higher connectivity provided by acetate ions, results in the two L2− ligands pointing on opposite sides of the chains, thus providing the divergent connections leading to the formation of sheets. Adjacent layers along the a axis face each other with a bump-to-hollow match of the protruding adamantane skeletons, and the hydrogen bonds formed by the water molecules with the carboxylate atom O3 are intra-sheet ones. The estimated KPI is 0.72, which shows that no significant porosity is present.

The asymmetric unit in complex 3 contains four independent terbium atoms, with the environments of Tb1 and Tb3 being similar while those of Tb2 and Tb4 are slightly different (see Section 2). Apart from the water molecules, the only ligands are L2− anions, but they display two different coordination modes: either bis-chelating or chelating/bridging bidentate (with one of the former, bound to Tb4, being additionally bridging), as shown in Fig. 3. Tb1 and Tb3 are bound to four chelating carboxylate groups from four different ligands and one water molecule, the nine-coordinated environment being intermediate between capped square prismatic and capped square antiprismatic, with the water molecule in the capping position. Tb2 is bound to four monodentate carboxylate groups from four ligands and four water molecules and the coordination geometry is square antiprismatic with each family of ligands defining a square base. Finally Tb4 is bound to one chelating and three monodentate carboxylates (thus connecting four ligands, as Tb2) and three water molecules, the environment being distorted square antiprismatic with each base containing both water and carboxylate donors. The Tb–O bond lengths, in the overall range 2.244(3)–2.708(2) Å, are unexceptional except for the largest one which corresponds to Tb4–O24, the chelating group being very asymmetric in this case. Two 1D assemblies running along [11¯1] are formed, one corresponding to Tb1 and Tb2 and the other to Tb3 and Tb4. Both chains display an alternation of groups of two ligands, isolated terbium atoms (twice) and groups of two terbium atoms related by an inversion centre with coordination polyhedra either separated (Tb2) or sharing a common edge (Tb4). As a result of the latter difference, the metal···metal separations within the latter dimers are different, 4.2671(3) Å for Tb2 and 3.8927(3) Å for Tb4. The total point symbol for both chains is {42}{43}2{46}{4} (successive symbols for Tb1/Tb3, chelating/bridging ligands, Tb2/Tb4 and the purely bis-chelating ligands). The chains are arranged in tightly packed sheets, with an overall packing of the sheets analogous to that in complex 2. The large number of coordinated and free water molecules (the latter located within the sheets) ensures the formation of an intricate hydrogen bonding network which links adjacent chains in the sheets, while the hydrophobic adamantane skeletons protrude on either side. The estimated KPI, 0.72 (0.70 with solvent excluded), is equal to that in 2, in agreement with the similarity of the packings.

Two complexes were obtained when the reaction was performed in DMF in the presence of additional cucurbit[6]uril molecules, [Tb2(L)2(CB6)(H2O)6](NO3)2·6H2O (4) and [H2NMe2]2[Tb(L)(HCOO)2]2·CB6·3H2O (5), which differ by CB6 being coordinated only in 4 and dimethylammonium cations being present in 5. The asymmetric unit in 4 contains one terbium atom located on the mirror plane perpendicular to the c axis (Wyckoff position 4h of space group Pbam), this plane bisecting the L2− ligands, while the CB6 molecule admits both a mirror plane perpendicular to c and a twofold axis of symmetry parallel to c (Fig. 4). The terbium atom is chelated by two carboxylate groups from two L2− ligands with Tb–O bond lengths of 2.449(2) and 2.460(2) Å, and it is bound to two carbonyl oxygen atoms from two CB6 molecules with a bond length Tb–O3 of 2.357(2) Å, and to three water molecules located in the mirror plane, which gives a nine-coordinated environment of capped square antiprismatic geometry, with the water oxygen atom O8 in the capping position. Coordination of lanthanide cations by cucurbiturils is well-documented, since the first report by Fedin׳s group in 2002 [9]. In the case of CB6, one to three carbonyl groups may be involved in the bonding, only one being coordinated here probably as a result of the bulkiness of the other ligands. The CSD contains six crystal structures of terbium(III) complexes with CB6 or CB7, with Tb–O bond lengths in the range 2.32–2.44 Å [average 2.38(4) Å]. The dicarboxylate ligand in 4 is bis-chelating as in the previous cases, while each CB6 is bound to four cations. Zigzag chains built by carboxylate-bridged terbium atoms run along the a axis direction, which are connected to one another along both the b and c directions by CB6 molecules to form a 3D framework with the total point symbol {4.84.12}2{42.82.102}{8}2 (successive symbols for terbium atoms, CB6 and L2− ligands). There is no channel of significant width since, although the CB6 molecules are arranged into columns, their portals are obstructed by the coordinated species, and the estimated KPI is 0.69 (0.60 with nitrate counter-ions and solvent molecules excluded).

The asymmetric unit in 5 corresponds to half the formula unit, the CB6 molecule being centrosymmetric (Fig. 5). CB6 is not bound to the metal cation, and its two portals are occupied by the dimethylammonium counter-ions which are held by ion–dipole interactions and hydrogen bonding [N13···O(carbonyl) distances in the range 2.808(3)–2.882(3) Å], an usual arrangement [5a,c]. The terbium atom is bound to two carboxylate groups from two bis-chelating L2− ligands and to five oxygen atoms from four formate anions (two of them bridging bidentate and the others chelating and bridging). The Tb–O bond lengths are in the range 2.3432(17)–2.6006(19) Å, the two extreme values corresponding to Tb–O7 and Tb–O7j. The nine-coordinated environment is capped square antiprismatic, with atom O7j in the capping position. Tetra-formate-bridged dimers are thus formed, with adjacent coordination polyhedra sharing one edge (O7–O7j). Such a fourfold formate bridge seems to be unprecedented with lanthanide ions, but it is quite common with d-block metal cations and all formate ions bridging bidentate, a geometry of which more than 40 occurrences are found in the CSD. In complex 5, these dimers are connected to one another by the dicarboxylate ligands to form a 2D assembly parallel to the bc plane, with the point symbol {12}{46.129}{4}2 (for L2−, terbium atoms and formate ions, respectively). These anionic sheets are separated from one another along the a axis direction by layers of counter-ions/CB6 groups. The estimated KPI of 0.75 (0.71 with solvent excluded) shows that there is no significant porosity.

4. Conclusions

The complexes formed by reaction of europium or yttrium nitrates with 1,3-adamantanedicarboxylate (L2−) under purely hydrothermal conditions and in the absence of any additive have previously been shown to crystallize as 3D open frameworks in which each cation is bound to six ligands, with all the carboxylate groups being chelating and bridging [8]. Obviously, solvo-hydrothermal conditions with DMF or DMA as organic solvents are not conducive to the production of such remarkable species and, although a framework was obtained with CB6 co-ligands, it does not display significant porosity. A series of complexes including 1,10-phenanthroline was also reported, but the terminal nature of this co-ligand limits the dimensionality of the species formed, which are generally 1D [7]. The present structures involve bridging formate or acetate co-ligands generated in situ, which enables the formation of both 1D (1, 3) and 2D (2, 5) architectures, the carboxylate groups of L2− being either chelating or bridging bidentate. The overall arrangement in 5, which includes CB6 molecules in close interaction with dimethylammonium cations, is reminiscent of those which have been found in uranyl complexes with carboxylic or sulphonic acids and CB5 or CB6 [5a,b,11b,d]. In all these cases, sheets or columns of CB molecules, either free or complexed, crystallize together with two-dimensional uranyl–organic assemblies, giving hybrid layered materials. In the present series, a 3D framework is generated only in the presence of additional CB6 co-ligands (4). There are some previous examples of compounds including lanthanide ions, CBs and carboxylates; the first to have been reported involve isonicotinate, and display tetranuclear lanthanide assemblies bound to the CB6 portals, the carboxylate anion being coordinated and included in the CB6 cavity [9h,i]. Complexes involving various ammoniocarboxylates [9l,m,p] or sulfonates [9n,o] were also reported, but these complexes are either mononuclear or display sandwich or chain-like arrangements, so that complex 4 is the first example of a 3D framework obtained with such a system.

  • Cucurbit[n]uril/metal ion complex-based frameworks and their potential applications

    2021, Coordination Chemistry Reviews

    Since the concepts of metal organic frameworks and coordination networks were proposed, the preparation of new structures and investigations on their properties has lead to the remarkable development of reticular chemistry and its applications with the basic principle of connecting simple building units to generate periodic frameworks. A rigid cavity with two opening portals make cucurbit[n]urils (Q[n]s) suitable as the building units for frameworks related to Q[n]/metal ion(Mn+) complexes. According to the type of Q[n]/Mn+ complexes used, the frameworks related to Q[n]/Mn+ complexes can be classified as those constructed using i) simple Q[n]/Mn+ complexes, ii) 1D coordination polymers, iii) 2D coordination polymers, and iv) 3D coordination polymers. The construction of frameworks related to Q[n]/Mn+ complexes not only obtains elegant framework structures, but more importantly, can be used to explore the their adsorption characteristics and applications in separation, detection, catalysis, etc., to clarify the relationship between the structure and functional features of the Q[n]/Mn+-based frameworks, and to establish new research branches for Q[n] chemistry.

Especially, study on 1,3-adamantanedicarboxylate sensitizing luminescence of lanthanide(III) ions has not been observed up to now.

    Five one-dimensional (1D) lanthanide dicarboxylate coordination polymers (CPs) have been successfully synthesized under hydrothermal conditions, namely, [Ln2(1,3-adc)3(H2O)4]·xH2O [Ln = Eu (1, x = 1), Tb (2, x = 1), Dy (3, x = 2), and Er (4, x = 1), 1,3-H2adc = 1,3-adamantanedicarboxylic acid] and [Yb(1,3-adc)(1,3-Hadc)(H2O)3]·H2O (5). Property investigation indicates that CPs 1–3 and 5 display characteristic luminescence of the Eu(III), Tb(III), Dy(III), and Yb(III) ions, respectively. CP 3 shows a field-induced slow magnetic relaxation behavior.

  • Electronic structure and spectral properties of terbium(III) nitrate complex with hexamethylphosphoramide

    2017, Spectrochimica Acta - Part A: Molecular and Biomolecular Spectroscopy
    Citation Excerpt :

    Research of excitation processes of electronic f-f-transitions in lanthanide complexes is of considerable interest in fundamental and applied aspects [8–10]. Luminescence intensity of rare earth complex compounds is determined by the efficiency of electronic excitation energy transfer from the levels of ligands onto the metal ion resonance levels, so a search for new ligands promoting the luminescence sensitization is an urgent task of photonics [11–14]. In chemistry and spectroscopy of coordination compounds, phosphorus compounds are especially attractive as ligands [15–20], which are also significant at development of promising optical materials based on phosphorus-containing glasses and ceramics [21,22].

    Spectral properties of terbium(III) nitrate complex with hexamethylphosphoramide have been studied by quantum-chemical methods within the density functional theory and methods of luminescent and X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy. Analysis of the luminescence excitation spectrum of the complex has indicated the absence of intramolecular transfer of electronic excitation energy from the ligand levels to the resonance levels of the rare earth central ion, so luminescence of the complex is associated with the electronic f-f-transitions of Tb3 + ion (transitions 5D4 → 7FJ, J = 3–6). According to quantum-chemical modeling of the excited singlet and triplet levels of the complex, the excitation energy transfer from the ligands onto the central ion does not occur because of the significant difference of energies of their excited states.
