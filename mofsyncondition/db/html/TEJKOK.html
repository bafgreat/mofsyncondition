A series of lanthanide compounds based on mixed aromatic carboxylate ligands: Syntheses, crystal structures and luminescent properties

Author links open overlay panel
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.inoche.2012.03.018Get rights and content

Abstract

A series of lanthanide complexes, [LnL1L21/2(H2O)2]n [Ln = La(1), Sm(2), Gd(3), Eu(4), H2L1 = 2,4-bis(4-methylbenzoyl)isophthalic acid and H2L2 = 2,5-bis(4-methylbenzoyl)terephthalic acid] have been prepared under hydrothermal conditions and characterized by PXRD, IR, elemental analysis and single crystal X-ray diffraction. Complexes 1–4 are isostructural and comprise paddlewheel dinuclear lanthanide secondary building units linked by four carboxylic groups. L1 ligands link the dinuclear lanthanide building blocks to form one-dimensional double-stranded looplike chains. The chains are further connected together to form two-dimensional layers by L2. Furthermore, the luminescent properties of 2 and 4 were also studied.

Graphical abstract

Four isostructural lanthanide compounds constructed with two different substituted aromatic carboxylate ligands have been prepared hydrothermally. 2,4-Bis(4-methylbenzoyl)isophthalic acids link the paddlewheel dinuclear units into 1D double-stranded looplike chains. And the chains are further connected into 2D layers by 2,5-bis(4-methylbenzoyl)terephthalic acids. The luminescent properties of 2 and 4 were also studied.

A great deal of effort has been invested in the design and synthesis of coordination polymers due to their intriguing network topologies [1] and promising applications in the areas of gas storage and separation, magnetic devices, luminescence and catalysis, etc. [2], [3], [4]. Up to now, it is still a great challenge to construct lanthanide-organic frameworks with high stability, though numerous 1D chains and ladders, 2D grids, 3D porous motifs, interpenetrated mode and helical staircase coordination polymers have been synthesized by the judicious choice of appropriate metal ions and multifunctional organic ligands [5]. As functional metal centers, lanthanide metals have a special configuration of 4f electrons and variable coordination environments, which endow the lanthanide compounds with applications especially in optical field [6]. For example, in luminescent realms, Sm, Eu, Tb and Dy were often chosen for their sharp transitions and highly efficient light conversion in the visible range, so that they are one of the most important components in pursuit of new luminescent materials [7].

Considering that lanthanide ions have high affinities for hard donor atoms, the ligands containing oxygen atoms, especially polycarboxylate ligands [8], have been successfully employed to link the nodes of the single metal ions or discrete metal-carboxylate clusters (single nuclear [9], multi nuclear lanthanide clusters [10] or 3d–4f heterometallic clusters [11]) as secondary building units (SBUs) in lanthanide-organic frameworks. Among these polycarboxylate ligands, aromatic multicarboxylic acids such as benzene-dicarboxylic acid [12], benzene-1,3,5-tricarboxylic acid [13] and benzene-1,2,4,5-tetracarboxylic acid [14] have been extensively studied. Some lanthanide coordination polymers constructed by aromatic acid and a secondary ligand (such as N-contained ligands [15], flexible carboxylate ligands [16] and so on) have also been obtained, however, lanthanide-containing compounds constructed with bulky benzoyl substituted aromatic multicarboxylic acids, especially with two different substituted aromatic carboxylate ligands, are seldom reported [17], and their luminescent properties are even more rarely studied. Considering the factors above, it is necessary to investigate the structures and luminescent properties of lanthanide coordination polymers containing two different substituted aromatic carboxylate ligands.

In our previous work, we have used several modified aromatic carboxylic acids, such as 2,4-dibenzoylisophthalic acid, 2,5-dibenzoylterephthalic acid and 4,6-bis(4-methylbenzoyl)isophthalic acid to construct robust MOFs with transition metal ions (such as Co2 +, Ni2 +, Cu2 + and so on) [18]. In the present study, we have successfully employed 2,4-bis(4-methylbenzoyl)isophthalic acid (H2L1) and 2,5-bis(4-methylbenzoyl)terephthalic acid (H2L2) along with a series of lanthanide nitrate salts to generate four new 2D metal–organic polymers [LnL1L21/2(H2O)2]n [Ln = La(1), Sm(2), Gd(3) and Eu(4)] [19]. Moreover, the luminescent properties of 2 and 4 and the thermal stability of 4 were also studied.

Single crystal X-ray diffraction analyses [20] show that 1–4 are isostructural 2D networks and crystallize in triclinic space group Pī. Herein only the structure of 4 will be discussed in detail as a representation. The asymmetric unit of 4 consists of one Eu(III) ion, two halves L1 anions, half one L2 anion and two coordinated water molecules. As shown in Fig. 1a, the central Eu(III) ion is eight-coordinated by four O atoms (O1, O2A, O5 and O6) from three L1 ligands, two O atoms (O3 and O4A) from two L2 ligands and two water molecules (O1W and O2W), respectively, defining a distorted bicapped trigonal prism coordination environment (Fig. 1b). The Eu–O distances range from 2.306(7) to 2.491(7) Å for 4 (La − O range from 2.423(4) to 2.595(4) Å for 1, Sm–O range from 2.318(5) to 2.540(5) Å for 2 and Gd–O range from 2.283(3) to 2.484(3) Å for 3), all of which are comparable to those reported for other Ln − O donor compounds [21]. It can be seen that Ln–O bond lengths slightly decrease as atomic numbers of Ln increase, and the decrement of the average Ln–O distances is only 0.1162 Å from complexes 1 to 4, being consistent with lanthanide contraction rule.

It is less reported that two different substituted aromatic carboxylate ligands coordinated to lanthanide in one complex. In complex 4, L1 and L2 coordinate simultaneously to Eu(III) ions and all carboxyl groups are deprotonated. L1 adopts μ3-quadridentate (C23 carboxyl adopts exhibiting μ1-η1:η1 chelating coordination mode and C24B (B, − 1 + x, y, z) carboxyl shows μ2-η1:η1 bridging fashion) (Fig. 2a) and L2 adopts μ4-quadridentate coordinated mode (C25 and C25C (C, 2 − x, 2 − y, 1 − z) carboxyl both take μ2-η1:η1 bridging coordination modes) (Fig. 2b), respectively. Four μ2-η1:η1 bridging carboxyl groups from two L1 ligands and two L2 ligands, respectively, adopt dimonodentate coordination fashions to link two neighboring Eu atoms into a dinuclear lanthanide paddlewheel SBU [Eu2O2(CO2)6] with the Eu···Eu distance of 4.130 Å (Fig. 3a). L1 ligands bridge the dinuclear lanthanide building blocks into a 1D double-stranded looplike chain along a axis and array on both sides of the chain (Fig. 3b). And then the double-stranded looplike chains are further linked into a 2D layer structure by the two carboxyl groups of L2 with a 44 network in the ab plane (Fig. 3c). In order to facilitate the coordination of lanthanide atoms and carboxyl groups, the toluene groups of the two ligands are all stretched out of the central-benzene rings as shown in Fig. 2a and b. The dihedral angle between the two toluene rings in L1 is 38.289° (Fig. 2a), while the two toluene rings in L2 are parallel to each other (Fig. 2b). It is clear that steric hindrance plays an important role in the whole framework.

The synthesized products of complexes 1–4 have been characterized by powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) (Fig. S1). The experimental PXRD patterns correspond well with the results simulated from the single crystal data, indicating the high purity of the synthesized samples. The IR spectra of complexes 1–4 show similar vibration peaks because of their isostructures (Fig. S2) [22]. Therefore, only the IR spectrum of 4 is described in detail. The strong and broad absorption bands in the range of 3300–3600 cm− 1 are assigned to characteristic peaks of OH vibration. The bands at about 1680 cm− 1 and 1394 cm− 1 are assigned respectively to the asymmetric and symmetric stretching vibrations of carboxylate groups. The absence of ν(COOH) absorption bands of the ligands at around 1700 cm− 1 in all spectra indicates that the carboxyl groups of ligands are completely deprotonated.

Owing to the excellent luminescence properties of Sm(III) and Eu(III) ions, the solid photoluminescence behaviors of complexes 2 and 4 were determined in the visible region at the excitation wavelength of 360 nm under ambient temperature. Fig. 4a displays the typical emission bands of Sm(III) and transition region of partial L1 and L2 ligands in 2 [14]. The peaks at 561, 596, 643, and 700 nm correspond to the transition of Sm(III) from 4 G5/2 → 6HJ (J = 5/2, 7/2, 9/2, 11/2), respectively. Among these peaks, the emission at 643 nm from the 4 G5/2 → 6H9/2 transition is the strongest. This indicates that complex 2 displays red emission. In 2, it should be mentioned that the wide emission bands in the region of 380–550 nm are ascribed to the linkers L1 and L2 transition, indicating that the energy is not fully transferred from the linkers L1 and L2 to Sm(III) ions. Fig. 4b shows the emitting spectra of Eu(III) ion in complex 4. The four peaks at 579, 590, 613, and 689 nm are assigned to the transition of 5D0 → 7FJ (J = 0, 1, 2, and 4). The intensity of 5D0 → 7 F2 is much stronger than the intensity of 5D0 → 7 F1, indicating that the Eu(III) in 4 occupies sites with low symmetry and possess a non-centrosymmetric coordination environment in agreement with the crystal structural analysis [21], [23]. This is also supported by the appearance of the emission 5D0 → 7 F0, which is forbidden in a symmetry field [24]. The 5D0 → 7 F2 transition is predominantly due to the electric dipole character, depending on the nature of bonded ligands around the Eu(III) ions. Whereas the 5D0 → 7 F1 one is mainly sensitive to the magnetic dipole effect, reflecting crystal field environment. The red emission of the 5D0 → 7 F2 transition at 613 nm is the most intense, which suggests that the ligands are suitable for the sensitization of red luminescence of the Eu(III) ion at room temperature.

The isomorphous frameworks lead to the similar thermal decomposition processes (Fig. 5 and Fig. S3), so we took complex 4 (Fig. 5) as representative examples for thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) to study the thermal stability of the complexes. Sample 4 was heated to 1000 °C under N2 atmosphere with a heating rate of 10 °C/min. For complex 4, it shows two obvious steps of weight loss. The first weight loss of 4.65% from 152 to 202 °C corresponds to the loss of two coordinated water molecules (calcd 4.63%). From then on, almost no loss was observed until 400 °C, at which temperature the complex began to decompose. The final residual is Eu2O3. The fact that the compound shows an high thermal stability is noteworthy, in comparison with those of many of MOFs involving 3d transition metals with 2,4-dibenzoylisophthalic, 2,5-dibenzoylterephthalic acid or 4,6-bis(4-methylbenzoyl)isophthalic acid [18a,b]. Employment of oxophilic metals such as lanthanide metals provides potential for construction of MOFs with high thermal stabilities, probably due to the ionic bond character and high coordination number against multidentate ligands.

In conclusion, the lanthanide complexes based on 2,4-bis(4-methylbenzoyl)isophthalic acid and 2,5-bis(4-methylbenzoyl)terephthalic were successfully synthesized in aqueous solution. Complexes 1–4 are isostructural with 2-D nets. On the one hand, the result of this study illustrates that the introduction of the bulky steric hindrance of benzoyl can affect the structure of coordination polymers. On the other hand, the complexes of 2 and 4 exhibit characteristic lanthanide-centered luminescence and may be good candidates for red-light emitting diode devices. Subsequent works will be focused on the construction of novel MOFs by reacting 2,4-bis(4-methylbenzoyl)isophthalic acid and 2,5-bis(4-methylbenzoyl)terephthalic with more metal ions.
