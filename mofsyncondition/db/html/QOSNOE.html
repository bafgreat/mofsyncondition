Introduction

In recent years, studies on the crystal engineering of coordination polymers (CPs) have attracted continuous interest not only for their fascinating structural architectures and topologies but also due to their versatile applicability in catalysis, adsorption, magnetism, molecular recognition, nonlinear optics, and sensors.1 Cobalt-based coordination compounds are a major research focus in this area owing to their interesting magnetic properties and also ever-expanding rich biochemistry.2 In order to design such a type of supra-molecular architecture from combinatorial assembly of complimentary building blocks, several intriguing factors such as the coordination geometry of the metal atom, flexibility of the ligand, solvent system, pH value, temperature, template, ligand-to-metal ratios and counter ions are to be kept in mind.3 The oxidation state in the coordination complexes of cobalt in most cases is either +3 or +2 depending upon the nature of the co-ligands and methods of synthesis. Besides, a few mixed valence (+3/+2) clusters of cobalt ions of various shapes and sizes, from simple linear,2c cubanes,4 and defective dicubanes2d to wheels,5 chinese lanterns,6 discs,7 and rods8 are known in the literature. In most of these complexes, oxidation of the metal centre has been carried out using oxidizing agents like perdisulphates, hydrogen peroxide, iodine etc. during their synthesis.4,9 However, the number of coordination polymers comprised of mixed valence cobalt cores is relatively limited in the literature.10 To date, most of the mixed valence cobalt complexes have been synthesized directly from metal ions and organic ligands, whereas exploitation of a “metalloligand” precursor as a reliable synthon to produce the same has not been encountered. In the very recent past, our group has designed a “metalloligand” strategy to produce mixed valence MnII/III complexes,11 where we used a metal–Schiff base ligand precursor with a definite oxidation state of the metal ion i.e., [MnIIIL12]− (H2L1 = N-salicylidene-l-alanine), which was allowed to react with a second metal centre with another specific oxidation state i.e., [MnII(bpy)2]2+. Such a technique does not involve an in situ change of the oxidation state of the metal ion and thus is expected to afford improved control over the synthesis of the targeted complex.

In recent times, the design and construction of transition metal complexes of Schiff bases derived from amino acids have gained a great deal of attention from researchers for their potentiality to behave as functional models of metalloenzymes.11,12 But, such bio-mimetic activities of amino acid Schiff base complexes of cobalt are not well studied to date.13 One of the most important metalloenzymes that utilizes molecular dioxygen for biological oxidation processes is phenoxazinone synthase (PHS).14 It is an oligomeric pentanuclear copper containing oxidase found naturally in the bacterium Streptomyces antibioticus, and catalyzes the oxidative coupling of two molecules of a substituted 2-aminophenol to the phenoxazinone chromophore in the final step in the biosynthesis of actinomycin D (Scheme 1).15 This is used clinically for the treatment of many tumors, including Wilm's tumor, gestational choriocarcinoma, and many other tumors, where the phenoxazinone chromophore intercalates with DNA, thereby inhibiting the DNA dependent RNA synthesis.16 Structural analysis shows that PHS exists in two oligomeric forms with distinct catalytic activities: low activity dimers and high activity hexamers. Each subunit of the hexamer contains three types of copper-binding motifs: one type 1 (blue), two type 2 (normal), and one binuclear type 3 centre. The fifth copper centre, which is a type 2 copper, is located at a distance of 25 Å from the blue copper and the other normal type 2 copper and has a structural role for maximum catalytic activity (Fig. 1).14b Although a literature survey shows that several CoII or CoIII complexes can mimic phenoxazinone synthase activity, study of such catalytic activity in the case of mixed valence CoII/III complexes is rare.2d,17

Herein, we report the synthesis and X-ray single crystal structure analysis of a series of unique one-dimensional mixed-valence chains {[CoIII(L1)2][CoII(bpy)2]·ClO4·H2O}∞ (1), {[CoIII(L1)2][CoII(phen)2]·ClO4·H2O}∞ (2) and {[CoIII(L2)2][CoII(bpy)(CH3OH)2]·ClO4·H2O}∞ (3) (H2L1 = N-salicylidene-l-alanine, H2L2 = N-salicylidene-l-phenylalanine, bpy = 2,2′-bipyridine and phen = 1,10-phenanthroline) obtained simply by mixing precursor complexes i.e., [CoIII(L1/L2)2]− and [CoII(X)3]2+ (X = bpy for 1 and 3, phen for 2) in methanol. The uncoordinated oxygen atom of the pendent carboxylate arm of the Schiff base in [CoIII(L1/L2)2]− replaces a bpy/phen group in the CoII precursor complex and thus the coordination polymer is formed by a μ1,3syn–anti carboxylato bridge between the [CoIII(L1/L2)2]− and [CoII(X)2]2+/[CoII(X)(CH3OH)2]2+ (X = bpy for 1 and 3, phen for 2) units. To the best of our knowledge, the synthetic procedure, architecture and stoichiometry of the compound are unprecedented among the plethora of reported CoIICoIII species. All three complexes exhibit model bio-mimetic phenoxazinone synthase-like activity under aerial conditions, but their catalytic efficiencies are distinctly different. UV-vis spectroscopy and electrospray ionization (ESI) mass spectroscopy analysis have been performed to determine the possible intermediates of this catalytic reaction and consequently to develop a probable mechanism.

Experimental section

Starting materials

Salicylaldehyde, LiOH, 2,2′-bipyridine, 1,10-phenanthroline, l-alanine, l-phenylalanine and o-aminophenol were purchased from Spectrochem, India and were of reagent grade. They were used without further purification. {[CoII(bpy)3]·(ClO4)2·H2O} and {[CoII(phen)3]·(ClO4)2·H2O} were synthesized using a similar procedure to that described previously.18 The other reagents and solvents were of commercially available reagent quality, unless otherwise stated.

Caution! Although not encountered during the experiment, perchlorate salts of metal complexes with organic ligands are potentially explosive. Only a small amount of material should be prepared and it should be handled with care.

Synthesis of the Schiff base ligands LiHL1 and LiHL2

Lithium salts of monocondensed Schiff base ligands N-salicylidene-l-alanine (LiHL1) and N-salicylidene-l-phenylalanine (LiHL2) were prepared according to a reported procedure.19 To a hot methanolic solution (30 mL) of LiOH (0.41 g, 10 mmol) and l-alanine (0.89 g, 10 mmol) (for LiHL1)/l-phenylalanine (1.65 gm, 10 mmol) (for LiHL2), salicylaldehyde (1.04 mL, 10 mmol) was added. The bright yellow solution was then refluxed for ca. 2 h and allowed to cool. The ligand solution thus produced was used directly for complex formation.

Synthesis of {[CoIII(L1)2][CoII(bpy)2]·ClO4·H2O}∞ (1)

To a methanolic solution (10 mL) of Co(ClO4)2·6H2O (0.366 g, 1 mmol), LiHL1 (2 mmol) and LiOH (2 mmol) in methanol (10 mL) were added. The solution was stirred for ca. 15–20 min and the colour of the solution changed to intense red. To this solution, a 10 mL methanolic solution of {[CoII(bpy)3]·(ClO4)2·H2O} (0.743 g, 1 mmol) was added. The entire mixture was stirred for ca. 45 min and then filtered. The filtrate was allowed to stand overnight till needle shaped dark red X-ray quality single crystals of 1 appeared at the bottom of the beaker. The crystals were collected, washed with a methanol–water mixture and then dried in a desiccator containing anhydrous CaCl2.

Physical measurements

Elemental analysis (C, H and N) of the complexes (1–3) were carried out using a PerkinElmer 2400 series II CHN analyzer. IR spectra of the complexes (1–3) in KBr pellets (4000–500 cm−1) were recorded using a PerkinElmer RXI FT-IR spectrophotometer. UV-vis spectra (1000–200 nm) of the complexes (1–3) in spectroscopic grade methanol solution were recorded using a Hitachi U-3501 spectrophotometer. Electrospray ionization mass spectrometry (ESI-MS positive) ion mass spectra were acquired using a Xevo G2-S QTof (Waters) mass spectrometer, equipped with a Z-spray interface, over a mass range of 100–1200 Da, in continuum mode. Aqueous sodium formate was used for Q-oa-Tof calibration. l-Leucine was used as the external mass calibrant lock mass [M + H]+ = 556.2771 Da. Solutions of complexes were injected at a flow rate of 5 μl min−1.

Crystallographic data collection and refinement

Suitable X-ray quality single crystal of the complexes (1–3) were mounted on a Bruker-AXS SMART APEX II diffractometer equipped with a graphite monochromator and Mo-Kα (λ = 0.71073 Å) radiation. Crystals were positioned at 60 mm from the CCD. 360 frames are measured with a counting time of 5 s. Structures were solved using the Patterson method by using SHELXS 97.20 Subsequent difference Fourier synthesis and least-squares refinement revealed the positions of the remaining non-hydrogen atoms. Non-hydrogen atoms were refined with independent anisotropic displacement parameters. Hydrogen atoms were placed in idealized positions and their displacement parameters were fixed to be 1.2 times larger than those of the attached non-hydrogen atom except in the interstitial water molecules for all the three complexes (1–3) and for the oxygen atoms of the coordinated methanol moieties at the CoII centre in complex 3 where the hydrogen atoms could not be located. Successful convergence was indicated by a maximum shift/error of 0.001 for the last cycle of the least squares refinement. In complexes 1 and 3, the perchlorate and the oxygen atom in the solvent water moiety with very large thermal parameters were refined as disordered each with two sets of sites. The atoms in these sets were refined isotropically with occupation factors of x and 1 − x, x refining to 0.46 and 0.40 for complexes 1 and 3, respectively. Absorption corrections were carried out using the SADABS program.21 All calculations were carried out using SHELXS 14,22 SHELXL 14,23 PLATON 99,24 ORTEP-32,25 and WINGX 1.64.26 For complexes 1 and 3, disorder treatment was carried out by using Olex2.27 Data collection and structure refinement parameters and crystallographic data for the complexes (1–3) are given in Table 1.

Kinetics of the phenoxazinone synthase-like activity

The phenoxazinone synthase mimicking activity of the complexes (1–3) was studied by taking o-aminophenol as the substrate. The reaction was studied spectrophotometrically under aerobic conditions in air-saturated methanol solution by monitoring the increase in the absorption maxima of the phenoxazinone band at 430 nm (ε = 9095 M−1 cm−1) as a function of time (time scan). Various kinetic parameters including the turnover numbers of the complexes (1–3) were determined by measuring time course spectral profiles using variable concentrations of the substrate with a fixed concentration of the complexes under pseudo-first order conditions. All the kinetic measurements were carried out for a period of 10 min and the initial rate of the reaction was determined by linear regression from the slope of the absorbance vs. time plot.

Magnetic measurements

All the magnetic measurements for the finely powdered samples of complexes 1, 2, and 3 were carried out using a superconducting quantum interference device vibrating sample magnetometer (SQUID-VSM, Quantum Design) under an applied DC magnetic field. Magnetic susceptibilities were measured in the temperature range 2–300 K at a constant magnetic field. Diamagnetic contributions to the experimentally found susceptibilities were corrected using Pascal's tables.28

Results and discussion

Syntheses

Coordination complexes of cobalt with a Schiff-base derived from amino acid ligands are very rare.13 Here, we have prepared the lithium salts (LiHL1 and LiHL2) of the Schiff-base (H2L1 and H2L2) by the condensation of l-alanine or l-phenylalanine and salicylaldehyde, respectively, according to the reported procedure.19 In an open atmosphere, during the reaction with LiHL1/2, the metal centre gets oxidized and this results in the formation of [CoIII(L1/2)2]− where CoIII is hexa-coordinated by the coordination of two binegative tridentate Schiff base ligands (L(1/2)2−). Interestingly, one of the oxygen atoms of the carboxylate group of each amino acid in the Schiff base ligand within the [CoIII(L1/2)2]− unit remains uncoordinated and in the presence of CoII precursors i.e., {[CoII(bpy)3]·(ClO4)2·H2O} and {[CoII(phen)3]·(ClO4)2·H2O}, respectively, links with the CoII centre by replacing the bpy/phen group, to form the μ1,3syn–anti carboxylato bridged one-dimensional chain complexes (Scheme 2).

IR and UV-vis spectra of the complexes (1–3)

The three polymeric complexes were initially characterized by elemental analysis followed by FT–IR spectroscopy. Due to the presence of the azomethine group (CHN), sharp bands were found around 1600 cm−1 for all the complexes. The carboxylate group of the amino acid part of the Schiff base shows characteristic symmetric stretching at 1645 cm−1 (for 1), 1648 cm−1 (for 2), and 1645 cm−1 (for 3) and antisymmetric stretching at 1454 cm−1 (for 1), 1445 cm−1 (for 2), and 1459 cm−1 (for 3). The non-coordinated perchlorate gives a sharp peak around 1088 cm−1 for all the three complexes. In addition, the appearance of a broad band around 3400 cm−1 in the spectrum of 1, 2, and 3 can be attributed to the presence of water molecules found in the interstitial site of the complexes (Fig. S1–S3, ESI†).

UV-vis spectroscopic studies of the complexes were done in the wavelength range 200–1000 nm in the solution state using spectroscopic grade methanol as a solvent. All three complexes show a broad peak corresponding to a d–d transition at 670, 669, and 668 cm−1 respectively. In addition to these, absorption peaks at 380 nm (for 1), 379 nm (for 2), and 378 nm (for 3) are observed due to ligand to metal charge transfer and at 246 nm and 221 nm (for 1), 241 nm and 214 nm (for 2), and 267 nm and 227 nm (for 3) due to intra-ligand charge transfer transitions (Fig. S4, ESI†). The peak maxima along with the corresponding molar extinction coefficient values of the above three complexes are presented in Table S1 (ESI†).

Description of the structures of the complexes (1–3)

In all three structures, the dipositive cobalt based mononuclear units [CoII(X)2]2+/[CoII(X)(CH3OH)2]2+ (X = bpy for 1 and 3, phen for 2) are connected to tripositive cobalt based mono-anionic mononuclear units [CoIII(L1/2)2]− (HL1 for 1 and 2, HL2 for 3) to give rise to a mixed valence 1D zigzag chain together with a discrete perchlorate anion and an interstitial water molecule. However, the perchlorate anions and the water molecules are not involved in any close interactions with the metal atoms. Bond Valence Sum (BVS) calculations have been performed to assign the oxidation states of the two cobalt centres.29 The BVS values obtained on the basis of bond lengths are 3.43/3.41/3.38 and 1.77/1.76/1.86 for the Co1 and Co2 centres, respectively, in complexes 1–3. However, the reason behind the high values of BVS for the CoIII (Co1) centre in all the three complexes is not certain. It is to be noted that there have been examples of high BVS values of a CoIII centre in mixed valence systems.29d

{[CoIII(L1)2][CoII(bpy)2]·ClO4·H2O}∞ (1)

The asymmetric unit of 1 consists of a mononuclear [CoII(bpy)2]2+ unit connected to a [CoIII(L1)2]− anionic unit, as shown in Fig. 2 together with the atomic numbering scheme for the metal coordination spheres. Selected bond lengths and angles are summarized in Table 2.

Within the asymmetric unit, both the metal centres (CoII and CoIII) adopt distorted octahedral geometry. The equatorial plane of the CoIII centre (Co1) is formed by phenoxido oxygen O2, carboxylato oxygen O3, and imine nitrogens N2 and N1, and the axial positions are occupied by phenoxido oxygen O1 and carboxylato oxygen O5, all from two chelating deprotonated Schiff base ligands. The bond lengths in the equatorial plane fall in the range of 1.873(8)–1.930(8) Å for Co–O and of 1.870(12)–1.871(10) Å for Co–N. The root mean squared (r.m.s.) deviation of the above four basal donor atoms (O2, O3, N2, and N1) from their mean plane around Co1 is 0.023 Å, and Co1 deviates from this mean plane by 0.024(4) Å in the direction of the axial phenoxido oxygen O1 of the Schiff-base ligand. On the other hand, the Co2 centre is coordinated by two oxygen atoms O4a and O6 (symmetry operation: a = 1 − x, −1/2 + y, 2 − z) from the pendant carboxylate groups of the Schiff-base ligands with Co–O bond lengths 2.068(8) and 2.085(9) Å, respectively, and the nitrogen atoms (N3, N4, N5, and N6) from two chelating bpy moieties with Co–N bond lengths ranging from 2.122(13) to 2.164(13) Å. Here, the r.m.s. deviation of the four basal donor atoms, N3, N4, N6, and O4a, around Co2 is 0.019 Å, where Co2 deviates from this mean plane by 0.065(7) Å in the direction of the axial carboxylato oxygen atom (O6). The pyridine moieties of bpy are not in the same plane and make an angle of 6.98° with respect to each other. These two alternating units, i.e., CoIII and CoII, constitute the syn–anti carboxylato bridged mixed valence one-dimensional helical chain structure, which is a left handed helix. The crystal packing in complex 1 shows that the perchlorates are involved in intermolecular hydrogen bonding to these 1D helical chains to generate a two-dimensional network (Fig. 3). The interaction parameters are listed in Table S2 (ESI†).

{[CoIII(L1)2][CoII(phen)2]·ClO4·H2O}∞ (2)

The structure of 2, shown in Fig. 4, is isomorphous to that of 1. The bond lengths, listed in Table 2, are also similar to complex 1. The Co1, O2, O3, N2, and N1 atoms that constitute the equatorial plane with Co–O distances of 1.871(7)–1.921(6) Å and Co–N distances of 1.871(10)–1.885(10) Å show an r.m.s. deviation of 0.030 Å. The Co1 centre deviates in the direction of the axial phenoxido oxygen atom (O1) from this mean plane by 0.034(5) Å. Hexa-coordination around the CoIII centre is further satisfied by O1 and O5 with corresponding bond distances of 1.878(7) and 1.910(7) Å, respectively, at the axial positions. Here also, the two pendant oxygen atoms O4a and O6 (symmetry operation: a = 1 − x, −1/2 + y, −z) of the carboxylate groups coordinate at Co2, with corresponding bond distances of 2.080(6) and 2.098(7) Å, respectively. The coordination mode of Co2 in complex 2 is very much similar to that in complex 1 and the Co–N lengths are within the range 2.123(9) to 2.147(11) Å. The r.m.s. deviation of the four basal donor atoms (N3, N4, N6, and O4a) from their mean plane around Co2 is 0.044 Å where Co2 deviates from this mean plane by 0.065(7) Å in the direction of the axial carboxylato oxygen atom (O6). The pyridine moieties of phen make an angle of 1.66° with respect to each other.

The bridging of the mononuclear units in 2 to form a helical one-dimensional polymer and their further connection to build up a two-dimensional framework is also similar to that in 1, as shown in Fig. S5 (ESI†) with the interaction parameters listed in Table S3 (ESI†).

Synthesis of {[CoIII(L2)2][CoII(bpy)(CH3OH)2]·ClO4·H2O}∞ (3)

The structure of 3 is also very similar to that of 1 and 2, with a slight difference in the coordination environment around Co2, as shown in Fig. 5.

The coordination environment around Co1 is similar to that of the previous two complexes with O1, O2, N2a (symmetry operation: a = 1/2 + x, 3/2 − y, −z) and N1 atoms in the equatorial plane and the axial positions coordinated by O5a and O8a, all of them coming from chelating Schiff-base ligands. The bond lengths, listed in Table 2, are also similar compared to complex 1 and 2. The r.m.s. deviation of the four basal donor atoms (O1, O2, N2a, and N1) from their mean plane around Co1 is 0.200 Å, where Co1 deviates from this mean plane by 0.051(7) Å in the direction of the axial phenoxido oxygen atom O8a. The dipositive metal centre i.e., Co2 is coordinated by two donor oxygen atoms (O3 and O4) coming from the pendant carboxylate groups of the tridentate Schiff base ligands and two donor nitrogens (N3 and N4) of chelating bpy at the equatorial site, whereas the axial positions are occupied by oxygen atoms (O6 and O7) from the coordinated methanol molecules unlike the cases of the two other complexes (1 and 2) with corresponding bond distances of 2.103(8) and 2.102(8) Å respectively. The r.m.s. deviation of the four basal donor atoms (N3, N4, O3, and O4) from their mean plane around Co2 is 0.053 Å, where Co2 deviates in the direction of the axial oxygen atom (O7) of the solvent molecule from this mean plane by 0.005(2) Å. The pyridine moieties of bpy are not coplanar, but rather make an angle of 7.30° with respect to each other. In the case of complex 3, the carboxylato bridged 1D zigzag chain is further stabilised through two intra-molecular hydrogen bonding interactions both involving the phenoxido oxygen atoms and one of the (–CH2) benzylic hydrogen atoms of the (–CH2C6H5) group of the Schiff base ligand. One is between O1 and H19A coming from C19 and the other one is between O8 and H9B coming from C9. In addition, two strong intra-molecular CH⋯π interactions observed between the bpy moiety and the π cloud of the phenyl ring of the Schiff base also contribute in the stabilization of complex 3 (Fig. 6). The geometric features of the interactions are listed in detail in Tables S4 and S5 (ESI†), respectively.

It is to be noted here that in the literature there are several examples of coordination complexes of various metal ions derived from amino acid Schiff base ligands.12 However, very few coordination complexes of cobalt and amino acid Schiff base ligands are reported.13 Most of them except one have cobalt in the (2+) oxidation state and a very few of them are structurally characterized. Huang et. al in 2006 first reported the crystal structure of a mononuclear CoII complex derived from the Schiff base of 5-formylsalicyldehyde and glycine.13c Later, Li et. al in 2012 reported the structure of two CoII coordination polymers.13a One is obtained from the Schiff base of o-hydroxynaphthaldehyde and glycine and the other has l-alanine instead of glycine in the amino acid residue. Rodionova et al. first reported a binuclear mixed valence CoII/III complex from the Schiff base of 5-chloro-2-hydroxy-3-nitrobenzaldehyde and l-valine. This complex has a bis-chelated monoanionic CoIII species linked with a penta-aqua CoII centre via the pendent carboxylato oxygen of the amino acid coming from the Schiff base ligand.13d Recently, we have developed a unique “metalloligand” strategy to get a mixed valence MnII/III complex11 and in the present work a series of mixed valence CoII/III 1D coordination polymers have been reported using [CoIII(L1/L2)2]− and [CoII(X)3]2+ precursors, by employing the same technique.

Phenoxazinone synthase-like activity and kinetic studies

The phenoxazinone synthase mimicking activity of the complexes (1–3) was studied by observing the oxidation of o-aminophenol spectrophotometrically in dioxygen-saturated methanol at 25 °C as reported previously (Scheme 3).11

Before detailed kinetics studies, the catalytic activity of the complexes (1–3) for the oxidation of o-aminophenol (OAPH) to 2-aminophenoxazin-3-one (APX) in air saturated methanol was examined. For this purpose, equal volumes of a 1 × 10−4 M solution of the complexes (1–3) and a 1.0 × 10−2 M solution of the substrate were mixed in dioxygen saturated methanol at 25 °C and UV-vis spectra of the mixtures were recorded at every 5 min time interval. The changes in the spectral pattern during the wavelength scan for a period of ∼1 h (at 5 min intervals) after the addition of OAPH are shown in Fig. 7 for complex 2. Similar plots for 1 and 3 are shown in the supporting information, Fig. S6 and S7 (ESI†), respectively. The spectral scans reveal the continuous increase of the peak intensity at ca. 430 nm, characteristic of the phenoxazinone chromophore, signifying the oxidative dimerization reaction of OAPH in aerobic conditions. It may be noted here that a blank experiment without catalyst does not show significant growth of the band at 430 nm in identical reaction conditions, Fig. S8 (ESI†).

In order to get insight into the catalytic efficiency of the complexes (1–3), a detailed kinetic study is performed. For this purpose, equal volumes of a 1.0 × 10−4 M solution of the complexes and different concentrations of o-aminophenol (Table S6, ESI†) were mixed under aerobic conditions and the time scan at the maximum band (i.e., at 430 nm) of 2-aminophenoxazin-3-one was carried out for a period of 10 min and the initial rate was determined by linear regression from the slope of the absorbance vs. time plot, considering the value of the extinction coefficient of 2-aminophenoxazin-3-one as 9095 M−1 cm−1 in methanol.17d The initial rate of the reaction vs concentration of the substrate plot shows rate saturation kinetics with increasing concentration of the substrate as depicted in Fig. 8 for complex 2 and for complexes 1 and 3 in Fig. S9 and S10 (ESI†), respectively. The Michaelis–Menten model was then applied to get a Lineweaver–Burk plot as well as to evaluate kinetic parameters Vmax, KM, and kcat (Table S7, ESI†) for such rate saturation kinetics. The turnover numbers (kcat in h−1) of the present catalysis were obtained by dividing the value of Vmax by the concentration of catalyst used and are reported to be 1.2, 11.5, and 2.7 for complexes 1–3, respectively. Thus, it is to be noted that the turnover number (kcat in h−1) for the oxidation of OAPH follows the order 2 > 3 > 1.

ESI-mass spectrometric study

In order to investigate the probable complex–substrate intermediate as well as to draw mechanistic inference on the phenoxazinone synthase-like activity during the oxidation of o-amino phenol, we have recorded the ESI-MS spectra of the complexes (1–3) and a 1 : 1 mixture (v/v) of the above complexes with the substrate in methanol within 10 minutes of mixing. We focused here only on the spectrum of complex 2 along with its 1 : 1 mixture with OAPH during catalysis. For complex 2, the spectrum shows peaks at m/z 209.53 (calc. 209.53), 270.99 (calc. 270.73), 451.06 (calc. 451.03), 613.43 (calc. 613.20), 657.45 (calc. 657.09), 872.11 (calc. 872.05), and 894.10 (calc. 894.16) corresponding to [CoII(phen)2]2+, [CoII(phen)2(H2O)5(CH3OH)]2+, [CoIII(L1)(ClO4)(H2O)2(CH3OH) + H]+, [CoIII(L1)2(CH3OH)3(H2O)4 + 2H]+, [CoIII(L1)2(CH3OH)3(H2O)4 + 2Na]+, [CoIII(L1)2CoII(phen)(ClO4)(H2O)2(CH3OH) + Na]+, and [CoIII(L1)2CoII(phen)2(CH3OH)]+ species, respectively (Fig. S14, ESI†). The nature of the ESI-MS spectra changed drastically after addition of OAPH to the solution of complex 2 in methanol (Fig. S18–S22, ESI†). The peaks at m/z 209.53 (calc. 209.53) and 270.99 (calc. 270.73) are retained in the mass spectrum (Fig. S18, ESI†) but the intensity is severely reduced as compared to the mass spectrum of complex 2 itself indicating the participation of the CoII centre of the complex in formation of the catalyst substrate intermediate. The spectrum shows a base peak at m/z = 346.04 (calc. 346.04) (Fig. S19, ESI†) assignable to [CoII(phen)(OAP˙−)]+. This peak along with those at m/z = 347.04 (calc. 347.05) (Fig. S19, ESI†) and 369.07 (calc. 369.03) (Fig. S20, ESI†) corresponding to formation of the species [CoII(phen)(OAP−)]+ and [CoII(phen)(OAP2−) + Na]+, respectively, explains the substrate–catalyst binding via replacement of one phen moiety in the CoII centre. In addition to these, the peaks at m/z = 301.21 (calc. 301.15) (Fig. S21, ESI†), 431.06 (calc. 431.07), and m/z = 450.06 (calc. 450.09) (Fig. S20, ESI†) due to [CoIII(L1)(CH3OH)(H2O)]+, [CoIII(phen)(L1)]+, and [CoII(phen)(L1)(H2O) + H]+, respectively, indicate the participation of the CoIII centre during oxidative dimerization of OAPH. In addition, the peak due to the protonated 2-aminophenoxazine-3-one (APX) appears at m/z 213.06 (calc. 213.05) (Fig. S22, ESI†) indicating catalytic conversion of the reactant to the product. The explanation of the peaks in the mass spectra of complexes 1 and 3 after addition of OAPH is similar to that of complex 2. The differences in the peak positions in the mass spectra of complexes 1 and 3 with respect to those of complex 2 after addition of OAPH are explainable on the basis of changes in the coordination environment around the CoII and CoIII centres in terms of the chelating ligand (for complexes 1 and 3 it is bpy and for complex 2 it is phen) and the amino acid part in the Schiff base ligand (for complexes 1 and 2 it is l-alanine and for complex 3 it is l-phenylalanine) and also the nature of the coordinated solvent in the respective metal centers. During the catalysis process by all the complexes (1–3), we have checked that no H2O2 is formed as a by-product, so, water is produced rather as a side product.

Mechanistic insight

On the basis of thorough ESI-mass spectrometric study, a probable mechanism for the oxidative dimerization reaction is proposed here for complex 2. Complex 2 initially dissociates in solution into its two constituent fragments i.e., [CoIII(L1)(CH3OH)(H2O)]+ (A) and [CoII(phen)2]2+ (B). After the addition of OAPH, the substrate loses its phenolic hydrogen and gets coordinated to the CoII centre, substituting one of its chelated phen moieties to form [CoII(phen)(OAP−)]+ (D). The replaced phen ligand then gets coordinated to the CoIII centre to form [CoIII(phen)(L1)]+ (C). Then, in the next step, one of the protons from the amino (–NH2) group in D is lost to form [CoII(phen)(OAP2−) + Na]+ (E). E is then converted into [CoII(phen)(OAP˙−)]+ (G) with a concomitant reduction in the metal centre in C to form [CoII(phen)(L1)(H2O) + H]+ (F). In the next step, the species G releases the radical anion OAP˙−, which then undergoes oxidative coupling in the presence of another molecule of the substrate to form the product APX along with simultaneous re-oxidation of the metal centre in F to form C. The metal centre in [CoII(phen)]2+ again becomes able to accommodate another molecule of OAP− to form D to start the catalysis process again. During the entire process, 3/2O2 is reduced to form 3H2O.

We have checked whether our synthetic precursors i.e., Co(bpy)22+ and Co(phen)22+ are also active in the present catalysis reaction. However, no significant increase of the phenoxazinone band under identical reaction conditions like that of complexes 1–3 at 430 nm after 30 minutes is observed (Fig. S11 and S12, ESI†). This clearly validates the presence of cooperativity between two mixed valence metal centres in this model bio-mimetic oxidase process of phenoxazinone synthase. The CoII centre here remains redox innocent and acts as a Lewis acidic centre to bind the incoming substrate molecule whereas the CoIII centre here is redox active and is involved to transfer electrons from the substrate to molecular oxygen. The probable scheme of the catalysis is shown in Scheme 4. Recently, our group has reported a similar type of mixed valence MnII/III coordination polymer that shows high phenoxazinone synthase-like activity. The probable mechanistic pathway described here is also very much similar to that of the previously reported one.11

It is to be noted here that, in the literature, some mono/di/trinuclear cobalt complexes with tetradentate or tridentate ligands are known to exhibit phenoxazinone synthase-like activity (Table S8, ESI†). The kcat values for such complexes are reported to be in the ranges 3.3 h−1 to 511.2 h−1 for CoII,2d,17a,17b,30a and 3.8 h−1 to 29952.0 h−1 for CoIII complexes.17b–g,30b–h,31 In the recent past, our group has reported for the first time a trinuclear mixed valence CoII/III complex showing phenoxazinone synthase-like activity with a kcat value of 153.6 h−1.17h Then, some more mixed valence Co complexes were reported that also exhibited phenoxazinone synthase-like activity with relatively lower kcat values.17d–g But, mechanistic detail was not provided in any study. Complexes 1–3 in our present study are the first example of mixed valence cobalt coordination polymers showing phenoxazinone synthase-like activity and detailed mechanistic insight is provided here.

Comparison of the phenoxazinone synthase-like activity

Although on the basis of the ESI-mass spectra it seems that the probable mechanism for the catalysis by complexes 1–3 is similar, the turnover number (kcat) is highest for complex 2 followed by complexes 3 and 1, respectively. Detailed investigations with copper or cobalt based model complexes of phenoxazinone synthase-like activity have shown that various factors may influence their catalytic activity, such as the metal–metal distance, conformational flexibility of the ligand, nature of substitution of the ligand, nature of the exogenous bridging ligand and coordination geometry around the metal centre.17,30–32 Therefore, a close look at the X-ray structure of the three complexes (1–3) can guide us for a probable explanation of the difference in catalytic efficiency.

The more extended π electron conjugation in the phen ring as compared to bpy makes the former a better π acceptor than the latter. Thus, the negative charges over the electronegative oxygen and nitrogen atoms in the catalyst–substrate intermediates (intermediates D, E and G) after introduction of OAPH in the reaction are more stabilized for complex 2 due to the greater extent of charge delocalization via the CoII centre in the presence of the phen ring as compared to that of complexes 1 and 3. The increase in negative charge density in the metal centre during reduction of C to F is also accelerated in complex 2 due to this better π acceptor property of the chelated phen moiety. This explains the greater stability of the intermediate species in the catalysis for complex 2, which drives the reaction to the forward side and accounts for the highest turnover number for complex 2.

Now, between complexes 1 and 3, 1 is least effective in terms of catalytic output as evinced from its lowest (kcat) value. For complex 3, there is one chelating bpy group along with two easily replaceable solvent molecules around the CoII centre, whereas in complex 1 there are two bpy moieties. Thus, attack of the substrate molecule on the CoII centre is more favorable in complex 3. In addition to this, the presence of the –CH2C6H5 group in the amino acid part of the Schiff base ligand around the CoIII centre makes the reduction of the metal centre faster during catalysis due to its (−I) effect. However, this reduction is not so spontaneous in complex 1 due to the (+I) effect of the –CH3 group in l-alanine.

The factors responsible for the least efficiency of complex 1 are also present in complex 2 but the much enhanced stability of the reaction intermediate species during catalysis i.e., D, E and G due to greater stabilization of the negative charge over nitrogen and oxygen atoms by a better π acceptor phen ring neutralizes those factors and drives the reaction to the forward side with the best catalytic efficiency.

Magnetic properties

The temperature dependent magnetic susceptibility for the complexes (1–3) was investigated in the temperature range 2–300 K in an applied field of 0.05 T. The χMT vs. T plots are shown in Fig. 9 and Fig. S23 and S24 (ESI†) for complexes 1–3, respectively. Since all the complexes are one-dimensional chains, each asymmetric unit having two consecutive Co centres was taken into account in order to calculate the value of χM. At room temperature, the χMT values of 1–3 range from ∼2.3–2.6 cm3 mol−1 K, which is significantly larger than the spin-only value (1.874 cm3 mol−1 K, g = 2.0, S = 3/2) for CoII high spin centres, suggesting a reasonable orbital contribution to their magnetic moments.33 When the sample is cooled down, the χMT values gradually decreased to ∼1.5 cm3 mol−1 K. The other adjacent CoIII centre is likely to be low spin and diamagnetic. Thus, the CoII/III moiety under consideration is expected to be behaving as a single CoII high spin centre. As a consequence, the magnetic data have been analyzed by the following Hamiltonian. Ĥ = αλ·Ŝ + Δ[z2 − 2/3] + βH(α + gŜ) (1) Here, λ is the spin–orbit coupling and α is the orbital reduction factor defined as α = Aκ. The reduction of orbital momentum due to the delocalization of an unpaired electron is defined by the parameter κ and the A parameter is defined as the admixture of the upper 4T1g(4P) state with the 4T1g(4F) ground state. The presence of the A factor is due to the T–P isomorphism.34,35Δ represents the crystallographic splitting of ground state 4T1g terms for their (CoII centres in 1, 2 and 3) respective ligand environments.

The experimental magnetic data were fitted using PHI36 software and the best-fit parameters in the entire temperature range are the following: α = −1.27, λ = −162 cm−1, Δ = 488 cm−1, TIP = 496 × 10−6 with R = 1.9 × 10−5 for 1; α = −1.23, λ = −165 cm−1, Δ = 472 cm−1, TIP = 1358 × 10−6 with R = 1.3 × 10−5 for 2; and α = −1.21, λ = −172 cm−1, Δ = 473 cm−1, TIP = 357 × 10−6 with R = 2.6 × 10−5 for 3.

Conclusion

In this present work, we have successfully employed the “metalloligand” strategy to synthesize three new mixed valence one-dimensional chains of alternating CoII and CoIII units using a tridentate Schiff base derived from l-alanine/l-phenylalanine and salicylaldehyde along with neutral N,N-donor chelating ligands bpy/phen and structurally characterize them. In each complex, the metal centres i.e., CoII and CoIII are connected through a μ1,3syn–anti carboxylate bridge. All of them catalyze the oxidation of o-aminophenol to the corresponding phenoxazinone derivative and these are the first examples of mixed valence CoII/CoIII coordination polymers to exhibit phenoxazinone synthase activity. The kcat value of complex 2 is significantly high in comparison with the other two complexes. The highest catalytic activity of complex 2 can be attributed to the presence of the enhanced π acceptor property of the phen ring, which stabilizes the reaction intermediates, unlike the cases of complexes 1 and 3, where the phen group is replaced by a relatively less conjugated bpy moiety. During catalysis, it is evident from the mechanistic point of view that for all three complexes the CoII centre binds to the o-aminophenol, while CoIII shows redox activity, indicating the efficacy of the cooperative effect of the metal centres of two different oxidation states.
