Introduction

In the past decades, coordination polymers (CPs) with novel topologies and potential applications have attracted great attention in the fields of coordination chemistry and crystal engineering.1 Many factors affect the construction of CPs, including the central metal ion, reactant molar ratio, nature of organic ligands, systematic pH value and reaction temperature etc.2 Among these factors, the selection of organic ligands plays a crucial role in the self-assembly process of the desired CPs. The structural changes of the organic ligands, including the angle, length, flexibility and functional substituent groups, can lead to the formation of diverse coordination frameworks.3 Aromatic dicarboxylates, as an important family of multidentate O-donor ligands, such as 1,2-benzenedicarboxylic acid (1,2-H2BDC), 1,3-benzenedicarboxylic acid (1,3-H2BDC), and 1,4-benzenedicarboxylic acid (1,4-H2BDC), have been widely used to construct new CPs owing to their strong coordination ability, versatile coordination modes and high stability,4 and numerous coordination complexes with fascinating structures and interesting properties have been obtained.5 However, the reports on the complexes based on 5-aminoisophthalic acid (5-H2AIP) are still limited.6 The amino group of 5-H2AIP not only can coordinate to metal ions, but also as an electron-donating substituent may have some effect on the electron density and coordination of the 5-H2AIP. So, it is worth investigating the CPs based on the ligand 5-H2AIP.

Recently, the utilization of mixed-ligand systems based on carboxylates and N-donor ligands has been proved to be an effective strategy to construct more intricate CPs.7 Among the numerous N-donor ligands, introduction of the bis(pyridylamide) ligands into metal–carboxylate systems for the generation of intriguing CPs with various structures has been relatively less explored, especially for the flexible bis(pyridylamide) ligands with different –(CH2)n– spacers.8,9 Recently, Cao's group have prepared a complex Zn(2,6-ndc)0.5(L1)0.5(SO4)3·(CH3NHCH3) by using a flexible bis(pyridylamide) with a –(CH2)4– spacer (L1 = N,N′-di(4-pyridyl)adipoamide) and 2,6-naphthalene dicarboxylic acid (2,6-H2ndc) mixed ligands, which displays a 2D → 3D inclined polycatenation framework based on a 44-sql layer.9a By replacing 2,6-H2ndc with 1,4-H2BDC, Chen et al. have obtained two interpenetrated diamondoid networks {[Zn(L1)(1,4-BDC)]·H2O}n and {[Cd(L1)(1,4-BDC)]·2H2O}n, which show distorted cages with 8- and 9-fold interpenetrating modes. Lately, Chen et al. have obtained another complex constructed by a flexible bis(pyridylamide) with a –(CH2)6– spacer (L2 = N,N′-di(4-pyridyl)octanediamide),9d [Ni2(L2)(SDA)2]·6H2O (H2SDA = 4,4′-sulfonyldibenzoic acid), which exhibits a 2D → 3D inclined polycatenation framework with the new (42·68·8·104)(4)2 topology. By using a longer bis(pyridylamide) ligand with a –(CH2)10– spacer, Chen et al. generated a compound [Zn2(L3)(1,4-BDC)2]·2CH3OH (L3 = N,N′-di(4-pyridyl)dodecanamide), which shows a 3-fold interpenetrating framework with the pcu topology.9b More recently, our group has reported a series of copper(ii) complexes constructed from a flexible bis(pyridylamide) and different polycarboxylates as mixed ligands.9e–g To the best of our knowledge, the complexes based on the flexible N,N′-di(3-pyridyl)octanediamide (L) with –(CH2)6– spacer and carboxylates as mixed ligands have not been reported so far, which prompted us to initiate this research.

As an extension of our previous work on the assembly of metal–carboxylates–bis(pyridylamide) mixed ligand systems,8a–c,9e–g in this work, the flexible ligandL is purposefully selected as the main ligand and three dicarboxylates serve as the auxiliary ligands to assemble with CoII and CuII ions, aiming at investigating the role of polycarboxylates in the assembly of the transition metal coordination complexes. As a result, six cobalt(ii)/copper(ii) complexes with different dimensionality and structures, namely, [Co(1,2-BDC)(L)1.5(H2O)] (1), [Co(1,3-BDC)(L)(H2O)2]·H2O (2) [Co(5-AIP)(L)0.5(H2O)]·2H2O (3), [Cu(1,2-BDC)(L)] (4), [Cu(1,3-BDC)(L)(H2O)]·3H2O (5), and [Cu2(5-AIP)2(L)2(H2O)]·6H2O (6), have been hydrothermally synthesized. The effect of the dicarboxylates and central metal ions, as well as the conformation of ligandL, on the structural diversity of the title complexes has been systematically investigated. Moreover, the electrochemical properties of the title compounds bulk-modified carbon paste electrodes, the solid state fluorescence properties of the six compounds and the photocatalytic activities of compounds 1, 3, 4 and 5 have been investigated.

Experimental section

Materials and characterization

The ligandL was synthesized by the previous reported literature method.10 All chemical reagents were purchased from Aladdin and used without further purification. IR spectra (KBr pellets) were performed on a Varian FT-IR 640 spectrometer for 2 and a Magna FT-IR 560 spectrometer for 1, 3–6. The elemental analyses (C, H and N) were carried out on a Perkin-Elmer 240C elemental analyzer. The fluorescence spectra were measured on a Hitachi F-4500 fluorescence spectrophotometer. Thermogravimetric data for the title compounds were collected on a Pyris Diamond thermal analyzer under N2 atmosphere (heating rate 10 °C min−1). The electrochemical experiments were carried out using a CHI 440 Electrochemical Quartz Crystal Microbalance. A conventional three-electrode cell was used at room temperature, and the title compounds bulk-modified carbon paste electrodes (CPEs) were used as working electrodes.11 An SCE and a platinum wire were used as the reference and auxiliary electrodes, respectively.

X-Ray crystallographic study

Crystallographic data for compounds 1–6 were collected on a Bruker APEX II area-detectordiffractometer with Mo Kα (λ = 0.71073 Å) by ω scan mode. The data were collected at 296(2) K, and there was no evidence of crystal decay during data collection. These structures were solved by direct methods utilizing the SHELXS program of the SHELXTL package and refined by full-matrix least-squares methods on F2 using the SHELXTL package.12 Cobalt(ii) or copper(ii) ions in the compounds were located from the E-maps. Most non-H atoms have been refined anisotropically. However, the O1W, O1, and C25 of compound 2 and O10, C40, C41, C42, and C43 of compound 6 have been refined isotropically. The O10 of compound 6 were disposed by half-occupancy method. The O1W, O1 and C25 of compound 2, C7, C8 and C9 of complex 4 and O10, C40, C41, C42 and C43 of complex 6 were disposed by the disorder method. The distances between C41′ and C42′, as well as C43 and C42 were restricted by ‘dfix’ command in complex 6. Results and discussion

Description of crystal structures

[Co(1,2-BDC)(L)1.5(H2O)] (1)

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis shows that compound 1 is a 3D supramolecular network based on a 1D coordination polymeric chain. As depicted in Fig. 1a, the crystallographically independent Co(1) ion is six-coordinated by three pyridylnitrogen atoms (N(1), N(3), N(6)#1) belonging to three Lligands [Co(1)–N(1) = 2.1752(17) Å, Co(1)–N(3) = 2.1988(17) Å, Co(1)–N(6)#1 = 2.2092(18) Å], two oxygen atoms from two carboxyl groups of one 1,2-BDC ligand [Co(1)–O(4) = 2.0762(15) Å, Co(1)–O(7) = 2.0593(14) Å], and one oxygen atom of a coordinated water molecule [Co(1)–O(1W) = 2.1345(17) Å], showing a distorted octahedral geometry. In compound 1, ligandL displays two different conformations, La and Lb (see Chart 1). Two Lb ligands link two CoII ions forming a Co2Lb2 dinuclear loop with the size of 20.114 × 4.919 Å2. The adjacent Co2Lb2 dinuclear loops are connected by the Laligands to build a 1D [Co–L1.5]n chain, in which the Laligands and Co2Lb2 dinuclear loops are alternating and the non-bonding distances of Co⋯Co are 19.273 Å (La) and 20.114 Å (Lb), respectively (Fig. 1b, Chart 1). Although the ligand 1,2-BDC adopts a bis(monodentate) mode (Chart 1), its two carboxyl groups coordinate with one CoII ion, so it does not play the role of extending the dimensionality of compound 1. Based on the isomeric ligand of L, Chen's group has reported a complex [Ni2(L2)(SDA)2]·6H2O with 2D → 3D polycatenation structure,9d in which the L2ligands showing only one conformation connect the neighbouring metal NiII ions to form a 1D infinite linear chain, then the SDA anions bridge the adjacent chains to build the 2D network. Compared with the compound [Ni2(L2)(SDA)2]·6H2O obtained by Chen's group,9d the Lligand in compound 1 shows greater flexibility. The results highlight that the various conformations of ligands and the diverse carboxylates play an important role in the formation of the final polymeric structures. In compound 1, these neighbouring 1D chains are further extended into a 3D supramolecular structure via two types of hydrogen bonding interactions: one is between the nitrogen atoms N(5) of the amide groups and the carboxyl oxygen atoms O(6) of 1,2-BDC [N(5)–H(2)⋯O(6) = 2.9275 Å, 167°], the other is between the carboxyl oxygen atoms O(5) of 1,2-BDC and the oxygen atoms from coordinated water molecules O(1W) [O(1W)–H(1WA)⋯O(5) = 2.7471 Å, 171°] (Table S7, ESI†), as shown in Fig. 1c.

[Co(1,3-BDC)(L)(H2O)2]·H2O (2)

Compound 2 crystallizes in a triclinic system with the P space group and displays a 2D (4,4) grid coordination network with sql topology. As shown in Fig. 2a, the asymmetric unit of compound 2 contains two crystallographically independent CoII ions [Co(1) and Co(2)]. Each CoII ion adopts distorted octahedral coordination geometry, completed by two nitrogen atoms and four oxygen atoms. The coordination modes of Co(1) and Co(2) are similar. Thus the coordination environment of the Co(1) ion has been taken as an example and discussed in detail. The Co(1) is six-coordinated by two pyridylnitrogen atoms of two Lligands [Co(1)–N(1) = Co(1)–N(1)#1 = 2.211(6) Å], two carboxyl oxygen atoms from two 1,3-BDC ligands [Co(1)–O(8) = Co(1)–O(8)#1 = 2.074(5) Å], and two oxygen atoms belonging to two coordinated water molecules [Co(1)–O(1W) = Co(1)–O(1W)#1 = 2.101(6) Å], respectively. The Lligands connect adjacent CoII ions in a bidentate mode to form a 1D zigzag chain with a Co(1)⋯Co(2) distance of 19.65 Å (Chart 1, Fig. S1a, ESI†). Meanwhile, the CoII ions are bridged by the 1,3-BDC ligands to form a 1D linear chain (Fig. S1b, ESI†). Obviously, the two kinds of 1D chains crossed with each other by sharing CoII ions, generating a 2D (4,4) grid-like sql network (Fig. 2b). The 2D grid is not planar but undulated, resulting from the inherent bent conformation of the L molecules and 1,3-BDC ligands. The schematic of the 2D polymeric layer of compound 2 is shown in Fig. 2c. The adjacent 2D networks are linked by the N–H⋯O hydrogen bonds between the amide groups of Lligands and carboxylic oxygen atoms of 1,3-BDC ligands [N(2)–H(2B)⋯O(6) = 3.1158 Å, 165°], constructing a 3D supramolecular framework (Table S8, ESI,† and Fig. 2d).

[Co(5-AIP)(L)0.5(H2O)]·2H2O (3)

Crystal structure analysis reveals that compound 3 is a 2D polymeric network. The asymmetric unit consists of one CoII ion, half of an Lligand, one 5-AIP ligand, one coordinated water molecule and two lattice water molecules. As shown in Fig. 3a, the CoII ion is six-coordinated by a pyridylnitrogen atom from one Lligand [Co(1)–N(1) = 2.162(2) Å], one aminonitrogen atom of 5-AIP [Co(1)–N(3)#2 = 2.3156(16) Å], three carboxylic oxygen atoms from two 5-AIP ligands [Co(1)–O(5) = 2.023(2) Å, Co(1)–O(3)#1 = 2.158(2) Å, Co(1)–O(2)#1 = 2.1735(17) Å], and one oxygen atom of a coordinated water molecule [Co(1)–O(1W) = 2.090(2) Å], completing a distorted octahedral geometry. In compound 3, each 5-AIP anion exhibits a three-connected chelating–monodentate–monodentate coordination mode (Chart 1) with its two carboxyl groups and the amino group, and connects the neighbouring CoII ions to produce a 1D [Co-5-AIP]2n channel-like chain with a tunnel size of 7.728 × 5.324 Å2 (Fig. S2, ESI†). The 1D channel-like chains are further linked by the Lligands to form the 2D terraced layer (Fig. 3b). For perspicuous representation, considering the CoII ions as 4-connected nodes, the ligands 5-AIP serving as 3-connected nodes, and keeping the Lligands as linear spacers, thus the 2D layer of 3 shows 3,4-connected {62.10}{6} topology, as shown in Fig. 3c. Adjacent 2D architectures are further extended into a 3D supramolecular network by hydrogen bonding interactions between the lattice water molecules O(2W) and the oxygen atoms O(4) of amide groups from Lligands [O(2W)–H(2WB)⋯O(4) = 2.8962 Å, 133°], as well as between the lattice water molecules O(2W) and the carboxyl oxygen atoms O(1) of 5-AIP anions [O(2W)–H(2WA)⋯O(1) = 2.8024 Å, 166°] (Table S9, ESI,† and Fig. 3d).

[Cu(1,2-BDC)(L)] (4)

When the cobalt(ii) salt in 1 is replaced by a copper(ii) salt, a wave-like 2D polymeric network 4 is obtained, which is different from 1. Single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that each asymmetric unit consists of one CuII ion, one 1,2-BDC anion, and one Lligand. Each CuII ion is six-coordinated by two pairs of O atoms [O(2), O(4), O(2)#3, O(4)#3] from two 1,2-BDC anions, and two pyridyl N atoms (N(1), N(1)#3) from two Lligands (Fig. 4a), showing a distorted octahedral coordination geometry. The Cu–O bond lengths range from 2.0054(16) to 2.492(3) Å, and the Cu–N bond lengths are both 1.999(2) Å. In 4, the ligandsL link metal CuII ions to form a 1D meso-helical [Cu–L]n chain (Fig. S3a, ESI†). Meanwhile, 1,2-BDC anions adopt a bis(chelating) bridging coordination mode (Chart 1) to connect the CuII ions, constructing a 1D [Cu-1,2-BDC]n chain, as shown in Fig. S3b, ESI.† Two types of 1D chains constructed from CuII ions and different ligands (L or 1,2-BDC) are linked with each other by sharing CuII ions to form a 2D wave-like structure (Fig. 4b). When the CuII ions are simplified as 4-connected nodes, the 1,2-BDC anions linking two CuII ions are regarded as 2-connected nodes, and the Lligands are regarded as linear linkers, the topology of the 2D wave-like layer can be best described as a 2,4-connected network with Schläfli symbol of {64.8.10}{6} (Fig. 4c). The adjacent 2D layers are further packed into a 3D supramolecular framework by N–H⋯O hydrogen bonding interactions between the nitrogen atoms N(2) of the amide groups and the carboxyl oxygen atoms O(4) of the 1,2-BDC ligands [N(2)–H(2)⋯O(4) = 2.9752 Å, 164°] (Table S10, ESI†), as shown in Fig. 4d.

[Cu(1,3-BDC)(L)(H2O)]·3H2O (5)

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction study reveals that compound 5 crystallizes in the P21/c space group. The asymmetric unit of 5 consists of one CuII ion, one L linker, one 1,3-BDC bridging ligand, one coordinated water molecule and three lattice water molecules. As shown in Fig. 5a, the CuII ion is five-coordinated by two pyridylnitrogen atoms from two Lligands [Cu(1)–N(1) = 2.025(3) Å, Cu(1)–N(2) = 2.032(3) Å, N1–Cu1–N2 = 167.72(10)°], two carboxyl oxygen atoms from two 1,3-BDC ligands [Cu(1)–O(1) = 1.995(2) and Cu(1)–O(3) = 1.953(2) Å], and an oxygen atom of one coordinated water molecule [Cu(1)–O1W = 2.328(2) Å], displaying a distorted tetragonal pyramid geometry. The bond angles of N–Cu–O are from 89.14(9)° to 97.08(9)°, and the bond angles of O–Cu–O are from 82.50(9)° to 174.24(9)°. In compound 5, the adjacent CuII ions are bridged by the Lligands with a bidentate bridging coordination mode to build a 1D [Cu–L]n zigzag chain (Fig. S4a, ESI†), in which L displays two kinds of conformations with the Cu⋯Cu distances of 16.997 Å (La) and 19.460 Å (Lb) (Chart 1). The ligand 1,3-BDC adopts a bis(monodentate) bridging coordination mode, which links neighbouring CuII ions to form a 1D [Cu-1,3-BDC]n ‘V’-like chain (Fig. S4b, ESI†). The combination of two types of 1D chains ([Cu-1,3-BDC]n chains and [Cu–L]n chains) leads to the formation of an intriguing 2D coordination network (Fig. 5b, up), which is further connected by Lligands to form a 3D CdSO4-type framework (Fig. S4c, ESI†).

Topologically, the CuII ion is linked by two Lligands and two 1,3-BDC anions, which can be defined as a 4-connected node, the L and 1,3-BDC ligands can be regarded as linear and ‘V’-like spacers, then the 3D framework of 5 can be simplified as a CdSO4-type network with {105.12}{10} topology (Fig. 5b, down).13 There exist two types of large cavities of approximately 21.407 × 31.861 Å2 and 18.463 × 30.867 Å2, respectively (as shown in Fig. 5b) in the 3D framework, which are occupied by another two identical frameworks, leading to the 3-fold interpenetrating structure of 5 (Fig. 5c).

[Cu2(5-AIP)2(L)2(H2O)]·6H2O (6)

Compound 6 crystallizes in the space group P. There are two CuII ions, two 5-AIP anions, two Lligands, one coordinated water molecule, and six lattice water molecules in the asymmetric unit (as shown in Fig. 6a). The Cu(1) ion is six-coordinated by two pyridylnitrogen atoms from two Lligands [Cu(1)–N(1) = 2.000(4) Å, Cu(1)–N(4)#1 = 1.992(4) Å, N(4)#1-Cu(1)–N(1) = 177.00(16)°], four carboxyl oxygen atoms [O(7), O(3), O(6), O(9)] from two 5-AIP anions [Cu(1)–O(7) = 1.987(3) Å, Cu(1)–O(3) = 2.511(3) Å, Cu(1)–O(6) = 2.550(2) Å, Cu(1)–O(9) = 1.979(3) Å], exhibiting an octahedral arrangement. The Cu(2) ion also shows a distorted octahedral geometry, coordinated by three oxygen atoms from two 5-AIP anions and a coordinated water molecule locating in the equatorial plane [Cu(2)–O(4) = 1.976(3) Å, Cu(2)–O(5) = 2.667(4) Å, Cu(2)–O(8)#2 = 1.929(3) Å, Cu(2)–O(1W) = 2.328(4) Å], and two nitrogen atoms from two Lligands occupying the axial sites [Cu(2)–N(7) = 2.021(4) Å, Cu(2)–N(9) = 2.016(4) Å].

It should be noted that the Lligand shows only one bidentate bridging coordination mode in compound 6, but three types of conformations with three different Cu⋯Cu distances (17.920 Å for La, 18.899 Å for Lb, 19.470 Å for Lc) can be simultaneously observed, which may be attributed to the different torsion angles of the spacer –(CH2)6– (Chart 1). LigandL with three different conformations (La, Lb, and Lc) links CuII ions to form two types of 1D [Cu–L]n zigzag chains ([La–Cu–Lc]n, chain A; [Lb–Cu–Lb]n, chain B), as shown in the Fig. S5a, ESI.† 5-AIP anions exhibit two different coordination modes: bis(chelating) bridging mode and chelating-monodentate bridging mode (Chart 1), which alternately connect the adjacent CuII ions to build a [Cu-5-AIP]n ‘V’-like chain, as shown in Fig. S5b, ESI.† The connection of chains B and [Cu-5-AIP]n chains results in the formation of a 2D sheet, which are further connected by ligandsLa and Lc to construct a 3D framework (Fig. 6b).

Each CuII ion [Cu(1) or Cu(2)] is linked to four adjacent CuII ions through two L and two 5-AIP ligands, which can be considered as a 4-connected node. The L and 5-AIP ligands linking two 4-connected CuII ions may be treated as linear and ‘V’-like linkers. Thus, the final architecture of compound 6 is described as a 4-connected 3D framework with the CdSO4-like topology (Fig. 6c). Similarly to 5, there also exist two types of large channels of 21.984 × 28.061 Å2 and 23.775 × 28.659 Å2 (Fig. 6b), which are occupied by another two identical frameworks to effectively reduce the void space, leading to the 3-fold interpenetrating architecture of 6 (Fig. 6d). Although the 3-fold interpenetrating networks of compounds 5 and 6 are similar, their building blocks are different: two kinds of 1D chains ([Cu-1,3-BDC]n chain and [Cu–L]n chain) for 5; three types of 1D chains ([La–Cu–Lc]n chain, [Lb–Cu–Lb]n chain and [Cu-5-AIP]n chain) for 6, which may be ascribed to the steric hindrance effect of the amino groups of 5-AIP. Finally, the tunnels in the 3D framework are occupied by the lattice water molecules, so only small voids (ca. 0.5%) can be found in compound 6.

Effect of the conformations of the Lligand, dicarboxylates and central metal ions on the structures of the title complexes

As described above, we have successfully prepared six new cobalt(ii) and copper(ii) coordination polymers based on the flexible bis(pyridylamide) ligandL and three different aromatic dicarboxylates. The flexible ligandsL adopt a μ2-bridging coordination mode in 1–6, which link adjacent metal ions to give different chain structures in compounds 1, 2, 4, 5 and 6, except for 3, exhibiting various conformations due to the flexibility of its –(CH2)6– backbone (Chart 1). In compounds 1–4, the L adopts an AAAAA trans conformation based on the relative orientation of the CO (or N–H) groups. In complex 5, the Laligand is the GAAAG trans conformation and the Lbligand displays AAAAA trans conformation. It is noted that the three independent La, Lb, and Lcligands in complex 6 show the GAAAG, AAAAA and AAAAA trans conformations, respectively. The A and G conformations are given when the C–C–C–C–C–C torsion angle (θ) is 180 ≥ θ > 90 and 0 ≤ θ ≤ 90, respectively14 (Table S11, ESI†). The differences of metal–L subunits further influence the final frameworks of the title compounds, which demonstrates that the versatile conformations of the flexible L play an important role in building polymeric structures.

The dicarboxylates with different substitute groups or a different position of the carboxyl group play an important role in tuning the dimensionality and fine structures of the title complexes with their seven different coordination modes (Chart 1). For complexes 1–3, based on the same cobalt(ii) ion and Lligand, when 1,2-BDC was used, complex 1 showed a 1D chain, in which the 1,2-BDC anion adopted a bis(monodentate) chelating mode. In compound 2, the 1,3-BDC anions with a bis(monodentate) bridging mode connected CoII ions to form 1D [Co-1,3-BDC]n chains, which were further extended to a 2D layer by Lligands. While in 3, the 5-AIP anions acted as three-connected linkers connecting the adjacent CoII ions to form 1D channel-like chains, which were extended by the Lligands to construct a 2D network. For complexes 4–6, containing the same copper(ii) ion and Lligand, the dicarboxylates also show great effect on their structures. In 4, 1,2-BDC with a bis(chelating) bridging mode links CuII ions from the adjacent parallel 1D [Cu–L]n chains to generate a wave-like 2D network. The 1,3-BDC anion with a bis(monodentate) bridging mode links CuII ions to form a 1D [Cu-1,3-BDC]n ‘V’-like chain in 5, which are further connected by Lligands to form a 3D 3-fold interpenetrating CdSO4-type framework; the 5-AIP anions in 6 show two types of coordination mode [a bis(chelating) bridging mode and a chelating-monodentate bridging mode], alternately connecting the adjacent CuII ions to build a [Cu-5-AIP]n chain, which is also connected by Lligands to form a 3D 3-fold interpenetrating CdSO4-type framework. The positions of the two carboxyl groups for dicarboxylates 1,3-BDC and 5-AIP are the same, so the 3D metal–organic frameworks of 5 and 6 are similar, except that the fundamental building units are different: two types of 1D chains based on ligands 1,3-BDC and L for 5; three types of 1D chains derived from ligands 5-AIP and L for 6.

Comparing 1 with 4 (2 with 5 and 3 with 6), based on the same Lligand and aromatic dicarboxylate, it can be found that different metal–organic frameworks are obtained by introducing the different metal ions (CoII and CuII), and the dimensionality of the copper(ii) coordination polymers is higher than that of the cobalt(ii) coordination polymers. The results indicate that both the aromatic dicarboxylates with variable coordination modes and the central metal ions show obvious effects on the final structures of the title complexes.

Thermal stability analyses and fluorescent properties

To estimate the stabilities of the titled compounds, their thermal behavior was investigated by TG analysis under an N2 atmosphere (Fig. S12, ESI†). The results indicate that all the title compounds exhibit good thermal stabilities. The fluorescence properties of compounds 1–6 and the free Lligand were studied, and the six compounds display slightly different luminescent behavior (Fig. S13, ESI†).

Electrochemical behavior of 1-CPE, 2-CPE, 3-CPE, 4-CPE, 5-CPE and 6-CPE

All the title compounds are insoluble in water and common organic solvents. Thus, the compounds 1–6 bulk-modified carbon paste electrodes (1-CPE, 2-CPE, 3-CPE, 4-CPE, 5-CPE and 6-CPE) were fabricated as the working electrodes to study the electrochemical properties of these complexes in 0.01 M H2SO4 + 0.5 M Na2SO4 aqueous solution. In the potential range of 600 to −600 mV for 1-CPE, 700 to −600 mV for 2-CPE, 400 to −700 mV for 3-CPE, 650 to −650 mV for 4-CPE, 600 to −500 mV for 5-CPE, and 800 to −800 mV for 6-CPE, a pair of redox peaks can be observed at the above modified CPEs (Fig. S14, ESI†), which can be attributed to the redox of CoIII/CoII or CuII/CuI.15 The mean peak potentials E1/2 = (Epa + Epc)/2 are −9 mV for 1-CPE, −2 mV for 2-CPE, 44 mV for 3-CPE, 34 mV for 4-CPE, −43 mV for 5-CPE, and −42 mV for 6-CPE. Here, 1-CPE and 4-CPE have been taken as examples to study the electrochemical properties of the CoII and CuII complexes in detail, respectively.

The scan rate effect on the electrochemical behaviour of 1-CPE was investigated in the potential range of 600 to −600 mV. As shown in Fig. 7a, with the scan rates increasing from 20 to 180 mV s−1, the peak potentials changed gradually: the cathodic peak potentials gradually shifted to the negative direction and the corresponding anodic peak potentials shifted to the positive direction. The peak currents are proportional to the scan rates up to 180 mV s−1, suggesting that the redox process for 1-CPE is surface-controlled (Fig. S15a, ESI†). Fig. S15b, ESI,† shows cyclic voltammograms for the electrocatalytic reduction of nitrite at 1-CPE in 0.01 M H2SO4 + 0.5 M Na2SO4 aqueous solution. With the addition of nitrite, the reduction peak currents increase gradually, while the corresponding oxidation peak currents decrease gradually at 1-CPE. The results indicate that 1-CPE possesses electrocatalytic activity toward the reduction of nitrite.

Fig. 7b shows the cyclic voltammograms of 4-CPE at different scan rates in 0.01 M H2SO4 + 0.5 M Na2SO4 aqueous solution in the potential range of 650 to −650 mV. When the scan rates were varied from 20 to 180 mV s−1, the peak potentials change gradually: the cathodic peak potentials shift to negative direction and the corresponding anodic peak potentials shift to positive direction with increasing scan rates, that is, peak-to-peak separation between the cathodic and anodic peaks increases with increasing scan rate, which confirms the quasi-reversible behavior of 4-CPE.16 The plot of peak currents versus scan rates is shown in Fig. S16, ESI,† which reveals that the anodic and the cathodic peak currents are proportional to the scan rates and suggests that the redox process for 4-CPE is also surface-confined.

Photocatalytic properties

Photocatalysts have attracted much attention in recent years due to their potential applications in purifying water and air by decomposing organic pollutants.17Methylene blue (MB) was often used as a model dye contaminant to evaluate the photocatalytic effectiveness in the purification of wastewater. The photocatalytic behavior of compound 3 towards MB was investigated under UV irradiation through a typical process: 150 mg of compound 3 was mixed together with 90 mL of an aqueous solution of MB (10.0 mg L−1), magnetically stirred in the dark for about 1 h to ensure the equilibrium of the working solution. Then, the solution was exposed to UV irradiation from an Hg lamp and kept stirring during irradiation. A 5.0 mL sample was taken out for analysis every 1 h. The photocatalytic properties of compound 3 are shown in Fig. 8. It can be clearly seen that the absorption peak of MB decreased obviously with increasing reaction time, and approximately 63.5% of MB had been decomposed during 6 h. The result shows that compound 3 possesses good photocatalytic activity for the photodegradation of the MB contaminant, and may be used as a potential photocatalyst in the decomposition of some organic dyes. When compounds 1, 4 and 5 were used as photocatalysts, almost no change for the absorption peaks of the MB solution can be found (as illustrated in Fig. S17, ESI†), implying no photocatalytic activity of compounds 1, 4 and 5 under UV irradiation.

Conclusion

We have designed and synthesized six cobalt(ii) or copper(ii) coordination polymers with different dimensionality and structures by using L as the main ligand and three different dicarboxylates as the auxiliary ligands. The dicarboxylates with different substituent groups or the situation of the carboxyl groups play an important role in constructing the title compounds and tuning their structures by exhibiting different coordination modes. The central metal ions have a great effect on the dimensionality of the coordination polymers. In addition, the flexible Lligand shows various conformations, which also influence the fine structures of the title compounds. Compound 1 shows electrocatalytic activity toward the reduction of nitrite and compound 3 possesses photocatalytic activity for the degradation of MB contaminant, and might be used as a potential material for photocatalysis. Further work for preparing novel coordination polymers with novel topologies and special applications based on other flexible bis(pyridylamide) ligands is in progress.
