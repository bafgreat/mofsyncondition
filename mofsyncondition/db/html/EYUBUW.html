Introduction

The past few years have witnessed a rapid expansion in the field of metal phosphonate chemistry, associated with their multitude of potential applications including catalysis, ion sensing and gas adsorption.1 We, and others,2 have been studying the outcome of reactions involving functionalized phosphonic acids, RPO3H2, with metal salts, and have shown that the presence of nitrogen donors provides further coordination capabilities affording cages, 1–3D polymers and bimetallic frameworks. Variation of the R group allows chemical functionality to be introduced into the solids and provides an opportunity to design robust, yet flexible solids with desired physical and chemical properties. Most reports involving functionalized aryl phosphonic acids have employed rigid molecules with the phosphonic acid moiety attached directly to the aromatic ring, which somewhat promotes product predictability.3 Metal phosphonates assembled from the reactions of more flexible ligands (i.e. with a carbon between the aromatic and phosphonic acid groups) e.g. pyridyl methyl phosphonic acids are not so well studied, possibly because a serendipitous assembly is more likely.4 Nevertheless, these ligands may show more interesting properties and afford porous structures that can be compared with products obtained from the analogous carbon-containing acids: pyridine methyl carboxylic acids (PyCH2COOH) that have been used to synthesize monomers, dimers and polymers.5 Based on our previous successful work with 2-pyridyl phosphonic acid,2a2-pyridylmethylphosphonic acid, 2PyCH2PO3H2 (Py = pyridine), was selected for our studies, with the rationale being that the additional CH2 group between the functionalities would yield a more flexible ligand, promoting higher dimensional polymeric materials. Surprisingly, studies involving 2PyCH2PO3H2 are limited,6 despite complexes of Tc and Pt having been shown to have viable medicinal uses.7 Our goal was to examine the coordination preferences of 2-pyridylmethylphosphonic acid to construct homo- and heterometal phosphonates and further examine their properties. An overriding aim of this study was that by investigating factors that influence the coordination modes of designed ligands, a strategy to manipulate the resultant structural motif could be developed. Herein we report the synthesis and characterization of a series of metal phosphonates and highlight the diverse potential of functionalized phosphonic acids.

Results and discussion

2-Pyridylmethylphosphonic acid was prepared according to a modified literature method.6a In a typical experiment stoichiometric quantities of the ligand and metal salt were dissolved in water and/or ethanol and placed in an open scintillation vial. The reactions were stirred at room temperature or with gentle heating for ∼3 hours. Crystalline materials were obtained by filtration of the reaction mixture to remove any impurities followed by slow evaporation of the solution at room temperature. The structures of all the compounds were determined by single crystal X-ray analysis. A comparison of the experimental powder diffraction data with that simulated from the single crystal data confirmed that the single crystal structures are representative of the bulk material (excluding 6, vide infra).

In order to examine products based on the preferred metal geometry and ligand coordination preferences, metal cations with open coordination sites were selected from the p and d block. Metal perchlorate salts8 were selected based on their solubility and the lability of the ClO4−group, which provides a source of ‘free’ metal ions for ligand coordination and generally yields crystalline products.

Manganese and cobalt phosphonates, 1 and 2, [{Mn(2PyHCH2PO3H)(H2O)}ClO4]n and [{Co(2PyHCH2PO3H)(H2O)}ClO4]n

The stoichiometric reaction of manganese(ii) and cobalt(ii) perchlorate with 2-pyridylmethylphosphonic acid in water at 100 °C for 3 hours afforded isomorphous and isostructural products: [{Mn(2PyHCH2PO3H)(H2O)}ClO4]n, 1, and [{Co(2PyHCH2PO3H)(H2O)}ClO4]n, 2. Products 1 and 2, Fig. 1, are two dimensional frameworks that crystallize in the monoclinic space groupP21/c. The cobalt and manganese atoms exhibit octahedral geometry, with four of the six octahedral sites occupied by oxygen atoms from the phosphonate group. The remaining two sites are occupied by water molecules in a trans arrangement. The metric parameters of 1 and 2 are unremarkable and similar to other reported manganese and cobalt phosphonates.9 The phosphonate group links two metal centers via the formation of µ2η2-phosphonato groups and the P–O bonds involved have similar bond lengths of 1.504(5) Å (P1–O1) in 1 and 1.508(3) Å (P1–O3) in 2. In both, the remaining uncoordinated phosphorus oxygen atoms are protonated (O3 in 1 and O2 in 2) with longer bond lengths of 1.579(5) Å and 1.576(3) Å respectively, and both have a protonated heterocyclic nitrogen atom. In 1 the hydrogen atom on O3 could not be located from the difference map, probably because of twinning that could not be resolved using rotational twin laws. In the extended polymeric structure the metallic octahedral arrangements are interconnected via tetrahedral [PCO3] units. The interconnection of these two different units affords 16-membered rings with bridged oxygen atoms that occupy 68% of available space. Within the 16-membered cores, it is possible to observe the antiparallel alignment of two pyridine rings corresponding to two different 2PyHCH2PO3H2 moieties (i.e. have alternate geometry, one up, one down, throughout the polymer). The displaced perchlorate ions are located in the lattice, sandwiched between layers made up of O–M–O rings in an arrangement that maximizes hydrogen bonding interactions. Hydrogen bonding occurs between the protonated nitrogen atom of the pyridyl group and the oxygen atom at the metal center from the phosphonate group (N1–O3 in 2). The distance separating the manganese and cobalt is ∼6.5 Å, which is too great for any magnetic communication.

Analysis of 1 and 2

The purity of the bulk material was confirmed by powder X-ray diffraction which was compared to the simulated pattern produced from the single crystal CIF file. UV/Vis spectroscopic analysis of 1 and 2 exhibited low intensity absorbance due to the ligand at 268 nm, however low intensities of octahedral Mn/Co(ii) complexes are not unusual.10 The IR spectra of complexes 1 and 2 have numerous characteristic peaks. Evidence for the presence of perchlorate can be obtained from indicative strong bands near 1100 cm−1 (asymmetric stretch) and a sharp band at 625 cm−1 (asymmetric bend) and a very weak absorption at 940–945 cm−1 (symmetric stretch).11 The coordinated water molecules are clearly observed in the infrared spectra, with the δ(H–O–H) bending/stretching vibrations observed at 1600–1650 cm−1 and a strong infrared peak at ∼3400 cm−1 assigned to an υ(OH) stretching frequency of water. In an electrochemical study of 1, irreversible one electron reduction of the ligand was observed (see ESI†) and a one electron irreversible oxidation of the metal. Similar results were also observed for 2. The chemical composition of 1 and 2 was confirmed by mass spectrometry.

Synthesis and discussion of 3, an iron phosphonate cage, [Fe4(2PyHCH2PO3)4(H2O)12(Cl)(ClO4)7·xH2O]

Continuing with the investigation of transition metal salts, the aqueous reaction of iron(ii) perchlorate with 2PyCH2PO3H2 was performed, Scheme 1.

Following slow evaporation of the reaction mixture, colorless crystals of 3 were isolated.

Single crystal X-ray analysis of 3 revealed a tetrameric iron cage, Fig. 2, in which the iron atoms are linked by bridged oxygen atoms from the phosphonate groups. The cage contains four octahedrally coordinated iron centers arranged in a cubane type structure. Three of the octahedral coordination sites are occupied by oxygen atoms from the phosphonate groups and the remaining three sites occupied by water molecules. Each vertex of the cube has a tetrahedrally coordinated phosphorus atom that links oxygen atoms which occupy all midpoints of the cube forming Fe–O–P bonds.

Compound 3 is fairly unusual as metal phosphonate cages usually require a phosphonic acid and a co-ligand in order to circumvent polymerization.12 In addition, phosphonic acids are not commonly used for the synthesis of discrete molecules because of their limited solubility.13 However, tetranuclear iron phosphates and phosphonates have been previously documented14 and have been fairly well studied because tetranuclear iron(iii) complexes are known to occur in nature and have been used as model systems for hemeproteins.15

The seven perchlorate ions, one chloride ion and four doubly deprotonated phosphonate groups bring the total negative charge to −16. With the pyridylnitrogen atoms protonated, this leaves a charge of +3 to be assigned to each iron atom.

The source of Cl− in the lattice is likely from reduction of a ClO4− anion, as no other chloride source was present. The lattice chloride ion lies on a two-fold axis and plays an important role in the extended structure. The packing diagram of 3 shows four water molecules in a tetrahedral arrangement, two from each tetranuclear cage, around each chloride ion which generates a polymeric chain, formed from chloride bridged tetranuclear molecules in the a direction, with the perchlorate ions arranged in channels between the tetranuclear units, Fig. 3 and 4.

The Fe–O (phosphonate) bonds in 3 are slightly longer than those in related iron phosphonate cages17 but is possibly due to the greater steric hindrance of the ligand compared to structures generated from unsubstituted phosphoric acids.17

Experimental efforts to prevent protonation of the pyridinenitrogen atom by addition of various bases (NaOH, Et3N and pyridine) were all unsuccessful and led to crystallization of various inorganic salts.

NMR confirms the paramagnetic nature of 3, with very broad signals being observed. However, the 31P NMR spectrum exhibited three broad peaks at 66, 50 and 19 ppm, indicating possible decomposition upon redissolution. Attempts to recrystallize the solution from the NMR tube to examine decomposition products were unsuccessful. As seen in 1 and 2, UV/Vis on 3 showed an absorption band at 268 nm that can be assigned to the ligand with no other absorption observed. Infrared spectroscopy of 3 shows a peak at 798 cm−1 that can be assigned to the asymmetric Fe–O–Fe stretch vibration and is comparable with other oxo-bridged Fe species.18 An electrochemical study on 3 in aqueous and DMF solutions revealed that 3 displayed an irreversible one electron reduction (Fe3+ → Fe2+). Continuous scanning saw passivation of the electrode and a decrease in response. To confirm the oxidation state of the iron atoms XPS (X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy) analysis on 3 was performed (see ESI† for spectrum). High-resolution spectra were acquired for the principal photoelectron peaks of several of the elements detected for the sample. The Fe 2p doublet had the broad, asymmetric structure typical of compounds in which the metal has unpaired d electrons. The more intense Fe 2p 3/2 component was centred at a binding energy of 712.5 eV. This value is at the high end of the range of binding energies reported for Fe compounds and suggests that the metal is present as Fe(iii); however, there is a substantial overlap in the ranges of reported binding energies for Fe(ii) and Fe(iii) complexes. According to the NIST (National Institute of Standards and Technology) XPS database, the binding energy measured for Fe 2p 3/2 in FePO4 is 712.8 eV, which is close to that measured for 3.19

Synthesis and discussion of 4 and 5, [Fe2(2PyHCH2PO3H)2(ClO4)]n and [Fe(2PyHCH2PO3H)(H2O)(ClO4)]n

As a continuation of the work on iron cages, we were interested in investigating iron precursors with different anions and varied solvent systems. To this end iron(ii) nitrate, bromide and oxalate were reacted with 2PyCH2PO3H2 in a range of solvents: DMF, CH3NO2, MeOH, etc. All reactions yielded oily or amorphous material. However, the stoichiometric reaction of 2PyCH2PO3H2 with Fe(ClO4)2 in D2O afforded a crystalline product. D2O was selected as it is known to alter molecular interactions and can perturb the usual crystallization process.20 Examination of the crystals under the microscope revealed two types of crystal—colorless blocks, 4, and colorless plates, 5. A single crystal X-ray diffraction study on 4 found it to crystallize in the trigonal space group, R, and is an aesthetically pleasing Fe(iii) phosphonate chain with three fold symmetry along the main axis. Fig. 5 depicts the atom connectivity and the highly symmetrical packing arrangement of the polymeric material. In the asymmetric unit of 4, are found one ligand and two iron centers that lie on crystallographically imposed −3 symmetry sites. The charge is balanced by the presence of a lattice perchlorate molecule and a singly deprotonated phosphonic acid. Each iron atom exists in close to perfect octahedral geometry and every coordinated oxygen atom is from a phosphonate group that bridges the iron centers.

Single crystal X-ray analysis on 5, the crystalline plate, found it to be monoclinic, space groupP21/c, Fig. 6. Crystalline 5 forms a polymeric sheet network that shows structural similarity to 1 and 2. The asymmetric unit has one iron atom on an inversion center, a phosphonic acidligand and one disordered perchlorate anion. As with 4, the iron is found in an octahedral environment; however, a water molecule occupies one of the coordination sites that in 4 is occupied by an oxygen atom from the phosphonate group.

The Fe–O bond lengths of 4 and 5 show distinct differences, with distances of ∼1.9 to 2.0 Å in 4 and 2.2–2.3 Å in 5. These differences are likely due to the different oxidation states of the iron centers, in 4; the iron centers are Fe3+ that have smaller ionic radii, while in 5, the iron is Fe2+.16 These bond lengths and angles are comparable to other documented species in similar oxidation states.21 A visible survey on the crystalline product under the microscope found 4 to be the major product, but due to the small size and similar crystal color, attempts at separation proved futile. Attempts to generate individual products of 4 and 5, such as by performing the reaction under N2, etc. were unsuccessful.

Synthesis of a bimetallic Pb/Ag phosphonate, 6, [AgPb(2PyCH2PO3)(NO3)]n

Bimetallic metal phosphonates of the late transition metals still remain fairly limited,22 however, may have useful properties that are not available for single metal systems. For the synthesis of bimetallic polymers, metal salt solubility, the coordination preference of the reactant metals, and potential metathesis reactions need to be considered. With these factors in mind the reaction of silver triflate, lead nitrate and 2-pyridylmethylphosphonic acid was performed in a 1 : 1 : 1 ratio. Slow evaporation of the solution resulted in the isolation of colorless crystals of 6. Single crystal determination showed that 6 crystallizes in the monoclinic space groupP21/c. In the asymmetric unit of 6 can be found one silver atom, a lead atom, one ligand and a coordinated nitrate ion, Fig. 7.

Unlike in polymeric 1 and 2, the pyridylnitrogen atom participates in bonding, which might not be too surprising, when considering the coordination preference of silver.23 The silver atom is ligated to the pyridylnitrogen atom and a symmetry generated phosphonate oxygen atom (O3) that forms a bridge between the silver and lead centers. The geometry around the silver atom is distorted from linear geometry with an angle of 161.5(3)°.

The coordination sphere of the lead atoms is occupied by oxygen atoms with Pb–O bond lengths between 2.383(7) and 2.757(7) Å that show no unusual discrepancies. If one considers Pb–O distances of less than 2.8 Å then each lead center is five coordinate in the distorted square pyramidal geometry, but if the Pb–O distance is increased to 2.8 Å then the lead atom would have seven coordinate geometry. The overall structural motif of 6 is quite complicated and when assembled in two dimensions, the anti-parallel packing arrangement of the pyridyl rings and nitrate groups can be observed, possibly because of the need to minimise lone pair repulsions. Overall, there are layers of Pb–O centers, that are linked by phosphonate groups and pyridyl–Ag–O bridges Fig. 8. The phosphonic acid is doubly deprotonated and along with a nitrate ion balances the +3 cationic charge.

XPS studies on 6 confirmed the presence of both Pb and Ag; the Pb 4f 7/2 component of the 4f doublet was located at 138.7 eV, which is within the range of values reported for Pb(ii) compounds. This value can be compared with the binding energy reported for the Pb 4f 7/2 component in Pb3(PO4)2 of 138.6 eV.19 The binding energies reported for Ag 3d 5/2 in silver and its compounds do not differ substantially, making it difficult to determine the oxidation state of silver in an unknown sample. It is possible to distinguish the element from its compounds, by also measuring the kinetic energy of the MNN Auger peaks and calculating the Auger parameter. The binding energy of the Ag 3d 5/2 peak in 6 was 367.9 eV and the kinetic energy of the Ag M4NN peak was 350.2 eV, yielding a modified Auger parameter of 718.1 eV. From this, it can be concluded that Ag is present as either Ag(i) or Ag(ii), however, as earlier stated, for charge balance, and based on previous results Ag(i) is more likely. X-Ray powder diffraction on bulk 6 did not match the simulated diffraction pattern. The XPS analysis showed the presence of sulfur and fluorine in the bulk powder, which arises from the triflate precursor. Other analysis was performed using the crystalline material.

Synthesis and discussion of the Pb(ii) phosphonate, 7, [Pb(2PyHCH2PO3H)(NO3)2]n and a silver polymer, 8, [Ag3(2PyCH2PO3H)(SO3CF3)2]n

In light of the isolation of the bimetallic Pb/Ag polymer, the separate reactions of both metal salts were performed to compare the individual metal structural motifs and to gain insight into the prediction of material architecture. Furthermore, compared to transition metal phosphonates, main group metal phosphonates remain much less well studied, with the exception of aluminium and calcium.24Lead(ii) has been shown to exhibit a different structural geometry from the similarly charged transition metals (Cd, Zn, Hg) due to the presence of lone pair electrons.25 To this end, the individual stoichiometric reactions of lead(ii) nitrate and silver triflate with 2PyCH2PO3H2 were performed. From the reaction of lead(ii) nitrate with 2PyCH2PO3H2 were isolated colorless crystals of 7, Fig. 9. In the asymmetric unit of 7 are found one lead atom, a phosphonateligand and two nitrate ions. The phosphonic acid is singly deprotonated while the pyridylnitrogen atom is protonated. The Pb1–O2 distance of 2.257(5) Å is on the shorter end of Pb–O distances but not unprecedented; the pillared, layered structure of catena-(bis(µ5-carboxyethylphosphanto)-trilead) has a Pb–O distance of ∼2.26 Å.26a Other Pb–O distances in the range 2.594(6)–2.718(6) Å are comparable to those observed in other lead(ii) phosphonates.26 The one dimensional chain arises from bridged nitrate ions that link the lead centers. 7 is not as thermally stable as expected (no coordinated or lattice water molecules) and melts at 125–127 °C; the thermal gravimetric analysis (TGA) shows decomposition of the organic ligand in this temperature range.

From the silver triflate reaction, concomitant with a metallic silver deposit colorless crystals of 8 were isolated. Solid state analysis of these crystals revealed a two dimensional polymer, Fig. 10.

A single crystal diffraction study on 8, Fig. 10, revealed that the asymmetric unit has three crystallographically unique silver atoms, which in the extended structure form a zigzag chain. The doubly deprotonated phosphonate ligand links the silver atoms with O1 bridging Ag2 and Ag3, while O2 bonds to Ag1. Each of the silver atoms has a different coordination sphere, and interestingly, when compared to the bimetallic polymer6, there is no coordination to the pyridylnitrogen atoms which remain unprotonated. Ag1 is four-coordinate, with two Ag interactions (Ag3, Ag3A) and bonds to bridged oxygen atoms from the phosphonate moiety. Ag2 is five-coordinate, with argentophilic interactions to Ag3 and a symmetry equivalent Ag2A. The remaining three coordination sites are taken up by an oxygen atom from the triflate anion (O4) and two phosphonate oxygen atoms (O1, O2). With a coordination number of six, Ag3 has three Ag bonding interactions (Ag1, Ag2, Ag1A) and the remaining sites occupied by oxygen atoms (one triflate (O7) and two phosphonate oxygen atoms (O1, O3)), Fig. 11.

Coordination of the triflate anion is not common,27 being a labile leaving group, but in polymer8, two triflate groups participate in bonding to two silver atoms (Ag2 and Ag3).28 This coordination, in conjunction with the Ag–Ag interactions, helps stabilize the polymeric network, as the energy of the Ag–Ag closed shell interaction falls into the same range as aromatic stacking.29 The silver–silver distances fall on the shorter end of documented Ag–Ag interactions, which vary from 3.09–3.47 Å.30,31 The mass spectral analysis showed a fragmentation pattern consistent with the X-ray structural determination and the Ag107, Ag109 isotope pattern was clearly observed.

The presence of fairly short argentophilic interactions, Fig. 11,32 prompted us to investigate the luminescence of 8. Excitation at 280 nm afforded an intense emission peak at 580 nm, and a broad emission at 340 nm, see Fig. 12 and ESI†.

Excitation of the free ligand at 265 nm shows emissions at 326 nm and 623 nm. The broad peak observed at 340 nm is attributed to emission from the ligand. In the solid state, 8 shows good photostability, although in solution slow decomposition to metallic silver occurs. Polymeric 8 is thermally stable with decomposition occurring at 250–256 °C.

Conclusion

The results demonstrate that modification of the organic moieties of the phosphonateligand RPO3(2−) can pose significant influences on the structures of the final products. A number of new coordination polymers with 2-pyridylmethylphosphonic acid have been synthesized and show the varying coordination modes possible and the rich variety of structures that can be obtained. In the majority of isolated structures the pyridinenitrogen atom did not coordinate to the metal centers. Nonparticipation of the pyridine group is quite unusual33 but probably due to the acidic medium and subsequent protonation of the heterocyclic nitrogen atom, which in turn limits polymer dimensionality. When comparing the bimetallic coordination polymer, 6, to the individual components, 7 (Pb) and 8, (Ag), it is clear that the goal of crystals by design remains an ongoing challenge. However, one can observe the important role that the anion plays in stabilizing the polymers and cages through hydrogen bonding. In our studies, employing perchlorate salts proved useful for obtaining crystalline products but always afforded complexes with lattice incorporated perchlorate anions, which can reduce polymer dimensionality and porosity.

Isolation of the tetrametric iron phosphonates, 3, demonstrates how functionalized phosphonic acids could be used as support ligands for the preparation of paramagnetic transition metal cages and as possible biological mimics. Work is underway for the synthesis of larger transition metal clusters and cages.

Experimental

All manipulations were performed under aerobic conditions. 2PyCH2PO3H2 was prepared by a modified published method.6a All other reagents were purchased from Aldrich and used as received. Crystal data were collected with a Bruker SMART 1000 diffractometer or an Oxford Gemini, molybdenum radiation (λ = 0.7107 Å) at −50, −60 or −100 °C. Crystals were mounted on glass fibers using paratone oil. The data were corrected for absorption. Structures were solved by direct methods34 and refined34via full-matrix least squares. Crystal data for 1–8 can be found in Table 1. Hydrogen atoms were placed in idealized positions except certain H2O molecules and NH atoms that were localized from the electron density map and refined with distant restraints. The 1H spectra were recorded at 300.05 MHz or 500.1 MHz on a Varian XL-300 or Bruker Avance 500 respectively. IR analysis was conducted as KBr discs on a MIDAC M4000 Fourier transform infrared (FT IR) spectrometer, and mass spectrometry analysis was carried out using an Ultraflex III tof/tof mass spectrometer (Bruker Daltonics, Germany), and run under positive ionization mode using α-cyano 4-hydroxycinnamic acid as the matrix. XPS analysis was run at La Trobe Centre for Materials and Surface Science. Fluorescence measurements were recorded on a Varian Cary Eclipse fluorescence spectrophotometer and analysed using Cary Eclipse Scan Application v 1.1(132). X-Ray powder diffraction was recorded using a Siemens Kristalloflex Diffraktometer D5000. Simulation of powder diffraction patterns was obtained using Mercury, V: 2.3. Melting points were determined in capillaries and are uncorrected.

General experimental

In order to elucidate the optimal experimental conditions for metal phosphonate formation, a combinatorial approach was used that examined metal precursors, solvent, temperature and pH. From this study it was found that metal perchlorates and nitrates were generally the most conducive to yielding crystalline material and using a mixture EtOH/H2O as a solvent provided the correct balance for solubility and generating crystalline material by evaporation in a reasonable time frame (1–2 days). Performing the reactions under basic conditions (NaOH, pyridine, or addition of NEt3) proved unsuccessful, affording either metal hydroxides or noncrystalline material.
