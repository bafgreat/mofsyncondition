Introduction

Energetic materials have been widely used in large amounts in mixtures for military and civil fields, for example in aerospace propellants, mining engineering, and pyrotechnic technology etc.1–7 The challenge to develop new energetic materials is to further improve three main requirements: performance (heat of formation, detonation velocity and pressure), safety (thermal stability and sensitivity), and practicability (synthesis and compatibility).8 Currently available pyrotechnics (fireworks and colored signal flares) use perchlorate oxidizers to produce the desired colors. Residual perchlorates from pyrotechnic devices may reach into groundwater and cause widespread contamination that requires remediation.9,10 Therefore, developing green energetic materials is still an ongoing project in numerous research groups worldwide.11–15

Energetic salts show several advantages, such as lower vapor pressures, high thermal stabilities and decreased sensitivities to outer stimuli (friction, impact, and electrostatic discharge), over conventional nonionic compounds.16,17 Moreover, their properties can be readily optimized via the judicious combination of different cation and anion components. Alkali metal and alkaline earth metal salts are continually investigated for applications in the fields of green pyrotechnics or primary explosives.18–21 For example, the use of dilithium, strontium and barium 5,5′-bis(tetrazole-1-oxide) as coloring agents in pyrotechnical mixtures22 and the dipotassium and dicesium salt as ingredients in NIR (near infrared region) flares23 are investigated. In 2015, Klapötke et al. reported new primary explosive potassium 1,1-dinitramino-5,5-bistetrazolate,24 which is desirable for use in initiators due to its extremely short DDT (deflagration to detonation) behavior as well as high sensitivities toward external stimuli.

Nitrogen-rich compounds represent a unique class of high-energy density compounds (HEDCs) and release environmentally benign nitrogen gas during explosion or combustion. Their energy mainly originates from largely positive heats of formation rather than the oxidation of the carbon backbone. High-nitrogen energetic salts for pyrotechnic applications as perchlorate replacements have been extensively investigated.25,26 Furthermore, 5-oxotetrazole, namely tetrazolone27 was first proposed in 1960, which exhibits appropriate thermal stabilities, low sensitivities, and good detonation performance. Klapötke and co-workers28 then investigated a variety of its energetic salts. However, the decomposition temperatures of nitrogen-rich salts were unexpectedly decreased compared to neutral mother tetrazolone. In addition, the intermediate diazonium in the preparation of tetrazolone is extremely sensitive and unstable. Moreover, low-concentration solutions can explode spontaneously even at 0 °C, breaking the glassware used for diazonium; therefore, there is a great need to improve the synthesis method for safe handling. Our approach involved more moderate strategy starting with benzyl isocyanate through electrophilic addition, cyclization and catalytic hydrogenation to give the product in comparable yields. We also present the synthesis, full analytical and spectroscopic characterization, X-ray structures, thermal stabilities and energetic properties of alkali (lithium 1, sodium 2, potassium 3, rubidium 4, cesium 5) and alkaline earth (magnesium 6, calcium 7, strontium 8, barium 9) metal salts of tetrazolone.

Results and discussion

Synthesis

Tetrazolone was synthesized via newly improved methods avoiding uncontrolled explosion of the intermediate diazonium in the preparation of tetrazolone from 5-aminotetrazole, recorded in previous literature.28 The reaction of commercially available benzyl isocyanate and trimethylsilane azide under the reflux of anhydrous toluene results in formyl benzylamine azide. Then, cyclization with trimethyl chlorosilane and trimethylamine forms 1-benzyl tetrazolone. Finally catalytic hydrogenolysis with Pd/C catalyst gives the target product. The starting material is further converted into various metal salts by adding respective metal hydroxides and carbonates to the aqueous solution of tetrazolone (Scheme 1).

The reaction mechanism can be summarized in Scheme 2, mainly including electrophilic addition, cyclization and hydrogenation processes. First, trimethylsilyl group attacks the electronegative oxygen atom of benzyl isocyanate forming Si–O bond, losing an azide anion. The produced carbenium ion combines the N3− of another molecular TMSN3 to generate a stable product. Then, the hydrolysis reaction of silicon ether results in trimethylsilyl alcohol and azide enol, which further rearranges into the amide structure.29 Second, amide transfers to enol anion in alkaline conditions and substitutes with trimethylchlorosilane forming silicon enol ethers. Then, the intramolecular rearrangement on azide of silicon enol ethers generates the tetrazole ring.30 After hydrolysis, Si–O bond breaks into hydroxyl bond and rapidly rearranges into substituted tetrazolone due to unstable 5-hydroxy tetrazolium. Third, the heterogeneous catalytic hydrogenation under H/Pd ultimately produces tetrazolone.31

Spectroscopy

The structures of tetrazolone and the new salts 1–9 were determined by mass spectrum, NMR and IR spectra technology as well as elemental analysis. The characteristic information of tetrazolone anion is given in Fig. 1.

Tetrazolone anion has the strongest peak (m/z 84.95) in the MS, which is agreement with its molecular weight. 13C NMR spectra were recorded for target compounds in DMSO-d6 solution, and chemical shifts were given with respect to tetramethylsilane as external standard. In all salts, the lone 13C signals occur at 166.49 ppm. The IR spectra of all compounds were recorded, and the frequencies were assigned according to the literature for positive identification and easier assignment of the target compounds. The carbonyl group has a significant stretching vibration ranged in the peaks between 1600 and 1700 cm−1. The vibration modes on N–N bonds and C–N bonds of the tetrazolone ring are expected in the region 1400–800 cm−1. In addition, all IR spectra show N–H or O–H bond stretching vibrations in the range of 3600–3100 cm−1, indicating either the presence of hydrogen of tetrazolone or crystal water or moisture.

Crystal structure

Single-crystal X-ray determinations were accomplished for tetrazolone salts 1–9 and are all discussed in detail. All compounds were recrystallized from water resulting in the formation of a hydrate as the crystalline species and only potassium, rubidium and cesium salts were obtained water free, which exhibit relatively high densities. The metal ions coordinate with the N and O atoms in the tetrazolone ring and water molecule in the assembly displaying 10-coordianted Ba2+, Cs+, octahedral-coordinated Ca2+, Rb+, Sr2+, hexa-coordinated Mg2+, Na+, hepta- coordinated K+, and tetra-coordinated in the case of Li+. The crystal structure of tetrazolone anion reveals similar structural properties. The torsion angles N3–N2–N1–C1 closely vary from 0.0° to 3.0°, indicating that the anion can be considered planar. The lengths of C–O bond are slightly longer than the standard CO double bond in tetrazolone molecule (1.244 Å) except for cesium salt with a shorter one. The NN and CN bond lengths of the tetrazole rings vary between the lengths typically observed for NN single and double bonds as well as CN single bonds. Selected data and parameters of refinements are given in Table S1,† and the selected bond lengths, bond angles, torsion angles and hydrogen bond lengths are listed in the ESI.†

Single crystal of lithium 5-oxotetrazolate (1) was obtained from water. Compound 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P2(1)/c with four molecular units in the unit cell and density of 1.479 g cm−3 (Fig. 2). Lithium has coordinative bonds to the oxygen atom O1 of the anion (Li1–O1 1.928 Å) and also to the atom N4 of the anion (Li1–N4 2.057 Å) as well as atom O2 of water molecule (Li1–O2 2.015 Å). This cation exhibits a tetrahedral coordination sphere consisting of one hydrate water molecule and three tetrazolone anions.

Both sodium 5-oxotetrazolate (2) and potassium 5-oxotetrazolate (3) crystallize in the monoclinic space group P2(1)/c with four molecular units per unit cell (Fig. 3). The calculated density of 3 is 1.946 g cm−3, which is higher than that of 2 with a value of 1.872 g cm−3 due to the absence of water molecules in the unit cell of 3. Each sodium ion is hexa-coordinated by two oxygen atoms of hydrate water molecules and three oxygen atoms as well as one nitrogen atom of the anion. The coordination sphere can be described as a distorted octahedron. The potassium ion is coordinated sevenfold by three O atoms and four N atoms only from the anion. Each oxygen atom of anion is coordinated to three cations in both the cases, which constructs a similar cube framework for metal ions.

The rubidium 5-oxotetrazolate (4) crystallizes in a triclinic cell of the space group P with two formula units per unit cell, and cesium 5-oxotetrazolate (5) crystallizes in the orthorhombic space group Pbam with four molecular units in the unit cell (Fig. 4). Both metal rubidium and cesium salts crystallize without inclusion of crystal water resulting in very high densities of 2.492 g cm−3 (4) and 3.074 g cm−3 (5), respectively. All nitrogen atoms and oxygen atoms of the anion are involved in coordination to the rubidium center with Rb–N distances (3.123–3.450 Å) and Rb–O (2.860–2.938 Å). Seven anions around rubidium accounts for a coordination number of nine in 4. Each cesium atom in 5 is ten-coordinated eightfold by four oxygen atoms (Cs–O 3.304–3.401 Å) and six nitrogen atoms (Cs–N 3.250–3.452 Å) of anions forming a complicated coordination sphere.

Magnesium 5-oxotetrazolate (6) crystallizes in the orthorhombic space group Cmca with four formula units per unit cell and a lower density of 1.767 g cm−3 (Fig. 5). The asymmetric unit of the compound is made up of one crystallographically independent Mg2+ cation and two tetrazolone anions as well as four water molecules. Each magnesium ion is hexa-coordinated by four oxygen atoms (Mg–O 2.051 Å) of hydrate water molecules and two further oxygen atoms (Mg–O 2.064 Å) of the anion, and the coordination sphere can be described as a common octahedral geometry similar to that of magnesium salts containing a nitrogen-rich anion. Calcium 5-oxotetrazolate (7) crystallizes in the orthorhombic space group Cccm with a density of 1.886 g cm−3. It shows a different geometry with two tetrazolone moieties, one calcium center, five water molecules and co-crystallizing with a tetrazolone hydrate in each unit cell. Calcium is coordinated eightfold by six oxygen atoms of water molecules and two nitrogen atoms of different tetrazolone anions each, which together form a distorted dodecahedron. The geometry of 1 : 2 stoichiometry and the presence of a large number of water molecules in the structure contribute to the low density both of 6 and 7 salts.

Strontium 5-oxotetrazolate (8) crystallizes in the orthorhombic space group Pnnm with four formula units in the unit cell and a density of 1.977 g cm−3 (Fig. 6). The asymmetric unit of 8 is made up of one Sr2+ cation and two tetrazolone anions as well as five water molecules. The strontium cation is coordinated eightfold by six oxygen atoms of (Sr–O 2.532–2.7789 Å) hydrate water molecules and two nitrogen atoms (Sr–N 2.856–2.856 Å) of the anions. Barium 5-oxotetrazolate (9) crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/c with four molecules in each unit cell, leading to a relatively high density of 2.618 g cm−3. The barium cation is ten-coordinated by four nitrogen atoms (Ba–N2 3.094 Å, Ba–N3 3.100 Å) and two oxygen atoms (Ba–O1 2.789 Å) of different anions and further four oxygen atoms (Ba–O2 3.004 and 3.011 Å) of water molecules. Compared to the structure of 8, the barium ion exhibits collectively larger ionic interactions between Ba and N as well as Ba and O because of the different sizes of ions and the minor difference in electronegativity.

In complex 8 (Fig. 7), there are strong intermolecular hydrogen bonds present in the structure, wherein the N1 atom of tetrazolone anion and O3, O4 and O5 atoms of hydrate water molecules act as donors, whereas the O1 atom and N4 atom act as acceptors. Some hydrogen bonds (N1–H1⋯O1, O4–H4C⋯N7, O4–H4D⋯O1, N5–H5⋯O2, O5–H5C⋯O2) are moderately strong with a D⋯A distance of 2.778(3)–3.012(3) Å, which are shorter than the sum of van der Waals radii (rw(N) + rw(N) = 3.10 Å; rw(N) + rw(O) = 3.07 Å).32 Other three hydrogen bonds show an electrostatic nature in the cases of O3–H3⋯N8 and O5–H5D⋯N4 with a D⋯A distance of 3.130(3) and 3.167(3) Å. Analogously, complex 1 contains a network of hydrogen bonds with D⋯A distances ranging from 2.903(3) to 2.968(3) Å and angles ranging from 166.00° to 179.00°. The hydrogen bonds of compound 2 show distances of 2.834(3)–3.228(2) Å and angles of 137.00°–169.0174.0°, anticipating the good stability of the molecule. All N atoms in anions of compound 6 are involved in hydrogen-bonding to water molecules with distances of 2.843(4)–2.918(4) Å. Again, the structure of 7 is dominated by extensive strong hydrogen bonds ranging from 2.72(3) to 3.151(12) Å and angles from 145.00° to 179.00°. Complex 9 is also stabilized via a number of hydrogen bonds formed by interaction between the tetrazolone anions and water molecules. In the structures of 7, 8 and 9, two asymmetric units are bridged by the two longer contacts to water molecules to form dimeric clusters, which are connected through hydrogen-bonding as well. These connection modes result in the formation of interesting porous energetic coordination polymers.

Thermal behavior

The thermal behaviors were investigated by differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) and thermogravimetric (TG) measurements in order to determine the melt- and decomposition temperatures as well as the dehydration temperatures of the crystal water contained in salts. Comparative curves of salts 1–9 are illustrated in Fig. 8.

The onset decomposition temperatures of the investigated compounds range from 215.0 °C (4) to 280.1 °C (1) for the alkali metal salts and from 225.0 °C (8) up to 328.2 °C (7) for the alkaline earth metal salts. Depending on the geometrical parameters of the crystal structure and its coordination of water, thermal behaviors are different. The thermal decomposition of these metal salts can be divided into two principal stages in the cases of salts 2, 6, 8 and 9. The first stage occurs in the range of 80.7–171.1 °C corresponding with the loss of the different water molecules. The exothermic decomposition was recorded in the second stage in the temperature range of 225.0–271.5 °C. The salt 1 has two endothermic signals in the DSC curves indicating a stepwise dehydration. The salt 3 and 5 show no endothermic signal except 4 (first melting 114.9 °C before decomposition); salt 3, 4 and 5 exothermally decompose (245.8, 215.0, 229.5 °C), which indicates exclusion of crystal waters in their structures. In the case of 7, dehydration is observed as a single step from the solvent water (56.6 °C), and then 7 directly decomposes at 328.2 °C, indicating that it is the most thermally stable among the metal salts studied herein.

Energetic properties

As potential energetic materials, the energetic properties of compounds 1–9 were assessed using standardized test methods. The results are tabulated in Table 1 together with other physical-chemical and energetic properties of interest.

With impact sensitivities of 34.79 J, the cesium salt 5 can be classified as less sensitive. The remaining salts can be considered as insensitive towards impact with a value higher than 40 J. For friction sensitivity, nearly the same trends as those for the impact sensitivity can be observed. All investigated salts can be considered as insensitive with regard to their friction sensitivities even higher than 360 N. All metal salts have good thermal stabilities and show decomposition temperatures (215.0–328.2 °C) significantly higher than previously reported nitrogen-rich salts with tetrazolone-based anions (generally 190 °C), even higher than neutral tetrazolone (239 °C)28 in the case of 1, 2, 3, 6 and 7. Most of them have hydrate water, which participates in hydrogen bonds and thus contributes to their thermal stabilities and low sensitivities.

These tetrazolone based compounds have high content of nitrogen and oxygen (even up to 80.0%) and negative oxygen balances. They also exhibit comparatively high densities except compound 1 and 6 ranging from 1.872 to 3.060 g cm−3, which could be explained by the stronger attractive ionic forces. Heat of formation is a significant parameter for energetic compounds. The constant-volume heat of combustion was measured by oxygen bomb calorimetry. Then, the heat of formation (ΔfH°) at 298.15 K was calculated on the basis of combustion reactions and the Hess thermochemical cycle. All of the salts investigated exhibit negative heats of formation varying between −80.4 (4) and −2129.2 kJ mol−1 (7) and show energetic characteristics as metal salts.

In addition, all metal salts of tetrazolone were tested in the “flame test”, where a few milligrams of sample was placed on a metal spatula held in the open flame of a Bunsen burner. Fast heating of the alkali metal salts results in normal burning by the characteristic color of the metal cation (i.e., fuchsia (1), yellow (2), lavender (3), purple (4), blue (5)) whereas the alkaline earth metal salts burn slightly more vigorously, also giving the corresponding flame color of the metal center (i.e., white (6), brick-red (7) magenta (8), green (9)).

Conclusions

Tetrazolone (5-oxotetrazole) was synthesized by a moderate strategy starting with benzyl isocyanate through addition, cyclization and catalytic hydrogenation, avoiding the uncontrolled explosion of the intermediate diazonium, as reported during previous preparations. Its alkali and alkaline earth metal salts with lithium (1), sodium (2), potassium (3), rubidium (4) caesium (5), magnesium (6), calcium (7), strontium (8) and barium (9) were prepared and well characterized using elemental analysis, IR and NMR spectroscopy, DSC and TG analysis. Single-crystal X-ray measurements were accomplished for delivering insight into structural characteristics and the inter- and intra-molecular interactions present in the tetrazolone-based salts. Most compounds have strong hydrogen bond interactions; furthermore, the crystal structures of 7, 8 and 9 reveal interesting porous energetic coordination polymers. They also exhibit comparatively high densities except for salt 1 and 6, with 1.872 g cm−3 for salt 2 and 3.060 g cm−3 for salt 5. All new salts have good thermal stabilities (up to 328.2 °C), significantly higher than those of the reported nitrogen-rich salt, even higher than that of neutral tetrazolone (239 °C) in the case of 1, 2, 3, 6 and 7. The heat of formation was evaluated by experimental measurement of oxygen bomb calorimetry. Sensitivity tests indicate that these tetrazolone-based metal salts can be classified as insensitive with impact sensitivities of 40 J and friction sensitivity of >360 N. In addition, the flame test gave corresponding flame color of the metal center (i.e., fuchsia (1), yellow (2), lavender (3), purple (4), blue (5), white (6), brick-red (7) magenta (8), green (9)), suggesting that they could be used as potential perchlorate-free pyrotechnics or environmentally friendly insensitive materials.

Experimental

General

All chemicals were used as obtained from commercial suppliers. Melting and decomposition points were determined by differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) with a PerkinElmer (PE) STA-6000 apparatus at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. Calorimetric measurements were performed with a Parr-6200 bomb calorimeter. 1H and 13C NMR spectra were recorded using a Bruker instrument. The chemical shifts were quoted in ppm relative to tetramethylsilane (1H, 13C). Infrared spectra were measured using a Nicolet Nexus 470 FT-IR spectrometer as KBr pellets. Mass spectra were measured using an Agilent 500-MS. Elemental analysis was performed on an Elementar Vario El III analyzer. Impact sensitivities were tested by fall hammer apparatus applying standard staircase method using a 2 kg drop weight, and the results were reported in terms of height for 50% probability of explosion (h50%). Collection of XRD data was performed on a Rigaku Saturn 724+ CCD diffractometer equipped with graphite monochromatized Mo Kα radiation. The structure was solved with SHELXS-9733 refined with SHELXL-97.34 The non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically and the hydrogen atoms were located and freely refined.
