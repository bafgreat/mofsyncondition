Introduction

In recent decades, metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have been extensively studied due to their various potential applications such as magnetism,1 luminescence,2 catalysis,3 gas storage4 and in terms of their topological features.5 Particularly, interest in rationally designing new MOFs continues to increase,6 partly motivated by their unusual structures and versatile properties. Therefore, various effective strategies, such as tuning inorganic anions,7 pH values,8 templates or solvent effect,9 as well as hydrothermal,10in situ,11 ionothermal synthesis12 and so on, have already been well documented. Among these strategies, the introduction of the second ligand13 plays a key role in constructing intriguing topology patterns and multi-functional materials, and the carboxylic acid and 4,4′-bipyridine (bpy) co-ligands in MOF materials were frequently observed.14,15 However, due to the competitive interactions between different ligands and metal ions, it is a challenge to embed three kinds of organic ligands into one MOF, and to our knowledge, the MOFs containing three kinds of organic ligands were rarely reported.16

To investigate the structures and properties of MOFs fabricated by multiple organic ligands and metal ions, in this work, the three kinds of organic ligands in Scheme 1 were chosen to prepare the products based on the following considerations. Firstly, the multicarboxyl ligand H2CMA has both a rigid backbone and a flexible arm (–OCH2–COO–) with a suitable length, indicative of the many coordination modes and structural diversities.17 Secondly, the rigid rod-like spacer 4,4′-bipyridine has proven to be a good candidate in the synthesis of mixed-ligand metal organic frameworks, leading to a high dimensional compound. Finally, the structure of HDPA is very similar to that of H2CMA, with only a –CH3 group being substituted for the carboxyl group in the benzene ring. As a result, both have a similar steric hindrance effect and the strong coordination ability of the flexible arm (–OCH2–COO–) may enhance the possibility that HDPA and H2CMA coordinate with the metal ions simultaneously. Fortunately, by the different combinations of these ligands, five novel MOFs, {[Cu(CMA)]·0.8H2O}n (1), {[Cu(CMA)(bpy)0.5]·0.5DMF}n (2), {[Cu(CMA)(bpy)(H2O)]·0.5H2O}n (3), {[Cu2(CMA)(DPA)(bpy)2(H2O)]ClO4}n (4) and {[Cu3(CMA)(DPA)(bpy)4(OH)](ClO4)2·3H2O}n (5) (H2CMA = 3-(carboxymethoxy)-5-methylbenzoic acid, HDPA = 2-(3,5-dimethylphenoxy)acetic acid, bpy = 4,4′-bipyridine) were obtained and structurally characterized. 1 contains the single-ligand of CMA2−; both 2 and 3 contain two kinds of ligands (CMA2− and bpy); both 4 and 5 comprise three kinds of ligands (CMA2−, DPA− and bpy). 1–3, and 5 are three-dimensional (3D) frameworks, while 4 is a 2D layer. Interestingly, 1 possesses two types of channels: six-star and hexagonal ones. 3 displays a three-fold interpenetrating 3D framework. The structural analyses reveal that the structural divergence of the compounds may have originated from different anions, solvents and temperatures. Magnetic investigations on 1, 2 and 4 were carried out, and the significant antiferromagnetic couplings between adjacent Cu2+ in these compounds were observed.

Experimental section

Materials and physical measurements

All the chemicals used for synthesis are of analytical grade and commercially available without purification. Analyses for C, H, and N were carried out with a Perkin-Elmer 240C analyzer. Variable-temperature magnetic susceptibilities were measured on a SQUID MPMS XL-7 magnetometer. Diamagnetic corrections were made with Pascal's constants for all the constituent atoms. Powder X-ray diffractions were recorded on a D/Max-2500 X-ray diffractometer using Cu Kα radiation. Simulations of the PXRD patterns were measured by the single-crystal data and the diffraction-crystal module of the Mercury (Hg) program.

Preparation of complexes 1–5

{[Cu(CMA)]·0.8H2O}n (1)

A mixture of Cu2(OH)2CO3·H2O (0.1434 g, 0.6 mmol), H2CMA (0.0840 g, 0.4 mmol), HDPA (0.0180 g, 0.1 mmol), bpy (0.0192 g, 0.1 mmol), H2O (6 mL) and C2H5OH (4 mL) were sealed in a 25 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel autoclave and heated at 100 °C for 3 days, and then slowly cooled to room temperature in 3 days. Finally, green block crystals for X-ray crystallographic analysis were obtained in 43% yield based on Cu. Elemental analysis, calc. (%): C, 41.94; H, 3.35. Found (%): C, 42.01; H, 3.28.

{[Cu(CMA)(bpy)0.5]·0.5DMF}n (2) and {[Cu(CMA)(bpy)(H2O)]·0.5H2O}n (3)

A mixture of Cu(Ac)2·H2O (0.0200 g, 0.1 mmol), H2CMA (0.0210 g, 0.1 mmol), HDPA (0.0180 g, 0.1 mmol), bpy (0.0196 g, 0.1 mmol), H2O (3 mL) and DMF (2 mL) were sealed in a 25 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel autoclave and heated at 100 °C for 3 days, and after slowly cooled to room temperature in 3 days, green block crystals 2 were obtained. When H2O (10 mL) was used as solvent, blue plate crystals 3 were attained. The yield of 2 is 66% based on Cu. Elemental analysis, calc. (%): C, 56.04; H, 4.39; N, 7.93. Found (%): C, 56.11; H, 4.30; N, 7.98. The yield of 3 is 75% based on Cu. Elemental analysis, calc. (%): C, 52.76; H, 4.18; N, 6.15. Found (%): C, 52.71; H, 4.20; N, 6.18.

{[Cu2(CMA)(DPA)(bpy)2(H2O)]ClO4}n (4) and {[Cu3(CMA)(DPA)(bpy)4(OH)](ClO4)2·3H2O)}n (5)

A mixture of Cu(ClO4)2·6H2O (0.1113 g, 0.3 mmol), H2CMA (0.0420 g, 0.2 mmol), HDPA (0.0360 g, 0.2 mmol), bpy (0.0384 g, 0.2 mmol) and H2O (10 mL) were sealed in a 25 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel autoclave and heated at 130 °C for 3 days, and after slowly cooled to room temperature in 3 days, green needle crystals 4 were attained. Yield: 17% (based on Cu). Calc. (%): C, 50.83; H, 3.92; N, 5.93. Found (%): C, 50.79; H, 3.98; N, 5.88. When heated at 100 °C for 3 days, then slowly cooled to room temperature in 3 days, blue block crystals 5 were obtained. Calc. (%): C, 48.92; H, 3.94; N, 7.61. Found (%): C, 48.88; H, 3.91; N, 3.89. Unfortunately, the yield of 5 is extremely low and only a few crystals could be obtained each time. Thus, it is very difficult to collect enough samples and as a result, the measurements of XRPD, TGA and magnetic properties of 5 are not available. However, the structure of 5 is rather interesting and is used to explore the structural diversity among 1–5.

X-ray structure crystallography

Single crystals of compounds 1–5 were mounted on an Oxford Diffractometer SuperNova TM with a graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) using the ω-scan technique (1, 2 and 4 at 122.3(2) K, 3, 5 at 293(2) K). All the structures were solved by direct methods and refined anisotropically by full-matrix least-squares techniques based on F2 using the SHELXS-97 and SHELXL-97 programs.18 Anisotropic thermal parameters were assigned to all non-hydrogen atoms. The hydrogen atoms were set in calculated positions and refined as riding atoms with a common fixed isotropic thermal parameter. For 1–5, the important crystal parameters, data collection, and refinements are summarized in Table 1.

Moreover, due to the high symmetry of the space group and low residual electron density, the positions of solvent cannot be determined by single crystal X-ray diffraction techniques, and therefore the SQUEEZE routine in the PLATON software package was applied. Detailed information is displayed in ESI† and CIF.

Results and discussion

Synthesis of complexes

During the synthesis, inorganic anions, solvents and temperature play different roles respectively (Scheme 2). Firstly, anion-induced structures change from a 3D framework containing two types of channels of 1, or a 3-fold interpenetrating 3D framework of 3 to a non-interpenetrating 3D framework of 5. Secondly, solvent-induced structures alter from a non-interpenetrating 3D framework of 2 to a 3-fold interpenetrating 3D framework of 3. Thirdly, temperature-induced structures transform from a 2D layer of 4 to a 3D framework of 5.

Description of crystal structures

{[Cu(CMA)]·0.8H2O}n (1)

Single crystal X-ray diffraction results reveal that compound 1 belongs to the trigonal system with space group Pc1. Each asymmetric unit includes one CMA2−, one Cu2+ and 0.8 isolated water molecules. Cu2+ is six-coordinated in a slightly stretched octahedral geometry defined by six O atoms (O1, O1A, O2B, O3, O4C, and O5D) (Fig. 1). The two O atoms in the axial positions weakly coordinate with Cu2+, and the Cu–O bond lengths are 2.438(3) and 2.655(3) Å, respectively, which may result from the Jahn–Teller effect. The lengths of Cu–O bonds in the equatorial plane fall in the range of 1.921(3)–2.002(3) Å. Two adjacent Cu2+ are bridged by four carboxyl groups, forming a paddle-wheel structure with the Cu–Cu distance of 3.2615(13) Å. Each CMA2− anion in 1 is coordinated to four separate Cu2+ through its flexible and rigid arms (Mode A, Scheme 3) which act as the bridges of the paddle-wheel structure. The paddle-wheel units are connected by the carboxyl O atoms of CMA2− into a 1D chain along the c direction, which is further linked by the CMA2− into a 3D framework.

Interestingly, two types of channels A and B exist in 1, which can be clearly seen along the a direction (Fig. 2a). Each six-star A channel has six hexagonal B channels as its neighbor, and the methyl of CMA2− is on the wall of the channel pointing at the center of the six-star. Each hexagonal B channel borders with three A channels and three B channels. Moreover, the cross-section of channel A comprises six Cu atoms, and in contrast, the cross-section of a B channel contains three Cu atoms. The diameter of channel A is 8.8 Å and the diameter of B is 5.5 Å (considering the van der Waals atom radius). The calculated solvent-accessible volume is 9.3% of the crystal structure by PLATON. By considering the paddle-wheel clusters as 6-connected nodes and the CMA2−ligands as 3-connected nodes (Fig. S1†), the framework can be simplified as a 3,6-connected net with the Point symbol {4.62}2{42.69.83.10} and the RCSR three-letter symbol is “csq” (Fig. 2b).

{[Cu(CMA)(bpy)0.5]·0.5DMF}n (2)

Single crystal X-ray diffraction results reveal that compound 2 crystallizes in the monoclinic system with space group C2/c. There are one CMA2−, one Cu2+, half of a coordinated bpy and half of a isolated DMF in an asymmetric unit. Cu2+ is five-coordinated with a distorted square pyramidal geometry defined by N1 in the axial positions and four O donors in the equatorial plane (Fig. 3). The Cu–O bond lengths fall in the range of 1.958(4)–1.980(4) Å and the Cu–N bond length is 2.138(4) Å. The crystallographically independent CMA2− anion in 2 is also connected to four separate Cu2+ through its flexible and rigid arms (Mode B, Scheme 3) acting as a μ2-bridge. Two adjacent Cu2+ are connected by four individual CMA2− into a paddle-wheel structure with the Cu–Cu distance of 2.6533(13) Å (abbr. as [Cu2] unit). CMA2− and [Cu2] units arrange alternately and form a 2D layered structure (Fig. S2†). Finally, the layers are linked by bpy into a 3D framework (Fig. 4a). Channels exist along the c direction and the total potential accessible volume calculated by PLATON, is approximately 17.6% after taking off the solvent molecules. If [Cu2] units and ligands are considered as nodes and lines, respectively, the framework can be simplified into the net with the Point symbol of a crystal lattice {48.66.8}, and the RCSR symbol is “rob” (Fig. 4b).

{[Cu(CMA)(bpy)(H2O)]·0.5H2O}n(3)

Single crystal X-ray diffraction results show that compound 3 belongs to the orthorhombic system with space group Fdd2. Each unit includes one CMA2−, one Cu2+, one coordinated bpy, one coordinated H2O and half of an isolated water molecule. Cu2+ is six-coordinated in a slightly distorted octahedral geometry, in which the equatorial positions are occupied by two O atoms and two N atoms (Fig. 5). The Cu–O bonds fall in the range of 1.997(11)–2.033(9) Å, and the Cu–N bonds are 1.991(9)–2.035(10) Å. The axial positions are occupied by two O atoms from one CMA2− and one coordinated water with bonds 2.494(14) Å and 2.558 (13) Å, which may result from the Jahn–Teller effect. Adopting a μ2-bridging mode (Mode C, Scheme 3), CMA2− is connected to Cu2+ alternately and forms a zig-zag chain, which is further connected by bpy into a 3D framework (Fig. S3†).

Interestingly, a large rhombic window exists along the a direction with the dimensions19.1 × 28.0 Å (Fig. 6a). Moreover, the flexible arm structure and a large porosity always lead to a 3-fold interpenetrating framework. This may be the result of the flexible groups, which can easily adjust their conformations to meet various coordination requirements of the metal ion. If CMA2− and bpy are considered as lines, and Cu2+ is considered as a node, the structure can be simplified into the lattice (Fig. 6b) with the Point symbol {42.84} and the RCSR symbol is “lvt”.

{[Cu2(CMA)(DPA)(bpy)2(H2O)]ClO4}n (4)

Single crystal X-ray diffraction results show that compound 4 belongs to the orthorhombic system with space group P21212. Each asymmetric unit contains two crystallographically independent Cu2+, one CMA2−, one DPA−, two bpy, one coordinated H2O and one isolated perchlorate anion. Cu1 is six-coordinated with a slightly distorted octahedral geometry defined by N1 and N2 in the axial positions (Fig. 7a). In the equatorial plane, Cu1 is coordinated with four O (O3, O5, O8, O9). Cu2 is also six-coordinated defined by four O (O1A, O2A, O4 and O5). In the axial positions, Cu2 is coordinated with N3 and N4 in a slightly distorted octahedral geometry. The Cu1–O bond lengths fall in the range of 1.949(5)–2.470(4) Å and the Cu1–N bond lengths fall in the range of 2.040(7)–2.056(7) Å. The Cu2–O bond lengths fall in the range of 1.954(5)–2.698(5) Å, which is a result of the Jahn–Teller effect and shorter than that reported in the literature,19 and the Cu2–N bond lengths are in the range of 2.029(6)–2.037(6) Å. Identical to 1, each CMA2− anion is also coordinated to four separate Cu2+ having the advantage of flexible and rigid arms (Mode A, Scheme 3), while the CMA2− and Cu2+ do not form a paddle-wheel structure. Cu1 and Cu2 are connected by the flexible arm of CMA2− at a distance of 4.5487(7) Å.

Interestingly, in the [1] direction, two Cu1 and two Cu2 constitute a linear tetranuclear unit (abbr. as [Cu4] unit) with the connection of CMA2−. [Cu4] units construct 2D layers by the connection of bpy (Fig. 7b). If the [Cu4] units are considered as nodes, and CMA2− and bpy are considered as lines, the structure can be simplified into a 2D lattice with the Point symbol {44.62} and the RCSR symbol is “sql” (Fig. S4†).

{[Cu3(CMA)(DPA)(bpy)4(OH)](ClO4)2·3H2O}n (5)

Single crystal X-ray diffraction results reveal that compound 5 belongs to the monoclinic system with space group P21/c. The asymmetric unit contains four crystallographically independent Cu2+, one CMA2−, one DPA−, four bpy, one μ2-OH, two isolated perchlorate anions and three H2O. Cu1 and Cu2 are five-coordinated with a distorted square pyramidal geometry (Fig. 8a). Cu1 is defined by three N (N3, N5, N6) and two O (O4, O9), and Cu2 is also defined by three N (N4, N7, N8) and two O (O9, O5). Cu3 is six-coordinated with a slightly distorted octahedral geometry defined by two N (N1, N1A) and four O donors from the carboxyl of CMA2− and two O atoms from water molecules. In the contrast, Cu4 is also six-coordinated with a slightly distorted octahedral geometry, and it is completed by two N (N2, N2A), two O atoms from the carboxyl of DPA− and two O atoms from water molecules, which is different from Cu3. The Cu–O lengths fall in the range of 1.934(5)–2.216(6) Å (Cu1–O), 1.936(5)–2.155(6) Å (Cu2–O), 1.997(5)–2.484(6) Å (Cu3–O), and 1.986(6)–2.471(8) Å (Cu4–O) (the longer Cu–O bonds may be a result of the Jahn–Teller effect). The Cu–N bonds are 2.040(7)–2.049(6) Å (Cu1–N), 2.039(7)–2.058(6) Å (Cu2–N), 1.980(7) Å (Cu3–N), and 1.979(8) Å (Cu4–N). The crystallographically independent CMA2− in 3 is coordinated to three separate Cu2+ and shows a tridentate coordinated mode (Mode D, Scheme 3).

Interestingly, in the c direction, Cu3 and Cu4, bpy and DPA− form 1D chain. Cu1, Cu2, bpy and μ2-OH constitute a 2D net structure in the ab plane (Fig. S5†), which is perpendicular to the chains. The chains and the planes construct a 3D framework by the connection of the CMA2− (Fig. 8b). The fact that CMA2− coordinates with Cu3 and DPA− coordinates with Cu4 proves our previous assumption that CMA2− and DPA− have a tendency to coordinate with metal ions simultaneously.

XRPD and TGA results

To study the thermal stabilities of these compounds, TG analyses conducted. Samples 1–4 were heated from 25 to 800 °C under a N2 atmosphere with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 (Fig. S6†).

TGA measurements reveal that 1 firstly losses a weight of 4.8% from 25 to 300 °C, which corresponds to the loss of the 0.8 isolated water molecules (calcd 5.0%). Then the weight loss of 66.8% from 300 to 390 °C is ascribed to the decomposition of the CMA ligand (calcd: 67.1%). The final residue of 28.4% is CuO (calcd 27.9%). The TGA curve of 2 shows a weight loss of 9.6% from 25 to 220 °C, which corresponds to the loss of half DMF (calcd 10.2%). The second weight loss of 67.5% in the range 255–410 °C corresponds to the decomposition of all organics (calcd 67.2%). The final residue of 2 is 22.9% at 800 °C, which is assigned to CuO (calcd 22.6%). The first weight loss of 3 is 6.2% from room temperature to 210 °C, corresponding to the loss of half a water molecule (calcd 5.9%). The second weight loss of 76.8% from 210 to 510 °C corresponds to the decomposition of all organics (calcd 76.5%). The final residue 17.0% corresponds to the CuO (calcd 17.6%). The TGA curve of 4 shows a weight loss of 1.8% from 25 to 220 °C, which is ascribed to the loss of one coordinated water molecule (calcd 1.9%). A further weight loss of 81.2% from 220 to 700 °C, corresponds to the decomposition of the organics (calcd 81.2%), the final residue of 4 is 17.0%, which is assigned to CuO (calcd 16.9%).

X-ray powder diffraction (XRPD) patterns have also been carried out for 1–4 to confirm their purities (Fig. S7†). The XRPD patterns for 1–4 are in agreement with the simulated patterns that come from the single crystal data of 1–4, respectively, proving the purities of the samples.

Magnetic properties

The phase purities of 1, 2 and 4 were confirmed by the comparisons between individual experimental and simulated PXRD patterns (Fig. S7†), and their variable temperature magnetic susceptibilities were measured at an applied magnetic field of 1000 Oe in the temperature range 2–300 K (Fig. 9–11). The value of χMT of 1 at 300 K is 0.371 cm3 K mol−1, which is slightly lower than the theoretical value of one Cu2+ (calc. 0.375 cm3 K mol−1 with g = 2). The χMT value decreases slowly from 0.371 to 0.351 cm3 K mol−1 in a temperature range from 300 to 180 K. In the range of 25–180 K, the χMT value sharply decreases with system cooling, which reaches 0.005 cm3 K mol−1 at 25 K and keeps constant below 25 K. The result indicates the presence of antiferromagnetic coupling between Cu2+. The adjacent Cu2+ are bridged by carboxyl groups and oxygen atoms into a 1D alternative chain, and two magnetic couplings parameters J and αJ (α is the alternation parameter) are employed to estimate the magnetic interactions between adjacent Cu2+ (Scheme 4). In the structure of 2, four syn–syn-carboxyl bridges exist in a paddle-wheel [Cu2] unit, giving rise to a very short Cu–Cu distance of only 2.654 Å, which allows for the direct overlap of the magnetic orbitals. The value of χMT of 2 at 300 K is 0.57 cm3 K mol−1, is lower than the theoretical value of two isolated Cu2+ (calc. 0.75 cm3 K mol−1 with g = 2). As the temperature decreases to 2 K, χMT promptly decreases to the minimum of 0.03 cm3 K mol−1, indicating a strong antiferromagnetic coupling between Cu2+. In 2, two magnetic exchange pathways may be considered: one is within the dimer [Cu2] and the other is the CMA2− bridges which connects adjacent dimer entities. Taking into account that the distance between the nearest [Cu2] units (9.160 Å) is much longer than that within the [Cu2] unit (2.965 Å), the exchange interaction between [Cu2] units can be expected to be very weak. Therefore, the magnetic susceptibility data may be fitted to the modified Bleaney–Bowers model21 for S = 1/2 dimers under spin Hamiltonian H = −JS1S2 (2) here, J is the exchange coupling parameter within [Cu2] unit and zj′ stands for the molecular field approximation. The parameter ρ denotes the fraction of paramagnetic impurity in the sample. The best least-squares fitting parameters between 2–300 K give g = 2.21, J = −131 cm−1, ρ = 4.6%, zj′ = −5.1 cm−1, R = 2.17 × 10−5. The result indicates that strong a antiferromagnetic interaction exists in 2.

The χMT value of 4 at 300 K is 1.37 cm3 K mol−1 is lower than the theoretical value of four isolated Cu2+ (1.5 cm3 K mol−1, g = 2). In a range of 100–300 K, the value of χMT decreases smoothly. While cooling the temperature below 100 K, χMT decreases sharply and reaches the minimum of 0.313 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K.

The values of J1 and J2 indicate that an antiferromagnetic interaction exists in 4. Moreover, the distance of Cu1–Cu1* (3.996 Å) is shorter than that of Cu1–Cu2 (4.548 Å), and different bridges exist in Cu1–Cu1* and Cu1–Cu2, which resulted in the magnetic interaction between Cu1 and Cu2 being weaker than that between Cu1 and Cu1*.

Based on the magnetic analyses mentioned above, it is important to explore the magneto-structural relationship in 1, 2 and 4. For 1, and 2, the reported similar structures with a paddle-wheel [Cu2] unit bridged by quadruple carboxyl groups, the magnetic coupling J value and the related structural parameters were summarized in Table 2. The results revealed that syn–syn bridged modes can mediate a strong antiferromagnetic interaction, which is consistent with the previously reported results. Comparably, the J value in 1 is significantly smaller than that in 2 and other similar compounds, and the large divergence could originate from the following factors: (1) the Cu2+ in 1 adopts six-coordinated mode. Three carboxyl bridges in one paddle-wheel [Cu2] unit reside in the equatorial plane, and the remaining one occupies an axial position, which causes the interaction between dz2 and dx2−y2 orbitals of the adjacent Cu2+. As a result, the carboxyl group in the axial position cannot become a good superexchange pathway to mediate a strong magnetic coupling. The Cu2+ in 2 and the other paddle-wheel [Cu2] units are five-coordinated, and the four carboxyl bridges lie in the equatorial planes. (2) The long distance of Cu–Cu with 3.271 Å will result in a relatively weak magnetic coupling, but 2 and other similar structures have only about 2.6 Å of Cu–Cu separation. (3) The large Cu–O–C angle exists in 1, which also exerts a great impact on magnetic interactions between adjacent metal ions.23 For 2, the structural features are similar to the typical paddle-wheel units, and the J value in 2 falls in the normal range. Additionally, in 1, the magnetic coupling (αJ) between adjacent Cu2+ bridged by two oxygen atoms is −8.26 cm−1, which is smaller than that in the compound [Cu2L2](PF6)227 (L = 2-[(bis(2-pyridylmethyl)amino)methyl]-4-methylphenol), possibly resulting from the long Cu–Cu distance of 3.625 Å in 1.

The structures and magnetic exchange parameters of 4 and some reported Cu-compounds with double carboxyl bridges were summarized in Table 3. It was clearly observed that the magnitude of magnetic exchange depends on the bridged modes and the distance between adjacent Cu2+. In 4 and compound [Cu2(phen)2(Hpht)2(NO3)(H2O)]NO3·2H2O,23 two carboxyl groups employ syn–syn bridged modes, giving rise to a strong antiferromagnetic coupling, but the J value in 4 is slightly smaller than that in the compound [Cu2(phen)2(Hpht)2(NO3)(H2O)]NO3·2H2O due to the long Cu–Cu distance in 4. Comparably, both the syn–anti bridged modes of carboxyl groups and the long the Cu–Cu distance should be responsible for their very weak magnetic coupling in the compounds {[Cu(CH3O-ip)(bpa)]·H2O}n28 and [Cu(L3)2(bpetha)]n.29

Conclusion

In summary, five novel metal organic frameworks were obtained by tuning the synthetic factors and structurally characterized. Compound 1 displays a 3D framework with six-star and hexagonal channels. Compound 2 is a 3D framework containing two kinds of organic ligands. Compound 3 is a 3-fold interpenetrating 3D framework. Compound 4 displays a 2D layered structure and 5 shows a 3D framework covering three kinds of organic ligands. Magnetic studies indicate antiferromagnetic coupling between the adjacent copper ions in compounds 1, 2 and 4, and the corresponding magneto-structural relationship was explored. It is hoped that this work will be helpful for synthesizing crystals containing multiple ligands.
