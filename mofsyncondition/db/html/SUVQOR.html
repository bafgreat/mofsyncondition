Introduction

Multi-site coordination ligands/multi-podal ligands such as pyrazolylborates have been very well studied owing to their utility in allowing the formation of a variety of metal complexes.1 One of the features of this family of ligands is that their assembly is modular, in that it consists of linking the pyrazolyl ligands to a central boron support (Chart 1).2 The wide applicability of this design is an attractive feature for the preparation of multi-metallic assemblies. This concept has been used in other systems as well, most notably in cyclophosphazenes3,4 and more recently in cyclocarbophosphazenes.5 Thus, the reactivity of the P–Cl bonds in chlorocyclotriphosphazenes has been exploited in creating several hexapodal multi-site coordination ligands possessing P–N bonds.6 These have been used for the preparation of metal complexes with varying nuclearities and topologies.7 In one instance, the N-methyl hydrazine derivative such as N3P3[N(Me)NH2]6 has been used for assembling a hexa-ferrocene derivative.8 These successes have spurred the investigation of the next generation of cyclophosphazene ligands. Among these, the hexapodal pyridyloxycyclophosphazenes are quite remarkable (Chart 2).9 They possess considerable flexibility in their coordinating arms as a result of the linking oxygen atom that connects the pyridyloxy group to the cyclophosphazene motif. This feature allows greater flexibility of the ligand in comparison with pyrazolyl cyclophosphazenes where the pyrazole group is directly attached to phosphorus. Further, P–O containing ligands are hydrolytically more stable, upon metalation, than the corresponding pyrazolyl cyclophosphazenes,3,4 although in some instances metalation of pyridyloxycyclophosphazenes also leads to hydrolyzed products that are structurally interesting such as [(L′CuCl)2Co(NO3)}Cl] [L′ = P3N3(O-C5H4N-2)5O]9g (ESI†) and dispiro-{P3N3(O2C12H8)2O-(n-Bu2Sn)2(μ3-O)(μ-OMe)}·MeOH (ESI†).10d The coordination behavior of 2-pyridyloxycyclophosphazenes has revealed that the pyridyloxy nitrogen atoms of geminal substituents tend to coordinate to the same metal ion (ESI†).9 While this feature allows predictability in terms of an overall coordination response and generally molecular complexes are isolable, the versatility of the coordinating unit tends to become limited. On the other hand, if the coordinating 3- and 4-pyridyloxy arms on a cyclophosphazene ligand were to coordinate to different metal ions, the overall metal assemblies formed would have far greater structural diversity. This hypothesis was tested recently using dispiro-{P3N3(O2C12H8)2(O-C5H4N-3)2}, dispiro-{P3N3(O2C12H8)2(O-C5H4N-4)2} and spiro-{P3N3(O2C12H8)(O-C5H4N-3)4} (Chart 2) as well as the corresponding carbophosphazenes [NC(NMe2)]2[NP(O-C5H4N-3)2] and [NC(NMe2)]2[NP((O-C5H4N-4)2] (Chart 3).10 These ligands possess a minimum of two pyridyloxy arms and a maximum of four. It was of interest to examine the ligating behavior of the fully substituted derivative, hexakis(3-pyridyloxy)cyclophosphazene (L) (Scheme 1) which possesses six pyridyloxy arms particularly to examine if two-dimensional coordination polymers can be accessed. As anticipated, the coordination polymers, [{N3P3(O-C5H4N-3)6}2{Zn(H2O)3(NO3)}2{Zn(H2O)2(NO3)}2]n[NO3]2n·4nH2O·nCH3OH (1), [{N3P3(O-C5H4N-3)6}2{Zn(H2O)3(NO3)}2{Cu(NO3)}2]n[NO3]2n·4nH2O·nCH3CN (2) and [{N3P3(O-C5H4N-3)6}2{(NO3)Cd-(μ-(NO3)-Cd(NO3)2(μ-(NO3)Cd(NO3)}]·2nCH3OH·2nH2O (3) are formed by metalation of L. While 1 and 2 are 1D coordination polymers, 3 is a 2D coordination polymer. Further, while 1 and 3 are homometallic systems 2 is a rare example of a heterometallic coordination polymer. In addition, a molecular dimer, [{N3P3(O-C5H4N-3)6}2{Cd(H2O)3(NO3)}2][NO3]2·9H2O (4), which is a metallamacrocycle has also been isolated. These results are presented herein.

Experimental section

Materials and instrumentation

Solvents and other general reagents used in this work were purified according to standard procedures.11 Hexachlorocyclotriphosphazene (Nippon Soda, Japan) was recrystallized from n-hexane before use. All the starting materials were used as received from commercial sources. 1H and 31P{1H} NMR spectra were recorded on a JEOL spectrometer operating at 500.0 and 202.5 MHz, respectively. Chemical shifts are reported with respect to internal tetramethylsilane (1H) and external 85% H3PO4 (31P). ESI-MS analyses were performed on a Waters Micromass Quattro Micro triple quadrupole mass spectrometer. Electrospray ionization mechanism in a positive ion full scan mode was used for recording the mass spectra; methanol was used as the solvent and nitrogen gas for desolvation. Capillary voltage was maintained at 3 kV and the cone voltage was kept at 30 V. The temperature maintained for the ion source was 100 °C and for desolvation, 250 °C. IR spectra were recorded as KBr pellets on a Bruker Vector 22 FT-IR spectrophotometer operating from 400 to 4000 cm−1. Electronic spectra were recorded on a Perkin-Elmer-Lambda 20 UV–Vis spectrometer. EPR spectra were recorded on a Bruker EMX 1444 X-band EPR spectrometer (fitted with a quartz Dewar for measurements up to 120 K). The EPR spectra were calibrated with diphenylpicrylhydrazyl, DPPH (g = 2.0037). Elemental analyses of the compounds were obtained on a Thermoquest CE instruments CHNS-O, EA/110 model.

Results and discussion

Synthetic aspects

Hexakis(3-pyridyloxy)cyclotriphosphazene L was synthesized by a nucleophilic substitution reaction involving 3-hydroxypyridine and hexachlorocyclophosphazene using K2CO3 as the base (Scheme 1). The 31P{1H} NMR spectrum of L shows a singlet at 10.1 ppm while its ESI-MS shows a peak at m/z 700.18 for [L + H]+. Even though L was previously known in the literature,9c the coordination behavior of this ligand was completely unexplored.

We explored the coordination behavior of L with various metal ions and we found the formation of a series of coordination polymers (1–3) as well as a molecular complex (4) (Scheme 2). In contrast to the reactivity behavior of 2-pyridyloxycyclophosphazene ligands, L did not form soluble metal complexes with metal halides. Consequently, we chose to probe the reactivity of L with hydrated metal nitrate salts. This strategy was successful and we were able to isolate 1-D (1–2) and 2-D (3) coordination polymers along with a discrete dinuclear complex (4) which is a metallamacrocycle. It may be noted that in none of these instances all the six coordinating arms are involved fully. The maximum participation is limited to five arms in the 2D coordination polymer, 3.

The 1D-coordination polymers [{N3P3(O-C5H4N-3)6}2{Zn(H2O)3(NO3)}2{Zn(H2O)2(NO3)}2]n[NO3]2n·4nH2O·nCH3OH (1) (Scheme 2, Fig. 1 and S12; ESI†) and [{N3P3(O-C5H4N-3)6}2{Zn(H2O)3(NO3)}2{Cu(NO3)}2]n[NO3]2n·4nH2O·nCH3CN (2) (Scheme 2, Fig. 2 and S13; ESI†) were obtained in a solution reaction involving the ligand L and the hydrated metal nitrate salts {Cu(NO3)2·3H2O or Zn(NO3)2·6H2O} (see the Experimental section) (Scheme 2). These 1D coordination polymers are both homometallic [Zn(ii)] and heterometallic [(Zn(ii)/Cu(ii))] and possess L2M3/L2M2M′ cationic repeat units. The charge is balanced by two nitrate counter anions. Depending on the reaction conditions (see the Experimental section) the 2D-coordination polymer [{N3P3(O-C5H4N-3)6}2{(NO3)Cd-(μ-(NO3)-Cd(NO3)2(μ-(NO3)Cd(NO3)}]n·2nCH3OH·3nH2O (3) (Scheme 2, Fig. 4, 5 and S14; ESI†) and the molecular macrocycle [{N3P3(O-C5H4N-3)6}{Cd(H2O)3NO3)}2][NO3]2·9H2O (4) were isolated (Scheme 2, Fig. 6 and S15; ESI†).

Compounds 1 and 2 are soluble in polar, coordinating solvents such as methanol or DMF. However, in these solutions these compounds appear to fall apart as suggested by their ESI-MS data (see the Experimental section). In each case, peaks due to [L + M(NO3)]+ were obtained. The 31P NMR spectrum of 1 reveals a singlet at 10.0 ppm which is very similar to that observed for the ligand (10.1 ppm). However, in view of the fact that in solution the complexes seem to be breaking down it is quite possible that the observed chemical shift of 1 in solution does not represent the solid-state structure.

The ESI-MS of 2 which is a heterometallic compound containing Zn2/Cu motifs showed peaks for [L + Cu(NO3)]+, m/z 824.0179 as well as [L + Zn(NO3)]+, m/z 825.0278 fragments along with [{N3P3(O-C5H4N-3)6}{Zn(NO3)2}{Cu(NO3)}2]2+, m/z 921.0302 (Fig. S1; ESI†). The EPR spectrum of 2 shows a well-resolved four-line axial spectrum in the parallel region corresponding to an elongated octahedral Cu(ii) (d9 configuration) in DMF–toluene glass at −120 °C (Fig. S2; ESI†). The g⊥ value is 2.08 while the g‖ value is 2.51 with a A‖ value of 162 × 10−4 cm−1. Since the g values are greater than 2.0 and also since g‖ > g⊥ a dx2−y2 ground state is suggested.13

The 2D-coordination polymer, [{N3P3(O-C5H4N-3)6}2{(NO3)Cd(μ-(NO3)Cd(NO3)2(μ-(NO3)Cd(NO3)}]·2CH3OH·3H2O]n (3), is obtained in the reaction of L with Cd(NO3)2·4H2O in a room temperature synthesis carried out in chloroform solution. The 2D-coordination polymer 3 (Fig. 4 and 5) shows a singlet in its 31P{1H} NMR spectrum at 16.6 ppm (vide infra) similar to 4. However, in view of the fact that 3, like 4, shows probable dissociation in the solution, and shows a prominent peak in the ESI-MS at 874.999 [L + Cd(NO3)]+, it is likely that the solution 31P{1H} NMR spectrum is not representative of the solid-state structure of this compound.

The reaction of L with Cd(NO3)2·6H2O under hydrothermal conditions did not yield any crystals after the reaction. However, when the reaction mixture (a clear solution) obtained under these conditions was allowed to slowly crystallize at ambient temperature, a molecular dinuclear metallamacrocycle [{N3P3(O-C5H4N-3)6}2{Cd(H2O)3(NO3)}]2[NO3]2·9H2O] (4) crystallized out in reasonable yield (see the Experimental section). The metallamacrocycle is formed as a result of two cyclophosphazene ligands interacting with two metal ion motifs similar to that of the dimeric building unit of the compounds 1 and 2 (Scheme 2, Fig. 6).

The molecular metallamacrocycle 4 shows a peak due to the fragment [L + M(NO3)]+ in its ESI-MS, suggesting a breakdown of the dimer in solution. 4 shows a singlet in its 31P{1H} NMR spectrum at 16.6 ppm (cf. the ligand L is seen as a singlet at 10.1 ppm). The above solution state characterization reveals that 4 reveals a similar behavior as that of 3, indicating that these two compounds are two different kinetically trapped structural entities of the constitutional dynamic assembly system of ligand L and Cd(NO3)2·4H2O.14 It is noteworthy to mention that different kinetically isolated products, a discrete tetramer and an 1D-coordination polymer, from different metal ions have been previously reported by us utilizing the same tetrakis(3-pyridyloxy)cyclophosphazene ligand.10c

X-ray crystallography

1-D coordination polymers 1 and 2

As mentioned above, 1 and 2 are one-dimensional coordination polymers. The repeat unit of these polymers contains a 20-membered dimetallamacrocycle [L2{Zn(NO3)(H2O))4}2] and an inter-connecting [Zn(H2O)4(NO3)2] ion motif (for example, for 1 see Scheme 2, Fig. 1 and S12; ESI†). This structural motif is similar to that found in the previously reported complex, [{L1}2{Cd(CH3OH)2(NO3)2}2{Cd(NO3)2(H2O)}]n·3nCH3OH·3nH2O; L1 = spiro-N3P3(O2C12H8)(O-C5H4N-3)4}(Fig. 3).10c The formation of the dimetallamacrocycle occurs in the following manner. A pair of cyclophosphazene ligands are involved in coordination to two Zn(ii) centres to form the macrocycle. Thus, each of the Zn(ii) ions present in the macrocycle is bound by two geminal pyridyloxy arms emanating from a cyclophosphazene ligand on either side. The macrocycles, thus formed, are connected to each other through six-coordinated Zn(ii) motifs, the linkage being achieved as a result of the coordination action of a pyridyloxy arm of the cyclophosphazene ligand. Thus, the formation of 1 involves two cyclophosphazene ligands per repeat unit with the contribution of three pyridyloxy arms per cyclophosphazene unit. All the Zn(ii) ions present in the coordination polymer are six-coordinate. While those present in the macrocycle possess in their coordination sphere three water molecules and a monodentate nitrate ion along with two pyridyloxy nitrogen atoms, the connector Zn(ii) ions are also six-coordinate and only differ in that they contain an additional nitrate ion in the place of a water molecule. The immediate coordination environment in Zn(ii) ions and the bond parameters are shown in Table S1 (ESI†). The selected bond parameters of 1 are summarized in the caption of Fig. 1.

Compound 2 is also a 1D coordination polymer containing 20-membered dimetallamacrocycle motifs, [L2{Zn(NO3)(H2O)4}2]. However, the connecting part that links the metallamacrocycles is a [Cu(NO3)2] unit. Unlike the connector unit in 1, in 2, the connector Cu(ii) is flanked by each of the adjacent 3-pyridyloxy groups from the ligand L (Scheme 2, Fig. 2 and S13; ESI†). Thus, the coordination environment around the Cu(ii) centre is composed of four pyridyloxy nitrogen atoms and two unidentate nitrate ligands (4N, 2O) (Table S1†). The Cu(ii) centre in 2 is a Jahn–Teller ion and consequently the equatorial Cu–N distances are much shorter (av. 2.026 (1) Å) compared to the axial Cu–O bond distances (av. 2.519 (1) Å) (Fig. 2 and Table S1; ESI†). This feature also allows us to distinguish between Cu(ii) and Zn(ii) in this complex. The participation of the extra non-geminal pyridyloxy group (one each in opposite directions) in 2 induces helicity. Consequently, the one-dimensional coordination polymer acquires a double-stranded helix-like structure (Fig. S3, ESI†). Here the Cu(ii) ions act as nodes, the repeating length between two such Cu(ii) ions being 20.9 Å.

2D-coordination polymer (3)

The reaction of hydrated cadmium(ii) nitrate with L under room temperature reaction conditions affords a 2D coordination polymer, [{N3P3(O-C5H4N-3)6}2{(NO3)Cd(μ-(NO3)Cd(NO3)2(μ-(NO3)Cd(NO3)}]n·2nCH3OH·3nH2O (3). Unlike the structural types described, thus far, in this work, 3 is a 2D-coordination polymer that comprises of a trimeric cadmium nitrate {(NO3)Cd-(μ-(NO3)-Cd(NO3)2(μ-(NO3)Cd(NO3)}building block (Fig. 4 and S14; ESI†). Also, in the case of 3, five of the six pyridyloxy arms of the cyclophosphazene ligand are involved in coordination which occurs at four cadmium centres (Fig. 5 and S14; ESI†).

The 2D coordination polymer comprises of several sub-units comprising of varied sized (12, 16, 20, 24, 26 and 32) macrocyclic rings (see the ESI†). Similar to the instances described above for 1 and 2 as well as for the situation in 4, as described below, in 3 also a 20-membered dimetallamacrocycle (Fig. S6; ESI†) (see below, Fig. 4) is formed involving a pair of cyclophosphazene ligands (geminal pyridyloxy coordinating groups) and two cadmium centres. However, one notable difference found in 3, in comparison with 1, 2, and 4 is that the metal (cadmium) centers have additional pyridyloxy coordinating ligands. This results in a distortion of the 20-membered macrocycle. The N–Cd–N angles in the latter are more acute (91.1°) in comparison with 1, 2 and 4 where they are almost linear. The additional coordination of the pyridyloxy groups in 3 generates new 12-membered rings (Fig. S4; ESI†), one on either side of the 20-membered ring, in the opposite directions. Simultaneously a 24-membered ring is also generated (Fig. S7 and S8; ESI†). The macrocyclic system described so far is taken into a 1D polymer along the crystallographic ‘b’ axis through the involvement of neighbouring ligands. The bridging coordination action of the nitrate anion of the 24 membered metallamacrocycle [{L2{Cd(NO3)2}2}] in opposite directions leads to the formation of the 2D sheets in the crystallographic ‘bc’ plane. This also simultaneously results in the formation of the trimeric cadmium unit {(NO3)Cd-(μ-(NO3)-Cd(NO3)2(μ-(NO3)Cd(NO3)} (Fig. 4) along with the formation of 16-, 26- and 32-membered metallamacrocycles (Fig. S5, S9 and S10; ESI†).

The tricadmium motif of the 2D-polymer (Fig. 4) comprises of two terminal cadmium(ii) ions each containing a chelating anisobidentate nitrate ligand. These terminal cadmium ions are bridged to a central cadmium(ii) ion by bridging nitrate groups (Fig. 4). The central cadmium(ii) ion, in addition, has two monodentate nitrate ligands (Fig. 4). The terminal cadmium ions are coordinated on one side by two non-geminal pyridyloxy arms of a cyclophosphazene ligand and on the other side by two pyridyloxy nitrogen atoms derived from two different cyclophosphazene ligands. On the other hand, the central cadmium(ii) ion is bound only by two pyridyloxy arms derived from two different cyclophosphazene ligands. As a result of this coordination behaviour, the terminal cadmium ions are heptacoordinate (4N, 3O) while the central cadmium ion is hexacoordinate (2N, 4O). The bond parameters for 3 are summarized in Table S1 (ESI†).

Metallamacrocyclic dimer 4

The reaction of hydrated cadmium(ii) nitrate with L under hydrothermal reaction conditions at 120 °C afforded a discrete metallamacrocyclic dimer 4. This 20-membered metallamacrocycle is the basic building block of the various coordination polymers discussed above as well as those obtained from tetrakis- and hexakis(3-pyridyloxy)cyclophosphazene ligands (Fig. 6 and S15; ESI†). The Cd(ii) ions in 4 are heptacoordinate (2N, 5O) in a pentagonal bipyramidal geometry (Table S1; ESI†). The two pyridyl nitrogen atoms derived from two different cyclophosphazene ligands are present in the trans axial positions while the five oxygen atoms from the water are present in the equatorial plane. An anisobidentate chelating nitrate ligand and three coordinating solvent molecules contribute to the coordination in the equatorial plane. The latter assist in the termination of the compound into a molecular form, while their replacement by pyridyloxy nitrogen/nitrate ligands results in the formation of the 2D-coordination polymer 3 as discussed above. The bond distances and angles of 4 are summarized in Table S1† and in the caption of Fig. 6.

Comparison of the structural features of the cyclophosphazene ring in L and 1–4

The average P–N distance in L is 1.576 (3) Å. The average ring N–P–N angle is 118.0° while the average P–N–P angle is slightly wider, 121.9°. It must be mentioned here that in contrast to the situation found in metal complexes formed with pyrazolyl cyclophosphazenes,3,4 metalation does not cause any distortion in the cyclophosphazene ring skeleton in L. This feature is characteristic of the pyridyloxy cyclophosphazenes9,10 and has been attributed to the flexibility of the coordinating pyridyloxy arms. It has been argued that the spacer oxygen atom acts as a hinge and provides rotational flexibility to the coordinating arm.9 Because of this the ligand does not encounter any geometric constraint upon coordination to the metal ion. The P–N ring bond distances as well as the bond angles do not vary very much from the corresponding values of the ligand L (Table S2; ESI†). Since there is no direct involvement of the cyclophosphazene ring nitrogen towards the coordination, there is not much deviation in the mean plane of the cyclophosphazene ring (Table S3; ESI†).

Thermal analysis

Thermogravimetric traces of compound L and complexes 1–4 are given in Fig. S10 (ESI†). Under TGA conditions the ligand L continually loses weight until 400 °C where it reaches a char residue of 56% that remains almost constant even up to 700 °C. Compounds 1–4 show an initial weight loss of 2 to 6% between 110 and 180 °C (loss of uncoordinated solvent molecules) and a weight loss of 6 to 12% between 180 and 215 °C (further loss of coordinated solvent molecules). From this point onwards another weight loss of around 15 to 20% is observed up to 370 °C. The final char yields at 700 °C are reasonable indicating the thermal stability of these compounds: 1 (54%), 2 (66%), 3 (61%), 4 (56%).

Summary

In conclusion, N3P3(O-C5H4N-3)6 (L) has been shown to be an efficient multi-site coordination ligand towards various transition metal ions. Formation of 1D- and 2D-coordination polymers as well as a molecular dinuclear metallamacrocycle has been observed. All of these systems are characterized by the presence of a signature 20-membered macrocyclic motif comprising of two metal centres and a pair of cyclophosphazene ligands. The latter coordinate to the metal centres through two geminal pyridyloxy groups.

The architecture of L, where the spacer oxygen atoms separate the coordinating pyridyloxy substituents from the cyclophosphazene ring, allows a strain-free coordination response from the ligand. An interesting corollary of the latter feature is the ease with which the coordination response is modulated depending on the metal environment. Thus, a six coordinated M(ii) atom interacts with L to form a 1D-coordination polymer which contains 20-membered macrocycle motifs interconnected by single metal ion connectors (1, 2). A metal ion possessing higher coordination numbers, such as Cd(ii), generates a 2D-polymer (3). On the other hand, a variation of reaction conditions results in the formation of the molecular dinuclear metallamacrocycle (4) which happens to be the structural sub-unit of the coordination polymers. It is clear that the pyridyloxy cyclophosphazenes have much to offer in the construction of new coordination assemblies including MOF-like compounds.
