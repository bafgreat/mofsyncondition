1 Introduction

With the speedy advance of industry, toxic inorganic and organic pollutants have aroused remarkable research interest.1–5 Levofloxacin hydrochloride (LEV), belonging to the family of fluoroquinolone drugs, is widely exploited for antiseptic infections in soft tissues and the nervous system caused by Gram-positive or Gram-negative bacteria.6 The environmental antibiotic pollution and misuse of antibiotics have provoked deep concern, including acute and chronic poisoning, cancer, and other side effects.7–9 In general, benzaldehyde (BZH) is an essential chemical raw substance and pharmaceutical intermediate for the synthesis of ephedrine and chloramphenicol; however, BZH can stimulate the mucous membrane of the eyes and respiratory tract to some degree, and it is combustible when exposed to high heat or naked fire.10,11 Iron ions (Fe3+) are crucial in natural systems, e.g., oxygen transport, electron transfer, and cell metabolism.12–14 Nevertheless, absorption of excess Fe3+ leads to diabetes, liver and heart disease, and certain tumor symptoms.15 Compared with traditional detection methods for the contaminant, including atomic absorption spectroscopy, X-ray dispersion, voltammetry and ion mobility spectroscopy (IMS), luminescent chemosensors with quick-response, high simplicity, sensitivity, selectivity, portability, inexpensive nature, and ease of operation are in high demand for pollutant monitoring.16–19 Consequently, developing well-designed luminescent sensors for detecting levofloxacin, benzaldehyde, and Fe3+ ions is necessary and has attracted great attention.

Recently, metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have gained extensive attention because of their aesthetic topological features and potential applications in gas adsorbents and separation, drug delivery, chemical sensing, heterogeneous catalysis, and so on.20–23 They are essential for the rational assembly of selected metal centers and organic ligands.24–27 The d10 transition metals like Cd(ii) are the more frequently selected cations for the establishment of MOFs owing to their large radii, various coordination modes, and unique photophysical properties.28 Multidentate N- or O-donor ligands have sought-after potential in coordination frameworks.29 Among diverse organic ligands, flexible bis(pyridylbenzimidazole) derivatives are crucially assembling components, as their motivating special qualities include powerful coordination capability, abounding coordination types, diverse organic frames, π–π stacking interactions, and diverse configurations.30 Meanwhile, 2,5-thiophenedicarboxylic acid (H2tdc) presents assorted bridging and chelating styles and can be regarded as linkers or nodes to construct new MOFs.31–33

In this work, a mixed-ligand synthetic approach was adopted to prepare two innovative cadmium(ii) MOFs, namely, [Cd2(L1)(tdc)2(H2O)]n (1) and [Cd(L2)0.5(tdc)]n (2) (L1 = 1,5-bis(1-(pyridine-4-ylmethyl)-1H-benzo[d]imidazol-2-yl)pentane and L2 = 1,6-bis(1-(pyridine-4-ylmethyl)-1H-benzo[d]imidazol-2-yl)hexane). The crystal structures of these MOFs were presented. 1 and 2 possess excellent water, thermal and pH stability (pH = 3–12). Both of them are the first multi-responsive MOFs for sensing levofloxacin, benzaldehyde, and Fe3+ ions. The detection mechanisms of the two MOFs were investigated in detail.

2 Experimental

2.1 Synthesis of [Cd2(L1)(tdc)2(H2O)]n (1)

1 was hydrothermally synthesized with Cd(OAc)2 (53.3 mg, 0.2 mmol), H2tdc (34.4 mg, 0.2 mmol), L1 ligand (48.5 mg, 0.1 mmol), and 10 mL of distilled H2O in a Teflon autoclave reactor and heated at 140 °C for 3 days. The reaction mixture was then cooled to room temperature at a rate of 5 °C h−1. Colorless block crystals of 1 were collected in about 50.2% yield based on L1. Fourier transform infrared (FT-IR) (KBr pellet, cm−1): 3352 w, 1615 m, 1585 s, 1563 s, 1464 m, 1426 s, 1367 s, 824 w, 765 m, 478 w. Elemental analysis (%) calcd for C43H36Cd2N6O10S2 (Fw: 1085.70): C, 47.57; H, 3.34; N, 7.74. Found (%): C, 47.62; H, 3.56; N, 7.54.

2.2 Synthesis of [Cd(L2)0.5(tdc)]n (2)

MOF 2 was analogous to 1, except for the use of L2 (53.3 mg, 0.1 mmol) instead of L1. The yield was about 48.6% based on L2. FT-IR (KBr pellet, cm−1): 1616 w, 1547 s, 1528 s, 1424 m, 1379 s, 1341 m, 1016 w, 820 w, 775 m, 747 w. Elemental analysis (%) calcd for C22H18CdN3O4S (Fw: 532.85): C, 49.59; H, 3.40; N, 7.89. Found (%): C, 49.65; H, 3.23; N, 7.42.

2.3 Fluorescence sensing experiments

The luminescence responses of antibiotics were investigated according to literature approaches.34 4.0 mg of 1/2 finely ground powder samples were separately added into 4 mL aqueous solutions of 0.1 mM antibiotics to obtain suspensions by an ultrasound treatment, including amoxicillin (AMX), chlortetracycline (CTC), ciprofloxacin (CIP), doxycycline hyclate (DH), metronidazole (MDZ), nitrofurantoin (NFT), norfloxacin (NOR), oxytetracycline (OTC), ronidazole (RNZ), sulfadiazine (SDZ), sulfamethazine (SMZ), sulfamethoxazole (SMX), tetracycline (TC) and levofloxacin (LEV). Like the above method, the samples (4 mg) were immersed in 4 mL of various solvents (acetonitrile (MeCN), cyclohexane (CY), dichloromethane (DCM), ethanol (EtOH), ethylene glycol (EG), glycol (GL), methanol (MeOH), n-butanol (NB), n-methyl pyrrolidone (NMP) and BZH). The blank experiments were performed by the suspension of the as-synthesized samples in water. As for ions, the samples (4.0 mg) were powdered and suspended in EtOH–M(NO3)n (1 : 1, 4.0 mL, 0.5 mM) (M = Na+, K+, Mg2+, Ca2+, Sr2+, La3+, Sm3+, Gd3+, Tb3+, Er3+, Pb2+ and Fe3+) and dispersed in EtOH–water (1 : 1) for blank experiments. The resulting mixtures were ultrasonicated for 30 min to form stable turbid suspensions. Q = (I0 − I)/I0 × 100% (1) where Q is quenching efficiency, and I0 and I are the maximum luminescence intensities before and after the addition of the targeted species.

3 Results and discussion

3.1 Crystal structure of [Cd2(L1)(tdc)2(H2O)]n (1)

1 is crystallized in the monoclinic system I2/a. The asymmetric unit comprises one L1 ligand, two Cd(ii) centers, two tdc2− ligands, and two coordinated water molecules (Fig. 1a). The six-coordinated Cd1 center, which may be due to the presence of more defused orbitals, establishes a distorted octahedral {CdNO5} coordination geometry formed in one oxygen (O1W) atom from a water ligand, one nitrogen atom (N1) from the L1 ligand, four oxygen atoms (O1, O5, O7A, and O8A) from one chelate and two monodentate carboxyl groups.35 The Cd2 center is coordinated with two N atoms (N3B, N6B) from the same L1 ligand, one O atom (O2W) from the coordinated water, and two O atoms (O3A and O4A) from two different tdc2− ions (symmetry code: A = x, y + 1, z; B = 0.5 + x, 2 − y, z). The bond angles of Cd(ii) centers are from 93.06(1)° to 174.30(1)°, and the Cd–O/N bond distances are from 2.189(3) Å to 2.492(3) Å, which are comparable to the ranges of Cd(ii) complexes reported previously.36

The O-donor tdc2− ligands act as two coordination modes: (a) (κ1-κ0)-(κ1-κ1)-μ2 and (b) (κ1-κ1)-(κ1-κ1)-μ3, which allow the adjacent cadmium(ii) atoms to stretch into an infinite 1D double straight-line [Cd(tdc)]n chain (Fig. S1†). The 2D lattice is shaped by bridging neighboring 1D chains via L1 ligands (cis-conformation) on two sides (Fig. 1b). The examination was performed through the simplified underlying net for topological classification of the 2D metal–organic network in 1.37–41 The topological network was produced by omitting the terminal H2O ligands and reducing all bridged organic parts to their centroids, forming a binodal 3,4-connecting layer, with the 4-connected Cd1 and topologically equivalent 3-connected Cd2 and (κ1-κ1)-(κ1-κ1)-μ3 tdc2− nodes, as well as the 2-connected (κ1-κ0)-(κ1-κ1)-μ2 tdc2− and L1 linkers. Topological analysis of the sheet exposes a rare cem-d (Shubnikov plane net (33·42)/dual) network with the point symbol of {53}2{54·82} (Fig. 1c).

3.2 Crystal structure of [Cd(L2)0.5(tdc)]n (2)

2 falls within the monoclinic P21/n space group, and its asymmetric unit consists of one tdc2− anion, one L2 ligand, and one Cd(ii) atom. As shown in Fig. 2a, each Cd atom assumes a slightly distorted octahedral structure, being arranged by two nitrogen atoms (N1 and N3C) from two different L2 ligands and four oxygen atoms (O1, O2, O3A, and O4A) from two bis-chelating ligand mode tdc2− ligands (symmetry code: A = 0.5 + x, 0.5 − y, z − 0.5; B = 2 − x, 1 − y, −z; C = 2 − x, 1 − y, 1 − z). The Cd–O/N bond distances range from 2.270(2) to 2.370(2) Å, in good agreement with that found in Cd-MOFs.36

In 2, tdc2− ligands implement a (κ1-κ1)-(κ1-κ1)-μ2 coordination mode and sustain a fully deprotonated state. The adjoining Cd(ii) cations are joined by tdc2− ligands to create a 1D wave-like chain [Cd(tdc)]n with a non-bonding Cd⋯Cd separation of 10.368(1) Å. Meanwhile, the L2 ligands employ the trans-conformation (κ1)-(κ1)-(κ1)-(κ1)-μ4, while two pyridine rings at the symmetric positions are parallel to each other, linking adjacent Cd(ii) ions to produce an infinite 1D double straight-line [Cd(L2)]n chain with a Cd⋯Cd region value of 10.740(1) Å along the b-axis (Fig. S2†). The two types of 1D chains cross-connect, resulting in a sophisticated 3D network (Fig. 2b). Further topological analysis suggests that each Cd(ii) center and L2 ligand is viewed as a 4-connected node when the tdc2− ligands can be simplified as linear connections (Fig. 2c). Thus, the construction is abstracted as a 3D (4,4)-connected mog (moganite) network using the TOPOS 4.0 program with a Schläfli symbol of {4·64·8}2{42·62·82}.

3.3 Influence of the N-donor ligands on the frameworks of 1 and 2

The N-donor ligands in the Cd(ii) complexes are homologs differing by one methylene group, crucial in constructing the ternary MOFs. For 1, L1 ligands act as the (κ1)-(κ1)-(κ1)-μ2cis-conformation coordination mode connecting the adjoining Cd⋯Cd region of 11.119(1) Å with a dihedral angle of 70.628(1)°, while in 2, when the –CH2-group is introduced, N-including ligands show a different type of coordination (κ1)-(κ1)-(κ1)-(κ1)-μ4trans-conformation for L2 with the Cd⋯Cd separation bridges of 9.573(4) and 10.740(1) Å, separately. The diverse configurations of bis-[(pyridyl)-benzimidazole] ligands efficiently modify the construction of the crystal materials. The former confines the progress of the dimensions due to the conformation of the two pyridine rings in L1 chelated with Cd2+ centers, while the latter constitutes a 1D [Cd(L2)]n chain between tetradentate ligands L2 and cadmium ions, completing the alteration from 2D cem-d to 3D mog. These outcomes show that incorporating suitable N-donor ligands into Cd-MOFs 1 and 2 plays a significant role in building multidimensional coordination frameworks.

3.4 FT-IR, morphology, and PXRD

The structures of these two materials were verified through FT-IR methods and discussed in the ESI (Fig. S3†). These crystal materials’ morphologies were examined by scanning electron microscopy (SEM) (Fig. S4†). The SEM images display an irregular block structure as the dominant morphology. The crystalline phase purities of the title compounds were verified by powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) experiments at room temperature (Fig. S5†).

3.5 Stability test

Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) plots were obtained to affirm cadmium-based complexes’ thermal stability (Fig. S6†). There are two separate weight loss steps in the MOF-1 TGA curve. The first weight loss of 3.64% (calcd 3.32%) occurs from 65 to 152 °C, attributed to the loss of coordinated water molecules. The second mass loss of 72.5% (calcd. 73.6%) occurs from 251 to 600 °C and is assigned to the decomposition of L1 and tdc2−. A residue of 23.9% (calcd. 23.7%) suggests the formation of CdO as the final decomposition product. There is a large region plateau between 31 and 312 °C in 2, and the framework starts to collapse as the organic ligands decompose from 312 to 610 °C (obsd. 75.8%; calcd. 75.9%). The remaining weight of 24.2% is assumed to be that of CdO (24.1%) formed. PXRD results also indicate the thermostability of the Cd(ii) complexes, 251 °C for 1 and 312 °C for 2 (Fig. S5†).

The time-dependent luminous behaviors indicate that 1/2 exhibit groovy dispersions and stability in solutions with the luminescence intensities of the metal–organic coordination polymers being affected scarcely by the test time (Fig. S7†). In addition, the stability of the Cd(ii) MOFs in different pH solutions (pH = 1–14) was also examined. 1 and 2 show high stability in the pH range of 3 to 12 (Fig. S8†).

3.6 Luminescence properties

MOFs built with d10 metal cations have sought-after potential uses such as in displays and nonlinear optical devices because of their admirable photoluminescence (PL) performances.41 The solid-state fluorescence performances of the as-synthesized samples 1 and 2 were evaluated for comparison with free L1 and L2 ligands (Fig. 3). Upon excitation at 272 nm, the free L1 and L2 ligands show emissions at 301 and 304 nm, respectively, attributed to the π* → π or π* → n transition of the π-conjugated system.42 The title compounds emit strong luminescence peaks at 337 nm (λex = 272 nm) for 1 and 322 nm (λex = 266 nm) for 2, respectively. Compared with the free N-donor co-ligands, the wide emission bands of the fluorescent materials are all red-shifted by 36 nm and 18 nm, respectively.

Density functional theory (DFT) procedures show that the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) energy for Cd(ii) ions (−3.714 eV) is lower than that of its counterparts (−0.804 eV for L1, −0.766 eV for L2, and −1.984 eV for H2tdc). Notably, the LUMOs of L1 and L2 are located on pyridines, while the LUMO of the H2tdc ligand is located on the thiophene ring (Fig. 4). The results imply that electrons are transferred from the pyridines of L1 and L2 and the thiophene ring of H2tdc to Cd(ii), causing red-shifts, which can be summarized as ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT).43

3.7 Sensing of antibiotics

The fluorescence properties in various antibiotics were investigated to inspect the prospective applications of the fluorescent materials for identifying antibiotics. The structural formulas of selected antibiotics are depicted in Scheme S1.† As demonstrated in Fig. 5, different antibiotics emerge with diverse impact on the luminescence intensities of 1/2 in solution. Compared with other counterparts, LEV exhibits a noticeable quenching effect, with high quenching efficiencies Q (93.76% for 1 and 94.46% for 2, respectively). Furthermore, the anti-interference sensing experiments disclose that 1/2 can be used as LEV probes towards other co-existing species (Fig. S9†).

In order to determine the sensitivity of the photochemical transducers to LEV in water, the fluorescence performances of these two materials were explored in LEV solutions with various concentrations. The concentration-dependent measurements demonstrate that the luminous signals of MOFs 1 and 2 weaken with increasing LEV concentration (Fig. 6a). The relationships between the quenching effect and LEV concentration were further discussed, which can be analyzed with the Stern–Volmer (SV) mathematical statement: I0/I = 1 + Ksv[LEV]. The average Stern–Volmer constant (Ksv) values are 573 671 M−1 for 1 and 449 540 M−1 for 2, which depend on the linear relationship between the quenching efficiencies I0/I and the measurable concentration of the analytes (Fig. 6b). The low detection limits (LODs) are estimated to be 0.02 μM for 1 and 0.03 μM for 2, confirming that 1/2 can help perform trace analysis on LEV in solution. Compared to other materials for sensing of LEV, the title Cd(ii) complexes are the first luminescent MOFs as probes for sensing of LEV.44–46

3.8 Sensing of small organic molecules

The luminescence sensing experiments were carried out to survey the ternary Cd(ii) MOFs for identifying small organic molecules at room temperature. The fluorescence responses of the suspensions are affected by assorted small organic molecules to some extent (Fig. 7). Interestingly, BZH shows the most distinct quenching response on 1/2, with an excellent Q of 99.99% for 1 and 99.97% for 2. Competition experiments were performed to discover further the anti-interference sensing ability of the photochemical sensing materials towards BZH (Fig. S10†). The quenching efficiencies of BZH@1/2 are found to be unaffected by any other solvents.

The concentration-dependent experiments were performed in the presence of different concentrations of BZH in water to diagnose the quantitative sensitization of the quenching behavior of BZH@1/2 in detail. The fluorescence intensities of the cadmium-based MOFs gradually reduce with the incremental addition of the analyte (Fig. 8a). According to the SV plots for the fluorescence quenching efficiencies and BZH concentration, the relationships are nearly linear in the low concentration range (Fig. 8b). In the linear response range, the KSV values are 222 M−1 for 1 and 5288 M−1 for 2, and the LODs are estimated to be at the level of 24.27 μM for 1 and 3.14 μM for 2, indicating that Cd-MOFs 1/2 are excellent candidates as sensing materials for BZH.47–49

3.9 Sensing of metal ions

PL spectroscopy was considered with emission bands of 337 nm and 322 nm for the photochemical sensing materials with the metal ions in solution, respectively (Fig. 9). Ethanol was selected as the dispersant of the reaction system because as-synthesized 1/2 show good fluorescence performance in EtOH solution. Fe3+ cations show significant fluorescence quenching effects with Q values of 99.99% for 1 and 99.97% for 2; at the same time, there are negligible effects on the luminescence intensities to the Cd(ii) complexes for other cations. The anti-interference sensing experiments show no difference in Fe(iii)-induced luminous signals after introducing interfering cations, indicating the highly selective recognition ability of the sensors with Fe3+ ions (Fig. S11†).

The quantitative fluorescence responses of the MOF-based fluorescent chemosensors toward Fe(iii) ions were evaluated at room temperature. Luminescence titration experiments demonstrated that the fluorescence performances of 1/2 diminish significantly with the increasing concentration of the analytes (Fig. 10a). This can be rationalized by the change in the fluorescence intensity ratios (I0/I) as a function of Fe(iii) concentrations by the SV equation (Fig. 10b). The Ksv values are 3071 M−1 for 1 and 4219 M−1 for 2, respectively, by the linear regression of the plots. The calculated LOD values for Fe(iii) cations are 4.01 μM for 1 and 4.28 μM for 2, comparable to those of other well-designed fluorescent materials in previous reports.50–52

3.10 Reusability

Three cycles were carried out to evaluate the reusability of the complex powder as a fluorescent probe for the trace identification of test substances under the same conditions (Fig. S12†). After every cycle, the 1/2 powders were washed with EtOH, dried in air, and then subjected to the recognition of the next cycle. The strong emissions of 1 and 2 can be regenerated for three cycles. After three cycles, inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS) experiments confirmed the leaching of Cd(ii) with 0.16 μg L−1 for 1 and 0.22 μg L−1 for 2, respectively, indicating the integrity of the frameworks. PXRD measurements of the recovered Cd-MOFs 1 and 2 after LEV/BZH/Fe3+ ion sensing agree with the initial results, confirming the outstanding reusability and photostability of the title compounds in the recognition of LEV/BZH/Fe3+ ions.

3.11 Sensing mechanism

Due to the large surface area and the lack of porosity characteristics for the two MOFs, the Cd(ii) complexes are well dispersed in H2O and H2O–EtOH suspensions and cause more robust luminous signal responses (Table S3†).53,54 The analyte molecules may be adsorbed to particle surfaces, thus facilitating possible host–guest interactions. Given the π-electron-rich structures of the L1/L2 ligands, the quenching mechanism can be induced by photo-induced electron transfer (PET) between the electron-deficient analytes and the probes.55–57 Electrochemical Mott–Schottky experiments reveal that 1/2 are n-type semiconductors with conduction band (CB) edges at −1.44 eV for 1 and −1.31 eV for 2vs. Ag/AgCl, and after the corresponding conversion, the CB edges of the samples are found to be −1.22 eV for 1 and −1.09 eV for 2vs. normal hydrogen electrode (NHE) (Fig. S13 and S14†).58,59 As shown in Fig. S15,† fluorescence quenching can be caused by the excited electrons migrating from the high-lying CB of 1/2 to the low-lying LUMO of the LEV/BZH/Fe3+ ions, forming charge transfer complexes and then undergoing a non-radiative relaxation.60–62

Besides the PET mechanism, the competitive absorption and fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET) between the sensing materials and the targeted species also affect the luminescence sensing response.63 According to the UV-vis absorption spectra, obvious spectral overlaps were observed between the absorption spectra of the analytes (LEV and Fe3+ ions) and the coordination compounds’ fluorescence excitation and emission spectra, indicating FRET from the MOFs to LEV/Fe3+ ions as well as the light energy competition between 1/2 and LEV/Fe3+ ions (Fig. S16†).64,65 The UV-vis absorption spectrum of BZH exhibits overlap with the excitation spectra of 1/2, demonstrating that BZH and the Cd(ii) complexes competitively absorb the photon energy. As a result, the synergistic effect of competitive absorption, fluorescence resonance energy transfer, and photo-induced electron transfer is the primary mechanism for their fluorescence quenching.

4 Conclusion

Two new ternary cadmium(ii) luminescent MOFs were successfully assembled using structurally related pyridyl-bisbenzimidazole ligands under hydrothermal conditions. The Cd(ii) MOFs show excellent thermostability and high chemical stability (pH = 3–12). Luminescence sensing investigations demonstrate that 1 and 2 can be utilized as excellent tri-functional sensors for detecting LEV/BZH/Fe3+ ions with high selectivity and sensitivity, low detection limits, and good recycling performances.
