Introduction

The study of extended coordination networks, often referred to as metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) or coordination polymers, has become an important area of research over recent years.1 Interest in these materials was triggered by their high capacity for gas adsorption,2 but other potential uses such as separations,3 catalysis4 and drug delivery5 are attracting increasing attention.

N,N′-Ditopic ligands such as 4,4′-bipyridyl and pyrazine have been used extensively to bridge between metal centres, and a search of the Cambridge Structural Database6 reveals there are 693 structures containing bridging 4,4′-bipyridyl and 257 containing bridging pyrazine.7 Many of these structures are coordination networks, with these neutral ligands being used to form both cationic homoleptic networks or neutral mixed-ligand networks, typically in conjunction with polycarboxylates.8

Recently, there has been a growing focus on the formation of functionalised networks.9 One way to achieve this is to include a functional group into the ligand that does not interfere with network formation, but will project into the pores of the extended structure. This functional group can be directly available for interaction with a guest, or it can be modified post-synthetically into a more useful group.10 While polycarboxylate ligands containing additional functionalities are the subject of intense current interest,11 there has been rather less attention focussed on adding functionalities to neutral ligands. Most notable are reports of 4,4′-bipyridyls containing amide groups,12 and an enantiopure 4,4′-bipyridyl analogue, formed by fusing a chiral cyclohexane to one of the pyridyl rings.13Crown-ether-functionalised 4,4′-bipyridyls are also known, but have not as yet been used to form extended structures.14

The 3,5-dipyridyl-1H-pyrazoles HL11 and HL22 have been previously reported,15 and HL11 has been used in MOF chemistry as the anionic ligand [L11]−, coordinating through the deprotonated pyrazolyl group in addition to the two pyridyl groups.16 In contrast, up until now, there have been no reports of either HL11 or HL22 acting as a neutral ligand. We reasoned that these coordination modes could be of interest, as the HL1-21-2ligands contain both hydrogen bond donors and acceptors in the pyrazole ring. As such, HL11 and HL22 have the potential to assist with network formation or may be used to promote interactions with a guest molecule.

In this paper we report the reactions of HL1-21-2 with zinc(ii), copper(ii), cobalt(ii) and nickel(ii) salts, and detail the structures of seven new compounds.

Results and discussion

Synthesis

The reactions of HL11 with the nitrates and acetates of zinc(ii), copper(ii), cobalt(ii) and nickel(ii) in DMF, methanol and water were investigated. With zinc(ii) acetate, crystals were formed from the reactions in both DMF and methanol at room temperature and from the hydrothermal reaction in water. Powder X-ray diffraction studies indicated that the three products were different from each other, and subsequent single crystal X-ray analyses confirmed the identities of the compounds from DMF, methanol and water as [Zn3(OAc)6(HL11)4] 1, [Zn(OAc)2(HL11)]·0.5MeOH 2 and [Zn(OAc)2(HL11)2]·H2O 3 respectively. In a subsequent attempt to investigate the effects of the counter-ion on the structure of the product, HL11 was reacted with zinc(ii) chloride. Hydrothermal reaction conditions produced crystals of [ZnCl2(HL11)] 4. Despite the different ratios of Zn:HL11 observed in these compounds, it is notable that the identity of the isolated product was not affected by the ratio of starting materials used in the reactions.

The reactions of HL22 with zinc(ii), copper(ii), cobalt(ii) and nickel(ii) acetates gave either non-crystalline or microcrystalline solids, which could not be fully characterised. In contrast, the reactions with zinc(ii), cobalt(ii) and nickel(ii) nitrates in methanol gave crystals suitable for analysis. Single crystal X-ray analyses confirmed the zinc and cobalt compounds to be the isostructural compounds [M(MeOH)4(HL22)2](NO3)2·2H2O (5, M = Zn; 6, M = Co). Powder X-ray diffraction studies on the nickel compound (7) confirmed this to be isostructural with 5 and 6. These diffraction studies indicated that 6 and 7 were formed as the only crystalline products, whereas 5 is present as part of a mixture.

Structure of [Zn3(OAc)6(HL11)4] 1

The asymmetric unit of 1 contains one and a half zinc atoms, three acetates and two HL11ligands. The coordination environments around Zn(1) and Zn(2) are shown in Fig. 1(a) and (b). Zn(1) is located on a two-fold rotation axis, and has distorted octahedral coordination, with cis angles in the range 59.96(5)–103.13(5)° and trans angles between 155.31(5) and 156.51(7)°. It is bonded to two bidentate (κ2) acetate ligands and two pyridyl groups from different HL11ligands. Zn(2) is also six-coordinate, and contains an even more distorted coordination sphere, with cis angles lying between 58.28(5) and 125.66(6)° and trans angles between 144.40(5) and 178.27(6)°. Zn(2) is bonded to one bidentate acetate, two monodentate bridging acetates and two pyridyl groups. The bridging acetates connect the Zn(2) centres into Zn2(OAc)4(py)4 secondary building units (SBUs) with a Zn⋯Zn distance of 3.95 Å.

The two independent HL11ligands have different coordination modes, with that containing N(1) and N(4) bridging and ditopic, and that containing N(5) and N(8) monodentate, with the pyridyl group containing N(8) uncoordinated. The bridging HL11 ligands link the Zn(1) centres and Zn2(OAc)4(py)4 SBUs into double-stranded zig-zag chains, as shown in Fig. 1(c). These chains contain ‘hinge’ angles of 93° at the Zn(1) centres. The two HL11ligands are almost co-planar, and shortest C⋯C contacts of 3.4 Å suggest π⋯π interactions between them. The inclusion of both Zn2(OAc)4(py)4 and Zn(OAc)2(py)2 sites within the double-stranded chains means that each individual strand is not continuous, and is ‘broken’ after every fourth HL11ligand. The acetate bridges within the Zn2(OAc)4(py)4 unit ensure the double-stranded chain is polymeric.

N–H⋯O hydrogen bonds with the pyrazole NH groups acting as donors, and acetate oxygen atoms acting as acceptors link the zig-zag chains into a three-dimensional network [N(3)⋯O(4) 2.729, H(3)⋯O(4) 1.86 Å, N(3)–H(3)⋯O(4) 167°, O(4) generated by −x + 1, −y + 1, −z; N(7)⋯O(1) 2.832, H(71)⋯O(1) 2.00 Å, N(7)–H(71)⋯O(1) 157°, O(1) generated by −x + ½, −y + ½, −z]. The uncoordinated pyridyl group does not form any significant hydrogen bonds.

Structure of [Zn(OAc)2(HL11)]·0.5MeOH 2

The asymmetric unit of 2 contains one zinc atom, two acetates, one HL11ligand and half a molecule of included methanol. Each zinc centre is distorted octahedral in geometry, with an unsymmetrical bidentate acetate and two monodentate bridging acetates in the equatorial plane, and two pyridyl groups from different HL11ligands in the axial positions [N(3)–Zn(1)–N(4) 178.08(10)°, N(4) generated by x − ½, −y + ½, z + ½]. The bridging acetates connect the zinc centres into Zn2(OAc)4(py)4 SBUs, shown in Fig. 2(a), which are similar to those observed for Zn(2) in 1, but with a shorter Zn⋯Zn distance (3.80 Å). The HL11ligands connect the SBUs into double-stranded chains, as shown in Fig. 2(b). The chains are much straighter than those in 1, with only a gentle undulation. The distortion from linearity is due to the presence of the pyrazole ring in HL11, which generates an angle of approximately 156° between the two pyridyl lone pairs on each HL11ligand. The absence of Zn(OAc)2(py)2 units ensures that each chain in 2 is unbroken, in contrast to those in 1.

As in 1, the pair of HL11ligands bridging each SBU are parallel to each other, though the ligands are further apart than in 1, with the shortest C⋯C contacts measuring 3.6 Å. This is a likely consequence of the fact that each end of the ligand is coordinated to a zinc centre, and the Zn⋯Zn separation of 3.80 Å holds the ligands apart. N–H⋯O hydrogen bonds link the chains into a three-dimensional network [N(2)⋯O(1) 2.829, H(2)⋯O(1) 1.95 Å, N(2)–H(2)⋯O(1) 172°, O(1) generated by −x + 1, −y, −z + 1]. The included methanol molecules are disordered, but interact with the network through O–H⋯O hydrogen bonds [O(5)⋯O(2) 2.968, H(5A)⋯O(2) 2.19 Å, O(5)–H(5A)⋯O(2) 154°].

Structure of [Zn(OAc)2(HL11)2]·H2O 3

The asymmetric unit of 3 contains half of a zinc atom, one acetate, one HL11ligand and half a molecule of included water. The zinc centre is located on a two-fold rotation axis and it is coordinated to two acetates and two pyridyl groups, as shown in Fig. 3(b). The acetate is disordered over two sites in a 60 : 40 ratio, and the bond angles around the zinc centre range from 95.4(3) to 140.3(4)° for the major acetate components. The HL11ligands are coordinated through only one of the pyridyl groups, giving discrete [Zn(OAc)2(HL11)2] molecules.

Very short N–H⋯O hydrogen bonds involving the pyrazole NH groups and an acetate oxygen atom link the molecules of 3 into double-stranded zig-zag chains, as shown in Fig. 3(a). Although the hydrogen atoms on the included water molecule were not observed, N⋯O and O⋯O distances of less than 2.95 Å suggest that further hydrogen bonds are present between the uncoordinated pyridyl nitrogen atom and the water molecule, and between water molecules. Overall, these interactions interlink the complex molecules into a triply-interpenetrated three-dimensional network (Fig. 3(c)).

Structure of [ZnCl2(HL11)] 4

The asymmetric unit of 4 consists of one zinc atom, two chlorine atoms and one HL11ligand. The geometry about the zinc centre is distorted tetrahedral, and it is coordinated to two chlorides and two pyridyl groups, with bond angles ranging from 103.24(5) to 124.26(2)° (Fig. 4(a)). The HL11ligands are coordinated through both pyridyl groups, and link the ZnCl2 units into zig-zag chains.

In contrast to the structures of 1 and 2, N–H⋯Cl hydrogen bonds in 4 connect the chains into sheets, as shown in Fig. 4(b), rather than a three-dimensional structure. This difference arises because having only one HL11 ligand linking the metal centres reduces the number of hydrogen bond donors, hence limiting the dimensionality. These sheets contain R 22(20) and R 22(42) rings.

Structure of [M(MeOH)4(HL2)2](NO3)2·2H2O (5, M = Zn; 6, M = Co)

Compounds 5 and 6 are isostructural, so only the structure of 5 will be described in detail. The asymmetric unit of 5 contains half of a zinc atom, located on an inversion centre, one HL22ligand, two coordinated methanol molecules, one nitrate and one included water molecule, with the remainder of the formula unit generated by symmetry. The geometry about the zinc centre is distorted octahedral, with four methanol molecules coordinated in the equatorial plane and two HL22ligands coordinated in the axial positions (Fig. 5(b)). The cis angles range from 87.60(6) to 92.40(6)°. The HL22ligands are coordinated through only one of the pyridyl groups, so the structure contains discrete [Zn(MeOH)4(HL22)2]2+ ions rather than coordination polymers. The HL22ligands are in syn–anti conformations, with the uncoordinated pyridyl nitrogen atom on the same side as the pyrazolenitrogen atoms and the coordinated pyridyl nitrogen atom on the opposite side.

O–H⋯N hydrogen bonds involving two of the coordinated methanol molecules per metal centre as donors and the uncoordinated pyridyl groups as acceptors link the complex cations together into chains, as shown in Fig. 5(a) and (b). These chains interconnect the metal centres through pairs of HL22ligands, and are reminiscent of the chains present in the structure of 2, though with a methanolligand and hydrogen bonded pyridyl group in 5 taking the place of a metal centre and coordinated pyridyl group in 2.

O–H⋯O hydrogen bonds connect the other coordinated methanol molecules with the nitrate counter-ions. Further hydrogen bonds involving the included water molecules and the pyrazole groups link the chains into sheets. The hydrogen bonds form R 44(10) graph sets, in which the pyrazoles act as hydrogen bond donors and acceptors to form water-bridged dimers, as shown in Fig. 5(c). A search of the Cambridge Structural Database6 reveals that there are 16 previous examples of this water-bridged dimer motif involving pyrazole rings. Nine of these are flat, in a similar manner to that in 5, whereas the other seven are significantly puckered or bent. The water molecules in 5 also form O–H⋯O hydrogen bonds within the sheets, with oxygen atoms of the nitrate anions acting as acceptors.

Discussion

One of the aims of this research was to verify whether HL11 and HL22 could act as neutral bridging ligands in the construction of coordination networks. The structures of 1, 2 and 4 confirm that this is the case, but the structures of 3, 5 and 6 reveal the picture is not straightforward. While coordination networks can be observed, discrete neutral (3) and ionic (5 and 6) complexes in which the HL11 or HL22ligands are monodentate have also been isolated and characterised. There are a number of factors that can influence whether a particular combination of metal centre, ligand, counter-ion and solvent gives rise to an infinite or a discrete structure. The observation of both types of structure with HL11, both types of structure from zinc(ii) acetate and both types of structure with water and methanol as solvents suggests that no single factor determines whether infinite or discrete structures are observed in this system.

In all of the compounds 1–6, two HL1-21-2ligands are coordinated to each metal centre, with the remaining coordination sites occupied by counter-ions or solvent molecules. Decreasing the ligating ability of the counter-ion (NO3− < Cl−, OAc−) increases the tendency of solvent molecules to coordinate in the structure. However, this structural substitution of counter-ion for solvent does not necessarily affect the gross structure adopted, since the number of coordinated pyridyl groups is unaffected by the change in co-ligand.

Although a coordination network has not been observed with HL22, this is unlikely to be due to the structure of the ligand itself and the reduced angle between the lone pairs with respect to the analogous angle for HL11. 3,3′-Bipyridyl has a similar angle between lone pairs to that anticipated for HL22, and in all 16 of the crystal structures in the Cambridge Structural Database for this ligand it adopts a ditopic bridging coordination mode.

Although at first sight, the polymeric and discrete structures appear very different, closer inspection reveals they have features in common. The most notable of these is the prevalence of double-stranded chains, which are observed in some form in all of the structures, with the exception of 4. In 1 and 2, the two strands are linked together at half or all of the metal centres through bridging acetate ligands. In 3, 5 and 6, the two strands are interconnected by hydrogen bonds. For 3, N–H⋯O hydrogen bonds are formed, with the pyrazole group of HL11 providing the donor and an acetate oxygen atom acting as the acceptor. For 5 and 6, O–H⋯N hydrogen bonds are observed, with the coordinated methanol ligands acting as donors and the uncoordinated pyridine groups acting as acceptors.

A second aim of the study was to see whether the pyrazole NH group could interact with other functionalities viahydrogen bonding. This group was observed to form hydrogen bonds in all of the structures examined. In 1–4, the hydrogen bond acceptor is the coordinated counter-ion (acetate or chloride), and the hydrogen bonds link the chains or discrete molecules into the gross structure. In contrast, in 5 and 6, the pyrazole NH group forms a hydrogen bond with the included water molecule. This suggests that when hydrogen bonds to counter-ions can be prevented, the pyrazole group can be used to interact with guest molecules. Current research is aimed at investigating this further, and also at incorporating HL1-21-2 into porous mixed-ligand frameworks.

Experimental

General points

Compounds HL11 and HL22 were prepared by the previously reported method.15 All other compounds were obtained commercially and used without further purification. Powder diffraction measurements were recorded using a Bruker D8 powder diffractometer, fitted with Goebel mirrors, and using CuKα radiation of wavelength 1.5414 Å. Samples were placed in 0.3 mm to 0.7 mm diameter Lindemann capillaries, and measured with a 2θ range of 4–60°. The step size was 0.02° with time per step of 1.00 s. Unless noted otherwise, the observed powder diffraction patterns match those simulated from the single crystal data. Elemental analyses were conducted by Alan Carver (University of Bath) and Gillian Maxwell (University College London).

Synthesis of [Zn3(OAc)6(HL11)4] 1

HL11 (0.055 g, 0.25 mmol) was dissolved with warming in DMF (3 cm3) and added to a solution of Zn(OAc)2·2H2O (0.055 g, 0.25 mmol) in DMF (1 cm3). The resultant solution was left to stand at room temperature until crystals of 1 formed. These were separated by filtration and washed with DMF. Yield 0.050 g (56%). Found: C, 51.9; H, 3.85; N, 15.2%. C64H58N16O12Zn3·2H2O requires C, 52.1; H, 4.24; N, 15.2%.

Synthesis of [Zn(OAc)2(HL11)]·0.5MeOH 2

HL11 (0.022 g, 0.1 mmol) was dissolved with warming in methanol (3 cm3) and added to a solution of Zn(OAc)2·2H2O (0.022 g, 0.1 mmol) in methanol (1 cm3). The resultant solution was left to stand at room temperature until crystals of 2 formed. Found: C, 49.9; H, 4.00; N, 13.5%. C17.5H18N4O4.5Zn requires C, 49.8; H, 4.30; N, 13.3%.

Synthesis of [Zn(OAc)2(HL11)2]·H2O 3

A mixture of HL11 (0.112 g, 0.5 mmol), Zn(OAc)2·2H2O (0.111 g, 0.5 mmol) and water (8 cm3) was added to a 23 cm3 Parr Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel. The vessel was sealed and heated to 140 °C for 36 hours and then cooled to room temperature. Colourless crystals of 3 were separated by filtration. Yield 0.100 g (31%). Found: C, 55.5; H, 3.95; N, 17.4%. C30H28N8O5Zn requires C, 55.8; H, 4.37; N, 17.4%.

Synthesis of [ZnCl2(HL11)] 4

A mixture of HL11 (0.112 g, 0.5 mmol), ZnCl2 (0.070 g, 0.5 mmol) and water (8 cm3) was added to a 23 cm3 Parr Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel. The vessel was sealed and heated to 140 °C for 36 hours and then cooled to room temperature. Colourless crystals of 4 were separated by filtration. Yield 0.140 g (78%). Found: C, 43.4; H, 2.81; N, 15.5%. C13H10Cl2N4Zn requires C, 43.5; H, 2.81; N, 15.6%.

Synthesis of [Zn(MeOH)4(HL22)2](NO3)2·2H2O 5

HL22 (0.015 g, 0.068 mmol) was dissolved with warming in methanol (3 cm3) and added to a solution of Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (0.019 g, 0.065 mmol) in methanol (1 cm3). The resultant solution was left to stand at room temperature until crystals of 5 formed. The X-ray powder diffraction pattern confirmed that 5 was the major product, but with an unidentified by-product also present.

Synthesis of [Co(MeOH)4(HL22)2](NO3)2·2H2O 6

HL22 (0.015 g, 0.068 mmol) was dissolved with warming in methanol (3 cm3) and added to a solution of Co(NO3)2·6H2O (0.019 g, 0.065 mmol) in methanol (1 cm3). The resultant solution was left to stand at room temperature until crystals of 6 formed. Found: C, 45.1; H, 4.84; N, 18.0%. C30H40CoN10O12 requires C, 45.5; H, 5.09; N, 17.7%.

Synthesis of [Ni(MeOH)4(HL22)2](NO3)2·2H2O 7

HL22 (0.015 g, 0.068 mmol) was dissolved with warming in methanol (3 cm3) and added to a solution of Ni(NO3)2·6H2O (0.019 g, 0.065 mmol) in methanol (1 cm3). The resultant solution was left to stand at room temperature until crystals of 7 formed. Found: C, 45.0; H, 4.97; N, 17.6%. C30H40N10NiO12 requires C, 45.5; H, 5.09; N, 17.7%.

Crystallography

X-Ray data for compounds 1–6 were collected on a Nonius Kappa CCD diffractometer using Mo-Kα radiation. Details of the data collections, solutions and refinements are given in Table 1. Unless noted below, all non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically in the final least squares run, and hydrogen atoms were included at calculated positions. The structures were solved using SHELXS-97 and refined using full-matrix least squares in SHELXL-97.17 Refinements were generally straightforward with the following exceptions and points of note.

In compound 2, the methanol carbon atom is located at a special position, and consequently the solvent fragment is disordered. The partial methyl hydrogen atoms could not be reliably located, and consequently they were omitted from the final least-squares refinement. In compound 3, the acetate ligand is disordered over two sites in a 60 : 40 ratio. Distances therein were refined subject to similarity restraints between the two disordered fragments. In the structures of 5 and 6, the hydrogen atoms in the coordinated methanol and lattice water molecules were located and refined at fixed distances from the relevant parent atoms.
