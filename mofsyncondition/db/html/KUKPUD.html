Introduction

The rational design and assembly of metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have attracted enormous research interest in recent years not only for their intriguing nature of molecular connectivities and topologies1 but also for their potential applications in gas sorption,2 catalysis,3 sensing4 and drug delivery.5 In general, MOFs are composed of metal ions or metal-oxo clusters as nodes and organic linkers, so the appropriate selection of one or both of these building units is the key factor for the desired structures and properties.6 Over the past few decades, a large number of synthetic strategies have been applied,7 among which the method based on the diversity of polynuclear secondary building units (SBUs) is proven to be one of the most efficient ways.8 Selection of suitable SBUs can achieve targeted MOFs with the desired properties because SBUs not only have a significant impact on the topology and stability of MOFs, but also provide functional properties that can be granted to the whole framework.9 For example, a series of fantastic reticular MOFs with homogeneous periodic pores have been obtained based on Zn4O SBU. It is well known that the reaction of Cu(ii) ions with ligands containing carboxylate groups prefers to form a binuclear copper paddle-wheel cluster {Cu2C2O4},10 while trinuclear {Cu3(μ3-OH)N6} SBU could be afforded by the connection of Cu(ii) ions with the ligands containing triazole groups.11 As a result, using a ligand which features both carboxylate and azole groups should be a promising approach for the construction of new MOFs based on two different types of SBUs. However, it is still a challenge to rationally synthesize such types of MOFs because even a small change in the reaction conditions, such as solvents, concentrations of the reactants, temperature, pH values, and counter-ions, may lead to completely different SBUs.12 Although many interesting studies have succeeded in elongation or modification of ligands with the same SBU, limited examples were studied on the modification of the SBU based on the same ligand and the same metal salt, in particular when a bifunctional ligand was used.13 In this contribution, a bifunctional ligand Hcpt [4-(4-carboxyphenyl)-1,2,4-triazole was selected to study how the auxiliary solvents can influence the SBU variation in the construction of MOFs. Three Cu(ii)-MOFs with structural diversities, namely {[Cu4(cpt)4Cl4]·2DMF·dioxane·3H2O}n (1), {[Cu8(cpt)4(Hcpt)2Cl7(μ3-OH)2(H2O)4]Cl3·4CH3CN}n (2), and {[Cu8(cpt)4Cl4(μ3-OH)2(μ4-O)2]Cl2·4H2O·2CH3CN·3MeOH}n (3) (DMF = N,N-dimethylformamide), respectively, were obtained. The structures of Cu(ii) SBUs in these MOFs largely depend on the auxiliary solvents involved in the reactions. Their structural features as well as gas sorption performances for 1 and 3 were studied in detail.

Experimental

Materials and physical measurements

All the materials and reagents are commercially available and were used as received. The ligand 4-(4-carboxyphenyl)-1,2,4-triazole (Hcpt, Scheme 1) was prepared according to the literature procedure.14 FT-IR spectra were recorded with a Bruker Tensor 27 Spectrometer on KBr discs in the 4000–400 cm−1 region. Elemental analyses for C, H and N were carried out using a Perkin-Elmer analyzer. Thermal analyses (under a N2 atmosphere, heating rate of 10 °C min−1) were carried out in Labsys NETZSCH TG 209 Setaram apparatus from room temperature to 800 °C. Powder X-ray diffraction measurements were performed using a D/Max-2500 X-ray diffractometer with Cu Kα radiation. N2 and CO2 sorption experiments were carried out on an Autosorb-IQ2 gas adsorption analyzer (Quantachrome Instruments).

Synthesis of compounds

{[Cu4(cpt)4Cl4]·2DMF·dioxane·3H2O}n (1)

A mixture of CuCl2·2H2O (0.034 g, 0.2 mmol) and Hcpt (0.020 g, 0.1 mmol) was suspended in a mixed solvent of H2O (4 mL), DMF (2 mL) and dioxane (2 mL), and then heated at 120 °C for 72 h under autogenous pressure in a 25 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel reactor. After cooling to room temperature, green block crystals were harvested. The yield was 30% for 1 (based on Hcpt). Elemental analysis calcd (%) for 1 (C46H52Cl4Cu4N14O15): C 38.45, H 3.65, N 13.65; found: C 38.18, H 3.77, N 13.32.

{[Cu8(cpt)4(Hcpt)2Cl7(μ3-OH)2(H2O)4]Cl3·4CH3CN}n (2)

A mixture of CuCl2·2H2O (0.034 g, 0.2 mmol), Hcpt (0.020 g, 0.1 mmol), H2O (4 mL) and CH3CN (2 mL) was added into a 25 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel reactor, and then heated at 120 °C for 3 days under autogenous pressure. After cooling to room temperature, green long block crystals were obtained. The yield was 75% for 2 (based on Hcpt). Elemental analysis calcd (%) for 2 (C62H60Cl10Cu8N22O18): C 32.89, H 2.67, N 13.61; found: C 32.84, H 2.89, N 13.26.

{[Cu8(cpt)4Cl4(μ3-OH)2(μ4-O)2]Cl2·4H2O·2CH3CN·3MeOH}n (3)

A mixture of CuCl2·2H2O (0.034 g, 0.2 mmol), Hcpt (0.020 g, 0.1 mmol), H2O (4 mL), MeOH (2 mL) and CH3CN (2 mL) was added into a 25 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel reactor, and then heated at 120 °C for 3 days under autogenous pressure. After cooling to room temperature, green schistose crystals were obtained by filtration and washed with H2O and MeOH in sequence, and dried in air. The yield was 65% for 3 (based on Hcpt). Elemental analysis calcd (%) for 3 (C43H52Cl6Cu8N14O19): C 28.85, H 2.93, N 10.95; found: C 28.51, H 2.69, N 11.08.

Crystallographic studies

Data collections for 1–3 were performed on an Oxford Supernova TM diffractometer with graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) using the ω-scan technique. The structures were solved by direct methods and refined with the full matrix least-squares technique based on the SHELXS-97 and SHELXL-97 programs.15 Anisotropic thermal parameters were assigned to all non-hydrogen atoms. The hydrogen atoms of organic ligands were generated geometrically; the hydrogen atoms of the water molecules were located from difference maps and refined with isotropic temperature factors. For the highly disordered nature of the guest solvents, they could not be well modeled in the refinement. The final refinement was performed by modification of the structure factors for contribution of the disordered solvent electron densities using the SQUEEZE option of PLATON.16 For 1, DFIX command was used in the refinement of the disordered benzene ring. For 2, DIFX, SADI, DELU, and ISOR commands were used in the refinement of the two positional disordered cpt− ligands. The final chemical formulae of these three MOFs were derived from crystallographic data combined with elemental and thermogravimetric analysis data. The crystallographic data for the structures are reported in Table 1 and the selected bond lengths and bond angles are listed in Tables S1–S3.†

Results and discussion

Description of the structure

By adjusting the synthetic conditions, MOFs 1–3 with structural diversities can be obtained during the aggregation of the Hcpt with CuCl2, which could be attributed to the multiple coordination modes of the organic ligand (Scheme 1). MOF 1 is a 3D neutral framework based on the mononuclear {CuN4} and binuclear {Cu2C4O8} SBUs generated in a mixed H2O/dioxane/DMF solvent, which possesses 1D channels and large void spaces occupied by guest solvents. MOF 2 could be afforded in a mixed H2O–MeCN solvent with the same starting material as 1, exhibiting a 3D cationic framework based on the binuclear {Cu2C4O8} and trinuclear {Cu3N6(μ3-OH)} SBUs. MOF 3 was synthesized under the same conditions with MOF 2 except that 2 mL MeOH was added to the reaction mixture. MOF 3 shows a 3D cationic framework based on a rare tetranuclear {Cu4N4O4(μ3-OH)(μ4-O)} SBU. From the synthesis of these three MOFs, we found that the temperature and the stoichiometry of the metal to ligand greatly affect the quality of the final crystallized products, and the auxiliary solvents could lead to the variations of SBUs.

Crystal structure of 1

Single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that 1 crystallizes in the orthorhombic space group Cmmm, and consists of a 3D neutral framework. The asymmetric unit contains one Cu(ii) ion, one cpt− ligand, and one coordinated Cl− ion. There are two types of SBUs coexisting in the framework. One is the classic paddle-wheel cluster {Cu2C4O8}, and the other is mononuclear {CuN4} (Fig. 1a). In the binuclear copper cluster each five-coordinated Cu(ii) ion is associated with four oxygen atoms from four individual cpt− ligands and the remaining site was finished by Cl− ions. Remarkably the paddle-wheel units are linked by Cl− ions to generate a 1D chain along the a axis (Fig. 1b). As for the mononuclear copper SBU, the Cu(ii) ion is coordinated by two Cl− ions and four nitrogen atoms from four individual cpt− ligands. Each mononuclear copper unit is connected by the bridging Cl− ions to afford the 1D chain as well. The Cu–O bond is 1.957(8) Å and the Cu–N bond is 2.012(5) Å. The two parallel copper chains are supported by the rigid ligands to assemble a 3D framework. The resulting framework possesses 1D nano-sized channels with dimensions of 7.06 Å × 7.98 Å along the a axis and 54.2% solvent-accessible volume calculated by PLATON (Fig. 1c).17 To further understand the structural topology, when we took {Cu2C4O8} as a 6-connected node, the cpt− ligand as a 3-connected node and the {CuN4} as a 6-connected node, respectively, the framework could be simplified as a novel (3,6,6)-connected net with the point symbol {3·62}4{34·42·64·75}2{68·84·9·102} (Fig. 1c).18

Crystal structure of 2

When the reaction solvents were changed to H2O–MeCN, MOF 2 was isolated. It crystallizes in the orthorhombic space group Cccm, and there are two crystallographically independent Cu(ii) atoms, one fully deprotonated cpt− ligand, a half Hcpt ligand, one and three quarters of coordinated Cl− ions, a half μ3-OH, three quarters of disordered lattice Cl− ions, and one coordinated water molecule in the asymmetric unit. As shown in Fig. 2a, the framework consists of two different types of SBUs, one is the binuclear {Cu2C4O8} and the other is trinuclear {Cu3N6(μ3-OH)}. In the binuclear SBU, two adjacent penta-coordinated Cu(ii) ions adopt the same coordination model and are bridged by four cpt− ligands with a Cu⋯Cu distance of 2.640(3) Å. The axis sites of the Cu(ii) ion are occupied by Cl− ions which link the binuclear unit with two adjacent trinuclear units along the b axis (Fig. 2b). In the trinuclear SBU, the Cu(ii) ions exhibit two different coordination modes: Cu1 is hexa-coordinated by two nitrogen atoms from two cpt− ligands, two Cl− ions, and two oxygen atoms from one μ3-OH and one water molecule; Cu2 is penta-coordinated with two nitrogen atoms of two cpt ligands, one Cl− ion and two oxygen atoms from one μ3-OH and one water molecule. The Cu–O bond lengths are in the range of 1.973(5)–2.468(1) Å and the Cu–N bonds vary from 1.957(2) to 1.960(7) Å. The cpt ligands show two different connection models: one bonds with the binuclear copper and trinuclear copper SBUs to afford the 3D framework; the other only connects with the trinuclear copper SBU leaving the carboxylic oxygen atoms uncoordinated (Fig. 2c). Software TOPOS was used to simplify the whole network, which gives a new (6,6)-c 2-nodal net with the point symbol {413·62}{48·66·8}, upon considering the {Cu2C4O8} and the Cl− bridged two {Cu3N6(μ3-OH)} clusters as the 6-connected nodes (Fig. 2d). The PLATON software was used to calculate the effective volume, which is 1374 Å3 per unit cell, accounting for 17% of the crystal volume.

Crystal structure of 3

MOF 3 was synthesized under the same conditions as MOF 2 except that 2 mL MeOH was added to the reaction mixture. A single-crystal X-ray diffraction study reveals that 3 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group Cc and consists of a 3D cationic framework based on a rare tetranuclear {Cu4N4O4(μ3-OH)(μ4-O)} SBU. The asymmetric unit contains four independent Cu(ii) ions, two cpt− ligands, two coordinated Cl− ions, one μ3-OH, one μ4-O and one free Cl− ion, as shown in Fig. 3a. In the tetranuclear unit, Cu1 adopts a tetra-coordinated model and bonded to three oxygen atoms from one cpt− ligand, one μ3-OH, one μ4-O and one nitrogen atom from another cpt− ligand; Cu2 adopts a similar coordinated environment to Cu1, except for one Cl− ion occupying one coordination site of Cu2 along the c axis; Cu3 is penta-coordinated with four oxygen atoms from two cpt− ligands, one μ3-OH, one μ4-O and one Cl− ion; the tetra-coordinated Cu4 is linked by two nitrogen atoms from two different cpt− ligands, one μ3-OH, and one Cl− ion. The Cu–O bonds are in the range of 1.917(1)–1.991(1) Å, and the Cu–N bonds range from 1.989(1) to 2.003(1) Å. Each adjacent tetranuclear copper unit is connected with each other by the μ4-O and Cl− ion to give rise to the 1D SBU chain along the c axis (Fig. 3b); these chains are further linked by cpt− ligands along the a and b axes to afford the 3D network with trigonal channels (Fig. 3c). TOPOS software was used to analyse the framework and reveals that 3 possesses a six-connected rob topology with the point symbol {48·66·8}, upon considering the tetranuclear SBU {Cu4N4O4(μ3-OH)(μ4-O)} as a six-connected node and the cpt− ligand as a two-connected node. The accessible volume within the crystal is calculated to be 35.8% according to the program PLATON when the extra framework species are not considered. 3 possesses 1D channels extending along the c axis with the diameter of 3.95 × 4.88 Å2 revealed by the software Poreblazer.19

Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) and thermogravimetry analyses (TGA)

To confirm the phase purity of 1–3, a PXRD study was carried out at room temperature (Fig. S1†). The patterns of the as-synthesized bulk materials match well with the simulated ones from the single-crystal structure data, indicative of the pure solid state phases. The thermal stabilities of 1–3 were evaluated by TGA under a N2 atmosphere from 25 to 800 °C (Fig. S2†). 1 shows a weight loss of 9.9% from 25 to 113 °C (calcd 9.7%), corresponding to the loss of three water molecules and one dioxane molecule in the lattice and then a weight loss of 11.9% from 113 °C to 204 °C occurs (calcd 12.4%), which could be attributed to the loss of two DMF molecules. A sharp weight loss followed after 204 °C, indicating collapse of the whole framework. For 2, a weight loss of 10.9% from 25 to 159 °C is observed, corresponding to the release of four coordinated water and four lattice CH3CN molecules (calcd 10.7%), after which there is no obvious loss until about 230 °C. MOF 3 shows a weight loss of 14.5% from 25 to 142 °C (calcd 14.3%), which could be attributed to the escape of four water, two MeCN, and three MeOH molecules in the lattice. A sharp weight loss occurs after 225 °C, indicating the collapse of the whole framework.

Gas adsorption properties

The accessible volumes of 1 and 3 were calculated to be 54.2% and 35.8% respectively, which are adequate to study their gas sorption properties. The activated samples of 1 and 3 were obtained by exchange of solvent molecules with MeOH and then outgassed overnight under vacuum at 80 °C. The CO2 sorption isotherms of 1 and 3 were measured at 195 K to access their surface areas, and are plotted in Fig. 4. At 195 K, 1 showed a low CO2 adsorption with the maximum uptake value of 10.29 cm3 g−1, while complex 3 showed a CO2 uptake of 57.02 cm3 g−1. The Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface area is 169 m2 g−1 and the Langmuir surface area is 262 m2 g−1 for 3 based on the 195 K CO2 sorption isotherm. Although 1 possesses a much higher accessible volume, its CO2 uptake is much lower than that of 3. This discrepancy could be due to the partial collapse of the framework for 1 after guest removal, which has been verified by the PXRD patterns. The CO2 sorption behaviors of complex 3 around room temperature were also studied (Fig. 5a). Complex 3 exhibits a reversible type-I sorption isotherm with moderate CO2 capacity (29.51 cm3 g−1 at 273 K and 22.54 cm3 g−1 at 298 K). Importantly, 3 shows a rapid CO2 uptake in the initial stage, implying favorable interactions between CO2 molecules and the host framework.20 To gain more insights into the CO2–framework interaction, we calculated the isosteric heat of CO2 (Qst) via the Clausius–Clapeyron equation by fitting the CO2 adsorption isotherms measured at 273 and 298 K. As shown in Fig. 5b, the Qst value at zero coverage for 3 is 53 kJ mol−1, which is much higher than many MOFs with open metal sites or functional groups, and only a few MOFs show Qst values above 40 kJ mol−1.21 The relatively high Qst of 3 can be ascribed to the narrow pores presented in its framework, which could lead to a strong interaction of the framework with the CO2 molecule. The N2 sorption isotherms of 3 were collected to study its gas separation properties. However, only a little amount of N2 was absorbed. The CO2/N2 selectivity for 3 was calculated based on the Henry's law, and found to be 110 (273 K and 1 bar) and 86 (298 K and 1 bar).22 To predict the CO2/N2 selectivity under a more real condition, the ideal adsorbed solution theory (IAST) was used to calculate the multi-component adsorption behavior from the experimental pure gas isotherms.23 The CO2/N2 selectivity (20 : 80 molar ratios) of 3 at 298 K shows a decreasing trend from 84 to 64. More importantly, at 298 K and 0.16 bar (a typical partial pressure of CO2 in an industrial flue gas), 3 shows an excellent CO2/N2 selectivity of 84. This CO2/N2 selectivity is superior to that of many MOFs with highly polar functional sites in the pores or flexible structural nature, such as Cu-TDPAT (34), MOF-74-Mg (49), and SNU-M10 (6).24 To the best of our knowledge, MOFs with such high CO2/N2 selectivity at 298 K and 0.16 bar have been rarely reported so far.25 The high CO2/N2 selectivity of 3 may be explained by its high Qst value at low loading, which indicates that there is a specific intermolecular interaction between the framework and CO2 molecules.26

Conclusions

In summary, we have successfully synthesized three new 3D Cu(ii) MOFs with interesting SBU diversity from a bifunctional ligand by altering the auxiliary solvents. The auxiliary solvents play a vital role in the construction of framework materials with selected SBUs. This work not only illustrates that the variations of SBUs in MOFs can be achieved by adjusting the auxiliary solvents but also provides examples of constructing new MOFs with bifunctional ligands. These MOFs with different Cu(ii) SBUs will enrich the field of MOFs based on bifunctional ligands.
