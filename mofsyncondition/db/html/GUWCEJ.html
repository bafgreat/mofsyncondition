Introduction

The design of novel metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) is currently of great interest not only because of their amazing structural diversity but also because of their potential applications as ferroelectric, magnetic, electrocatalytic, and luminescent materials.1 Molecular ferroelectrics are a class of compounds that can switch their spontaneous polarization (Ps) by changing the direction of an electric field.2 MOF ferroelectrics exhibit many advantages, including light weight, high surface area, controllable structure, large spontaneous polarization (Ps), and high Curie temperature (Tc). Moreover, they show ferroelectric properties comparable to that of inorganic ferroelectrics.3 For instance, Mon et al. studied a postsynthetic methodology for designing ferroelectric MOFs, (CH3NH3)12 {Cu II24[(S,S)-hismox]12(OH2)3}·178H2O, which exhibit ferroelectric behavior below 260 K.4 Ye et al. reported a novel 3D Cd-MOF, which displayed ferroelectric behaviour associated with a high dielectric constant.5 It must be pointed out that the emergence of ferroelectricity is inseparable from molecular symmetry. These systems have potential ferroelectric performance only when their crystals belong to the ten polar point groups that have a polar axis (1, 2m, mm2, 3, 3m, 4, 4mm, 6, 6mm).6 Thus, the construction of low-symmetry MOF structures from chiral ligands is a great opportunity for the development of this field.7 In our previous research, we reported a chiral Zn-MOF, which was obtained from a chiral hydrogel.8 With this in mind, one idea would be to go one step further and synthesize two new chiral Co-MOFs using two different synthetic methodologies. Thus, the ferroelectric properties, chirality, thermal stability, and dielectric properties of the Co-MOFs were comprehensively investigated.

Based on the aforementioned content, Co-MOF-1 underwent a two-step transformation involving a hydrogel to yield Zn-MOF. Then, Zn-MOF was transformed into Co-MOF-1. Co-MOF-2 was directly synthesized using hydrothermal reaction conditions (Scheme 1). Compared to the crystal structure of Co-MOF-2, one ligand of Co-MOF-1 is not connected to the adjacent crystallographic equivalent metal center and changes in the content of unit cell induce a space group change from P21 to P1. Surprisingly, ferroelectric measurements reveal that Co-MOF-1 does not exhibit a hysteretic behaviour, which is contrary to the observation of an electric hysteresis loop of ferroelectricity for Co-MOF-2. As far as we know, it is extremely rare to modulate ferroelectricity by altering the molecular structural arrangement of metal complex ferroelectrics.

Experimental

Materials and measurements

All of the chemicals were purchased and used without further purification. Distilled water was prepared in the laboratory and was used throughout the work. The circular dichroism spectra of HL and the MOFs were recorded on a JASCO J-810 spectropolarimeter at room temperature. Powder X-ray diffraction patterns (PXRD) were obtained using crushed single crystals on an Empyrean Panalytical apparatus with Cu-Kα radiation (λ = 1.54178 Å). Fourier transform infrared (FT-IR) spectra were measured in the range of 4000–400 cm−1 using KBr pellets on a PerkinElmer spectrometer. Elemental analyses for N, C, and H were obtained using a PerkinElmer 2400 elemental analyser. The P–E hysteresis loop and leakage current curves were performed at room temperature using a Premier II ferroelectric instrument. The temperature dependence of the dielectric constant was measured using an E4980A Precision impedance analyzer. The electric polarization, dielectric constant, and leakage current curves were researched using a pellet of ground crystal powder coated with silver paste on two opposite surfaces. Thermogravimetric (TG) analysis was performed using an NETZSCH STA 449F3 analyzer under a flow of N2 at a heating rate of 5 °C min−1 in the range from 25 to 1000 °C. DSC curves were obtained using DSC Q2000 with a heating rate of 5 °C min−1 under a nitrogen atmosphere. For second harmonic generation (SHG) experiments, an unexpanded laser beam with low divergence (pulsed Nd:YAG at a wavelength of 1064 nm, pulse duration of 5 ns, peak power of 1.6 MW, and repetition rate of 10 Hz) was used. The instrument was Ins 1210058 INSTEC Instrument with a temperature range from 20 to 210°. Moreover, a Vibrant 355 II, OPOTEK laser was used.

Preparation of Co-MOF-1

The HL ligand and Zn-MOF were prepared by following a procedure previously reported in the literature.8 Zn-MOF (20 mg) was placed in a vial (5 mL). A 1 M cobalt nitrate aqueous solution (2.5 mL) was added to the vial and the mixture was left unperturbed. Red block single crystals, suitable for X-ray diffraction analysis, were formed after 10 days. Yield: 15 mg. Preparation of Co-MOF-2

HL (0.25 mmol), cobalt nitrate (0.1 mmol), and water (1.2 mL) were placed in a tightly capped 5 mL vial. The solution was heated to 90 °C for 40 h and then cooled to room temperature at a rate of 2 °C h−1. Red rod crystals, which were insoluble in water or common organic solvents, were collected by filtration and washed with deionized water. Finally, the crystals were dried at room temperature. Yield: 20 mg (68%, based on the metal salt). Elemental analysis (%) calcd for C28H28CoN4O5: C, 60.06, H, 5.00, N, 10.01; found: C, 59.85, H, 4.96, N, 9.84. Results and discussion

Crystal synthesis

Co-MOF-1 was obtained via a dissolution–recrystallization process that involved soaking Zn-MOF in an aqueous solution of Co(NO3)2·6H2O. For comparison, when aqueous solutions of Cu(NO3)2·3H2O and Ni(NO3)2·6H2O were used to soak single crystals of Zn-MOF for 10 days, transformations were not observed. Considering the abovementioned evidence, merely the existence of an aqueous solution of free Co(ii) can induce crystal transformation. Although the Zn-MOF to Co-MOF-1 transformation seems to be a “solid-state process”, it is via a dissolution–recrystallization process.9,10

Crystal structure

As shown in Fig. 1, Co-MOF-1 comprises one crystallographic Co(ii) ion with two ligands, which form an open channel with chiral side chains in space. The Co(ii) center exhibits a distorted octahedral coordination geometry, chelated by Ocarboxylate, protonated Namine, pyridyl groups, and two coordinated water molecules. The average distance of the Co–O bond is 2.102 Å and the average distance of the Co–N bond is 2.176. The O–Co–O and N–Co–N angles are in the ranges of 88.55°–167.46° and 86.68°–177.35°, respectively. The adjacent Co(ii) ions are bridged by the nitrogen of the amine group and pyridyl groups of the bridging ligand, extending the structure into a 1D framework. Interestingly, the use of hydrogen bonds (O–H⋯O) between carboxyl oxygen atoms of the ligand and coordinated water molecules in Co-MOF-1 expand their dimensionality to a 2D supramolecular network (Fig. S1a†).

Compared to the crystal structures of Co-MOF-1, there is one crystallographic Co(ii) ion, two L− ligands, and one coordinated water molecule in the asymmetric unit of Co-MOF-2. The Co(ii) center is six-coordinated by two pyridine N atoms, one protonated N from the ligands, two oxygen atoms derived from the carboxyl atoms of the ligands, and one coordinated water molecule. The average distances of the Co–O and Co–N bonds are 2.063 Å and 2.186 Å, respectively. The angle scopes of O–Co–O and N–Co–N are 87.50°–175.40° and 85.10°–172.50°, respectively. The adjacent Co(ii) ions are bridged by carboxylate groups and one pyridyl group, forming a 2D framework net. The crystal parameters are shown in Table S1† and selected bond lengths, angles, and partial hydrogen bond data are given in Tables S2–S4.†

PXRD patterns and CD spectra of Co-MOF-1 and Co-MOF-2

The phase purity of the MOF crystals was confirmed by the high similarity between the experimental and simulated PXRD patterns (Fig. 2a and b). Solid-state CD spectra of the Co-MOFs and HL were measured at room temperature. As shown in Fig. 2c, Co-MOF-1 and Co-MOF-2 exhibit a positive Cotton effect at 262 nm and 269 nm, respectively, while HL displays positive peaks at 273 nm. The distinct differences in the CD spectra of Co-MOF-1 and Co-MOF-2 are attributed to the different coordination environment of the Co(ii) ions. The coordination between the ligands and Co(ii) ions result in effective chirality transfer from the ligand to the Co-MOFs.11

Ferroelectric studies

X-ray single crystal diffraction analysis illustrates that Co-MOF-1 and Co-MOF-2 crystallized in two different polar point groups that are essential for ferroelectric behaviour.12 The ferroelectric studies were carried out at the low-frequency of 0.2 Hz at room temperature and the voltage was limited to between 700 and 900 V. Polarization versus electric field (P–E) hysteresis loops for Co-MOF-1 and Co-MOF-2 are shown in Fig. 3, where one can study the extent of induced polarization. Co-MOF-1 does not show a clear hysteresis loop, which is contrary to the observed electric hysteresis loop of ferroelectricity in the plot of Co-MOF-2. The remanent polarization (Pr) value of Co-MOF-2 is 0.03–0.15 μC cm−2 and the coercive field value is 1.31–7.42 kV cm−1. The saturation spontaneous polarization (Ps) is 0.26–0.38 μC cm−2, which is slightly lower than the expected values of 0.40 μC cm−2 (along a-axis) and 0.56 μC cm−2 (along b-axis) (Fig. S2 and Table S5†).13 The Ps value is slightly larger than that observed for Rochelle salt (NaKC4H4O6·4H2O, 0.25 μC cm−2) and several other reported molecule-based ferroelectrics.14 The distinct octahedral configuration units in the molecular structure of our MOFs lead to a small structural distortion but a notable symmetry change. The symmetry break in Co-MOF-2 is not accompanied by the cancellation of the dipole moment, and the reorientation of the dipoles gives rise to components along the polar axis (section S2.1 in ESI†). In addition, the leakage current of Co-MOF-2 is lower than 10−7 A cm−2 (Fig. S3†), which indicates that the observed hysteresis loop is mainly due to ferroelectricity.9b

Dielectric properties, thermal behavior, and second harmonic generation (SHG) effects

Dielectric measurements were performed to further confirm the polar character of Co-MOF-2. However, a distinct phase transition did not occur within the measured temperature range because its dielectric constant is almost temperature-independent. Moreover, dielectric anomalies were not observed (Fig. S4a†). The thermal stability of the Co-MOF-2 sample was checked by TG and DSC curves under a N2 atmosphere (Fig. S4b†). The TG curve reveals that Co-MOF-2 undergoes a two-step weight loss process. In the first step, the mass loss of approximately 3.2% in the temperature range between 55 °C and 150 °C is caused by the loss of coordinated water molecules. In the second step, abrupt weight loss is observed in Co-MOF-2 between 225 °C and 280 °C, which is attributed to the collapse of the main framework.

The SHG measurement of Co-MOF-2 was conducted from 20 °C to 210 °C and the results are shown in Fig. S4c.† As can be seen, a SHG response is not observed within this temperature range. This phenomenon was ascribed to the self-absorption of the emitted radiation by the colored compound.15 The crystals were not transparent. Thus, the laser showed weak emission attributed to the SHG effect.16 There was no obvious thermal variation in the DSC curve and SHG signal before the structure of Co-MOF-2 began to change, which is in agreement with the dielectric analysis, further verifying that ferroelectric phase transitions did not occur under the test conditions.

Conclusions

In summary, two chiral Co-MOFs were prepared using dissolution–recrystallization and hydrothermal synthetic methodologies. Moreover, the MOFs were structurally and ferroelectrically characterized. Ferroelectric measurements reveal that Co-MOF-1 does not exhibit hysteretic behaviour. However, Co-MOF-2 displays an obvious electric hysteresis loop of ferroelectricity, even though both MOFs derive from a combination of chiral amino acid-containing reduced Schiff base ligands and Co(ii) salts. The weak molecular dipole might originate from the distinct molecular structure, which explains the absence of a hysteresis loop for Co-MOF-1. However, further in-depth studies, as well as theoretical calculations, are required to corroborate this hypothesis, as the modulation of spontaneous switching corresponding to Co-MOFs presents a formidable challenge in designing new ferroelectric materials.
