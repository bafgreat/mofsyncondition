Introduction

We may not be able to pinpoint the beginning of the MOF era, but certainly since the mid-1990s there has been continuous exponential growth of the number of molecular materials produced with MOF characteristics; now metal–organic frameworks are almost everywhere in the inorganic chemistry and crystal engineering literature.1 Apart from the beauty, the geometrical aesthetics and their structural diversity2–5 that initially attracted researchers' interest, there is a wide range of applications which involve MOFs, including gas storage and separation,6–12 catalysis,13–16 sensing,17 magnetism18,19 and so on. During the quest for new MOFs with exploitable properties, a large number of rigid and flexible ligands have been used,20–24 allowing the incorporation of rational design elements25–27 leading to compounds with exciting topologies. The development of new synthetic strategies, for the discovery of new families of MOFs with novel structural features and potentially interesting physical properties, is essential.

A successful approach towards this is the employment of templates.28,29 Several reports on the templated synthesis of MOFs involve the use of various structure-directing agents such as amines,30 alcohols,31 alkali ions,32 polyoxometalates,33 metal complexes,34 surfactants,35,36 urea derivatives,37etc. Thereby, a vast number of new MOFs have been synthesized, with many of them showing unprecedented structural figures and in many cases intriguing properties. Amino- and pyridine-alcohol ligands are promising candidates for use as templates in the synthesis of new MOFs and surprisingly they have not been studied thoroughly. These ligands may achieve a variety of functions to accommodate the formation of new frameworks including deprotonation of the oxygen atoms of the polytopic organic ligands (e.g., carboxylic), templating effects, and ligation to the metal ions. They can also be quite effective as ligands for the metal ions displaying a variety of coordination modes, such as terminal, chelating, or bridging ligation, resulting often in the assembly of high nuclearity metal clusters. Furthermore, these organic molecules may be highly efficient as structure-directing agents due to their excellent capability for formation of hydrogen bonds.38–45 Thus, amino- and pyridine-alcohols could lead to MOFs with new structural types not only due to their strong templating effect but also because they are powerful ligands that may stabilize unprecedented secondary building units (SBUs). Previously, our research group reported a series of new MOFs obtained from the use of a combination of trimesic acid (H3btc), which typically leads to polymeric structures, and various amino-alcohols that acted either as structure-directing agents or ligands.46

With the aim of expanding our investigations and preparing new compounds with attractive architectures and exploitable physical properties, we initiated a systematic study on reactions of metal ion salts with pyridine-3,5-dicarboxylic acid (PDCH2) in the presence of a variety of amino- or pyridine-alcohol ligands (a diol is also used as a connection or reference point to our previous work). In general, multifunctional nitrogen and oxygen-donor connectors such as pyridine carboxylates generate zero-, one-, two- and three-dimensional architectures. These ligands also possess several discrete metal-binding sites and limited steric hindrance and can offer possibilities to form unexpected, unpredictable and interesting structures.47 PDCH2 was chosen for its size and rigidity that reduce the possibility of interpenetration, and its negative charge (when it is deprotonated), which eliminates the need for counter anions which claim cell space within the resulting molecular material, reducing this way the solvent-accessible area inside the bulk material.48 Additionally, PDCH2 is a well-studied ligand. A search in the CSD49 reveals 284 examples of structurally characterized complexes with PDCH2 in all its protonation forms, and with coordination modes varying from N-bonded monodentate50 to μ8 bridging.51 This, through comparisons, can allow us to assess the effect of the templates in our reaction systems.

Herein we report compounds [[(CH3)2NH2]2[Cd2(PDC)3]]n [1], [Mn(PDC)(DMF)]n [2], [Mn3(PDC)2(INA)2(DMF)1.5(H2O)0.5]n [3], [Zn(PDC)(NMP)]n [4], [Zn(PDC)(H2O)(DMF)]n [5], [Zn(PDC)(3hmpH)]n [6], [Zn2(PDC)2(2hmpH)4]·2(DMF) [7], [Co(PDC)(3hmpH)2]n [8] and [Cu(PDC)(3hmpH)2]n [9] which are the initial synthetic and structural results of these investigations. They have been isolated by the introduction of one of the following molecules as templates to reaction mixtures containing the metal salt and pyridine-3,5-dicarboxylic acid PDCH2: diethanolamine (deaH3), triethanolamine (teoaH3), N-phenyldiethanolamine (phdeaH2), 2-hydroxymethylpyridine (2hmpH), 3-hydroxymethylpyridine (3hmpH), 3-hydroxypropylpyridine (3hppH), 1,3-propanediol (pdH2), and 4-hydroxymethylpyridine (4hmpH) (Scheme 1). These new compounds have structures based on pyridine-3,5-dicarboxylic acid; however they present new structural-topological features and a remarkable diversity that is clearly induced by the various amino- or pyridine alcohols. The thermal stability of the compounds is also reported. To the best of our knowledge, such studies have been performed only by us with trimesic acid46 and by Andruh et al.52 with 1,2-, 1,3-, and 1,4-benzedicarboxylic acids; this study represents the initial systematic investigation on the use of a combination of polytopic nitrogen and oxygen-donor ligands such as PDCH2 and amino- or pyridine-alcohols for the synthesis of novel MOFs.

Results and discussion

Syntheses

Our group, over the last few years, has been investigating the use of polytopic organic ligands (i.e. the tricarboxylic ligand btcH3) together with various amino-alcohols as a method for the synthesis of new MOFs.46 An extension of these investigations included the use of the well-known PDCH2, which is similar to btcH3; it is rigid and tritopic with one less donor atom, since one of the carboxylate groups has been replaced by a heterocyclic nitrogen atom.

Compound [1] was isolated in ca. 62% yield from the reaction of CdCl2, PDCH2 and deaH3 or teoaH3 in a molar ratio of 1 : 0.55 : 38 or 27.65, respectively, in DMF at 100 °C. When the structure of compound [1] was elucidated, similar reactions were performed without or with the other amino or pyridine alcohols presented in Scheme 1, without obtaining any crystalline product. This was disappointing, but we decided to continue with other metal ions. It is worth mentioning that there are two examples of Cd–PDC coordination polymers in the literature formulated as [Cd(PDC)(H2O)2]n 53 and [Cd(PDC)(DMA)2]n,54 derived from different reaction mixtures; both of them have 2D honeycomb structures. It appears that in our case, DMF has completed the reaction system, through its hydrolysis, providing the necessary dimethylammonium cations for the formation of a novel anionic 3D polymer. Note that without the presence of deaH3 or teoaH3 in the reaction mixture, isolated powders have different diffraction patterns.

The corresponding Mn2+ system resulted in compounds [2] and [3]. The reaction of Mn(NO3)2·4H2O, PDCH2 in a molar ratio of 1 : 0.75 and an excess of phdeaH2, 2hmpH, 3hmpH or 3hppH or without any template in DMF at 100 °C resulted in compound [2] in ca. 58–59% yield. [2] is very similar to [Mn(PDC)(DMA)]n.54 Even though several alcohols gave the same result, the use of 4hmpH led to compound [3] in 72% yield. While hydroxyalkylpyridines, used as templates for the synthesis of [2], appear to be stable under the reaction conditions, 4hmpH is not. It was oxidized to form isonicotinic acid (INAH), which incorporated in the reaction product [3], resulting in a novel 3D polymer with a new topology.

Our initial attempts on the Zn2+/PDCH2 system involved reactions of Zn2+ salts and PDCH2 in a 1 : 0.66 molar ratio in the presence of templates. Some tuning of this ratio and the counter ion of the salt was essential to ensure reasonable yields, product purity and crystal quality (the best attempts are described in the experimental part). Compounds [5], [6] and [7] were isolated in this way. [5] can be isolated in the presence of phdeaH2, deaH3, teoaH3 or pdH2 and belongs to the 2D polymer family with a honeycomb topology formulated as M(PDC)(ligand)2;53–64 it is, though, the second57 example where the 2D layer contains two different axial ligands. [6] and [7] were isolated in the presence of 3- and 2-hydroxymethylpyridine, respectively. In both cases, the template is coordinated to Zn2+ leading to a 2D structure for [6], while the chelating ability of 2hmpH took control leading to a 0D binuclear compound. Utilizing 4hmpH as the template, we were not able to isolate crystalline products; powders were analysed as Zn(PDC)(4hmpH)2−x(solvent)x·n(solvents) but the amount and the identity of the solvents varied randomly depending on the experiment.

There is a 3D polymer in the literature prepared from reactions of Zn2+ salts and PDCH2 in DMA at 10565 and 120 °C54 formulated as Zn(PDC)(DMA); since in DMF at 100 °C all of our attempts led to 2D or 0D products, we decided to increase the temperature to 150 °C and change the solvent to NMP. The isolated product [4] is similar to the above mentioned one with DMA replaced by NMP. Note that complex [4] was isolated only when propanediol was used as the template.

Finally, reactions of Co(NO3)2·6H2O or Cu(NO3)2·2.5H2O, PDCH2 in molar ratios 1 : 0.87 or 1 : 0.70, respectively, and excess of 3hmpH in DMF at 100 °C resulted in complexes [8] and [9]. In both cases, 3hmpH acts as a terminal ligand completing the coordination sphere of the metal ions. [8] belongs to the 2D honeycomb family of compounds mentioned above, but [9] is a scarce ribbon shaped 1D polymer. In the presence of the other hydroxyalkylpyridines, we did not manage to isolate pure products with Co(ii) or Cu(ii), but when deaH3, teoaH3 or phdeaH2 was used as the template, known products were isolated. In the presence of amino alcohols, the Co(ii) reaction system yielded microcrystalline powders analysed as [Co(PDC)(DMF)2−x(H2O)x]. When we increased the amount of water in the reaction media (addition of 1–2 drops of water), [Co(PDC)(H2O)2]n was then isolated.58 Most of the Cu(ii)–PDCH2 reaction mixtures containing various amounts of amino alcohols led to the isolation of a known MOF formulated as [Cu2(PDC)2(H2O)]n·(solvents).66

The purity of the products was confirmed by the similarity between the experimental and simulated (from single crystal diffraction data) PXRD patterns (Fig. S1–S9 in the ESI†). Some very minor differences between the experimental and simulated from the crystal structure PXRD patterns are expected because: (i) the two measurements were performed under different conditions, (ii) some electron density has been removed during refinement of the structure and thus the powder sample is not exactly the same as the refined model, and (iii) the presence of preferred orientation effects in the powder sample.

Several reactions were performed with a variety of 3d metal ions : PDCH2 : amino or pyridine-alcohol ratios which revealed that in most cases a large excess of alcohol ligands was essential in order to obtain the desired compound in high purity and decent yield. Reactions were also performed without the presence of alcohols and only in the case of [2] the compound was isolated. In all other procedures, no result was obtained.

Description of the structures

Crystal structure of [1]

Compound [1] crystallizes in the orthorhombic space group Cmc21. The structure consists of a 3D anionic network, dimethylammonium cations and solvent molecules, which are disordered in the cavities of the network. There are two crystallographically unique Cd2+ ions (with half occupancy) in the structure [Cd(1) and Cd(2)] and three crystallographically independent PDC2− ligands denoted as L1, L2 and L3 (see Fig. 1 for the atom numbering scheme). L1 and L3 PDC2− ligands are half molecules with C2 axes passing through C(3), N(1) and Cd(1) for L1 and C(14) N(3) and Cd(1) for L3. The L2 PDC2− molecule is sitting on a mirror plane with all of its atoms having half occupancy. Cd(1) is seven coordinated in a distorted pentagonal bipyramidal environment consisting of five oxygen and two nitrogen atoms. Four of the O atoms belong to two symmetry related chelated carboxylates of L3, while the fifth one belongs to a monodentate carboxylate of L2. The N atoms belong to L1 and to a third symmetry related PDC2− (L3) anion. Cd(2) is again 7-coordinated but its coordination sphere consists of six oxygen atoms of three chelated carboxylates; two of them belong to symmetry related PDC2− (L1) molecules other than the previously described and the third is part of a symmetry related L2. The N atom belongs to the original L2. The coordination sphere of Cd(2) can be better described as tetrahedral considering the Cd–carboxylate bonds directed towards the centre of the carboxylates. Two of the PDC2− ligands (L1 and L3) use all of their donor atoms for coordination whilst the other one (L2) has a monodentate carboxylate group, utilizing only four of its available donor atoms for coordination.

The topological description of the network eventually formed is rather complicated considering that all three ligands serve as 3-connected (3-c) nodes while the two crystallographically independent Cd2+ centres adopt different connectivities. Cd(1) is a 5-connected node (the arrangement of the ligands–nodes around it adopts a distorted trigonal bipyramidal geometry) while Cd(2) is a 4-c node (the arrangement of the ligands–nodes around it adopts a distorted tetrahedral geometry). Cd(1) connects to all three ligands while Cd(2) is connected to PDC2− ligands L1 and L2. Although all three PDC2− ligands are 3-connected, each one has a different point symbol, thus resulting in a 5-nodal net with an overall point symbol of (6·82)(62·8)(63·83)(63)(66·83·10) which is, as expected, unique so far (Fig. 2). A simpler approach to the topology of the network formed can be made when the bonds of L1 and L2 to Cd(1) are ignored. This way, two different alternating 2D nets are formed parallel to the ac plane of the cell: a) a hexagonal hcb net between Cd(1) and L3, since Cd(1) has transformed from a 5-c into a 3-c node, and b) an undulating tetragonal sql net between Cd(2) L1 and L2, since L1 and L2 have been transformed into simple bridges and Cd(2) remained a 4-c node.

Zig-zag channels are formed parallel to the a axis of the cell (Fig. 3 and S10†) with a reasonable diameter (approximately 7–8 Å taking into account the van der Waals radii of the atoms) to accommodate (NH2Me2)+ cations and solvated molecules (most possibly DMF, H2O and/or deaH3). Unfortunately, severe disordering did not allow the assignment of those molecules. The solvent-accessible volume is 855.6 Å3 and corresponds to 20.5% of the unit-cell volume (4168.42 Å3).

Crystal structure of [2]

Compound [2] crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/n. Its crystal structure consists of a 3D neutral network. The asymmetric unit contains three Mn2+ ions, three PDC2− ligands and three coordinated DMF molecules (Fig. 4). Two of the MnII ions, namely Mn(2) and Mn(3), are in a rather regular octahedral environment as can be seen from the trans angles which span the range of 168.06–177.69°, while Mn(1) sits in the center of a distorted octahedron (range of trans angles 155.99–165.08°). This distortion is due to one of the L2′ carboxylates chelating Mn(1) with its μ-η1,η2 coordination mode. This also leads to an irregular arrangement of the Mn3 cluster, which is bent with the Mn(1)–Mn(2)–Mn(3) angle being approximately 159° and has different Mn⋯Mn separations (Mn(1)–Mn(2), 3.599; Mn(2)–Mn(3) 4.337 Å) (Fig. 4a). Two of the crystallographically independent PDC2− ligands, L1 and L3, are bonded in the same fashion, utilizing each of their donor atoms for one coordinative interaction, while the third one (L2) uses only carboxylate oxygen atoms for coordination. The two carboxylate groups, though, are bridging MnII atoms in different modes; one of them is μ-η1,η1 and the other μ-η2,η1 (Fig. 4b–d).

The central metal atom Mn(2) is surrounded by six carboxylate oxygen atoms [O(2), O(8), O(11), O(3)#2, O(6)#3 and O(10)#4] from six different PDC2− ligands [2 L1, 2 L2 and 2 L3]. Mn(1) is coordinated by four carboxylate oxygen atoms [O(4)#2, O(7), O(8) and O(12)] from three different PDC2− anions, one nitrogen atom [N(3)#1] from a fourth PDC2− anion (1 L1, 1 L2 and 2 L3) and a DMF molecule [O(3S)]. Mn(3) is surrounded by a [NO5] donor set, generated by three carboxylate oxygen atoms [O(1), O(5)#3 and O(9)#4] of three PDC2− units (1 L1, 1 L2 and 1 L3), one nitrogen atom [N(1)#5] from L1 and two coordinated DMF solvent molecules [O(1S) and O(2S)], respectively.

The topological description is straightforward, with the two crystallographically independent PDC2− ligands (L1 and L3) connecting three different Mn3 units each (3-c nodes) and the third one (L2) two Mn3 units, being a spacer. In fact, a pair of two L3 ligands bridge two neighbouring Mn3 units. Ligands L1 and L3 (3-c nodes) along with the Mn3 clusters form an undulated 3,6-connected kgd (Kagome lattice) net where the Mn3 units serve as 6-connected nodes. These layers are further connected to the third dimension by double L2 bridges (two L2 ligands bridge two Mn3 units belonging to neighbouring layers). Therefore, the overall connectivity of the Mn3 nodes is 7. The underlying binodal net is 3,7-connected with a stoichiometry of (3-c)2(7-c) and a point symbol of (43)2(4·6610·85) (Fig. 5). This net is not new since it has been enumerated and ascribed the topological type tfz-d-3,7-Immm.

This connectivity gives rise to a 3-D framework which exhibits a square grid of channels running parallel to the ab plane with the largest openings along the b-axis (Fig. 5, 6 and S11†). The channel wall distance is approximately 9 Å along a, but the hosting of the coordinated DMF molecules reduces the solvent accessible volume to 3.1%. In the absence of (the coordinated) solvent molecules, the solvent-accessible volume is 1027.2 Å3 and corresponds to 25.4% of the unit-cell volume (4039.5 Å3).

Crystal structure of [3]

Compound [3] crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/c and is a 3D coordination polymer. The asymmetric unit consists of three Mn2+ ions, two PDC2− and two INA− ligands and two coordinated DMF solvent molecules. All Mn2+ ions are in a distorted octahedral coordination geometry and form a trinuclear hourglass arrangement (Fig. 7a). The terminal manganese ions of the Mn3 unit (Mn(1) and Mn(3)) are surrounded by the same cis-[N2O4] donor set. In particular, they are coordinated to three carboxylate oxygen atoms [O(1), O(4)#1, and O(6)#3 for Mn(1) and O(7)#1, O(10) and O(11)#2 for Mn(3)] from two different PDC2− ligands and one INA− anion, respectively, a fourth O atom from a terminal DMF solvent molecule [O(2S) for Mn(1) and O(1S) for Mn(3)] and two nitrogen atoms [N(3)#5, N(4) for Mn(1) and N(1)#1, N(2) for Mn(3)] from one PDC2−/one INA− ligand. The central metal atom, Mn(2), is surrounded by six carboxylate oxygen atoms [O(2), O(3)#4, O(5)#3, O(8)#1, O(9), O(12)#2] belonging to four different PDC2− ligands and two different INA− ligands. The Mn3 cluster deviates significantly from linearity (the Mn(1)–Mn(2)–Mn(3) angle is ca. 150.5°) but the Mn⋯Mn separations are closer to each other (4.202, 4.129 Å), compared to the corresponding ones in [2], since the chelating–bridging carboxylate group is absent. Both PDC2− ligands serve as 3-connected (3-c) nodes, with each one bridging three different Mn3 clusters. The INA− ligands serve as spacers by simply bridging two Mn3 units. In fact, as in the case of complex [2], a pair of INA− ligands bridge two neighbouring Mn3 units. As in compound [2], the PDC2− ligands (3-c nodes) along with the Mn3 clusters form an undulated 3,6-coordinated kgd net where the Mn3 moieties serve as 6-coordinated nodes. These layers are further connected to the third dimension by two double INA− bridges [two pairs of INA− ligands (4 ligands in total) bridge two Mn3 units belonging to neighbouring layers]. Although this description resembles that of [2], there is a crucial difference between the frameworks and the underlying networks of these two structures. The Mn3 clusters in both complexes are surrounded (and connected) by six PDC2− ligands forming the kgd layer. In complex [2], a pair of other PDC2− ligands bridge two Mn3 clusters belonging to neighbouring layers, but in complex [3], two pairs of INA− ligands (4 INA− ligands in total) bridge a Mn3 cluster to two other Mn3 clusters with the latter two (Mn3 units) belonging to the same neighbouring layer. In this arrangement, each Mn3 cluster is surrounded by six PDC2− ligands (3-c nodes) and four INA− ligands which serve as two spacers. Therefore, the overall connectivity of the Mn3 units is 8. The underlying binodal net is 3,8-connected with a stoichiometry of (3-c)2(8-c) and a point symbol of (411·614·83)(43)2 which is unique so far (Fig. 8). Note that in complex [3] each Mn3 cluster is surrounded by six 3-c PDC2− ligands and four INA− spacers which in pairs serve as two spacers (two pairs of two ligands bridge a Mn3 unit to two other Mn3 units), whereas in complex [2] each Mn3 cluster is surrounded by six 3-c PDC ligands and two PDC spacers which serve as one (a pair of ligands bridge two Mn3 units) link. The 3D network risen in [3] because of the connectivity of the metal aggregates and the ligands exhibits channels running along the a axis. Those are of considerable size (approximate dimensions are 17 × 9 Å) but the accommodation of the coordinated DMF molecules in them reduces the solvent accessible volume to ∼6.1% of the unit cell volume (Fig. 8, 9 and S12†). In the absence of (the coordinated) solvent molecules, the solvent-accessible volume is 909.6 Å3 and corresponds to ∼21.2% of the unit-cell volume (4291.0 Å3).

Crystal structure of [4]

Compound [4] crystallizes in the orthorhombic space group P212121 and is a 3D polymer. The asymmetric unit consists of a fragment of the 3D polymer that contains one Zn2+ ion, a coordinated PDC2− ligand and a coordinated NMP solvent molecule. Zn(1) sits in the center of a distorted tetrahedron constructed from two carboxylate oxygen atoms [O(1), O(4)] belonging to two symmetry related PDC2− ligands, a nitrogen atom [N(1)] belonging to a third symmetry related PDC2− ligand, and an oxygen atom from a coordinated NMP solvent molecule [O(5)] (Fig. 10). Both PDC2− and Zn2+ ions serve as three connected nodes (3-c) and they are equivalent, leading to the creation of the classic srs uninodal 3D network with the point symbol 103 (Fig. 11). Wide channels, of approximate diameter 4–5 Å (taking into account the van der Waals radii of the atoms) are running along the a axis. The solvent accessible volume of [4] was calculated to be ∼36.1% of the unit cell volume using the program Mercury (Fig. 12 and S13 in the ESI†).

Crystal structure of [5]

Compound [5] crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/c and is a 2D coordination polymer. The asymmetric unit (Fig. 13a) consists of a Zn2+ ion which is in a significantly distorted trigonal bipyramidal coordination environment (trigonality index,67τ5 = 0.61). The three equatorial positions of Zn(1) are occupied by donor atoms belonging to three symmetry related PDC2− ligands [two carboxylate oxygen atoms, O(1), O(3)#1 and one pyridine nitrogen, N(1)#2], whereas the axial positions are filled by coordinated DMF [O(5)] and water [O(6)] solvent molecules. A two-dimensional layered structure is constructed on the bc-plane with, eventually, negligible openings along the a-direction (Fig. 13b). Those openings are covered by the solvent molecules coordinated at the Zn(1) apical positions of an adjacent layer. Both PDC2− and Zn2+ ions serve as three coordinated nodes giving rise to a uninodal 2D net, commonly known as honeycomb with the point symbol 63 and three letter code hcb. Interestingly, the terminal H2O molecules are situated on the same side of the layer and all terminal ligated DMF molecules on the opposite side. The hcb layers pack in pairs such that the terminal H2O molecules of one layer face the H2O molecules of the neighbouring layer. Additionally, there is an extensive array of conventional and non-conventional H-bonds that assist the stabilization of the structure (Table S11†). The most important of them are those formed by the coordinated water molecule [O(6)] as a donor towards the non-coordinated carboxylate oxygen atoms [O(6)–H(6A)⋯O(4)(−x + 1, −y + 2, −z + 1) and O(6)–H(6B)⋯O(2)(−x + 1, y + 1/2, −z + 1/2)]; this way, through interlayer H-bonds, double 2D layers are formed which interact to each other with weak van der Waals interactions via the coordinated DMF molecules. Extra stabilization of the double layer is provided by ring stacking interactions between PDC2− pyridine rings (plane distance: 3.38, offset of centroids: 1.18 Å). Eventually, this arrangement gives rise to two different interlayer distances of 3.380 Å between layers spaced by the terminal H2O molecules and 7.663 Å between layers spaced by the terminal DMF molecules (Fig. 14).

Compound [5] joins a small family of 2D PDC2− coordination polymers with similar structures and the same topology. The molecular formulas along with unit cell details of the members of this family and corresponding references are listed in the ESI† (Table S16).53–64 Compound [5] exhibits a series of differences from the known compounds. In particular, apart from the obvious differences among these compounds due to the metal ion and the axial ligand, there are some variations due to the coordination mode of the PDC2− carboxylates. Thus, in compounds [Cd(PDC)(DMA)2]n 54 and [5] both carboxylates are unidentate whilst in the remaining ones in Table S16† either both carboxylates are chelated or one chelated and the other is unidentate. Additionally, [5] and [Cd(PDC)(DMA)2]n 54 are the only examples with two different axial ligands leading to the formation of double layers.

Crystal structure of [6]

Compound [6] crystallizes in the orthorhombic space group P212121 and is a 2D coordination polymer. The asymmetric unit (Fig. 15a) consists of a Zn2+ ion which is in a slightly distorted tetrahedral geometry, one PDC2− ligand and one 3hmpH ligand connected to Zn(1) via N(1). The coordination sphere of Zn(1) is constituted by two oxygen atoms belonging to two different carboxylates of two symmetry related PDC2− molecules (O(2)#, O(4)#), one nitrogen belonging to a third symmetry related PDC2− ligand, and one nitrogen atom belonging to a 3hmpH molecule. Both PDC2− and Zn2+ ions serve as three connected nodes (3-c) giving rise to an undulated uninodal 2D layer commonly known as honeycomb with the point symbol 63 and three letter code hcb (Fig. 15b).

Although, the topology of compounds [5] and [6] is essentially the same, their crystal and molecular structures present significant differences. These are: i) the crystal systems and space groups in which these compounds crystallize (monoclinic P21/c for [5] and orthorhombic P212121 for [6]), b) the metal ion geometry (trigonal bipyramid for [5] vs. tetrahedral for [6]), c) the coordination sphere of each metal ion, where in [5] is completed by one water molecule and a DMF molecule and in [6] by a 3hmpH ligand, and d) the coordination mode of PDC2−; considering PDC2− a rectangle of four oxygen atoms in [5] uses its “trans” O atoms while in [6] the “cis” O atoms.

Crystal structure of [7]

Compound [7] crystallizes in the triclinic space group P and its unit cell contains dinuclear complexes formulated as [Zn(PDC)(2hmpH)2]2 and DMF solvent molecules filling the empty space of the structure. Zn2+ is in the center of a distorted trans octahedral coordination sphere consisting of two carboxylate oxygen atoms [O(3), O(5)] belonging to two bridging PDC2− ligands, two alcoholic oxygen atoms [O(1), O(2)] and two heterocyclic nitrogen atoms [N(1), N(2)] (on the axial positions) belonging to two chelating 2hmpH molecules (Fig. 16). An inversion center is located between the two Zn atoms. There are four strong intramolecular hydrogen bonds between the alcoholic hydrogen atoms and the non-coordinated carboxylate oxygen atoms [O(1)–H(1A)⋯O(4) and O(2)–H(2A)⋯O(6)(−x, −y + 1, −z + 2)] which stabilize the coordination sphere about Zn(ii) (Table S12†).

Interestingly, all aromatic rings are involved in π–π stacking interactions (one each) with symmetry related rings (Fig. 17). There are three π–π stacking interactions per asymmetric unit or six per dimer [ring N(1)–ring N(1)(−x, −y, −z + 2), least squares (ls) plane distance: 3.19, centroid offset: 1.67; ring N(2)–ring N(2)(−x + 1, −y + 1, −z + 2), ls plane distance: 3.57, centroid offset: 2.02; ring N(3)–ring N(3)(−x + 1, −y + 1, −z + 2), ls plane distance: 3.55, centroid offset: 1.25 Å]. Taking into account these weak interactions, each dimer can be considered as a 6-connected node leading to the construction of a uninodal pcu network [point symbol: (412·63)].

The structure of [7] can only be compared to two of the PDC2− complexes found in the CSD:49 [Ti(PDC)(cp)2]2 68 and [Cu(PDC)(terpy)]2 69 where the coordination sites of the metal ions are blocked by cp and terpy ligands, respectively, not allowing polymer formation.

Crystal structure of [8]

Compound [8] crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21 and it is a 2D coordination polymer. The asymmetric unit consists of a Co2+ ion which is in a distorted octahedral geometry, one μ3-PDC2− ligand and two N-bonded 3hmpH molecules. The four equatorial positions of Co(1) are occupied by three carboxylate oxygen atoms [O(2)#1, O(3)#2, O(4)#3] and a nitrogen atom [N(1)] from three different symmetry related PDC2− ligands, whereas the axial positions are filled by the nitrogen atoms of two different terminal 3hmpH [N(2), N(3)] ligands (Fig. 18a). Both PDC2− and Co(1) serve as three connected nodes giving rise to a planar 2D hcb net, as described previously, parallel to the ab plane (Fig. 18b). The terminal 3hmpH molecules are situated above and below the 2D layers. The hcb layers are equally spaced (interlayer separation 8.040 Å) and packed off-set such that the Co2+ ions of one layer are almost above and below the centers of the hexagonal cavities of its closest neighboring layers. In this arrangement, the pending 3hmpH molecules are holding the layers together with three distinct interactions leading to the formation of a 3D supramolecular architecture: a) O(5) acts as an H-bond donor towards a non-coordinated carboxylate oxygen atom belonging to an adjacent layer, O(5)–H(7A)⋯O(1)(−x, y + 1/2, −z); b) O(5) acts as an H-bond acceptor for O(6) belonging to a 3hmpH molecule of the previous layer, O(6)–H(6)(x, y, z − 1)⋯O(5) (Table S13†); c) the 3hmpH ligand which contains N(2) and O(5) sits between the symmetry related 3hmpH molecules containing N(3) and O(6) and belonging to the above mentioned layer interacting with them via π–π interactions (the interaction to one of the rings is quite weaker), ring N(2)–ring N(3)(x, y, z − 1), ls plane distance, 3.74, centroid offset, 0.83; ring N(2)–ring N(3)(x − 1, y, z − 1), ls plane distance, 3.52, centroid offset, 2.31 Å (Fig. 19). Note that, apart from the supramolecular interactions, the structure and topology of this 2D polymer are similar to those of [5]. It can be said that [8] is the 14th member of the family discussed above.

Crystal structure of [9]

Compound [9] crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/c and is a 1D polymer. The asymmetric unit consists of one and a half Cu2+ ions, one and a half PDC2− ligands, and three 3hmpH molecules (Fig. 20). Both copper ions have distorted square pyramidal geometries as shown by their trigonality67 index [Cu(1), τ5 = 0.30; Cu(2), τ5 = 0.21], but they have different coordination spheres. Cu(1) is surrounded by two carboxylate oxygen atoms [O(1), O(4)#1] belonging to two PDC2− ligands and two nitrogen atoms [N(3), N(4)] belonging to two 3hmpH ligands on the basal plane while the apical position is occupied by an oxygen atom [O(3)], belonging to a third PDC2− anion. For Cu(2), the basal plane consists of two carboxylate oxygen atoms, [O(5), O(5)#2], belonging to symmetry related PDC2− anions and two nitrogen atoms, [N(5), N(5)#2], from symmetry related 3hmpH molecules; N(1), from a third PDC2− ligand, sits on the apex of the pyramid. There are two crystallographically independent PDC2− ligands in this structure. The first one [denoted as L1, contains N(1)] utilizes two oxygen atoms and one nitrogen atom, while the second one [denoted as L2, contains N(2)] utilizes three oxygen atoms for coordination. One carboxylate group of L2 PDC2− anion bridges two symmetry related Cu(1) ions whereas the second one is bonded to Cu(2) via one O atom, leading to the formation of a zig-zag tape with alternating binuclear and mononuclear copper units. There are two symmetry elements responsible for the formation of the 1D tape present in the structure: a) an inversion center lying between the metal ions of the binuclear unit, and b) a C2 axis passing through the Cu(2)–N(1) bond. The structure extends to one-dimension running parallel to the [2 0 1] vector (Fig. 20b). There is a plethora of weaker interactions appearing in the structure of [9] involving H-bonds and π–π stacking which lead to the formation of a 3D supramolecular architecture. The most important are the H-bonds formed between the alcoholic hydrogen atoms of one ribbon to acceptors belonging to adjacent ribbons. Those are O(7)–H(7A)⋯O(6)(x − 1/2, y + 1/2, z), O(9)–H(9A)⋯O(2)(x + 1/2, y − 1/2, z) and O(8A)–H(8A)⋯O7(1 + x, y, z) (see Table S14† for details).

There is a handful of 1D polymers of PDC2− in the literature. Those are prepared either in a water-rich environment69 and at low temperatures58 or when the metal ion has its coordination sites strongly blocked as in triorganotin chemistry.70 [9] is not only new in PDC2− chemistry but also in polycarboxylate MOF chemistry since it is derived under solvothermal conditions, in the absence of water and contains two different bridging modes of PDC2− with relatively high denticity for bipositive metal 1D polymers.

Thermal stability studies

The thermal stability of the prepared compounds was investigated by means of thermogravimetric (TG) analysis in static air. The TG graphs accompanied with their 1st derivative (DTG) can be found in the ESI,† Fig. S15–S23.

The compounds, based on their thermal decomposition behaviour, can be separated into three distinct groups. The first one includes only compound [1] which does not exhibit coordinated solvent or template molecules, and, as an anionic network, contains dimethylammonium counter ions in its hollow space. The pristine material starts losing its water content right above room temperature and completes it up to 110 °C (found: 8.5; calcd. for 6 H2O: 8.9%). After a smooth curve inflection and up to 220 °C, the DMF content is lost (found: 24.5; calcd. for 4 DMF: 24.1%). This procedure overlaps with the decomposition of PDC2− which is completed with a sharp exotherm at ca. 440 °C (found: 38.1, calcd. for 3 PDC2−: 40.8%). Weight loss calculations suggest that the final residue is CdO (found: 22.5; calcd: 21.2%). The small discrepancies between experimental and calculated values for the weight loss of PDC2− and the final residue can be attributed to the overlapping decomposition procedures in connection with the graphical method of estimation, while slightly increased residues can be found when the residues are carbonaceous or contaminated with the corresponding carbonate.

The second group consists of compounds [2]–[5] which all contain coordinated and lattice solvent molecules. The TG curves for these materials suggest that after the lattice and coordinated solvent molecules have been successively removed upon temperature increase, the intermediates are stable up to ca. 400 °C where the decomposition of PDC2− begins and concludes at ca. 480 °C. The final residues are metal oxides. Table S14† contains detailed information on experimental and calculated weight losses.

The third group consists of compounds [6]–[9] which contain coordinated hydroxyalkylpyridine molecules. Their decomposition process is rather complicated with successive changes at curve inflections and without any significant plateaus (except for [7] which is a molecular complex) other than the final residue. Their common characteristics are their stability up to ca. 190 °C, the decomposition of the hydroxyalkylpyridines up to 300 °C which is followed by the decomposition of the PDC2− ligands and the final plateau reached above 400 °C and corresponds to the metal oxides. Detailed information about TG data for [1]–[9] is summarized in Table S15.†

Conclusions

In conclusion, eight new coordination polymers and one dinuclear complex based on the pyridine-3,5-dicarboxylate ligand have been synthesized under solvothermal conditions. The variety of their structures can be attributed to the endogenous metal ion properties, to the diversity of the coordination modes of the ligand and to the presence of various template molecules, which in certain cases participate in the final formulations. The last two factors are not independent of each other. It has become obvious that amino alcohols, though affecting the formation and identity of the final product, are not parts of its structure. This is in contrast to our previous experience,17 when the nodes of a polymer network are clusters. On the other hand, pyridine alcohols almost always participate in the structure of the final product.

The isolation of compounds [1]–[9] can lead to specific remarks concerning the role of amino- and pyridine-alcohol templates. Thus, in [1], teoaH3 or deaH3 in combination with DMF, which is partially hydrolysed to provide the positive charge necessary to balance the charge of the anionic network, is essential for its formation. Similarly, in compounds [6]–[9] the pyridine alcohol templates are coordinated to the metal ions and obviously their use in the reaction mixture is required for their isolation. On the other hand, amino alcohols appear to have no effect on the formation of [5] probably due to the strong H-bonds formed via the coordinated water molecules between adjacent layers. In addition, [2] is so stable that nothing from the available template library added into the reaction mixture affected its identity and structure, except for 4hmpH, which was oxidized to isonicotinic acid in the reaction mixture, producing [3]. In fact, compound [3] was proven valuable for the progress of our research efforts; it revealed, in combination with [2] and [Mn(PDC)(DMA)]n,55 that the formulation [Mn3(PDC)2]2+ is rather stable and led us to a new project using PDCH2 mixed with different bridging ligands in Mn2+ chemistry. These investigations are underway and the results will be presented in the future. Thus, the use of the template amino- and pyridine-alcohol ligands in the reaction mixtures is necessary for the isolation of all the reported compounds except for [2] and [5] which are isolated even without the addition of the template ligand.

These results also allowed some conclusive observations with respect to the size of the ligand used in the so-called “terminal co-ligand directed synthesis”.60 Grouping compounds [4], [5], [6], [Zn(PDC)(H2O)2]n,56 and [Zn(PDC)(DMA)]n,54,65 we can see that when the co-ligand is of smaller size (i.e. water or DMF, in compounds [5] and [Zn(PDC)(H2O)2]n) the coordination number of Zn2+ is 5 with a trigonal bipyramidal arrangement of the donors giving 3-c honeycomb planar layers. When the size of the co-ligand (i.e. DMA, NMP, compounds [4] and [Zn(PDC)(DMA)]n) is increased, the coordination number is reduced to 4, the geometry about the metal ion is tetrahedral and a 3D network is formed; a further increase of the co-ligand size (3hmpH, compound [6]) leaves the coordination number and geometry unaltered but leads to the formation of an undulating honeycomb network. For the formation of [7], [8] and [9], it appears that other factors than the size of the co-ligand take control. For [7], it is the chelating effect of 2hmpH, while for [8] and [9] the directional effect of the partially filled 3d orbitals and the ability of Cu2+ to form square pyramidal coordination geometries.

Overall, the combination of PDCH2 with aminoalcohols in MOF chemistry was proven a fruitful source of new coordination polymers, some of which exhibited unusual or even unprecedented network topologies. This is very surprising taking into account that H2PDC is well-studied and the amount of new multidimensional coordination polymers of this work. Clearly, the discussed synthetic method is invaluable for the isolation of new MOFs. Further studies in this area using different metal ions, polytopic ligands and/or templates are in progress and will be reported in due course.
