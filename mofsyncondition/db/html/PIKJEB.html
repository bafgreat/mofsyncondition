Rare-earth metal (Ln) complexes bearing organoimido ligands (RN2−) are well established for the +3 oxidation state,1–3 and recently the first examples of anionic cerium(iv) organoimides were described.4,5 In stark contrast, the existence of imide derivatives of the softer Ln(ii) cations was discussed only for the parent “inorganic” moiety NH2−.6,7 Such imides emerge from the thermal treatment of amide species Ln(NH2)2 (Ln = Eu and Yb) as isolable intermediates en route to rare-earth metal nitrides.8–12 Supported variants such as [LnNH]@AC13 or [LnNH]@zeoliteY14–16 were proposed as likely active species in heterogeneously catalysed alkene hydrogenation and isomerization as well as Michael reactions. While the structure of YbNH was analysed by powder X-ray diffraction,7 the existence of EuNH was discussed controversially on the basis of spectroscopic data.6–16 The apparent lack of studies on compounds of the type [LnNR]x can be due to the lack of suitable precursors and/or the negative Ln(iii)/Ln(ii) redox potentials.17–19 We have therefore set out to develop reaction protocols applicable for the synthesis of simple homometallic Ln(ii) organimides. The double protonolysis of Ln(ii) precursors carrying strongly basic ligands like [LnR2] (R = hydride, alkyl, alkenyl, aryl, and benzyl) with amine derivatives represents an interesting and convenient approach. Such protonolysis protocols have already been proved successful in the intramolecular alkane elimination of Ln(iii) alkyl–amide complexes,20–22 and even more importantly in closely related Ca(ii) chemistry.23 The similarity of the ionic radii observed for the alkaline-earth metals Ca(ii) and Sr(ii) compared to divalent rare-earth metals Yb(ii) and Sm(ii)/Eu(ii) is striking (ionic radii [Å], CN = 6: Ca(ii): 1.00, Yb(ii): 1.03, Sr(ii): 1.18, Sm(ii): 1.15, Eu(ii): 1.14),24,25 often leading to comparable and isostructural complexes.26,27 Since discrete Ln(ii) alkyl complexes like Ln[C(SiMe3)3]2 might involve kinetic constraints for double protonolyses,19,28–30 we envisaged benzyl derivatives as suitable precursors. Pivotal for this choice of precursor was the feasibility of alkaline-earth metal benzyl complexes [Ae(CH2Ph)2] (Ae = Ca, Sr, and Ba).31–33 Accordingly and in analogy to the Ae(ii) chemistry, the salt metatheses of LnI2(thf)2 with benzyl potassium in thf led smoothly to the Ln(ii) dibenzyl complexes of samarium, europium and ytterbium (Scheme 1).

After workup, the crude donor-free products [Ln(CH2Ph)2]n (1-Ln; tentatively assigned as solvent-free, see the ESI†) were obtained as amorphous solids (Ln = Sm, black, 98%; Ln = Eu, orange, 98%; and Ln = Yb, black, 87%). While for Ca(ii) the corresponding thf solvate [(thf)4Ca(CH2Ph)2] could be crystallised from a mixture of benzene and thf,32 we did not succeed in generating the respective crystalline Yb(ii) compound from this solvent mixture.

However, as shown in Scheme 1, tetrahydropyran (thp) and a 1,2-dimethoxyethane (dme)/toluene mixture allowed for the crystallisation of the corresponding solvates [(do)xYb(CH2Ph)2] (1-Ybdo, do = thp, x = 4; do = dme, x = 2) as dark red crystals. Both in 1-Ybthp (monoclinic, C2/c) and 1-Ybdme (monoclinic, P21/c) the Yb(ii) centres adopt a distorted octahedral coordination geometry involving four oxygen atoms of the donor molecules and two cis-arranged benzyl ligands (Fig. 1). The Yb–C distances (1-Ybthp, 2.617(3) Å; 1-Ybdme, 2.572(2) Å and 2.585(2) Å) match the Ca–C distances in the related thf solvate [(thf)4Ca(CH2Ph)2] (2.568(5)–2.595(5) Å),32 but are slightly shorter compared to the Yb–C distances found in the benzyl derivative [(thf)2Yb{CH(SiMe3)(C6H4NMe2-2)}2] (2.640(3) and 2.661(3) Å), which is stabilised by intramolecular Yb2+⋯NMe2 interactions.27 For further comparison, a mixed-valent benzyl complex [(thf)5Yb(CH2Ph)]+[(thf)2Yb(CH2Ph)4]− was found in an attempted synthesis of [(thf)3Yb(CH2Ph)3] from YbI3(thf)3.5 and benzyl potassium, exhibiting an Yb(ii)–C bond length of 2.57(1) Å.34 It is noteworthy that the two donor-stabilised complexes display different Yb–Cbenzyl–Cipso angles. However, for 1-Ybthp a typical value of 115.9(1)° was found, and the complex 1-Ybdme features two significantly more acute angles of 90.8(4)° (Yb1–C7–C1) and 100.7(1)° (Yb1–C8–C9). This distinct benzyl bonding is also revealed by the shorter Yb–Cipso contacts in 1-Ybdme (Yb1⋯C1, 2.943(9) Å; Yb1⋯C9, 3.172(2) Å) when compared to 1-Ybthp (Yb1⋯C2, 3.495 Å).

Crystallisation of the benzyl complexes containing the larger and less Lewis acidic Sm(ii) and Eu(ii) metal centres turned out to be more difficult and was successful only by applying solvent mixtures (Scheme 1). As aforementioned, both Sm(ii) and Eu(ii) feature ionic radii similar to Sr(ii) and we assumed the formation of crystalline donor solvates isostructural to [(thf)3Sr2(CH2Ph)4]. The latter benzyl strontium thf adduct shows a rather complicated ribbon-like polymeric arrangement in the solid state with two distinct Sr environments.33 When 1-Sm was crystallised from a mixture of thf and thp, exactly this very structural motif was obtained. Unsurprisingly, complex [(do)3Sm2(CH2Ph)4] (1-Smdo, do = thf/thp; for the solid-state structure and detailed metrics, see Fig. S3, ESI†) features similar metrical parameters as the isostructural Sr(ii) congener, e.g., Sm–Cbenzyl contacts in the range of 2.704(6)–2.901(4) Å (versus 2.691(2)–2.886(2) Å for Sr(ii); benzyl coordination modes: η3, μ2-η6:η1, μ2-η3:η3, μ2-η3:η1, considering Sm⋯C distances <3.4 Å). A comparison can be also drawn to the donor-stabilised benzyl derivative [(thf)2Sm{CH(SiMe3)(C6H4NMe2-2)}2] (2.768(3) and 2.789(3) Å).27 However, the formed crystals rapidly lost the coordinated donor solvent, which complicated further analysis of the mixed thf/thp donor solvate (cf. ESI†).

In contrast, 1-Eu could be crystallised from a mixture of thf and n-hexane and it surprisingly showed a different molecular connectivity in the solid state with [(thf)2Eu4(CH2Ph)8] as the asymmetric unit (1-Euthf, Scheme 1 and Fig. 2). Like the solvated Sr(ii) and Sm(ii) derivatives, compound 1-Euthf features two distinct Eu(ii) environments and a complicated two-dimensional network in the solid state (cf. Fig. S4 (ESI†); benzyl coordination modes: μ2-η6:η1, μ2-η3:η3, μ2-η3:η2, considering Eu⋯C distances <3.4 Å). While Eu1 and Eu3 are coordinated exclusively by benzyl ligands, Eu2 and Eu4 additionally accommodate one molecule of the donor solvent thf. The Eu–Cbenzyl distances range from 2.753(8) Å (Eu1–C43) to 2.888(7) Å (Eu2–C15). Additional interactions are found with the ipso carbon atoms (2.908(7) Å to 3.251(8) Å) and ortho carbon atoms (2.993(7) Å to 3.380 Å). Moreover, Eu1 and Eu3 engage in η6-arene coordination with one benzyl ligand (Eu–CAr 2.884(7) Å to 3.182(7) Å).

A rapid loss of coordinated thf was also found for 1-Euthf, complicating microanalysis and characterization by IR spectroscopy. In addition, the paramagnetic properties of Sm(ii) and Eu(ii) hampered meaningful NMR spectroscopic investigations in the cases of 1-Sm, 1-Smdo, 1-Eu, and 1-Euthf. Complex 1-Ybdme decomposed in [D8]thf solution over time at ambient temperature. The decomposition was accompanied by the formation of toluene and methyl vinyl ether and the generation of an insoluble precipitate (most likely an Yb–OMe species), indicative of ether cleavage through an α-attack of dme (see the ESI†). It is noteworthy that all three compounds (1-Yb, 1-Ybthp and 1-Ybdme) feature nearly the same chemical shifts when measured in [D8]thf, indicating that both in 1-Ybthp and 1-Ybdme the coordinated donor molecules may be replaced by the stronger Lewis base thf. In all cases, the 1H NMR spectra recorded at ambient temperature do not show any coupling of the benzylic CH2 group to the NMR-active isotope 171Yb (I = 1/2, a = 14.3%), as for example observed in the heteroleptic Yb(ii) hydrocarbyl complexes [(BDI)Yb{CH(SiMe3)2}(thf)] (2JH–Yb = 28.9 Hz, BDI = {N(C6H3iPr2-2,6)C(Me)}2CH)35 or [(TptBu,Me)YbCH(SiMe3)2] (2JH–Yb = 28.0 Hz, TptBu,Me = tris(3-tBu-5-Me-pyrazolyl)borato).36

Next, we examined such easily accessible lanthanide(ii) benzyls [Ln(CH2Ph)2]n (1-Ln) as potential precursors for protonolysis reactions with amine derivatives. Initially, NMR-microscale reactions were performed by employing diamagnetic 1-Yb and one equiv. each of 2,6-diisopropylaniline (H2NDipp) and triphenylsilylamine (H2NSiPh3). The latter amines stand out due to their relatively low pKa values and considerable steric demand, both being important factors for a successful twofold protonolysis and formation of low-aggregated imide complexes. In addition, the results may be directly compared to the respective Ca(ii) chemistry, since for [Ca(CH2Ph)2]n the H2NDipp reaction in thf afforded only the mono-deprotonated amide species [Ca]–NHDipp, a second deprotonation was not observed, even after prolonged reaction times at elevated temperatures. With H2NSiPh3, however, twofold deprotonation was complete after 2.5 d at 40 °C and the tetrameric calcium imide [(thf)Ca(μ3-NSiPh3)]4 had formed.23

In sharp contrast, treatment of 1-Yb with H2NSiPh3 in various solvents and solvent mixtures did not allow for the isolation of an Yb(ii) imide species, but led to the formation of an ill-defined reaction mixture, also containing paramagnetic Yb(iii) species (see Fig. S20, ESI†). Surprisingly, and again in sharp contrast to the corresponding Ca(ii) chemistry (vide supra), an initial NMR-scale experiment pointed to a double protonolysis of 1-Yb with H2NDipp in [D8]thf. Slow formation of an Yb(ii) imide species was indicated by the vanishing NH resonances and the appearance of a new set of signals (cf. Fig. S21, ESI†). Comparing the in situ1H NMR spectra of the reaction of 1-Yb with 1 equiv. of H2NDipp with the spectra of the starting materials and also that of the in situ prepared homoleptic [Yb(NHDipp)2] did not assist in conclusively elucidating any intermediate(s) formed during the protonolysis. Despite a small shift of approximately 0.1 ppm for some resonances compared to 1-Yb or [Yb(NHDipp)2], the formation of both homoleptic and heteroleptic benzyl-amide moieties seems likely (cf. Fig. S22, ESI†). Upscaled reactions revealed that the best results with respect to the reaction time, yield, and purity of the formed imide species were obtained from the reactions performed in thp as a solvent. Analysis of dark red crystals grown from a thf solution by means of X-ray crystallography revealed that the product consists of the tetrameric Yb(ii) imide [(thf)Yb(μ3-NDipp)]4 (2-Yb, Scheme 2 and Fig. 3).

The compound which crystallises in the monoclinic space group Pc was additionally analysed via NMR and IR spectroscopic methods as well as via microanalysis. The solid-state structure of 2-Yb features a heterocubane core with almost perpendicular Yb–N–Yb and N–Yb–N angles of 90.2(2)°–94.2(2)° (average 91.5°) and 87.0(2)°–90.6(2)° (average 88.5°), respectively. The Yb–N distances for the μ3-bridging imido ligands in 2-Yb range from 2.352(4) Å to 2.430(5) Å and are comparable to the Yb–N distances found in the four-coordinate Yb(ii) β-diketiminate–amide complex [(BDI)Yb{NH(Dipp)}(thf)] (2.327(2) Å, terminal)35 and the five-coordinate dimeric ytterbium(ii) amide [Cp*Yb{N(SiMe3)2}]2 (μ2-bridging, 2.453(2) and 2.622(2) Å).37 The Yb–N distances also match the Ca–N contacts found in the closely related Ca(ii) imide [(thf)Ca(μ3-NSiPh3)]4 (av. Ca–N, 2.3472 Å).23 Relatively short contacts were also detected for Yb1⋯C12 (2.954(6) Å), Yb2⋯C36 (2.945(6) Å), and Yb4⋯C33 (3.090(7) Å) as well as for the four ipso carbon atoms (Yb–Cipso 2.956(5) Å to 3.188(5) Å). Encouraged by the feasibility of imide complex 2-Yb, we tackled the corresponding protonolysis of deployed 1-Sm and 1-Eu with H2NDipp under identical conditions. While for Eu(ii) the tetrameric imide [(thf)Eu(μ3-NDipp)]4 (2-Eu, Scheme 2, P21, Fig. 3) was obtained straightforwardly, in the case of Sm(ii) the targeted imide complex could not be isolated. Instead, a yellow tint of the reaction mixture was observed, indicating at least partial oxidation to Sm(iii). Despite the larger Eu2+ ions compared to Yb2+ (vide supra), complex 2-Eu is isostructural to 2-Yb and aside from the slightly elongated bond lengths the metrical parameters are very similar. The Eu–N distances were found in the range of 2.473(4)–2.519(5) Å with average Eu–N–Eu and N–Eu–N angles of 93.9° and 86.0°, respectively. Again additional interactions between the Eu centres and the CH3 groups of the iPr substituents (shortest distances: Eu2⋯C21 3.252(6) Å and Eu3–C35 3.247(6) Å) as well as with all four ipso carbons (Eu⋯Cipso 2.930(5) Å to 2.973(5) Å) are found. It should be mentioned that the heterocubane motif of the tetrameric metal–imide complexes 2-Yb and 2-Eu is also a known arrangement of Ln(iii) imide species [(C5Me4SiMe3)Ln(NR)]4 (Ln = Y: R = Et, CH2Ph; Ln = Lu, R = CH2Ph)38,39 and [(L)2Sm4(NR)4] (R = Ph, SiMe3; L = [Me2Si{NC(Ph)N(2,6-iPr2Ph)}2]2−),40,41 in which the neutral donor ligand thf formally is replaced by another mono-anionic ligand.
