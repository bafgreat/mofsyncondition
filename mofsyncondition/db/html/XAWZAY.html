Introduction

The crystal engineering of coordination polymers has not only gained great recognition as an important interface between synthetic chemistry and materials science but also provides a solid foundation to understand how molecules can be organized and functions can be achieved.1 The interesting point in these systems is justified by the rich diversity of structural motifs and applications, which include catalysis, sorption, photochemistry, magnetism and numerous other potential applications, with structures ranging from simple zero-dimensional oligomers to three-dimensional polymers.2–4 The crystallization is a complicated process and the formation of molecular motifs depends on the combination of several factors, such as the coordination geometry of metal ions, metal–ligand ratio, the structural characteristics of ligands, the solvent system, the counter anions, temperature, pH value, templates and so on. Among them, both the suitable choice of a versatile ligand and the selection of suitable spacer ligands are both important to control and adjust the topology of coordination frameworks.5,6

The ligands containing flexible backbones are among the most important types of organic ligands because their flexibility and conformational freedom allow for greater structural diversity. According to our previous report, V-shaped 2,3,2′,3′-thiaphthalic acid (2,3,2′,3′-tdpa) and 2,3,3′,4′-thiaphthalic acid (2,3,3′,4′-tdpa) are useful to construct infinite rod-shaped metal carboxylate SBUs or infinite metal–organic chains with or without helicity, and will provide effective routes to an intrinsic packing arrangement of rods in the crystal structure.7–9 On one hand, due to the fact that the tdpa ligand is a multidentate and flexible connecter, it is likely to form metal–organic chains or rod-shaped chain SBUs.10 On the other hand, as there are four carboxyl groups, tdpa may have various coordination modes, from which a rich variety of one-, two- and three-dimensional metal–organic polymeric architectures have been predicted.11 In previous studies, research on the coordination chemistry of the new ligand was focused on the coordination geometry of different metal ions and the influence of an auxiliary neutral organic motif. But the influence of neutral module factors had not been systemically investigated, such as whether the length and the functional group of the neutral nitrogenligands can afford a tremendous breadth of structural motifs, and control of the cavity size and the properties of the coordination polymers.12 As has been mentioned above, to further understand and prepare new materials with interesting structural topologies and excellent physical properties, we have recently engaged in the research of coordination polymers based on these different characteristics. Herein, we choose the combination of H4tdpa and transition metals (Cd and Zn), and focus on the effect of the neutral nitrogenligands and the central ions on mediating the conditions of crystallization and further influencing the superstructures of the crystal seeds under solvothermal synthesis conditions (Scheme 1). Utilizing this strategy, five new coordination polymers {[Cd2(tdpa)(H2O)3]·H2O}n (1), {[Cd2(tdpa)(bpe)(H2O)3]}n (2), {[Cd2(tdpa)(bix)]}n (3), {[Cd2(tdpa)(bpg)(H2O)4]·3H2O}n (4) and {[Zn4(tdpa)2(bpy)2(H2O)11]·4H2O}n (5) were obtained. Moreover, X-ray powder diffraction (XRPD), thermogravimetric analysis (TG), and photoluminescent behaviours of the five complexes have also been studied.

Experimental

Materials and general methods

The ligand 2,2′,3,3′-tdpa was prepared according to the literature procedure,13nitrogenligands bpg (meso-α,β-bi(4-pyridyl) glycol) and bix (1,4-bis((1H-imidazol-1-yl)methyl)benzene) were prepared according to the literature14 and all other starting materials were of analytical grade as obtained from commercial sources without further purification. Elemental analyses for C, H and N were performed on a Perkin-Elmer 240 elemental analyzer. The FT-IR spectra were recorded from KBr pellets in the range of 4000 to 400 cm−1 on a Bruker VECTOR 22 spectrometer. Thermal analysis was performed on a SDT 2960 thermal analyser from room temperature to 800 °C with a heating rate of 20 °C min−1 under nitrogen flow. Luminescence spectra for the solid samples were recorded on a Hitachi 850 fluorescence spectrophotometer. The Schläfli symbol is calculated by TOPOS software.15

Preparation of {[Cd2(tdpa)(H2O)3]·H2O}n (1)

A mixture of 2,2′,3,3′-tdpa (18.1g, 0.05 mmol), Cd(NO3)2·6H2O (30.8 g, 0.1 mmol), acetonitrile (2 ml) and KOH (11.2 g, 0.2 mmol) in distilled water (6 ml) was placed in a Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel, heated to 120 °C for 3 days, and then cooled to room temperature with a cooling rate of 5 °C h−1. Colourless block-like crystals of 1 were obtained in 84% yield based on cadmium. Anal. calc. for C16H14Cd2O12S (655.13): C, 29.33; H, 2.15; N, 3.92%. Found: C, 29.39; H, 2.11; N, 3.86%. IR/cm−1 (KBr): 3373(s), 1541(s), 1458(s), 1384(s), 859(m), 770(m), 732(m), 700(m).

Preparation of {[Cd2(tdpa)(bpe)(H2O)3]}n (2)

Then the reaction system was cooled to room temperature with a cooling rate of 5 °C h−1. Colourless block single crystals were collected (yield: 78% based on cadmium). Anal. calc. for C44H32Cd4N2O21S2 (1438.44): C, 36.74; H, 2.24; N, 1.95%. Found: C, 36.69; H, 2.27; N, 61.91%. X-Ray crystallography

Single-crystal X-ray analyses were conducted on a Bruker SMART APEX CCD diffractometer16 using graphite-monochromatized Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at room temperature using the ω-scan technique. Lorentz polarization and absorption corrections were applied. The structures were solved by direct methods with SHELXS-9717 and refined with the full-matrix least-squares technique using the SHELXL-9718 program. All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. The hydrogen atoms of the coordinated water molecules, hydroxide groups and ligands were included in the structure factor calculation at idealized positions by using a riding model and were refined isotropically. The hydrogen atoms of the solventwater molecules were located from the difference Fourier maps, and then restrained at fixed positions and refined isotropically. Analytical expressions of neutral atom scattering factors were employed, and anomalous dispersion corrections were incorporated. The crystallographic data and selected bonds lengths for 1–5 are listed in Tables 1 and 2.

Results and discussion

Crystal structures

[Cd2(tdpa)(H2O)3]·H2O (1)

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that complex 1 crystallizes in the P21/c space group and has a 2D structure. As illustrated in Fig. 1, there are two crystallographically non-equivalent Cd(ii) atoms, one 2,2′,3,3′-tdpa4− anion, three coordinated and one solventwater molecules in each asymmetric unit (Fig. 1a). Cd1 exhibits distorted pentagonal bipyramidal coordination geometry. It is surrounded by one water molecule and six carboxylate O atoms offered by two tdpa4− anions. Cd2 is six-coordinated by four carboxylate O atoms from three different tdpa4− anions and two water molecules, and displays distorted octahedral geometry. The dihedral angle between the two phenyl rings of tdpa4− is 48.4° and the bond angle ∠C3S1C9 is 101.1°.The four (2-, 2′-, 3- and 3′-COO−) carboxylate groups make dihedral angles of 87.4, 31.2, 77.6 and 70.7° with the corresponding linked benzene rings, respectively. The 2,2′,3,3′-tdpa4− anion adopts a trans-conformation with four carboxylate groups coordinating to seven Cd(ii) cations. The 3- and 2′-COO− groups of tdpa4− adopt monocoordination/bridge bidentate coordination modes, the 2-COO− groups adopt a chelate/bridge tridentate coordination mode and the 3′-COO− groups adopt monocoordinated/bridge tridentate coordination modes to connect Cd1 and Cd2 ions.

Based on these connection modes, four cadmium atoms (two Cd1 atoms and two Cd2 atoms) are connected by 2-, 3- and 2′-COO− groups (O7–O8, O5–O6 and O4–O3) from the tdpa4−ligand to form a tetranuclear cadmium SBU {Cd12Cd22C6O12} (Fig. 1b). Furthermore, there are two different kinds of connection role of tdpa4−. One kind of tdpa4−ligand connects to two adjacent tetranuclear cadmium SBUs to construct a rod-shaped ribbon {Cd12Cd22C6O10}n Cd-chain SBU (Fig. 1c and 1d). The other kind of tdpa4− ligand interlinks the rod-shaped ribbon Cd-chain SBUs resulting in packing layers (Fig. 1e), and then the layers are further linked by π⋯π and C–H⋯π interactions to form a 3D supramolecular structure and the solventwater molecules are fastened by hydrogen bonds in the channels between each two adjacent layers, as shown in Fig. S1†. A further simplified structure of compound 1 can be gained by reducing the multidimensional structure to simple rod geometries and associated nets.19 Considering the tdpa4− as three-connected nodes (connected to three Cd1 tetranuclear cadmium units), and the tetranuclear cadmium units as six-connected nodes (connected to six tdpa4−ligands), the structure of 1 can be described as a (3, 6)-connected network with a Schläfli symbol of (43)2(46·66·83) topology (Fig. 1f).

[Cd2(tdpa)(bpe)(H2O)3] (2)

When the bpe ligand acts in the role of a space extending bridging auxiliary linkage, compound 2 displays a 3D coordination network consisting of Cd–tdpa chains and bpe bridge ligands. As illustrated in Fig. 2a, the asymmetric unit of 2 contains two independent Cd(ii) ions, one 2,2′,3,3′-tdpa4− anion, one bpe molecule, and three coordinated water molecules. The Cd1 ion is in the capped-octahedral coordination geometry coordinated by N1 from the bridging bpe, O3, O4 and O2#1 from two different tdpa4− ions, and another three oxygen atoms O1W, O2W and O3W of coordinated water. The Cd2 ion adopts a pentagonal-bipyramidal coordination geometry coordinating to O3, O6, O7#1, O8#1, O2#2, O5#2 from three different tdpa4− ions. The donor ligand bpe acts as a two-connector to link two Cd atoms, and two coordinated pyridine groups of the bpe locate in trans positions. The 2,3,2′,3′-tdpa employs a twisted conformation with a bond angle around S1 of 99.4°, and the dihedral angle between the two phenyl rings is 80.2°. The dihedral angles between the 2-, 3-, 2′- and 3′-carboxylate groups and their corresponding phenyl rings are 79.6°, 32.6°, 81.7° and 23.2°, respectively. It is worth noting that the tdpa4−ligand adopts an octadentate coordination mode: the 2-COO− group adopts a chelate/bridge tridentate coordination mode to connect two Cd1 ions, the 2′-COO− group adopts a monocoordinated/bridge bidentate coordination mode and the 3-COO− groups adopts a chelate/bridge tridentate coordination mode to connect Cd1 and Cd2 ions, whereas the 3′-COO− group is bidentate to Cd2.

The binuclear cadmium units are formed by Cd1 and Cd2 atoms bridged by O6, O2, O3 and O4 of the 2-COO−, 2′-COO− and 3-COO− groups, such units are further associated by O5 of the 2-COO− groups as well as O7 and O8 of 3′-COO− to form infinite rod-shaped ribbon Cd-chain SBUs (Fig. 2b), and then the rod-shaped ribbon SBUs further connect four other rod-shaped ribbon Cd–tdpa chain SBUs by bpe ligands along four orientations to form a 3D network structure (Fig. 2c). To gain further insight into the structure of 2, it is necessary to simplify the ligands and the building blocks from the topological point of view. From the above description we can see that the connectivity pattern of the Cd1 and Cd2 atoms in the Cd-chain is repeated infinitely as {(Cd1–Cd2)(CO)5(H2O)2} to create rods along the c direction, so each binuclear cadmium unit can be abstracted as a five-connected node which links to three tdpa4−ligands and two binuclear cadmium units. Each tdpa4−ligand can be simplified as a three-connected node (connected to three binuclear cadmium units). Based on this simplification, the overall 3D networks of 2 can be described as a (3,5)-connected network with a Schläfli symbol of (2·4·5)(32·42·5·6·103·11) topology (Fig. 2d).

[Cd2(tdpa)(bix)] (3)

For comparison with compound 2, when bpe is replaced by a longer auxiliary ligand, bix, as an extending bridging linkage, compound 3 displays a 3D coordination network consisting of tetranuclear cadmium SBU chains and bix bridging ligands. As illustrated in Fig. 3a, the asymmetric unit of 3 contains two independent Cd(ii) ions, one 2,2′,3,3′-tdpa4− anion and one bix molecule. Cd1 adopts a distorted pentagonal-bipyramidal geometry and Cd2 adopts a capped-octahedral configuration. At first glance, Cd1 should take a distorted square-pyramidal geometry: three oxygen atoms from three different tdpa4−ligands and one nitrogen atom of bix, N1. However, the distance of 2.781 Å between Cd1 and O5#1 of 3′-COO− and the distance of 2.656 Å between Cd1 and O8 of 2′-COO− of the tdpa4− anion molecule suggest non-negligible interactions. Thus, the Cd1 center can be regarded as adopting pentagonal bipyramidal geometry. In contrast, the Cd2 ion is coordinated to O1, O2, O8, O#1, and O3#3 from 2-, 2′-, 3′-, and 3-COO− groups of three different tdpa4−ligands, and N4#4 from another bridging bix ligand. The donor ligand bix acts as bridging linkage to link two Cd atoms, the dihedral angles between the phenyl rings and imidazole are 31.8° and 49.8°, then two coordinated imidazole groups of bix locate in cis positions. As for the tdpa4−ligand, the dihedral angle between the two phenyl rings is 82.1° and the bond angle ∠C1S1C7 is 101.2°. The dihedral angles between the 2-, 3-, 2′- and 3′-carboxylate groups and their corresponding phenyl rings are 86.5°, 12.9°, 81.1° and 20.2°, respectively. It is worth noting that the tdpa4−ligand adopts an octadentate coordination mode: the 2- and 2′-COO− groups adopt a chelate/bridge tridentate coordination mode, the 3-COO− groups adopt a monocoordinated/bridge bidentate coordination mode and the 3′-COO− groups adopt a chelate bidentate coordination mode to bond Cd1 and Cd2 ions.

Due to the variety and flexibility of the tdpa4− anion, the quaternion cadmium unit [Cd12Cd22(CO2)6]n is set up, which is stabilized by the 2-COO−, 2′-COO− and 3′-COO− groups (O1–O2, O3 and O7–O8) (Fig. 3b). Then, quaternion cadmium units are further associated together through the tdpa4− anion to form infinite rod-shaped zigzag Cd-chain SBUs (Fig. 3c and 3d). Bridging neutral nitrogenligands connect two adjacent SBUs to form the 3D framework structure (Fig. 3e). To deepen the understanding of the structure, the structure can be simplified by reducing the multidimensional structure to simple rod geometries and associated nets. If the tdpa4−ligand is considered as a three-connected node (connected to three quaternion cadmium units [Cd12Cd22(CO2)6]n), and the quaternion cadmium unit [Cd12Cd22(CO2)6]n is taken as a ten-connected node (connected to four quaternion cadmium units [Cd12Cd22(CO2)6]n and six tdpa4− anions), the structure of 3 can be described as a (3,10)-connected network with a Schläfli symbol of (415·630)(43)2 topology (Fig. 3f).

[Cd2(tdpa)(bpg)(H2O)4]·3H2O (4)

Using a functionalized auxiliary ligand to construct coordination polymers may obtain beautiful architectures. The functionalized flexible ligandmeso-α,β-bi(4-pyridyl) glycol (bpg) would be a good choice to construct porous coordination polymers because of its novelty and flexibility, but previous reports on its complexes are rare.14 Unlike compounds 2 and 3, the auxiliary bpg ligand acts as not only an extending bridging linkage, but also an interlocking ligand in the 3D network to stabilize the structure (bpg is simplified as blue line in Fig. 4c). A single crystal XRD study has revealed that the 3D framework structure of 4 is built from the interlinking of Cd–tdpa chains (see Fig. 4b) and crystallizes in the Pna21 space group. The asymmetric unit contains two crystallographically non-equivalent Cd atoms (Cd1 and Cd2), one 2,2′,3,3′-tdpa4−, one bpg, and four coordinated and three solvated water molecules (Fig. 4a). Both Cd1 and Cd2 display pentagonal-bipyramidal geometry but Cd2 has a little deformation. The Cd1 center coordinates to O3, O4, O5#1 and O6#1 from the 3- and 2′-COO− groups of two different tdpa, N2 from bpg and O1W and O2W from two coordinated water molecules. The Cd2 center coordinates to O7, O8, O1#2 and O2#2 from the 3′- and 2-COO− groups of two different tdpa, N1#3 from another bpg and O3W and O4W from two coordinated water molecules. The donor ligand bpg also acts as a two-connected linkage which coordinates two Cd atoms, the two coordinated pyridine groups of the bpg locate in trans positions. As for 2,2′,3,3′-tdpa4−, the dihedral angle between the two phenyl rings is 83.3°, the 2- and 2′-carboxylate groups are almost perpendicular to the plane of the corresponding linking phenyl rings, with the dihedral angles between them being 88.5° and 83.9°, respectively. The 3- and 3′-carboxylate groups have 29.2° and 26.5° dihedral angles with the plane of the corresponding linking phenyl rings, respectively. The combination of the twisting and nonlinear flexibility environment around the S1 atom results in the formation of the metal–tdpa chains with the angle ∠C3S1C9 of 100.9°.

The 2-, 2′-, 3- and 3′-carboxylate groups all adopt a chelate bidentate coordination mode. Based on this coordination mode carboxylate groups associate to the adjoined Cd1 and Cd2 atoms to form Cd–tdpa chains. More specifically, unlike in the former three structures, the metal–organic chains in this structure are not called rod-shaped chain SBUs, in which the carboxylates lock each metal in an isolated and discontinuous environment. Four kinds of differently orientated tdpa exist in this chain (marked as S1–S4), then the Cd–tdpa chain further connects to four other Cd–tdpa chains through tdpa to form a 3D network structure (Fig. 4c). Viewed from the b axis, the 3D network can be described as a herringbone structural motif and the structure is strengthened by the interlocking bpg (two terminal nitrogen atoms coordinate to Cd1 and Cd2) between neighbouring Cd–tdpa (Fig. 4c). Viewed parallel to the bc plane, the channels between adjacent Cd–tdpa chains are occupied by remaining water molecules, which are associated by strong O–H⋯O hydrogen bonds. The hydrogen bond lengths (Å) and angles (°) are listed in Table 2 and the average distance of O⋯O is 2.827 Å. In addition, the hydrogen-bonds exist not only among the solventwater molecules but also among the oxygen atoms of carboxylate groups, bpg and coordinated water molecules (Fig. 4d). By the interaction of hydrogen bonds, the whole structure is interconnected more stably. A better understanding of this complicated structure can be achieved via topological considerations. If the tdpa4−ligand is considered as a four-connected node (connected to two Cd1 cations and two Cd2 cations), the Cd1 cation as a three-connected node (connected to two tdpa4− anions and one Cd2 cation), and the Cd2 cation as a three-connected node (connected to two tdpa4− and one Cd1 cation), the structure of 4 can be described as a (3,3,4)-connected network with a Schläfli symbol of (5·82)2(52·83·9)(52·8) topology (Fig. 4e).

[Zn4(tdpa)2(bpy)2(H2O)11]·4H2O (5)

In order to investigate the influence of the metal ion on the structure of the complexes, reactions of tdpa with Zn(NO3) in the presence of 4,4′-bpy were carried out, and 5 was obtained. Complex 5 crystallizes in a triclinic fashion with space group of P. As illustrated in Fig. 5a, the asymmetric unit of 5 contains four Zn(ii) atoms, two tdpa4− anions, two 4,4′-bipyridine (bpy) molecules, eleven coordinate water molecules and four solventwater molecules. The Zn1 is in the octahedral geometry coordinated by N4#1 from the bridging 4,4′-bipyridineligand, O3 from the 2-COO− groups of the tdpa4−ligand and O1W–O4W are coordinate waters. The Zn2 ion is in a distorted square-pyramid geometry (τ4 = 0.81)20 coordinated by N3 from the bridging 4,4′-bipyridineligand, O6 and O15#2 from the 2- and 3′-COO− groups of two different tdpa4− and one coordinate water O5W. The Zn3 ion is in a distorted trigonal-biyramid geometry (τ5 = 0.93)21 coordinated by O7 and O14#2 from the 2′- and 3′-COO− groups of two different tdpa4−ligands, N1 from the bridging 4,4′-bipyridineligand and two coordinate waters O6W and O7W. Zn4 is also in the octahedral geometry coordinated by N2 from the 4,4′-bipyridineligand, O11 from the 2-COO− groups of the tdpa4−ligand and O8W–O11W from coordinated waters. As for 2,2′,3,3′-tdpa4−, there are two different tdpa4− and the angles around S1 and S2 are ∠C6S1C7 = 103.5° and ∠C32S2C33 = 102.9°, respectively. The dihedral angles between the two phenyl rings are 85.5° and 75.6°. The 2- and 2′-carboxylate groups have a big torsion angle to the plane of the corresponding linking phenyl rings, with the dihedral angles between them being 85.7°, 84.5°, 83.3° and 53.6°. The 3- and 3′-carboxylate groups have 87.2°, 58.2°, 34.2° and 14.6° dihedral angles with the plane of the corresponding linking phenyl rings.

The 2-, 2′-, 3- and 3′-carboxylate groups all adopted a monodentate coordination mode. Two different tdpa4− are connected to four Zn ions and further linked by 4,4-bpy to construct Zn–tdpa chains. Viewing from the ac plane, the Zn–tdpa chains are connected by π⋯π interactions and S⋯π interactions to form the ac plane (Fig. 5c). Owing to the nonlinear flexibility of the tdpa4−ligand around the S atom and twisted carboxylate groups, the inter-chain π⋯π stacking interactions between benzene rings and pyridine rings (centroid⋯centroid A = 3.60 Å, B = 3.57 Å) and the S⋯π interactions between the divalent S atoms and pyridine rings (S⋯centroid 1 = 4.23 Å, 2 = 4.31 Å) from two neighbouring chains are obvious and intense. So the 2D layers are further connected by the inter-chain C–H⋯π interactions (C–H⋯centre A = 3.85 Å, B = 3.73 Å, C = 3.50 Å, D = 3.73 Å) along the b axis to form a 3D supramolecular structure, as shown in Fig. S2† and Fig. 5d. Furthermore, solventwater molecules occupy the channel of the adjacent chains by the O–H⋯O hydrogen bonds (Fig. S3†). The hydrogen bond lengths (Å) and angles (°) are listed in Table 2. The hydrogen-bonds exist not only among the solvent waters but also among the oxygen atoms of the carboxylate groups, solvent waters and coordinated waters. Adjacent chains are stabilized by the hydrogen bonding interaction.

The S⋯π interactions are worth noting. Thorough Cambridge Structural Database (CSD) research conducted by us indicates that S⋯π interactions involving heteroaromatic moieties occur widely in small molecules, to the best of our knowledge, such an interaction type has not yet been thoroughly discussed.22 The angle between the S⋯centroid axis and the ring plane is denoted by ϕ, and the C–S⋯centroid angle is represented by α. In the former context, values of r varying from 3.5 to 5.0 Å are reported to be statistically preferred to S⋯centroidarene separation and S⋯C sp2 van der Waals contact.23 The S1 and S2 atom points towards the pyridine ring centre at r = 4.232 Å and 4.316 Å, respectively. The C–S–C motifs are orientated almost perpendicular to the pyridine ring, with the C7–S1⋯centroid angle (α) 92.74° and the C6–S1⋯centroid angle (α′) 148.11°; C33–S2⋯centroid angle (α) 94.24° and the C32–S2⋯centroid angle (α′) 140.82°. ϕ angles are 62.8° and 50.6°, respectively. The distances from S1 to the closest ring edge atoms (d) are 3.808 (S1–C50) and 4.036 (S1–C48) Å; S2 to the closest ring edge atoms (d) are 3.612 (S3–C23) and 3.994 (S3–C22) Å, as show in Scheme 2.

IR spectral analyses

Strong absorption bands between 1500 and 1750 cm−1 in the IR spectra of complexes 1–5 can be assigned to the coordinated carboxylate groups. In complexes 1, 2, 4 and 5, the characteristic bands observed at 3200 to 3600 cm−1 in the IR spectra can be assigned to crystal water. The characteristic bands observed at around 3100 cm−1 are attributed to the C–H stretching vibration of the benzene ring. The thioether characteristic bands are measured at around 1390 cm−1 (vs).24 All this agrees well with the results of the X-ray single-crystal analyses.

Thermal properties and powder XRD studies

Thermal analyses for 1–5 were carried out from room temperature to 800 °C under a nitrogen atmosphere (see Fig. S4† for TGA curves). The TGA curve of 1 shows that the first weight loss of 8.41% that appeared between 30 and 175 °C corresponds to the loss of the coordinated water molecule (calculated, 8.2%), then the network was decomposed quickly at 324 °C, resulting in the residue. Compound 2 shows a first weight loss of 3.69% between 30 to 137 °C also corresponding to the loss of three lattice water molecules per formula unit (calculated, 3.75%), and the anhydrous component of compound 2 began to decompose at 202 °C. In the case of compound 3, no obvious weight loss was observed until the temperature reached 293 °C, indicating that this metal–organic framework material has high thermal stability. The TGA curve of 4 shows that a weight loss of 13.14% appeared between 25 and 304 °C corresponding to the loss of four coordinated water molecules and three solventwater molecules per formula unit (calculated, 13.61%), and the framework collapsed in the temperature range of 305–629 °C. As for compound 5, weight loss of coordinated water molecules and solventwater molecules took place in the range of 23–315 °C (observed, 8.23%; calculated, 8.7%), and the anhydrous component began to decompose at 321 °C.

Powder X-ray diffractions of 1–5 have been studied, shown in Fig. S5–S9†. All the peaks displayed in the measured patterns (b) are similar to those in the simulated patterns (a) generated from single-crystal diffraction data, indicating single phases of 1, 2, 3 and 5 are formed, the PXRD for 4 suggests that single phases of 4 are formed and it is the major component of the product, but it isn't absolutely phase pure which may be caused by an unknown impurity. The characteristic peaks of 1, 2 and 4 are still existent when the water molecules have evolved from the sample by heating the sample to 100 °C under vacuum, which indicates that the compounds are still crystalline after dehydration. The characteristic peaks of 5 began to disappear after dehydration by heating the sample to 100 °C in a high vacuum, which indicates that removal of the water molecules causes a significant structural change of crystalline to amorphous in the framework of 5. The structure of compound 5 could not be recovered when the samples were exposed to water for one day.

Photoluminescence properties

The photoluminescence properties of the compounds were investigated. While the free H4tdpa ligand displays weak luminescence (λex = 370 nm, λem = 480 nm) in the solid state at room temperature, the compounds exhibits distinct radiation emission maxima. Excitation at 369 nm leads to a single peak at 468 nm for 1. Compound 2 shows a broad band with a single peak at 481 nm upon excitation at 391 nm. Compound 3 shows a single peak at 429 nm upon excitation at 321 nm. Compound 4 displays a strong single peak at 481 nm upon excitation at 371 nm. Compound 5 shows a broad band with a single peak at 450 nm upon excitation at 375 nm (Fig. 6). The peaks at 429 and 481 nm of compounds 1 and 5 are probably due to π–π* transitions of the anion ligands because similar peaks also appear for the free H4tdpa. The enhancement of luminescence may be attributed to ligand chelation of the metal centre, which effectively increases the rigidity of the ligand and reduces the loss of energy by radiationless decay.25 Hypochromatic shifts of emissions occur in 1, 3, 4 and 5 which are probably due to the differences of the coordination environment around the Cd and Zn ions since photoluminescence behavior is closely associated with the metal ions and the ligands coordinated around them.26 In addition, the strong luminescence of 1 and 4 may be ascribed to the presence of metal clusters, since the μ3–COO−ligand may tighten the whole skeleton, resulting in much weaker vibrations.27

Discussion

According to the above structural description of the five coordination polymers, there are four different coordination modes of the 2,3,2′,3′-tdpa4−ligand. In 1, the tdpa4− anion with a trans-conformation adopts a μ7-bridging mode with four carboxylate groups to coordinate seven Cd atoms with μ7: η2η2η2η3 coordination modes (Scheme 3a), while in the case of 2, tdpa4− adopts a μ5-bridging mode with four carboxylate groups to coordinate to five Cd atoms (μ5: η3η2η2η1) as show in Scheme 3b. The tdpa4−ligand in 3 also adopts a μ5-bridging fashion with four carboxylate groups to coordinate to five Cd atoms with μ5: η1η2η2η2 coordination modes (Scheme 3c), whereas in 4, the tdpa4−ligand adopts a μ4-bridging fashion with each of the four carboxylate groups in the same chelate modes (μ4: η1η1η1η1) (Scheme 3d). Unlike the above four structures, tdpa4− in 5 adopts two kinds of μ3-bridging fashion, one with the three carboxylate groups in the same monodentate mode (μ3: η1η1η1) as show in Scheme 3f, the other, with three carboxylate groups coordinated, adopts two kinds of modes (monodentate and chelate modes) to coordinate to three Cd atoms with μ3: η1η1η1 coordination modes (Scheme 3e).

The rigidity of V-shaped multicarboxylate ligands is known to be essential for the construction of polymeric structures, similar to 2,2′,3,3′-oxydiphthalic acid (2,2′,3,3′-H4odpa) and 1,1′-biphenyl-2,3,3′,4′-tetracarboxylic acid (H4bptc) ligands. According to a former report, the tdpa ligand behaved much like the odpa ligand in the way that the nonlinear flexibility around the etheric oxygen or sulfur atom and the twisted conformation of the carboxylate groups make it easy to form infinite rod-shaped metal carboxylate SBUs with or without a helix. But in this work, tdpa showed dramatic distinction against the prior research: three of the nickel–tdpa coordination polymers which had been reported contained helical rod-shaped metal carboxylate SBUs structures,10 whereas all the five tdpa complexes have no helix in this work, which proved that the central ions are the crucial factor in the formation of the architectures; on the other hand, neutral nitrogenligands also play a key role in constructing structures. Three structures were obtained from tdpa, 4,4′-bpy and M (Zn and Cd), reported in 2007, which contain helical structures,11 which is inconsistent to the five tdpa complexes in current work.

Results also imply that the secondary neutral nitrogenligands play an important role in determining the structural diversity in MOFs: length, from short to long, bpy, bpg, bpe and bix have different 3D frameworks. In complexes 2 and 3, Cd atoms are bridged by tdpa4− anion ligands to form infinite Cd–tdpa chains, and then the nitrogenligands act in a bridging role. However, in complex 4, Cd atoms are bridged by tdpa4− anion ligands to form a 3D framework, and bpg ligands only filled in the cavity to stabilize the framework. Unlike in the formerly reported Zn–tdpa coordination polymer11 that had a lamellar structure and 4-bpy played the role of a connector to link adjacent Zn–tdpa chains. In complex 5, it shows endless chain structure and the 4-bpy only lies in the Zn–tdpa chains to help construct organic–metal chains. It indicates that changing the metal–ligand ratio can decide the structure's spatial degree.

Conclusions

In this paper, a series of five MOFs constructed from 2,2′,3,3′-tdpa have been hydrothermally synthesized and structurally characterized. The different structures of compounds 1–5 indicate that auxiliary N-donor ligands can mediate structure variability in 3D frameworks. The results also reveal that small adjustments of the central ions and so on may result in large differences of the assembly of metal–organic networks. Their structural variability should originate from the intrinsic characteristics of transition metal ions as well as from the organic ligands with adaptable existing forms, coordination fashions, and conformations.

In conclusion, the 2,2′,3,3′-tdpa ligand could be used as a versatile ligand to construct novel MOFs by the appropriate choice of N-donor ligands and metal-centers. Accordingly, our present findings will enrich crystal engineering strategies and offer the possibility for controlled preparation of such desired network-based solids. Further studies are ongoing to construct more novel inorganic–organic hybrid materials using various N-donor ligands and transition metal ions.
