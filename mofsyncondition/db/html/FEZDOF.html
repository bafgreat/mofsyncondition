Over the past few decades, intensive efforts have been directed at the development of crystalline porous materials for applications in, for example, gas storage, separation, and heterogeneous catalysis.1 In the area of porous materials chemistry, it has been a challenge to synthesize frameworks consisting of redox active building units. Charge transfer between the host framework and guests via the redox process is interesting for the chemical sorption of guest molecules, electrochemical catalysis, and ion insertion. A useful type of compound for the design of porous architectures with redox activity is porous coordination polymers (PCPs) or metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), which are constructed via coordination bonds.2 As PCPs/MOFs consist of metal ions and organic ligands, we can employ redox active building units at either the metal site or the ligand site, or even both. However, the systematic synthesis of redox active frameworks is still limited,3 and more research into the rational synthesis of frameworks with reversible redox activity is required.

The Fe2+ ion is a candidate for inclusion in PCPs because of its low redox potential and rich coordination geometries. There are only a few reports on Fe-based coordination frameworks with permanent porosity compared with those with other first-row transition metals, and even fewer reports on their redox properties.4 The Fe2+ ion exhibits a tendency to undergo hydrolysis to afford hydrated iron oxides, making it difficult to obtain a crystalline coordination network in pure phase. Moreover, the difficulty of the coexistence of reversible redox activity and permanent porosity is acknowledged.

We developed a series of PCPs/MOFs possessing a flexible structure of 2-D layers constructed from electrically innocent divalent metal cations and dicarboxylates, and N-donor ligands.5–8 In this work, we synthesized a series of four Fe2+ open frameworks {[Fe(isophthalate)(bpy)]⊃DMF}n (FeCID-1⊃DMF) where bpy = 4,4′-bipyridyl and CID = coordination polymer with an interdigitated structure, {[Fe(3,5-pyridinedicarboxylate)(bpy)]⊃DMF}n (FeCID-2⊃DMF), {[Fe(5-NO2-isophthalate)(bpy)]⊃DMF}n (FeCID-5⊃DMF), and {[Fe(5-MeO-isophthalate)(bpy)]⊃DMF}n (FeCID-6⊃DMF) with permanent porosity and structural flexibility. We investigated the reversible redox property of Fe2+ ions and the resulting spontaneous reduction of guest molecules in the channels with dynamic structure rearrangement.

Crystal structures of these compounds are shown in Fig. 1. The Fe ions are coordinated to four oxygen atoms of a carboxylate and two nitrogen atoms of a bpyligand to form an octahedral coordination environment. Eight-member rings are formed with an Fe dimer, as shown in Fig. 1A, and the ligands are linked to have infinite 2-D layers. All compounds possess an interdigitated structure of 2-D layers, and the accommodated DMF molecules are not observed because of their heavy disorder. Because of differences in substituent groups of the dicarboxylates and structure flexibility, each compound has a different void volume, as calculated by PLATON software (17.1% for FeCID-1⊃DMF, 15.0% for FeCID-2⊃DMF, 19.8% for FeCID-5⊃DMF and 16.3% for FeCID-6⊃DMF).9 We have previously reported that the Zn2+-based isostructures and crystal structures of FeCID and ZnCID are almost identical.5,7

To evaluate the structural flexibility, the crystal structures of FeCID-1 with/without guests were determined (Fig. 1B and C). The porous structure is contracted compared with the as-synthesized phase (FeCID-1⊃DMF). As the guest-accessible void volume of FeCID-1⊃DMF (17.1%) changes to 11.5% for FeCID-1, the FeCID-1 has structural flexibility. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) studies on FeCID-2, -5 and -6 before and after DMF accommodation suggest that the FeCID-5 also has structural flexibility, whereas FeCID-6 has almost no flexibility, and the porous structure does not change even after guest evacuation. FeCID-2 shows a similar PXRD pattern change to that of FeCID-1. The distinct flexibility of FeCID-5 and FeCID-6 is also observed in the case of Zn2+ compounds, as has been reported previously.7 The structural flexibility is dependent on several factors, such as the effect of the substituent group of the dicarboxylate and the overall packing system of 2-D layers.

To investigate the valence state of Fe ions in the frameworks, we chose FeCID-1 and measured the 57Fe Mössbauer spectrum under vacuum (Fig. 2A). It exhibits a doublet signal at 290 K, which is characteristic of high-spin Fe2+, with an isomer shift of 1.06 mm s−1 and a quadrupole splitting of 2.75 mm s−1. As a result, an S = 2 high-spin ground state Fe2+ is dominant at 290 K. The sample gradually oxidized upon exposure to air, from Fe2+ to Fe3+. This air-oxidation behaviour was also confirmed by the Mössbauer spectrum. The pure Fe2+ state of FeCID-1 remains intact in an inert (Ar) atmosphere.

To characterize their gas adsorption properties, adsorption isotherms were determined, as shown in Fig. 3. For CO2adsorption at 195 K, FeCID-1, FeCID-2 and FeCID-6 show similar Type-I isotherms, which is a typical profile for robust microporous adsorbents.10 Total uptakes of CO2 for FeCID-1 and FeCID-6 are 62 mL g−1, and 50 mL g−1 for FeCID-2. On the other hand, the adsorption isotherm of FeCID-5 has a “gate-opening”-type profile at very low pressure (Fig. 3, inset).6,11 The total CO2adsorption is 98 mL g−1. The gate-opening behaviour is observed for compounds showing structural transformation from non-porous phase to porous phase.7,12 The gas adsorption study indicates that the four compounds possess permanent porosity and distinct structural flexibility. We should note that these compounds did not show N2adsorption at 77 K. The negligible adsorption uptakes are because of the low kinetics of N2 gas into these channels.

We then investigated the specific accommodation property of iodine (I2). I2 is known as an electron acceptor. Its kinetic diameter is 5.2 Å, which is comparable to the cavity size of these frameworks (5 × 6 Å2).13 I2adsorption on porous materials is also of interest because of the capture and storage of gaseous and volatile radioactive nuclides.14–17 After direct exposure of powdered samples of FeCID-1 to I2 vapour at 293 K, we observed clear colour changes for all samples, which suggested that I2 insertion into the micropores had taken place. These samples were then evacuated at room temperature to remove I2 attached on the crystal surfaces.

For further characterization, we studied FeCID-1 after the introduction of iodine (FeCID-1⊃I). The TGA profile of FeCID-1⊃I shows a two-step weight decrease; a total of 45 wt% of iodine is released in the temperature range of 373–473 K. The adsorbed amount corresponds to five iodine atoms per Fe2+ dimer.5 Even though the PXRD of FeCID-1⊃I shows a low-intensity pattern because of the heavy disordering of iodine atoms in the cavities, after evacuation of FeCID-1⊃I at 150 °C, a similar PXRD powder pattern to that of FeCID-1 was obtained. The iodine insertion therefore does not affect the crystallinity of the porous structure.

Of importance is that we could not observe any adsorption property of I2 for isostructural frameworks that included Zn2+, Mn2+ or Ni2+ with the same ligand system, as determined by TGA and PXRD. The iodine accommodation by only an Fe2+ compound is presumably because of the electron-donating property of Fe2+ frameworks. To characterize the valence state of FeCID-1⊃I, the 57Fe Mössbauer spectrum at 290 K was measured (Fig. 2B) and compared with that of FeCID-1. A new doublet peak appears and peak simulation indicates the existence of both Fe2+ and Fe3+ at a molar ratio of 1 : 1. The change in the spectrum suggests the charge transfer from the Fe2+ framework to iodine, and half of the Fe ions in the framework are oxidized to Fe3+. Consequently, dimers with both Fe2+ and Fe3+ would be formed.

To determine the structure of iodine in FeCID-1⊃I, Raman spectra were recorded (Fig. 4). Peaks at 105, 147 and 160 cm−1 in FeCID-1⊃I are assigned to the adsorbed iodine. These wave numbers confirmed that the iodine inside is predominantly of the pentaiodide (I5−) form.18 The two-stepwise release of iodine in the TGA profile is due to the stepwise desorption as the I2 form. This is accompanied by charge transfer from the anionic iodine species to the framework, as the reduction of Fe3+ to Fe2+. Note that iodine in electrically innocent PCPs/MOFs is incorporated as neutral I2.15,16,19 In the present system, even though all Fe2+ ions are octahedrally coordinated, the guest-accessible void spaces in the 2-D layers provide the charge-transfer environment. To investigate the reversibility of the redox property in FeCID-1 via iodine insertion, we evacuated FeCID-1⊃I at 150 °C for 6 hours to remove the adsorbed iodine and then measured the Mössbauer spectra again at room temperature. The observed spectra appear the same as those of the initial state of FeCID-1 with Fe2+ ions, but now a very small shoulder at around 0.6 mm s−1, which is attributed to a small fraction of Fe3+, is also observed. This is due to incomplete iodine release or an aerial oxidation during the sample handling. The results of this study suggest that almost all Fe3+ is reduced to Fe2+ as iodine is released, and prove its redox reversibility.

To obtain direct evidence of the mode of iodine accommodation in the flexible framework, we elucidated the crystal structure of FeCID-1⊃I from a single crystal (Fig. 5). We recorded a relatively high R value (R = 0.15), and we could not obtain details about the bond distances and angles; nonetheless, the observed structure still provided fruitful information. In FeCID-1⊃I, the planes of the dicarboxylates in the 2-D layers become parallel along the c-axis compared with that in FeCID-1⊃DMF (Fig. 1B) and the guest-accessible void volume largely changes from 11.5% (FeCID-1) to 29.3% (FeCID-1⊃I), where the void volume was calculated by the use of the host framework after the removal of inserted iodine. This means that the pore volume becomes 2.5 times larger after iodine insertion. Consequently, disordered iodine alignments are observed in the 1-D bottleneck-type channels. The crystal structure also suggests the formation of V-shaped I5− in a disordered fashion.20 There is one report on the spontaneous reduction of iodine in PCPs/MOFs with a redox system,21 but it does not show permanent porosity for gas adsorption. FeCID-1 has multifunctions—the property of reversible redox and a flexible open structure in crystalline phases.

Iodine assembly in the restricted spaces in solids is of interest because of the resulting unique electrical conductivity behaviour.15,17,22 Accordingly, we studied the electronic conductivity of FeCID-1⊃I. Impedance spectra of FeCID-1 and FeCID-1⊃I were recorded from 25 to 100 °C. FeCID-1 shows negligible conductivity (<10−10 S cm−1). On the other hand, FeCID-1⊃I shows a clear impedance response, which can be resolved into two components (bulk and grain boundary). The bulk conductivity is 1.9 × 10−6 S cm−1 at 25 °C and it increases to 5.5 × 10−5 S cm−1 at 100 °C. Because the Nyquist plot converged on a finite real number in the low frequency region (100–1 Hz), this is not an ionic conduction, but an electronic conduction. We also prepared FeCID-1⊃0.3I, which contained half the amount of iodine, and measured the conductivity. The observed conductivities are 102 times lower than those of FeCID-1⊃I in the temperature range studied. This result suggests that the iodine ions in the pores are involved in the electron conduction path.

Conclusions

In summary, we synthesized four Fe2+-based interdigitated 2-D PCPs under an Ar atmosphere. We were able to effectively construct Fe2+-based open frameworks under air-free conditions. The compounds thus obtained were stable, with porous functionality and structural flexibility. One of the frameworks promoted the reduction of iodine by accommodation, via the reversible redox property, attributed to the Fe2+ and Fe3+ species, and the obtained composite exhibited electric conductivity. We propose that the systematic synthesis of electrically and structurally flexible Fe-based PCPs/MOFs under an inert atmosphere will enable the fine-tuning of structure and redox behaviour, and contribute to the achievement of advanced porous functionality.
