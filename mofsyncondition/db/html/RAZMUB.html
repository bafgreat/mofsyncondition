Coordination polymers incorporating copper(II) and manganese(II) centers bridged by pyridinedicarboxylate ligands: Structure and magnetism

Author links open overlay panelBrian O.Patrick
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.poly.2005.03.085Get rights and content

Abstract

Two heterobimetallic coordination polymers, [Cu(2,4-pydc)2Mn(H2O)4]x (1) and [Cu(2,5-pydc)2Mn(H2O)2]x · 4xH2O (2), have been synthesized and structurally characterized by single crystal X-ray diffraction. Both compounds have extended 2-D sheet structures. In 1 the copper centers are linked in chains by double ligand bridges and these chains are cross-linked through the manganese coordination spheres and O–C–O bridges to form polymeric sheets. In 2 separate O–C–O bridged Cu and Mn chains are connected in an alternating array by additional ligand bridging to generate the overall 2-D structure. Analysis of magnetic data of 1 reveals that ferromagnetic exchange between the O–C–O bridged copper and manganese centers dominates the magnetic properties of this system. The magnetic data for 2 fit well to a model incorporating antiferromagnetic exchange in independent S = 1/2 and S = 5/2 linear chains with J(Cu) = −0.073 cm−1 and J(Mn) = −0.32 cm−1. Unlike the situation in 1, there is no evidence for heterometallic exchange. In both 1 and 2 the significant exchange occurs via O–C–O bridges. To study the effect of thermal dehydration on the magnetic properties of these systems, the compounds Cu(2,4-pydc)2Mn · H2O (1d) and Cu(2,5-pydc)2Mn · H2O (2d) were synthesized and studied.

1. Introduction

The use of unsymmetrical bridging ligands in the design of novel coordination polymers with potential exploitable properties in the areas of magnetism, catalysis, separation, non-linear optics and molecular sensing is very much a topic of current interest [1]. Azine carboxylate ligands with a carboxyl group in a 2-position next to a ring nitrogen are known to readily form stable, five-member, N–C–C–O–M metal–chelate rings [2]. In the case of the dianions of the 2,n-pyridinedicarboxylic acids (2,n-pydc2−, where n = 3, 4 or 5) there exists the possibility of these unsymmetrical ligands forming complexes in which the ring nitrogen and a 2-carboxyl oxygen form a chelate interaction with one metal while the other 2-carboxyl oxygen or one or more oxygens of the n-carboxyl group bridge to a different kind of metal thus forming novel heterometallic polymeric systems. This structural motif is realized in the two coordination polymers reported here: [Cu(2,4-pydc)2Mn(H2O)4]x (1), and [Cu(2,5-pydc)2Mn(H2O)2]x · 4xH2O (2). In both of these systems, not surprisingly, the chelate rings incorporate the softer of the two metals, copper, while the harder metal, manganese, is encased in an oxygen-only ligand environment. In an earlier communication, we reported the 2,3-pyridinedicarboxylate analogue of 1 and 2, namely [Cu(2,3-pydc)2]x[Mn(H2O)6]x · 2xH2O (3) [3]. Unlike the situation in 1 and 2, in 3 there is no coordinative link between the copper and manganese centers. While the copper ions are bridged in chains by the ligands in 3, the manganese ions are present in the crystal lattice as isolated Mn(H2O)62+ entities in this compound [3]. The structures of both 1 and 2, reported here in detail, reveal extended arrays of regularly alternating, coordinatively linked, paramagnetic centers of different spin (S = 1/2 and 5/2), a classical set-up for ferrimagnetism [4]. With this in mind in particular, we carefully examined the magnetic properties of these systems as well as the magnetic properties of two thermally condensed forms, Cu(2,4-pydc)2Mn · H2O (1d) and Cu(2,5-pydc)2Mn · H2O (2d).

2. Experimental

2.1. Syntheses

2,4-Pyridinedicarboxylic acid monohydrate and 2,5-pyridinedicarboxylic acid were used as supplied (Aldrich). Solutions of 0.10 M Cu(NO3)2 · 3H2O (Baker) and 0.10 M Mn(ClO4)2 · 6H2O (Alfa) were prepared in-house. 0.10 M NaOH (Fisher) was diluted according to package directions. Water was deionized in-house.

[Cu(2,4-pydc)2Mn(H2O)4]x (1) was prepared by dissolving 2,4-pydcH2 · H2O (1.0 mmol, 0.185 g) in H2O and NaOH (2.0 mmol, 20 ml × 0.10 M) to a final volume of 100 ml. Cu(NO3)2 and Mn(ClO4)2 (each 0.5 mmol, 5.0 ml × 0.10 M) were combined and then added to the proligand solution. The reaction mixture turned bright blue. After sitting for several weeks at room temperature, the mixture was filtered to recover small blue crystals suitable for single-crystal X-ray studies. Anal. Calc. for CuMnC14N2H8O9: C, 36.0; H, 1.7; N, 6.0. Found: C, 35.8; H, 1.9; N, 5.9%.

[Cu(2,5-pydc)2Mn(H2O)2]x · 4xH2O (2) was prepared as above substituting 1.0 mmol (0.167g) 2,5-pydcH2 in place of the 1.0 mmol 2,4-pydcH2 · H2O. In this case small blue crystals suitable for single-crystal X-ray studies formed after only a few days and these were removed by filtration. Anal. Calc. for CuMnC14H6N2O8 · H2O: C, 36.0; H, 1.7; N, 6.0. Found: C, 35.7; H, 1.6; N, 6.0%.

Cu(2,4-pydc)2Mn · H2O (1d) was prepared by heating a finely ground sample of 1 to 190 °C under dynamic vacuum for seven days to yield a dark blue powder. Anal. Calc. for CuMnC14N2H8O9: C, 36.0; H, 1.7; N, 6.0. Found: C, 35.8; H, 1.9; N, 5.9%.

Cu(2,5-pydc)2Mn · H2O (2d) was prepared as above from a finely ground sample of 2. Compound 2d is also a dark blue powder. Anal. Calc. for CuMnC14H6N2O8 · H2O: C, 36.0; H, 1.7; N, 6.0. Found: C, 35.7; H, 1.6; N, 6.0%.

2.2. Physical measurements

All elemental analyses were performed on finely powdered samples. TGA data were recorded on a TA 51 thermogravimetric analyzer at a 10 °C/min ramp under a 100 cc/min stream of nitrogen. Magnetic susceptibilities were measured using a Quantum Design (MPMS) SQUID magnetometer. The sample holder and details regarding the use of the equipment have been described elsewhere [5]. Magnetic susceptibilities were corrected for background signal of the sample holder. Corrections for the diamagnetism of all atoms [6] came to −221 × 10−6 for 1, −185 × 10−6 for 1d, −245 × 10−6 for 2, and −185 × 10−6 for 2d. All magnetic measurements were done on finely powdered samples, and the data are reported here on a per mole copper basis. X-ray studies were performed on single crystals mounted on glass fibers. All measurements were made on a Rigaku/ASDC CCD area detector with graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation. The positions of hydrogen atoms bonded to carbon were calculated, while those bonded to oxygen were located by electron density.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. [Cu(2,4-pydc)2Mn(H2O)4]x (1)

Compound 1 was prepared by a one-step synthesis similar to that developed for 3 [3] and recovered as royal blue crystals suitable for single-crystal X-ray studies. TGA studies show 1 to be thermally robust, with no loss of mass until 205 °C, at which temperature a mass equivalent to four water molecules is lost (calc. 14%, obs. 15%). No further loss of mass is observed until 346 °C, at which temperature the compound decomposes.

Structurally, single-crystal X-ray studies show that 1 is a sheet polymer (see Fig. 1 for repeat unit, Fig. 2 for a portion of the sheet, Table 1 for crystallographic data and Table 2 for selected bond lengths and angles). As in the previously reported structure of 3 [3] the copper(II) centers in 1 are linked into chains. Each copper(II) center in 1 has a tetragonally distorted pseudo-octahedral coordination sphere with the equatorial plane occupied by two rotationally equivalent 2,4-pydc2− moieties binding via the ring nitrogen and one 2-carboxyl oxygen, with respective bond lengths of 1.967(2) and 1.936(1) Å (as compared to the Cu–N and Cu–O1 bond lengths of 1.942(2) and 1.962(1) Å, respectively, for 3). The axial positions are occupied by long bonds (2.664(2) Å, compared to 2.693(1) Å for 3) to one oxygen of the 4-carboxyl group on adjacent repeat units, resulting in a double bridge linking the copper centers into a chain similar to that observed in the structure of 3 (Fig. 2). The 4-carboxyl in 1 twists at an angle of 41.8(1)° to the ring to facilitate this bond; in 3 the 3-carboxyl twists at an angle of 77.3(2)°. As in 3, the copper(II) chain structure of 1 is further stabilized by a π–π stacking interaction between the pyridine rings forming the double bridge (Table 3). Unlike 3, however, the copper(II) chains of 1 are linked via covalent bonds into polymetallic sheets through the manganese(II) coordination spheres (Fig. 2), in a structure similar to that of the previously reported compound [Cu(2,3-pydc)2Na2(H2O)8]x, where copper(II) chains are linked into polymetallic sheets via Na2(H2O)82+ ions [3]. Each manganese(II) center in 1 is ligated by four water molecules in two rotationally equivalent pairs, with the remaining two positions of the pseudo-octahedral coordination sphere filled by bonds to the second 2-carboxyl oxygen atoms of 2,4-pydc2− moieties in adjacent polymetallic chains. The Mn–O distances (2.158(2) and 2.197(2) Å for Mn–O5 and Mn–O6, 2.174(1) Å for Mn–O2) all fall within the range of Mn–O distances observed in the Mn(H2O)62+ ion in 3 [3]. This connectivity means that 1 is a sheet polymer consisting of copper(II) chains doubly bridged by 2,4-pydc2− ligands in one direction, and chains of alternating copper(II) and manganese(II) bridged by carboxyl groups in the second dimension. The sheets are stabilized by both π–π stacking interactions and hydrogen bonding between the water hydrogens and nearby carboxyl oxygens (Table 4). Additional hydrogen bonds between adjacent sheets (Table 5) further stabilize the extended structure.

DC magnetic susceptibility measurements for 1 were made from 300 to 2 K at an applied field of 10 000 G and from 100 to 2 K at 500 G. The observed increase in moment on decreasing temperature (Fig. 3) indicates ferromagnetic exchange. The data obtained at 10 000 G show the moment to pass through a maximum at 4 K, decreasing in value at lower temperatures. That this effect is likely the result of magnetic saturation at high applied fields is indicated by its absence in the lower field data, and also by the results shown in Fig. 4. Plots of magnetization versus applied field are shown in Fig. 4 for four different temperatures. These data clearly show the magnetization to saturate at high fields and low temperatures. That there is no long-range magnetic ordering in this system above 2K was indicated by AC susceptibility data taken from 50 to 1.8 K at an applied AC field of 1.0 G and frequencies of 10, 100, and 1000 Hz. This study showed no maxima in either χ′ or χ″. This result was confirmed by identical zero-field cooled and field-cooled magnetization plots measured from 25 to 2 K and by the absence of magnetic hysteresis when the magnetization was measured at 1.8 K by sweeping the field between +2000 and −2000 G. The magnetization plots of Fig. 4 reveal that at 2.5 K the highest magnetization measured is 35 920 cm3 G mol−1 at an applied field of 55 000 G. This value compares favorably with the saturation magnetization value of 34 070 cm3 G mol−1 calculated [7] for a ferromagnetically coupled S = 1/2 + S = 5/2 system (assuming g = 2.2 and 2.0, respectively). Ferrimagnetic coupling is clearly ruled out by the fact that the calculated saturation magnetization for a S = 5/2 − S = 1/2 system is 21 780 cm3 G mol−1, significantly below the measured magnetization value.

It is somewhat unexpected that 1 proved to exhibit ferromagnetic behavior. The herringbone-type copper(II) chains seen in 1 were previously reported [3] to be weakly antiferromagnetic, suggesting that the Cu–Cu interaction is not dominating the magnetic behavior of 1, instead that it is Cu–Mn coupling that dominates. Anti-parallel coupling between the S = 1/2 and the S = 5/2 centers would have generated the anticipated ferrimagnetic behavior for this system, whereas the observed coupling is clearly ferromagnetic. Adding to the unexpected nature of these results is the fact that earlier studies by others on similar O–C–O bridged Cu/Mn systems have shown negligibly small interactions [8].

Heating a bulk sample of 1 at 190 °C under dynamic vacuum produces Cu(2,4-pydc)2Mn · H2O (1d). DC magnetic susceptibility measurements were made on 1d in an applied field of 10 000 G. The magnetic properties of 1d are in sharp contrast to those of the parent compound 1. The magnetic moment of 1d decreases as T decreases, indicating antiferromagnetic behavior (Fig. 5). Attempts to model the data assuming a linear chain antiferromagnetic model for copper(II) coupled with a zero field splitting model for manganese(II) failed to produce an acceptable fit. It is likely that more complex exchange pathways are extant in this system. It seems reasonable to assume that the structure of 1d retains the copper(II) chains observed in 1, and that the changes to the structure are chiefly in the manganese(II) chromophore, which loses three ligating water molecules. An examination of the hydrogen-bonding interactions of the water molecules ligating manganese(II) in 1 shows that the 4-carboxyl oxygens on adjacent sheets are relatively near the manganese(II) ion, and suggests that the coordination sphere of manganese(II) in 1d is filled by bonds to these atoms, resulting in O–C–O bridges between manganese(II) and copper(II) ions on adjacent polymetallic sheets. This adds another dimension to the connectivity in this “condensed form” of 1, a connectivity which leads to overall antiferromagnetic behavior. Further speculation on the nature of the magnetic coupling in 1d is not warranted in the absence of a definitive X-ray determined structure.

3.2. [Cu(2,5-pydc)2Mn(H2O)2]x · 4xH2O (2)

A one-pot synthetic approach, similar to that used to prepare 1, gave compound 2 as dark blue crystals suitable for single-crystal X-ray studies. Thermogravimetric studies show 2 to be moderately thermally robust; it is stable up to 90 °C, at which temperature it loses a mass equivalent to two water molecules (calc. 6%, obs. 7%). A mass equivalent to the remaining four water molecules (calc. 13%, obs. 13%) is lost at 175 °C, and the dehydrated compound is then stable to 340 °C, at which temperature it decomposes. Heating a portion of 2 in a drying pistol at 190 °C yields the stable compound Cu(2,5-pydc)2Mn · H2O (2d).

The structure of 2 consists of copper(II) ions and manganese(II) ions in pseudo-octahedral coordination spheres linked via the 2,5-pydc2− ligands into a sheet polymer (see Fig. 6 for repeat unit, Fig. 7 for a portion of the sheet, Table 6 for crystallographic data and Table 7 for selected bond lengths and angles). Locally, each copper is chelated by two symmetrically equivalent 2,5-pydc2− ligands via the ring nitrogen and one 2-carboxyl oxygen (Fig. 7). The axial positions of the pseudo-octahedral copper(II) coordination sphere are filled by long bonds to the second 2-carboxyl oxygens of the 2,5-pydc2− moieties in adjacent repeat units, forming a double-O–C–O-bridged copper chain (Fig. 7). The manganese(II) ions are ligated in the equatorial plane by the 5-carboxyl oxygens of 2,5-pydc2− ligands bound to copper(II), with each manganese(II) ion linked to two adjacent repeat units along two adjacent copper(II) chains, such that the manganese(II) ions are linked by double-O–C–O-bridges into chains and the separate copper and manganese chains are cross-linked by double ligand bridges to form an overall extended sheet structure (Fig. 7). The axial positions of the manganese(II) chromophores are filled by two ligated water molecules; the remaining four water molecules occupy crystal lattice positions. Both ligated and lattice water molecules participate in an extensive three-dimensional hydrogen-bonding network reinforcing the structure of the sheets and linking adjacent sheets (Table 8). Adjacent sheets are also linked by π–π stacking interactions, albeit weak ones (Table 9).

DC magnetic susceptibility measurements were made on 2 from 300 to 2 K at 10 000 G. The magnetic moment data (Fig. 8) show a regular decrease in value with decreasing temperature indicative of overall antiferromagnetic coupling. To model the magnetic properties, we made the initial simplifying assumption that magnetic coupling between Cu2+ and Mn2+ centers via the N–C3–O ligand pathway is weak enough to be ignored. With this assumption, the overall model becomes one in which models for independent copper(II) and manganese(II) chains are simply added. The only justification for this approach comes with its ultimate success. To model the copper(II) chains in 2, we considered that they have a bridging structure similar to that of [Cu(2,4-pydcH)2]x · 2xCH3OH [9]. This compound is weakly antiferromagnetic and its susceptibility data have been successfully modeled with the Weng model for copper(II) linear chains using the Hiller coefficients for S = 1/2 [10] and the parameters J = −0.073(3) cm−1 and g = 2.217(1), and a TIP correction of 0.000017(4) cm3 mol−1. These parameters were used with the Weng model to model the copper(II) chains within the polymetallic structure of 2. No suitable structural model was found for the manganese(II) chains, so the magnetic data for 2 were initially modeled by adding the chosen model for the copper(II) chains to the zero-field splitting model for magnetically dilute manganese(II). The attempt at fitting the data with this combined model was not successful, suggesting that coupling between the manganese(II) centers cannot be ignored. The data were then fit with the model for the copper(II) chain added to the Weng model for manganese(II) chains utilizing the Hiller coefficients for S = 5/2 and fixing g(Mn) at 2.00. J(Mn) was employed as the variable parameter. The goodness of fit was measured by the least squares fitting function, F:(1)F=1n∑1nχicalc-χiobsχiobs2.This time the fit, shown in Fig. 8, was reasonable (F = 0.03), with the parameter J(Mn) = −0.32(1) cm−1. In summary, the magnetic data for 2 are reasonably well explained by weak coupling along each of the separate O–C–O bridged metal chains (J(Cu) = −0.073(3) cm−1 and J(Mn) = −0.32(1) cm−1) with no significant Mn–Cu interactions between these chains.

In order to monitor the effect of thermal dehydration on the magnetic properties of 2, magnetic data were collected for Cu(2,5-pydc)2Mn · H2O (2d). Data were taken from 300 to 2 K at a field of 10 000 G. The magnetic moment data for 2d (Fig. 8) are very similar to those for 2, the most noticeable difference being a slightly more pronounced decrease in moment with temperature for 2d suggesting stronger antiferromagnetic coupling in this material. In order to analyze these data, certain assumptions were made about the structure of 2d. The five water molecules removed from 2 by thermal treatment were assumed to be four lattice water molecules plus one of the two waters ligating manganese(II). The loss of these water molecules therefore would affect the coordination environment of the manganese centers with the potential to affect the strength of the Mn–Mn exchange interactions, but would not be expected to significantly affect the copper(II) chain structure, or its capacity for mediating magnetic exchange. The structural model used for 2 was therefore assumed to also be valid for 2d. Accordingly, the data were fit to the same equation used for 2, the summed Weng models for copper(II) and manganese(II) with the fixed parameters as before and J(Mn) as the only variable parameter. The fit was successful (F = 0.01) with the variable parameter J(Mn) found to be −0.55(1) cm−1. This value is 23 standard deviations higher than that found for 2, indicating that the loss of lattice and ligated water did increase the strength of antiferromagnetic exchange along the Mn–Mn chains in the material.

4. Summary and conclusions

This work has demonstrated the potential of 2,4-pydc2− and 2,5-pydc2− to bridge different kinds of metals, in this case copper(II) and manganese(II), and form extended arrays in which the metals exhibit a regular alternation. These ligands chelate via nitrogen and one 2-carboxyl oxygen to the “softer” metal of two and utilize one or more of the other carboxyl oxygen atoms to bridge to the “harder” metal. Analyses of the magnetic properties of 1 and 2 reveal that the N–C4–O and N–C3–O coordinative links between metals, involving the ring N and a 4- or 5-carboxyl oxygen, provide relatively weak pathways for magnetic exchange. More pronounced coupling appears associated with the O–C–O links in these systems. The result is ferromagnetic coupling between the copper and manganese centers in 1 and weak antiferromagnetic coupling within each of the copper and manganese chains in 2 with no measurable coupling between the two different metals in this latter compound.

On thermal dehydration, 1 forms a material 1d in which the overall exchange is antiferromagnetic. This dramatic change from the overall ferromagnetism of the parent compound is likely caused by structural changes on dehydration opening up an additional pathway for antiferromagnetic exchange in the material. In contrast, the magnetic properties of 2 are little altered on dehydration indicating only marginal differences between the structures of 2 and 2d such that the dominant antiferromagnetic pathways extant in 2 remain in 2d.

Finally, it should be noted that in spite of the presence of regularly alternating S = 1/2 and S = 5/2 centers in both 1 and 2, and presumed to be present in 1d and 2d, none of these systems gave evidence for ferrimagnetic behavior.

  • Synthesis, structural characterization and microbial activity of 2D Ag(I)-5-aminoisophthalate coordination polymer with a new coordination mode

    2015, Journal of Molecular Structure

    In this study, a novel polynuclear Ag(I)-5-aminoisophthalate complex [Ag(μ4-Haip)]n (1) (H2aip = 5-aminoisophthalic acid) has been synthesized. The molecular structure of this complex has been determined by the single crystal X-ray diffraction. The two-dimensional polynuclear complex is crystallized in the triclinic crystal system with space group P-1. The Ag(I) ion is four-coordinated by three carboxylate oxygen atoms of three different Haip ligands and one nitrogen atom in a distorted tetrahedral geometry. Furthermore, a novel coordination mode has shown by H2aip. This complex exhibits photoluminescence in the solid state at room temperature. Antimicrobial activity of complex was evaluated by the agar diffusion method. The complex showed antimicrobial activity against tested microorganism strains (Gram positive, gram negative bacteria, clinic isolate yeast and mold). Moreover this complex showed particularly high antifungal activity against yeast and mold.

  • The syntheses, crystal structures, spectroscopic and thermal characterization of new pyridine-2,5-dicarboxylate compounds

    2015, Polyhedron

    In this study, four new M(II)-isocinchomeronic acid (pyridine-2,5-dicarboxylic acid) complexes with ethylenediamine (en) and 2,2′-bipyridine (bipy), namely [Co(en)3][Co(pydc)2(H2O)2]·2H2O (1), [Ni(Hpydc)2(H2O)2] (2), [Cu(en)2(H2O)][Cu3(pydc)4(en)2(H2O)2]·3H2O (3) and [Cu(bipy)3][Cu(pydc)(bipy)2]·2Cl·12H2O (4) (H2pydc = pyridine-2,5-dicarboxylic acid), have been synthesised. Elemental, thermal analyses and IR spectroscopic studies have been carried out in order to characterise the complexes. The molecular structures of the complexes have been determined using single crystal X-ray diffraction. The geometry displayed by the Co(II) (1) and Ni(II) (2) ions is distorted octahedral. The Cu(II) ions display a square pyramidal geometry and distorted octahedral geometry in 3, while each Cu(II) ion exhibits a distorted octahedral geometry in 4. Molecules of each complex are linked into a 3D framework structure by N–H⋯O, O–H⋯O and C–H⋯O hydrogen bonds.

  • Syntheses, characterization, antimicrobial and cytotoxic activities of pyridine-2,5-dicarboxylate complexes with 1,10-phenanthroline

    2013, Journal of Trace Elements in Medicine and Biology

    In this study, four mononuclear M(II)-pyridine-2,5-dicarboxylate (M = Co(II), Ni(II), Cu(II) and Zn(II) complexes with pyridine-2,5-dicarboxylic acid or isocinchomeronic acid, 1,10-phenanthroline (phen), [Co(Hpydc)2(phen)]·H2O (1), [Ni(pydc)(phen)2]·6.5H2O (2) [Cu(pydc)(phen)(H2O)2] (3) and [Zn(pydc)(phen)(H2O)2]·H2O (4) have been synthesized. Elemental, thermal and mass analyses, molar conductance, magnetic susceptibilities, IR and UV/vis spectroscopic studies have been performed to characterize the complexes. Subsequently, these ligands and complexes were tested for antimicrobial activity by disc diffusion method on Gram positive, negative bacteria and yeast. In addition, cytotoxic activity tests were performed on rat glioma (C6) cells by MTT viability assay for 24 and 48 h. Antimicrobial activity results demonstrated that when compared to the standard antibiotics, phen displayed the most effective antimicrobial effect. The effect of synthesized complexes was close to phen or less. Cytotoxic activity results showed that IC50 value of phen was determined as 31 μM for 48 h. (1) and (2) compared to the alone ligand had less toxic activity. IC50 values of (3) for 24 and 48 h treatments were 2.5 and 0.6 μM, respectively. IC50 value of (4) for 48 h was 15 μM. In conclusion, phen, (3) and (4) may be useful as antibacterial and antiproliferative agents in the future.
