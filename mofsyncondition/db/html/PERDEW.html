Silver(I)-barbital based frameworks: Syntheses, crystal structures, spectroscopic, thermal and antimicrobial activity studies

Abstract

Three novel polymeric complexes in the silver(I)-barbital (barb) system with the formulae Na3[Ag3(μ-barb)6] (1), {[Ag2(μ-barb)2][Ag2(μ-en)2] ·  5H2O}n (2) and [Ag(barb)(μ-bpe)]n (3) [barb = 5,5-diethylbarbiturato; en = ethylenediamine; bpe = trans-1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)ethylene] have been synthesized and characterized by elemental analyses, IR, 1H NMR, DTA-TG and single-crystal X-ray diffraction. The structure of 1 reveals a 2D supramolecular arrangement of [Ag(barb)2] units by means of H-bond interactions, and the barb anions act as a bridge between the sodium(I) cations through the carbonyl groups, giving rise to a 3D network. Bi- and tridentate bridging modes of the barb ligand were observed simultaneously in 1. Silver(I) is linearly coordinated by two N atoms of two barb ligands, while sodium(I) has a highly distorted {NaO6} coordination geometry by barb O atoms. The average Ag⋯Na separation of 3.58 (5) Å possibly indicates a kind of ‘metallophilic’ interaction between Ag and Na atoms. Complex 2 consists of 1D anionic and cationic chains interconnected by way of Ag⋯Ag interactions leading to an unusual 2D grid-type framework, which is further extended to a 3D network with open channels by extensive hydrogen bonds. Complex 2 also exhibits some unusual features, such as the presence of the barb dianion and its bidentate bridging mode via the both negatively charged N atoms. Complex 3 is a 1D coordination polymer, in which the bpe ligand acts as a bridging ligand, while the barb ligand is monodendately N-coordinated resulting in a tri-coordinate silver(I) centre. Spectral and thermal analysis data for 1–3 are in agreement with the crystal structures. While none of the barb ligands and other coligands showed any significant antimicrobial activity, complexes 1–3 exhibited a wide spectrum of antimicrobial activity against the bacteria and fungi tested.

Graphical abstract

Three AgI-barb coordination polymers have been reported. Na3[Ag3(μ-barb)6] is a 3D mixed-metal complex, while {[Ag2(μ-barb)2][Ag2(μ-en)2] · 5H2O}n contains the barb dianion and features 2D grid-type layers, which are further connected by extensive hydrogen bonds resulting in a 3D framework with open channels. [Ag(barb)(μ-bpe)]n consists of 1D helical chains connected by hydrogen bonds between barb ligands to form 2D supramolecular sheets, which are further linked by aromatic π–π stacking interactions into a 3D network. The barb ligand has been shown to be able to exhibit different coordination modes. Different coordination modes of barb are seen in Na3[Ag3(μ-barb)6] and {[Ag2(μ-barb)2][Ag2(μ-en)2] · 5H2O}n. Antimicrobial studies of the title complexes were also performed. While barb and the other coligands did not show any significant antimicrobial activity, complexes 1–3 exhibited a wide spectrum of antimicrobial activity against selected bacteria and fungi.

1. Introduction

Barbiturates are widely used as sedative hypnotic drugs under a variety of conditions and are also employed for anesthesia [1]. 5,5-Diethylbarbituratic acid (barbH), also known as barbital, veronal or diemal, is chemically the simplest barbiturate derivative used especially in the form of its soluble salt. Due to their importance in medicine, the structural properties of barbiturates have received much attention and the crystal structures of barbital [2] and its sodium [3] and calcium [4] salts have been studied. Transition metal complexes of barbiturates have also been prepared because of their potential use in clinical detection and estimation of these drugs. Levi and Hubley [5] studied the reaction of 12 different clinically important barbiturates with copper(II) in the presence of pyridine (py) and on the basis of IR spectral data, suggested that the barbiturate ligands are coordinated to copper(II) through an oxygen atom. However, X-ray structures of [MII(barb)2(im)2] (MII = Co and Zn; im = imidazole) [6], [Ni(isoamylbarb)2(im)2] [7], [Cu(barb)2(py)2] [8], [Cu(barb)2(pic)2] · 2H2O [9] and [Zn(barb)2(pic)2] (pic = picoline) [10] showed that in all these complexes, the barbital moiety is deprotonated and the donor site of the barbiturate anion is the deprotonated N atom. On the other hand, it was recently reported that in [Cu(C4N2H3)2(H2O)3] [11], the barbiturate ligands were found to be O-coordinated. Furthermore, in the case of platinum metals, 1,3-dimethybarbiturate (dmbarb) exhibited linkage isomerism. For example, in [Pd(en)2(dmbarb)2] · 5.5H2O [12] and [Pt(en)2(dmbarb)2] (en = ethylenediamine) [13], one barbiturate ligand coordinates at the deprotonated carbon atom and the other at a deprotonated enol oxygen, while both barbiturate ligands in [Pt(en)2(dmbarb)2] ·  2H2O [13] are C-bonded.

The presence of several potential donor atoms such as two amine nitrogen atoms and three carbonyl oxygen atoms makes barbiturates very interesting and polyfunctional ligands in coordination chemistry. They have often been used as building blocks in ordered organic supramolecular assemblies with specific and directional hydrogen bonds [14], [15], [16], [17], [18], and they also seem to be capable of bridging metal centres to form polymeric structures. However, a literature survey indicates that metal complexes of barbiturates are rare. Recently, we have reported the synthesis, thermal, spectral and structural characterization of two new copper and cadmium complexes of barb with en, namely cis-[Cu(barb)2(en)] [19] and {[Cd(barb)2(μ-en)] · 2H2O}n [19]. The barb ligand in both complexes acts as a bidentate chelating ligand via the negatively charged imino N atom and one of the carbonyl O atoms adjacent to the imino N atom. In the case of zinc, [Zn(barb)2(en)] and [Zn(barb)2(bpy)] · H2O (bpy = 2,2′-bipyridine) complexes were obtained [20], and both barb ligands in [Zn(barb)2(en)] are N-bonded. It was shown that the barbiturate coordination modes depend on the nature of the barbiturate anion (Scheme 1). This has prompted us to study the AgI-barb system to probe the coordination modes of barb with silver(I) in the solid state, and herein we report the syntheses, crystal structures, thermal behaviors and spectroscopic properties of three novel AgI-barb frameworks containing en and trans-1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)ethylene (bpe), namely Na3[Ag3(μ-barb)6] (1), {[Ag2(μ-barb)2][Ag2(μ-en)2] · 5H2O}n (2) and [Ag(barb)(μ-bpe)]n (3). The structures of the complexes were determined by single-crystal X-ray diffraction and also supported by IR, 1H NMR and thermal decomposition studies. The antimicrobial activity of the ligands and complexes was also studied.

2. Experimental

2.1. Materials and measurements

All commercial reagents were used as supplied. Elemental analyses for C, H, and N were performed using a Carlo Erba 1106 microanalyser. IR spectra were recorded with a Jasco FTIR-430 spectrophotometer with samples as KBr pellets in the 4000–400 cm−1 range. 1H NMR spectra were recorded at room temperature (298 K) with a Bruker AC200 spectrometer. Thermal analysis curves (TG and DTA) were obtained using a Rigaku TG8110 thermal analyzer in a static air atmosphere with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 and a sample size of 5–10 mg.

2.2. Preparation of Na3[Ag3(μ-barb)6] (1)

A solution of AgNO3 (0.34 g, 2 mmol) in distilled water (10 mL) was mixed with a solution of Nabarb (5,5-diethylbarbituric acid sodium salt) (0.41 g, 2 mmol) in distilled water (10 mL) with continuous stirring at room temperature. The solution immediately became milky. The addition of N-(2-hydroxyethyl)ethylenediamine (0.21 mL) together with 10 mL of 2-propanol to the milky suspension resulted in a clear solution. The resulting solution was allowed to stand in darkness at room temperature and colorless prisms of 1 were obtained after 3 days. Yield 72%. Anal. Calc. for C48H66N12O18Ag3Na3: C, 38.65; H, 4.46; N, 11.27. Found: C, 38.53; H, 4.59; N, 11.39%. 2.3. Preparation of {[Ag2(μ-barb)2][Ag2(μ-en)2] · 5H2O}n (2)

The preparation method of 2 was the same as described for 1 with ethylenediamine (en) replacing N-(2-hydroxyethyl)ethylenediamine. The colorless crystals were obtained after 2 days. Yield 56%. Anal. Calc. for C20H46N8O11Ag4: C, 23.87; H, 4.61; N, 11.13. Found: C, 23.73; H, 4.52; N, 11.25%. IR (solid KBr pellet): ν (cm−1) 3197 sb, 3088 vw, 2973 w, 1676 sh, 1615 vs, 1431 vs, 1337 vs, 1258 s, 1013 s, 951 w, 829 m, 768 m, 543 m, 451 m, 420 w. 1H NMR (200 MHz, [D6]DMSO, 25 °C): δ = 0.65 (t, 3H, CH3), 1.72 (q, 2H, CH2), 2.80 (s, 2H, CH2–NH2), 3.37 (s, H, NH2) ppm.

2.4. Preparation of [Ag(barb)(μ-bpe)]n (3)

Compound 3 was synthesized in a similar manner to 2 except that trans-1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)ethylene (bpe) was used instead of en. Colorless crystals suitable for X-ray were formed by slow evaporation of the resulting solution in darkness at room temperature after a few days. Yield 87%. Anal. Calc. for C20H21N4O3Ag: C, 50.74; H, 4.47; N, 11.84. Found: C, 50.85; H, 4.57; N, 11.72%. IR (solid KBr pellet): ν (cm−1) 3170 sb, 2979 w, 1677 vs, 1584 vs, 1414 vs, 1347 vs, 1275 sh, 987 s, 837 s, 760 m, 554 s, 452 m, 410 w. 1H NMR (200 MHz, [D6]DMSO, 25 °C): δ = 0.69 (t, 3H, CH3), 1.75 (q, 2H, CH2), 7.57 (s, H, CHCH), 7.63 (dd, 2H, py ring), 8.62 (dd, 2H, py ring), 10.65 (s, H, NH) ppm.

2.5. Antimicrobial testing

For antimicrobial activities, the compounds were tested against standard strains of Gram-negative Escherichia coli MC4100 and Pseudomonas aeruginosa ATCC27853, Gram-positive Bacillus subtilis and Staphylococcus aureus ATCC 43300, and two yeasts Candida albicans ATCC 10231 and C. parapsilosis ATCC 22019. Antimicrobial activity tests were carried out using the broth dilution method. For the broth dilution method, cultures were started inoculating a single colony from a freshly grown culture on nutrient broth agar (Acumedia) and grown overnight in nutrient broth (Acumedia) at 37 °C for 18 h in an orbital shaker incubator (GLF, Germany) shaking at 200 rpm. These cultures were used as starter cultures. Approximately, 106 cells from starter cultures were inoculated in 5 ml nutrient broth media. For Candida strains cultures were initially grown on PDA plates (Merck) 37 °C for 48 h and used for inoculation in fresh nutrient broth medium. The inoculum amount was same as that of bacterial strains. All the test cultures were grown at 37 °C in the shaker incubator. The incubation period was 24 h for bacterial strains and 48 h for fungal strains. Nutrient broth containing test tubes first supplemented with various amounts of each compound, made serial dilutions in nutrient broth from 100 to 0.1 μg/ml. The compounds were first tested in 10-fold serial dilution; eventually the ranges were narrowed to define more exact values. The minimum inhibitor concentration, at which no growth was observed, was taken as the MIC value (μg/ml), and represents the mean of at least three determinations. Ampicillin (Applichem) was used as a reference inhibitor for bacterial strains and nystatin (BDH) for fungal strains. For comparison, AgNO3 was tested for MIC values. All the ligands and the solvents used were also tested for antimicrobial activity.

2.6. X-ray crystallography

The intensity data of the complexes 1–3 were collected using a STOE IPDS 2 diffractometer with graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073). Complexes 1 and 2 were measured at 100 K, while complex 3 was measured at 293 K. The structures were solved by direct methods and refined on F2 with the shelxl-97 program [21]. All non-hydrogen atoms were found from the difference Fourier map and refined anisotropically. All hydrogen atoms were included using a riding model. 3. Results and discussion

3.1. Synthetic aspects

Silver complexes 1–3 were obtained by the direct reaction of sodium barbital with AgNO3 in the presence of the co-ligands. Initially, we aimed to prepare mixed-ligand silver(I)-barb complexes with some diamine ligands such as N-(2-hydroxyethyl)ethylenediamine (heen), en and bpe. However, in the case of heen, the desired silver(I) complex could not be prepared since the coordination of the ligand seemed to be unsuccessful in the present conditions. Instead, a new anionic complex, Na3[Ag3(μ-barb)6] (1), was obtained. The direct synthesis of 1 from sodium barbital and AgNO3 in the absence of heen was attempted, but the reaction resulted in the rapid precipitation of a polycrystalline powder, presumably [Ag(barb)]n which is barely soluble in known solvents.

Complexes 1–3 are air stable and do not exhibit a melting point, but decompose at 310, 36 and 232 °C, respectively. They were obtained in reasonably high yields (over 55%). The analytical data are consistent with the proposed formulation of the complexes. All three complexes are highly soluble in a 4:1 mixture of water and 2-propanol, and slightly soluble in pure water and common alcohols such as methanol, ethanol and 2-propanol.

3.2. IR and 1H NMR spectra

The IR spectra (KBr disks) of complexes 1–3 were recorded in KBr in the 4000–400 cm−1 range. For comparative purposes the IR spectra of the free ligands were also obtained. The complexes display the characteristic bands of the ligands, somewhat modified by coordination. The strong and broad absorption bands at 3170–3197 cm−1 in the spectra of the complexes indicate the presence of the NH groups. The relatively weak bands in the 2970–3080 cm−1 range are attributed to the C–H stretching vibrations. The sharp absorption bands in the frequency range 1560–1700 cm−1 characterize vibrations of the carbonyl groups. The barb ligand exists in the triketo form and all carbonyl groups interact with sodium(I) in Na(barb) [3]. As will be discussed later, similar interactions of the carbonyl groups with the sodium(I) ion were observed in 1 and, therefore, the IR spectra of Na(barb) and 1 both possess two sharp bands at 1697 and 1666 cm−1, in accord with the presence of two different carbonyl groups. Two types of strong absorption bands of the carbonyl group were also observed at 1676 and 1615 cm−1 for 2, whereas only a single band was found at 1677 cm−1 in the spectrum of 3. In 2 and 3, carbonyl groups do not participate in coordination and the apparent red-shifts may be associated with strong hydrogen bonding. The strong bands at 1584 and 1558 cm−1 in the spectra of 1 and 3, respectively, are due to N–H deformation vibrations of the barb ligand. The absence of this band in the spectrum of 2 clearly suggests the complete deprotonation of both amine groups. These conclusions are supported by the results of X-ray diffraction studies. The bands with strong intensity between 1464 and 1311 cm−1 correspond to the C–H deformation vibrations and the band centered around 1265 cm−1 is attributed to the C–N stretching vibrations [5]. The weak or medium intensity bands in the region 455–410 cm−1 can be assigned to the Ag–N stretching vibration.

The 1H NMR spectra of complex 1 show all expected resonances of the barb moiety. The signal at 10.66 ppm was assigned to the NH proton of barb and was shifted to downfield by about 1 ppm, compared to Na(barb). The NH proton appears as a sharp band in DMSO and is absent in the spectra of complex 2, confirming deprotonation of the barb monoanion to a dianion, also proposed above from its IR spectra. In the NMR spectra of 2, signals at 2.80 and 3.37 ppm correspond to the CH2–NH2 and NH2 groups, respectively, and are shifted upfield compared to those of free en; this is quite similar to findings reported for platinum complexes with en derivatives [22]. The protons of the water molecules appear as a broad band centered at 3.65 ppm. For complex 3, the 1H NMR spectra are similar to that of complex 1, the only differences arising from the py rings of the bpe ligand. The spectra show that the chemical shifts of the protons of the py ring exhibit two sets of signal at 7.64 ppm, as a doublet, and 8.62 ppm, also as a doublet. Moreover, the protons of the ethylene moiety appear at 7.57 ppm as a singlet.

3.3. Thermal studies

To study the thermal decomposition behaviors and thermal stabilizes of complexes 1–3, thermogravimetric (TG) and differential thermal analyses (DTA) of the compounds were performed in an atmosphere of air in the temperature range 20–900 °C. The final decomposition residues were identified by elemental analysis and also confirmed by mass loss calculations. Complex 1 shows a very high thermal stability and begins to decompose at 309 °C. Its thermal stability is comparable to Na[Ag(sac)2] (sac = saccharinate) [23]. The degradation of the barb moiety is completed in a single step with a continuous mass loss in the TG curve between 309 and 450 °C, and the DTA curve presents an endothermic peak at 330 °C and three highly exothermic peaks at 377, 398 and 439 °C, giving a final decomposition product containing silver, sodium and oxygen.

Complex 2 shows a two-step decomposition process and in the first step, it starts to lose water molecules between 36 and 198 °C with a mass loss of 8.57% (calc. 8.94%). The initial temperature of dehydration is relatively low and suggests that the water molecules are uncoordinated. In the second step, the anhydrous compound begins to decompose at 204 °C with a continuous mass loss and due to overlapping decomposition processes of both en and barb ligands it is impossible to calculate mass loss values for the elimination of each ligand. However, the overall mass loss of the second step is 49.05% (calc. 48.11%). The end product at 398 °C was presumably silver oxide.

Complex 3 is thermally stable up to 232 °C, a significantly lower temperature than that of 1. It shows a continuous decomposition in the temperature range 232–381 °C. The endothermic peak at 309 °C corresponds to the decomposition of the bpe ligands, while the violently exothermic peak at 356 °C characterizes the decomposition of the barb ligands. The total weight loss value of 74.46% is in agreement with the calculated value 75.52%. The end decomposition product was suggested to be silver oxide.

3.4. Description of the structures

3.4.1. Na3[Ag3(μ-barb)6] (1)

The asymmetric unit of 1 is shown in Fig. 1 and selected bond lengths and angles are listed in Table 2. Complex 1 is a coordination polymer, in which the barb anions simultaneously bridge the silver and sodium cations giving rise to a 3D network. The structure of 1 contains two different silver and Na atoms, as depicted in Fig. 1. Both silver(I) ions are linearly coordinated by the negatively charged N atoms of the two barbital ions. In contrast to atom Ag1, atom Ag2 is coordinated a barb ligand and its symmetry-equivalent partner. All Ag–N bond distances are almost identical, resulting in an average value of 2.075(4) Å. The barb anions act as a bridge between the sodium(I) cations through their carbonyl groups. The coordination mode of the three distinct barb ligands in 1 differs: ligands barb1 and barb2 with atoms N1, and N3, respectively, behave as tridentate ligands through a N and two carbonyl O atoms, while ligand barb3 with atom N5 acts as a bidentate bridging ligand via one of the carbonyl O and a N atom. To the best of our knowledge, the simultaneous presence of two coordination modes of the barb ligand has not been reported until now.

In contrast to the tetrahedrally coordinated sodium(I) ions in Nabarb [3], in 1 both sodium(I) ions are octahedrally coordinated by the carbonyl O atoms from six different barb ligands with Na–O bond distances ranging from 2.333(5) to 2.700(4) Å and cis-O–Na–O bond angles ranging between 71.81(15) and 112.30(15), as listed in Table 2. The Na–O bond distances were found to be significantly longer than those reported for Nabarb [3]. Since the total van der Waals radii of sodium and silver (rNa = 2.27 Å and rAg = 1.72 Å) is 3.99 Å, the Ag⋯Na separations of 3.537(2), 3.651(2) and 3.638(2) Å may indicate a kind of ‘metallophilic’ interactions between silver(I) and sodium(I) ions. The minimum Ag⋯Ag and Na⋯Na distances are 5.733 and 7.188 Å, respectively.

The pyrimidine rings in the three barb ligands are essentially planar and the three carbonyl groups are not significantly displaced from the mean planes of their attached pyrimidine ring. The dihedral angle between the barb1 and barb2 mean planes is 5.90(18)°, indicating the co-planarity of these barb ligands. Conformation, bond lengths and angles of the barb ligands in 1 are similar to those of the barb ion [3], [4]. The two ethyl groups in barb2 are disordered over two positions with approximately equal occupancy. Fig. 2 shows the packing of anionic [Ag(barb)2] units in the absence of sodium(I) ions. [Ag(barb)2] units are linked by N–H⋯O hydrogen bonds generating a 2D supramolecular framework and the sodium(I) cations further connect these layers to a 3D network (Fig. 2).

3.4.2. {[Ag2(μ-barb)2][Ag2(μ-en)2] · 5H2O}n (2)

The asymmetric unit of complex 2 is shown in Fig. 3, and Table 3 lists selected bond length and angles together with the hydrogen bonding geometry. The asymmetric unit contains four crystallographically unique Ag atoms, each having a significantly different environment. As shown in Fig. 4, complex 2 consists of two different chains: an anionic 1D linear chain with [Ag(μ-barb)] units and a cationic 1D zig-zag chain containing [Ag(μ-en)] units. These anionic and cationic chains are interconnected by way of Ag⋯Ag interactions to form an unusual 2D framework, in which the chains interact each other leading to a rectangle-grid motif with dimensions of 17.04 × 34.55 Å2 (Fig. 4). In complex 2, the barb ligand is further deprotonated to form a dianion, which acts as a bidentate bridging ligand via both negatively charged N atoms. Again, to the best of our knowledge, this is the first example of a barb dianion coordinating to a metal ion, and the N,N′-bridging coordination mode of the ligand has also been observed for the first time in this work. As a result, Scheme 1 should be extended to include the new anionic form of barb.

In both anionic and cationic units, each AgI center has a slightly distorted linear {AgN2} coordination geometry. The average Ag–Nen bond distance of 2.13(5) Å is similar to that observed in [Ag2(sac)2(en)(H2O)]n [24], but is noticeably longer than the Ag–Nbarb bond distances in 2. The silver atoms appear to be interacting based on the Ag–Ag distances of 3.236(1) and 3.404(1) Å, which are significantly shorter than twice the van der Waals radius of silver (3.44 Å) and clearly indicate a bonding interaction as observed in [Ag2(sac)2(en)(H2O)]n [24]. The intra-chain Ag–Ag distances are 5.827(5) and 7.449(4) Å in the anionic and cationic chains. Ag⋯Ag interactions have been a subject of discussion: Jansen [25] suggested a upper limit value of 3.30 Å for an Ag⋯Ag contact in silver(I) coordination compounds, but Dance et al. [26] argued that since the linear geometry around silver(I) indicates the fulfillment of its coordination requirements, there is no bonding interaction between silver(I) centers as close to each other as 2.886 Å in [Ag(SCMeEt)2]n. However, although the closed d10 configuration of silver(I) appears to cancel any intermetallic bonding in silver(I) complexes, there are many examples of silver(I) complexes with definite Ag⋯Ag attractive forces known as ‘argentophilic’ interactions [27].

Both barb ligands are almost planar, but the C atoms bearing the two ethyl groups deviate from the mean plane by 0.2 Å. The barb rings are co-planar with a dihedral angle of 3.98(15)°. The torsion angle of 178.6(7)° suggests that the non-hydrogen atoms of en lie in the same plane. Complex 2 exhibits a number of intra- and intermolecular N–H⋯O and O–H⋯O hydrogen bonds as listed in Table 3. The amine hydrogen atoms of en participate in weak hydrogen bonding with the O atoms of barb ligands and water molecules. The water O atoms do not participate in coordination with silver(I) and their hydrogen atoms form strong hydrogen bonds with the O atoms of barb ligands and water molecules. Thus, the 2D layers of 2 are linked by these hydrogen bonds into a 3D network with open channels (Fig. 5). Calculations with platon [28] show that in the absence of the water molecules, the structure of 2 has solvent accessible voids with a volume of 73.7 Å3, which is approximately 9% of the total cell volume, indicating that the channels are filled with water molecules.

3.5. Antimicrobial activities

The metal-free ligands and their silver(I) complexes were tested for their in vitro antimicrobial activities against two Gram-positive bacteria, B. subtilis and S. aureus, two Gram-negative bacteria, E. coli and P. aeruginosa, and two fungi C. albicans and C. parapsilosis. The results are presented in Table 5 as minimum inhibitor concentration (MIC) values (μg/ml) which is the minimum concentration to inhibit the growth of bacteria or fungi.

The free ligands and solvents showed no antimicrobial activity against either bacterial or fungal strains tested at a concentration of 100 μg/ml, but the ligands in the form of silver complexes had significant levels of activity due to facts that in general silver(I) complexes are kinetically labile and a ligand exchange process is quite likely to occur. The antimicrobial activity of AgNO3 was, almost without exception, superior to that of complexes 1–3 across all of the bacterial and the fungal strains studied. Complex 1 had a higher activity on all the strains especially against P. aeruginosa and C. parapsilosis. The MICs for four strains, E. coli, B. subtilis, S. aureus and C. albicans, were ca. 25 μg/ml, while the growth of the other two strains, P. aeruginosa and C. parapsilosis, was inhibited more effectively at ca. 12 μg/ml. For complex 2, the growth of the strains P. aeruginosa, E. coli and S. aureus was inhibited at a concentration of ca. 15 μg/ml of the complex, while the MICs were 47 μg/ml for B. subtilis and 35 μg/ml for both fungal strains. Complex 3 showed similar MIC values for all the strains. Compared to complexes 1 and 2, the MICs of 3 were found to be noticeably higher and this may be consequence of the slow precipitation tendency of this complex in the growth medium. Among the three silver-barbital complexes, complex 2 seems to be more effective against all bacterial strains. Compared to ampicillin, all three complexes demonstrated a remarkable microbial activity on bacterial strains with exception of S. aureus. It is interesting to note that P. aeruginosa was significantly affected by complexes 1 and 2. These facts are consistent with recently observed results for polymeric silver(I) complexes containing Ag–N and Ag–O bonds [29], [30], [31], [32]

Nystatin and AgNO3 inhibited the growth of the fungi at MIC values less than 10 μg/ml. Complexes 1–3 are remarkably active against both the Candida strains. Both strains were more susceptible to complex 1, while similar MIC values were found for the other two complexes conclusively, having less activity than AgNO3 and nystatin.

In the case of silver(I) complexes, two key factors were proposed for a wide range of antimicrobial activities: (i) the type of the coordinating ligands around silver(I), and (ii) the ease of their replacement with biological ligands such as proteins, enzymes, amino acids and nucleic acids [33], [34], [35], [36]. As can be seen from Table 1, Table 2, Table 3, the silver(I) ion has a AgN3 core in complexes 1–3 and the Ag–N bond lengths increase in the order of 1, 2 and 3. Although complex 3 seems to have higher ligand exchange ability among the three complexes, its precipitation in the growth medium makes complex 2 a good inhibitory agent. On the other hand, there are contrasting effects regarding drug solubility. For example, a water-insoluble polymeric complex, sulfadiazinesilver(I) (commercially known as silvadene or flamazine), is used to treat burn wounds [37] and its antimicrobial activity is mainly attributed to the slow liberation of silver. In conclusion, the title complexes show effective antimicrobial inhibition and their low solubility in water promises some potential antibacterial and antifungal applications.
