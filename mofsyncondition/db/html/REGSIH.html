Introduction

Crystal engineering of heterometallic–organic frameworks (HMOFs) is of great current interest not only because of their many practical applications in luminescence, magnetism, gas storage and heterogeneous catalysis as new materials1–4 but also due to their impressive structural diversity in architectures.5 A useful way to synthesize HMOFs based on self-assembly is using mixed metal ions and organic ligands containing hybrid donor atoms.6 A great deal of 3d-4f, 4d-4f and 3p-3d-4f HMOFs or heterometallic clusters have been successfully synthesized and characterized.7,8 However, this research field remains in its infancy since we can hardly control the extended structures or local coordination modes of individual components, let alone the chemical composition of the final homometallic or heterometallic compounds.6b,9 These synthesis processes are affected by many factors, such as the structural characteristics of the ligands, coordination nature of the metal ion, solvent system, template, pH value of the solution, steric requirement of the counter-ion, reaction temperature and the metal-to-ligand ratio.8c,10 In particular, the competitive reactions between d- and f-metals chelated to the same ligand often result in homometallic complexes rather than heterometallic ones.3b,11 Therefore, the selection or design of ligands containing an appropriate N and O donor coordination site is crucial to constructing HMOFs.12

Recently, 1H-imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid has been developed to construct MOFs or HMOFs that exhibit excellent photoluminescence properties.10g,13 Its bridging angle M–Im–M (where M = metal ions and Im = imidazolate) coincides with that of the Si–O–Si angle in zeolites (145°), giving rise to many new zeolitic imidazolate frameworks.141H-benzimidazole-5,6-dicarboxylic acid (H3Bidc) is the analogue of 1H-imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid. To the best of our knowledge, Co–Ln HMOFs constructed using H3Bidc as a ligand have not yet been reported.

Lanthanide ions (Ln3+) are characterized by a gradual filling of the 4f orbitals, from 4f0 to 4f.14 These electronic [Xe]4fn configurations (n = 0–14) generate a variety of electronic energy levels, resulting in intricate optical properties.15 Especially, compounds containing Sm3+, Eu3+, Tb3+, and Dy3+ ions can exhibit intense luminescence in the visible part of the spectrum. The photophysical properties of the f–f transitions in lanthanide complexes are markedly dependent on their environments, and emission is sensitized by energy transfer from a suitable adjacent chromophore, such as phenyl or pyridyl, which is called the “antenna effect”.16 Compared with the ligand 1H-imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylate, an extended π-conjugated system is introduced by applying H3Bidc, resulting in possibly unique luminescence properties.

On the basis of the aforementioned points, our aim is to synthesize novel high-dimensional HMOFs through combining the respective merits of H3Bidc ligands, rare earth metal ions and cobalt metal ions. In this paper, the synthesis, structural characterization and properties of a family [Ln2Co(Hbidc)2(SO4)2(H2O)8]n HMCPs [Ln = Pr (1), Eu (2), Gd (3), Tb (4), Dy (5), Ho (6), Er (7)] is reported. X-Ray single crystal diffraction reveals that complexes 1–6 are isomorphous and crystallize in the space group C2/c, while 7 crystallizes in the space group P21/c. All compounds have a three-dimensional framework. The fluorescence of 2, 4, 5 and the magnetic properties of 3–5 have also been investigated.

Experimental section

Materials

All chemicals purchased were of reagent grade and were used without further purification. All syntheses were carried out in 25 mL Teflon-lined autoclaves under autogenous pressure. The reaction vessels were filled to approximately 50% volume capacity. Water used in the synthesis was distilled before use.

Physical measurements

The elemental analyses (C, H, and N) were carried out applying a PerkinElmer 240C elemental analyzer. FT-IR spectra were recorded on a Nicolet IR-470 spectrometer using KBr pellets. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns of the samples were recorded using an X-ray diffractometer (BRUKER D8 ADVANCE) with Cu Kα radiation. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) experiments were performed on a NETZSCH TG 209 instrument applying a heating rate of 5 °C min−1. Luminescence spectra were recorded on a FS920 steady state fluorescence spectrometer (Edinburgh Instruments) equipped with a Hamamatsu R928P red-sensitive photomultiplier. Luminescence decay measurements were obtained with a pulsed nitrogen laser (excitation wavelength of 337 nm, pulse length of 800 ps, repetition rate of 1 Hz) and a 1024-channel Intensified CCD (Andor Technology) attached to a 0.5 m Ebert monochromator. Variable-temperature magnetic susceptibilities were measured on a Quantum Design MPMS-7 SQUID magnetometer. Diamagnetic corrections were made with Pascal's constants for all constituent atoms.

Single crystal X-ray crystallographic study

The collection of single crystal crystallographic data was carried out on a Bruker SMART Apex CCD diffractometer using graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (0.71073 Å) at 293 K in the ω–2θ scan mode. An empirical absorption correction was applied to the data using the SADABS program.17 The structures were solved by direct methods and refined by full-matrix least-squares methods on F2 using the SHELXTL crystallographic software package.18 All non-H atoms were refined anisotropically. The H atoms were placed in calculated positions and refined by using a riding mode. The crystallographic data, selected bond lengths and angles for 1–7 are listed in Tables 1 and 2 and Table S1, ESI,† respectively.

Results and discussion

[Ln2Co(HBidc)2(SO4)2(H2O)8]n [Ln = Pr (1), Eu (2), Gd (3), Tb (4), Dy (5), Ho (6)]

From the single crystal X-ray diffraction study, it was found that complexes 1–6 are isostructural and crystallize in the space group C2/c. Therefore, the presentation and discussion will be restricted to complex 4 as a representative example.

As shown in Fig. 1, the unit cell of 4 contains one Tb(iii), one Co(ii) and a HBidc ligand. Tb1 is nine coordinated by five carboxylate oxygen atoms from three HBidc ligands (O1A, O2A, O3, O4, O4B), two oxygen atoms (O5A, O6) from two individual sulfate anions and the remaining two sites (O9, O10) are occupied by two oxygen atoms of the coordinated water molecules. Thus, the coordination geometry about Tb(iii) can be viewed as a monocapped square-antiprism. The Tb–O bond lengths range from 2.291(2) Å to 2.887(2) Å, similar to the reported Tb–O distance.12a,19 Co(ii) are coordinated by two nitrogen atoms (N1A, N1B) from different HBidc ligands and four oxygen atoms (O11, O12, O12A, O13) from coordinated water molecules. The coordination geometry about Co(ii) can be viewed as an octahedron. The Co–N bond lengths are both 2.109(3) Å, the Co–O bond lengths ranged from 2.091(3) Å to 2.122(4) Å. All Co–O bond and Co–N bond distances are within normal range.20 The HBidc ligands exhibit the following coordination mode in complex 4: (κ1-κ1-μ1)-(κ1-κ2-μ2)-κ1-μ4 (Scheme 1, model 1), and the carboxylates adopt two coordination modes: a bidentate bridging mode and a tridentate chelating bridging mode.

The crystal structure of 4 is built up of two distinct types of building blocks through the self-assembly process, a Tb2O8(SO4)2(H2O)4 center and a CoN2(H2O)4 center. In the Tb2O8(SO4)2(H2O)4 center, two crystallographically equivalent Tb atoms adopt a paddle-wheel binuclear cluster structure, bridged by two carboxylate groups from separate HBidc ligands and two sulfate anions. The Tb–Tb distance is 4.0838(11) Å. It is notable that the example of a paddle-wheel binuclear unit bridged simultaneously by two carboxylates and two sulfates is not reported, although paddle-wheel binuclear bridging by four carboxylates often appears in previous reports.21 The CoN2(H2O)4 center is coordinated by two nitrogen atoms from separate HBidc ligands. All Tb centers and Co centers are uniformly interconnected through a coordinating HBidc ligand by the oxygen atoms and an imidazolenitrogen atom to form a wavy one-dimensional (1D) chain, as shown in Fig. 2. The infinite 1D chain extends to a two-dimensional (2D) network as a result of the coordination of the HBidc ligand with the Tb center of another 1D chain (Fig. 3). It is worth noting that the Tb center is sandwiched in the interlayer regions of the two Co center planes alternately arranged, which are connected by HBidc ligands. Due to the further coordination of the Tb center by oxygen atoms from carboxylate groups of HBdc ligands, the 2D network further forms a highly ordered three-dimensional (3D) framework structure, as shown in Fig. 4. Topological studies were performed on 4 using the software package TOPOS 4.022 revealing that this complex can be simplified to a 4,4-connected PtS-type 3D framework with Schläfli symbol 42·84. The binuclear Tb(iii) centers were defined as 4-connected nodes and the units of two HBidc2− ligands linked by a Co(H2O)42+ substructure could be simplified as 4-connected nodes, as shown in Fig. 5.

[Er2Co(HBidc)2(SO4)2(H2O)8]n (7)

X-ray crystal structure analyses revealed that 7 crystallizes in the space group P21/c. There are two crystallographically unique Er(iii) cations, one Co(ii) cation, two sulfate anions and two HBidc ligands in the asymmetric unit, as illustrated in Fig. 6. Er1 is eight coordinated in a distorted square-antiprismatic configuration with four oxygen atoms (O1, O5, O6, O7) from carboxyl groups of three HBidc ligands, two oxygen atoms (O9, O13) from sulfate anions and two terminal-coordinated water molecules (O17, O18). Er2 is nine coordinated in a slightly distorted monocapped square-antiprismatic configuration with O1, O2, O3A, O4A and O8 from carboxyl groups of three HBidc ligands, O10 and O14 from sulfate anions and two oxygen atoms of terminal-coordinated water molecules (O19, O20). The Er(iii)–O distances range from 2.295(3) to 2.786(3) Å, similar to the average reported Er–O distance.23 It is remarkable that the HBidc ligands exhibit two different coordination modes in complex 7: (κ1-κ1-μ1)-(κ1-κ2-μ2)-κ1-μ4 and (κ1-κ1-μ1)-(κ1-κ1-μ2)-κ1-μ4, depicted in Scheme 1 (model 1 and model 2). The carboxyl O1 adopts a μ2-O bidentate coordination mode and carboxyl O7 and O8 employ a μ1-O monodentate coordination mode to link the Er1 and Er2, which results in a binuclear Er(iii) centre with the Er–Er distance equal to 4.0723(7) Å. In complex 7, Co(ii) cations and sulfate anions are in the same chemical environment and play the same role as those in complex 4. The 2D network and the 3D framework are packed via the same bridging modes as in 4, the packing results are the same as those depicted in Fig. 3 and 4. Simplified, its topological network can be described similar to the prior six complexes.

The considerable change in the size of the Ln ions with increasing atomic number leads to compounds of lanthanides with different structures, such as a decrease of Ln coordination number and Ln–O bonds lengths and a change of the dimensionality of the compounds.24 Compounds 1–7 crystallize in two structural types with the Ln ions coordination number steadily decreasing from nine only (1–6) to nine and eight (7) as a result of lanthanide contraction. The average lengths of Ln–O are decreasing continuously from 2.535 Å (1), 2.485 Å (2), 2.466 Å (3), 2.458 Å (4), 2.444 Å (5), 2.431 Å (6) to 2.390 Å (7) by a linearly decreasing trend.

PXRD and TGA

Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) analyses of compounds 1–7 have been performed at room temperature (see Fig. S1, ESI†). The perceived patterns for 1–7 are in good agreement with the calculated patterns obtained from the single-crystal structures, confirming that the obtained purities equal those of the single crystal samples.

TGA curves for polycrystalline samples of 1–7 are depicted in Fig. S2, ESI,† which were carried out in the temperature range of 20–1000 °C under a N2 atmosphere at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. Since the TGA curves of the seven complexes are very similar, the complex 4 has been chosen for a detailed description. The first weight loss of polymer4 from 50 to 200 °C is about 12.59%, corresponding to the loss of the eight coordinated water molecules (calcd: 13.22%). From 200 to 600 °C, the weight loss of 46.41% is ascribed to the removal of organic moiety. Above 600 °C, the remaining component continues to break down, but we cannot confirm the final composition, in which we only speculate that that there are sulfates and oxides of the rare earth element and cobalt.

Luminescence properties

The photoluminescence properties of complexes 2, 4 and 5 were investigated at room temperature. The emission spectra (Fig. 7) were recorded under excitation with ultraviolet (UV) light (395 nm, 315 nm and 305 nm, for 2, 4 and 5, respectively). These spectra are solely composed of the 4fn–4fn transitions typical for each rare earth ions.

In 2, the main emission lines are situated in the red part of the spectrum, originating from the 5D0 to 7FJ transitions in Eu3+, with J = 0, 1,…,6. The emissions terminating at the 7F2 level (peaking around 615 nm) dominate the spectrum, which is consistent with the lack of inversion symmetry at the rare earth sites. The crystal field splitting of the 7FJ-levels can clearly be observed. Emission from higher excited states (5D1) is very weak, pointing at a strong cross-relaxation leading to efficient non-radiative depopulation towards the lowest excited state (5D0). For 4, the typical green emission of Tb3+ is observed, with transitions from 5D4 to 7F6 (488 nm), 7F5 (546 nm), 7F4 (583 nm) and 7F3 (621 nm). Also for this ion, no transitions from the higher excited states were observed. For 5, the blue (482 nm), yellow (575 nm) and red (660 nm) emission lines of Dy3+ are observed (originating from transitions from the 4F9/2 level to 6H15/2, 6H13/2 and 6H11/2, respectively).

The excitation spectra of these rare earth compounds can contain contributions from internal 4fn–4fn transitions, 4fn–4fn−15d transitions and/or charge transfer transitions (Fig. 8). For 2, the 4f–4f absorptions are prominent, along with charge transfer bands around 300 nm. Also for 5 this is the case. For 4, the excitation spectrum in the range from 250 nm to 350 nm looks markedly different, presumably due to the presence of a 4f8–4f75d excitation band at 315 nm, superposed on the charge transfer transitions.

For all three compounds, the luminescence lifetimes were measured upon pulsed excitation. During the luminescence decay, no significant spectral changes were observed. A single exponential decay is not observed for any of the three complexes (Fig. 9). Also, the decay is much faster than would be expected for the forbidden 4fn–4fn transitions in Eu3+, Tb3+ and Dy3+, which give rise to decay constants in the millisecond range. The decay constant τ is defined such that the emission at any given time can be expressed as a function of the initial emission intensity I0, by I(t) = I0exp(−t/τ). For the studied compounds, two to three such exponential components are required to fit the experimental decay profiles, as displayed in Fig. 9. For 2, the decay constants are 4.1 μs (25%), 21.4 μs (50%) and 120 μs (25%), for 4 they are 1.2 μs (5%), 72 μs (22%) and 375 μs (73%) and for 5 they are 0.6 μs (70%) and 2.2 μs (30%). The values in brackets give the fraction of the integrated light emission for each decay component. The fast decay is an indication that the quantum efficiency of these materials is low. This is in line with their weak emission under UV excitation upon visual inspection. The low efficiency (and fast decay) can be related to the presence of many non-radiative decay paths. These can have several origins, such as energy transfer between rare earth ions (due to their relatively close proximity), the presence of Co ions and H2O molecules, and other crystal defects. In recent work, the luminescence properties of similar complexes with Ni instead of Co were studied.7f,25 For the Eu complex, decay constants of 2.3 μs and 230 μs were found, which is in the same range as for the present Co compound. As Ni2+ and Co2+ have a similar energy structure, energy transfer to these ions is likely to lower the quantum efficiency, due to the crowded energy level structure of these transition metal ions.

Magnetic properties for 3–5

The magnetic susceptibilities of 3–5 were measured in the 2–300 K temperature range with an applied magnetic field of 1000 Oe, as shown in plots of χMT vs. T in Fig. 10. The χMT values are 17.57, 25.18 and 30.07 cm3 K mol−1, respectively, at 300 K, which is lower than the theoretical values of 17.62, 25.50 and 30.20 cm3 K mol−1, respectively, based upon binuclear GdIII ions (g = 2), TbIII ions (g = 3/2), DyIII ions (g = 4/3) and one uncoupled CoII ion (g = 2).26 With cooling, the χMT values slowly decrease to a minimum of 14.89, 17.67 and 17.86 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K, respectively. The χM−1vs. T plots of 3–5, which are presented in Fig. S3, ESI,† obey the Curie–Weiss law 1/χM = (T − θ)/C, corresponding to Curie constants C = 17.78 cm3 K mol−1, 25.29 cm3 K mol−1 and 30.30 cm3 K mol−1, and Weiss constants θ = −2.09 K, −1.98 K and −3.55 K, respectively. For 3, the thermal decreases of the χMT value might also originate from antiferromagnetic coupling between binuclear Gd and the zero-field split of CoII. For 4 and 5, only judging on the trends of the χMT values and the negative value of θ, it is impossible to unambiguously confirm an antiferromagnetic coupling due to the thermal depopulation of excited states and/or magnetic anisotropy of both TbIII/DyIII and CoIII.

The field dependencies of magnetization (M) for 4 and 5 have been tested at 2, 3 and 5 K in the range of 0–7 T and M vs. H/T curves are given in Fig. 11. For 4, below 5 K, magnetization rapidly increases at low magnetic fields, while at higher fields, magnetization increases up to 13.88 Nβ at 2 K and 8 T but does not reach the expected saturation value. For 5, M values rapidly increase at low magnetic fields. However, at higher fields, the M value increases slowly to reach a maximum value of 14.87 Nβ at 8 T, which is less than the saturation value. Those unsaturated phenomena in 4 and 5 indicate the presence of magnetic anisotropy and/or low-lying excited states in the system.

Temperature dependencies of the alternating-current (ac) magnetic susceptibility for 4 and 5 were collected at zero direct-current (dc) field with an oscillation of 3 Oe between 111 and 2311 Hz. Plots of χM′ vs. T and χM′′ vs. T are given in Fig. S4, ESI,† and Fig. 12. For 4, the intensities of out of phase signals is very weak compared with that of the in phase signals, so no conspicuous frequency dependence was observed. While for 5, overtly, frequency dependent out of phase signals are observed and indicate slow magnetization relaxation, which might be the signature of an SMM (single molecule magnet) behavior. Although SMM behavior of Dy–Co complexes is often investigated,26 SMM behavior is relatively rarely observed in 3D MOF systems.27

Conclusion

In summary, a series of Ln–Co heterometallic organic framework containing a three-dimensional 4,4-connected PtS-type topology have been synthesized. The study of their photophysical properties show that complexes 2, 4 and 5 display characteristic lanthanide-centered luminescence with low quantum efficiency, presumably due to the crowded energy level structure of these transition metal ions or the presence of water molecules. Besides, ac magnetic susceptibility shows that complex 5 may be a single molecule magnet. The properties and functionalities of such compounds could be tuned by the appropriate choice of different transition metal ions and lanthanide cations. This continuous research is currently going on in our group.
