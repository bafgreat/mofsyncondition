1. Introduction

Microporous metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) that have crystalline structures and that are typically characterized by large internal surface areas, uniform but tunable cavities and tailorable chemistry show great promise for a myriad of applications, including gas storage,1,2 chemical separation,3 magnetism,4 sensing5 and drug delivery.6 Their rich structural diversity, generally controlled by the weak and dynamic supramolecular contacts such as hydrogen bonding, π⋯π stacking, van der Waals forces and electrostatic or dipole interactions, enables access to pores of variable sizes7,8 or fine-tuning of their physical behavior by the penetration of guest molecules and tuning of the host–guest interaction.9 Owing to the relatively weak and dynamic host–guest interactions, guest-modified MOF materials show reversibility, allowing convenient dissociation and reconstruction of supramolecular systems at low energy cost.10 Understanding the host–guest interactions in these systems is crucial if new generations of improved materials are to be developed. However, the process of guest encapsulation is still vague, although the single-crystal X-ray diffraction technique has been used in the study of dynamic structural changes.11–13

Recently, there has been escalating interest in the encapsulation of various guests into the cavity of MOFs, and the resulting guest-dependent MOFs can be used as platforms for host–guest chemistry studies.14–16 In guest-dependent MOF systems, the guest molecule is incorporated as an entity into the cavity of MOFs via pore breathing,17 chemical vapor deposition,18,19 solvothermal deposition20 or cation exchange,21 endowing the composites with excellent performance in catalysis, gas adsorption, magnetism, etc.14,16,22 Despite the success, these methods are usually restricted by the window size of MOFs, resulting in inaccessibility for larger sized molecules and possible leaching for smaller sized molecules. Co-assembly of the guest during the crystal growth of MOFs offers a solution to the above-mentioned problem.23,24 More recently, Tsung's group introduced a new concept for incorporating large guest molecules as an entity into MOFs via expanded apertures created by dissociative linker exchange.25 However, for the co-assembly approach, the addition of guest molecules would probably disrupt the formation of the expected MOFs in the self-assembly process due to the coordination or template effect. The resulting MOFs are generally not isostructural to the parent MOF, making it hard to predict or manipulate the outcome of encapsulation. Herein, we report a versatile strategy to introduce not only large guest molecules into microporous MOFs but also distorted cavities maintaining the parent framework through one-fell-swoop assembly of metal ions, organic linkers and the expected guest molecules. To address this issue, we adopt a thermodynamics-directed approach via in situ microcalorimetry,26 based on the same reaction system as the original MOF, for the encapsulation of more guest molecules of larger size into the MOF pores and remarkably distorted cavities. These strategies might offer new insights into such host–guest systems and create new interest in developing more general and facile approaches for the preparation of these types of composites with intriguing properties.

In this contribution, we successfully prepared a 3D flexible MOF, [Co3(OH)2(L)2]·4H2O (1·4H2O) (H2L = benzotriazole-5-carboxylic acid), featuring 1D nanosized rhombic channels with approximately 12 × 7 Å2 after exclusion of the van der Waals radii of the surface atoms. Then, we employed [Co3(OH)2(L)2] (1) as the host and ethanol and N,N-dimethyl formamide (DMF) as the model guest molecules, respectively. Expectedly, 1 exhibits pore breathing capability due to the framework expansion and contraction upon uptake and release, transforming into [Co3(OH)2(L)2]·EtOH (1·EtOH). Also, [Co3(OH)2(L)2]·2DMF (2·2DMF) was solvothermally attained at higher temperature, resulting in the elongation of the crystal unit via the b axis during the reconstruction of the framework and the dramatic change in the Co–O bond length and the Co–O–Co angle. Employing in situ microcalorimetry, for 1·EtOH and 2·2DMF, the pore-breathing processes and one-fell-swoop assembly were monitored online, respectively. The results would provide some detailed thermodynamics support for understanding the subtle interaction between the guest molecule and the host framework and the assembly mechanism.26–28 Interestingly, such geometrical or structural changes in the encapsulated DMF guest molecules dictated by Co-MOFs are found to directly lead to the change in magnetism and adsorption properties depending on the nature of the guest (Scheme 1). Moreover, the synthetic approach outlined here can likely be applied to other flexible MOFs, enabling materials scientists to design fascinating adsorbents and magnets for specific applications.

2. Results and discussion

2.1 Microcalorimetry information on the synthesis process

Solvothermal reactions of CoSO4·6H2O and benzotriazole-5-carboxylic acid (H2L) in a 5 : 2 molar ratio in mixture solvents (VDMF/VH2O = 5 : 3) at 150 °C for 72 h yielded [Co3(OH)2(L)2]·4H2O (1·4H2O).29 [Co3(OH)2(L)2]·EtOH (1·EtOH) was attained by a pore breathing approach, where dehydrated samples 1 were immersed in enough ethanol. To confirm the SC–SC transformation, we conducted a TG-IR study (Fig. S2, ESI†). The TGA trace of the as-synthesized 1·4H2O in nitrogen shows that the trapped solvent water molecules can be easily removed at 95 °C, while losing ethanol at 75 °C for 1·EtOH (Fig. S3, ESI†). For 1·EtOH, the first step shows a sharp maximum of the Gram–Schmidt signal, which is positioned at 95 °C, and the signals at 2899 and 1048 cm−1 could be attributed to ethanol.

To attain the larger pore sized Co-MOF, we thermodynamically monitored the self-assembly processes online by using a Setaram C80 microcalorimeter (Fig. S4, ESI†).30 In this contribution, a mixture of H2L (3.0 mg), CoSO4·6H2O (7.2 mg), N,N-dimethylformamide (1.2 mL), and H2O (0.8 mL) was prepared with mild stirring, which was sealed in a C80 membrane mixing vessel (volume 12.5 mL) in an air atmosphere and then heated from 25 to 200 °C at a 0.5 °C min−1 heating rate. There are two obvious large endothermic peaks at 25–200 °C (150.13 and 179.64 °C). It can be seen from these data that the difference between the temperatures of the two peaks is greater than 20 °C, that is, it has good operability to control oriented syntheses through temperature. Furthermore, the intensive exothermic peaks mentioned above imply that the new phases are generated as the temperature increases, and the phase at a higher temperature (179.64 °C), whose structure ([Co3(OH)2(L)2]·2DMF) is further confirmed by single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis, released less heat than that in the construction process (Table 1). Undoubtedly, [Co3(OH)2(L)2]·2DMF (2·2DMF) was expected to be solvothermally prepared in the same reaction system at 180 °C. TGA of 2·2DMF displays a weight loss of 22.4% from the ambient temperature to 253 °C under a N2 atmosphere (Fig. S3, ESI†), corresponding to the release of two DMF molecules per formula unit (calc. 21.5%), and that the resulting desolvated MOF is stable up to 430 °C.

According to Fig. S4 (ESI†), the reaction heat of these two peaks is different, which may be due to the self-assembly process or the hydrogen-bond number and intensity connecting the framework and guest molecules. The large values and negative signs of the molar reaction enthalpy ΔH θm (−409.6 ± 0.9 kJ mol−1 (2·2DMF) and −625.6 ± 0.6 kJ mol−1 (1·4H2O)) indicate that the crystalline reactions are exothermic (Table 1). For the same systems, the value of the molar formation enthalpy of 2·2DMF is more than that of 1·4H2O, showing that the thermodynamic stability of compound 1·4H2O is superior to that of 2·2DMF. Along with static structure determination, it is noted that the energy needed to form hydrogen bonds among the framework and DMF molecules is obviously larger than that between the framework and H2O molecules. As for 2·2DMF and 1·EtOH, the entropy changes are very small because they possess similar host frameworks in the guest encapsulation reaction, which is thermodynamically explained as an enthalpy-dominated process.

2.2 Structural description

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that [Co3(OH)2(L)2]·2DMF (2·2DMF) crystallizes in the monoclinic C2/c space group, and the asymmetric unit consists of two crystallographically independent Co(ii) ions, one L2− deprotonated ligand and one μ3-OH group. As shown in Fig. 1a, Co1(ii) features an octahedral geometry constructed by two sets of symmetrical triazole nitrogen atoms (N2A and N2B), carboxylate oxygen atoms (O1 and O1C) and μ3-OH oxygen atoms (O3A and O3B). The Co2(ii) ion is five-coordinated by two nitrogen atoms (N1A and N3) from two different L2− ligands, one carboxylate oxygen atom (O2) and two μ3-OH oxygen groups (O3 and O3B), giving rise to a slightly distorted square pyramidal coordination geometry. The Co1–O bond distances are approximately equal [2.078(6) and 2.080(5) Å], while the Co2–O separations are diverse from each other [2.012(6), 2.157(6) and 2.018(6) Å]. The Co1–N bond distance [2.146(7) Å] is slightly longer than the Co2–N separations [2.082(7) and 2.069(7) Å]. The Co1A–O–Co2, Co1–O–Co2, and Co2A–O–Co2 bond angles are 106.3(2), 117.0(3), and 101.1(2)° (A: −x + 2, −y + 2, −z), respectively (Table S3, ESI†). Each μ3-OH group bridges two Co2(ii) ions and a Co1(ii) ion and each Co(ii) center is ligated by two μ3-OH groups, leading to a 1D [Co3(OH)2] chain (Fig. 1c). The deprotonated L2− ligands link adjacent [Co3(OH)2] chains in a μ5-bridging fashion to form a 3D architecture with 1D rhombic channels (Fig. 1b and d), in which the DMF molecules are located, via strong O–H⋯O hydrogen-bond interactions between DMF and μ3-OH groups (Table S4, ESI†). For the rhombic channel, the shortest [Co3(OH)2] interchain distance is 11.00 Å, and the diagonal distances are 13.31 Å and 17.52 Å, respectively (Fig. 1e). Without guest solvent molecules, the total accessible volume in 2·2DMF is estimated to be 44.2% using PLATON software.31

For 1·4H2O, the whole topological structure and bonding fashions between metal ions and ligands are identical to those of 2·2DMF (Fig. S9, ESI†), whereas the unit cell is very different, especially the b axis of the unit cell shrinks significantly (from 13.309(9) Å in 2·2DMF to 11.523(6) Å in 1·4H2O) owing to the lower preparation temperature and smaller water molecule occupation in the channel (Table S2, ESI†). Such shrinkage makes all the Co–N and Co–O bond distances in 1·4H2O shorter than the corresponding ones in 2·2DMF. Most of the Co–O bond distances fall in the small range of 2.006(4)–2.074(4) Å, but the Co2–O1B bond distance is much longer, 2.146(4) Å (B: −x, −y + 2, −z). Similarly, the Co2–N bond distances [2.061(5) and 2.074(5) Å] are much shorter than the Co1–N bond length, 2.124(5) Å (Table S3, ESI†). Notably, the Co1–O–Co2B bond angle decreases to 116.20(18)°, but the Co2–O–Co1 bond angle increases to 108.34(17)° instead (Table S5, ESI†). The 1D rhombic channels encapsulate guest water molecules, giving the shortest [Co3(OH)2] interchain distance of 10.84 Å, and the diagonal distances of 11.52 Å and 18.37 Å, respectively (Fig. S27, ESI†). The total accessible volume also reduces to 37.4% when eliminating the solvent molecules. For 1·EtOH, it was isolated by impregnating the desolvated compound of 1·4H2O in EtOH (Fig. S8, ESI†), and all the structural characteristics of 1·4H2O are retained. Only subtle configuration self-adjustment occurs, because there is no cleavage and reformation of the chemical bonds during the synthesis. The detailed comparisons of geometric parameters are seen in Fig. S27, S28 and Tables S5, S6 (ESI†).

2.3 Effect of guest molecule incorporation on structures

In most cases, the incorporation of guest molecules into the host framework does not bring intensive changes in the physical and chemical properties from the host framework. However, the addition of guest molecules in the self-assembly process would probably disrupt the formation of the expected MOF framework due to the distortion of the coordination geometry or the template effect.20,21 As shown in Fig. S5 (ESI†), the biggest difference observed in the PXRD between samples 1·4H2O and 2·2DMF is that the main peaks of 2·2DMF shift toward the lower angles as a result of the elongation of the crystal unit via the b axis during the reconstruction of the framework (13.309 Å for 2·2DMF and 11.523 Å for 1·4H2O). Differently, the PXRD pattern of EtOH soaked in the desolvated sample of compound 1·4H2O and the single-crystal simulated pattern of 1·EtOH are in excellent agreement, implying the incorporation of ethanol into the cavity of 1·4H2O. Thus, the host matrix gets distorted at different extent through the synthesis approach, the size and number of guests. For 1·4H2O, 2·2DMF and 1·EtOH, PXRD analyses show that the three-dimensional (3D) frameworks remain intact upon dehydration, confirming the phase purity. Compared with compound 1·4H2O, Co1–Co2B bond lengths are slightly stretched by 0.041 Å and 0.067 Å, and Co1–O–Co2A angles are limited to 1.1° and 2.0° for 1·EtOH and 2·2DMF, respectively (Table S5, ESI†). The changes in bond lengths and bond angles in 2·2DMF are obviously greater than those in 1·EtOH, in good agreement with the PXRD results, probably bringing about fascinating magnetism. PLATON calculation31 reveals that the free volume of the channels in the desolvated compound of 1·EtOH is expanded to 938 Å3 per unit cell or 38.9% of the total volume, while the free volume is 1147 Å3 for the desolvated compound of 2·2DMF (void ratio = 44.2%), showing promising properties for gas or cation capture (Table S6, ESI†).

2.4 Gas adsorption performance of 2·2DMF

The gas sorption experiments confirmed the permanent porosity of 2 (the desolvated sample of 2·2DMF), which reveals type-I sorption isotherms for N2 and H2 at 77 K and CO2 at 195 K with loadings of 162.2, 126.8 and 154.9 cm3 g−1 at 100 kPa, respectively (Fig. 2). On the basis of the CO2 sorption isotherm, a BET surface area of 566 m2 g−1 (Langmuir surface area 605 m2 g−1) is available; the median pore diameter of 3.9 Å according to the Horvath–Kawazoe model coincides with that obtained from the crystal structure. The relatively lower adsorption amounts of 2 for CO2 compared to N2 can be attributed to the large molecular sizes of CO2 (CO2, 3.4 × 3.3 × 5.4 Å3; N2, 3.1 × 3.1 × 4.0 Å3),32 and as a result, some voids in 2 are inaccessible for CO2 molecules. This phenomenon reveals that gas sorption is a physics-dominated process, different from the common observations that MOFs adsorb more CO2 due to a smaller kinetic diameter of CO2 (3.3 Å) than N2 (3.64 Å).33

To further evaluate the sorption properties of 2, the CO2 and CH4 sorption isotherms were also measured at 273 K and 298 K, respectively. At 273 K, 2 exhibits high uptake for CO2 with 99.6 cm3 g−1 at 100 kPa, while only surface sorption for CH4 (28.7 cm3 g−1) is observed (Fig. 2). Measurements of CH4 and CO2 sorption of 2 at 298 K (Fig. 2) show that 2 has low CH4 uptake (18.4 cm3 g−1), however, at a high CO2 loading of 68.0 cm3 g−1 (13.4 wt%), similar to those of SNU-50 (13.7 wt%)34 and CuBTTRi (14.3 wt%).35 The value is considerably higher than those reported for some well-known, high surface area MOFs and ZIFs, including MOF-5 and MOF-177.36 The result suggests the advantages of the small cavity in 2 for CO2 capture owing to the multiple potential field overlaps with the pore walls.37 Besides the confined cavity, the coordinated –OH groups in the {Co3(OH)2} chain in pores can also produce strong interactions with CO2, such as through O–H⋯OCO hydrogen bonds. To evaluate the affinity of 2 for CO2, the adsorption enthalpies (Qst) were calculated using the virial method by fitting sorption isotherms of CO2 at 273 and 298 K (Fig. S1a, ESI†). The Qst for CO2 is around 22.9–27.0 kJ mol−1 in the whole CO2 coverage region (Fig. S1b, ESI†). These materials can be used as promising MOFs for CO2 capture from flue gas.

2.5 Effect of guest molecule incorporation on gas sorption

As shown in the inset of Fig. S9 and S10(†), the desolvated samples of 1·4H2O and 1·EtOH display low N2 uptake values of 33.8 cm3 g−1 and 37.1 cm3 g−1 at 77 K, respectively. For 2·2DMF, the calculated Langmuir surface area is 4–5 times larger than the two above-mentioned desolvated samples (the Langmuir surface area is 127 m2 g−1 and 146 m2 g−1 for desolvated samples of 1·4H2O and 1·EtOH), due to the size of the guest molecules and incorporation approaches, in agreement with the result of PXRD patterns.

The selectivity and uptake capacity of adsorption are determined by interactions between the adsorbates and their porous host materials. For 1·4H2O and 1·EtOH, the frameworks are very similar, but their void ratios are slightly different (Table S6, ESI†). Compared with the two above-mentioned compounds, the crystal unit via the b axis in 2·2DMF is larger (Table S2, ESI†), which is further verified by PXRD, implying that the host framework is also different from the others due to the effect of contraction after desolvation. The results inspired us to study the adsorption heat in order to elucidate the effect of guest molecules on the adsorption capacity of porous materials.

We monitored the absorption processes using in situ microcalorimetry online and quantitatively obtained the apparent energy change of host–guest interaction using a RD496-III type microcalorimeter.12,38,39 In the case of the determination of the heat of absorption, the guest molecules DMF/H2O/EtOH and the corresponding desolvated samples were placed at the upper and the lower ends of the glass tube. When the calorimeter was in the equilibrium state, the guest molecule was dropped into the lower end of the glass tube and kept soaking the sample. The adsorption process continued until the heat flow curve returned to the baseline. Based on the area of the heat flow curve, both values of ΔH(adsorption) for the desolvated samples of 2·2DMF (−29.87 ± 0.4 kJ mol−1) are more than twice those for 1·4H2O (−12.56 ± 0.7 kJ mol−1) and 1·EtOH (−14.35 ± 0.9 kJ mol−1) (Fig. S6, ESI,† and Table 2). The results indicate that the three processes mainly involve hydrogen-bond interaction (−ΔH in 5–30 kJ mol−1), and the cavity of 2·2DMF is more liable to uptake guest molecules than others.40,41 Variation in the MOF pore size influences the host–guest and guest–guest interactions and hence the equilibrium adsorption capacity. As expected, the desolvated samples of 2·2DMF possess fascinating gas adsorption capacity from intensive hydrogen-bond interaction.

2.6 Cr(vi) uptake, kinetics, and isotherm

As for 2·2DMF, the fascinating CO2 adsorption results inspired us to evaluate Cr(vi) uptake from its water adsorption capacity. The role of pH in an adsorption process is to influence the charge density on the adsorbent surface and to determine the existing species of the adsorbate in solution.42 Evaluation of the pH effect on Cr(vi) adsorption by 2·2DMF revealed that the process is dependent on the pH parameter in the experimental pH range (1–10). Except for the highly acidic environment, where most of the MOFs are apt to be decomposed, 2·2DMF adsorbed mainly CrO42− over a wide range of pH values (4–10) (Fig. 2c). Time-course adsorption measurements further indicated that Cr(vi) capture by 2·2DMF is kinetically efficient (Fig. S12, ESI†), with a pseudo-second-order adsorption rate constant of 0.112 g mg−1 min−1 that reaches equilibrium within ∼5 min (Fig. S13, ESI†). The adsorption capacity of Cr(vi) onto 2·2DMF increased with an increase in time and nearly 95% of the removal rate of Cr(vi) was attained in 5 min. This time required for Cr(vi) adsorption is much faster than traditional adsorbents such as activated carbon and molecular-sieves, which need several hours to reach better removal percentage.43–45

We assessed the overall Cr(vi) adsorption capacity of 2·2DMF from fitting of adsorption isotherms collected after equilibrating the MOF with a wide range of Cr(vi) levels (1–90 mg L−1, Fig. 2d). In order to derive an appropriate correlation between the experimental and theoretical data, the Freundlich model46 was applied (R2 = 0.992), which is represented by eqn (S5) (ESI†). The Freundlich isotherm is applicable to actual adsorption with all chemical and physical equivalent adsorptions (Fig. S14, ESI†). The Freundlich exponent 1/n of 0.64 (0.1–1) was obtained, which implies that the adsorption process is easy to perform. Furthermore, the binding energy constant kf of 52.5 indicates that the saturation capacity is higher (125 mg g−1) than most MOFs reported in the references, such as FIR-53 (74 mg g−1) and FIR-54 (103 mg g−1).46,47 For 1·4H2O and 1·EtOH with a similar host framework, the maximum adsorption capacities obtained are 65 mg g−1 and 72 mg g−1 at 298 K, respectively, which are equal to that of FIR-53 (Fig. S15, ESI†). The greater differences might have derived from the formation of new electrostatic interactions between the oxygen of the carbonyl from DMF during the adsorption process. The presence of Cr(vi) species was confirmed by FT-IR spectroscopy. The FT-IR spectrum of 2·2DMF shows a band at 1658 cm−1 corresponding to a DMF molecule before adsorption (Fig. S16, ESI†). Moreover, there is a new band at 898 cm−1 after Cr(vi) adsorption, which can be attributed to the Cr–O stretching vibration (Fig. S16, ESI†),47,48 verifying the assumption. To confirm the adsorption mechanism, we conducted a microcalorimetry study. The molar enthalpy ΔH(adsorption) is −47.29 ± 0.5 kJ mol−1, which might be due to the formation of chemical bonds (−ΔH > 40 kJ mol−1). With this performance, compound 2·2DMF might be applicable for the capture of Cr(vi) from wastewater.

2.7 Magnetic properties

The variable temperature magnetic susceptibility data for a powdered crystalline sample of 2·2DMF were measured at a field of 1000 Oe over the 2–300 K temperature range (Fig. S17, ESI†). The χMT value (where χM is the molar magnetic susceptibility) at 300 K is 7.37 cm3 K mol−1 per Co3 unit for 2·2DMF, which lies within the range of 2.1–3.4 cm3 K mol−1 for experimentally observed highly anisotropic CoII ions.49,50 Upon lowering the temperature, the χMT value decreases gradually to a minimum of 4.62 cm3 K mol−1 at 45 K, which could be ascribed to anisotropy effects from CoII ions;51,52 it then abruptly increases to a sharp maximum of 39.81 cm3 K mol−1 at 10 K, after which it drops rapidly up to 2 K. Such a minimum suggests the appearance of ferrimagnetic-like interactions, which likely generates within the linear trimeric clusters.53 The χM−1versus T curve above 45 K follows the Curie–Weiss law with C = 8.63 cm3 K mol−1 and θ = –54.4 K for 2·2DMF (Fig. S18, ESI†). The large negative value of the Weiss constant (θ) elucidates that the antiferromagnetic coupling is dominant within the chain.54

The magnetic behavior of 2·2DMF can be simulated as a –J1J1J2– alternating chain. As shown in (Fig. 3a), in the Con alternating chain there are two sets of connection between the spin centers presenting the ratio of 2 : 1, where J1 is the intrachain coupling between Co1 and Co2 ions and J2 is the intrachain coupling between Co2 ions. To evaluate intra-chain coupling constants J1 and J2, the expression can be deduced using the spin Hamiltonian ( ).55 The magnetic data above 30 K can be fitted using the above-mentioned equation, and the best fit gives parameters: g = 2.481, J1 = −3.9 cm−1 and J2 = 7.3 cm−1 (Fig. 3b). In addition, we fitted the magnetic data of per CoII by using the above-mentioned alternating 1D Heisenberg chain (S = 3/2) model with an orbital correction term θ.29 The fitting resulted in a set of parameters: g = 2.450, J1 = −21.55 cm−1, J2 = 27.51 cm−1 and θ = −51.4(7) K (Fig. S19, ESI†). Both the fitting results above show that the value of J2 is greater than that of 1·4H2O (J2 = 2.1 cm−1), which implies that the ferromagnetic coupling increases.29,55,56

The field dependence of magnetization was determined at 1.8 K, 3.0 K and 5.0 K in the field range of 0–5 T (Fig. S20, ESI†). The magnetization vs. field measurement at 1.8 K (Fig. S21, ESI†) displays a characteristic S-shape, indicating a transition from a state with no net spin to a paramagnetic state at 1700 Oe. This field is directly related to the antiferromagnetic interaction that leads to magnetic transition. The M vs. H curve shows that the magnetization first increases steeply in the initial low field, and then increases slowly up to 2.49 Nβ at 50 kOe without achieving saturation,57 far below the common saturation value of three CoII centers (at 2 K, the effective spin is S = 1/2), exhibiting the existence of large anisotropy and AF interaction.53 The magnetization versus H/T plots at different temperatures cannot be superimposed, indicating the magnetic anisotropy and/or low-lying excited states.58 Magnetization hysteresis is another important characteristic of the magnetic bistability of a magnet, so the direct-current (dc) magnetization is measured at 1.8 and 5 K within ± 40 kOe (Fig. S22 and S23, ESI†). At 1.8 K, 2·2DMF exhibits a hysteresis loop that shows the double sigmoidal shape typical of metamagnetism, depending on the anisotropy of the CoII ion.53,59 Remarkably, the compound behaves as a hard magnet with a relatively small coercive field of 350 Oe and a remnant magnetization of 0.73 Nβ (Fig. S21, ESI†). At 5 K, 2·2DMF affords a weak butterfly-shaped hysteresis loop (Fig. S23, ESI†). In this sense, as the temperature is elevated, the hysteresis loop drastically diminishes and almost disappears, indicating that the material is transformed from a hard to a very soft magnet.60,61

To explore the low-temperature magnetic behavior of 2·2DMF, field-cooled (FC) and zero-field-cooled (ZFC) magnetization data were measured under a 10 Oe applied field (Fig. 3c). The divergence below 5.3 K for FC and ZFC magnetic susceptibility curves demonstrates the history dependence of the magnetization process.62,63 The specific heat capacity measurements provide further evidence (Fig. 3d). The curve under zero field affords an explicit λ-type anomaly about 5 K, confirming the existence of a 3D long-range ordering state due to intermolecular exchanges.

To probe into the possibility of single-chain magnet (SCM) behaviors of 2·2DMF, the alternating-current (ac) magnetic susceptibilities versus temperature measurements were carried out in a zero applied dc field with an oscillating 2.0 Oe ac field in the temperature range of 1.8–15 K. As shown in Fig. 4, both the real χ′ and the imaginary χ′′ components of the ac susceptibility show a frequency dependent cusp. This fact indicates a cooperative freezing of the individual magnetic moments, characteristic of spin glasses, superparamagnets or single-chain magnets.64,65 The frequency dependence of the ac susceptibility was studied using the equation Φ = ΔTf/[TfΔ(log ω)], where Tf is the freezing temperature and ω is the frequency, and the estimated value of Φ for 2·2DMF is 0.076, which lies within the normal value (Φ ≤ 0.08) for canonical spin glass behavior.66 Furthermore, the dynamics of the magnetization of 2·2DMF has been studied by measuring the frequency dependencies of the alternating-current (ac) susceptibility. The χ′ and χ′′ signals of 2·2DMF show frequency dependencies under an applied dc field of 0 Oe (Fig. 5). Fitting the frequency dependency data to the generalized Debye equation gives temperature-dependent relaxation times, τ (Table S1, ESI†). A plot of ln(τ) against 1/T shows one pseudo-linear section and fitting the data with the Arrhenius equation gives Ueff = 59.9 K, τ0 = 1.9 × 10−12 s (Fig. S24, ESI†). The relaxation time τ0 is in the range of 10−10–10−13 s for typical spin glass (SG) behavior.66

To study the solvatomagnetic effect, the magnetic properties of 2 and 1·EtOH were also investigated. The χMT versus T curves of 2 and 1·EtOH show a shape similar to that of 2·2DMF (Fig. S25, ESI†). For 2 and 1·EtOH, the magnetization versus field measurement at 1.8 K displays no characteristic metamagnetism (1.80 Nβ for 2 and 2.07 Nβ for 1·EtOH), quite different from those of 2·2DMF and 1·4H2O (Fig. S26, ESI†). In consideration of similar H/T plots of 2 and 1·EtOH, we take 2 as an example for ac magnetic susceptibility investigation. The isostructural nature of the four frameworks (2·2DMF, 2, 1·EtOH and 1·4H2O29), and especially the very similar core structures of the trinuclear nodes, might be anticipated to give rise to similar magnetization dynamics at low temperature. However, for the evacuated MOF 2, although both the real χ′ and the imaginary χ′′ components are present in the ac susceptibility measurement, both of the real χ′ component and the imaginary χ′′ component show very weak frequency dependence (Fig. 4c and d), indicating the dominance of different magnetization dynamics from 2·2DMF, 1·4H2O to 129 depending on the guest molecules present within the pores and the pore size.

Based on the summarized data of 2·2DMF and 1·4H2O in Table S4 (ESI†), the Co⋯Co distance (intramolecular), the Co⋯Co distance (intermolecular) and the Co–O–Co angle in 2·2DMF are in the range of these reported Co-MOFs. In detail, these Co⋯Co distances (intramolecular and intermolecular) of 2·2DMF are larger than previously reported 1·4H2O29 with a similar framework, probably induced by the presence of the size of guest molecules (dDMF > dH2O). Indeed, the Co–O–Co angle affected by the μ2-O bridge will modify the overlap between the magnetic orbitals of CoII and therefore will influence the intradinuclear magnetic interactions, although such interactions are expected to be very weak. Notably, the value of the Co1–O–Co2A angle of 2·2DMF is smaller than that of 1·4H2O, while the coordination geometries undergo distortion on account of the elongation of the crystal unit via b axis. Thus, 2·2DMF affords a lower energy barrier of 59.9 K with a pre-exponential factor (τ0) of 1.9 × 10−12 s under a zero applied field than 1·4H2O(an energy barrier of 122.6 K with a pre-exponential factor (τ0) of 2.6 × 10−11 s), resulting from the presence of slightly different coordination geometries. In other words, the coordination geometry, together with the exchange interactions between them (the intramolecular distance of Co⋯Co, the intermolecular distance of Co⋯Co and the Co–O–Co angle) induced by the μ2-O bridges, has an influence on the dynamic magnetic behavior of Co-MOFs.67 The intriguing roles of magnetic exchange-coupling and dipole–dipole interactions between the Co ions, effected by the intramolecular distance, the intermolecular distance and the angle, are helpful to further understand the mechanism and source of SCMs.

3. Conclusion

In conclusion, we have demonstrated an effective strategy to encapsulate various guest molecules within a Co-MOF, [Co3(OH)2(L)2]·4H2O (1·4H2O), in a controllable way, generating two Co-MOF materials (1·EtOHand 2·2DMF) endowed with different adsorption and magnetic properties. Notably, the encapsulation process in 2·2DMF, followed by single-crystal X-ray diffraction, visually outlines a putative role of guest molecules by inducing “non-innocent” distortions in the cavity geometry and, ultimately, leaving a “fingerprint” in the cobalt environments. To understand the self-assembly regularity of 2·2DMF, in situ microcalorimetry reveals that isostructural transformations are enthalpy-dominated processes. These results would open up new possibilities for the isostructural transformation reaction mechanism and the accurate guide syntheses using in situ microcalorimetry. Nitrogen-sorption isotherm experiments demonstrate that the calculated Langmuir surface area of 2·2DMF is nearly 5 times more than those of 1·4H2O (127 m2 g−1) and 1·EtOH (146 m2 g−1), in agreement with the results of distorted cavities. Due to the two DMF molecules in the cavity, 2·2DMF (125 mg g−1) displays much higher capability for the capture of Cr(vi) than both 1·4H2O (65 m2 g−1) and 1·EtOH (72 m2 g−1) from wastewater by the formation of a new electrostatic interaction between the oxygen of carbonyl from DMF and Cr(vi). Moreover, the fitting results of magnetic data show that the value of J2 in 2·2DMF is greater than 1·4H2O, which implies that the ferromagnetic coupling increases. A comparison of the above-mentioned Co-MOFs shows that they exhibit distinct slow magnetic relaxation (Ueff = 59.9 K of 2·2DMF; Ueff = 122.6 K of 1·4H2O) due to the intramolecular distance of the Co⋯Co bond length, the Co–O–Co angle and the subtly distorted cavity. In principle, this strategy can be extended to a wide range of MOFs·nG (G = guest)—provided the appropriate guest molecules are present—thus generating a family of MOF materials endowed with fine-tuned structural and functional properties.
