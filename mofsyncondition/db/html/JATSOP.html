Two unique isomorphic Zn2 + and Co2 +-based metal-organic frameworks comprising octahedral cage

Highlights

•

Two unique isomorphic transition-metal-based coordination polymers have been synthesized and characterized.

•

Both compounds exhibit a 2-D square-grid layer and one-dimensional rhombus channel along the c axis.

•

Compound 1 can serve as a highly sensitive luminescent sensors for the detection of Fe3 + ion in DMAc solution.

Abstract

Two Zn2 + and Co2 +-based metal-organic frameworks, [Zn4(L)4(4,4′-bpy)(H2O)2] 2H2O 2CH3CN (1) and [Co4(L)4(4,4′-bpy)(H2O)2].2H2O (2), have been synthesized hydrothermally from mixed ligands of H2L (4,4′-dicarboxy-4″-nitrotriphenyl-amine) and 4,4′-bpy (4,4′-bipyridine). Single-crystal X-ray crystallography reveals that compounds 1 and 2 are isomorphic both featuring a 2-D square-grid layer assembled from one-dimensional cage-connected-cage chain and 4,4′-bpy pillar. Both compounds exhibits one-dimensional rhombus channel with the dimension of about 14.12 × 7.62 Å2 along the c axis. Compound 1 shows strong photoluminescence which has the capability of selective sensing of Fe3 + ion in DMAc.

Metal-organic frameworks (MOFs) or porous coordination polymers (PCPs), a new class of crystalline materials, continue to attract immense attention owing to their charming architectures and potential applications [1], [2], [3], [4], which are typically self-assembled through inorganic nodes and organic linkers. Amongst them, cage-based MOFs are of special interest as functional materials for gas separations [5], [6], [7] and sensor [8], [9], [10], [11], which largely benefits from their intrinsic porosity of cage. Compared to the rigid or flexible organic ligands, the semi-rigid ones have many advantages to construct the cage-based MOFs because their inherent flexuous nature offers additional options to tune the structures [12], [13], [14], [15], [16]. In this regard, semi-rigid triphenylamine-centered polycarboxylate ligands (such as 4,4′-(phenylazanediyl)dibenzoic acid and 4,4′,4″-tricarboxytriphenylamine) have recently raised considerable attention owing to their advantages for constructing topologically novel frameworks and luminescent MOFs [17], [18], [19], [20], [21]. Moreover, the employment of auxiliary ligands has also been shown to be an effective method to impart greater tunability of the resultant crystal structure than those with single ligand [17].

In this work, we have successfully obtained two isomorphic octahedral-cage-based metal-organic frameworks, namely [Zn4(L)4(4,4′-bpy)(H2O)2] 2H2O 2CH3CN (1) and [Co4(L)4(4,4′-bpy)(H2O)2].2H2O (2), by use of mixed ligands of H2L (4,4′-dicarboxy-4″-nitrotriphenyl-amine) and 4,4′-bpy (4,4′-bipyridine) (Scheme 1). Although some similar ligands including 4,4′-dicarboxydiphenylamine [22], [23], 4,4′-(hexafluoroisopropylidene)bis(benzoic acid) [24], 4,4′-dicarboxy-ethyl diphenylamine [24], 4,4′-diacarboxybiphenyl-sulfone [24], [25], 4,4′-oxybis(benzoic acid) [24], [26], 4,4′-(phenylazanediyl)dibenzoic acid [17], [18], [27], [28] have been already extensively investigated for constructing MOFs materials with interesting 2D or 3D aesthetic architectures and functional properties (such as optical, catalytic and magnetic properties), there is no report that has been found on coordination polymers based on semi-rigid triphenylamine centered ligand of H2L. Especially, example of cage-based MOFs with these semi-rigid ligands is still rare as far as we know. Moreover, it is found that compound 1 exhibits high sensitivity and selectivity for luminescent detection of Fe3 + ion in DMAc solution.

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that compounds 1 and 2 are isomorphic, both crystallizing in the monoclinic system and space group C2/m. Thereby, only the structure of 1 is discussed as a representation.

As shown in Fig. 1a, the asymmetric unit of 1 contains two kinds of independent Zn(II) atoms adopting different coordination geometries. The Zn1 atom is hexacoordinated by four carboxylic O atoms from four L2 − ligands and two O atoms from two μ3-H2O to form a distorted octahedron geometry (Zn1-O 2.093–2.105 Å), whereas the Zn2 atom is a four-coordinated adopting a distorted tetrahedron geometry completed by two carboxylate O atoms and one O atom from μ3-H2O, and one N atom from 4,4′-bpy (Zn2-O 1.902–1.953 Å, Zn2N 2.011 Å). For L2 − ligand, the central N atom is sp2 hybridized as evidenced by unusually short NC bond lengths and CNC average angles of approximately 120° (CN: a.v. 1.417 Å, CNC: a.v. 119.97°), and the dihedral angle of three phenyl rings are 82.8°, 77.1° and 53.8° respectively. Meanwhile, the mean plane of the nitro group is also tilted by 17.5° to the attached benzene (Fig. S1). In 1, two carboxylate groups of the H2L ligand take different coordination modes (bis-monodentate coordination mode and a monodentate mode) (Fig. S2) to bridge four Zn atoms along with two μ3-water molecules, forming a tetranuclear [Zn4(H2O)2(CO2)8] secondary building units (SBUs) (Fig. S3).

Two neighboring SBUs are quadruply spaced by four L2 − ligands to form a octahedral cage (Figs. 1b–c) and the internal cavity are occupied by guest water and acetonitrile molecules. In a cage-connected-cage manner by sharing the common [Zn4(H2O)2(CO2)8] clusters, a 1-D beaded chain is formed extending along a axis (Fig. 1d). All 1-D chains are aligned in parallel and further pillared by 4,4′-bpy, leading to a 2-D square-grid layer (Fig. 1e). Within the 2-D layer, the uncoordinated nitro-substituted phenyl ring of the ligand protrudes from both side of the 2-D layer. Viewed along the c axis (Fig. 1f), compound 1 has one-dimensional rhombus channel of about 14.12 × 7.62 Å2, and the total solvent accessible volume of 1 is about 17.0% calculated by using the PLATON program. Finally, all 2-D layers are aggregated in …AAA…stacking fashion to give 3-D packing structure (Fig. 1f).

The phase purity of bulk samples was confirmed by PXRD measurement, which shows that the experimental pattern agrees well with the simulated one. Compounds 1 and 2 exhibits excellent hydrolytic robustness since these water-soaked samples of 1–2 show no significant change in PXRD pattern compared to those of the pristine samples (Figs. S4–S5).

The thermal stability of 1 and 2 was evaluated by TGA technique. For 1, an initial weight loss of 7.62% in the range of 55–200 °C corresponds to the loss of lattice water, acetonitrile and bridging water molecules (Calcd. 7.42%), and beyond 360 °C decomposition occurs (Fig. S6). For 2, the weight loss of 3.52% starts from 40 °C to 200 °C owing to the loss of lattice water molecules and bridging water molecules (Calcd. 3.66%) followed by a plateau in the range of 200–370 °C. Beyond 370 °C, a drastic weight loss happens (Fig. S7). In addition, variable-temperature PXRD indicates that both compounds can remain their framework integrity as high as 175 °C (Figs. S8–9).

Solid-state photoluminescence properties of 1–2 were investigated at room temperature. Compound 1 exhibits photoluminescence (λem = 564 nm, λex = 467 nm) (Fig. S10). Compare to the free H2L (λem = 521 nm, λex = 467 nm), a large red-shift was observed with 1, which may be ascribed to metal-to-ligand charge transfer (MLCT) or cluster-to-ligand charge transfer [29]. Compound 2 is non-luminescent largely because the unpaired electrons in d7 Co2 + atom of 2 quench the photoluminescence of the H2L ligand [30].

Furthermore, the photoluminescence properties of 1 suspended in the different solvents were examined. As depicted in Fig. S11, the photoluminescence intensity of 1 depends heavily on the identity of solvent, and an almost 22-fold enhancement was observed in DMAc solvent than in water. Therefore, we selected DMAc as medium to investigate the sensing ability of 1 towards to different metal ions.

In a fluorescence titration setup, the suspension (2.0 ml) of 1 was added to DMAc solution (0.85 ml) of different MCln (M = Pb2 +, Cr3 +, Cu2 +, Fe3 +, Zn2 +, Cd2 +, Co2 +, Ni2 +, Mn2 +, CM n + = 2 × 10− 2 mol/L). After the resultant solution is left standing for 5 h, the luminescence spectra were then recorded. As can be seen from Fig. 2, almost no obvious change in photoluminescence intensity of 1 were found for Pb2 +, Zn2 +, Cd2 +, Co2 +, Ni2 +, Mn2 + and slight decline in luminescence intensity was found for Cr3 +, Cu2 +. By sharp contrast, a drastic luminescence quenching was observed for Fe3 + ion. The fluorescence quenching efficiency was further analyzed using the Stern-Volmer (S-V) equation, (I0/I) = Ksv[M] + 1. As shown in Fig. S12, the fluorescence intensity of 1 drops with the increase in Fe3 + concentration, and shows a linear correlation (R = 0.9921, Ksv = 1.1 × 103 M− 1) with Fe3 + concentration below 3 mM. The Fe3 + detection limit of 1 was calculated to be 0.1 mM (S/N = 3).

The sensing selectivity of 1 towards Fe3 + in the presence of other interfering metal ion was further assessed. 0.85 ml of competing metal ion (Pb2 +, Cr3 +, Cu2 +, Zn2 +, Cd2 +, Co2 +, Ni2 +, Mn2 +, 2 × 10− 2 M) were added to the suspension containing 1 in DMAc, and the fluorescence spectra were recorded. Then this solution was treated by equivalent Fe3 + and the fluorescence spectra were recorded again. As shown in Fig. 2b, 1 still displays a significant fluorescence turn-off response towards Fe3 + even in the presence of other competing metal ion, which can avoids off-target reactivity and false response in a complex detection system.

In summary, two new isomorphic metal-organic frameworks [Zn4(L)4(4,4′-bpy)(H2O)2] 2H2O 2CH3CN (1) and [Co4(L)4(4,4′-bpy)(H2O)2] 2H2O (2), have been successfully synthesized by the self-assembly of semi-rigid ligand H2L and auxiliary ligands 4,4′-bpy under the same solvothermal condition. Both compounds show a 2-D layer structure built by 4,4′-bpy-pillared octahedral-cage-based 1-D beaded chain. Both compounds show good thermal stability and hydrolytic robustness. In addition, compound 1 shows strong photoluminescence in DMAc solution, and can selectively detect Fe3 + ion as a chemical sensor.
