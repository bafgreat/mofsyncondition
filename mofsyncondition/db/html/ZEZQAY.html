Introduction

In recent years, scientists have paid great attention to designing and synthesizing lanthanide based compounds due to their potential applications in magnetism,1 luminescent materials,2catalyst,3 and gas absorption.4 Lanthanide (Ln) ions have become good candidates for the preparation of SMMs because most of them have a large unquenched orbital angular momentum,5 which may bring significantly magnetic anisotropy to the system. Therefore, much effort is devoted to SMMs containing 4f ions, including 3d–4f and pure 4f systems, and many impressive SMMs have been reported.6–14 Nevertheless, most 3d–4f/4f compounds with SMM behaviors focused on the discrete clusters, and the investigations of two- and three-dimensional frameworks featuring slow magnetic relaxation were less developed.15 On the other hand, the luminescence of many lanthanide compounds have attracted much attention due to their narrow and strong emission, high color purity and long luminescence lifetime.16 Nevertheless, the absorption and emission spectra intensity of the Ln(iii) ions is very weak owing to the forbidden intra-configuration 4f–4f transitions in lanthanide ions.17 To overcome this shortcoming, the organic ligand can act as “antennas”, absorbing and transferring energy efficiently to lanthanide ions (intra-molecular energy transfer), which results in the enhancement of luminescence intensity.18 Therefore lanthanide coordination polymers are good candidates for pursuing multi-function materials.

In this contribution, eight three-dimensional lanthanide–organic frameworks: [Ln(BPDC)1.5(DMF)(H2O)2]·2H2O (Ln = Eu (1), Gd (2), Tb (3), Dy (4); BPDC = 4,4′-dicarboxylate-2,2′-dipyridine anion), [Ln(BPDC)(DMF)2(NO3)] (Ln = Eu (5), Gd (6), Tb (7), Dy (8)) were fabricated and structurally characterized. Magnetic measurements were performed on compounds 2–4 and 6–8, only compounds 4 and 8 exhibit slow relaxation of the magnetization. Geometrical differences in the crystal fields of Dy3+ in 4 and 8 seem to be responsible for the divergence of their magnetic behaviors. Luminescence properties of Eu3+, Tb3+, and Dy3+ containing coordination polymers were investigated, and the luminescence lifetime analyses were also performed.

Experiment section

Materials and physical measurements

All chemicals purchased were of reagent grade and used without further purification. Water used in the reactions is distilled water. Analyses for C, H and N were carried out on a Perkin-Elmer analyzer. The emission spectra in the visible region were measured on a Cary Eclipse fluorescence spectrophotometer, and the luminescent lifetimes were taken on an Edinburgh Analytical Instrument F900. Magnetic susceptibilities were performed on a Quantum Design PPMS-9 ACMS magnetometer. Diamagnetic corrections were made with Pascal's constants for all the constituent atoms.

Crystallographic studies

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction measurements of 1–8 were carried out at room temperature on a Rigaku SCXmini X-ray diffractometer equipped with graphite monochromated MoKα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). Lorentz polarization and absorption corrections were applied. The structures were solved by direct methods and refined by full-matrix least-squares techniques using the SHELXS-97 and SHELXL-97 programs.19,20 All the nonhydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic parameters while H atoms were placed in calculated positions and refined using a riding model. Some DMF groups were rotationally disordered and were refined with two sets of partial carbon atoms and nitrogen atoms; suitable restraints were applied to the geometries and to the thermal parameters of the partial atoms. Results and discussion

Crystal structures of compounds [Ln(BPDC)1.5(DMF)(H2O)2]·2H2O (Ln = Eu (1), Gd (2), Tb (3), Dy (4))

The structure analyses of 1–4 reveal that the four compounds are isomorphous, belonging to the triclinic system with space group P, and therefore, the structure of compound 4 is described here representatively. A structure similar with compound 2 has been reported,21 and herein compound 2 are only used to discuss the structural and magnetic comparison investigations. The asymmetric unit in 4 (Fig. 1) consists of one crystallographically independent Dy3+, one and a half BPDC ligands, one coordinated DMF, two coordinated water molecules and two lattice water molecules. Dy3+ is coordinated by five carboxylic oxygen atoms from five BPDC ligands, one acyl oxygen atoms from DMF and two water molecules with the Dy–O bonds of 2.261(8)–2.454(9) Å. The coordination geometry for the eight-coordinated Dy3+ can be described as a distorted square-antiprism, and the local symmetry of Dy3+ is nearly D4d (Fig. 1, insert). The skew angle between the upper and lower planes is 43.97°, and the interplanar distance calculated by the distance between the center of the upper and lower planes is 2.564 Å. In compound 4, the ligand adopts two different coordination modes: mode A (μ4–η1:η1:η1:η1), each BPDC anion bridges four Dy3+ ions; mode B (μ3–η1:η1:η1), each ligand bridges three Dy3+ ions, as show in Scheme 1. The Dy3+ is connected into a 1D chain through the carboxyl groups (Fig. 2), and the 1D chain is further assembled into a 3D framework by the linking of the ligand, as shown in Fig. 3. From the topological viewpoint, the mode A ligand can be simplified as a four-connected node while the mode B ligand can be simplified as a three-connected node, and Dy3+ can be considered as a five-connected node. Thus, the network of compound 4 can be described by an Schlafli symbol with three nonequivalent points: (42·6)(42·84)(43·6·86), as shown in the ESI (Fig. S1†).

Crystal structures of compounds [Ln(BPDC)(DMF)2(NO3)] (Ln = Eu (5), Gd (6), Tb (7), Dy (8))

Single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that the four compounds are isomorphous, of which 6–8 belong to monoclinic system with space group C2/c while 5 belong to triclinic system with space group P (Fig. S2–S4†), and therefore, the structure of compound 8 is described here in detail. There exists one crystallographically independent Dy3+, one BPDC ligands, two coordinated DMF, one coordinated nitrate anion in the asymmetric unit (Fig. 4). Four carboxylic oxygen atoms from BPCD ligands, two acyl oxygen atoms from DMF and two nitrate oxygen atoms finish the coordinated sphere of Dy3+, and the coordination geometry for the eight-coordinated Dy3+ can be described as a distorted bicapped trigonal prism, in which O2 and O5 act as the two capping atoms. The Dy–O bond distances are in the range of 2.316(3)–2.472(5) Å. The BPDC anion acts as a tetra-dentate linker (Scheme 1A): every carboxylic oxygen atom coordinates with one Dy3+ ion, which means every carboxyl group bridges two Dy3+ ions. Viewed along the a direction, Dy3+ are connected into a 1D chain through carboxyl groups (Fig. 5). And the 1D chains are further assembled into a 3D structure through the linking of the ligand, as shown in Fig. 6. From the topological viewpoint, Dy3+ can be considered as a four-connected node, while the ligand can be simplified as a four-connected node. Thus, the network of compound 8 can be described by a Schlafli symbol with two nonequivalent points: (42·84) (42·84), as shown in Fig. S5.†

Comparison of structures

The two types of structure all feature a 3D framework. Take 4 and 8 as an example to compare their structure, compound 4 belongs to triclinic crystal system with space group P, while compound 8 belongs to monoclinic system with space group C2/c. Both the metal centers of the two compounds are eight-coordinated; however, the coordination geometry for 4 and 8 are different: for 4, the coordination geometry is a distorted square-antiprism, while for 8, is a distorted bicapped trigonal prism. The Dy3+ are connected into a 1D chain through carboxylic groups in both 4 and 8. However, Dy3+ in 4 coordinated with five BPDC anions, while Dy3+ in 8 coordinated with four BPDC anions (Fig. 7), and the coordination modes of BPDC anions in them are not identical. The ligand in 4 has two coordination modes, as shown in Scheme 1, while the ligand in 8 only adopt μ3–η1:η1:η1 mode to connect metal ions. These divergences should originate from the different reaction conditions, as show in Scheme 2.

Magnetic properties

The magnetic susceptibilities of 2–4 and 6–8 were measured on the microcrystalline samples in the temperature range from 2 to 300 K and under 1000 Oe field, as shown in Fig. 8 and 9. The magnetic properties of 2 and 6 were firstly discussed because of no orbital contribution in the Gd3+-based compounds. At room temperature, the χMT value for 2 and 6 is 7.82 and 7.83 cm3 K mol−1, respectively, which is consistent well with the spin-only value of 7.88 cm3 K mol−1 expected for one Gd3+ ion (S = 7/2, L = 0, 8S7/2, g = 2).22 For 2, on lowering the temperature, the χMT values increases slightly from 7.82 at 300 K to 7.88 cm3 K mol−1 at 9 K, which may indicate the presence of very weak ferromagnetic coupling between Gd3+. With further cooling, the χMT value of 2 decreases gradually to 7.46 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K, and this behavior may accounts for the inter-chain antiferromagnetic interactions and/or the zero field splitting. For 6, as the temperature is lowered, χMT values remain constant until 10 K, and then increase gradually to 8.17 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K. The behavior indicates the presence of weak ferromagnetic coupling between adjacent Gd3+. The linear fit of χM−1vs. T for 2 and 6 above 40 K and 2 K obeys the Curie–Weiss law 1/χM = (T − θ)/C, for 2, C = 7.84 cm3 K mol−1, θ = 1.29 K (Fig. S6†), for 6, C = 7.87 cm3 K mol−1, θ = 0.14 K (Fig. S7†). The positive value of θ further confirms the result of the existence of ferromagnetic coupling between Gd3+.

Based on the Hamiltonian Ĥ = −JŜAŜB with ŜA = ŜB = 7/2, a corresponding equation as following was deduced, and the magnetic properties of 2 and 6 can be appropriately described by it.23a

Least-squares fitting of the experimental data for 2 above 18 K and 6 above 2 K give the J and g value, and the agreement factor R, defined as R = ∑(χobsd − χcalcd)2/∑(χobsd)2. For 2, J = 0.01 cm−1, g = 2.00 and R = 1.43 × 10−3; for 6, J = 0.006 cm−1, g = 2.00 and R = 1.45 × 10−3, which are in the normal range of Gd3+ containing compounds.23b The positive and small J value for 2 and 6 indicates very weak ferromagnetic coupling interaction between adjacent Gd3+.

Although compounds 3 and 4 are isomorphous with 2, and compounds 7 and 8 are isomorphous with 6, they exhibit different magnetic properties because of the significant orbital contributions of Ln3+ ions. As for lanthanide ions, due to the existence of interelectronic repulsion and spin–orbit coupling, the 4fn configuration may be split into 2S+1LJ states. Influenced by the crystal field perturbation, each of these levels may be further split into Stark sublevels which are thermally populated at room temperature. But a progressive depopulation of these sublevels occurs with lowering the temperature, causing χMT decreases with decreasing temperature. It is difficult to analyze the temperature dependence of the susceptibility due to their intrinsic complicated magnetic characteristics, including the presence of spin–orbit coupling and magnetic anisotropy.

For 3 and 7, χMT is equal to 11.80 and 11.85 cm3 K mol−1 at room temperature, which is consistent with the theoretical value of 11.82 cm3 K mol−1 expected for one Tb3+ ion (S = 3, L = 3, 7F6, g = 3/2). For 3, the χMT values almost remain consistent between 300 and 80 K, this behavior may account for the competition balance between ferromagnetic interaction and thermal depopulation of Stark sublevels of single Tb3+ ions. With further cooling, the χMT values decrease quickly to 7.00 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K, which may indicate the thermal depopulation of Stark sublevels dominates the magnetic properties at low temperature. The linear fit of χM−1vs. T for 3 above 2 K obeys the Curie–Weiss law 1/χM = (T − θ)/C with C = 11.89 cm3 K mol−1, θ = −1.41 K (Fig. S8†). Compound 3 has a small negative θ value, however, it is difficult to conclude this small Weiss temperatures indicates antiferromagnetic interactions between adjacent Tb3+ because of the presence of strong spin–orbital coupling effects in Tb3+ ion. For 7, as the temperature is lowered, χMT values increase gradually to 17.59 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K. The behaviors indicate the presence of weak ferromagnetic coupling between Tb3+ ions. The linear fit of χM−1vs. T with Curie–Weiss law above 80 K got C = 11.45 cm3 K mol−1, θ = 15.15 K (Fig. S9†), which further confirms the result of the existence of ferromagnetic coupling between Tb3+ ions in 7.

For 4 and 8, the χMT value is 14.21 and 14.16 cm3 K mol−1, respectively, which is consistent with the theoretical value of 14.17 cm3 K mol−1 expected for one Dy3+ ions (S = 5/2, L = 5, 6H15/2, g = 4/3).22 For 4, the χMT values increases slightly from 14.21 at 300 K to 14.40 cm3 K mol−1 at 80 K, which may indicate the presence of weak ferromagnetic coupling between Dy3+ ions. With further cooling, the χMT values decrease quickly to 12.16 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K, which may originate from the thermal depopulation of single ions. For 8, upon cooling, the χMT values of 8 increase gradually to 25.41 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K. This behavior indicates the presence of ferromagnetic coupling between Dy3+ ions. The linear fit of χM−1vs. T for 4 and 8 above 50 K and 40 K obeys the Curie–Weiss law 1/χM = (T − θ)/C, for 4, C = 14.26 cm3 K mol−1, θ = 0.90 K (Fig. S10†), for 8, C = 13.66 cm3 K mol−1, θ = 13.56 K (Fig. S11†). The positive value of θ further confirms the existence of ferromagnetic coupling between Dy3+ ions.

The depopulation of Stark sublevels may cause χMT decreases with decreasing temperature. We noticed that the χMT value of compounds 3 and 4 remain fairly consistent at high temperature, which accounts for the competition balance between ferromagnetic interaction and thermal depopulation of Stark sublevels. Namely, ferromagnetic interactions exist in 3 and 4. As mentioned before, 2 shows very weak ferromagnetic coupling between Gd3+. Comparably, compounds 6–8 clearly show weak ferromagnetic interactions. The divergence among them may origin from the impact of different coordination geometry around the metal ions. The coordination geometry of Ln3+ in 2–4 is close to D4d with high C4 axis symmetry, while that of Ln3+ in 6–8 belongs to a distorted bicapped trigonal prism with low symmetry. The different coordination geometry may cause the 2S+1LJ states split into different Stark sublevels.

The field dependence of magnetization of compounds 2, 3, 6 and 7 has been measured at 2 K in the 0–80 kOe field. For 2 and 6, the magnetization increases rapidly at low field, and reach saturation with the value of 7.02 μB and 6.95 μB, respectively, as show in Fig. 10. For 3 and 7, the magnetization shows a gradual increase at low fields, following a lack of saturation even at 80 kOe. The field dependence of the magnetization of compounds 4 and 8 were collected in range of 2–4 K temperatures, as shown in Fig. 11 and 12. The magnetization shows a gradual increase at low fields, following a lack of saturation even at 80 kOe. The behaviors of 3, 4, 7 and 8 indicate the presence of magnetic anisotropy and/or the lack of a well-defined ground state suggesting the presence of low-lying excited states. The non-overlapping of M vs. H/T curves for 4 and 8 further proved this point.

To further investigate the dynamics of magnetization, the alternating current (AC) magnetic measurements on 2–4 and 6–8 have been performed. There is no out-of-phase susceptibility detected above 2 K for 2, 3, 6 and 7, and compounds 4 and 8 display clear frequency-dependent out-of-phase signals at low temperature. The measurements were made in the 311–2311 Hz frequency range and at temperatures between 2 and 20 K. Compounds 4 (Fig. 13 and S12†) and 8 (Fig. 14 and S13†) exhibit slow relaxation of the magnetization, suggesting that it may have SMM behaviors, however, no maxima in χ′′ are observed in the temperature window technically available. Furthermore, since compounds 2 and 3 are isomorphous with 4, and compounds 6 and 7 are isomorphous with 8, however, only 4 and 8 exhibit slow magnetic relaxation properties. No out-of-phase signals are detected in corresponding isomorphous compounds containing other lanthanide metal ions, thus the magnetic slow-relaxation behavior of 4 and 8 are attributed to the anisotropy of Dy3+ ions, and the similar results were obtained in other lanthanide compounds.11

For lanthanide ions containing compounds, SMM behavior with magnetic relaxation was partially influenced by quantum effects, and the application of a direct current (DC) field may remove the ground state degeneracy and thus reduce the possibility of quantum tunneling.24 To investigate the dynamic properties of the slow magnetic relaxation and obtain an effective energy barrier, a field of 2000 Oe was applied during the AC measurements for 4 (Fig. 15 and S14†) and 8 (Fig. 16 and S15†) to give good peak shapes in both in-phase and out-of- phases curve. The good peak shapes of AC signal after the application of a DC field indicate a quantum tunnelling of the magnetization at zero field, and the quantum tunnelling was quenched by a direct current (DC) field. The correlation between peak temperatures, TP, and their corresponding frequencies can educe a linear plots of 1/TPversus ln(2πf), and obey the Arrhénius law 1/Tp = −kB/ΔE(ln(2πf) + ln(τ0)). The best fitting yields, for 4, the energy barrier ΔE/kB = 79.80 K and the pre-exponential factor τ0 = 1.28 × 10−10 s; for 8, ΔE/kB = 38.15 K and τ0 = 2.47 × 10−9 s, which are in the normal range for a SMM. To our knowledge, the highest energy barrier up to now was reported by the Winpenny group, they report a Dy5 complexes show a high energy barrier up to 528 K.12d To explore whether the magnetic behaviors of 4 and 8 belong to spin glass, the shift of the peak temperature (TP) of χM′′ is measured by a parameter Φ = (ΔTp/Tp)/Δ(log f). The value of 0.16 for 4 and 0.21 for 8 were obtained, which exclude the possibility of a spin glass (0.01 < Φ < 0.08).25

For lanthanide ions, the 4f electrons are well shielded by the outer 5s25p6 electrons, the magnetic coupling between lanthanide ions is relatively weak, and therefore the slow magnetization relaxation of 4 and 8 may originate from the single-ion behavior of Dy3+, which was theoretically explored.11,14c So the SMM behavior observed in lanthanide-based magnets is strongly dependent on the coordination geometry around the metal ions. In Jiang's paper,14c they mentioned that ligand fields with a high-order single axis Cn (n > 2; for example n = 4) may give rise to a higher energy barrier for relaxation.11,12a,e Actually, the coordination geometry of Dy3+ in 4 is close to D4d with high symmetry of C4 axis, while that of Dy3+ in 8 belongs to a distorted bicapped trigonal prism with low symmetry. Therefore, 4 should have higher energy barrier than 8, which is consistent with the results obtained from the experiment data (ΔE/kB = 79.80 K for 4 and 38.15 K for 8). These geometrical differences in the crystal fields of Dy3+ in 4 and 8, even if small, seem to be sufficient to responsible for the large divergence of their magnetic behaviors.

Luminescent properties

The solid-state luminescence properties of compounds 1, 3, 4 and 5, 7, 8 were investigated in the visible region at room temperature. The six compounds all displayed their characteristic emissions. Compounds 1 and 5 were excited at 324 nm, and the four peaks at 593, 614, 652 and 699 nm for 1 and 593, 619, 652 and 700 nm for 5, correspond to the transitions of Eu3+ from 5D0 to 7FJ (J = 1, 2, 3, 4). The peaks of 1 (538, 557 nm) and 5 (537, 555 nm) correspond to the transitions of Eu3+ from 5D1 to 7FJ (J = 1, 2), as shown in Fig. 17.26 The intensity radio of the two emission peaks of 1 (593, 618 nm) and 5 (593, 619 nm) corresponding to 5D0→7F1 (magnetic dipole transition) and 5D0→7F2 (electric dipole transition) emission27 is about 1 : 3, which means electric dipole transition 5D0→7F2 dominates the luminescence emission. This behavior shows that the symmetry of the coordination environment of Eu3+ ions is low, which is consistent well with the crystal structure. Compounds 3 and 7 are excited at 330 nm, and the four peaks at 490, 545, 588, and 621 nm for 3 and 489, 546, 587, and 622 nm for 7 are assigned to the transition of 5D4 to 7FJ of Tb3+ (J = 6, 5, 4, 3),28 in which the emission of 5D4→7F5 is the strongest one, as shown in Fig. 18. The emission spectra of compounds 4 and 8 upon excited at 336 nm exhibit the two characteristic transitions, 482 and 574 nm for 4 and 481 and 574 nm for 8, which can be assigned to the transitions of 4F9/2→6H15/2 and 4F9/2→6H13/2 (Fig. 19),28 respectively. In addition, the halfwidth of the strongest band is less than 10 nm, indicating that these compounds exhibit high color purity.

Conclusion

In summary, eight lanthanide coordination polymers have been constructed and structurally characterized. Systematic magnetic studies for them have been performed, and compounds 4 and 8 show clear frequency dependence of the out-of-phase susceptibility, which is very rare in lanthanide-based 3D coordination polymers. With the application of external DC field (2000 Oe), good peak shapes of the out-of-phase and in-phase signal was obtained in both 4 and 8, and give an energy barrier of 79.80 K and 38.15 K, respectively. The geometrical differences in the crystal fields of Dy3+ in 4 and 8 should be responsible for the large divergence of their magnetic behaviors. The coordination polymers of Eu3+, Tb3+, and Dy3+ show their characteristic luminescence in the visible region, and the luminescence lifetime analyses were also performed.
