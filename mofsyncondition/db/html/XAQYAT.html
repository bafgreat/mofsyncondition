Introduction

The influence and potential applications of radical activity on coordination polymeric compounds have long been a topic in literature. They range from the use of porous coordination polymers as lattices for radical polymerization1 to the induction of structural changes within the lattice structure of a compound by tautomery.2 Especially ligand promoted polymerization reactions, either by redox reactions,3 or by Lewis-acidity4,5 to activate a surface or pore bound substrate, provide a much sought after ability in the pursuit to create more complex catalytic systems able to mimic enzymatic processes.6 Molecules known to undergo electrochemical reactions with transition metals, that are used in coordination polymers to create materials with tuneable magnetic properties,7 have been investigated in this regard.8,9 As a prominent example, the tetracyanochinodimethane (TCNQ) ligand has been promoted for its magnetic properties based on radical systems with transition metal compounds10–12 or organic salts.13 While many successful attempts to create such radical based ligands rely on quinones,14 compounds like tetracyanoethylene (TCNE) that are not based on the principle of restoration of aromaticity exist, and have been used with success.15 When it comes to the induction of free radical species, besides ligands like TCNQ that undergo electrochemical reactions with coordinated transition metals based on their redox-potential, compounds able to form free radicals by photoexcitation of electrons are also reported in literature.16 Such molecules are of particular interest due to their photoinducable electron transfer (PET) behaviour that can be applied in a variety of methods ranging from sensing,17 molecular scale electronics,18 photovoltaics19 and catalysis.20

With the trend to create smaller ligands in order to minimize the length of the (diamagnetic) π-conjugated system between central metals in coordination polymers for magnetic applications, molecules like tricyanomethanide21,22 have come into focus. Transition metal compounds of this ligand exhibit interesting magnetic properties23 and show charge transfer behavior.24,25 With polycyanated cyclopentadienides as the next smallest, but better configurable and largely unexplored, stable ligand system, research on the properties of these molecules could lead to coordination polymeric compounds usable in magnetic applications, or catalysis, as might have already been documented by the use of tetracyanocyclopentadienide salts in hydrolysis reactions by Sakai.26 In regard to PET and the formation of free radicals, cyclopentadienyl based systems are an interesting subject: they are one of the most accessible aromatic free radical systems and, as an important system in organometallic chemistry, several thermally stable free radicals are known to literature. While some of them are stabilized by bulky side groups,27–33 several compounds like halogen substituted cyclopentadienyl radicals have also been reported.34–36 Photoinduced cyclopentadienyl radicals are reported in sandwich-type coordination compounds with transition metals and lanthanides,37–39 and the mechanisms of generation and stabilization of the free radicals, as well as the role of the ligand itself in this process, has been discussed on several occasions.40,41 Since capto-dative stabilization plays a major role in the formation of free radical species, cyano-substituted cyclopentadienyl compounds are likely to show the free radical activity exhibited in other polynitrile substituted aromatic systems, especially if paired with amine or similar substituents.42

As the most prominent of the 5-substituted 1,2,3,4-tetracyanocyclopentadienides, the coordination chemistry43–46 and physical properties47 of five-fold nitrile substituted 1,2,3,4,5-pentacyanocyclopentadienide has been documented in literature and depicts an inert, weakly coordinating ligand. With some reports of the reductive activity of this ligand in copper(ii) compounds,48,49 and a largely unnoticed report indicating radical induced Jahn–Teller distortion in the X-ray study of bis(ethylenedithio)tetrathiafulvalene pentacyanocyclopentadienide,50 we started to look into the electrochemical and photochemical behaviour of derivatives of this ligand. Among the 5-substituted 1,2,3,4-tetracyanocyclopentadienides first prepared by Webster in 1965,51,53 5-amino (ATCC, 1) and 5-nitro (NTCC, 2) substituted derivatives (Scheme 1) are most likely to stabilize observable free radicals due to capto-dative or mesomeric stabilization, which led us to investigate these two ligand systems. In an effort to characterize their properties, we prepared compounds of potassium(i), silver(i), iron(ii) and iron(iii), as well as crystal structures of their silver(i) compounds and potassium(i) NTCC.

Results and discussion

The compounds were prepared by means of salt metathesis from the silver(i) compounds with chlorides of the respective metals (Scheme 2).

Due to the weakly coordinating nature of the ligands, many analytical approaches usually applied in the characterization like infrared-spectroscopy, as well as methods relying on solvatisation of the compounds in general, do not yield satisfying results. Mass spectrometry can identify the ligands and, in case of [Ag(2)(H2O)] and Fe(2)3, negatively charged fragments of the compounds. Besides single crystal X-ray diffraction, further structural characterization can be performed via elemental analysis and yields satisfying results in all compounds but Fe(2)3 that exhibits strong hygroscopy when put into contact with air.

In the case of iron(iii) compounds, PXRD gives no results, which could hint to an amorphous structure (vide infra). While compounds of silver(i) and potassium(i) can be expected to exhibit coordination polymers and were characterized by XRD in all cases except K(ATCC)·0.75 MeOH·H2O, characterization of iron(iii) compounds indicates monomeric compounds similar to structures of [Fe(C5(CN)5)2(H2O)4] and [Fe(C5(CN)4H)2(H2O)4] mentioned in earlier reports.53 Compounds of both 1 and 2 exhibit an active electrochemistry that can be observed by EPR measurements and cyclic voltammetry, and leads to an unexpected reductive behaviour contrary to the electron-deficit nature of the ligands.

Cyclic voltammetry

While cyclic voltammetry of K(1) was discussed in detail in an earlier publication54 and shows quasi-reversible oxidation steps, a measurement of K(2) (0.1 mM) only shows one irreversible oxidation step during oxidative scanning at approx. 1.91 V (Fig. 1). A second oxidation step in 1 can be associated with oxidation of the amine function specifically, and it is reasonable to assume that further oxidation does not take place in the 5-nitro substituted compound. Standard potentials put 1 with [E(ATCC/ATCC˙) = +0.85 V vs. SCE] in the vicinity of several transition metals, while 2 can be found at a much higher potential of [E(NTCC/NTCC˙) = +2.27 V vs. SCE], beyond the standard potentials of transition metals.

However, despite the potential, several noteworthy interactions of both ligands with transition metals can be observed. While iron(iii) compounds of both ligands are readily reduced to iron(ii), silver(i) compounds of 1 can be stored in direct sunlight over a period of months to years without showing any blackening associated with the formation of silver(0), which might be an indication for electrochemical interactions between elemental silver and the ligand that leads to the stabilization in form of the silver(i)compound.

The interaction with iron(iii) compounds especially shows similarities to pentacyanocyclopentadienide, that was reported to presumably facilitate the reduction of copper(ii) to copper(i), again with a CV measurement indicating the redox potential of the ligand to be out of reach of metals like copper(ii).49

Mößbauer measurements

While the reduction of copper(ii) was observed as the result of a X-ray study in the case of the pentanitrile and could have been interpreted as the result of an impurity during crystallization, Mössbauer spectroscopy offers a way to quantify this reaction in iron(iii) compounds of 1 and 2, and shows a complete reduction to iron(ii) after preparation (Fig. 2). Isomeric shifts of 1.04 in the compound of 2 and 1.06 in the iron(ii)-complex of 1 at 4.2 K correspond to values often observed in tetrahedral FeX42− ions with halogenides,55 as do the values of ΔEQ = 3.15 in case of 1 and ΔEQ = 3.22 in case of 2.

These findings from Mößbauer measurements contradict with the elemental analysis of the iron(iii) compounds that indicate a fit for an octahedrally coordinated iron(iii) in a monomeric compound at least in case of 1 with a formula of approx. Fe(H2O)6(1)3, suggesting the presence of three polynitrile ligands per iron center. The determination of an exact composition in the case of the iron(iii) complex of 2 proved difficult due to the high hygroscopy of the compound, Mössbauer measurements however show similar results as observed in Fe(1)3. If compared to octahedrally coordinated FeCl2·4H2O, isomeric shifts show values too small to correlate for both compounds.56 This could be interpreted as a reduction of the iron(iii) in the coordination compound, and while only two thirds of the ligand in the iron(iii) compound would be needed to reduce the metal to its divalent state if the formation of a free radical is assumed, this would also indicate that some polynitriles within the coordination complex investigated by elemental analysis no longer act as anionic ligands to the metal center. Since this would likely lead to an amorphous compound, the lack of evaluable PXRD measurements in iron(iii) compounds of both ligands might support this theory.

Electron paramagnetic resonance measurements

Solid state EPR measurements on compounds of both 1 and 2 show the presence of unpaired electrons in compounds with silver(i) and potassium(i) that show activity with g = 2.001 for compounds of 1,54 and g = 2.000 to 2.004 for Ag(2) and K(2) respective, as well as the tetraethylammonium precursor to Ag(2) with g = 2.001. All values correspond to ligand bound free radical systems (Fig. 3). The highest intensity EPR signal can be observed in the compound of Ag(2). If 2,2′-bipyridine is added to the silver(i) compound, a complete quenching of EPR activity, measured in solid state EPR, can be observed, which is likely induced by isolation of the metal center by the chelating ligand and the resulting breaking up of the coordination polymer.

Since both ligands are (at least initially) negatively charged building blocks for use in coordination polymers, and also showed EPR activity in main group metals that are unlikely to be reduced, we looked for external causes that might be responsible for the initiation of the observed radical activity, and investigated if the formation of neutral radicals by photoinduced electron transfer is a possibility.

When EPR measurements of K(1) and K(2) in MeCN were taken initially, they did not show signals when the compound was stored in darkness, but then gave small signals after exposure to UV light. In K(1), hyperfine coupling with 14N leads to three signals, likely caused by interaction with the amine group of the ligand, while in K(2), only a single line spectrum of smaller intensity was observed at first. When irradiated under N2 atmosphere in degassed and anhydrous MeCN however, signals that can be resolved into hyperfine couplings can be detected in case of K(2) also, and correspond to four 14N interactions, thereby localizing the free radical in the nitrile groups of the compound (Fig. 4). This behaviour likely leads to the observed reduction of transition metal centers with iron(iii) and copper(ii).

UV-Vis measurements

Further indication of photoinductive behaviour can be observed in UV-Vis spectroscopy of the solid compounds. The compounds exhibit different spectra when stored in light and dark, and can be induced by excitation with UV light. UV-Vis spectra measuring total reflectance of crystalline powder of K(2)(H2O) show an almost identical progression as shown in K(1)·0.75 MeOH·H2O54 and a change likely induced by the formation of free radical species can be observed when irradiation with UV-light occurs (Fig. 5). While the intensity of bands in solid state UV-Vis measurements cannot be reliably discussed, a broad band spanning the visible spectrum and the UV can be observed in spectra of the potassium compound of both ligands before irradiation. The spectra show a fall-off in absorbance at 260 nm that is replaced by a large band with maxima at 243 nm and a shoulder at 293 nm when irradiation with UV light over several hours occurs. If the potassium-compounds are stored in the dark after irradiation, the spectrum reverts over the course of several hours.

This, however, cannot be observed in case of powder of [Ag(2)(H2O)], that does not show a fall off, and instead depicts a spectrum similar to the irradiated compound of K(2)(H2O) with a maximum at 234 nm and a shoulder at 293 nm even if stored dark. If measured from MeOH instead of a solid compound, only a broad band with no distinct signals can be observed in the visible spectrum and no change indicating the presence of a free radical can be induced by UV-irradiation. While the observed bands could be linked directly to the free radical, it is also possible they show, at least in part, the pathway of photoquenching in potassium compounds of the ligands that provide no target for reductive behaviour.

X Ray diffraction studies

[Ag(ATCC)]·MeCN (1a)

Crystals of 1a can be obtained from a mixture of acetonitrile in toluene that is subjected to slow vapour evaporation. The compound crystallizes from a monoclinic space group P21/n.

The asymmetrical unit is comprised of a silver(i) ion, an ATCC anion and one molecule of acetonitrile acting as a guest molecule (Fig. 6). The metal center is coordinated tetrahedrally by four ATCC ligands with three ligands coordinating via their nitrile groups taking up a planar configuration, while the last ligand coordinates via the amine function (Fig. 7). With bond angles between 90.8° and 98.1° between the Ag–N5 interaction and the coordinating nitrile functions of other ligands, the coordination environment can be described as a distorted tetrahedron. The metal center is almost located in the plane created by the three ATCC ligands coordinating via N1-, N3- and N4-nitrile functions with the cyano groups directed towards the silver ion with angles between 157.9° and 169.9° (Fig. 7 and 8). Bond lengths are almost identical between the Ag–N1/4 interactions of coordinating nitrile groups with 2.203 Å to 2.209 Å and only the Ag–N3 interaction shows a larger bond length with 2.272 Å, being overall shorter than observed in compounds of the pentacyanocyclopentadienide ligand.57 Since 1 is one of the most electron rich 5-substituted tetracyanocyclopentadienides, shortened metal ligand interactions can be expected. Bond lengths of Ag–N5 interactions facilitated by the amine group of the ligand however are unusually long for a secondary amine with 2.569 Å.50 C5–N5–Ag interactions show a tetrahedral angle of 106°. Moreover, cation pi interactions can be formulated between a neighbouring ATCC ligand and the metal center with a distance of 3.42 Å (Fig. 8) that can also be observed in silver(i) compounds of similar ligands (Table 1).57 MeCN is integrated into the lattice structure as a guest molecule via N5–H1⋯N6 hydrogen bonding interactions facilitated by the amine group of the ligand. With 2.25 Å, the hydrogen bond is relatively short and directed toward MeCN with an angle of 171°. Due to the participation of the amine function in coordinative bonding, the lattice structure is more complex and several ring systems can be formulated to describe the 3D-structure of the compound. A coordination of two silver(i)-centers by two ligands using 1,2-located nitrile functions yields a 14-membered ring.

A larger, 32-membered ring is formed by three ligands coordinating three metal centers via 1,3-located nitrile groups. Another, 29-membered ring is formed if interactions via two 1,3-coordinating nitrile functions, one 1,2-coordinating ligand that uses its amine function and another 1,3-coordinating ATCC, also utilizing the C5-bound amine group, is taken into account. A fourth, 21-membered ring can be formulated from one 1,3-coordinating ligand using its nitrile functions and two ligands using their amine function, of which one is coordinating via 1,2 located groups and the other using 1,3 coordination. Since –NH2 functions coordinate the metal center with a C5–N5–Ag angle of 106°, each ligand is coordinating silver ions located in a neighbouring plane if using the amine group, while rings formed of only nitrile functions are planar (Fig. 9). With a distance of only approximately 3.2 Å between neighbouring planes and only amine functions linking the lattice structure into a three-dimensional network, this might explain the long bond distance of N5–Ag interactions with steric causes. This leads to a lattice structure with a repeat of AgATCC-bands dictated by inversion centers between the planes and rotoinversion axis running along the crystallographic b-axis. The compound exhibits pores along the crystallographic a-axis with walls framed by nitrile and amine functions of the ligand and two guest molecules of acetonitrile present in the voids (Fig. 10).

K(NTCC)(H2O) (2a)

Crystals of 2a can be obtained from a solution of methanol that is subjected to slow evaporation. The compound crystallizes in an orthorhombic space group P212121. One NTCC ligand, one coordinating H2O ligand and one potassium ion make up the asymmetric unit (Fig. 11). Compared to other potassium compounds of this ligand class,44 a lesser interconnected lattice structure with solvent coordination of H2O molecules is present in this case. While the potassium compound of 1,2,3,4,5-pentacyanocyclopentadienide shows nitrile functions in a 1,1-bridging function, this cannot be observed in this coordination polymer. Instead, one oxygen of the nitro-function coordinates two potassium ions.

The metal center is coordinated in a hexahedral environment by six NTCC anions and two H2O ligands. Both H2O molecules share the same edge of the coordination polyhedron and form two faces with one of two NTCC ligands coordinating via their nitro functions. Five NTCC molecules take up parallel orientations and link into parallel planes, while the last ligand takes up an almost orthogonal orientation (Fig. 12). Each NTCC anion coordinates via all four nitrile functions, as well as one oxygen of its nitro group that coordinates two metal centers in a 1,1-bridging interaction, effectively leading to a classification as a μ6 ligand in this case. H2O molecules coordinate in a similar fashion to the nitro group of the ligand by bridging two metal centers. Bond lengths of the K–N interactions show a high variation with K–N4 being the shortest (2.87 Å) and K–N5 the longest (2.94 Å). Since the ligands nitro function is involved in a 1,1-bridging coordination with its K–O2 interaction, bond lengths are exceptionally large with 3.25 Å (Table 1).

Several ring systems can be identified in the lattice structure of this compound. The smallest, 4-membered ring system can be found between two potassium ions that are linked via one H2O ligand and the O2 oxygen of the NTCC ligands nitro group. Two 14-membered rings can be formulated by 1,2-coordination of two potassium centers by two NTCC ligands either using only nitrile functions, or one nitrile and nitro function in bridging interactions. A 21-membered ring is formed by three NTCC ligands and three metal centers with one ligand using 1,2-bridging interactions of two nitrile functions, one 1,2-coordinating ligand using its nitro functions, and one anion forming 1,3-bridging interactions using only nitrile functions. If H2O ligands are taken into account, another, 18-membered ring can be formed using three potassium ions, one H2O ligand, one 1,2-coordinating NTCC ligand using its nitro functions, and one 1,3-coordinating ligand that initiates bridging interactions only via nitrile groups (Fig. 13).

The lattice structure shows stacking sheets along the crystallographic a-axis with 4-periodical repeats induced by the three different 2-fold rotoinversion axes, one between the sheet structures, and two between neighbouring NTCC ligands of the same sheet. With one NTCC ligand of the coordination polyhedron taking up a different orientation, the resulting lattice takes up a corrugated form of sheet structures if viewed along the b-axis (Fig. 14).

[Ag(NTCC)(H2O)] (2b)

When recrystallized from a mixture of acetonitrile and toluene by vapour diffusion, crystals of 2b can be obtained. The complex compound crystalizes from the monoclinic space group Pnma and is comprised of one NTCC ligand, one H2O ligand and one silver(i) ion as the asymmetrical unit. The nitro function of the ligand is disordered over two positions, which leads to issues when characterizing the lattice structure (Fig. 15).

Each metal center is coordinated tetrahedrally by three NTCC molecules and one water with bond angles between 96.9° and 114.4° between O1w Ag N2/3 interactions (Fig. 16). Bond lengths within the coordination polyhedra differ between 2.26 Å and 2.23 Å for coordination of nitrile groups with Ag–N3 interactions being shorter, and 2.42 Å for coordinating H2O, slightly deviating from an ideal tetrahedron.

Weak cation–π interactions exist with a distance of 3.60 Å between the metal center and the center of a cyclopentadienide ring of NTCC below the tetrahedron (Table 1). Units of NTCC are located in the same plane with water acting as a capping ligand, restricting the polymeric structure to a one dimensional sheet motif (Fig. 17). A similar structure can be observed in the lattice of silver(i) 1,2,3,4,5-pentacyanocyclopentadienide, in which MeCN takes the place of H2O,57 and a nearly identical lattice is exhibited when the same ligand is recrystallized from DMF.58

Given the similar solvatisation properties of 2b, it is interesting to see that in this case, H2O is not only preferred over the kinetic choice of MeCN, but also against the softness of the ligand, which makes acetonitrile the preferred solvent in the compound of the pentacyanocyclopentadienide at least. Moreover, when first prepared from a mixture of toluene and acetonitrile, elemental analysis does not correspond to any amount of H2O in the structure. Instead, a fit for [Ag(NTCC)]·1 MeCN can be obtained, indicating a slow exchange of MeCN for H2O if diffusion is allowed during crystallization and storage, a motif that can be observed in many transition metal compounds of this ligand class (usually with silver(i) as the exception),53 and might be linked to the photochemical activity of this ligand system that is most pronounced in compounds of 2. While a nearly identical lattice structure to [Ag(C5(CN)5)(MeCN)] increases the probability of 2b exhibiting porous structures that can be observed in several supramolecular isomers of its derivate also, given the affinity for H2O that can be observed in other compounds,59 a strictly anhydrous crystallization environment will likely be a necessity.

One stack of sheets exhibit a parallel configuration while neighbouring sheets take up a different orientation, creating a corrugated motif similar to the one exhibited in 2a (Fig. 18). Sheets are integrated into a 3D-structure via O1w–H1–O2B and O1w–H1–N1 hydrogen bonding interactions between parallel sheets.

Experimental

All solvents used in the preparation of the compounds were p.a. grade purity or higher. All ligands were first isolated as organic salts with Et4N+ as the counter ion. Like the other precursors, they were prepared according to literature procedures.51,52,60,61 The silver(i) salts were prepared according to a previously published procedure,62 and X-ray quality crystals of [Ag(ATCC)]·1 MeCN and [Ag(NTCC)(H2O)] were obtained after recrystallization from a mixture of acetonitrile and toluene, using vapour diffusion at room temperature. The potassium salt of the ATCC ligand was prepared according to a previous publication.54 The coordination polymers were prepared from anhydrous metal chlorides, however, unless mentioned otherwise, no anhydrous solvents were used in metathesis reactions or recrystallization of the compounds.

Preparation of K(NTCC)·1.25MeOH·0.25H2O

To a solution of [Ag(NTCC)]·1MeCN (89.5 mg, 360.06 g mol−1, 0.25 mmol) in methanol (30 mL) was added KCl (18.6 mg, 74.55 g mol−1, 0.25 mmol). The solution is stirred at RT and under exclusion of light for 16 h before the solution was filtrated to remove AgCl. The solvent was removed to a volume of 1 mL, filtrated again and the product was isolated as a red powder by precipitation with dichloromethane. Yield: 50 mg, 74%. X ray quality Crystals of K(NTCC)(H2O) were obtained after recrystallization from MeOH over the course of several weeks at room temperature. 13C{1H} NMR (100.16 MHz, +25 °C, d4 MeOH), δ = 113.81(s), 113.70(s), 103.42(s), 97.00(s), 49.28(s). MS: m/z (MALDI-) = 210.4 [C5(CN)4(NO2)−]. EA (K(NTCC)·1.25 MeOH·0.25 H2O): calc. C 41.90, N 23.84, H 1.89, found C 41.95, N 23.98, H 1.50.

Preparation of Fe(ATCC)2·0.41 MeOH·3.76H2O

To a solution of [Ag(ATCC)]·1 MeCN (164.5 mg, 329.01 g mol−1, 0.50 mmol) in methanol (30 mL) was added FeCl2 (31 mg, 126.72 g mol−1, 0.25 mmol). The solution is stirred at RT and under exclusion of light for 16 h before the solution was filtrated and the solvent was removed to a volume of 1 mL. After a second filtration step, precipitation with dichloromethane yielded the product as a yellow powder. Yield: 71 mg, 69%. MS: m/z (FAB−) = 180.1 [C5(CN)4(NH2)−]. EA (Fe(ATCC)2·0.41 MeOH·3.76 H2O): calc. C 44.49, N 28.18, H 2.67, found C 44.49, N 28.15, H 2.64.

Preparation of Fe(ATCC)3·0.18 MeOH·5.68H2O

To a solution of [Ag(ATCC)]·1 MeCN (100 mg, 329.01 g mol−1, 0.30 mmol) in methanol (30 mL) was added FeCl3 (17.6 mg, 162.20 g mol−1, 0.11 mmol). The solution is stirred at RT and under exclusion of light for 16 h before the solution was filtrated and the solvent was removed to a volume of 1 mL. After a second filtration step, precipitation with dichloromethane yielded the product as a red powder. Yield: 40 mg, 34%. MS: m/z (FAB−) = 180.1 [C5(CN)4(NH2)−]. EA (Fe(ATCC)3·0.18 MeOH·5.68 H2O): calc. C 46.35, N 29.83, H 2.59, found C 46.26, N 29.74, H 2.50. For moessbauer measurements, the product was not precipitated with dichloromethane and was instead isolated by complete removal of the solvent after the second filtration step to ensure a complete characterization of all components except AgCl and leftover [Ag(ATCC)].

Preparation of FeNTCC2·6MeOH·1.9H2O·2.4CH2Cl2

To a solution of [Ag(NTCC)]·1.5 MeCN (189.2 mg, 378.45 g mol−1, 0.5 mmol) in methanol (anhydrous, 30 mL) was added FeCl2 (31 mg, 126.72 g mol−1, 0.25 mmol). The solution is stirred at RT and under exclusion of light and argon atmosphere for 16 h before the solution was filtrated and the solvent was removed to a volume of 1 mL. After a second filtration step, precipitation with dichloromethane yielded the product as a yellow powder. Yield: 49 mg, 42%. 13C{1H} NMR (100.16 MHz, +25 °C, d6 DMSO), δ = 136.44(s), 112.63(s), 112.43(s), 101.50(s), 95.37(s). MS: m/z = 210.2 [C5(CN)4(NO2)−], EA (Fe(NTCC)2·6 MeOH·1.9 H2O·2.4 CH2Cl2): calc. C 34.98, N 15.45, H 3.63, found C 35.53, N 14.88, H 3.36.

Preparation of FeNTCC3

To a solution of [Ag(NTCC)]·1.5 MeCN (227.0 mg, 378.45 g mol−1, 0.6 mmol) in methanol (anhydrous, 30 mL) was added FeCl3 (32 mg, 162.20 g mol−1, 0.2 mmol). The solution is stirred at RT and under exclusion of light and argon atmosphere for 16 h before the solution was filtrated and the solvent was removed to a volume of 1 mL. After a second filtration step, precipitation with dichloromethane yielded the product as a highly hygroscopic yellow powder. Yield: 28 mg, 21%. MS: m/z (FAB-) = 210.2 [C5(CN)4(NO2)−], 686.5 [Fe(C5(CN)4(NO2))3−]. For moessbauer measurements, the product was not precipitated with dichloromethane and was isolated by complete removal of the solvent after the second filtration step to ensure a complete characterization of all components except AgCl and leftover [Ag(ATCC)].

Conclusions

Both 5-nitro- and 5-amino substituted 1,2,3,4 tetracyanocyclopentadienide ligands are able to form coordination polymeric networks with at least one porous structure easily accessible from silver(i) 5-amino tetracyanocyclopentadienide. Both initially negatively charged and electron deficient ATCC and NTCC ligands exhibit photoinducable electron transfer, leading to oxidation to their neutral radicals, as can be observed in UV Vis and EPR spectra of their potassium and silver(i) compounds. While standard electron potentials of ATCC are in the vicinity of values reported for several transition metals, NTCC values are much higher. The reductive behaviour can also be observed in moessbauer spectra of iron(iii) compounds of these ligands that, while showing indications of an iron(iii) configuration, only give signals of iron(ii) when measured. In EPR measurements of both potassium compounds in solvating MeCN, weak signals can be observed when excited by UV-light prior to the measurements. They, however, are short lived compared to measurements under exclusion of oxygen and water. In their solid state compounds, EPR-signals indicating free radical activity last the longest, even when exposed to air. Compared to the organic amino tetracyanocyclopentadienyl radical that showed decomposition products in mass spectra and elemental analysis when subjected to air,54 no such products could be found in the solid state compounds of the discussed coordination polymer. This could be accounted to a slow decomposition of the compound on the surface of the solid only due to a “shielding effect” of the coordination polymer. The measured potassium compounds of polynitrile ligands exhibit a densely packed lattice structure not only in the structures reported in this work, but all compounds reported in literature, and might provide a good template for characterizing the electrochemical activity of polynitrile compounds in the solid state. When dissolved however, the compounds show definitive signs of decomposition while exposed to air, which would support this theory. Given the results of UV-Vis spectroscopy on these compounds, a quenching of radical activity by emission is viable in some compounds of this substance class, while the electron deficient nature of most tetracyanocyclopentidienide ligands, with the pentacyanocyclopentadienide anion being classified as a potential superacid for example,46,47 could allow for alternate pathways to quench free radical activity, especially if transition metal compounds are involved. This could lead to the reduction of the transition metal centers observed in the iron(iii) compounds, as well as the hygroscopy exhibited by those compounds, which could hint to a quenching mechanism involving surrounding solvent molecules in non-transition metal compounds also.

A dominant motif in the X-ray structures of silver(i) with polynitrile-cyclopentadienide ligands is the presence of cation–π interactions between the center of the cyclopentadienide ring and the metal center, which we believe to be linked to the free radical activity in the compounds. With one porous coordination polymer of [Ag(ATCC)]·MeCN presented in this report and indications of further porous compounds being achievable, as well as porous compounds of related ligands and a number of sheet structures available, applications of this ligand class in photochemistry could be viable.
