Introduction

Coordination polymers (CPs) are network structures containing metal ions linked by ligands and through the self-assembly process, one- (1D), two- (2D), or three-dimensional (3D) structures can be obtained.1–3 Because of their interesting structures and potential applications in areas such as gas storage and separation, ion exchange, magnetism, optical, luminescence, catalysis and sensing, a great effort has been made to design and synthesize CPs with desired properties. The structures of CPs are subject to many factors like metal ions, linkers, counterions, the ratio of the metal and ligand, solvents, temperature and pH values of the reaction environment. Therefore, employing and controlling the appropriate factors to construct CPs have become an important topic in CPs. On the other hand, structural transformation is intriguing in CPs due to their potential applications as switches and sensors, which can be initiated through the stimulus of solvent adsorption and removal, as well as anion and cation exchange.4 However, the factors that govern the structural transformation of CPs are rarely investigated because of the complicated structural changes that involve breaking and forming coordinate covalent bonds in more than one direction. The responses to solvents, temperature, anions and light, etc. are the inevitable factors in the dynamic nature of coordination bonding,5 which can be carried out by exchanging the coordinated solvents,6 and solvents and anions trapped in the cavities,7–10 by changing the coordination number of the central metal with or without transforming into higher dimensions11,12 or by changing the coordination number with different anions and solvents.13,14

The chemistry of CPs containing flexible bis-pyridyl-bis-amide (bpba) ligands with or without the auxiliary polycarboxylate ligands has become the subject of intensive investigation during recent years.15,16 The nature of the dicarboxylate ligands, the identity of the metal ions and the isomerism and flexibility of the bpba ligands play important roles in determining the structural diversity of the divalent CPs thus prepared. Recently, we have investigated the factors that govern the structural transformation for the flexible bpba-based CPs. In the CPs constructed from N,N′-di(3-pyridyl)suberoamide and 1,4-naphthalenedicarboxylic acid (1,4-H2NDC), the irreversible structural transformation was ascribed to the changes of bonding modes of the 1,4-NDC2− ligand,17 while the reversible structural transformation in the CPs containing the bis(N-pyrid-3-ylmethyl)suberoamide ligand and 1,4-NDC2− ligand was proposed due to the reorientation of amide oxygen atoms that results in the ligand conformation change upon heating in water and ethanol, respectively.18 Calderón-Casado et al. reported the reversible crystal-to-amorphous transformation induced by solvent exchange in monomers [Ni(PDC)((py)2C(OH)2)(ROH)]·xROH (R = H, Me, Et, iPr) where PDC is pyridine-2,5-dicarboxylate(2−) packed through hydrogen bonds and π interactions.19 In this report, we have demonstrated a reversible structural transformation between a 1D ladder and a 2D structure where the coordinated solvent and cocrystallized solvent are exchanged, which is rare in structural transformation.

Herein, semi-rigid N,N′-bis(3-pyridyl)terephthalamide (L1) and N,N′-bis(4-pyridyl)terephthalamide (L2) with phenyl groups as spacers, Fig. 1, were used to react with the dicarboxylic acid and divalent metal salts in various solvents to investigate the effect of rigidity and the donor atom position of the spacer ligands on the structural diversity as well as the impact on the properties. The syntheses, structures and properties of {[Cd(L1)(5-tert-IPA)(H2O)]·2H2O}n (5-tert-H2IPA = 5-tert-butylbenzene-1,3-dicarboxylic acid), 1, {[Ni(L1)1.5(5-tert-IPA)(H2O)2]·2.5H2O}n, 2, {[Ni(L1)(5-tert-IPA)(CH3OH)]·2CH3OH}n, 3, [Zn(L1)0.5(5-tert-IPA)]n, 4, and {[Ni(L2)(5-tert-IPA)(H2O)]·2H2O}n, 5, form the subject of this report. Complexes 2 and 3 exhibit reversible structural transformation upon coordinated and cocrystallized solvent exchange and the factors that govern the structural transformation will be discussed.

Experimental

General procedures

IR spectra (KBr disk) were obtained from a JASCO FT/IR-460 plus spectrometer. Elemental analyses were performed on a PE 2400 series II CHNS/O analyzer or an Elementar Vario EL-III analyzer. Thermal gravimetric analysis (TGA) measurements were carried out on an SII Nano Technology Inc. TGA/DTA 6200 over the temperature range of 30 to 800 °C at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 under nitrogen atmosphere. UV-vis spectra were obtained from a Varian Cary 100 spectrometer and emission spectra were recorded on a Hitachi F-4500 spectrometer. Powder X-ray diffraction patterns were measured on a Bruker D2 PHASER diffractometer with CuKα (λα = 1.54 Å) radiation.

N,N′-bis(3-pyridyl)terephthalamide (L1) and N,N′-bis(4-pyridyl)terephthalamide (L2) were prepared according to published procedures.15

Structural transformation from 2 to 3

A 23 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel autoclave was sealed with complex 2 (0.050 g, 5.97 × 10−5 mole) and 10 mL of CH3OH, which was then heated at 120 °C for 2 days and then the reaction system was cooled to room temperature at a rate of 2 °C per hour. The crystal in the container was then collected, washed with CH3OH and dried under a vacuum. Yield: 0.026 g (63%).

Structural transformation from 3 to 2

A 23 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel autoclave was sealed with complex 3 (0.050 g, 7.21 × 10−5 mole) and 10 mL of H2O, which was then heated at 120 °C for 2 days and then the reaction system was cooled to room temperature at a rate of 2 °C per hour. The crystal in the container was then collected, washed with H2O and dried under a vacuum. Yield: 0.020 g (33%).

Powder X-ray analysis

In order to check the purity of the large number of products, powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) experiments were carried out for all the complexes, Fig. S1–S5,† where the black and red lines represent the simulation and experimental patterns for the complexes. The peak positions of the experimental PXRD patterns and simulated ones are in agreement with each other, indicating the bulk purities of the complexes. The differences in intensity may be owing to the preferred orientation of the powder samples.

X-ray crystallography

The single crystal X-ray diffraction data for complexes 1–5 were collected on a Bruker AXS SMART APEX II CCD or Bruker D8 QUEST PHOTON III CPAD diffractometer at 296 K, which was equipped with a graphite-monochromated Mo Kα (λα = 0.71073 Å) radiation source.20 Data reduction was carried out by using standard methods with well-established computational procedures. The structure factors were obtained after Lorentz and polarization corrections. Empirical absorption correction based on “multi-scan” was applied to the data for all the complexes. The position of some of the heavier atoms was located by the direct method. The remaining atoms were found in a series of alternating difference Fourier maps and least-squares refinements, while the hydrogen atoms except those of the water molecules were added by using the HADD command in SHELXTL 6.1012.21Table 1 lists the crystal data for 1–5.

Results and discussion

Syntheses

All the complexes were prepared by hydro(solvo)thermal reactions by modifying the metal salts, ligands and solvents to analyze the structural diversity and their properties. Complexes 1 and 4 were prepared by using L1 and 5-tert-H2IPA in MeOH/H2O (1 : 1) with Cd(OAc)2·2H2O and Zn(OAc)2·2H2O, while 2 and 5 by using Ni(OAc)2·4H2O and 5-tert-H2IPA in NaOH solution with L1 and L2, respectively, and 3 with L1 in MeOH.

Crystal structure of 1

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis shows that 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/c and the asymmetric unit consists of one Cd(ii) cation, one L1 ligand, one 5-tert-IPA2− ligand, one coordinated water molecule, and two lattice water molecules. Fig. 2(a) shows a drawing showing the coordination environment of the Cd(ii) ion, which is six-coordinated by four oxygen atoms [Cd–O = 2.2063(16) − 2.5730(15) Å] from two 5-tert-IPA2− ligands and one water molecule, and two pyridyl nitrogen atoms from two L1 ligands [Cd–N = 2.2927(18) − 2.4073(18) Å], resulting in a distorted octahedral geometry. The Cd(ii) ions are linked by 5-tert-IPA2− to form 1D chains, Fig. 2(b), which are further doubly bridged by the L1 ligands to form a 1D chain with metallocycles. Considering the Cd atoms as 4-connected nodes and L1 and 5-tert-IPA2− ligands as 2-connected nodes, the structure of 1 can be regarded as a 1D net with the unique {4·84·12}{4}{8}-2,2,4C1 topology, Fig. 2(c). The structure of 1 can be further simplified as a 1D ladder if the L1 and 5-tert-IPA2− ligands are considered as linkers.

Crystal structure of 2

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis shows that 2 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/c, with each asymmetric unit consisting of one Ni(ii) cation, one and a half L1 ligands, one 5-tert-IPA2− ligand, two coordinated water molecules and two and a half lattice water molecules. Fig. 3(a) depicts a drawing showing the coordination environment of the Ni(ii) ion, which is six-coordinated by one oxygen atom from the 5-tert-IPA2− ligand, two oxygen atoms from two water molecules [Ni–O = 2.039(2) − 2.113(2) Å] and three pyridyl nitrogen atoms from three L1 ligands [Ni–N = 2.125(3) − 2.133(3) Å], resulting in a distorted octahedral geometry. The Ni(ii) cations are connected with L1 and 5-tert-IPA2− ligands to afford 1D ladders, Fig. 3(b), which can be regarded as a 1D net with the {82·12}2{8}3-2,2,3C4 topology.22 Moreover, the dangling 5-tert-IPA2− ligands of one ladder interdigitate to the others, Fig. 3(c), supported by the O–H–O [H–O = 1.7875(2) Å; ∠O–H–O = 167.213(10)°] hydrogen bonds, Fig. 3(d).

Crystal structure of 3

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis shows that 3 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/n. The asymmetric unit is comprised of one Ni(ii) cation, one L1 ligand, one 5-tert-IPA2− ligand, one coordinated methanol and two lattice methanol molecules. Fig. 4(a) depicts a drawing showing the coordination environment of the Ni(ii) ion, which is six-coordinated by three oxygen atoms from two 5-tert-IPA2− ligands, one oxygen atom from the methanol molecule [Ni–O = 2.035(3) − 2.171(3) Å] and two pyridyl nitrogen atoms from two L1 ligands [Ni–N = 2.047(4) − 2.086(4) Å], resulting in a distorted octahedral geometry. The Ni(ii) ions are further linked together by the L1 and 5-tert-IPA2− ligands to afford a 2D layer, Fig. 4(b). If the Ni(ii) cations are defined as 4-connected nodes, and the 5-tert-IPA2− and L1 ligands as 2-connected nodes, the structure of 3 can be simplified as a 2D net with the {84·122}{8}2-2,4L2 topology, Fig. 4(c). Further inspection of the structure shows the pairs of interdigitated 2D nets, Fig. 4(d), which are supported by the N–H–O [H–O = 2.0067(1) Å; ∠N–H–O = 164.058(5)°] hydrogen bonds, Fig. 4(e).

Crystal structure of 4

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis shows that 4 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/c and each asymmetric unit consists of one Zn(ii) cation, one half L1 ligand and one 5-tert-IPA2− ligand. Fig. 5(a) depicts a drawing showing the coordination environment of the Zn(ii) ion, which is four-coordinated by three oxygen atoms from three 5-tert-IPA2− ligands [Zn–O = 1.9182(13) − 1.9434(13) Å] and one pyridyl nitrogen atom from one L1 ligand [Zn–N = 2.0544(15) Å], resulting in a distorted tetrahedral geometry, with a weak Zn–O interaction of 2.8599 (0) Å, which is significantly shorter than the sum of the van der Waals radius of Zn (1.39 Å) and O (1.52 Å). The metal atoms are linked by the 5-tert-IPA2− ligands to form various dinuclear units and the distances between the two Zn(ii) metal centers are 6.6046(1) and 3.7905(1) Å for the large and small rings, respectively, Fig. 5(b). The 1D looped chains are further linked by the L1 ligands to form a 3D framework. If the Zn atoms are considered as 4-connected nodes and the 5-tert-IPA2− ligands as 3-connected nodes, while the L1 ligands as linkers, the structure of 4 can be simplified as a 3,4-connected net with the {42·6·102·12}{42·6}-coe-3,4-C2/c topology, Fig. 5(c), which also shows 3-fold interpenetration, Fig. 5(d). There are 22 complexes with the {42·6·102·12}{42·6}-coe-3,4-C2/c topology and four of them are 3-fold interpenetrated.22 Considering the small dinuclear Zn(ii) units as 4-connected nodes, the structure of complex 4 can be further simplified as a 4-connected net with the cds topology, Fig. 5(e). The 3D net penetrates the adjacent two units to form a 3D structure with 3-fold interpenetration, Fig. 5(f).

Crystal structure of 5

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis shows that 5 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P with each asymmetric unit consisting of one Ni(ii) cation, one L2 ligand, one 5-tert-IPA2− ligand, one coordinated water molecule and two lattice water molecules. Fig. 6(a) depicts a drawing showing the coordination environment of the Ni(ii) ion, which is six-coordinated by three oxygen atoms [Ni–O = 2.004(3) − 2.177(3) Å] from two 5-tert-IPA2− ligands and one water molecule and two pyridyl nitrogen atoms from two L2 ligands [Ni–N = 2.105(3) − 2.112(3) Å], resulting in a distorted octahedral geometry. The Cd(ii) ions are linked by the 5-tert-IPA2− ligands to afford 1D chains, which are further connected by the L ligands to form a 2D layer, Fig. 6(b). If the Cd(ii) ions are considered as 4-connection nodes and the tert-IPA2− and L2 ligands as linkers, the structure of 5 can be simplified as a 2D net with the sql topology, Fig. 6(c).

Ligand conformations and bonding modes

The relative orientations of the two CO groups determine cis and trans arrangement. When the CO groups appear in the same direction, it is defined as cis; on the other hand, the opposite direction is defined as trans, Fig. 7. Due to the difference in the orientations of the pyridyl nitrogen atoms, three more orientations, anti–anti, syn–anti and syn–syn, based on the relative positions of the pyridyl nitrogen and amide oxygen atoms, can also be shown for L1, Fig. 8.15 Accordingly, the ligand conformations of L1 and L2 in 1–5 are listed in Table 2, which also shows the bonding mode of the 5-tert-IPA2− ligands.

The 5-tert-IPA2− ligands in 1–5 adopt three different coordination modes, Fig. 9. While the 5-tert-IPA2− ligands in 2 and 4 bond to one Ni(ii) and three Zn(ii) ions, respectively, those in 1, 3 and 5 bridge two. Moreover, the two carboxylate groups of the 5-tert-IPA2− ligands in 1, 3 and 5 coordinate to the M(ii) (Cd, 1; Ni, 3 and 5) ions through one and two oxygen atoms (coordination mode I), respectively, and one of the carboxylate groups of 2 coordinates to the Ni(ii) ion through one oxygen atom, leaving the other one uncoordinated (coordination mode II), while each of the two carboxylate groups in 4 chelates and bridges the Zn(ii) ion (coordination mode III).

Structural comparison

Complexes 1 and 4 are good examples to analyze the structural diversity caused by the metal center and 2 and 3 are by the solvent nature which contribute to the ligand conformation of the L1 ligand and the bonding mode of the 5-tert-IPA2− ligand, resulting in 1D nets with the 2,2,4C1 and 2,2,3C4 topologies, a 2D net with the 2,4L2 topology and a 3D net with the cds topology for 1–4, respectively. Moreover, structural comparisons of 2, 3 and 5 with Ni(ii) and 5-tert-IPA2− further verify the important effect of the orientation of the donor atom positions (L1vs.L2) on the structural diversity.

The structural diversity in 2 and 3 caused by the nature of the solvent motivated the study of structural transformation and it has been studied by using solvothermal reactions, vide infra. The exchange of both coordinated and cocrystallized solvents with the same solvent on reversible structural transformation between 2 and 3 has been studied and verified by powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns and FT-IR spectra.

Thermal properties

Thermal gravimetric analysis (TGA) was carried out to examine the thermal decomposition of 1–5, which was recorded at 25–800 °C and 10 °C min−1 under a N2 atmosphere, Fig. S6–S10† and Table 3. The TGA curve of 1 shows the gradual weight loss of two cocrystallized water molecules and one coordinated water molecule (calculated 7.65%, observed 7.37%) at 150–230 °C. The weight loss of 80.61% at 330–800 °C corresponds to the decomposition of one L1 ligand and one 5-tert-IPA2− ligand (calculated 84.50%). The TGA curve of 2 shows the gradual weight loss of two and a half cocrystallized water molecules and two coordinated water molecules (calculated 9.67%, observed 9.50%) at 40–230 °C. The weight loss of 82.52% at 295–780 °C corresponds to the decomposition of one and a half L1 ligands and one 5-tert-IPA−2 ligand (calculated 83.58%). The TGA curve of 3 shows the gradual weight loss of two cocrystallized methanol molecules and one coordinated methanol molecule (calculated 13.86%, observed 12.92%) at 50–210 °C. The weight loss of 71.10% at 305–800 °C corresponds to the decomposition of one L1 ligand and one 5-tert-IPA2− ligand (calculated 77.88%). The TGA curve of 4 shows the gradual weight loss of 80.96% at 190–800 °C, which corresponds to the decomposition of a half L1 ligand and one 5-tert-IPA2− ligand (calculated 85.44%). The TGA curve of 5 shows a weight loss at 30–280 °C (calculated 7.74%, observed 5.99%), which indicates the removal of the cocrystallized and coordinated water molecules. The weight loss of 82.40% at 345–625 °C corresponds to the decomposition of L2 and 5-tert-IPA2− (calculated 82.91%).

Structural transformation

Complexes 2 and 3 provide a unique opportunity for the investigation of structural transformation due to solvent exchange between H2O and CH3OH under hydrothermal conditions. Complex 2 was first solvothermally reacted with CH3OH at 120 °C for two days. After allowing to cool to room temperature, the crystals were collected and investigated by measuring their PXRD patterns and IR spectra. Fig. 10 shows that the experimental PXRD pattern matches well with the simulated PXRD pattern of 3 and similar counterparts can also be observed for their IR spectra, Fig. S11,† indicating the structural transformation from 2 to 3 (conversion rate = 63%). On the other hand, the hydrothermal reaction of 3 in H2O for two days demonstrates the transformation from 3 to 2 (conversion rate = 33%), as shown by measuring the experimental PXRD patterns, Fig. 11, and IR spectra, Fig. S12.† The reversible structural transformations in 2 and 3 upon solvent exchange between H2O and CH3OH are thus verified, Fig. 12. The exchange of coordinated and co-crystallized solvents between 2 and 3 is the peculiar behavior on the structural transformation.

Structural transformations in complexes 2 and 3 can also be performed through the desolvated products of 2 and 3. The crystals of 2 and 3 were first heated separately at 240 °C to prepare the desolvated products, which were then immersed in CH3OH and H2O for five days, respectively. On heating complex 2, it loses coordinated and co-crystallized water molecules at 240 °C and converts into its amorphous state, which is immersed in CH3OH, affording 3, Fig. 13. In contrast, immersion of the desolvated product of complex 3 in NaOH solution gave 2, Fig. 14.

The reversible structural transformation in 2 and 3 due to the solvent exchange under hydrothermal conditions can be regarded as the disconnection and connection of the rung L1 ligands from a ladder structure having trans anti–anti conformation in 2 and coordination of the dangling and interdigitating carboxylate ligands to the Ni(ii) ions of the adjacent ladder to transfer into 3 by the influence of MeOH.

Similarly, a pair of interdigitated 2D nets of 3 undergo the disconnection of the carboxylate ligand and the release of L1 ligand, thereby changing its conformation from cis syn–syn to trans anti–anti that connects each individual chain to convert into 2 with the support of water. The dangling and interdigitating carboxylate ligands in 2 and the interdigitated 2D net in 3 are thus important arrangements in the structural transformations that are triggered by the exchange of solvents.

Luminescence properties

The CPs of metal ions with d10 electronic configuration usually have luminescence properties, where the metal ion may enhance, shift, and quench the emission of organic ligands, which are of great interest due to the potential applications of these CPs in areas such as sensors and displays. Table 4 summarizes the luminescence properties of complexes 1 and 4 and the ligands L1 and 5-tert-H2IPA and Fig. S13–S16† show their excitation and emission spectra. Free L1 and 5-tert-H2IPA show emission at 431 and 322 nm, upon excitation at 276 and 273 nm, respectively, which can be ascribed to the intraligand (IL) n → π* or π → π* transitions, while complexes 1 and 4 show emission at 450 and 400 nm, upon excitation at 276/384 and 275/331 nm, respectively. It is well known that the Zn(ii) and Cd(ii) ions are hardly susceptible to oxidation or reduction and it is thus not possible that the emission of 1 and 4 is due to ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT) or metal-to-ligand charge transfer (MLCT).23 Therefore, this emission may be attributed to intraligand or ligand-to-ligand charge transfer (LLCT). While comparing the emissions of 1 and 4 with L1 and 6-tert-H2IPA, those of 1 and 4 are mainly due to IL or LLCT transitions of the L1 ligand, which has a comparable maximum emission peak. Moreover, the red shifts of the emission peaks in 1 and 4 compared to that of L1 are probably attributed to the different configurations, coordination environments and bonding types upon coordination.

Conclusions

Five new divalent CPs containing the rigid bpba and polycarboxylate ligands have been synthesized and characterized. Complexes 1 and 2 display 1D ladders with the 2,2,4C1 and 2,2,3C4 topologies, while 3 and 5 are 2D layers showing 2,4L2 and sql topologies, and 4 is a 3D framework with the cds topology, respectively. Structural comparisons of 1–5 demonstrate that metal identity, solvent nature and ligand isomerism play important roles in determining the structural diversity. Reversible structural transformations in 2 and 3 can be carried out hydrothermally in CH3OH and H2O, respectively, which are triggered by the exchange of solvents that results in the alternations of the bonding modes of 5-tert-IPA2− ligands that are accompanied by change in the conformation of the L1 ligands. Structural transformation can also be performed through the desolvated products of 2 and 3. The reversible structural transformations in 2 and 3 demonstrate the importance of the various bonding modes of the 5-tert-IPA2− ligands as well as the semi-rigidity of the L1 ligands in the structural changes. The exchange of coordinated and co-crystallized solvents between 2 and 3 reveals a peculiar phenomenon in the structural transformation.
