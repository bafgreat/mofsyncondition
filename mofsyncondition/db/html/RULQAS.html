Coordination polymers assembled from semirigid fluorene-based ligand: A couple of enantiomers

Abstract

A couple of Mg(II)-based coordination polymer enantiomers [MgL(DMF)(H2O)3]n (R-MgL and S-MgL), and a Zn(II)-based coordination polymer [ZnL(DMF)]n (ZnL) have been synthesized by the solvothermal reactions between the achiral ligand 4,4′-(9,9-dimethyl-9H-fluorene-2,7-diyl)dibenzoic acid (H2L) and the corresponding metal salts. The MgL was obtained as the racemic conglomerate from the one pot reaction. The single crystal X-ray structural analyses reveal that MgL crystallize in the chiral space group P21 and possesses the right- or left-handed homochiral 1D Mg–O–C helical chain. The ZnL crystallize in the non-centrosymmetrical space group Aba2 and possesses the 2D network comprised of 1D Zn–O–C meso-helical chains and ligands. The MgL and ZnL complexes exhibit strong coordination-perturbed ligand-centered blue emissions when excited at 320 nm. Their second-order nonlinear optical effects and thermal properties have also been studied.

1. Introduction

Fluorene derivatives display significant modificability in both molecular structures and luminescence properties. In the past two decades, a large amount of researches and studies on structure modification and adjustment of luminescent properties of fluorene derivatives have been reported [1], [2], [3]. The luminescence property of a fluorene derivative is governed by the electronic property [4], which is closely related with molecular configuration [5], [6], [7]. So, various elegant strategies have been developed to design new fluorene derivatives for potential applications, for example, LED [8], [9] and sensing [10]. Miteva et al. reported the polyfluorene homopolymers, for which the emission with tunable wavelength has been demonstrated with impressive efficiency via end-capping [11].

Coordination polymers (CPs) which have infinite 1D chains or 2D or 3D networks that composed of metal ions and organic ligands have been an active research area because of their multi-functional properties [12] and potential applications in material science [13], [14] such as sensing [15], [16], catalysis [17], and nano-materials [18]. Among all of the factors that could impose effects on the construction of CPs, ligands have drawn great attention as they are the most important influencing factor. It is known that ligands usually induce significantly diversities on both structures and properties of CPs by only tiny changes in molecular structure. As continuation of our previous work, we dedicate to prepare functional coordination polymers constructed from new fluorene-based ligands that exhibit strong luminescence. The A–B–A type molecules (A and B connected by single covalent bond, A and B are the different aromatic systems. In this work, B is fluorene.) attracted our attention. Due to the rotation of A relative to B through the covalent bond between A and B, the conformations of the molecules could be devided into two types. When two A rotate along the same direction, the cis-conformation is expected to be observed. On the contrary, the trans-conformation is to be observed. Thus, unlike 2,7-fluorenedicarboxylic acid [19], [20], the longer and semirigid fluorene-based A–B–A ligands should in principle be subject to a large abundance of luminescence properties and accommodate a wide range of network topologies. However, only a few coordination polymers constructed from fluorene-based A–B–A ligands are known so far. Zhang et al. [21] had reported a serial of nano coordination polymers that constructed from fluorene-based A–B–A ligands, for which the single crystals were not obtained. Chen et al. had reported two coordination polymers based on 5,5′-(9H-fluorene-2,7-diyl)diisophthalic acid [22] and 5,5′-(9,9-dimethyl-9H-fluorene-2,7-diyl)diisophthalic acid [23] (in this case, A is isophthalic acid), in which the ligand exhibits cis-conformation. Dong et al. [24] had reported a Cu-coordination polymer based on the fluorene-based A–B–A ligand with pyridine as A, in which the ligand exhibits trans-conformation. Herein, we report three coordination polymers, R-MgL, S-MgL and ZnL, which constructed from 4,4′-(9,9-dimethyl-9H-fluorene-2,7-diyl)dibenzoic acid (H2L, Scheme 1). These coordination polymers were characterized by single crystal X-ray diffraction, X-ray powder diffraction, TGA and fluorescence.

2. Experimental

2.1. General

The chemicals used in this work are of analytical grade and available commercially and were used without further purification.

The FT-IR spectra were measured with KBr pellets in the range of 4000–400 cm−1 on a PerkinElmer FT-IR Spectrometer. Elemental analyses for C, H and N were performed on a Perkin-Elmer 240C analyzer. The thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were performed in a SHIMADZU DTG-60 simultaneous DTA-TG apparatus instrument, under dynamic N2 atmosphere (20 mL/min) and heating rate of 10 °C/min from room temperature to 800 °C. The 1H and 13C NMR spectra in CDCl3 or d6-DMSO were recorded at room temperature on a Bruker 400 M NMR spectrometer with tetramethylsilane (TMS) used as internal standard. Powder X-ray diffraction investigation on polycrystalline MgL and ZnL were carried out with a Bruker D8 advanced diffractometer equipped with a diffracted-beamed monochromator set for Cu Kα (λ=1.5418 Å) radiation. The data were collected using a Ni-filtered Cu-target tube at room temperature in the 2θ range from 5° to 50° at an angular rate of 0.1 s/step, with a scan step width of 0.02°. The circular dichroism spectra were recorded on a JASCO J-810 spectropolarimeter with KBr pellets. The second-order nonlinear optical effects were determined on a LAB130 Pulsed Nd:YAG laser. Photoluminescent spectra were measured using a PerkinElmer LS55 Fluorescence Spectrometer.

2.2. Synthesis of H2L

9,9-dimethyl-9H-2,7-dipinacolborylfluorene: the solution of 9,9-dimethyl-9H-2,7-dibromofluorene (17.6 g, 50 mmol) in anhydrous THF (100 mL) was stirred and cooled to −78 °C, to which the n-Butyllithium (2.5 M, 44 mL) was added over a period of 30 min. Two hours later isopropoxyboronic acid pinacol ester (25.5 mL) was added and the mixture was still stirred at room temperature for two hours. After the addition of NH4Cl (20%, 20 mL), the solvents were removed and the residue was extracted by dichloromethane. The solvent was removed in a vacuum and the crude product was pure enough for next step directly. Yield: 90%. 1H NMR (CDCl3) δ 1.38(s, 24H), 1.52(s, 6H), 7.76(d, 2H), 7.82(q, 2H), 7.89(s, 2H) (Fig. S2).

Dimethyl 4,4′-(9,9-dimethyl-9H-fluorene-2,7-diyl)dibenzoate: the mixture of 9,9-dimethyl-9H-2,7-dipinacolborylfluorene (8.9 g, 20 mmol), methyl 4-Iodolbenzoate (11.5 g, 44 mmol), K2CO3 (13.8 g, 100 mmol), 3% [Pd(PPh3)4] and 12% PPh3 were deaerated under nitrogen for 20 min. Then fresh anhydrous THF (200 mL) and methanol (100 mL) were added to the mixture. The mixture was heated at 80 °C for 3 days under N2. After cooling to room temperature, 50 mL water was added, the solid was separated, washed with water and extracted with dichloromethane. The CH2Cl2 solution was evaporated to dryness. White powders (8.5 g) were obtained. Yield: 92%. 1H NMR (CDCl3) δ 1.61(s, 6H), 3.96(s, 6H), 7.64(d, 2H), 7.70(s, 2H), 7.75(d, 4H), 7.84(d, 2H), 8.14(d, 4H) (Fig. S3).

4,4′-(9,9-dimethyl-9H-fluorene-2,7-diyl)dibenzoic acid: the dimethyl 4,4′-(9,9-dimethyl-9H-fluorene-2,7-diyl)dibenzoate (0.46 g, 1 mmol) was suspended in methanol (20 mL), to which 20 mL of 20% NaOH solution was added. The mixture was stirred under 100 °C for 3 days and boiling water (200 mL) was added. Then the solution was acidified using 2 M HCl solution. The precipitate was collected by filtration, washed with water, and dried to give 4,4′-(9,9-dimethyl-9H-fluorene-2,7-diyl)dibenzoic acid (0.41 g). Yield: 95%. 1H NMR (6d DMSO) δ 1.56(s, 6H), 7.74(d, 2H), 7.89(d, 4H), 7.98(t, 4H), 8.04(d, 4H) (Fig. S4).

2.3. Synthesis of MgL

A mixture of H2L (0.05 mmol, 21.7 mg), Mg(NO3)2·6H2O (0.1 mmol), DMF (5 mL), ethanol (2 mL) and H2O (1 mL) was sealed in a 15 mL Parr Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel and heated at 80 °C for 2 days. Colorless crystals were obtained in 85% yield, which were washed with ethanol, and dried under ambient condition. Elemental analysis calc. for C32H33MgO8N: C, 65.82; H, 5.70; N, 2.40%. Found: C, 65.72; H, 5.53; N, 2.58%. IR (KBr, cm−1) 3430, 2974, 1671, 1593, 1542, 1407, 787, 870, 838, 702 (Fig S5).

2.4. Synthesis of ZnL

ZnL was prepared by a method similar to that of MgL by using Zn(NO3)2·6H2O instead of Mg(NO3)2·6H2O. Colorless crystals were obtained in 80% yield. Elemental analysis calc. for C32H27ZnO5N: C, 67.32; H, 4.77; N, 2.45%. Found: C, 67.22; H, 4.89; N, 2.31%. IR (KBr, cm−1) 2975, 1654, 1600, 1541, 1406, 779, 870, 836 (Fig S5).

2.5. X-ray crystallography

X-ray diffraction data of the single crystals of R-MgL, S-MgL and ZnL were collected on a Bruker Smart Apex CCD area detector diffractometer using graphite-monochromated Mo-K radiation (λ= 0.71073 Å) at room temperature. Cell parameters were retrieved using SMART software and refined using SAINT [25] on all observed reflections. Data were collected using a narrow-frame method with scan widths of 0.30° in and an exposure time of 15 s/frame. The highly redundant data sets were reduced using SAINT and corrected for Lorentz and polarization effects. Absorption corrections were applied using SADABS [26] supplied by Bruker. Structures were solved by direct methods using the program SHELX-97 [27] program package. The positions of the metal atoms and their first coordination spheres were located from direct-method E maps; other non-hydrogen atoms were found using alternating difference Fourier syntheses and least-squared refinement cycles and, during the final cycles, were refined anisotropically. Hydrogen atoms were placed in calculated positions and refined as riding atoms with a uniform value of Uiso. Crystallographic details for R-MgL, S-MgL and ZnL have been summarized in Table 1. Selected bond lengths and angles for R-MgL and S-MgL are listed in Table S1 and for ZnL listed in Table S2. CCDC reference numbers for R-MgL, S-MgL and ZnL are 1,062,090, 1,062,086 and 1,062,087, respectively.

3. Results and discussion

For both MgL and ZnL, the powder patterns of the bulk samples are well matched with those calculated from their single-crystal structure data (Fig. S1). No signal in solid-state circular dichroism (CD) spectroscopy for powder sample of MgL is observed, suggesting that the MgL crystallized as the racemic conglomerate. According to the principle proposed by Kurtz and Perry [28], we estimated the second-order nonlinear optical effects of MgL and ZnL, and they show the second harmonic generation (SHG) efficiencies with the values of 0.4 and 0.8 times that of urea, respectively.

3.1. Crystal structures

The R-MgL and S-MgL are a couple of coordination polymer enantiomers and the single-crystal X-ray diffraction analyses reveal they crystallize in the chiral space group P21. As shown in Fig. 1a, the asymmetric unit of MgL contains one magnesium(II) ion, one L2− ligand, one coordinated DMF molecule and three coordinated water molecules. The carbon atoms in DMF are disordered over two sites with about 80%: 20% split occupancies. The Mg(II) cation has a distorted octahedral coordination geometry and is six-coordinated by six oxygen atoms: four from coordinated DMF (O8) and water molecules (O5, O6 and O7), two from two L2− ligands (O3i for R-MgL, O3ii for S-MgL and O4, symmetry codes: ix, y−1/2, 1−z; ii 2-x, y+1/2, 1−z), respectively. The ligand in MgL exhibits tran-conformation. The μ2-L2− ligand uses only two oxygen atoms from a carboxylate group to bridge two Mg(II) ions to lead to the right-handed homochiral 1D Mg–O–C helical chain for R-MgL (Figs. 1b and 3a), left-handed helical chain for S-MgL. Fig. 1c shows the crystal packing for R-MgL. The Mg–O bond lengths vary from 2.043(2) to 2.106(2) Å, which are comparable to those reported for other Mg–O complexes [29], [30].

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that ZnL crystallizes in the non-centrosymmetrical space group Aba2. As shown in Fig. 2a, the asymmetric unit of ZnL contains one zinc(II) ion, one L2− ligand and one coordinated DMF molecule. The Zn(II) cation is five coordinated by four oxygen atoms from three L2− ligands (O1, O2, O3i and O4ii, symmetry codes: i 1/2−x, y+1/2, z; iix, y+1/2, z+1/2) and one from DMF molecule (O5) with a distorted square pyramidal coordination geometry (O4ii in the axial position). The ligand in ZnL exhibits cis-conformation. The μ3-L2− ligand bidentate chelates to a Zn(II) ion by a carboxylate group and bridges another two Zn(II) ions by the second carboxylate group. The Zn(II) ions are linked by the carboxylate groups to form 1D Zn–O–C meso-helical chains, which are further linked by the L2− ligands to form the 2D networks (Figs. 2b and 3b). The projection drawing in ab plane of the meso-helical chain is a lemniscate. As shown in Fig. 2c, the adjacent layers are stacked in an offset -ABAB- manner. The Zn–O bond lengths are in the range of 1.954(2)–2.248(3) Å, which are comparable to those reported for other Zn–O complexes [31], [32].

The two benzene rings of L2− ligand have a dihedral angle of 69.7°, and the dihedral angles are 38.9° and 147.8° between two benzene rings and fluorene unit in MgL polymer, respectively. The two benzene rings of L2− ligand have a dihedral angle of 30.0°, and the dihedral angles are 33.3° and 36.8° between two benzene rings and fluorene unit in ZnL polymer, respectively. So the ZnL polymer is more conjugated than MgL polymer. Interestingly, all of the five coordination polymers with the fluorene-based A–B–A ligands crystallize in the non-centrosymmetrical space groups, of which only the MgL crystallizes in the chiral space group [22], [23], [24].

3.2. Thermal properties

In order to study the thermal stability of the coordination polymers, thermogravimetric analyses (TG) were also investigated (Fig. 4). In the temperature range of 25–200 °C, MgL undergoes weight loss of 22.8%, corresponding to the departure of the coordinated DMF and three water molecules and is in agreement with calculated value of 21.8%. The decomposition of ligand starts at about 480 °C. For ZnL, a weight loss of 14.4% (calculated 12.8%) is observed from room temperature to 297 °C, corresponding to the departure of the coordinated DMF, and the decomposition of ligand starts at about 430 °C.

3.3. Luminescent properties

Excitation of free ligand H2L, racemic conglomerate of R-MgL and S-MgL as well as ZnL with 320 nm resulted in intense blue emissions in the solid state at the room temperature (Fig. 5). Intense emission of the free H2L was observed with wavelength from 378 to 550 nm (λmax=423 nm). The maximum emission wavelength of the racemic conglomerate of R-MgL and S-MgL was 407 nm, blue-shifted by about 16 nm. While that of the ZnL was 411 nm, blue-shifted by about 12 nm. The ligand in the Zn(II) polymer is more conjugated than that in Mg(II) polymer and the Zn(II) polymer exhibits the less blue-shift emission. The blue-shift of the emissions observed in these coordination polymers should attribute to the decrease in the degree of conjugation for ligands, due to the rotation of benzene rings relative to the fluorene unit as observed in MgL and ZnL, in order to meet the requirement of coordination and decrease the system's energy.

3.4. IR spectra

The presence of water in MgL is confirmed by the broad band at 3430 cm−1 characteristic of O–H vibration. For MgL and ZnL, the low intensity bands between 3000 and 2840 cm−1 show the symmetric and asymmetric C–H stretching vibrations of −CH3, and the bands in the region between 1780 and 1630 cm−1 could be assigned to C=O vibration of DMF molecules. The typical bands in the region between 1630 and 1480 cm−1 are due to the C–C stretching vibrations of aromatic rings. Bands in the 1450–1320 cm−1 region represent the symmetric COO− stretching.

4. Conclusion

In summary, as we expected, the hydrothermal reactions of Mg(II) or Zn(II) salts with 4,4′-(9,9-dimethyl-9H-fluorene-2,7-diyl)dibenzoic acid have afforded a couple of coordination polymer enantiomers, R-MgL and S-MgL and a Zn(II)-based coordination polymer ZnL. In MgL, there exist the right- or left-handed homochiral 1D Mg–O–C helical chain. However, in the 2D network of ZnL, the 1D Zn–O–C meso-helical chain was observed. Both the MgL and ZnL exhibit strong ligand-centered blue emission and the second-order nonlinear optical effects. Subsequent works will be focused on the structures and properties of a series of coordination polymers constructed by semirigid ligands containing fluorene.
