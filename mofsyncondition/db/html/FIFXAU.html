Syntheses and characterizations of four mixed-ligand hydrogen bonding supramolecular architectures with different structural motifs

Author links open overlay panelRuihuWang
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ica.2004.09.049Get rights and content

Abstract

The reactions of 4-aminobenzoic acid (4-abaH), 4,4′-bipyridine (4,4′-bipy) and transitional metal ions (ZnII, MnII and CuII) gave rise to four supramolecular architectures, namely, [(4-abaH)2(4,4′-bipy)] (1), {[Zn2(4,4′-bipy)2(4-aba)4] (H2O)5}n (2), {[Mn(4,4′-bipy)2(H2O)4] (4-aba)Br(H2O)3} (3) and {[Cu2(4,4′-bipy)3(H2O)2(4-aba)2](NO3)2(H2O)4}n (4). Their crystal structures were determined by X-ray diffraction and show different structural motifs. 1 is a one-dimensional hydrogen bonding ladder constructed by 4-abaH and 4,4′-bipy. In 2, 4,4′-bipy bridges Zn(4-aba)2 units forming a one-dimensional zigzag chain, which is extended into a three-dimensional framework by crystalline water molecules through hydrogen bonding interactions. Three-dimensional network of 3 is constructed by mononuclear [Mn(4,4′-bipy)2(H2O)4]2+ cations, neutral crystalline water molecules, and 4-aba and Br− anions through extensive hydrogen bonding and π–π interactions. However, one-dimensional ladder formed by 4,4′-bipy and Cu(4-aba) units in 4 is extended into a three-dimensional architecture through interpenetration of the lateral 4-aba arms into squares of the adjacent Cu–(4,4′-bipy) ladders and extensive hydrogen bonding interactions.

1. Introduction

Design and syntheses of organic and metal–organic compounds with unusual and tailorable structures are fundamental steps to discover and fabricate various functional supramolecular devices or technologically useful materials [1], [2], [3], [4]. A considerable progress has recently been made on the crystal engineering of supramolecular architectures organized and sustained by means of coordinate covalent, supramolecular contacts (such as hydrogen bonds, π–π interaction), aurophilicity interaction and so on [5], [6], [7], [8], [9]. The current interest has been focused on the controlled assembly of donor and acceptor building blocks in order to generate an entirely supramolecular polymer. In this context, the rational design of organic ligands and judicious selection of coordination geometry of metal ions have crucial effect on the formation of desirable frameworks. As an excellent building block in the design and construction of supramolecular polymers, 4,4′-bipyridine (4,4′-bipy) has been relatively well-known. It can not only act as an exo-bidentate bridging ligand [10], [11], [12], [13], [14], [15], [16], [17], [18], but can also serve as a terminal ligand or an uncoordinated guest molecule, which may be further involved in hydrogen bonding and/or π–π stacking interactions [19], [20], [21], [22]. The metal–organic frameworks constructed by the bridging 4,4′-bipy and metal ions are strongly dependent on the geometric preference of the metal ions and the metal-to-ligand molar ratio. In principle, the 1:1 metal-to-ligand molar ratio usually only generates linear [10] or zigzag chain structures [11], the 1:1.5 metal-to-ligand molar ratio can generate molecular ladder [12], [13], honeycomb [14] and molecular bilayer [15]; the 1:2 metal-to-ligand molar ratio results in square grid [16], [17] and diamondoid-like structures [18].

As a part of our work towards rational design and preparation of functional supramolecular complexes with intriguing structures and potential applications in optics, electrical conductivity, magnetism and host-guest chemistry [23], [24], [25], [26], we have recently carried out a systemic study of metal–organic complexes from multifunctional organic ligand 4-aminobenzoic acid (4-abaH) [26], [27], [28]. Owing to 4-abaH combining characteristics of amine and carboxylic groups: (1) it can act as a good monodentate ligand through amide or carboxylate group coordinating to a metal center; (2) it can act as hydrogen donors and/or acceptor; (3) deprotonated 4-abaH can serve as bridging ligand through carboxylate and/or amine coordinating to different metal centers; (4) deprotonated 4-abaH can act as a counter anion and/or guest molecule and (5) protonated 4-abaH can serve as an organic cation templating agent. Most importantly, the assembly process of 4-abaH and metal ions has been highly influenced by counter anions, pH value, solvent system, temperature and metal to ligand ratio besides the coordination nature of the metal ions. In some cases, a subtle alteration in any of these factors can lead to new complexes with different structural topologies and different functions. Through controlling and tuning these effect factors, we [26], [27], [28] and others [29], [30], [31], [32], [33] have prepared a series of novel supramolecular complexes from 4-abaH, but metal–organic complexes from mixed ligands of 4-abaH and bipyridyl groups have been seldom reported [17], [34]. The reactions of both 4-abaH and 4,4′-bipy with metal ions may produce fascinating supramolecular architectures based on the previous respective characteristics of 4,4′-bipy and 4-abaH. As an extension of our course of ongoing studies on transitional metal complexes from 4-abaH, we have introduced 4,4′-bipy into the reaction system. Herein, we wish to report the syntheses and crystal structures of four complexes from 4-abaH and 4,4′-bipy, [(4-abaH)2(4,4′-bipy)] (1), {[Zn2(4,4′-bipy)2(4-aba)4] (H2O)5}n (2), {[Mn(4,4′-bipy)2(H2O)4](4-aba)Br(H2O)3} (3) and {[Cu2(4,4′-bipy)3(H2O)2(4-aba)2](NO3)2(H2O)4}n (4).

2. Experimental

2.1. General

All reagents were commercially available and used as purchased without further purification. The IR spectra as KBr disk were recorded on a Magna 750 FT-IR spectrophotometer. Elemental analyses were determined on an Elementar Vario ELIII elementary analyzer by elemental analysis group of this Institute.

2.2. Syntheses

2.3. X-ray crystallography

Intensity data for complexes 1–4 were measured on a Siemens Smart CCD diffractometer with graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073Å) at room temperature. Empirical absorption corrections were applied by using the sadabs program for the Siemens area detector. The structures were solved by direct methods and all calculations were performed through using the shelxl–97 program. The coordinate of metal atoms was obtained from an E-map, successive difference Fourier syntheses gave all the coordinates of the non-hydrogen atoms. The structures were refined by full-matrix least-squares minimization of Σ(Fo − Fc)2 with anisotropic thermal parameters for all atoms except the H atoms. The positions of H atoms were generated geometrically (C–H bond fixed at 0.96 Å), assigned isotropic thermal parameters, and allowed to ride on their parent carbon atoms before the final cycle of refinement. 3. Results and discussion

3.1. Syntheses and characterization

The directed assembly of 4-abaH and transitional metal ions with different counter anions has resulted in a few interesting metal–organic networks containing head-to-head hydrogen bonds [27], [28], [31]. However, when introducing 4,4′-bipy into reaction system, the straightforward mixture of 4-abaH, Zn(NO3)2 · 6H2O and 4,4′-bipy produced a ladder-like organic adduct constructed by Naba–H⋯OCOO and Npy⋯H–OOH hydrogen bonding interactions, in which ZnII has not involved in coordination. By increasing pH value of reaction mixture to 5 ∼ 6, complex 2 consisting of one-dimensional zigzag chains has been successfully isolated. Moreover, changing the ratio of metal to 4,4′-bipy under the similar reaction conditions has no effect on the structural motif of 2. However, when MnII and CuII were used under the same conditions, complexes 3 and 4 with drastically different structures have been generated. The ratio of metal to 4,4′-bipy in mononuclear [Mn(4,4′-bipy)2(H2O)4]2+ cations of 3 and one-dimensional ladder skeleton of 4 is different from that in reaction system, suggesting that coordination nature of metal ions plays an important role in their formation. It is also worthy to mention that a two-dimensional square grid has been previously reported using 1:1:2 instead of 1:1:1 of CuII:4,4′-bipy:4-abaH in 4 [17].

The IR spectrum of 1 has a strong absorption band for the CO stretching at 1685 cm−1, which indicates the presence of carboxylic group of 4-abaH. On the other hand, the absence of a strong peak in the 1700–1680 cm−1 region and the presence of strong peak at the 1608 cm−1 for the antisymmetric stretching and 1384 cm−1 for the symmetric stretching in the IR spectra of 2 and 4 clearly indicate deprotonation of 4-abaH. The separation (Δ) between νasym (CO2) and νasym (CO2) is 224 cm−1, revealing the chelation of carboxylate of 4-aba in 2 and 4. For complexes 3, the characteristic bands of carboxylate group are shown at 1599 and 1384 cm−1 for the antisymmetric stretching and symmetric stretching, respectively. The Δ value is 215 cm−1.

3.2. Structures

3.2.1. [(4-abaH)2(4,4′-bipy)] (1)

Single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that compound 1 is a one-dimensional hydrogen bonding ladder constructed by 4-abaH and 4,4′-bipy. As shown in Fig. 1, 4-abaH acts as both hydrogen bonding donor and acceptor, one carboxylic oxygen atom of 4-abaH and NH2 of the adjacent 4-abaH forms a N–H⋯O hydrogen bond [N(1)i–H ⋯O(12) 2.947(3) Å; symmetry code: (i) x − 1, y, z]; the other carboxylic oxygen atom of 4-abaH and one nitrogen atom of 4,4′-bipy forms a O–H⋯N hydrogen bond [O(11)–H⋯N(1) 2.701(2) Å]. Thus, 4,4′-bipy serves as a hydrogen bonding acceptor and links two adjacent 4-abaH into a one-dimensional hydrogen-bonding ladder, in which 4,4′-bipy and 4-abaH serve as inner and side rails, respectively. Two pyridyl rings of 4,4′-bipy are coplanar, the dihedral angle of phenyl ring of 4-abaH and pyridyl rings of 4,4′-bipy is 69.8°. In 4-abaH, the carboxylic group is not coplanar with phenyl ring with the dihedral angle between them being 6.9°.

3.2.2. {[Zn2(4,4′-bipy)2(4-aba)4](H2O)5} (2)

Complex 2 consists of one-dimensional zigzag chains formed through two independent 4,4′-bipy ligands alternatively linking five-coordinated ZnII centers in a herringbone pattern. As shown in Fig. 2(a), ZnII adopts a highly distorted trigonal-bipyramidal geometry by coordinating to two pyridyl nitrogen atoms from different 4,4′-bipy, one chelating carboxylate group and one monodentate carboxylate group from different 4-aba. The dihedral angle of two pyridyl rings attached to ZnII is 75.4° , exhibiting two coordinated pyridyl rings of ZnII center having a great distortion. 4,4′-bipy serves as an exo-bidentate ligand and links ZnII into a one-dimensional zigzag chain with the separation of Zn⋯Zn across 4,4′-bipy being 11.254 and 11.279 Å. Similar to 1, two pyridyl rings in 4,4′-bipy are completely coplanar. Each ZnII center is coordinated by two types of 4-aba: one behaves in an unsymmetrical chelating mode [Zn–O(11) 1.998(6); Zn–O(12) 2.464(7) Å]; the other acts as a monodentate ligand through one carboxylate oxygen atom [Zn–O(21) 1.955(6) and Zn–O(22) 2.728 Å], the uncoordinated carboxylate oxygen atom O(22) and an adjacent crystalline water molecule form O–H⋯O hydrogen bond [O(22)⋯H–OW1 2.786(10) Å]. The dihedral angle between phenyl rings of the two 4-aba is 74.8° , closed to that between two pyridyl rings coordinating to ZnII center. Interestingly, the amino group of 4-aba neither takes part in coordination, nor involves in hydrogen bonding interaction (the shortest distance of N to its surroundings is 3.138 Å), which is great different from other hydrogen bonding complexes from 4-abaH [27], [28], [31], [32], [33]. The hydrogen bonding interactions between crystalline water molecules and chelating carboxylate group of one 4-aba [O(11)⋯H–OW2 = 2.773(9); OW3–H⋯O(12)i = 2.835(8) Å; symmetry code: (i) x, −y + 1/2, z − 1/2] as well as between crystalline water molecules [OW2⋯OW3 2.740(5) Å] extend the zigzag chain into a three-dimensional structure (Fig. 2(d)).

3.2.3. {[Mn(4,4′-bipy)2(H2O)4](4-aba)Br(H 2O)3} (3)

Compound 3 is made up of mononuclear [Mn(4,4′-bipy)2(H2O)4]2+ cations, neutral crystalline water molecules, and 4-aba and Br− anions. As shown in Fig. 3(a), MnII is in a slightly distorted octahedral geometry and is coordinated by two trans -pyridyl nitrogen atoms from different 4,4′-bipy [Mn–N = 2.289(4) Å] and four water molecules [Mn–O = 2.168(3)–2.193(4) Å]. Each [Mn(4,4′-bipy)2(H2O)4]2+ cation features a complementary binary hydrogen-bonding system. On the one hand, it acts as a hydrogen-bonding acceptor forming two equivalent N⋯H–O hydrogen bonds between uncoordinated nitrogen atoms of two 4,4′-bipy and coordinated water molecules of two adjacent [Mn(4,4′-bipy)2(H2O)4]2+ [N(2)⋯H–O(w2)i = 2.779(5) Å; symmetry code: (i) −x + 1/2, −y, z + 1/2]. On the other hand, the same [Mn(4,4′-bipy)2(H2O)4]2+ cation serves also as a hydrogen-bonding donor forming two additional equivalent O–H⋯N hydrogen bonds between its two coordinated water molecules and uncoordinated nitrogen atoms of two 4,4′-bipy form adjacent [Mn(4,4′-bipy)2(H2O)4]2+. Through the complementary binary hydrogen-bonding interactions, mononuclear [Mn(4,4′-bipy)2(H2O)4]2+ are linked into a two-dimensional layer structure (Fig. 3(b)). Different from complex 2, each 4,4′-bipy behaves in a monodentate fashion, the twisting angle of two pyridyl rings in 4,4′-bipy is 35.4°. The uncoordinated pyridyl ring of 4,4′-bipy and two adjacent coordinated pyridyl rings of 4,4′-bipy from different mononuclear cations are approximately parallel to each other with the dihedral angle between them being 3.7° and the distance between their centroids being 3.583 and 3.723 Å, indicating the significant π–π stacking interactions [35].

4-aba, Br− and uncoordinated water molecules reside between two adjacent layers and extend the two-dimensional layer into a three-dimensional architecture through hydrogen-bonding interactions involving in water molecules, 4-aba and Br−. As shown in Fig. 2(c), the uncoordinated water molecules and coordinated molecules form OW⋯H–OW hydrogen bonds [OW2⋯OW4 = 2.800(5) Å, OW1⋯OW3ii = 2.788(5) Å, OW1⋯OW4iii = 2.766(5) Å; symmetry code: (ii) −x, −y, −z + 1, (iii) −x, −y, −z]. Two carboxylate oxygen atoms of 4-aba and uncoordinated water molecules form O⋯H–OW hydrogen bonds [O(11)⋯H–OW4 = 2.750(7), O(11)⋯H–OW4iv = 2.750(7), O(12)–OW3 = 2.671(10) Å; symmetry code: (iv) x, −y + 1/2, z]. The uncoordinated water molecules and Br− form Br⋯H–OW hydrogen bonds [Br⋯H–OW4i 3.280(4), Br⋯H–OW4iii 3.280(4) Å; symmetry code: (i) −x, −y, −z + 1, (iii) −x, −y, −z]. Thus, 4-aba, Br− and uncoordinated water molecules can be regarded as the linkers of adjacent [Mn(4,4′-bipy)2(H2O)4]2+ layers and consolidate the whole structural framework through extensive hydrogen bonding interactions.

It is noteworthy that compound 3 is greatly different from the three-dimensional compound [Mn(4,4′-bipy)2(H2O)4](ClO4)2(4,4′-bipy)4 [19], in which the terminal 4,4′-bipy ligands extend the mononuclear [Mn(4,4′-bipy)2(H2O)4]2+ cations into a linear chain solely via π–π stacking interaction of uncoordinated pyridyl rings. The parallel chain is further interconnected into a three-dimensional network through hydrogen bonding interactions involved in the coordinated water molecules and uncoordinated 4,4′-bipy molecules. Although metal–organic complexes from bipyridyl ligands sustained by hydrogen bonding and π–π stacking interactions have been extensively investigated [19], [20], [21], [22], compound 3, in which all bipyridyl ligands behave in a monodentate mode and all adjacent pyridyl rings form π–π stacking interactions, is unprecedented. Moreover, the complexes containing the complementary binary hydrogen bonding interaction have seldom been reported [20], [22].

3.2.4. {[Cu2(4,4′-bipy)3(H2O)2 (4-aba)2](NO3)2(H2O)4}n (4)

Single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that complex 4 consists of cationic one-dimensional molecular ladders, neutral crystalline water molecules and nitrate anions. As shown in Fig. 4(a), each CuII is coordinated by three nitrogen atoms from different 4,4′-bipy ligands, a oxygen atom of 4-aba and one water molecule in a distorted pseudo-octahedral geometry. Three 4,4′-bipy ligands form a slightly distorted T-shaped arrangement at five-coordinated CuII center [N(2A)–Cu–N(1) 176.52(11); N(2A)–Cu–N(11) 88.04(11); N(1)–Cu–N(11) 95.11(11)°], in which two nitrogen atoms (N1 and N2) stand at the apical positions, the remaining one nitrogen atom (N11), two oxygen atoms of carboxylate group and one coordinated water molecule (O21, O22 and OW1) consist of the equatorial plane, CuII is approximately coplanar with the plane defined by equatorial atoms with a deviation of 0.044 Å. Different from complexes 2 and 3, 4,4′-bipy in an exo-bidentate mode links CuII forming a one-dimensional ladder structure (Fig. 4(b)). The dihedral angle between two pyridyl rings of side 4,4′-bipy is 27.6°, however, two pyridyl rings of inner 4,4′-bipy are coplanar. The adjacent Cu⋯Cu distances alongside 4,4′-bipy and the inner 4,4′-bipy are 11.099 and 11.177 Å, respectively. 4-aba serves as a lateral arm of the one-dimensional ladder through its carboxylate group coordinating to CuII and prevents the formation of an extended motif. Interestingly, 4-aba arms of each molecular ladder are threaded into the [Cu4(4,4′-bipy)4] squares of adjacent molecular ladders and each square is oppositely penetrated by two 4-aba arms from different molecular ladders. Such penetration is not only effectively filled into the void space of square rings, but also results in a three-dimensional supramolecular network with one-dimensional channels along a axis. The nitrate ions and uncoordinated water molecules are accommodated in the channel through the hydrogen bonding interactions between coordinated water molecules and uncoordinated water molecules [OW1⋯OW2i 2.709(4), OW1⋯OW3ii 2.769(4) Å; symmetry code: (i) −x + 1, −y + 2, −z + 2, (ii) x, −y + 3/2, z + 1/2 ], between uncoordinated water molecules and oxygen atom of nitrate [O(3)⋯H–OW2 2.810(5), OW3–H ⋯O(2)iii 2.872(7) Å; symmetry code: (iii) −x, y − 1/2, −z + 3/2] as well as between uncoordinated water molecules and carboxylate oxygen atoms of 4-aba [O(21)⋯H–OW2 2.791(4), O(22)⋯H–OW3 2.723(4) Å]. The structural motif in 4 is greatly different from molecular ladders with only inner rails [36], [37] as well as with both inner rails and lateral arms of bipyridyl ligands [38], but is similar to the architectures constructed by 4,4′-bipy, 4-hydroxybenzoate and CuII or CoII [12].

4. Conclusion

Combining the characteristics of 4-aminobenzoic acid (4-abaH) and 4,4′-bipy, we have successfully synthesized four mixed-ligand hydrogen bonding supramolecular complexes. These compounds exhibit different structural motifs. In 1, 4-abaH acts as both hydrogen bonding donor and acceptor to form one one-dimensional zigzag chain, uncoordinated 4,4′-bipy serves as hydrogen bonding acceptor and further links the zigzag chains into a supramolecular ladder. However, 4,4′-bipy in 3 behaves in a monodentate fashion through one pyridyl ring coordinating to MnII center and the other pyridyl ring serving as a hydrogen bonding acceptor. Moreover, two pyridyl rings in 4,4′-bipy are involved in strong π–π interactions. Interestingly, 4,4′-bipy bridges five-coordinated ZnII and five-coordinated CuII centers forming a one-dimensional zigzag chain of 2 and cationic molecular ladder of 4, respectively. 4-aba, coordinating to ZnII through both a chelating and a monodentate carboxylate group in 2 and coordinating CuII through only a monodentate mode in 4, forms hydrogen bonding interaction with water molecules. However, 4-aba in 3, which has not involved in coordination, acts as a counter anion to balance charge and hydrogen bonding acceptor through carboxylate group. In summary, this work demonstrates that the combination of 4,4′-bipy and 4-aba building blocks is an excellent choice to prepare supramolecular architectures with different structure and functions. Moreover, the assembly with metal ions has highly been influenced by coordination preference of metal centers and the pH value of reaction system.
