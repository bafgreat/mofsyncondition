In recent years, design and synthesis of functional metal–organic materials (MOMs) based on the strategies of crystal engineering have attracted much attention due to their potential applications and fascinating structural diversities.1 Remarkable progress has been made in this field, largely owing to efficient design strategies.2 However, the preparation of MOMs with predictable structures is still a challenge since the self-assembly process of metal ions and organic ligands depends on many factors, including solvents, concentrations of metal ions and ligand precursor, pH value, temperature, etc. The use of metal–organic polyhedra (MOPs) as supramolecular building blocks (SBBs) for the construction of porous frameworks is a powerful strategy, because MOPs themselves have cavities and possess high symmetry, leading to the possibility of a crystal engineering strategy for MOMs fabrication with highly specific control over the topology. Successful examples include tetrahedra,3hexahedra (cubes),4 octahedra,5cuboctahedra,6 truncated cuboctahedra (small rhombihexahedron),1e,7,8 and trigonal prism.9 Among these MOPs reported, small rhombihexahedron possesses Oh symmetry and consists of 12 square molecular building blocks (MBBs) that generate 8 triangular and 6 square open “windows”(Fig. 1b). It was extensively studied by several groups8,10 since the angle between its vertices is 120° corresponding exactly to the angle subtended by meta-substituted benzene rings. It has been demonstrated that flexible cross-linked bdc (benzene-1,3-dicarboxylate) ligands at the 5-position of the isophthalate moieties can link square windows or faces to generate 6-connected pcu (primitive cubic) nets,8 whereas the rigid linear cross-linked bdc ligands can afford 3,24-coordinated rht nets if triangular metal clusters1e or triangular organic moieties are utilized.10 This communication focuses on the metal–organic polyhedra strategy and structural diversity by demonstrating how cross-linking of Cu2(O2CR)4 paddlewheels with a flexible ligand can afford a new framework with pcu topology sustained by small rhombihexahedron SBBs that connects to others via six square open windows in three orthogonal directions. In the ligand molecule, 1,1-bis-[3,5-bis(carboxy) phenoxy]methane, H44L, two bdc moieties were linked by a methylene group at the 5-position. The obtained MOF is stable after removing solvent molecules and exhibits a noticeable CO2adsorption hysteresis and a comparable methane adsorption capacity at room temperature.

H44L was prepared using a modified approach as reported previously in the literature.11 The solvothermal reaction of H44L with CuCl2·2H2O in 1 : 2 stoichiometric ratio in dimethylformamide (DMF) with water at 85 °C for 48 h produced the blue-green crystals of, [Cu6(L)3(DMF)·(H2O)5]n·(DMF)x, 1 (Table S1, ESI†). Single crystal X-ray diffraction experiments revealed that 1 crystallized in the tetragonal P4/mcc.‡ As shown in Fig. 1, two copper ions are linked by four bdc moieties to form the square paddlewheel secondary building unit (SBU, Cu2(COO)4), with a short Cu–Cu internuclear separation of 2.628(2) Å. Twelve SBUs are bonded together by twenty four phenyl rings to give a small rhombihexahedron SBB, which has also been termed a nanoball,8a with the vertices occupied by phenyl rings and the SBUs occupying the centers of twelve square faces (Fig. 1b).

Every SBB connects to six others via six square open windows in three orthogonal directions, affording a new porous pcu net, in which the small rhombihexahedron serves as an octahedral node (Fig. S1†). This connecting strategy led to the generation of three different types of open cages (Fig. 1c) in the overall framework of 1 that afford three types of 3D channels along three axes (Fig. S3†). The largest cage has a nanoscale rectangle open window (10.57 × 10.67 Å2) with a diameter of 27.85 Å along the diagonal (centroid–centroid distance of the paddlewheels, Fig. S2a†), delimited by 24 paddlewheel MBBs and 12 ligand molecules, and therefore surrounded by 12 cylinder type cages (Fig. S2b†) and 8 nanoballs (Fig. S2c†). The cylinder cage is constructed of 8 paddlewheel MBBs and four ligand molecules. As the axial ligands and solvent molecules were removed, compound 1 exhibited 70.7% void space as calculated by PLATON.12

Two similar pcu nets where the octahedral nodes were connected through four square SBUs and two square open windows8a or through two square SBUs and four open windows,8b respectively, have been reported, in which the flexible tetracarboxylate ligands showed two crystallographically independent orientations. But in the case of 1, all ligand molecules adopted one similar conformation (Fig. S4†) in the whole framework that facilitated the highly symmetrical interconnection of nanoball nodes. To our knowledge, 1 is a new framework with pcu topology based on Oh symmetric small rhombihexahedron. This pcu net does not exhibit interpenetration, presumably due to the shorter length of the bridge between 1,3-bdc moieties.8b

Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) data confirmed the purity of the bulk sample of 1 and the stability of the activated one (Fig. S5†). The thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) showed that the as-synthesized sample has a continuous mass loss of 51% up to a temperature of 583 K due to the removal of guest solvent molecules in the channel, which is a little bit lower than the calculated data of 53.9% (Table S2†) due to the evaporation of partial solvent molecules on surface before TGA tests. For the methanol exchanged or the activated samples, a plateau was observed up to 573 K after the loss of solvent molecules, indicating that the framework of 1 was thermally stable up to 573 K (Fig. S5 and S6†).

The large accessible windows, high-density open metal sites, and high porosities allow the framework to be prospective for gas storage.13 As shown in Fig. 2, the capacities of N2 and O2adsorptions were 633 and 704 cm3 g−1, respectively at 77 K and 1 bar for the activated 1 sample, which exhibited type I reversible sorption isotherms, suggesting the characteristic of a microporous material. The Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) and Langmuir surface areas of 1 calculated based on the nitrogen absorption isotherm were 2010 and 2665 m2 g−1, respectively. The Horvath–Kawazoe (HK) model indicated a median pore diameter of 5.9 Å and a maximum pore volume of 0.72 cm3 g−1 in 1. This aperture dimension is considered suitable for methane uptake.14

The high-pressure gas (complex 1) adsorption measurements were performed at various temperatures and at a pressure range of 0–6200 mm Hg. It was shown that 1 could adsorb N2, O2, CO2 and CH4 (Fig. S7†). The adsorption amounts of the gases increased as the pressure enhanced. The sample adsorbed almost the same amount of nitrogen and oxygen no matter what the pressure was. The relative low N2 and O2adsorption capacity of 1 at room temperature is possibly due to the weak interaction between gas molecules and the MOF framework. In the case of methane adsorption, the amount of methane adsorbed was up to 90 cm3 g−1 at 295 K and 6026 mm Hg. The methane storage capacity of 1 is comparable to that of IRMOF-6 (ref. 14) and UTSA-20 (ref. 13b) under similar conditions. The methane sorption data were fitted according to the Langmuir equation15 with high accuracy over the entire range of the adsorption pressure (Fig. S8, Table S3†). By extrapolating the results to high pressure, reliable estimates could be obtained for the methane uptake in 1. The predicted methane storage capacity at the expected application conditions (35 bar, 295 K) was up to 200 cm3 (STP) g−1, corresponding to the volumetric value of 167 cm3 (STP) cm−3 converted by using the crystallographic density of 1 (0.835 g cm−3), which approaches the target of 180 cm3 (STP) cm−3 set by U.S. Department of Energy.16 The ability of 1 to uptake methane could be ascribed to the high density of open copper sites and ether oxygen atoms within 1 as well as the high specific surface area.17 It has been demonstrated that the open metal site within the framework is one of the main types of strong methane adsorption sites that are responsible for the high capacity of methane adsorption.13

To reveal the methane affinity of 1, methane adsorption isotherms at 273, 289 and 295 K were recorded (Fig. S8†). The isosteric heat of methane adsorption was calculated using the Clausius–Clapeyron equation.15 The enthalpy of methane adsorption of 1 decreased slightly from low coverage to high coverage in the range of 15.2–15.7 kJ mol−1 under the experimental conditions (Fig. S9†). The Qst values reflect a moderate interaction of CH4 molecule with the MOF surface, consistent with those of other reported MOF systems,18 implying the contribution of open copper sites and ether oxygen atoms within 1.

The high-pressure CO2adsorption isotherms of 1 at two temperatures were presented in Fig. 3. The amounts of CO2 adsorbed at 273 and 295 K under 5999 mm Hg were 91 and 68 cm3 g−1, respectively. These values are comparable to those of YO-MOF,19CPM-24 (ref. 20) and MOF-2,21 but lower than those of some of the best MOFs.10g,22 It is noteworthy that the CO2 sorption isotherm displays an obvious hysteresis. For most of the 3D MOFs, reversible type-I isotherms were observed frequently,21,22 while hysteretic gas-uptakes were rarely reported.23 This hysteretic isotherm indicates that the flexible framework of 1 has a dynamic performance under the inducing effect of adsorbed CO2 molecules, which is an anticipated behavior of the porous materials for gas separations.

In summary, a new 3D microporous MOF based on metal–organic polyhedral SBBs cross-linked by flexible ligand with pcu topology has been prepared and structurally characterized. The interconnection of SBBs for 1 illustrated a new strategy to achieve structural diversity of MOMs based on small rhombihexahedron and flexible ligands. High pressure gas adsorption studies showed that 1 exhibited a noticeable CO2adsorption hysteresis and a comparable methane adsorption property at room temperature. Furthermore, 1 displayed a selective adsorption for CH4 over N2 and O2 at high pressure.
