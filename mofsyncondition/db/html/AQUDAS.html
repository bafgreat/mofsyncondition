A new host 3D copper(I/II)-pyrazinecarboxylate framework intercalated with Keggin polyoxotungstates

Author links open overlay panel, , , , ,
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ica.2011.04.006Get rights and content

Abstract

Two new Cu(I/II) coordination polymers based on α-Keggin polyoxotungstates, [Cu6(2-pzc)5(H2O)2(XW12O40)]·H2O (X = Si, 1; Ge, 2) (2-pzc = 2-pyrazinecarboxylate), have been synthesized hydrothermally and characterized structurally. Single crystal structure analysis reveals that they are isomorphic, and exhibit a novel 3D framework constructed from Cu(I/II) ions and 2-pzc ligands, in which there exist nano-sized channels viewed along the a axis and the c axis. The [XW12O40]4− anions are filled in the channels, acting as not only a template, but also a hexa-dentate ligand. Additionally, the photocatalytic hydrogen production of 1 and 2 was investigated. It is found that 1 and 2, as heterogeneous catalysts, exhibit the photocatalytic activity in the UV region.

Graphical abstract

Two isomorphic 3D Cu(I/II) coordination polymers based on α-Keggin polyoxotungstates, [Cu6(2-pzc)5(H2O)2(XW12O40)]·H2O (X = Si, 1; Ge, 2) (2-pzc = 2-pyrazinecarboxylates), have been synthesized hydrothermally and characterized structurally. This is the first example of a 3D framework constructed from Cu(I/II) ions and 2-pzc ligands by employing POMs as a template. Additionally, the photocatalytic hydrogen production of 1 and 2 was investigated.

1. Introduction

In recent years, it has been reported that metal organic framework (MOF)-supported polyoxometalates (POMs), as heterogeneous catalysts in oxidation and esterlysis reactions, exhibit excellent dispersity of POMs at the molecular level and high immobilization of POMs, as well as enhancement of catalytic properties [1], [2], [3]. It inspired greatly to develop multi-dimensional and porous MOFs containing POMs [4], [5], [6]. To date, numerous MOFs based on POMs have been reported [1], [4], [7], [8], [9], [10], [11]. Such POMs may adopt a variety of roles in MOF materials: (1) charge compensating anions; (2) ligands bonded directly to transitional metal ions; (3) templates inducing the self-assembly of MOFs [12], [13], [14], [15], [16], [17], [18]. POMs exhibit great diversity in negative charges, shapes and coordinative modes et al. that endow them to be employed with transitional metal ions and organic ligands for the construction of intriguing MOFs [6], [19]. The Cu/2-pzc polymer system exhibits attractive MOFs since 2-pzc is a multidentate bridge ligand and Cu(I/II) ions exhibit versatile geometries in complexes [12], [20], [21], [22], [23]. By the introduction of POMs into the system, new MOFs have been reported. For examples of [Cu4(2-pzc)4(H2O)8][Mo8O26]·2H2O and [Cu3(2-pzc)4(H2O)2][V10O28H4]·6.5H2O [12], the corresponding MOFs are a 1D chain and a 2D layer, respectively, where the POMs play a role not only in charge balance in view of molecular structure, but also in templates for the formation of Cu(II)/2-pzc MOFs. For H4{[Cu3(2-Hpzc)2(H2O)4](H2W12O42)}·13H2O reported by Peng and co-workers [22], the POMs are employed not only as a template, but also as a ligand, forming a 3D architecture. As extension of the Cu/2-pzc/POM system, we synthesized two new coordination polymers, formulated as, [Cu6(2-pzc)5(H2O)2(XW12O40)]·H2O (X = Si, 1; Ge, 2). They exhibit a new Cu(I/II)/2-pzc 3D framework intercalated with Keggin polyoxotungstates.

2. Experimental

2.1. Materials and methods

H4SiW12O40·19H2O and H4GeW12O40·28H2O were synthesized according to the literature methods [24], [25] and identified by IR spectra. All other chemicals were purchased from commercial sources and used without further purification. Elemental analyses (C, H, and N) were performed on a Perkin-Elmer 2400 CHN Elemental Analyzer. X-ray photoelectronic spectra (XPS) were performed on a VG ESCALAB MK II spectrometer with an Mg Kα (1253.6 eV) achromatic X-ray source. IR spectra were recorded in the range 4000–400 cm−1 on a TENSOR27 Bruker AXS spectrometer with a pressed KBr pellet. The thermalgravimetric analysis (TGA) was carried out on a Pyris Diamond TG/DTA instrument in immobile airflow with a heating rate of 10 °C/min. Solid-state diffuse reflectance spectra of the samples were obtained for the dry pressed disk samples using Cary 5000 UV–Vis–NIR spectrophotometer. The X-ray powder diffraction (XRPD) patterns were recorded on a D8 Advance instrument in the angular range of 2θ = 5–65° at 293 K with Cu Kα radiation.

2.2. Photocatalytic hydrogen production

The photocatalytic activity of 1 and 2 was examined in a self-made closed quartz reaction vessel (290 ml). 0.1 g of 1 or 2 was suspended in 100 ml 20% methanol solution, respectively. The photocatalytic reaction was performed in a N2 atmosphere. 1.2 wt% Pt was loaded on 1 and 2 by situ photoreduction method using H2PtCl6 solution [26], [27], [28]. The light source was a 125 W mercury lamp. The amount of the produced H2 was analyzed using GC-7890T gas chromatograph with a thermal conductivity detector and an Ar carrier. The blank test was carried out in the same condition without 1 and 2.

2.3. Synthesis

A mixture of H4SiW12O40·19H2O (0.48 g, 0.15 mmol) for 1, H4GeW12O40·28H2O (0.52 g, 0.15 mmol) for 2, 2-Hpzc (0.07 g, 0.60 mmol), Cu(CH3COO)2·H2O (0.15 g, 0.70 mmol) and H2O (15 ml) was stirred for 1 h, the suspension was put into a 25 ml Teflon-lined autoclave and kept under autogenous pressure at 160 °C for 3 days. After cooling the autoclave to room temperature, dark red block crystals were selected after filtration, washed with distilled water, and dried at ambient temperature (Yield: ca. 56% based on W for 1, 51% based on W for 2). Anal. Calc. for 1: C, 7.64; H, 0.54; N, 3.57. Found: C, 7.61; H, 0.57; N, 3.51%. Anal. Calc. for 2: C, 7.56; H, 0.53; N, 3.53. Found: C, 7.51; H, 0.56; N, 3.48%.

2.4. X-ray crystallography

Crystal data of 1 and 2 were collected on a SMART APEX II-CCD X-ray single crystal diffractometer with graphite-monochromatic Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at 293 K. The structures were solved by direct methods and refined by Full-Matrix Least-squares methods on F2 using the shelxs-97 software. All of the non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. The hydrogen atoms attached to carbon atoms were added theoretically. A summary of the crystallographic data and structural determination for 1 and 2 is provided in Table 1. Selected bond lengths and bond angles of 1 and 2 are listed in Tables S1 and S2.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Structural description

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that 1 and 2 are isomorphic. Their structure can be described as a 3D framework constructed from Cu(I/II) ions and 2-pzc ligands and intercalated with polyoxotungstates [XW12O40]4−. As shown in Fig. 1a, [XW12O40]4− anions are a classical α-Keggin structure [29], [30].

There are six crystallographically independent Cu centers (Cu1, Cu2, Cu3, Cu4, Cu5, and Cu6) in the unit cell. They exhibit five coordination environments (Fig. 1b). Cu1 and Cu2 are six-coordinated in octahedral geometries {CuN2O4}, which are completed by two terminal oxygen atoms from two [XW12O40]4− anions (Cu–O = 2.358(18)–2.60(3) Å), two nitrogen atoms and two carboxylic oxygen atoms from two 2-pzc ligands (Cu–N = 1.93(3)–1.99(3) Å, Cu–O = 1.92(2)–1.96(2) Å). Both Cu3 and Cu4 exhibit distorted tetrahedral geometries {CuN2O2}. Cu3 is coordinated by two nitrogen atoms from two 2-pzc ligands (Cu–N = 1.87(2)–1.99(3) Å), one water molecule (Cu–O = 2.52(3)–2.57(3) Å), one carboxylic oxygen atom from one 2-pzc ligand (Cu–O = 2.07(3)–2.08(3) Å). Cu4 is coordinated by two nitrogen atoms from two 2-pzc ligands (Cu–N = 1.87(3)–1.95(2) Å) and two carboxylic oxygen atoms from two 2-pzc ligands (Cu–O = 2.157(19)–2.26(2) Å). Cu5 is five-coordinated in a square pyramidal geometry {CuNO4}, coordinated by one nitrogen atom and one carboxylic oxygen atom from one 2-pzc ligand (Cu–N = 1.98(3)–2.01(3) Å, Cu–O = 1.95(2)–1.97(2) Å), one water molecule (Cu–O = 1.92(3)–1.99(2) Å), one carboxylic oxygen atom from another 2-pzc ligand (Cu–O = 1.89(2) Å) and one terminal oxygen atom from one [XW12O40]4− anion (Cu–O = 2.78(3)–2.79(2) Å). Cu6 exhibits trigonal geometry {CuNO2}, coordinated by one nitrogen atom from one 2-pzc ligand (Cu–N = 1.89(2)–1.89(3) Å), one terminal oxygen atom from one [XW12O40]4− anion (Cu–O = 1.88(3)–1.92(3) Å) and one carboxylic oxygen atom from one 2-pzc ligand (Cu–O = 2.14(3)–2.17(2) Å).

The XPS spectra of 1 and 2 (Fig. S1) give the peaks at 943.3 and 962.4 eV for 1, 943.7 and 963.2 eV for 2 attributed to Cu(II) and the peaks at 932.5 and 952.4 eV for 1, 933.8 and 963.2 eV for 2 attributed to Cu(I) [31], [32], [33]. Further, bond valence sum (BVS) calculations [34] show that Cu1, Cu2, Cu5 atoms in 1 and 2 are all in +II oxidation state, Cu3, Cu4, Cu6 atoms are all in +I oxidation state. The 3D host framework of {Cu6(2-pzc)5(H2O)2} is fabricated through coordinating Cu(I/II) ions with the 2-pzc ligands. On the ac plane, Cu1 and Cu4 atoms are weaved with 2-pzc ligands into a 2D wave-like layer, so do Cu2 and Cu3 atoms (Fig. 2). The layers are denoted as A and B, respectively. As shown in Fig. 3, layers A and B are linked together by {Cu2(2-pzc)}2+ subunits to generate a unique 3D [Cu6(2-pzc)5(H2O)2]4+ framework. Viewed along the a axis or the c axis, there are nano-size channels in the 3D [Cu6(2-pzc)5(H2O)2]4+ framework.

The [XW12O40]4− anions, as guests, are filled in the channels (Fig. 4). Each [XW12O40]4− anion acts as a hexa-dentate ligand coordinating to six Cu atoms through six terminal oxygen atoms (Fig. 1a). They play an important role not only in charge balance for the polymers and coordination demand for Cu(I/II) ions, but also in a template for nano-channels of the 3D [Cu6(2-pzc)5(H2O)2]4+ framework. This is attributed to nano-size and oxo-riched surface of polyoxometalates [8], [35], [36], [37], [38]. This is the first example of a 3D framework constructed from Cu(I/II) ions and 2-pzc ligands by employing POMs as a template.

3.2. IR spectra

The IR spectra (Fig. S2) exhibit the Keggin characteristic peaks at 921, 968, 881, and 799 cm−1 for 1 attributed to νas(Si–O), νas(W–Ot), νas(W–Ob–W) and νas(W–Oc–W) vibrations, 833, 961, 888, and 779 cm−1 for 2 attributed to νas(Ge–O), νas(W–Ot), νas(W–Ob–W) and νas(W–Oc–W) vibrations, respectively. Furthermore, a series of characteristic bands in the 1177–1651 cm−1 region are associated with 2-pzc ligands in 1 and 2.

3.3. Thermal analysis

The TGA curves of 1 and 2 (Fig. 5) exhibit a continuous weight-loss process in the range of 30–580 °C for 1, and in the range of 30–600 °C for 2, which are ascribed to the loss of water molecules and 2-pzc ligands. The total weight loss is about 16.48% for 1, and 16.77% for 2 in consistence with the calculated value of 17.18% for 1, and 16.98% for 2.

3.4. UV–Vis diffuse reflectance spectra

As shown in Fig. S3, the UV–Vis diffuse reflectance spectra of 1 and 2 are similar, and exhibit absorption in the range of 210–700 nm. Their maximum absorptions appear in the UV region of 220–400 nm.

3.5. XRPD patterns

X-ray power diffraction patterns of 1 and 2 are shown in Fig. S4. The diffraction peaks of both simulated and experimental patterns match well in key position, thus indicating that the phase purities of 1 and 2 are good.

3.6. Photocatalytic hydrogen production

The photocatalytic activities of 1 and 2 were examined in the UV region. In the initial stage, the H2PtCl6 in the solution is reduced to Pt0 on surface of 1 and 2 as the photocatalytic H2 evolution promoter [26]. When the Hg lamp illuminates the photocatalytic system, the photocatalytic reaction takes palce, which can be described as follows [39]:XW12O40n-+hv→∗XW12O40n-∗XW12O40n-+CH3OH→XW12O40(n+1)-+CH2OXW12O40(n+1)-+hv→∗XW12O40(n+1)-∗XW12O40n-+CH3OH→XW12O40(n+1)-+CH2OXW12O40(n+2)-+H++Pt→XW12O40(n+1)-+1/2H2+Pt

The continuous H2 evolution is observed for 1 and 2, as shown in Fig. 6. The average H2 evolution rate is 2.70 μmol/h for 1, and 2.29 μmol/h for 2. Compared with the blank test, it is found that 1 and 2 are obviously active for photocatalytic H2 evolution. These are heterogeneous systems of polyoxometalates for photocatalytic H2 evolution [3]. The [Cu6(2-pzc)5(H2O)2]4+ frameworks in 1 and 2 play a part in immobilization and dispersion of polyoxotungstates.

4. Conclusion

Two isomorphic 3D Cu(I/II) coordination polymers based on [XW12O40]4− anions have been obtained. In the 3D [Cu6(2-pzc)5(H2O)2]4+ framework, there exist nano-sized channels viewed along the a and the c axis. The [XW12O40]4− anions, as guests, are filled in the channels. They play an important role not only in charge balance for the polymers and coordination demand for Cu(I/II) ions, but also in a template for nano-channels of the 3D [Cu6(2-pzc)5(H2O)2]4+ framework. This is the first example of a 3D framework constructed from Cu(I/II) ions and 2-pzc ligands by employing POMs as a template. Additionally, the polymers, as a heterogeneous photocatalyst, show activity for photocatalytic H2 evolution under UV irradiation.

An analogous reaction of [NH4][VO3] with Hpca and H2O2 (30% aq) in H2O at rt, followed by addition of EtOH, led to the formation of light red crystals of the oxo-peroxo complex [NH4][VO(O2)(pca)2]·2H2O (2).

    The development of new metal complex catalysts and efficient protocols for the mild and selective oxidation of alkanes, arenes, olefins, alcohols, and other organic substrates is a challenging topic in areas of homogeneous catalysis, coordination, and organic chemistry. In these oxidation reactions, the activity of many metal complex catalysts is dramatically improved upon addition of certain co-catalysts or promoters, which can also act as ligands in simple catalytic systems generated in situ. Given the fact that 2-pyrazinecarboxylic acid (Hpca) and analogous heteroaromatic acids such as 2,3-pyrazinedicarboxylic (H2pdca), picolinic (Hpic), and dipicolinic (H2dipic) acids are remarkably efficient and versatile co-catalysts in a high diversity of oxidation systems, the present review summarizes the state-of-the-art knowledge in this field. In particular, this contribution focuses on the use of Hpca as the most active co-catalyst and describes its coordination chemistry with regard to the oxidative transformations of various organic substrates, providing an overview of isolated vanadium, iron, and some other transition metal complexes derived from Hpca. The review also summarizes the applications of Hpca-assisted and related systems in the oxidation of various organic substrates by different oxidants, and highlights the main selectivity, kinetic, and mechanistic features of these oxidative transformations. The paper covers the application of catalytic systems wherein Hpca, H2pdca, Hpic, and H2dipic are used either as co-catalysts (additives) or as ligands within a metal complex catalyst. A special emphasis has been made on the oxidation of alkanes as very inert substrates, and the use of simple and highly efficient [VO3]−/Hpca/H2O2 system. The multifaceted roles of Hpca and analogous co-catalysts have been identified and the analysis of main mechanistic pathways and possible intermediates has been performed.

    The first generation of hydrogen from direct photocatalytic water splitting in the absence of organic agents using POM/TiO2 composites such as (SiW12O40)4−/TiO2 was realized by Kiwi and Gratzel [175].     Polyoxometalates (POMs) have peculiar optoelectronic properties and high reduction potential, playing as excellent electron pools. Thus, during the recent decade, POMs have been increasingly recognized as important building blocks for highly efficient photocatalysts and photoelectrochemical devices by hybridizing with photofunctional semiconductor nanostructures and organic/inorganic molecules. In this review, development of various molecular and nano composites derived from POMs are discussed with regard to photocatalytic environmental remediation, hydrogen production, carbon dioxide reduction and photoelectrochemical solar cells. The mechanisms involved in photo-induced interfacial electron transfer processes and subsequent photochemical reactions are explored along with a brief description about their advances in emerging solar application areas. More fundamental information of the photocatalytic activities of the POM-based composites would be very useful in constructing next generation artificial photosynthetic systems with higher spectral response in visible region for efficient solar energy conversion into electricity and fuels.
