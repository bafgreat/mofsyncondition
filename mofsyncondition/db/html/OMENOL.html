Introduction

Polynuclear coordination polymers (CPs) have emerged as an active area of research in recent years, not only owing to their potential applications as catalytic, optoelectronic, magnetic and luminescent materials,1 but also due to the aesthetically pleasing structures.2 Moreover, molecular magnetic materials based on discrete transition-metal aggregates have received much attention as they typically display properties of single molecular magnets.3 Metal cluster complexes based on carboxylic groups are the most frequently encountered in the research of coordination materials.3d,4 In addition, carboxylate groups can adopt a variety of coordination modes such as μ2–η2 and μ2–η1:η1 (syn–syn/syn–anti/anti–anti), which can provide intriguing structural diversity and further afford a better understanding of the relationship between structure and magnetism, such as M2(CO2)2 (M = Fe2+, Ni2+, Cu2+),5 M3(CO2)6 3e (M = Mn2+, Co2+, Ni2+), [Co5(OH)2(COO)8]6 and [Co8(OH)4(COO)12].6 Thus, polycarboxylate ligands that contain multiple bridging moieties and multidentate chelating modes are one of the most widely used linkers in the design of polynuclear complexes with interesting magnetic properties.7 One of them, biphenyl-3,4′,5-tricarboxylic acid (H3BPT),8 has recently attracted much attention since carboxylic groups can provide a variety of connection modes with metal centers to construct abundant structures. The H3BPT ligand can conform to the coordination geometries of the metal ions because its two phenyl rings can be rotated around the C–C single bond. Moreover, it can also act as a rigid organic linker to connect metal ions in forming BUs.

Keeping this in mind, we further adopt the Y-shaped bridging ligand H3BPT to synthesize a series of polynuclear complexes from discrete multi-nuclear clusters to form 1D, 2D or 3D networks. In cluster-based CPs, the coexistences of water, hydroxide and carboxylate have commonly been found to connect the metal ions to afford clusters, especially for Ni(ii) systems. Thus, the assemblies of Ni(ii) with multi-carboxylates are apt to form various clusters such as Ni3,4c,9 Ni4,9b Ni5,9c,10 Ni6,9c,11 Ni7,11,12 Ni8,13 Ni914 and even Ni14 15 as the BUs of CPs.

Generally, it is viable to select appropriate metal centers and certain organic linkers to form networks with predetermined structures and desired properties. However, in real chemical reactions, the synthesis of CPs is unpredictably affected by the environment, such as metal salts,4a temperature,16 solvents,17 pH values18 and ratio of reactants.19 To the best of our knowledge, we firstly constructed polynuclear Ni-complexes based on H3BPT and found that minor changes of reaction systems may lead to different architectures. As a contribution to this field, and a part of our work on Ni(ii)-cluster systems, we report herein the syntheses, crystal structures and the magnetic behaviours of a family of 1D, 2D and 3D frameworks based on Ni5, Ni4 and Ni7 BUs (Scheme 1).

To induce aggregation of Ni(ii) ions with the H3BPT linker, auxiliary ligands (2,2′-bipy or 1,4-bib) were employed. Three polynuclear nickel complexes, [Ni5(HBPT)4(OH)2(H2O)12]n (1), Ni4(BPT)2(OH)2(H2O)6]n·4nH2O (2) and [Ni7(BPT)2(1,4-bib)2(OH)6(HCO2)2]n·3nH2O (3), were synthesized successfully. The complexes exhibit 1D, 2D and 3D frameworks (Scheme 1) with penta-, tetra- and heptanuclear units as BUs, respectively. This work indicates that the various coordination modes of the H3BPT, as well as different N-donor auxiliary ligands under various solvothermal conditions, can effectively tune the kinds of BUs and final structures. Notably, the 2,2′-bipy in the synthesis of complex 2 only acts as an environmental factor instead of terminal ligand preventing a further extended complex. Furthermore, the bridging carboxylates and hydroxides were found to mediate ferromagnetic interactions between the nickel(ii) centers of the penta- and tetra-nuclear species. Complex 3, with heptanuclear BUs, was found to be an alternating magnetic chain (AF/F) with an S-shaped chain.

Experimental section

Materials and methods

H3BPT (biphenyl-3,4′,5-tricarboxylic acid) and 1,4-bib (1,4-bi(1H-imidazol-1-yl)benzene) were received from Jinan Camolai Trading Company, China. Other reagents and solvents were commercially available and used without further purification. Elemental analyses (C, H and N) were performed by a CHNO-Rapid instrument. IR spectra were obtained on a Bruker Tensor27 spectrometer in the 4000–400 cm−1 region with KBr pellets. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) data were collected on a Bruker D8 Advance with Cu-Kα radiation (λ = 1.5418 Å). TGA were carried out with a Dupont thermal analyzer in the temperature range 298–1073 K under N2 atmosphere with a heating rate of 10 K min−1. Magnetic susceptibility measurements were obtained with a SQUID magnetometer (Quantum MPMS) in the temperature range of 2.0–300 K by using an applied field of 1000 Oe.

Green needle crystals of 1 were obtained after cooling to room temperature. X-ray crystallography

Suitable single crystals of complexes 1, 2 and 3 were glued to thin glass fibers after selecting under an optical microscope. Diffraction data were collected using a Bruker Smart Apex II diffractometer equipped with a 1 K CCD instrument by using a graphite monochromator utilizing Mo-Kα (λ = 0.71073 Å) radiation at room temperature. The cell parameters were determined by SMART software.20 Data reduction was performed with SAINT Plus.21 Program SADABS22 was used for absorption corrections.

All structures were solved via the Patterson method using the program SHELXS-97.23 The refinement and further calculation were carried out with SHELXL-97.23 All non-H atoms were refined anisotropically, using a weighted full-matrix least squares on F2. Hydrogen atoms attached to C and O (R-COOH) atoms were placed geometrically and refined using a riding model approximation. Hydrogen atoms attached to O (water and coordinated hydroxy) were located from difference Fourier maps and their global Uiso values were refined. Some solvent water molecules in 2 and 3 are disordered using partial occupancies (see cifs). The crystallographic data and details of refinements for the complexes are summarized in Table 1. Results and discussion

Synthesis and characterization

The Y-shaped H3BPT has six carboxylate O atoms with strong coordination abilities, binding to metal ions, and results in varied coordination modes. From the prescriptions shown in Scheme 1, complexes 1 and 2 were obtained with the same synthesis conditions except the presence of auxiliary ligand (2,2′-bipy) in 2. In fact, the 2,2′-bipy in the synthesis process of 2 only acts as an organic base governing the deprotonation of H3BPT, which leads to the expansion of the structure. In addition, complex 3, with a 3D skeleton, was obtained by using the auxiliary ligand 1,4-bib and regulating the solvents.

It is noteworthy that there are different deprotonated results of the tricarboxylic ligand in the synthesis processes of the three complexes. Maybe the addition of auxiliary N-donor ligands makes for a more alkaline environment for the reaction system. Thus, the tricarboxylic ligand in complexes 2 and 3 are fully deprotonated.

FT-IR spectra

In the IR spectra, broad bands in the region of 3570–3250 cm−1 (3384 cm−1 for 1, 3531 and 3401 cm−1 for 2 and 3405 cm−1 for 3) indicate O–H stretching vibrations of the coordinated water molecules and/or hydroxyl groups in the complexes.24 The characteristic peaks of the asymmetric vibration and symmetric stretching vibration of the carboxylate groups are at 1615 and 1370 cm−1 (1), 1611 and 1379 cm−1 (2) and 1620 and 1366 cm−1 (3), respectively. They are obviously shifted to lower wave numbers in contrast to the free H3BPT ligand (1720 and 1391 cm−1), suggesting that the carboxylate groups in the complexes have been coordinated to the nickel(ii) ions.25 There are no bands in the region of 1720–1680 cm−1, indicating complete deprotonation of the carboxylic groups in the ligands (BPT3−) of 2 and 3. The characteristic peak at 1684 cm−1 in 1 reveals the partial deprotonation of the carboxylic groups in HBPT2−. The features are consistent with the results of the X-ray diffraction analysis below. In addition, the absorption peak located at 1305 cm−1 for 3 should be attributed to the CN stretching vibration of the 1,4-bib.24b

Descriptions of crystal structures

[Ni5(HBPT)4(OH)2(H2O)12]n (1)

Complex 1 crystallizes in the triclinic crystal system with a P space group and features 1D chain structures. The asymmetric unit consists of two and a half crystallographically independent Ni(ii) atoms, two bridging HBPT2− ligands, one μ3-OH− anion and six coordinated water molecules. Complex 1 extends its structure to a 1D chain based on [Ni5(μ3-OH)2]8+ cores via bridging HBPT2−, in which each Ni(ii) ion can be described as a slightly distorted octahedron and each Y-shaped ligand offers only two branches bonding to Ni(ii) cations. The Ni1 ion is encompassed by four carboxylate oxygen atoms (O1/O1i and O7/O7i, symmetry code: i 1 − x, 1 − y, 1 − z) from two HBPT2− (μ3-HBPT2− and μ2-H2BPT−) and two μ3-OH− (O13/O13i) anions (Ni1–O1 = 2.090(8) Å, Ni1–O7 = 2.052(8) Å, and Ni1–O13 = 2.008(8) Å). Besides the sharing atom O13 with Ni1 & Ni3, the Ni2 atom is coordinated by four coordinated water molecules and one O2 atom from a HBPT2− ligand, while the Ni3 atom is bounded by one sharing water molecule (O17) with the Ni2 ion, two coordinated water molecules (O18 and O19) and two oxygen atoms (O4i and O8) from HBPT2− ligands with the normal Ni–O bond length in the range of 2.004(8)–2.126(9) Å24a,26 (Fig. 1a).

Five Ni(ii) ions are connected by two symmetry-related μ3-OH− to generate a BU of [Ni5(μ3-OH)2]8+ in which the Ni1 ion locates at the inversion center. Thus, it can be viewed as two reverse triangles sharing a common vertex (Ni1). The BU is further connected by two coordinated water molecules and four carboxylate groups forming the pentanuclear [Ni5(μ3-OH)2(H2O)2(μ1,3-R-COO)4] cluster (Fig. 1b), and then the cluster links with two HBPT2− ligands to extend the structure. Two HBPT2− ligands with a μ2–η1:η1 mode take part in to form the pentanuclear BU and the others with a μ3–η1:η1:η1 mode that links the neighbouring Bus, thus generating the 1D chain structure (Fig. 1c).

[Ni4(BPT)2(OH)2(H2O)6]n·4nH2O (2)

Complex 2 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P and displays 2D layered framework based on the [Ni4(μ3-OH)2]6+ core by BPT3− linkers. As shown in Fig. 2a, the asymmetric unit of 2 is comprised of two independent Ni(ii) ions, one bridging BPT3−, one μ3-OH− anion, three coordinated water molecules and two guest water molecules. The environment of each Ni(ii) ion is an octahedral coordination geometry [NiO6] and five O atoms of each Y-shaped ligand contribute to the CP. The Ni1 ion is coordinated by three oxygen atoms from three BPT3− ligands (O1, O3iii and O5i, symmetry codes: i − x, −y, 2 − z; iii 1 − x, 1 − y, 2 − z), one coordinated water molecule (O8) and two μ3-OH− anions (O7/O7ii, symmetry code: ii 1 − x, −y, 1 − z) with bond lengths of 2.029(2)–2.107(2) Å, while the six O atoms coordinated to Ni2 ion from three individual BPT3− ligands (O2, O3iii and O6iv, symmetry code: iv 1 + x, y, −1 + z), two coordinated water molecules (O9 and O10) and one hydroxyl anion (O7) shared with other nickel(ii) ions. The distances of Ni–O bonds are in the range from 1.996(2) to 2.137(2) Å, which are comparable to other reported complexes containing Ni–O segments.19,21

As shown in Fig. 2b, four Ni(ii) ions are connected by two μ3-OH− groups to form a cluster [Ni4(μ3-OH)2]6+ BU, which can be considered as two reverse triangles sharing a common edge. The BUs are further bridged by six carboxylate groups with μ2–η1:η1 and μ2–η2 coordinated modes of Y-shaped ligands to generate the tetranuclear [Ni4(μ3-OH)2(μ1,3-R-COO)4(μ1,1-R-COO)2] cluster. Thus, each Ni4 cluster in 2 is linked by six BPT3− ligands, while each BPT3− ligand with the same μ6–η1:η1:η1:η1:η2 style connects with three [Ni4(μ3-OH)2]6+ cores forming a unique 2D framework. Interestingly, the adjacent BPT3− ligands are located at opposite positions (Fig. 2c).

[Ni7(BPT)2(1,4-bib)2(OH)6(HCO2)2]n·3nH2O (3)

Complex 3 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/c and displays a 3D framework based on [Ni7(μ3-OH)4]10+ cores by bridges of BPT3−, 1,4-bib and formate. The asymmetric unit of 3 consists of three and a half independent Ni(ii) ions, one bridging BPT3−, one 1,4-bib, three μ3-OH− anions, one formate (deriving from hydrolysis of DMF)24b,27 and one and a half guest water molecules (Fig. 3a). Both Ni1 and Ni2 take the distorted octahedral coordination of [NiO5N]. The coordination geometry of the Ni1 ion is completely enclosed by two oxygen atoms from two individual BPT3− ligands (O1 & O4i, symmetry code: i x, 1 − y, 1/2 + z), two oxygen atoms (O7 & O7ii, symmetry code: ii 1 – x, 2 − y, 1 − z) from two μ3-OH− anions and one formate (O10ii), as well as one nitrogen atom (N1) from the 1,4-bib ligand. Whereas the coordination geometry of the Ni2 ion is constructed by three oxygen atoms (O7, O8iii & O9ii, symmetry code: iii −1/2 + x, 1/2 + y, z) from three μ3-OH− anions, O2 atom from one BPT3− ligand, O11i atom from one formate and one nitrogen atom (N4iv, symmetry code: iv −1/2 + x, 3/2 − y, −1/2 + z) from the 1,4-bib ligand. The Ni3 and Ni4 atoms adopt the same slightly octahedral coordination geometries [NiO6]. Six oxygen atoms coordinated to Ni3 atom locating at a 2-fold axes come from two BPT3− ligands (O3/O3v, symmetry code: v 1 – x, y, 1/2 − z), two μ3-OH− anions (O9/O9v) and two formates (O10/O10v), while six oxygen atoms around Ni4 are from three μ3-OH− anions (O7vii, O8/O8vi, symmetry codes: vi 2 − x, 1 − y, 1 − z; vii 3/2 – x, 3/2 − y, 1 − z), one formate (O11viii, symmetry code: viii 1/2 + x, 1/2 − y, 1/2 + z) and one μ2–η1:η1 carboxylate group (O5 and O6). The bond lengths of Ni–O and Ni–N are in the range of 1.992(7)–2.169(6) and 2.059(7)–2.076(8) Å, respectively.

In 3, seven Ni(ii) ions are bridged by six μ3-OH− groups (O8iii/O8vi, O9i/O9ii and O7/O7ii), three carboxylate groups and two formates, generating a [Ni7(μ3-OH)6(R-COO)3(CHOO)2] cluster that can be regarded as a [Ni4(μ3-OH)4(μ1,1-CHOO)2]6+ butterfly connected to a V-shaped [Ni3(μ3-OH)2(μ1,3-R-COO)2(μ1,1-CHOO)2] unit via different sharing bridges of two μ3-OH, one μ1,3-R-COO and one μ1,3-CHOO, respectively (Fig. 3b). The Ni7-cluster BUs are linked together by the bridges of a hydroxyl (O9) and a formate (O10–C28, H–O11) forming a 1D metal cluster chain along the crystallographic c direction like a crawling snake (Fig. 3c). Then, the 1D chains extend via the BPT3− ligands with two μ2–η1:η1 carboxylate groups generating 2D layers, which are further linked by 1,4-bib ligands building a 3D structure (Fig. 3d).

Comparison of the structures

As discussed above, complexes 1, 2 and 3 are composed of BUs with [Ni5(μ3-OH)2(H2O)2(R-COO)4], [Ni4(μ3-OH)2(R-COO)6] and [Ni7(μ3-OH)6(R-COO)3(CHOO)2] clusters, respectively. All the Ni(ii) centers adopt distorted octahedral geometries with [NiO6] in 1 and 2, while [NiO5N] and [NiO6] in 3. Then, the Ni5, Ni4 and Ni7 cores are further connected by various deprotonated HBPT2− or BPT3− (μ2- and μ3- for 1, μ6- for 2, and μ5- for 3, respectively, Scheme 2) forming 1D, 2D and 3D frameworks, respectively. The 1D chain structure of 1 is formed only by the partially deprotonated μ3–η1:η1:η1 HBPT2− ligands, while the other μ2–η1:η1 H2BPT− ligands are simply bridged to form Ni5 clusters. In 2, the wholly deprotonated BPT3− ligands (μ6–η1:η1:η1:η1:η2) extend Ni4 clusters to form a 2D layered architecture through alternating connections. For 3, the framework is extended by 1,4-bib and wholly deprotonated BPT3− ligands (μ5-η1:η1:η1:η1:η1:η1). In this work, complexes 1 and 2 were prepared by identical solvothermal conditions, except for the addition of 2,2′-bipy in 2. Obviously, the auxiliary ligand 2,2′-bipy in 2 mainly adjusts the reaction process instead of preventing the further interconnection as a terminal ligand in our previous work.28 The complex 3 was obtained by regulating the solvents and auxiliary ligand. In this sense, the chemical reaction conditions played an important role in governing the coordination clusters and the final supramolecular structures in the reported assembled systems.

XPRD and thermal analyses

In order to confirm the phase purity of complexes 1–3, PXRD data was received. The experimental PXRD patterns are in agreement with the simulated ones from the single-crystal X-ray data, demonstrating the high phase purity of the complexes (Fig. S2†). The TGA of complexes 1–3 was performed in the range of 298–1073 K with a heating rate of 10 K min−1 under N2 atmosphere (Fig. 4). For 1, the weight loss of 15.1% from room temperature to 548 K corresponds to the loss of all the coordinated water molecules (calcd 14.9%). Then, the framework begins to decompose upon further heating. For 2, the first weight loss of 17.0% from room temperature to 642 K, corresponds to the loss of three coordinated and two lattice water molecules (calcd 17.7%). The TGA of 3 indicates an initial weight loss of 3.23% from 402 to 590 K, which can be attributed to the loss of three guest water molecules (calcd 3.28%). Upon further heating, the framework decomposes vigorously.

Magnetic measurements and modelling

The variable temperature magnetic susceptibilities of the three CPs were recorded in the range of 2 to 300 K. The diamagnetic contributions of the samples were corrected from Pascal's constants. Accurate analytical expressions for the energy levels of the multi-nuclear nickel(ii) complexes with multi-dimensional systems from their Hamiltonians are very complex, and they probably could not provide a unique solution. In order to fit the experimental data, the theoretical functions of these Hamiltonians are further simplified and limited to multi-nuclear systems for isotropic exchange couplings without the zero-field splitting parameter D.

[Ni5(HBPT)4(OH)2(H2O)12]n (1)

The χMT and χMversus T plots of 1 are shown in Fig. 5. The χMT value at 300 K is 7.48 cm3 mol−1 K, which is expected for the total spin-only value of five Ni(ii) with S = 1 and g = 2.45 isolated high-spin d8 ions. The χMT product increases slightly with cooling from room temperature to 100 K, and then it increases rapidly to 33.84 cm3 mol−1 K at 5 K with ferromagnetic interactions inside the pentanuclear units. Below 5 K, the χMT product slowly decreases to 21.14 cm3 mol−1 K at 2.0 K, probably due to weak inter-molecular antiferromagnetic interactions and/or small anisotropy.29 The temperature dependence of χM−1 obeys the Curie−Weiss law χ = C/(T − θ) with C = 7.27(2) cm3 mol−1 K and θ = 5.78(1) K (see Fig. 5, inset), which indicates ferromagnetic interactions between the Ni(ii) ions.

It is necessary to find all possible magnetic pathways in the Ni5-cluster in order to fit the magnetic susceptibility data of 1 reasonably. A simplified model (Scheme 3) reveals that there are two different exchange pathways with two J coupling constants for the bowtie structure. J1 is associated with interactions between the Ni1 and Ni2 (or Ni3) ions bridged by OH− anions (O13 and O13i) with the Ni⋯Ni distances of 3.483(2)–3.552(2) Å; J2 is associated with the Ni2⋯Ni3i and Ni3⋯Ni2i interactions bridged by coordinated water molecules (O17/O17i) with the Ni⋯Ni distance of 3.138(2) Å. The spin Hamiltonian for the system, illustrated in Scheme 3, is given by eqn (1).30 H = −2J1(S1S2 + S1S2′ + S1S3 + S1S3′) − 2J2(S2S3′ + S2′S3) (1) (2) (3) where, S23′ = S2 + S3′, S32′ = S3 + S2′, ST = S1 + S23′ + S32′, A = Ng2β2, (N, k, g, & β take the usual meanings), zJ′ for an additional coupling parameter, and TIP for the temperature independent paramagnetism of the complex. A good fit of the experimental data with a temperature range of 2–300 K gives parameters of J1 = 1.85(3), J2 = 2.25(4), zJ′ = −0.42(1) cm−1, g = 2.04(3), θ = 11.64(2) K with R = 4.0 × 10−4 based on χM–T by fixing TIP at 500 × 10−6 cm3 mol−1. The positive values of J1 and J2 suggest the presence of dominant ferromagnetic interactions among the nickel(ii) centers in 1. These magnetic parameters agree quite well with the literature.30

The variable-field ac magnetic susceptibility of complex 1 was measured from 111 to 3111 Hz at low temperature, but no obvious χ′′M peak was observed for this complex.

Structurally, complexes with the [Ni5] unit exhibit varied topologies, such as linear, bowtie, tetragonal pyramid and tetrahedron. For the complexes containing a [Ni5] unit with the bowtie topology, a comparison of magnetic and structural parameters is summarized in Table 2. Only complex 1 is a 1D coordinated polymer connected the bowtie cores as BUs by HBPT2− bridges. The magnetic couplings between nickel(ii) ions in 1 and the literature30 are similar because they have the closed-structure characteristic. Obviously, the influence of the long bridge with a space of 10.24 Å on the magnetic behaviour of the complex is very limited. From the comparison shown in Table 2, there are significant variations in the magnetic couplings of the bowtie structures with different bridging patterns.

[Ni4(BPT)2(OH)2(H2O)6]n·4nH2O (2)

As shown in Fig. 6, the χMT value of 2 is 5.15 cm3 mol−1 K at 300 K, which is slightly higher than the spin-only value of 4.84 cm3 mol−1 K expected for four magnetically uncoupled Ni(ii) ions with S = 1 and g = 2.2. The χMT value for 2 continuously increases as the temperature decreases and reaches a value of 7.14 cm3 mol−1 K at 2 K, indicating the dominant ferromagnetic behaviour between the Ni(ii) ions in 2. The temperature dependence of χM−1 obeys the Curie–Weiss law χ = C/(T − θ) with C = 5.26(2) cm3 mol−1 K and θ = 6.32(1) K (Fig. 6, inset).

By careful inspection of the structure, the two main magnetic exchange pathways between the neighbouring Ni(ii) ions for 2 have been found, in which there are the pairs of nickel ions bridged by carboxylate groups with the Ni⋯Ni distances of 3.022(7)–3.525(7) Å and other interactions between Ni1⋯Ni1ii through OH− anion (O7/O7ii) with the Ni⋯Ni distance of 3.118(7) Å. Compared with other tetranuclear nickel magnetic structures with symmetries of Td, S, C3v, D2d and C2v, complex 2 has lower symmetry with a C2 point group. To simplify the magnetic model, the difference of structural parameters in complex 2 was ignored. Thus, the magnetic coupling topology deduced from the planar Ni4-cluster36 with a C2v symmetry has been considered, as shown in Scheme 4. The spin Hamiltonian is in such a system and a correction is done by eqn (3).

The best fit parameters thus obtained are as follows: J = 5.76(6), zJ′ = −1.49(7) cm−1, g = 2.23(1), θ = 16.25(8) K with R = 1.2 × 10−3 based on χM–T by fixing TIP at 400 × 10−6 cm3 mol−1. The J value obtained corresponds to a weak ferromagnetic coupling between the adjacent Ni(ii) ions.

The temperature-dependent ac susceptibility of complex 2 was also measured from 111 to 3111 Hz. There was no obvious relaxation of the magnetization for the cluster.

[Ni7(BPT)2(1,4-bib)2(OH)6(HCO2)2]n·3nH2O (3)

The magnetic behaviour of complex 3 is obviously different from the magnetic behaviours of complexes 1 and 2. The room temperature χMT value for complex 3 is 6.99 cm3 mol−1 K, which is close to the expected value (7.00 cm3 mol−1 K) for seven isolated Ni(ii) ions (g = 2.0) (Fig. 7). The χMT value gradually decreases with temperature to reach the value of 0.88 cm3 mol−1 K at 1.8 K, indicating weak antiferromagnetic exchange among the nickel(ii) centers. The temperature dependence of the reciprocal susceptibility (χM−1) above 50 K follows the Curie–Weiss law χ = C/(T − θ) with a Weiss constant θ = −62.71(2) K and C = 8.65(1) cm3 mol−1 K (Fig. 7, inset). The larger negative Weiss constant also indicates a dominant intramolecular anti-ferromagnetic interaction between these adjacent Ni(ii) ions through the oxygen bridges.

The spin Hamiltonian for the heptanuclear snake chain system, illustrated in Scheme 5, is given by eqn (4). Namely, the spin Hamiltonian is a summation of the heptamer and the latter is divided into two parts, a line trimer and a parallelogram. J1 is associated with interactions between the Ni1 and Ni3 ions bridged by OH− anion (O9) with the Ni⋯Ni distance of 3.114(6) Å; J2 is associated with the Ni2⋯Ni4iii/Ni4iv, Ni2v⋯Ni4iii/Ni4iv and Ni4iii⋯Ni4iv interactions bridged by OH− anions with the Ni⋯Ni distances of 3.006(2)–3.219(2) Å and 3.045(2) Å. J3 is associated with interaction between the line trimer and parallelogram with the Ni1⋯Ni2 distance of 2.995(7) Å. H = ∑Hheptamer = [Htrimer + Htetramer]n (4)

Attempting to fit the experimental data with the theoretical calculations of the magnetic susceptibility using the full Hamiltonian (4) came to nothing because an accurate analytical expression for eqn (4) is very complicated. Some exchange couplings between heptanuclear nickel ions are neglected as an approximation method.

In order to obtain the g factor of the anti-ferromagnetic interaction of the heptanuclear Ni(ii) system from the experiment, the electron spin resonance of the d8 electron configuration was employed. As shown in Fig. 8, the X-band EPR spectra of complex 3 of the crystal powder sample at 2 and 50 K had broad signals and the g value obtained was 2.102. Thus, the good quality of fitting shown in Fig. 7 was obtained by fixing the experimental g value and TIP = 700 × 10−6 cm3 mol−1, suggesting that suitable fitting parameters are J1 = −2.64(3), J2 = −23.22(19), J3 = 12.02(5), zJ = 0.87(1) cm−1, θ = −17.16(1) K with R = 4.6 × 10−4 based on χMT–T of the experimental data with a temperature range of 2–300 K or J1 = −2.89(1), J2 = −24.33(29), J3 = 10.95(4), zJ = 1.07(1) cm−1, θ = −20.85(1) K, with R = 5.3 × 10−7 based on χM–T of the experimental data with a temperature range of 2–300 K. The values of J1 and J2 suggest the presence of dominant antiferromagnetic interactions intra-trimer and intra-tetramer among the nickel(ii) centers, while J3 reveals ferromagnetic coupling between the two multinuclear bodies in 3. Thus, the S-shaped chain may be considered as an alternating magnetic chain (AF/F).

As indicated above, complexes 1–3 exhibit two ferromagnetic behaviours (1 & 2) and one AF/F alternating magnetic chain (3) inside their Ni5, Ni4 and Ni7 cores. There are common characteristics in the three coordinated polymers, namely, carboxylate bridges controlling their structures that further influences their magnetic features.

Conclusions

In summary, we have successfully synthesized three new complexes with different cluster units constructed from the assembly of H3BPT with Ni(ii) ion by selected N-donor ancillary ligands under various solvothermal conditions. Complex 1 forms a 1D chain based on bowtie [Ni5(μ3-OH)2(H2O)2(R-COO)4] clusters and partly deprotonated HBPT2−. Complex 2 displays a 2D framework with [Ni4(μ3-OH)2(R-COO)6] clusters and wholly deprotonated μ6-BPT3− ligands. While complex 3 is a 3D coordination polymer based on [Ni7(μ3-OH)6(R-COO)3(CHOO)2] clusters, wholly deprotonated μ5-BPT3− and 1,4-bib ligands. This work demonstrates that coordination modes of the H3BPT ligand, as well as different N-donor auxiliary ligands under various solvothermal conditions, can effectively tune the kinds of BUs and final structures. Magnetic studies show the occurrence of ferromagnetic exchange interactions for complexes 1 and 2, while an alternating magnetic chain (AF/F) is observed in 3. This work illustrates a facile and simple method to obtain new Ni cluster-based CPs with unique structures and properties by using auxiliary ligands and regulating the solvents.
