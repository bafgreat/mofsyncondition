Most materials expand along all three directions when heated due to anharmonic lattice phonon vibration and usually the coefficients of positive linear thermal expansion are smaller than 20 × 10−6 K−1.1 However, some materials have been found to contract in 1D, 2D and even 3D upon heating which are called negative thermal expansion (NTE).2–4 NTE can be caused by a variety of mechanisms, such as transverse vibration of bridging atoms,4a–f spontaneous volume ferroelectrostriction (SVFS),2 charge transfer,5 and magnetic transition.4d What's more, some framework structures with geometric flexibility have been found to possess large anisotropic thermal expansion.6 The emergence of these materials can meet the demands of artificial muscle and molecule machines.7 Most of the reported flexible frameworks are based on hinge- or fence-like structure, such as Ag3[Co(CN)6],6a methanol monohydrate,6c HMOF-1,6d MCF-346eetc. Upon heating, these materials expand along one direction, leading to contraction along the perpendicular directions. Generally, framework structures with larger pores and extended linkers possess larger NTE. The existence of large pores can provide more spaces for the thermal motion of linkers, which can be proved by the weakened NTE of interpenetrated structures and guest loaded structures.8 The linkers with extended lengths can also provide more degrees of freedom, thus causing enhanced NTE as proved in IRMOFs.9 Some porous coordination polymers have been found to possess NTE, however, the thermal expansion properties will be easily influenced by the environment, such as atmosphere, humidity etc.8b Compared with porous ones, nonporous NTE coordination polymers will possess more stability. Although nonporous coordination polymers have been found to possess many intriguing characteristics,10 less focus has been paid to the NTE properties.11

With interest in extending NTE in nonporous framework materials, we synthesized two nonporous coordination polymers M(eim)2 (M = Zn, Cd; eim = 2-ethylimidazole) according to previously reported methods with a little modification.12 Both compounds crystallized in space group P3121/P3221 with the qtz topology. In the structure, the metal ion adopts a tetrahedral coordination geometry connected by four imidazole groups forming 3D networks. There exist helical structures along the c direction (Fig. 1b). Both of the two compounds are thermally stable up to 350 °C and no weight loss or phase transition occurred at this temperature (Fig. S1 and S2, ESI†). Variable temperature powder X-ray diffraction (VT-PXRD) was performed to determine the thermal expansion properties of Zn(eim)2 and Cd(eim)2 from 123 K to 473 K with an interval of 25 K, and the reversibility was verified by cooling from 423 K to 123 K with an interval of 100 K. Temperature dependent lattice parameters are extracted from VT-PXRD using the Lebail method in the GSAS suite of programs.13 The fitted results and PASCal results of Cd(eim)2 are shown in Fig. 2b.14 Both of the compounds show obvious anisotropic thermal expansion with the linear thermal expansion coefficients of αa = 30 × 10−6 K−1, αc = −21 × 10−6 K−1 for Zn(eim)2 and αa = 66 × 10−6 K−1, αc = −55 × 10−6 K−1 for Cd(eim)2 (Fig. 2a). It should be noted that the linear thermal expansion coefficient of Cd(eim)2 along an axis is even larger than that of mercury in a clinical thermometer (αl = 61 × 10−6 K−1 at 293 K).15 The remarkable value αc is five times larger than that of the famous NTE material ZrW2O8 (αa = −9.1 × 10−6 K−1) and is comparable with those of the dicyanometallate based flexible structures.6b Compared with dicyanometallate coordination polymers, imidazole bridged materials have shorter linkers (2.2 Å for imidazole and 6.2 Å for NC–Au–CN) and more steric hindrance (the existence of imidazole groups and ethyl). What is more, the thermal expansion coefficients are much larger than those of other nonporous coordination polymers, such as Zn(BTC)(HBPP) and [FeL3][BF4]2·xH2O.16

To further reveal the mechanism leading to large anisotropic thermal expansion of Cd(eim)2, the variable temperature single-crystal X-ray diffraction (VT-SCXRD) study was carried out from 150 K to 300 K at an interval of 50 K (Table S3, ESI†). During the heating process, no large bond length changes were found (αl < 27 × 10−6 K−1) (Table S4, ESI†). We surmise that the anomalous anisotropic thermal expansion may be caused by the framework flexibility. Considering the rigidity of an imidazole group and no obvious changes upon heating (Table S4, ESI†), the imidazole group could be treated as an “atom” with the same framework geometry structure of Cd(eim)2 and it is quite similar to β-quartz. In each cell, there exist two helical columns, which are built from two chains of Cd–eim–Cd. The helical chain can be seen as a spring and the crystal structure will be a spring array perpendicular to the ab plane. Upon heating, the chain will be compressed along the c axis and expand in the ab plane (Fig. 3c). This spring-like thermal motion can be proved by the changes in θ which indicates the angle between three neighboring cadmium atoms along the chain (Fig. 3c). The changed value of θ is 0.92° from 150 K to 300 K, which is comparable with the values of the reported flexible structures. The relative changes in d2, which equals to the lattice parameter c, are nearly ten times larger than the value of d1, which indicates the distance between two neighboring cadmium atoms (Fig. 3e). Thus confirming that the thermal expansion property is indeed a geometry effect rather than a certain bond length change. Due to the same crystal structures of Zn(eim)2 and Cd(eim)2, the mechanism causing anisotropic thermal expansion may be the same. As for the difference of thermal expansion coefficients between Zn(eim)2 and Cd(eim)2, it may be caused by a different chain component, just like springs made from different metals possess different elasticities. Owing to the absence of single crystals of Zn(eim)2, direct comparison of the structural changes upon heating could not be performed. However, the thermal expansion coefficient of the Zn–N bond (10 × 10−6 K−1) extracted from EXAFS shows nearly one time smaller than the value of Cd–N (27 × 10−6 K−1, 10 × 10−6 K−1) (Fig. 3g).

In addition, the temperature dependent solid state photoluminescence of Cd(eim)2 was investigated. The free ligand 2-ethylimidazole shows poor luminescence properties. However, when coordinated with cadmium, the emission intensity of Cd(eim)2 shows a significant enhancement, which is approximately ten times larger than that of the free ligand (Fig. S5, ESI†). Temperature dependent luminescence results are shown in Fig. 4a. With the increase of temperature, the emission spectra show a small shift in wavelength, but in the 175–350 K range the emission intensities decrease dramatically. The decrease of emission intensity can be fitted linearly with temperature in the 175–350 K range, thus making it a candidate for luminescence thermometers.

In summary, we have studied two nonporous coordination compounds with large anisotropic thermal expansion. The spring-like thermal motion plays a key role in the lattice parameter changes. Temperature dependent emission spectra show a dramatically linear decrease. The combination of large anisotropic thermal expansion and a linear decrease in emission intensity makes it a candidate for luminescence thermometers or other thermo-responsive devices.
