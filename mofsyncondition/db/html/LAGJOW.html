Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have aroused a great deal of interest in view of their fascinating structures with permanent porosities, large surface areas and highly tunable functionalities, and wide applications in catalysis, luminescence sensing, gas separation, drug delivery, and biomedical imaging.1–4 However, the instability of MOFs significantly hampers their practical applications.5 Researchers have proved that constructing rigid metal cluster-based nodes can largely enhance the stability of MOFs due to their stable geometries. Besides, cluster-based nodes endow MOFs with larger channels and unique physicochemical properties.6 Therefore, recently, cluster-organic frameworks became a rapidly growing field in materials chemistry and physics science.

To date, a large number of cluster-organic frameworks with interesting properties have been reported, of which the majority contain only one type of metal cluster. For example, hypersensitive dual-function luminescence switching of Ag12bpy contains [Ag12] clusters;7 highly stable PCN-777 contains [Zr6O4OH4(COO)12] clusters;8 sodium-coupled electron transfer reactivity of MIL-125 and NH2-MIL-125 contain [Ti8O8] clusters.9 Introducing other metals into the monometallic clusters may give heterometallic cluster-organic frameworks with fascinating architectures and multi-functionality owing to the synergistic effect among different metals.10,11

In recent years, a number of p–d, d–d and d–f heterometallic cluster-organic frameworks built from different organic ligands have been synthesized, and the typical examples are [Bi3Cu4], [Bi7Cu12],12 [Fe2M] (M = Co, Ni, Zn),13 [X4Cu6] (X = Cd, Mn),14 [Cu12Zn4],15 [Cd4Mn10],16 [Ln4Cu10] (Ln = Sm, Gd),17 [Ni6Ln6] (Ln = Gd, Tb, Dy),18 [Eu6Zn],19 [Eu24Ti8],20 and [Gd12Fe14].21 Nevertheless, few researches about p–f heterometallic clusters, especially heterometallic cluster-organic frameworks, have been reported so far. We are especially interested in the design and construction of [LnmGen] 4p–4f heterometallic cluster-organic frameworks for the following reasons: i) lanthanide metals exhibit flexible coordination geometries, such as octahedron, trigonal prism, capped trigonal prism and capped triangular cupola, benefitting the creation of versatile clusters with different shapes, sizes, and coordination numbers; ii) P block metal Ge also possesses GeX4 tetrahedral, GeX5 trigonal bipyramid/square pyramid and GeX6 octahedral geometries (X = O, OH, F), and these geometries can further form well-defined clusters, such as Ge7, Ge8, Ge9, Ge10, and Ge13;22,23 iii) more importantly, lanthanide metals show excellent luminescence and magnetic properties, and Ge-based clusters display unique applications in photocatalysis, ion-exchange, and gas adsorption and separation. Constructing cluster-organic frameworks with the coexistence of lanthanide and Ge metals can not only give interesting structures with striking properties but also promote the development of p–f heterometallic cluster-organic frameworks.24,25

Herein, we successfully prepared two new [Ln11Ge12] p–f heterometallic cluster-organic frameworks by using bis(carboxyethylgermanium) sesquioxide (H2E2Ge2O3, E = –CH2CH2COO–) under mild hydrothermal conditions: [Ln11Ge12(μ3-O)24E12(pza)8(H2O)12]·OH·7H2O (Ln = Pr L1, Nd L2; Hpza = 2-pyrazinecarboxylic acid). In L1 and L2, H2E2Ge2O3 acts as a germanium source and an organic bridging ligand. The [Ln11Ge12] cluster displays a sandwich-like geometry, which is built from two Ln-capped Ge6-rings and a gear-like [Ln9] cluster, and the as-synthesized [Ln11Ge12] heterometallic cluster-organic frameworks present 2D layered structures. As we all know, 2D layered materials have been proved to possess unique physical and chemical properties originating from their ultrathin thickness and 2D morphology.26,27 2D cluster-organic frameworks, as a new type of 2D layered materials, possess many promising features, such as highly ordered pores, large surface area, and tunable structures.28 In particular, the functions of cluster-organic frameworks could be tuned by changing the metal ions and ligands. In consideration of those merits, the as-constructed 2D layered [Ln11Ge12] heterometallic cluster-organic frameworks may have promising application prospects. Thus, for L1, the property of luminescence sensing of aqueous heavy metals was studied in detail. Interestingly, L1 can selectively and sensitively detect Fe3+, demonstrating its promising application in environmental monitoring. Furthermore, the synthesized 4p–4f heterometallic cluster-organic frameworks in this work may provide a model for the building mechanism of this type of material.

Experimental section

Materials and methods

Lanthanide salts and other reagents were purchased commercially and used without further purification. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns were collected on a Rigaku Smartlab 9 kW instrument with Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5406 Å). Elemental analyses of C, H, and N were conducted on a vario EL cube analyzer. The Pr and Nd contents in the as-synthesized compounds were measured by inductively coupled plasma atomic emission spectrometry (ICP-AES, ICPE-9000 Shimadzu). Fourier transform infrared (FT-IR) spectra were obtained on a Nicolet 460 E.S.P. spectrophotometer in the range of 4000–500 cm−1. The UV-vis absorption spectra were collected on a PerkinElmer Lambda 750 spectrophotometer using BaSO4 as a reference. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was carried out on a Mettler TGA/SDTA 851 thermal analyzer in the temperature range of 25–764 °C in an air atmosphere with a heating rate of 5 °C min−1. Photoluminescence spectra were recorded on an Edinburgh FLS980 fluorescence spectrometer at room temperature. The emission quantum yield (QY) was determined using an integrating sphere from an Edinburgh FLS980 phosphorimeter.

Preparation of L1 and L2

H2E2Ge2O3 (0.084 g, 0.247 mmol), Pr(NO3)3·6H2O (0.218 g, 0.50 mmol) or Nd(NO3)3·6H2O (0.219 g, 0.50 mmol) and Hpza (0.124 g, 1.0 mmol) were dissolved in 10 mL deionized water under stirring. Then, triethylamine was added to adjust the pH to 6.0. After stirring for 30 min, the mixture was sealed in a 25 mL Teflon-lined steel autoclave, which was heated at 170 °C for 4 days and then cooled to room temperature. Block crystals were obtained in 57% yield for L1 (52% for L2) based on H2E2Ge2O3 after being filtered and washed with distilled water. Elemental analyses (%) calcd for C76H111N16O84Ge12Pr11: C 18.20, H 2.25, N 4.47, Pr 30.90. Found: C 17.62, H 2.18, N 4.20, Pr 30.71; calcd for C76H111N16O84Ge12Nd11: C 18.07, H 2.23, N 4.44, Nd 31.41. Found: C 17.49, H 2.16, N 4.17, Nd 31.23. IR (KBr, cm−1): 3409 (br), 2956 (w), 2922 (w), 1624 (m), 1571 (vs), 1418 (m), 1366 (m), 1289 (w), 1165 (m), 1055 (w), 1031 (w), 764 (vs), 602 (w), 526 (w) (Fig. S1†).

X-ray crystallography

The structural data for L1 and L2 were collected on a Bruker-AXS D8 Advance X-ray diffractometer with graphite monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073). Both structures were solved by the direct methods and refined on F2 using the SHELXTL 97 program package. Absorption corrections of the two compounds were made by the multi-scan technique. All the non-hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic displacement parameters. Hydrogen atoms associated with carbon and nitrogen atoms were positioned with an idealized geometry and refined isotropically. Nevertheless, no hydrogen atoms attached to water molecules were located from the difference Fourier map. In both compounds, only partial lattice water molecules can be accurately assigned from the residual electron peaks, the rest were directly included in the molecular formula based on the elemental analysis, TG analysis, and calculations of the electron count in the voids with SQUEEZE. Relevant crystallographic data and structural refinements for L1 and L2 are summarized in Table S1.† The CCDC numbers have been deposited at the Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre: 1975865 and 1975867 for L1 and L2, respectively.

Luminescence-sensing experiment

5 mg of ground L1 was immersed in 4 mL 0.001 M aqueous solutions of M(NO3)x (Fe3+, Mn2+, Co2+, Ni2+, Cu2+, Zn2+, Cd2+, Pb2+ and Bi3+), CrCl3 and HgCl2, respectively, at room temperature for 12 h before luminescence measurements. The anti-interference experiments: 5 mg of L1 was added to 2 mL aqueous solutions containing other metal ions (1 × 10−3 M) for collecting luminescence spectra. Then, 2 mL Fe3+ solution (1 × 10−3 M) was added to them for collecting luminescence spectra, respectively. Time dependent-luminescence: 5 mg of L1 was immersed in 4 mL Fe(NO3)3 aqueous solutions for different times: 5 min, 10 min, 20 min, 30 min, 40 min, 50 min, and 60 min, respectively. The quantitative fluorescence titration experiments: 5 mg of L1 was dispersed in deionized water (2 mL) for 12 h to form a stable suspension, then the Fe3+ content was gradually increased.

Results and discussion

Structural description

Compounds L1 and L2 crystallize in the monoclinic space group Cc with Flack parameters of 0.039(16) for L1 and 0.02(2) for L2 corroborating the absolute configuration in the crystals. L1 and L2 are isomorphic, so only the structure of L1 is discussed in detail. Its asymmetric unit consists of eleven Pr3+ ions, six E2Ge2O32− ligands, eight pza− ligands, twelve coordination water molecules, seven lattice water molecules, and one hydroxyl molecule (Fig. 1). Six Pr3+ ions constitute a six-membered ring (Pr6-ring) by edge-sharing oxygen atoms, among which four Pr3+ ions (Pr1, Pr2, Pr4, and Pr6) are eight-coordinated in bicapped trigonal prisms by eight O atoms (two from E groups, four μ3-O from Ge2O3 and two from pza− ligands), whereas one additional water molecule coordinates to Pr3 and Pr5, respectively, leading to the nine-coordinated tricapped trigonal prism geometries (Fig. S2†). Three other Pr3+ ions are attached to the Pr6-ring by edge-sharing O atoms, forming a gear-like [Pr9] unit (Fig. 1a and b). All the attached Pr3+ ions are in nine-coordinated tricapped trigonal prism geometries but have different coordination environments; for Pr9: two O atoms from E groups, three N and three O atoms from pza− ligands and one terminal water molecule; for Pr10: two O atoms from E groups, two N and three O atoms from pza− ligands and two terminal water molecules; for Pr11: three O atoms from E groups, two N and O atoms from pza− ligands and two terminal water molecules. Here, the pza− ligands display two different coordination modes: μ3-η1:η2:η1 and μ2-η1:η1:η1 (Fig. S3†). Six pza− ligands with the μ3-η1:η2:η1 coordination mode act as bridges between the Pr6-ring and the attached Pr3+ ions, and the remaining one with the μ2-η1:η1:η1 coordination mode links adjacent [Pr11Ge12] clusters. The remaining two Pr3+ ions are occupied in ten-coordinated monocapped triangular cupola geometries by six μ3-O atoms, one N atom, and one O atom from a pza− ligand and two terminal water molecules for Pr7, and six μ3-O and four terminal water molecules for Pr8. The Pr–O/N bond distances are in the range of 2.323(14)–2.893(15) Å, and the O–Pr–O/N angles are in the range of 56.9(4)–149.7(4)°, which are comparable with those in references.29–31 For the six E2Ge2O32− ligands, every three E2Ge2O32− ligands are linked to a six-membered ring (Ge6-ring) by corner-sharing oxygen atoms (Fig. 1c–f). Each Ge6-ring is capped by the ten-coordinated Pr3+ ion, forming into a Pr-capped Ge6-ring. All the Ge atoms are in four-coordinated tetrahedral geometries by three O atoms and one C atom, and the E groups linked to both Pr-capped Ge6-rings bend towards the same direction along the equatorial plane (Fig. 1g and h). The Ge–C and Ge–O bond lengths fall in the range of 1.723(13)–1.854(13) Å and 1.882(18)–1.980(19) Å, respectively, consistent with those reported previously.32

The most striking structural feature of L1 is the presence of the 2D layered [Pr11Ge12] 4p–4f heterometallic cluster-organic framework (Fig. 2). The [Pr11Ge12] cluster is formed with two Pr-capped Ge6-rings sandwiching the gear-like [Pr9] unit. Adjacent [Pr11Ge12] clusters are connected by pza− ligands and E groups into a 2D layered cluster-organic framework with distorted square windows with the dimensions of 9.820 × 8.747 Å2 (Fig. S4†). The pza− rings and the lattice water molecules fill in the windows. The pza− ligands not only act as linkers between adjacent [Pr11Ge12] clusters but also increase the stability of the structural framework.

PXRD and thermal properties

In order to confirm the purity of compounds L1 and L2, PXRD measurements were performed. The results demonstrate that the experimental PXRD patterns of both compounds are well consistent with the simulated one based on single-crystal diffraction data, indicating that compounds L1 and L2 are pure phases (Fig. 3). In addition, the PXRD patterns of L1 were nearly unchanged after being immersed in aqueous solutions with different pH values (ranging from 3 to 13) and aqueous solutions containing different metal ions for 24 h at room temperature, which fully indicated the good stability of L1 (Fig. 3 and S5†). The thermal stabilities of L1 and L2 were studied in a dry air atmosphere from 25 to 764 °C. As shown in Fig. S6,† compounds L1 and L2 present similar TGA curves, and therefore only L1 is discussed here. L1 exhibits two steps of weight loss between 25 and 764 °C. The first weight loss of 7.10% (calcd 7.16%) between 25 and 230 °C corresponds to the loss of one hydroxyl, seven lattice and twelve coordinated water molecules. The second weight loss of 18.93% (calcd 19.64%) between 230 and 660 °C corresponds to the loss of eight pza− ligands.

Optical absorption spectra

The solid-state UV-vis absorption spectra of H2E2Ge2O3, L1, and Fe3+-treated L1 and L2 were measured. As shown in Fig. S7,† H2E2Ge2O3 exhibits an absorption band ranging from 200 to 340 nm attributed to π → π* electronic transitions of H2E2Ge2O3. The absorption spectra of L1 and L2 compared with that of H2E2Ge2O3 display red-shifts in the UV region assigned to the coordination between lanthanide ions and H2E2Ge2O3. In addition, there are several weak sharp peaks after 400 nm that are the characteristic f–f electronic transitions of Pr3+ for L1 and Nd3+ for L2, respectively.33,34 In comparison with L1, the absorbance below 350 nm of Fe3+-treated L1 decreased significantly, which may be owing to the binding interaction between the uncoordinated carboxyl oxygen atoms of the E2Ge2O32− ligand and Fe3+. This binding interaction weakened the absorption ability of light for the organic ligand. Although Fe3+ can absorb light in this region, the light absorbed by Fe3+ cannot balance the decreased absorbance by the E2Ge2O32− ligand, and owing to that, only a trace amount of Fe3+ ions exhibit binding interaction with L1 in such a liquid–solid system. In addition, the colour of Fe3+-treated L1 changed into light yellow from light green of original L1 (Fig. S8†), making Fe3+-treated L1 more easily absorb light in a longer wavelength region, thus resulting in the poor absorbance below 350 nm. UV-vis diffuse-reflectance spectra of H2E2Ge2O3, L1 and L2 were also recorded so as to determine their photophysical properties (Fig. S9†). According to the Kubelka–Munk function, the optical bandgaps of H2E2Ge2O3, L1 and L2 were calculated to be 3.47, 3.52 and 5.19 eV.10

Luminescence properties

Lanthanide-based compounds display fascinating luminescence properties such as high color purity, relatively long luminescence lifetimes and large Stokes shifts, making them especially attractive for sensing applications.35–37 Firstly, the solid-state photoluminescence of H2E2Ge2O3, L1, and L2 was investigated. When excited at 307 nm, the H2E2Ge2O3 ligand shows a strong broad peak in the range of 400–500 nm with the maximum centered at around 432 nm and a weak peak at about 543 nm (Fig. S10†), which can be attributed to ligand-centered electronic transitions.38 When excited at 368 nm (Fig. S11a†), L1 exhibits a sharp peak at 420 nm, assigned to the ligand-centered emission, and another peak at 459 nm, corresponding to the 3P2 → 3H4 transition of Pr3+ ions (Fig. 4a).39,40 The observed blue-shift of L1 at 420 nm as compared to that of H2E2Ge2O3 (432 nm) can be ascribed to the energy transfer from the H2E2Ge2O3 ligand to Pr3+ ions.41 As for L2, when excited at 587 nm (Fig. S11b†), it displays near-infrared emission with three peaks at 899, 1063 and 1347 nm, corresponding to the 4F3/2 → 4I9/2, 4F3/2 → 4I11/2 and 4F3/2 → 4I13/2 transitions of the Nd3+ ions (Fig. 4b), respectively, which agrees with that of Nd-containing compounds.42,43 The peak at 1063 nm is the most intense emission.

Detection of Fe3+

Considering the good luminescence properties of the as-synthesized heterometallic cluster-organic frameworks, herein we investigated the fluorescence sensing properties of L1 and L2 for heavy metal ions, which significantly endanger the environment and human health.44–49 For the detailed processes, please see the Experimental section. As shown in Fig. 5a and S12,† when excited at 368 nm, the luminescence intensities of L1 monitored at 420 nm exhibit negligible changes when Hg2+ and Cr3+ are involved, and Bi3+, Co2+, Zn2+, Mn2+, Ni2+, Cd2+, Pb2+, and Cu2+ cause the enhancement of the luminescence of L1 to different degrees, which may be ascribed to the binding interaction between these ions and the E2Ge2O32− ligand.50 To confirm this hypothesis, taking L1 isolated from Bi3+ solution as the representative, XPS was performed (Fig. S13†). The Bi 4f5/2 and Bi 4f7/2 peaks of Bi3+ were located at 164.21 and 158.93 eV, respectively, demonstrating the presence of Bi3+ in Bi3+-treated L1.51 The O 1s peak of L1 at 531.25 eV shifted to 531.15 eV after dispersing in the Bi3+ solution,52 demonstrating the binding interaction between Bi3+ and the carboxyl oxygen atoms of the E2Ge2O32− ligands in Bi3+-treated L1. Meanwhile, Fe3+ can largely quench the luminescence of L1 with a quenching percentage of 74%, which indicates that L1 can act as a promising chemical sensor of Fe3+ in water. To precisely evaluate the emission intensities, the emission quantum yields of L1 dispersed in aqueous solution containing different metal ions were measured at the excitation wavelength of 368 nm, and the data are listed in Table 1. Clearly, the variation trend of the quantum yields of different analytes is similar to that of the emission. As for L2, it shows no fluorescence sensing property for heavy metal ions, although it is isostructural to L1. No emission signals were detected when L2 was dispersed in aqueous solutions containing different metal ions. This phenomenon may be ascribed to the weak interaction between L2 and solvent water molecules quenching the near-infrared luminescence of Nd3+ completely. Previous reports have proved that the near-infrared Ln3+ (such as Nd3+ and Y3+) emission can be quenched efficiently by the harmonics of high energy O–H vibrations of water molecules.43,53

Generally, various metal ions may coexist in water. To investigate the influence of other metal ions on the detection of Fe3+, L1 was immersed in aqueous solutions with the coexistence of Fe3+ and another heavy metal ion. The measurements of the luminescence intensities at 420 nm display that the quenching effect of Fe3+ is not changed after introducing other metal ions (Fig. 5b). This result demonstrates that L1 can selectively sense Fe3+ among the above-mentioned heavy metal ions.

In order to explore the sensitivity toward Fe3+, time-dependent luminescence was measured for L1 to sense Fe3+ by immersing L1 in Fe(NO3)3 aqueous solution for different times. Fig. 6a shows that the luminescence intensity at 420 nm for L1 decreased nearly by half within 5 min of interaction time, which clearly reveals that L1 can detect Fe3+ fast. Then, the fluorescence titration experiment was conducted. The luminescence quenching efficiency of Fe3+ on L1 can be quantitatively acquired using the Stern–Volmer equation at low concentrations, I0/I = Ksv[M] + 1,54 where I0 and I are the luminescence intensities at 420 nm of L1 in the absence and presence of Fe3+, respectively; Ksv is the quenching constant; [M] is the molar concentration of Fe3+. As shown in Fig. 6b, with increasing concentration of Fe3+, the luminescence gradually decreased and a good linear relationship between the emission intensity of L1 at 420 nm and the concentration of Fe3+ was observed. The Ksv value is 1.31 × 103 M−1, indicating the strong quenching of Fe3+ on the emission of L1. The detection limit of L1 towards Fe3+ was calculated to be 17.84 μM according to the equation: detection limit = 3Sb/S (Sb: standard deviation from six blank measurements; S: slope),55 indicating the high sensing sensitivity of L1 towards Fe3+ (Table S2†). Based on the above-mentioned results, a conclusion can be deduced that L1 can selectively and sensitively detect Fe3+ in aqueous solution.

Furthermore, the tentative fluorescence quenching mechanism of L1 on Fe3+ was investigated. As shown in Fig. S5,† the obtained PXRD patterns of L1 before and after being dispersed in the Fe3+ solution are nearly identical, indicating that the framework of L1 is well maintained after being immersed in Fe3+ solution. Therefore, the luminescence quenching is not caused by the collapse of the framework. The UV-vis absorption spectra of the aqueous solutions containing different metal ions were monitored (Fig. S14†). Clearly, no absorption bands were observed for the other metal ions, while the Fe3+ solution has an obvious absorption band below 500 nm. It should be noted that the UV-vis absorption spectrum below 400 nm of the Fe3+ aqueous solution has a good overlap with the excitation spectrum of L1 in water suspension. Thus, there is strong competitive adsorption of light energy between Fe3+ and L1, which is mainly responsible for the fluorescence quenching of L1.54 Also, an overlap between the UV-vis absorption spectrum of the Fe3+ aqueous solution and the emission spectrum of L1 in water suspension above 400 nm was observed, which suggests that there is resonance energy transfer from L1 to Fe3+, contributing some to the fluorescence quenching.56

In addition to the absorption competition and the resonance energy transfer, the weak binding interaction between Fe3+ and the uncoordinated carboxyl oxygen atoms in L1 for the fluorescence quenching cannot be excluded. In order to check the binding interaction between Fe3+ and L1, the FT-IR spectra of L1 before and after being treated with the Fe3+ aqueous solution were collected. As shown in Fig. S1,† the FT-IR spectra of L1 before and after being treated with the Fe3+ aqueous solution were similar. However, the strong shoulder absorption bands of the antisymmetric stretching of –COO− groups in the range of 1570–1630 cm−1 were transformed into a main peak at 1579 cm−1 for Fe3+-treated L1, which may be ascribed to the weak binding interaction between the Fe3+ ions and the uncoordinated carboxyl oxygen atoms in L1. The binding interaction between Fe3+ and L1 will definitely consume the adsorbed energy, thus leading to the fluorescence quenching of L1. Meanwhile, the UV-vis absorption intensity before 435 nm of the Fe3+ aqueous solution is enhanced after the addition of L1, confirming that there is absorption of Fe3+ by L1 (Fig. S15†). This absorption is ascribed to the binding adsorption of Fe3+ onto the layer surface of L1.56 Moreover, the luminescence lifetime of L1 was slightly shortened after sensing Fe3+ (L1 in water: τ1 = 1.07, τ2 = 8.74, A1 = 4481.72, A2 = 585.66, τ = 5.03 μs; L1 in Fe3+ solution: τ1 = 1.01, τ2 = 8.37, A1 = 4367.12, A2 = 611.27, τ = 4.96 μs), also confirming the existence of weak binding interaction between Fe3+ and L1 (Fig. S16†).54 The above-mentioned results are consistent with the conclusion from the XPS analysis. Fig. S17a† clearly displays the presence of Fe3+ in L1 with the Fe 2p peaks observed at 711.54 and 725.84 eV,57 which correspond to the Fe 2p3/2 and Fe 2p1/2 of Fe3+, respectively. The O 1s peak of Fe3+-treated L1 shows a blue shift from 531.25 to 531.34 eV in comparison with that of original L1 (Fig. S17b†)58 ascribed to the weak binding of Fe3+ ions to the uncoordinated carboxyl oxygen atoms in the E2Ge2O32− ligands.

In order to further confirm the binding interaction between Fe3+ and L1, we designed an experiment: 5.00 mg ground L1 was dispersed in 4 mL deionized water (denoted L1s). A certain amount of L1s was placed into a circular tube. And the circular tube was inserted into a square quartz cell containing deionized water to collect the emission data (denoted L1s + w). Then, the deionized water in the square quartz cell was replaced with 0.001 M Fe(NO3)3 aqueous solution and the emission data were collected under the same conditions (denoted L1s + Fe3+). By contrast, L1s in the circular tube and the Fe(NO3)3 aqueous solution in the square quartz cell were mixed together to collect the emission data under the same conditions (denoted L1 + Fe3+). As shown in Fig. 7, the emission intensity of L1s + Fe3+ decreased by over 50% in comparison with that of L1s + w, which was resulted from the absorption of the light energy and the emission of Fe(NO3)3. However, the emission intensity of L1 + Fe3+ was quenched to ca. 20% of L1s + w. The higher quenched intensity compared with that of L1s + Fe3+ can be ascribed to the weak binding interaction between Fe3+ and the uncoordinated oxygen atoms in L1, further confirming the binding interaction between Fe3+ and L1. Based on the above results, the competitive absorption between L1 and Fe3+, the energy transfer from L1 to Fe3+, and the binding interaction between Fe3+ and the uncoordinated carboxyl oxygen atoms in L1 together contribute to the luminescence quenching effect of L1 in Fe3+aqueous solution.

Conclusions

In summary, two novel 4p–4f [Ln11Ge12] (Ln = Pr, L1; Nd, L2) heterometallic cluster-organic frameworks were successfully synthesized under mild hydrothermal conditions. Both compounds possess 2D layered structures. The study of solid-state luminescence indicates that L1 shows intense ligand-centered luminescence in the visible region, while L2 reveals characterized near-infrared emission of Nd3+. More importantly, L1 with a robust structure is capable of selectively and sensitively detecting Fe3+ in aqueous solution.
