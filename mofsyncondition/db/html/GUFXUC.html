Introduction

In recent years a new class of microporous materials known as metal–organic frameworks has attracted more and more not only fundamental, but also industrial, attention.1–3 Their encapsulation inside the porous frameworks' hydrophobic cavities is primarily based on optimal filling of the cavity volume, rather than on specific interactions. Because “nature abhors a vacuum and interpenetration reduces voids”, it is believed that enhanced porosity and high internal surface area are counter-intuitive to the presence of interpenetrating lattices.4 These compounds consist of rigid multidentate organic ligands (linker) and metal atoms or metal clusters (secondary building units).1 These molecular containers, also called capsules or flasks, present a number of attractive features: (i) they can be easily assembled in one step by combining relatively simple components via reversible supramolecular interactions; (ii) they contain confined chemical environments that can serve as platforms for host–guest chemistry, molecular recognition, sensing, or catalysis; and (iii) they are often aesthetically appealing. The networks can reach high surface areas and pore volumes.4–7 Due to the high porosity, the tunable pore size, and functionality, porous frameworks are predestinated for applications such as gas storage,8–12 separation,13–15 catalysis,16–18 and drug delivery.19,20 To assemble both inorganic and organic components into metal–organic frameworks, also known as coordination polymers or coordination networks, the new type of organic–inorganic hybrid materials is certainly very promising as a multifunctional luminescent material, because both the inorganic and the organic moieties can provide the platforms to generate luminescence, while metal–ligand charge transfer related luminescence can add another dimensional luminescent functionalities.21 Furthermore, some guest molecules or anions can also emit and/or induce luminescence. There is an increasing trend in the exploration and discovery of functional luminescent metal–organic frameworks.22

In addition, these porous networks may contain potential or strain energy and undergo changes in structure and properties with the release of that energy in response to a specific stimulus.23,24 Appropriate external stimuli can perturb the coordination sites of the network and its connectivity by inducing the breaking and making of bonds.25–27 Such a system offers us an experimentally unexplored field to study and expand the intermediate subtle region between robustness and flexibility through dynamic single-crystal-to-single-crystal (SC–SC) and/or solid transformations, which involve cooperative movements of atoms in the solid state, have received considerable attention in recent years.28 The SC–SC transformation is extremely useful and desirable, although it occurs relatively rarely, because it allows unequivocal assignment of the product obtained.29 So far, most of the reports regarding SC–SC transformation described the breathing of 3D porous materials through solvent exchange (Scheme 1).30

Generally, the preparation of such materials can be influenced by many factors, such as the nature of organic ligands, the crystallization conditions, metal–ligand ratio, reaction solvent system and so on. Particularly, the organic ligands play important roles in the construction of metal–organic frameworks with distinctive structures. It has been well-known that five-membered heterocycles, such as imidazole, triazole, pyrazole and tetrazole, are good candidate ligands in the construction of functional metal–organic frameworks. Among them, 1,2,4-triazole, especially its derivatives, gains more interest as ligands to bridge different metal ions to form functional coordination polymers because of their potential bridging fashions.31 We are also interested in exploiting the coordination chemistry and application of 1,2,4-triazole and its derivatives.32 To further explore this attractive topic, in this work the new multi-dentate ligand 1-(4-aminobenzyl)-1,2,4-triazole (abtz) is also have been designed and synthesized based on below points: (1) to the best of our knowledge, abtz is never reported in the coordination chemistry, the development of new ligand systems is continuously an important aspect for the chemistry of metal–organic coordination polymer; (2) recently a micro-porous cluster-based framework [Cd3(μ3-L1)2(μ2-L1)2(μ2-Cl)2Cl4]n (L1 = 4-(4-amino benzene)-1,2,4-triazole) been reported by us.32h Similarly, abtz is also a multi-functional ligand containing one triazole group and one amino group on the aromatic benzene rings, which can be expected to build many novel micro-porous frameworks; (3) Abtz contains conjugated aromatic benzene rings, it can be expected that these various d10 metal ions such as CdII and AgI frameworks can generate good photo-luminescent properties. As shown in Schemes 2 and S2,† abtz ligand has been connected to form series of 2D triazole-functionalized metal–organic frameworks {[Cd(abtz)2(H2O)2]·(ClO4)2·H2O}n (1), {[Cd(abtz)2(H2O)2]·(SiF6)}n (2), {[Cd(abtz)2 (H2O)2]·(ClO4)1.64·(HSO4)0.36·1.36H2O}n (3), {[Cd(abtz)2(NCS)]·(ClO4)}n (4) and {[Ag2(μ2-abtz)(μ3-abtz) (ClO4)]·(ClO4)}n (5). They have been characterized by single-crystal X-ray diffraction, elemental analysis and photo-luminescent studies. The solid state luminescent properties and PXRD patterns of 1–5 have been investigated. Additionally, luminescent measurements illustrate that complex 4 shows highly sensitive response to acetone and Fe3+. Besides, 5 is the first report of multidimensional polymers based on triazole derivatives as luminescent response to small biological molecules such as vitamin C in the water solutions.

Experimental section

General methods

Ligand 1-(4-aminobenzyl)-1,2,4-triazole (abtz) were prepared by literature methods.33 All the other reagents were purchased commercially and used without further purification. Deionized water was used as solvent in this work. C, H, and N microanalyses were carried out with a Perkin-Elmer 240 elemental analyzer. Powder X-ray diffraction analysis has been determined on a D/Max-2500 X-ray diffractometer using Cu-Kα radiation. FT-IR spectra were recorded from KBr pellets in the range 4000–400 cm−1 on a Bio-Rad FTS 135 spectrometer. The solid-state photoluminescence spectra were measured by MPF-4 fluorescence spectrophotometer with a xenon arc lamp as the light source. The luminescent sensing measurements were performed on an Cary Eclipse Fluorescence spectrophotometer (Agilent Technologies) equipped with a plotter unit and a quartz cell (1 cm × 1 cm) in the phosphorescence mode. The fluorescent emission spectra were recorded in the wavelength range of 280–480 nm upon excitation at 277 nm. The slit width of excitation and emission was 10 and 5 nm, respectively. The photomultiplier tube (PMT) voltage was set at 650 V.

X-ray crystallography

Diffraction intensities for complexes 1–5 were collected on a Bruker SMART 1000 CCD diffractometer with graphite-monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) by using the ω–φ scan technique. Lorentz polarization and absorption corrections were applied. The structures were solved by direct methods and refined with the full-matrix least-squares technique using the SHELXS-97 and SHELXL-97 programs.34,35 Anisotropic thermal parameters were assigned to all non-hydrogen atoms. The organic hydrogen atoms were generated geometrically; the hydrogen atoms of the water molecules were located from difference maps and refined with isotropic temperature factors. Analytical expressions of neutral-atom scattering factors were employed, and anomalous dispersion corrections were incorporated. Results and discussion

As is listed in Table 2 and Scheme S2,† it is also noted that different dihedral angles between triazole rings and aromatic benzene rings of abtz in 1–5 can be observed, for example, dihedral angles between triazole rings and aromatic benzene rings of abtz are 71.9° and 75.8° in 1, these dihedral angles are 70.7° and 83.0° in 4, while these dihedral angles are 86.1° and 86.3° in 5. The large flexible dihedral angles also indicate strong spatial distortion effect. On the other hand, the abtz ligand can adopt two different bridging coordination modes (bidentate and tri-dentate, as listed in Scheme S1†). Especially two bridging coordination modes of abtz simultaneously exist in compound 5. Therefore flexible dihedral angles and different bridging coordination modes indicate that abtz has great potential in the construction of these highly-dimensional micro-porous coordination frameworks.

The structure of {[Cd(abtz)2(H2O)2]·(ClO4)2·H2O}n (1), as shown in Fig. 1 and 2, exhibits a two-dimensional (2D) framework structure. In complex 1, there are one crystallographically independent CdII ion (Cd1), two abtz ligands, two terminal-coordinated aqua molecules, two free perchlorate anions and one lattice solvent water molecule (Fig. 1). The coordination geometry of each cadmium site is a slight distorted octahedral {CdN4O2} site, with the equatorial plane defined by three bridged triazole ligands (N3, N7 and N8A) and one coordination water molecule O6, and the axial sites occupied by one bridged triazole ligand nitrogen atom N4 and another coordination water molecule O5. The bond lengths associated with central CdII ion in 1 (varying from 2.296(3) Å to 2.395(3) Å) can be compared with previously reported bond distances.36

1 contains a 2D infinite network. These bridging abtz ligands link these neighboring CdII ions, on the other hand, each central CdII ion link four neighboring CdII ions via these bridging abtz ligands, ultimately forming a new two-dimensional (2D) rectangular-grid metal–organic framework with the dimensionality of 11.700(8) Å × 10.916(1) Å (Fig. 2). All CdII ions in each layer are strictly coplanar (Fig. S1†), and Cd⋯Cd separation along Cd⋯abtz⋯Cd is 11.700(8) Å and 10.916(1) Å, respectively. The corresponding dihedral angles between benzene and triazole moieties in abtz are 71.9° and 75.8°, respectively, indicating strong spatial distortion effect. The neighboring 2D layers are densely stacked in an offset ABCABC⋯ stacking way. The interlayer distances between two neighboring layers are 8.216(3) Å and 9.122(5) Å, respectively. These guest perchlorate anions are embedded between the adjacent 2D layers. Side view of these 2D layers two kinds of different directional perchlorate anions can be observed, as shown in Fig. S2.† No obvious π–π interactions in the structures of 1 are observed (the shortest separated interplanar center-to-center distances between aromatic rings are 3.977(8) Å). However coordinated water molecules (O5), free perchlorate anions and the nitrogen atoms of triazole groups are also involved in O–H⋯O and O–H⋯N hydrogen bonds with relatively linear geometry (O–H⋯O and O–H⋯N angles range between 160° and 178°), which also extend the 2D framework of 1 into the 3D hydrogen bonding framework. Such weak interactions also help to encapsulate these free guest perchlorate anions in the cavities of the 2D porous framework of 1, which play a crucial role in stabilizing the whole network.37 To the best of our knowledge, such arrangement of a 2D framework trapping guest perchlorate anions is still scarcely reported in the published literature.

When CdSiF6 is used to replace Cd(ClO4)2 in the reaction system, the crystalline product {[Cd(L)2(H2O)2]·(SiF6)}n2 can be obtained, X-ray analysis of 2 reveals a 2D framework with the same (4,4) net topology as the structure of 1. The SiF62− anions occupy the positions of ClO4− anions of 1 in the crystal lattice. As shown in Fig. 3, each abtz ligand links two Cd(ii) ions by its triazole nitrogen atoms and amion nitrogen atoms acting as a bridging bidentate ligand. Each Cd(ii) ion is linked by four abtz ligands with four neighboring Cd(ii) ions to form a 2D rhombohedral grid similar to that of 1. The 2D cation grid motif has the dimensionalities of 12.149(1) Å × 12.149(1) Å (metal–metal distances). The free SiF62− anions are located within these neighboring 2D layers. No obvious π–π interactions in the structures of 2 are observed (the shortest separated interplanar center-to-center distances between aromatic rings are 3.857(6) Å). However these coordinated water molecules O1, O2 and O5 and free SiF62− anions are also involved in O–H⋯F hydrogen bonds with relatively linear geometry (hydrogen angles range between 164° and 174°), which also extend and stabilize the 2D micro-porous framework of 2.

SC–SC structural transformations, which involve cooperative movements of atoms in the solid state, have received considerable attention in recent years.38 The SC–SC transformation is extremely useful and desirable, although it occurs relatively rarely, because it allows unequivocal assignment of the product obtained.39 Usually, through the SC–SC transformation, new complexes, which cannot be obtained under conventional conditions, can be gained in a high yield.40 Materials that show reversible structure transformation and a characteristic response toward specific external stimuli such as light,41 temperature,42 and guest molecules43 are vitally important for applications in sensing, molecular capture, switches, and so on.44 As an important property of coordination polymers, anion exchange has attracted increasing attention in recent years because it makes such polymers potentially attractive as anion exchange materials. The anions trapped in the cavity, which is weakly H-bonded, can be exchanged with different anions. From the reported studies, neutral N-donor ligands have been found to be most useful building blocks for the construction of coordination polymers that are able to show anion exchange properties. In most of the reported cases, anion exchange was proven by IR spectroscopy and PXRD patterns, as crystallinity is not maintained in such cases. However, it should be noted that to absolutely confirm a SC–SC anion exchange process, microscopic studies of crystal morphology is important. Recently, we have obtained a 2D crystalline coordination framework {[Cu(tatrz)2 (NO3)2]·(CH3OH)⋯4H2O}n (tatrz = 1-(9-(1H-1,2,4-triazol-1-yl)anthracen-10-yl)-1H-1,2,4-triazole), in which anion exchange experiment indicate NO3− anions can be completely exchanged by ClO4− anions, as evidenced by the anion-exchanged crystalline product.32g Taking inspiration from the aforementioned points, in this work, we investigated the guest anion loaded host–guest complex in a SC–SC fashion. A suspension mixture of 1 (0.3565 g, 0.5 mmol) and NaHSO4⋯H2O (0.7023 g, 5 mmol) in water–methanol (10 mL, v/v = 1 : 1) was stirred for 6 h. The crystals can retain their crystalline appearances. Well-shaped colorless block crystals suitable for X-ray diffraction analysis were isolated. These free ClO4− anions of 1 could be slowly and partly exchanged by HSO4− anions. The anion exchanged product is designated as {[Cd(abtz)2(H2O)2]⋯(ClO4)1.64⋯(HSO4)0.36⋯1.36H2O}n (3). X-ray analysis of 3 reveals the same (4,4) net topology as the parent structure of 1. The HSO4− anions occupy the positions of the displaced ClO4− anions in the crystal lattice. Elemental analysis (for {[Cd(abtz)2(H2O)2]⋯(ClO4)1.64⋯(HSO4)0.36⋯1.36H2O}n (3): C, 30.43; H, 3.61; N, 15.81%) also indicates that ca. 36% of ClO4− in 1 has been exchanged by HSO4−. However complete anion replacement in the solid state is not observed. As mentioned in the 2D framework structure of 1, two kinds of different directional perchlorate anions can be alternatively observed between two neighboring layers. As shown in Fig. 5, within one layer these free ClO4− anions can be exchanged by HSO4− anions, while the other layer these free ClO4− anions are not exchanged, which may have stronger interactions with neighboring layers and be hardly exchanged by other anions. Similar example can be observed in the previously reported porous Ag(i) compound [Ag(BIPS)]0.5 [Ag(BIPS)]2·2.5ClO4·5H2O (BIPS = bis(4-(imidazol-1-yl)phenyl)dimethylsilane).45 The intercalated HSO4− can be reversibly exchanged by soaking {[Cd(abtz)2(H2O)2]⋯(ClO4)1.64⋯(HSO4)0.36⋯1.36H2O}n (3) into solutions containing excessive ClO4−. As shown in Fig. 4 and 5, These HSO4− anions and ClO4− anions are alternatively located within these neighboring 2D layers.

For the 2D framework of 3, as shown in Fig. 4, each abtz ligand links two Cd(ii) ions by its triazole nitrogen atoms and amino nitrogen atoms, which acts as a bridging bidentate ligand. Each Cd(ii) ion is linked by four abtz ligands with four neighboring Cd(ii) ions to form a 2D rhombohedral grid similar to that of 1. The 2D cation grid motif has the dimensionalities of 10.969(1) Å × 11.754(8) Å (metal–metal distances). (Fig. 4). These free HSO4− anions and ClO4− anions are alternatively located within these neighboring 2D layers. Side view of these 2D layers, the neighboring 2D layers are also densely stacked in an offset ABCABC⋯ stacking way. The interlayer distances are 8.088(3) Å and 8.211(3) Å, which is more densely stacked from that the stacking structure of 1. This should be ascribed to similar 2D frameworks containing different guest anions between 1 and 3. These coordinated water molecules O1, O2 and O5 and free anions are also involved in O–H⋯N and N–H⋯O hydrogen bonds with relatively linear geometry (hydrogen angles range between 166° and 175°), which also extend and stabilize the 2D micro-porous framework of 3. Such hydrogen bonding roles are key and important for stabling the framework.

On the other hand, we also try to use other small anions for ion exchange reaction such as NaN3 or NaNCS, however the framework cannot remain intact, which may be correlated with the strong coordination abilities of N3− or NCS− anions. However, interestingly when 1 is as a precursor to react with NH4NCS under hydrothermal conditions, the 2D bilayer framework {[Cd(abtz)2(NCS)]·(ClO4)}n (4) can be isolated. As shown in Fig. 6, single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis results reveal that the fundamental unit consists of one independent Cadmium(ii) center, two abtz ligands, one bridging NCS− ligand and one free perchlorate anion. Cd1 shows a distorted octahedral coordination, ligating to four Nabtz atoms and one nitrogen atom N9A, one sulfur atom S1 from two thiocyanate ligands. As shown in Fig. 7(b), each Cd(ii) center is connected by four abtz ligands and each abtz ligand is coordinated to two Cd(ii) centers, leading to a 2D rhombohedral [Cd(abtz)2] grid similar to that of 1. It is interesting that these μ2-bridging thiocyanate ligands further link neighboring [Cd(abtz)2] sheets to form a novel bi-layer framework of 4 (Fig. 7(a) and (c)). This also presents a novel example of structural transformation with bond rupture/formation from a mono-layer framework 1 to a 2D bi-layer framework 4. Such arrangement of a 2D bilayer framework containing 2D [Cd(abtz)2] sheets is still scarcely reported and it is also indicated that auxiliary thiocyanate ligand has strong coordination ability to construct more novel metal–organic frameworks. No obvious π–π interactions in the structures of 4 are observed (the shortest separated interplanar center-to-center distances between aromatic rings are 3.994(5) Å). However these nitrogen atoms of triazole groups and free perchlorate anions are also involved in N–H⋯O hydrogen bonds with relatively linear geometry (N–H⋯O angles range between 160° and 170°), which also extend the 2D framework of 4 into the 3D hydrogen bonding framework.

Crystallization of abtz with AgClO4 in the mixed methanol–water solvent system afforded the infinite two-dimensional sheet structure (5) under hydrothermal conditions of 160 °C. The metal-to-ligand ratio is 1 : 1 in the reaction system. Single-crystal X-ray analysis revealed (as shown in Fig. 8) that there are three different Ag(i) centers (Ag1, Ag2 and Ag3) in 5. The first Ag(i) center Ag1 lies in a trigonal planar coordination environment, which consists of two amino nitrogen donors (Ag(1)–N(4) 2.247(3) Å and Ag(1)–N(8) 2.270(3) Å) from two abtz ligands, one triazole nitrogen donor (Ag(1)–N(6) 2.426(3) Å) from the third abtz ligand and one oxygen O1 atom of perchlorate anion (Ag(1)–O(1) 2.620(7) Å), respectively. The second Ag(i) center Ag2 has linear coordination with two triazole nitrogen atoms from two abtz ligands (N(3)-Ag(2)–N(3A) 180.00(11)° and Ag(2)–N(3) 2.144(3) Å). The third Ag(i) center Ag3 also has linear coordination with two triazole nitrogen atoms from two abtz ligands (N(7)–Ag(3)–N(7A) 179.99(11)°, Ag(3)–N(7) 2.153(3) Å). The Ag–Namino bond length is considerably longer than those of the Ag–Ntriazole bonds by 0.156–0.282 Å, but all the Ag–N bond distances found in 5 are within the normal range observed in N-containing heterocyclic Ag(i) complexes. For one neutral bridging μ2-abtz ligand, one nitrogen atom from one triazole group and one amino nitrogen atom are involved in the coordination and adopt the bi-dentate bridging coordination modes (mode (a) in Scheme S1†). For the other neutral bridging μ3-abtz ligand, two nitrogen atoms from two triazole groups and one amino nitrogen atom are involved in the coordination and adopt the tridentate bridging coordination modes (mode (b) in Scheme S1†). It is noted that dihedral angles between aromatic benzene and triazole rings in the μ2-abtz and μ3-abtz ligands are 86.1° and 86.3°, respectively, indicating large spatial distortion effect.

The Ag⋯Ag separations are 6.849(4) Å and 7.032(4) Å, which are much longer than the sum of the van der Waals radii of two Ag atoms (3.44(0) Å). In the solid state, Ag(1), Ag(2) and Ag(3) centers are linked via these μ2-bridging and μ3-bridging abtz ligands together into a novel two-dimensional sheet, which is parallel to the crystallographic ac plane. As shown in Fig. 9, a single 2D net consists of two different individual rings. The large one comprises a hexmeric unit, in which six Ag(i) centers are linked together by abtz ligands through both amino groups and triazole N-donors into an elliptical 48-membered macrocycle. The approximate crystallographic dimensionality of the ring is 20.698(11) × 9.613(1) Å, in which the perchlorate anions are not coordinated to central AgI center. The small one consists of a 32-membered tetrameric unit. The effective cross-section is 6.469(4) × 7.032(4) Å, in which perchlorate anions are weakly coordinated to central AgI center. These two-dimensional nets are not planar but undulating, which results from the crooked conformation of the ligand (Fig. 9). All the two-dimensional macrocycle-containing nets stack together along the crystallographic ac plane. As shown in Fig. 10, if these Ag(i) ions can be considered as basic nodes and these bridging abtz ligands are considered as linkers, the overall 2D framework of 5 can be seen as the unique (2,4,5) connected network, to the best of our knowledge, such (2,4,5) connected topology is still quite scarcely reported in these metal–triazole coordination frameworks. Weak coordinated ClO4− counterions and free ClO4− counterions are located between the interlayer spaces. A face-to-face alignment between the counter triazole moieties and aromatic benzene rings with the separated interplanar center-to-center distance of 3.515(2) Å and another edge-to-face alignment between the coordinated triazole moieties and aromatic benzene rings with the separated interplanar atom-to-center shortest distance of 3.706(2) Å exhibit two different π–π stacking interactions. Furthermore, amino nitrogen atoms are hydrogen bonded to perchlorate oxygen atoms (N4–H4A⋯O8: 2.986(4) Å; N4–H4B⋯O7: 3.219(4) Å; N8–H8A⋯O3: 3.083(5) Å), which also extend and stabilize the 2D microporous framework of 5.

FT-IR spectra display characteristic absorption bands for water molecules, benzene and triazole moieties of abtz. Compounds 1–3 also shows medium and broad peaks around 3400 cm−1, which indicate the present of coordination and lattice guest water molecules. For all compounds 1–5, the vibration bands around 800 cm−1 can be ascribed to the presence of aromatic benzene rings. The bands around ca. 3100 cm−1 and bands in the 1100–1300 cm−1 range can be related to ν(C–H) and ν(C–N) or ν(N–N) of triazole moieties. Aromatic organic molecules and inorganic–organic hybrid coordination complexes were investigated for their photoluminescent properties and for potential applications as luminescent materials, such as light-emitting diodes (LEDs).46 Owing to the ability of adjusting the emission strength and wavelength of organic materials, the construction of inorganic–organic coordination complexes through the judicious incorporation of transition metal centers and conjugated organic spacers can be an efficient strategy for synthesizing new kinds of photoluminescent materials. As we know, a double bond is constituted by σ and π bonds, and the emissions of the organic ligands are usually ascribed to the π* → n or π → π* transitions.47 It is then expected that the double bond-based abtz ligand would exhibit photoluminescence property. Previous research also confirms that coordination polymers have the ability to affect the emission strength and wavelength of organic materials through the judicious incorporation of different central metal ions.48–50 Therefore, it gives us an impetus to investigate the solid-state photo-luminescent properties of complexes 1–5.

As shown in Fig. 11, at room temperature, the free abtz ligand in the solid state is luminescent and shows the broad emission maximum at 361 nm (λex = 280 nm). For the abtz ligand the chromospheres are the aromatic benzene and triazole rings, the observed emission is due to the π–π* transition. In comparison with that of free abtz ligand, upon excitation of solid samples of 4 at 280 nm, the 2D bilayer framework 4 shows similar emission bands with maximum at ca. 363 nm. The emissions should be assigned to intraligand π–π* transitions. Compared with free abtz ligand, the slight red-shift and enhancement of luminescent band in 4 is probably due to the unique coordination of abtz to central CdII ions increasing the ligand conformational rigidity, thereby reducing the non-radiative decay of the intraligand (π–π*) excited state.

For these other 2D frameworks 1, 2, 3 and 5, it is noted that large red-shift of luminescent emissions can be observed (emission bands with maximum at 520 nm for 1 (λex = 360 nm), 501 nm for 2 (λex = 360 nm), 520 nm for 3 (λex = 360 nm) and 550 nm for 5 (λex = 360 nm). Because no emission bands for abtz ligand can be observed in the region of 500–600 cm−1, the photoluminescence origin of the emission bands for these 2D coordination polymers should be attributed to metal-to-ligand charge transfer (MLCT) as discussed in the previous literature.51 Because 1, 2 and 3 contain similar 2D structural motifs, different band shapes in the luminescent emissions of 1, 2 and 3 can be ascribed to different guest ClO4−/SiF6−/HSO4− anions, which can greatly affect luminescence emission bands. On the other hand, 5 possesses larger red-shifted luminescent emissions than that of 1–3, which may be correlated that hexmeric and tetrameric {Ag-(abtz)2-Ag} units are embedded in the 2D framework of 5. These hexmeric and tetrameric {Ag-(abtz)2-Ag} units increase the ligand conformational rigidity, thereby reducing the non-radiative decay of the intraligand (π–π*) excited state. Previous examples also reveal that some coordination polymers with triazole derivatives are excellent candidates for potential photoactive materials.52 The syntheses of new Cd(ii)/Ag(i) complexes with triazole derivatives can be an efficient method for obtaining new types of luminescent materials.

In particular, the products of 4 as the luminescent probes were carefully explored for sensing diverse metal ions and small solvent molecules, owing to its strong visible blue light when excited by ultraviolet light. In order to study the luminescent responses of 4 to different metal ions, the products of 4 were ground into powder and suspended in the water solution containing different salt solutions of the same concentration of Fe2+, Fe3+, K+, Na+, Al3+, Mn2+, Cu2+, and Zn2+, Cs+, Ba2+. Aqueous solutions of Fe2+, Fe3+, K+, Na+, Al3+, Mn2+, Cu2+, Zn2+, Cs+ and Ba2+ were prepared from FeCl2, FeCl3·6H2O, KCl, NaCl, AlCl3·9H2O, MnCl2·4H2O, CuCl2·2H2O, ZnCl2·7H2O, CsCl and BaCl2·2H2O, respectively. Fig. 12 shows the photoluminescence emission band of the water solutions of 4 is centered at around 360 nm, which is more likely to be due to intra-ligand transitions than to ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT). The luminescence intensity of 4 decreased continuously upon adding Fe3+, while there was only a negligible effect on the luminescence intensity for other canions. The results showed that only Fe3+ gave significant fluorescence quenching effect of 4, indicating the high selectivity of 4 for the detection and specific recognition of Fe3+ in aqueous solution. Quantitatively, the luminescent quenching effect of various metal ions and the different concentrations of Fe3+ can be treated with the Stern–Volmer equation, I0/I = 1 + Ksv[Q], where Ksv is the quenching constant and [Q] is the quencher concentration (Fig. 13 and S10†). The reduction in luminescence intensity is proportional to the concentration of the Fe3+ metal ion. Therefore 4 may be chosen as a candidate for the selective sensing of Fe3+. Furthermore, this selective detection of Fe3+ has no interfering effects from other metal ions such as Fe2+, K+, Na+, Al3+, Mn2+, Cu2+, and Zn2+, Cs+, Ba2+. It is also noted that there was only a negligible effect on the luminescence intensity for Fe2+. This also reveals that MOFs 4 is a luminescent probe for discrimination and detection of Fe3+. As illustrated in the previous literature, Iron species are essential for virtually all organisms for their function as a cofactor in central cellular processes such as respiration, DNA synthesis and repair, ribosome biogenesis, metabolism, etc. Therefore in this regard, effective probing of different iron species such as Fe3+ still becomes a challenging issue, particularly with the emergence of fluorescent imaging technology.53 To our knowledge, work on these d10 triazole-based MOFs as luminescent probes for discrimination and detection of Fe3+ is still limited. To confirm the integrity of the frameworks after compound 4 suspended in the analyte solution, the PXRD (powder X-ray diffraction) of the used samples also has been measured indicating the integrity of the frameworks (Fig. S8†). The luminescent quenching results for Fe3+ indicate that the energy transfer from the organic ligands to metal ions is changed. As illustrated in the previous literature,54 Fe3+ may establish coordinative interactions with cation-π interactions with aromatic groups. The addition of Fe3+ may cause the energy transfer from conjugated aromatic benzene rings and Fe3+, which is also in consequence luminescent quenching resulting in 4.

Moreover, luminescent sensing for different solvent molecules was also investigated carefully. The products of 4 were ground into powder and suspended in different solvents such as methanol, ethanol, DEF (N,N-diethylformamide), DMA (N,N-dimethylacetamide), acetone, hexane, octane, CYH (cyclohexane), DCM (methylene chloride) for 24 h. It was found that the luminescent intensities of 4 showed that only acetone gave significant fluorescence quenching effect, indicating the high selectivity of 4 for the detection and specific recognition of acetone (Fig. 14). The luminescent intensity of 4 was completely quenched by acetone molecules compared to other solvents. Meanwhile, acetone molecules were added slowly to the suspension of 4 in the water solution to study the quantitative quenching effect of 4, showing the distinctly gradual decrease of luminescent intensities (Fig. 15). Quantitatively, the luminescent quenching effect of various solvent molecules can be treated with the Stern–Volmer equation, I0/I = 1 + Ksv[Q], where Ksv is the quenching constant and [Q] is the quencher concentration, The reduction in luminescence intensity is proportional to the concentration of acetone molecules (Fig. S11†). As illustrated in the previous literature,55 the quenching behavior of the acetone might be ascribed to the interaction between “CO” of acetone and the framework of compound 4. The interaction of the crystals and solvent plays an important role in such solvent-dependent luminescent property as in the previous reported Cd-MOF materials [Cd2(TBA)2(bipy)(DMA)2] (bipy = 4,4′-bipyridine; H2TBA = 4-(1H-tetrazol-5-yl)-benzoic acid).

On the other hand, we also investigate of the luminescent sensing properties of the 2D framework 5. Interestingly 5 shows luminescent response to small biological molecules such as vitamin C in the water solutions. The products of 5 were ground into powder and suspended in the water solution containing different salt solutions of the same concentration of biological molecules such as GLU (glutamic acid), urea, GLY (glycin), AA (vitamin C), LYS (lysine), HIS (histidine), PHE (l-phenylalanine). Fig. 16 shows the photoluminescence emission band of the water solutions of 5 is also centered at around 360 nm, which is more likely to be due to intra-ligand transitions than to ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT). The luminescence intensity of 5 decreased continuously upon adding vitamin C, while there was only a negligible effect on the luminescence intensity for other biological molecules. The results showed that only vitamin C gave significant fluorescence quenching effect of 5, indicating the high selectivity of 5 for the discrimination and detection of vitamin C in aqueous solution. Meanwhile, vitamin C molecules were added slowly to the suspension of 5 in the water solution to study the quantitative quenching effect of 5, showing the distinctly gradual decrease of luminescent intensities (Fig. 17). Quantitatively, the luminescent quenching effect of different concentrations of vitamin C can be treated with the Stern–Volmer equation, I0/I = 1 + Ksv[Q], where Ksv is the quenching constant and [Q] is the quencher concentration (Fig. S12†). The reduction in luminescence intensity is proportional to the concentration of vitamin C. Therefore 5 may be chosen as a candidate for the selective sensing of some small biological molecules such as vitamin C. Furthermore, this selective detection of vitamin C has no interfering effects from other biological molecules such as GLU (glutamic acid), urea, GLY (glycin), AA (vitamin C), LYS (lysine), HIS (histidine), PHE (l-phenylalanine). To the best of our knowledge, 5 is the first report of multidimensional polymers based on triazole derivatives as luminescent response to small biological molecules such as vitamin C in the water solutions.56 To confirm the integrity of the frameworks after compound 5 suspended in the analyte solution, the PXRD (powder X-ray diffraction) of the used samples also has been measured indicating the integrity of the frameworks (Fig. S9†). Because the photoluminescence emission band of the water solutions of 5 is also centered at around 360 nm, which should be due to intra-ligand transitions. If the added analytes could affect the energy absorbed by the organic ligands, the intra-ligand luminescence would be substantially reduced. The emitted light of the organic fluorophore may be dissipated and the intra-molecular energy transfer may be restricted in 5, which is somewhat similar to the previously reported luminescent Tb framework TbMA–EDTA (EDTA = ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid; MA = mercaptoundecanoic acid).57

In conclusion, a new flexible multi-dentate 1-(4-aminobenzyl)-1,2,4-triazole (abtz) has been designed and synthesized. With the help of abtz as building block, a series of two-dimensional microporous triazole-functionalized metal–organic frameworks, namely {[Cd(abtz)2(H2O)2]·(ClO4)2·H2O}n (1), {[Cd(abtz)2(H2O)2]·(SiF6)}n (2), {[Cd(abtz)2(H2O)2]·(ClO4)1.36·(SO4)0.36·H2O}n (3), {[Cd(abtz)2(NCS)]·(ClO4)}n (4) and {[Ag2(abtz)2(ClO4)]·(ClO4)}n (5) have been isolated. Firstly, when abtz and corresponding cadmium(ii) salts are employed, the new non-interpenetrated two-dimensional (2D) rectangular-grid coordination frameworks containing free ClO4− or SiF62− anions, {[Cd(abtz)2(H2O)2]·(ClO4)2·H2O}n (1) and{[Cd(abtz)2(H2O)2]·(SiF6)}n (2), can be isolated. In 1 weakly H-bonded ClO4− anions present within the voids can be exchanged by HSO4− anions to generate the anion exchanged crystalline product {[Cd(abtz)2(H2O)2]·(ClO4)1.64·(HSO4)0.36·1.36H2O}n (3) in single-crystal-to-single-crystal (SC–SC) manner. On the other hand, 1 is used as a precursor to react with NH4NCS to obtain a 2D bilayer framework {[Cd(abtz)2(NCS)]·(ClO4)}n (4), which shows an novel example from mono-layer to 2D bi-layer structural transformation with bond rupture/formation leading to the formation of a new 2D bi-layer coordination polymer. Further when AgClO4 is used to replace Cd(ClO4)2 in the reaction system, another interesting 2D layer framework {[Ag2(abtz)2(ClO4)]·(ClO4)}n (5) with unique (2,4,5) connected topology can be isolated. The solid state luminescent properties and PXRD patterns of 1–5 have been investigated. Additionally, luminescent measurements illustrate that complexes 4 and 5 represent the novel examples as luminescent probes of new sensing materials. 4 shows highly sensitive response to acetone and Fe3+ through luminescence quenching effects. Besides, 5 shows highly sensitive response to small biological molecules such as vitamin C in the water solutions. The result also reveals the new abtz ligand can adopt versatile coordination modes and flexible dihedral angles, which has great potential in the construction of these coordination polymers with diverse structural motifs and interesting properties. We also believe that these polymers with potential properties in electrical conductivity and magnetism will be synthesized by using other transition metals with different properties than Cd(ii) and Ag(i) (such as Cu(ii), Co(ii), Ni(ii) or Mn(ii) ions). On the basis of this work, further syntheses, structures and properties studies of abtz with other metals are also under way in our laboratory.
