Encapsulated discrete octameric water cluster, 1D water tape, and 3D water aggregate network in diverse MOFs based on bisimidazolium ligands

Abstract

Four new metal–organic frameworks, [Zn(2-mBIM)2(SO3CF3)2·(H2O)4] (1), [Zn(BMIE)(1,4-BDC)]·(H2O)3 (2), [Cd(BIM)2(OH)(H2O)2(PF6)]·(H2O)4 (3), and [Cd(PA-BIM)2 (ClO4)2]·11.33H2O (4) (2-mBIM = bis(2-methylimidazol-1-yl)methane, BMIE = 1,2-bis[1-(2-methylimidazole)-diethoxy]ethane, BIM = bis(imidazol-1-yl)methane, and PA-BIM = 1,1-bis [(2-phenylazo)imidazol-1-yl]methane) have been prepared and structurally characterized. Complex 1 exhibits an infinite 1D cationic beaded-chain structure, which encapsulated discrete octameric water clusters that are comprised of a chair-like hexameric water cluster with two extra water molecules dangling on two diagonal vertices of the chair. Complex 2 forms a 1D infinite zigzag metal–organic chain structure with a 1D T4(0)A(4) water tape. Complexes 3 show a 2D grid-like sheet structure with the 1D water tape T4(0)A(0)2(0) motif. Complex 4 is a porous 3D MOF with tetrahedron-coordinated Cd(II) centers and trans-conformation PA-BIM ligands. These holes are occupied by a fascinating three-dimensional water clathrate network, which consists of cage-shaped structural tetradecameric water cluster (H2O)14 units and six independent bridged water molecules. The results suggest that the bisimidazolium ligands and anions play crucial roles in the formation of the different host structures and different guest water aggregations. Additionally, the thermal stabilities and photoluminescence spectra of the complexes have been discussed.

Introduction

As a burgeoning field in the last two decades, metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have attracted intense scientific interest owing to the aesthetics of diverse network structures and their potential applications in areas, such as zeolite-like materials, ion exchange, electrical conductivity, catalysis, and molecule-based magnets [1]. Although the self-assembly of these architectures is influenced by environmental factors such as the solvent system, templates, pH, and counterions, it is mainly controlled by the coordination nature of the metal ions and the structure of the ligands [2]. Carboxylate ligands have often been applied for the self-assembly of porous MOFs, and many water polymers have been encapsulated within the channels and/or hollows in MOFs by hydrogen bonding interactions between the water molecules and the functional groups in the organic ligands [3].

Recently, much attention has been focused on the structural investigations of water molecules, both theoretically and experimentally, not only because of the diversity of water patterns that exists in nature, but also because of the fundamental importance of water for human life and in biological and chemical processes [4]. A variety of discrete water clusters including hexamers, octamers, and decamers have been identified, and significant progress has been made with respect to the characterization of one-dimensional (1D) water chains and tapes [5]. Two-dimensional (2D) water/ice layers containing large water rings have also been obtained [6]. However, three-dimensional (3D) water cluster networks are rarely documented in the literature [7]. In fact, the structure of a 3D water cluster should provide novel structural aspects of water and help us to understand the relationship between cluster and bulk water [8]. The complexities of the intermolecular interactions between the water and the host still present a roadblock towards the complete understanding of water. Therefore, more structural demonstrations of various water aggregations in diverse microenvironments are necessary for a better understanding of water.

In our previous work, we described a symmetrical 3D water network containing cage-shaped structural tetradecameric water cluster (H2O)14 units observed in crystalline 3D metal–organic framework materials based on the ligand PA-BIM [9]. As an extension of this work, we introduced a series of bisimidazole ligands, shown in Scheme 1, on the assembly of the MOF, along with different water aggregates. Four new MOFs encapsulating a discrete octameric water cluster, 1D T4(0)A(4) and T4(0)A(0)2(0) water tapes, and a fascinating 3D water clathrate network were prepared. Herein, we report the syntheses, crystal structures, and photoluminescence properties of four new coordination polymers, namely, [Zn(2-mBIM)2(SO3CF3)2·(H2O)4] (1), [Zn(BMIE)(1,4-BDC)]·(H2O)3 (2), [Cd(BIM)2(OH)(H2O)2(PF6)]·(H2O)4 (3), and [Cd(PA-BIM)2(ClO4)2]·11.33H2O (4) (2-mBIM = bis(2-methylimidazol-1-yl)methane, BMIE = 1,2-bis[1-(2-methylimidazole)-diethoxy]ethane, BIM = bis(imidazol-1-yl)methane, and PA-BIM = 1,1-bis[(2-phenylazo)imidazol-1-yl]methane). The results suggest that the bisimidazolium ligands and anions play crucial roles in the formation of the different host structures and different guest water aggregations.

Experimental section

Materials and measurements

All chemicals were obtained commercially available and used as received without further purification. Ligands bis(imidazol-1-yl)methane (BIM), bis(2-methyl-imidazol-1-yl)methane (2-mBIM), 1,1-bis[(2-phenylazo)imidazol-1-yl]methane (PA-BIM) and 1,2-bis[1-(2-methyl imidazole)-diethoxy]-ethane (BMIE) were prepared by literature’s methods [10]. The FT-IR spectra were recorded from KBr pellets in the range of 4500–500 cm−1 on a Nicolet 5700 infrared spectrometer. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) measurements were made using a Pyris diamond TG/DTA Thermogravimetric differential Thermal Analyzer. Samples were heated at 10 °C/min from 40 to 800 °C in a dynamic nitrogen atmosphere. Elemental analyses were performed on a Perkin–Elmer 2400 CHN elemental analyzer. The photoluminescence measurements were carried out on crystalline samples at room temperature and the spectra were collected with a Hitachi F-4500 spectrophotometer.

Synthesis of [Zn(2-mBIM)2(SO3CF3)2·(H2O)4] (1)

An ethanol solution (5 ml) of 2-mBIM (88 mg, 0.5 mmol) was slowly diffused into an aqueous solution (5 ml) of Zn(SO3CF3)2 (91 mg, 0.25 mmol) in test tube. Colorless crystals of 1 were formed at the interface of solvent in two weeks and were obtained in ca. 57% yield. Anal. An empirical absorption was applied using SADABS program [11]. All of the non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. Hydrogen atoms were located from difference Fourier maps and included as riding atoms with isotropic displacement parameters 1.2–1.5 times Ueq of the attached C atoms. The hydrogen atoms attached to oxygen were refined with O–H = 0.85 Å and Uiso(H) = 1.2Ueq(O). The crystal data and structure refinement of compound 1–4 was summarized in Table 1. Selected bond lengths and angles were listed in Table 2.

Results and discussion

Description of the crystal structures

[Zn(2-mBIM)2(SO3CF3)2·(H2O)4] (1)

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction revealed that complex 1 crystallized in the monoclinic space group P2(-1)/c. The asymmetric unit of 1 consists of one Zn(II) atom, two CF3SO3− anions, four uncoordinated water molecules, and two 2-mBIM ligands. The crystal structure of 1 together with the atom numbering scheme is shown in Fig. 1a. Each Zn(II) atom was tetrahedrally coordinated by four nitrogen atoms of the imidazolyl rings from four individual 2-mBIM ligands (N1 and N5, N4 and N8). The Zn−N bond lengths were 1.990(7), 1.992(6), 1.998(6), and 1.989(7) Å, respectively. The tetrahedral geometry around the Zn(II) was distorted, with the N−Zn−N angles being in the range of 104.8(3)–112.6(3)°, deviating significantly from the ideal values of 90°. Two independent 2-mBIM ligands were coordinated with each Zn atom center through a trans-configuration with dihedral angles between the two imidazole rings in the same 2-mBIM ligand of 84.7° and 86.1°. Two 2-mBIM ligands bridged two different Zn atoms to form a 16-membered macrometallacyclic tetragonal box structure with a Zn–Zn separation of 8.008 Å. Each 2-mBIM ligand also coordinated to two coordinated Zn(II) cations, acting as a bridging ligand to form an infinite 1D cationic beaded-chain structure (Fig. 1b). The two neighboring macrometallacycles in the polymeric chain were perpendicular to each other because of the tetrahedron coordination mode of the Zn center.

The most striking feature of 1 is the existence of a discrete octameric water cluster. As shown in Fig. 1c, the centrosymmetric, octameric water cluster consists of four water molecules (O1w–O4w) and their centrosymmetric equivalents. The short contacts and the reasonable angles between them indicate the existence of hydrogen bonds, which drive the formation of the hydrophilic octameric water cluster. The water octamer can be seen as a chair-like hexamer in the core, linking two extra water molecules dangling at two diagonal vertices of the chair with a distance of 10.265 Å, which is structurally analogous to the simple hydrocarbon 1,4-dimethylcyclohexane. In the cyclic hexamer, each water molecule acts as a single hydrogen bond donor and a single hydrogen bond acceptor. The O−O distance in the octameric water cluster falls in the range of 2.661–2.804 Å with an average value of 2.747 Å, compared to 2.759 Å (−90 °C) in hexagonal (Ih) ice [13] and 2.741 Å in cubic (Ic) ice [14]. The O−O−O angles vary from 99.7° to 125.1° with an average value of 113.9°, which deviates slightly from the angle of 109.3° in hexagonal ice. This octameric water cluster is similar to that found in [Zn(bime)(glu)4H2O]n (bime = 1,2-bis(imidazol-1′-yl)ethane, H2glu = glutaric acid), with a slightly different O−O distance [5], but it is different from the cube-like (H2O)8 in a Co complex [15] and the ice-like, cyclic (H2O)8 in an organic supramolecular complex [16].

[Zn(BMIE)(1,4-BDC)]·(H2O)3 (2)

The single crystal X-ray structural analysis of 2 shows that each Zn(II) atom exhibits a distorted tetrahedral environment, composed of two carboxylic O atoms from the 1,4-BDC anions (Zn−O = 1.961(2) Å and 1.947(2) Å) and two N atoms from the same BMIE ligand (Zn−N = 2.010(2) Å and 2.012(2) Å), as shown in Fig. 2a. The BMIE ligand adopts a cis-configuration with a dihedral angle between the two imidazole rings of 113.5°. Each 1,4-BDC anion coordinates to two Zn atoms, acting as a bridging ligand to afford an infinite 1D zigzag chain structure (Fig. 2b).

The lattice water molecules in compound 2 are self-assembled to generate an interesting T4(0)A(4) water tape containing two oxygen atoms of the carboxylic acid anions, using the nomenclature proposed by Infantes [17], [18] (Fig. 2c), where the number in parentheses is the number of water molecules shared between the adjacent rings. The water tape is composed of one (H2O)4 cluster (two O7w and two O9w) and one water dimer (O8w), which alternate with the same conformations. Within each (H2O)4 unit, O7w and O9w act as both a donor and an acceptor to form the cyclic water tetramers, which are almost coplanar, with an average hydrogen-bonded distance of 2.757 Å, which is comparable to the value of 2.759 Å in Ih ice at −90 °C. Through the strong O9w−O4 and O8w−O4 hydrogen bonds, with distances of 2.757 Å and 2.951 Å between the tetramer and dimer water clusters, respectively, the (H2O)4 and (H2O)2 units connect through the O4 bridging of the carboxylic acid anion to form a 1D infinite water tape. The resulting 1D water tapes are encapsulated in the infinite 1D zigzag metal–organic chains. Furthermore, the O7w of the (H2O)4 cluster and the O6 of the carboxylic acid anion form an extensive hydrogen-bonding network, extending into an overall three-dimensional supramolecular architecture (Fig. 2d).

[Cd(BIM)2(OH)(H2O)2(PF6)]·(H2O)4 (3)

The single crystal X-ray diffraction studies reveal that the asymmetric unit in 3 contains the basic building block of two independent BIM ligands, one cadmium center, one PF6− and one OH− anion, two coordinated water molecules, and four additional crystal water molecules. As shown in Fig. 3a, the Cd(II) ion is coordinated to two oxygen atoms of two coordinated waters and four nitrogen atoms of four independent BIM ligands, forming a six-coordinate environment. Three nitrogen donors of the BIM ligands lie in the equatorial plane, and the other nitrogen donors of the BIM ligands lies in the apical positions. The Cd(1)–N(1), Cd(1)–N(5), Cd(1)–N(4), Cd(1)–N(8) distances of 2.315(3), 2.293(2), 2.312(3) and 2.294(2) Å are within the range expected for such species [19]. The trans-axial Cd(1)–O(1), Cd(1)–O(2) distances are 2.417(3) Å and 2.419(3) Å, respectively. Two Cd(II) centers are bridged by two BIM ligands with a Cd(1)–Cd(1A) separation of 9.248 Å and 10.649 Å, with the dihedral angles between the two imidazole rings in the same BIM ligand being 67.1° and 65.9°, respectively. Each BIM ligand coordinates two Cd(II) cations to form a 32-membered macrometallacycle containing four Cd(II) centers and four BIM ligands. Each Cd(II) center is coordinated to four BIM ligands, extending the 32-membered macrometallacycle of four metals and four ligands to a 2D grid-like sheet structure with [4,4] tops (Fig. 3b).

The interesting structural feature of 3 is that the water molecules exist in the space of the MOF and link to each other by hydrogen bonds to form 1D wave-like water tapes. The water tape is composed of two different (H2O)4 clusters (O1w, O3w, O1w, O6w and O2w, O4w, O2w, O4w) and one water dimer (O5w, O7w), which alternate and have the same conformations shown in Fig. 4. Within each (H2O)4 unit, O4w and O3w serve as hydrogen bond acceptors to link O2w and O1w, respectively. O2w and O1w acts as hydrogen donors to bond with O4w and O3w, respectively. Dimer O5w bonds with O4w, O6w, and O7w as hydrogen donors and acceptors. All the cyclic water tetramers are nearly coplanar, with average hydrogen-bonded distances of 2.822 Å and 2.794 Å, which are longer than the value of 2.759 Å in Ih ice at −90 °C, but slightly shorter than those observed in liquid water (2.854 Å) [20] and comparable to those in the ice II phase (2.77–2.84 Å) [21]. The dimer O5w and O7w distance is 2.899 Å, which is longer than the value of 2.854 Å in liquid water. The wide variation in the hydrogen-bonded O−O distances (2.743–2.901 Å) and O−O−O angles (85.1–113.3°) provide flexibility to the water molecules in this environment so that they can be accommodated in the voids and maximize the interactions among themselves. By the strong O5w−O4w and O5w−O6w hydrogen bonds between the tetramer and dimer water clusters, the (H2O)4 and (H2O)2 units connect with each other in an alternating fashion to form a 1D infinite water tape. The present infinite tape motif can be named as T4(0)A(0)2(0) using the nomenclature proposed by Infantes. Recently, a few 1D water tapes constructed from tetrameric water clusters, which can be either vertex- or edge-sharing were reported [22]. Compared to previous water tapes with (H2O)4 clusters as the substructure, such a T4(0)A(0)2(0) motif is neither vertex-nor edge-sharing and is still scarcely investigated. The O1w and O2w of the (H2O)4 cluster are coordinated to the metal center, resulting in the 1D water tape being encapsulated between the 2D metal–organic sheets, extending into an overall 2D supramolecular network with AABAABAA mode, in which A is the metal–organic layer and B is the water tape layer (Fig. 5).

[Cd(PA-BIM)2(ClO4)2]·11.33H2O (4)

Single-crystal X-ray analysis revealed that 4 crystallized in the high-symmetry cubic space group Ia-3d. The asymmetric unit contains one crystallographically independent Cd(II) ion, two unique PA-BIM ligands, and two uncoordinated ClO4− anions. As shown in Fig. 6a, each Cd(II) center is four-coordinated by four imidazolium nitrogen atoms from four different PA-BIM ligands to form a slightly distorted tetrahedral geometry. The N–Cd–N angles vary from 100.1(9)° to 130.5(2)°, and the Cd–N distance of 2.231(4) Å is within the range expected for such a species. In each PA-BIM ligand, the benzene and imidazole rings are not coplanar, with a dihedral angle of 24.6°. Each PA-BIM ligand is coordinated to two Cd(II) cations in a trans-conformation. Acting as a bridging ligand, each group of six PA-BIM ligands were linked to each other by six metal Cd(II) centers, resulting in a 48-membered macrometallacycle (Fig. 6b). One phenylazo group in the bridged PA-BIM ligand points into the center of the macrometallacycle in alternating up/down mode to form a cavity inside each macrometallacycle. The other phenylazo group in the bridged PA-BIM ligand points into another 48-membered macrometallacycle center. Consequently, each metal Cd(II) center exists in four different 48-membered macrometallacycles to form a three-dimensional porous metal–organic framework (Fig. 7a). The tetra-coordinated cadmium metal–organic cation network can be simplified as an lcs topological array (Fig. 7b), with long and short Schläfli symbols of 6.6.6(2).6(2).6(2).6(2) and 66, respectively.

More interestingly, an unusual 3D water clathrate network containing cage-shaped structural tetradecameric water cluster (H2O)14 units was encapsulated in the three-dimensional porous metal–organic framework (Fig. 8a). A view of a single (H2O)14 cluster unit in compound 4 is shown in Fig. 8b. The cage-shaped structural tetradecameric water cluster (H2O)14 unit consists of fused water cyclic hexamers, each with a book conformation. However, the water hexamer unit always exists in a chair or boat conformation in other reported water (H2O)14 or (H2O)n clusters [23]. In each single (H2O)14 cluster unit, the O−O distance ranges from 2.682 Å to 2.848 Å, resulting in an average O−O distance of 2.756 Å, which is similar to that in reported supramolecular water clusters and very close to the corresponding value of 2.759 Å in Ih ice. The Ow−Ow−Ow bond angles vary from 99.9° to 119.7° and are slightly different from the tetrahedral angle found in Ih and Ic ice. It should be noted that there are only a few structural reports on discrete (H2O)14 clusters so far [24].

The 14-membered water cluster links to six other (H2O)14 clusters by the hydrogen bonding interaction of O5 with the bridging water molecule O3, with the O3−O5 distance being 2.894 Å, which compared to 2.854 Å in liquid water, is extended to a three-dimensional water clathrate network (Fig. 9a). If the (H2O)14 cluster can be regarded as a node and O3 as a bridge, then the 3D water clathrate network can be considered as a bcs network (Fig. 9b), in which the long and short Schläfli symbols are 4.4.4.4.4.4.6(4).6(4).6(4).6(9).6(9).6(9).6(9). 6(9).6(9) and (46)(69), respectively. To the best of our knowledge, this is a rare demonstration of a 3D water clathrate network trapped in a cavity of a metal–organic framework where there are no typical hydrogen bonding interactions between the 3D water cluster networks and the skeleton of the 3D metal–organic framework. Most of the previous reported water cluster networks in metal–organic frameworks often exist in a hydrophilic inner wall, and there is obvious hydrogen bonding between the water clusters and the organic ligands in the metal–organic framework [25].

TGA analysis and photoluminescent properties

The thermal analysis (TGA) under nitrogen atmosphere showed that the complex 1 possessed a three-step weight-loss process. The first-step occurred from 65 °C to 100 °C with a total weight-loss of 9.3%, which can be attributed to the loss of four crystal water molecules (calcd: 9.1%), and the second-step from 340 °C to 480 °C with a total weight-loss of 46.1%, which can be attributed to the loss of the 2-mBIM ligand (calcd: 44.1%), while the third-step, which was observed in a temperature range from 550 °C to 660 °C, corresponding to the release of the SO3CF3 anion and indicating the complete decomposition of 1 with 9.4% residual weight corresponds to ZnO (calcd: 10.3%). complex 2 also possessed a three-step weight-loss process. The first-step occurred from 70 °C to 98 °C with a total weight-loss of 10.2%, which can be attributed to the loss of three crystal water molecules (calcd: 9.6%), and the second-step from 360 °C to 400 °C with a total weight-loss of 51.2%, which can be attributed to the loss of the BMIE ligand (calcd: 49.5%). The third-step was observed in a temperature range from 470 °C to 510 °C corresponding to the release of the anion and indicating the complete decomposition of 2 with 13.6% residual weight corresponds to ZnO (calcd: 14.5%). For complex 3, the first-step occurred from 70 °C to 105 °C and the second-step from 105 °C to 130 °C with a total weight-loss of 16.9%, which can be attributed to the loss of five crystal water molecules and one coordination water molecule (calcd: 16.3%), while the third-step, which was observed in a temperature range from 310 °C to 340 °C, corresponding to the release of the BIM ligand and indicating the complete decomposition of 3 with 18.9% residual weight corresponds to CdO (calcd: 19.4%). For 4, the thermal gravimetric analyses show that the gradual weight loss begins from room temperature to 108 °C in a total loss of 18.2% corresponding to loss of all water molecules (calculated value 16.6%). There is a 1.6% discrepancy between the measured and computed mass depletion, maybe come from the absence of many water molecules in 4. The complete decomposition of compound 4 is achieved about 270 °C. Those results indicate that the 3D water clathrate network is easy to remove from the 3D metal–organic framework which is stable up to higher temperature (<270 °C). It also confirms that the 3D water clathrate network does not exist strongly hydrogen bonding interactions with the cationic skeleton of host molecule MOF [26]. Because that all water molecules were completely removed at higher temperature (>130 °C) when hydrogen bonding was formed between water cluster and metal–organic frameworks [27].

The photoluminescent properties of complexes 1–4 have been investigated. The photoluminescent emission was not observed for 1 and 4. It can be observed that compound 2 exhibit strong photoluminescence upon photoexcitation at 329 nm. The main emission peaks are located at 425 nm. In contrast, the free BMIE ligand emits at 402 nm upon the 316 nm. And compound 3 exhibit weak photoluminescence upon photoexcitation at 293 nm. The main emission peaks are located at 358 nm. The free BIM ligand emits at 338 nm upon the 290 nm. The red-shifted emission peaks of 2 and 3 compared to free ligand BMIE and BIM is assigned to the intraligand π − π* and n − π* transitions of the ligands.

Conclusions

In summary, we have characterized four new MOFs encapsulating a discrete octameric water cluster, 1D T4(0)A(4) and T4(0)A(0)2(0) water tapes, and a fascinating 3D water clathrate network. Complexes 1 and 2 exhibit infinite 1D cation beaded-chain structures. Complex 3 shows a 2D grid-like sheet structure, and complex 4 is a porous 3D MOF with tetrahedral Cd(II) centers and trans-conformation PA-BIM ligands. The holes in this MOF are occupied by a fascinating three-dimensional water clathrate network, which consists of cage-shaped structural tetradecameric water cluster (H2O)14 units and six independent bridging water molecules. The new associated mode of the (H2O)14 cluster containing a fused book-conformation water hexamer and other new water aggregates. The results suggest that the bisimidazolium ligands and anions play crucial roles in the formation of the different host structures and different guest water aggregations. These observations will help us understand the formation of water clusters in nature and biological systems. Further studies in our laboratory will focus on the synthesis of other 3D metal–organic frameworks that can trap other 3D water clusters with different sizes and shapes.
