The V-shaped semi-rigid di-4-pyridyl sulfone-N,N′-dioxide ligand and its silver(I), manganese(II) and zinc(II) complexes: Anion tuning and supramolecular assembly

Author links open overlay panel
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.poly.2013.10.033Get rights and content

Abstract

A series of silver(I) complexes bearing the V-shaped di-4-pyridyl sulfone-N,N′-dioxide ligand (abbreviated as L), namely [Ag(L)(NO3)(H2O)]∞ (1), [Ag(L)(CF3SO3)]∞ (2), [Ag(L)(CF3CO2)]∞ (3), [Ag(L)(C2F5CO2)]∞ (4) and {[Ag(L)](ClO4)}∞ (5), and the manganese(II) complex [Mn(L)2(H2O)4](ClO4)2 (6) and the zinc(II) complex [Zn(L)2(H2O)4](ClO4)2 (7) of L have been synthesized and characterized. Under the tuning effect of anions with different coordination capacities and steric bulk, the semi-rigid ligand L exhibits versatile μ2-, μ3- and μ4-bridging modes with the N-oxide and/or sulfonyl O atoms functioning as bonding sites to form a coordination chain structure in complex 1, isostructural nets in 2–4, and a three-dimensional framework in 5. The isostructural mononuclear complexes 6 and 7 exhibit the η1–ON-oxide bonding mode of L. Unusual lone-pair aromatic contacts and anion–π interactions result in supramolecular aggregations of complexes 1–7. The FT-IR spectra and luminescent properties of these complexes have been observed and discussed.

Graphical abstract

The semi-rigid bi-4-pyridyl sulfone-N,N′-dioxide ligand functions as a flexible linkage in a series of Ag(I), Mn(II) and Zn(II) complexes, which exhibit distinct coordination motifs depending upon the cooperative effect of the metal ion and counter anion present.

1. Introduction

Compared to a pyridyl N atom (sp2), the O atom of pyridyl-N-oxide has a different electron configuration (sp3), and thus exhibits more flexible bonding modes as it links to metal ions [1]. Rigid linear 4,4′-bipyridine-N,N′-dioxide [2] and pyrazine-N,N′-dioxide [3], as well as related flexible ligands such as 1,2-bis(pyridin-4-yl)ethane-N,N′-dioxide [4], [5] and 1,3-bis(4-pyridyl)propane-N,N′-dioxide [6], have been employed as versatile linkages with μ2-, μ3- and μ4-bridging modes through the linkage of their O atoms to one or more metal ions [2], [4], [6]. However, the coordination chemistry of N,N′-dioxides derived from V-shaped pyridyl ligands bearing CH2 (methylene), O, S, SO (sulfinyl), OSO (sulfonyl) [7](a), [7](b), [7](c), [7](d), [7](e), [7](f) or a meta-connected benzene hinge group [7](g), [7](h), [7](i), [7](j) remains little explored. To our knowledge, so far only metal complexes of 2,5-bis(4-pyridyl-N-oxide)-1,3,4-oxadiazole have appeared in the literature [8], although the parent pyridyl-based ligands have been successfully used as a V-shaped modularity to furnish abundant metal-organic frameworks (MOFs) with attractive topological structures and physicochemical properties.

Our recent study revealed that semi-rigid di-4-pyridyl sulfoxide (SO) and di-4-pyridyl sulfone (OSO) prefer taking a similar μ2-N,N′-bridging mode in a series of chain-like silver(I) complexes, which exhibit markedly different supramolecular architectures through the presence or absence of attractive OS(sulfoxide)···π (pyridyl) interactions [7f]. Herein, we report a new V-shaped ligand, di-4-pyridyl sulfone-N,N′-dioxide (hereafter abbreviated as L) derived from di-4-pyridyl sulfone, and its Ag(I) complexes, namely [Ag(L)(NO3)(H2O)]∞ (1), [Ag(L)(CF3SO3)]∞ (2), [Ag(L)(CF3CO2)]∞ (3), [Ag(L)(C2F5CO2)]∞ (4) and {[Ag(L)](ClO4)}∞ (5), together with [Mn(L)2(H2O)4](ClO4)2 (6) and [Zn(L)2(H2O)4](ClO4)2 (7). In combination with various metal ions and counter anions, the L ligand exhibits flexible ligation modes (I–IV) (Scheme 1) for the N-oxide and/or sulfonyl O atoms. The lone-pair aromatic ON-oxide···π contact (in crystalline L) and OSO···π interaction (in 4), and the common domain anion–π interactions (in 1–7) are found to result in supramolecular aggregations of L and these complexes. FT-IR spectroscopy, elemental analyses and luminescent properties of these compounds are also reported.

2. Experimental

2.1. Material and methods

All organic reagents and metal salts were purchased from the Bai-ling-wei company (J&K) and they were used without further purification. Bis-4-pyridylsulfide was prepared according to literature methods [9]. Elemental analyses (C, H, N) were performed using an Elemental Vario EL analyzer. FT-IR spectra were recorded on a Bruker Equinox 55 FT-IR spectrometer with dry KBr pellets in the range 400–4000 cm−1. Fluorescence spectra were measured on an F-4500FL spectrophotometer in the solid state at room temperature. The 1H NMR spectrum of L was performed on a Bruker AVANCE III 600 spectrometer. All samples used for analysis measurements were collected from the crystals deposited, with no further purification. A metal:ligand ratio of 2:1 was used to synthesize complexes 1–7, as shown in the following experimental section. The same products as 1–7 could also be respectively obtained with a ratio of 1:1, while other ratios of metal to ligand in each case generally led to oil-like or powder-like residues of unknown composition.

Caution! Perchlorate salts in the presence of organic ligands are potentially explosive and heating has to be avoided. Treatment of only small amounts of the materials is necessary and they should be handled with care.

2.2. Synthesis of di-4-pyridyl sulfone-N,N′-dioxide (L)

In a round-bottomed flask (25 ml) equipped with a magnetic stirrer, bis-4-pyridylsulfide (576 mg, 2.62 mmol) was dissolved in EtOH (5 ml). H2O2 (30%, 5.5 mmol, 0.6 ml) and 65% HNO3 (3 mmol, 0.2 ml) were then added to the flask and stirred at room temperature for about 3.5 h. Then, the mixture was quenched by adding H2O (5 ml), extracted with ethyl acetate (4 × 25 ml). The combined extracts were dried with anhydrous MgSO4 and concentrated to give a white solid. The product was further purified by column chromatography on silica gel using ethyl acetate/CH2Cl2 (v:v = 6:5) as the eluent. Di-4-pyridyl sulfone-N,N′-dioxide was obtained as a white powder (376.38 mg). Yield: 57%, mp 246–249 °C. 1H NMR (600 MHz, CDCl3) δ: 8.41–8.42 (d, J = 6 Hz, 2H), 7.97–7.98 (d, J = 6 Hz, 2H) (Supporting information Fig. S1). Anal. Calc. 2.10. Crystal structure determination and refinement

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction data of complexes 1–7 were collected on a Bruker Smart APEX II CCD diffractometer, operating at 50 kV and 30 mA using Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at room temperature. Each selected crystal was mounted inside a Lindemann glass capillary, and each data reduction was performed using the SMART and SAINT software [10]. An empirical absorption correction was applied using the sadabs program [11]. All structures were solved by direct methods and refined by full-matrix least-squares on F2 using the shelxtl program package [12]. The ordered non-hydrogen atoms in each structure were refined with anisotropic displacement parameters, while the hydrogen atoms were placed in idealized positions and allowed to ride on the parent atoms. All positions of the hydrogen atoms of the water molecules in 1, 6 and 7 were obtained from different Fourier-difference maps. In complex 3, the F2 and F3 atoms are disordered over two positions with ratios of 3:2 and 1:3, respectively, thus non-covalent interactions involving with these atoms are not discussed in the supramolecular architecture. The crystallographic data and refinement parameters of the ligand L and complexes 1–7 are summarized in Table 1.

3. Result and discussion

3.1. Crystal structure

3.1.1. The di-4-pyridyl sulfone-N,N′-dioxide ligand (L)

In the crystalline structure of di-4-pyridyl sulfone-N,N′-dioxide (L), the average SO bond length is 1.442(4) Å, comparable to the value of 1.44 Å in alkyl sulfones [13]. The N → O bond lengths are 1.299(3) and 1.290(4) Å, lying within the range of 1.314–1.326 Å in 1,2-bis(pyridin-4-yl)ethane-N,N′-dioxide [5](a), [5](b). The C(sp2)–S–C(sp2) angle is 106.3(2)°, with the pair of pyridyl rings exhibiting a dihedral angle of 67.87(4)°, being a typical V-shaped conformation that can be utilized in a coordination supramolecular assembly. The L molecules are aggregated into a two-dimensional network almost parallel to the ac plane through ON-oxide···πpyridyl (Fig. 1) (O6a···Cg1 = 3.156(3) Å, O3···Cg2 = 3.254(3) Å) contacts and C10a–H10···O3b interactions (Fig. 1) (a x, −y + 1, z − 0.5; b −x, −y + 2, z − 0.5, see Table S1 in the supporting information). The lone-pair aromatic interaction is between a lone pair of electrons and the face of a π system, which appears counterintuitive and is of current interest and attracts more attention due to its important role in supramolecular assembly [14](a), [14](b), [14](c), [14](d). The present ON-oxide···π contacts belong to one type of lone-pair aromatic interaction that has not been documented. Herein, the O···centroid distances of 3.156(3) and 3.254(3) Å are around the sum of the O radius (1.40 Å) [15] and the half thickness of phenyl (1.84 Å) [16], with corresponding N → O···centroid angles of 157.94(3)° and 128.77(3)°, close to 180°. The layer-like nets formed are further stabilized through C–H···Osulfonyl hydrogen-bonding interactions (C9–H9···O4c, C4–H4···O1c, c x, y − 1, z. Table S1) to engender a three-dimensional framework.

3.1.2. [Ag(L)(NO3)(H2O)]∞ (1)

The Ag(I) center in complex 1 exhibits a distorted O4-tetrahedron geometry surrounded by one aqua ligand, one nitrate oxygen atom and two N-oxide O atoms from independent L ligands (Fig. 2). Each L ligand exhibits a μ2-O,O′ bridging mode (II, Scheme 1) with two η1–ON-oxide atoms linking to the Ag(I) ions, forming an infinite zigzag chain along the [101] direction (Fig. 3a). The chains are connected to form a layer via incorporative C–H···Osulfonyl and O–Haqua···ON-oxide interactions (Fig. 3a, Table S1). The formed layers are further stacked and stabilized by another set of O–Haqua···ON-oxide hydrogen-bonding interactions (Table S1) and interesting O(NO3-)···π contacts [14](e), [14](f), [14](g), [14](h), [14](i), [14](j) to form a three-dimensional framework (Fig. 3b, Table 2). The anion–π contact is analogous to the lone-pair aromatic interaction [14](a), [14](b), [14](c), [14](d). Herein, the O(NO3-)···centroidpyridyl distances of 3.372(2) and 3.516(2) Å are comparable to the sum of the van der Waals radii of the contacting atoms (3.24 Å, taking the half thickness of a phenyl ring as 1.84 Å [16] and the van der Waals radius of oxygen as 1.40 Å [15]), and to the mean O(NO3-)···centroidpyridyl distance of 3.302 Å surveyed by Reedijk co-workers. through a CSD search [14f]. Detailed metric parameters of the O(NO3-)···π interactions are listed in Table 2 for comparison.

3.1.3. Isostructural complexes [Ag(L)(CF3SO3)]∞ (2), [Ag(L)(CF3CO2)]∞ (3) and [Ag(L)(C2F5CO2)]∞ (4)

With the aim of investigating the ligand behavior and forming silver(I) complexes with L in the presence of CF3SO3-, CF3CO2- and C2F5CO2-, which have a relative stronger bonding ability as compared to NO3- [17], the silver(I) complexes 2–4 were respectively synthesized and obtained through the reaction of L with the corresponding silver(I) salt under similar conditions. Interestingly, 2–4 are isostructural complexes bearing an Ag2 unit bridged by a pair of η1: η1: μ2-bridging anions (CF3SO3- in 2, CF3CO2- in 3 and C2F5CO2- in 4). As shown in Fig. 4a, each Ag(I) center of 2 exhibits a distorted O5-coordination geometry, surrounded by two ON-oxide from separate L ligands, two independent triflate O atoms and one weakly bound sulfonyl O atom at Ag···O = 2.613(4) Å (2.708(3) Å in 3, and 2.740(2) Å in 4). Such two symmetry related Ag(I) ions (Ag1, Ag1a) exhibit an argentophilic interaction [18] to form an Ag2 unit with Ag···Ag distances of 3.092(4) Å in 2, 2.899(3) Å in 3 and 2.970(3) Å in 4, which are shorter than the sum of the van der Waals radii of two silver atoms (3.44 Å), and slightly longer than twice the radius of metallic silver (2.89 Å) [19]. The coordination geometries of the Ag(I) centers in 3 and 4 are similar to that in 2, and are not discussed here. For the L ligands in 2–4, two N-oxide O atoms link to two separate Ag(I) centers and one sulfonyl O weakly bonds to a third Ag(I) ion, exhibiting a η1: η1: η1: μ3-bridging mode (III, Scheme 1), different from the μ2-bridging mode II in complex 1.

By using the Ag2 units as nodes and the η1: η1: η1: μ3-bridging L ligands (mode III) as linkers, a similar layer-like coordination motif, almost parallel to the ab plane, is furnished in the isostructural complexes 2–4 (Fig. 4b). C–H···Osulfonyl and C–H···ON-oxide hydrogen-bonding interactions (Table S1) occur between adjacent layers in 2 and 4 to form their corresponding three-dimensional supramolecular architectures. Moreover, two types of unusual interactions, a short OSO···π(pyridyl) contact and a halogen···halogen [20] (F···F) interaction, are also found in the supramolecular architecture of 4 (Fig. 5b). The O(sulfonyl)···π(pyridyl) interaction represents one type of lone-pair aromatic affinity, similar to the above mentioned ON-oxide···π(pyridyl) interaction in the packing structure of L. Herein, the O1(sulfonyl)···centroid(N2a-containing ring) distance is 3.429(2) Å, with the distance from O1 to the closest ring atom (N2a) being 3.130(2) Å and the SO···centroid angle being 103.09(6)° (a −x, −y + 1, −z), similar to a reported CO···π(pyridyl) contact [14c]. For the F···F interaction in 4, the F3···F3b distance is 2.544(3) Å with a C–F···F angle of 148.75 (2)° (b −x + 1.5, −y + 1.5, −z + 1), which is shorter than those in substituted isoquinolines (2.777(2) Å, 170.0(1)°) [20f]. Further scrutiny reveals that the pair of triflate bridging anions to the Ag2 unit in 2 (Fig. 4a) and C2F5CO2- in 4 (Fig. 5a) not only function as co-ligands, but also exhibit F···π(pyridyl) contacts, another type of lone-pair aromatic interaction [14c]. The F···centroid distances vary from 3.084(3) to 3.506(2) Å, comparable to the sum (3.19 Å) of the F radius (1.35 Å) [15] and the half thickness of a phenyl ring (1.84 Å) [16]. Detailed metric parameters of the F···π interactions are listed in Table 2 for comparison.

3.1.4. {[Ag(L)](ClO4)}∞ (5)

A three-dimensional coordination framework is furnished in the silver(I) complex 5 with the perchlorate anion which is markedly different from the infinite chain in 1 and the layer-like structures in complexes 2–4. Moreover, unlike the η1-bonding nitrate anion in 1 and the η1: η1: μ2-bridging carboxylate/triflate anions in 2–4, here the perchlorate is distant from the Ag(I) center due to its weak coordination capability with respect to the former anions. Four L ligands thus facilely bond to the metal center via the N-oxide O atoms, forming a distorted O4-tetrahedral coordination geometry (Fig. 6). For the ligand L, each exhibits a η2: η2: μ4-bridging mode (IV, Scheme 1) with two η2–ON-oxide bonding sites, a new ligation mode differing from the η1: η1: μ2-bridging mode (II) in complex 1 and the η1: η1: η1: μ3-bridging mode (III) in complexes 2–4.

Along the c direction, the Ag(I) ions are bridged by the η2–ON-oxide atoms to form a chain moiety (Fig. 7a), and the chains are linked via the η2: η2: μ4-bridging L ligands from different directions to furnish a three-dimensional coordination framework with channels (Fig. 7b). Viewed along the c direction, the perchlorate anions are accommodated within the channel region and are anchored to the channel wall through C-H···O(ClO4-) hydrogen bonds (Table S1) and anion(ClO4-)···π interactions. As shown in Fig. 7c, each perchlorate is embraced by a pair of pyridyl rings of L with O7···centroid(pyridyl) and O6···centroid(pyridyl) distances of 2.949(3) and 3.620(3) Å (Table 2), respectively. The O···centroid distances well compare with the mean of 3.267 Å for O(ClO4-)···centroid(pyridyl) distances surveyed through CSD research[14f]. In comparison, the N → O bond lengths in 5 are 1.326(4) and 1.322(4) Å, comparable to those in 1–4 (1.305(3)–1.333(3) Å). Due to the Ag-ON-oxide bonding, all the N → O bond lengths of complexes 1–5 are longer than those in the free L ligand (1.299(3) and 1.290(4) Å).

3.1.5. Complexes Mn(II) (6) and Zn(II) (7)

Mn(II) and Zn(II) are widely used as 6-connected nodes with an octahedral coordination geometry to construct distinct MOFs with various organic ligands. Considering those in mind, we synthesized and obtained the perchlorate Mn(II) and Zn(II) complexes of di-4-pyridyl sulfone-N,N′-dioxide, namely [Mn(L)2(H2O)4](ClO4)2 (6) and [Zn(L)2(H2O)4](ClO4)2 (7). The mononuclear complexes 6 and 7 are isostructural, bearing a centrosymmetric trans-O6-octohedral geometry with four aqua ligands at the equatorial sites and two N-oxide O atoms at the axial sites. As shown in Fig. 8, L functions in a η1–O coordination mode (I, Scheme 1), different from the bridging modes II–IV in complexes 1–5. For complex 6, the [Mn(L)2(H2O)4]2+ units are interconnected by the N-oxide and sulfonyl O atoms, involving O–Haqua···O interactions (Table S1) and giving rise to a two-dimensional layer almost parallel to the ab plane (Fig. S3). The formed layers are stacked with perchlorate anions embedded within the interlayer region (Fig. 9a) and fixed in site through O–Haqua···Operchlorate, Caryl–H···Operchlorate (Table S1) and O(ClO4-)···π(pyridyl) interactions. Regarding the O(ClO4-)···π interactions, each perchlorate in 6 is embraced by two pyridyl rings of L, as in complex 5, forming a π···anion···π contact mode via the O6 and O7 pointing to the pyridyl rings (Fig. 9b). The O6···centroid and O7···centroid distances are 2.999(2) and 3.405(2) Å, respectively, comparable to the values (2.949(3) and 3.620(3) Å) in complex 5 (Table 2) and the mean O(ClO4-)···centroid(pyridyl) distance (3.267 Å) from CSD research results [14f]. Due to the driving forces of these multiple π···(ClO4-)···π interactions of the wing-like pyridyl rings of L, the C(sp3)-S-C(sp3) angles of 101.92(1)° in 6, 102.39(2)° in 5 and 101.92(1)° in 7 are smaller than those in the free ligand L (106.3(2)° and 106.4(2)°). The structure of complex 7 is similar to that of 6, and details of the non-covalent interactions are also listed in Table S1 for comparison. However, attempts to obtain other transition metal complexes of L or other Mn(II) and Zn(II) complexes with various counter anions failed.

3.2. Role of the anion and metal ion

The counter anions NO3-, CF3SO3-, CF3CO2-, C2F5CO2- and ClO4-, displaying different coordination capabilities, molecular geometries, steric bulk and van der Waals volumes, have been rationally utilized to regulate the desired supramolecular architectures of numerous metal complexes [17]. In the present study, CF3SO3-, CF3CO2- and C2F5CO2- exhibit the same η1: η1: μ2 mode to bridge an Ag2 sub-building unit, leading to the isostructural complexes [Ag(L)(CF3SO3)]∞ (2), [Ag(L)(CF3CO2)]∞ (3) and [Ag(L)(C2F5CO2)]∞ (4) under similar reaction conditions. In contrast, NO3- has a relatively weak bonding capability [17a], small steric bulk and typically a C3 geometry, which together are responsible for the formation of the different Ag(I) coordination environment in [Ag(L)(NO3)(H2O)]∞ (1), having an infinite chain structure. Also, it is understandable that ClO4- is distant from the O4-coordinated Ag(I) center in {[Ag(L)](ClO4)}∞ (5) due to its weak coordination capability as compared to the other anions [17a], thus four V-shaped L ligands facilely bond to the metal ion to form the three-dimensional framework in 5 that is different from those of 1–4. Furthermore, the ClO4- anion preferentially engages in anion–π interactions with a common π ···anion···π mode in complexes 5–7.

Regarding the metal ion, Ag(I) with d10 configuration generally adopts flexible coordination geometries upon subtle tuning effect of the counter anions and/or neutral ligands around [21]. The variable coordination environments of Ag(I) in complexes 1–5 can be rationally viewed as responses to the tuning effects of counter anions. The semi-rigid L ligand thus exhibits flexible μ2-, μ3- and μ4-bridging modes (Scheme 1) to form the final different coordination motifs in complexes 1–5. In comparison, the Mn(II) and Zn(II) ions preferentially adopt octahedral coordination geometry, and hence the semi-rigid L ligands adopt the η1–ON-oxide mode and bonds to the axial sites of the metal coordination polyhedron, leading to the mononuclear complexes 6 and 7 with the perchlorate ion separating from the metal ions.

3.3. Photoluminescence properties

The luminescence of complexes 1–7 and the ligand L were investigated in the solid state at room temperature. The ligand L exhibits two moderately strong emission bands at λem = 428 and 458 nm upon excitation at 388 nm. Similar emissions at about 430 and 460 nm (λmax = 430 and 454 nm in 1, 429 and 457 nm in 2, 430 and 460 nm in 3, 430 and 454 nm in 4, 430 and 453 nm in 5, 431 and 454 nm in 6, and 429 and 452 nm in 7) are observed in complexes 1–7 with λex = 379 nm for complex 2 and 388 nm for all the other complexes (Figs. S4 and S5). Such luminescence might be attributed to an intra-ligand π–π∗ transition of the ligand [22], thus the photoluminescence of complexes 1–7 are based on a ligand-based emission.

3.4. FT-IR spectroscopic study

The FT-IR spectrum of L shows ν(CN) peaks at 1600 and 1443 cm−1, exhibiting a blue shift with respect to those of the pyridy ring (1550 and 1390 cm−1) [23], [14](i) due to the electron-withdrawing effect of the N-oxide group. The characteristic OSO stretching vibrations, νas at 1309 cm−1 and νs at 1157 cm−1, and the bending and oscillatory vibrations of the OSO group [24], at 571 and 551 cm−1, respectively, are found (Fig. S6). The N → O stretching and bending vibrations occur at about 1290 and 849 cm−1, respectively [25]. On comparison, the ν(CN) absorptions in complexes 1–7 have almost the same values as those in free L. The broad band centered at 1157 cm−1 for the OSO vibration can be ascribed to multiple H-bonding interactions involving the sulfonyl group (Table S1). Due to η1–ON-oxide bonding in complexes 1–7, the N → O stretching vibrations undergo red shifts by about 63 cm−1 with respect to of the value of 1290 cm−1 in free L, exhibiting a broad band centered at about 1226 cm−1. These red shifts exceed the range 45–55 cm−1 for metal complexes of other N-oxide derivatives reported [25], suggesting that the M–O bonds in 1–7 are likely to be stronger. The N → O bending vibrations of 1–7 occur at about 845 cm−1, with ambiguous changes compared to that of 849 cm−1 in free L.

4. Conclusion

Semi-rigid di-4-pyridyl sulfone-N,N′-dioxide (L) acts in versatile μ2-, μ3- and μ4-bridging modes through its N-oxide and/or sulfonyl O atoms, bonding to metal centers in a series of silver(I) complexes 1–5 with various anions. Infinite chain in 1, layer network in 2–4 and three-dimensional framework structures in 5 were respectively obtained. L functions as an axial η1–ON-oxide bonding ligand to yield a trans-octahedral [M(L)2(H2O)4]2+ center in complexes 6 (M = Mn2+) and 7 (M = Zn2+), with a weakly coordinating perchlorate anion. Unconventional ON-oxide···π, OSO···π and anion–π interactions were revealed in the supramolecular architectures of 1–7, which provide further strong support for the counter-intuitive lone-pair aromatic interactions that are of importance in supramolecular chemistry [14]. In contrast to the μ2-N,N-bridging di-4-pyridyl sulfoxide or di-4-pyridyl sulfone [7f], di-4-pyridyl sulfone-N,N′-dioxide can be utilized as a flexible linkage to generate different transition metal complexes, and the coordination and supramolecular chemistry of such a V-shaped ligand is worthy of further investigation.

    The rigid N-donor ligands with fixed conformation are good candidates to help rationally synthesize and predict the target compounds in some degree, while the flexible N-donor ligands are prone to change their configurations and conformations to contribute to the diversity of structures and topologies [40–44]. However, recently more attention has been paid to asymmetric semi-rigid N-donor ligands [45–47] for the reason that they can combine the advantages of the rigid and flexible N-donor ligands which are feasible to lead to interesting structural architectures [41,48]. The Htbpp ligand (Htbpp = 4-(1-(4-(2H-tetrazol-5-yl)benzyl)-1H-pyrazol-3-yl)pyridine), a semi-rigid V-shaped ligand, possesses the aforementioned merits but is rarely explored.

    The rational design and synthesis of silver(I) coordination polymers have been widely studied. This study was motivated not only by the possibility of their application as functional materials i.e. fluorescent materials, but also by the prospect of obtaining fascinating structures that can be accessed by the variable coordination numbers from 2 to 6 and different conformations adopted by the silver ions [1–19]. The study of the coordination chemistry with the pyrazole-based ligands began in 1889 with a report of the polymeric [Ag(pz)]n complex [20].

    Five silver(I) compounds namely, [Ag(tdmpp)(NO3)]n (1), [Ag2(SeCN)2(tdmpp)]n (2), [Ag(tdmpp)]PF6 (3), [WS4Ag3Cl (tdmpp)1.5]·2CH3CN (4) and [WS4Ag4I2(tdmpp)]1.5CH3CN (5) were prepared by the reactions of 1,1,3,3-tetrakis(3,5-dimethyl-1-pyrazolyl)propane (tdmpp) with various silver(I) salts in order to investigate the impact of the variation of the inorganic anions on the structure of these complexes. In the chain structures of 1 and 2, each tdmpp ligand acts as a bridge between a pair of adjacent silver(I) centers. In the structure of 1, the nitrate ion act as a terminal, monodentate ligand, while in compound 2 the SeCN anion functions as bidentate-bridging ligand between two neighboring Ag(I) centers. The parallel adjacent chains in 1 and 2 are linked together by means of non-covalent interactions to generate two-dimensional structures. In contrast to the distorted AgN4O square–pyramidal structure of 1, in the structure of 2 each of the silver ions possesses a distorted tetrahedral with an AgN3Se coordination geometry. Crystals of compounds 3–5 were not suitable for X-ray diffraction studies.

    The distance between the centroid of triazole ring and Cl(4) atom is 3.361 Å, the angle Cd(2)-Cl(4)⋯centroid 170.6°.
