Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) are a family of new porous crystalline materials constructed from metal ions or clusters and polydentate organic ligands.1 Their large surface areas and pore volumes, versatile structures, tunable pore sizes, and controllable chemical environments make these materials suitable for a wide variety of applications, such as gas storage, drug delivery, adsorption and separation, sensing, magnetics, catalysis and so on.2–7 Among the above application areas, chemical sensing based on luminescent MOFs, especially lanthanide MOFs (Ln-MOFs), is by far the most widely explored in recent years owing to their optical properties, such as sharp and characteristic emissions, relatively long lifetimes, and high luminescence quantum yields.8 Ln-MOFs have been designed as luminescent sensors for the detection of various metal ions, anions, and small organic molecules including nitro explosives etc.9 In the past decades, prominent progress has been made in their unique applications in environmental monitoring and medical science. On the other hand, Ln3+ ions can also be introduced into non-lanthanide based MOFs via ion doping or exchange. The developed post-synthetic modifications (PSMs), which have been usually used to decorate organic ligands, further open opportunities for fabrication of Ln-MOFs.10 Taking advantage of their unique properties, Ln-MOFs could be the best candidate as luminescent probes.11 Fe3+ ions are widely distributed in water, vegetables, fruits and living animals, and play crucial roles in many biochemical processes. However, the deficiency and overload of Fe3+ ions will cause serious influences on human health, such as cirrhosis, anemia and agrypnia.12 Thus, rapid and sensitive detection of Fe3+ ions, especially for trace amounts of Fe3+ in water, is very significant.13

Herein, we reported six CdII-based MOFs constructed from solvothermal synthesis, in which Cd-MOF-4 can be doped with Eu3+ ions by post-synthetic modification and the resulting Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 can be used as a highly selective and sensitive probe for detection and recognition of Fe3+ ions in aqueous solution. In this paper, isophthalic acid-based linkers having different substituents at the 5-position (R-H2BDC, R = H, NO2, Me, OH and tBu) along with trigonal n-TPT ligands were employed to synthesize Cd-MOFs. The R-substituted isophthalic acid and n-TPT were chosen in this study to fabricate MOFs based on the following considerations: (1) the multidentate carboxylate groups can strongly bind metal ions to give extended networks;14 (2) the R-substituents on the 5-position with different electronic properties and shapes may also influence the coordination modes of the related carboxylate groups, yielding MOFs with different topological structures and properties, although the R-substituents on the 5-position don't coordinate to the metal centres;15 (3) 3-TPT/4-TPT with C3 symmetry and the pyridyl ring may rotate along the C–C bond to give a twisted conformation, which offers possibilities of accessing MOFs with new topologies.16 As part of our study on this system,17 in this paper, we here presented the synthesis and structures of six Cd-MOFs by using R-substituted isophthalic acid (R-H2BDC, R = H, NO2, Me, OH and tBu) along with the trigonal n-TPT ligands (Scheme 1). These compounds, composed of dimeric Cd2(OCO)2 units, are formulated as {[Cd(BDC)(4-TPT)]·1.5H2O}n (Cd-MOF-1), {[Cd(NO2-BDC)(4-TPT)]·2H2O}n (Cd-MOF-2), {[Cd(Me-BDC)(4-TPT)]·2H2O}n (Cd-MOF-3), {[Cd(HO-BDC)(4-TPT)]·2.5H2O}n (Cd-MOF-4), {[Cd(tBu-BDC)(4-TPT)2/3]·DMF}n (Cd-MOF-5), and {[Cd(BDC)(3-TPT)2/3]·3H2O}n (Cd-MOF-6), where H2BDC = isophthalic acid, NO2-BDC = 5-nitroisophthalic acid, Me-BDC = 5-methylisophthalic acid, OH-BDC = 5-hydroxyisophthalic acid, tBu-BDC = 5-tert-butylisophthalic acid, 3-TPT = 2,4,6-tris(3-pyridyl)-1,3,5-triazine and 4-TPT = 2,4,6-tris(4-pyridyl)-1,3,5-triazine. All the compounds are constructed from the Cd2(OCO)2 binuclear units, connected by the mixed R-H2BDC and n-TPT ligands into 3D frameworks. Cd-MOF-1, 2 and 3 are isostructural, in which the Cd2(OCO)2 units are bridged into 1D chains extending along the [1 1 0] and [−1 1 0] directions, respectively, and are then further interlinked by the 4-TPT ligands into 3D networks with a CdS net topology. Cd-MOF-4 exhibits a 3D framework with a pcu (α-Po) topology, in which the dimeric Cd2(OCO)2 units are firstly connected into 2D Cd(OH-BDC)n sheets, and further “pillared” by 4-TPT into a 3D network. In Cd-MOF-5 and 6, the Cd2(OCO)2 units are firstly linked into a 3D NbO net by tBu-BDC2−/BDC2− ligands, which are further accommodated by the size-matching trigonal 3-TPT/4-TPT ligands into a novel (3,8)-connected 3D net. Interestingly, Cd-MOF-4 with open hydroxyl and pyridyl groups can be functionalized by Eu3+ ions via post-synthetic modification and the concentration effect of doping Eu3+ ions is also studied. The resulting 0.001Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 phosphor shows a significant quenching effect and high selectivity (Stern–Volmer constant KSV = 3.06 × 104 M−1) for Fe3+ ions. Subsequently, Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 can be developed as a highly selective and sensitive probe for detection and recognition of Fe3+ ions in aqueous solution.

Results and discussion

Experimental section

Materials and methods

The starting reagents 5-substituted isophthalic acid (R-H2BDC, R = H, NO2, Me, OH and tBu), Cd(NO3)2·6H2O, Eu(NO3)3·6H2O, M(NO3)x (M = Na+, Ag+, Ca2+, Sr2+, Mn2+, Co2+, Ni2+, Cu2+, Zn2+, Ba2+, Pb2+, Cr3+ and Fe3+), N,N′-dimethylacetamide (DMA), N,N′-dimethylformamide (DMF), acetonitrile (CH3CN), ethyl glycol (EG), isopropanol (IPA) and HBF4 were obtained from commercial sources and used directly without further purification. The 3-TPT and 4-TPT ligands were synthesized according to a modified method.18

The FT-IR spectra were recorded in the range of 500–4000 cm−1 using KBr pellets on a Nicolet NEXUS 670 spectrophotometer. Elemental analysis (EA) was carried out on an Elementar Vario El III elemental analyzer. TG analyses (TGA) were carried out on a Mettler Toledo TGA/SDTA851 instrument under a flowing N2 atmosphere at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. The powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns were collected on a Bruker D8 Advance X-ray diffractometer (Bruker, Germany) at 35 kV and 25 mA with Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5418 Å) within the 2θ range of 5–40°. Simulation of the PXRD spectra was carried out using the single-crystal data and diffraction-crystal module of the Mercury (Hg) program available free of charge via the Internet at http://www.iucr.org. EDX (energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy) analyses were carried out with an S-4800 HITACHI scanning electron microscope. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) was performed using a Physical Electronics PHI-5702 spectrometer with a monochromatic Al K X-ray source (1486.6 eV) operated at 150 W. The shift of the binding energy due to the surface electrostatic charging was corrected using C 1s as an internal standard at 284.6 eV. The ultraviolet-visible (UV-vis) absorption spectra of the samples were recorded on an Agilent Cary 5000 spectrophotometer over a range of 200–800 nm with BaSO4 as a reflectance standard. Fluorescence spectra were recorded at room temperature on a Hitachi F-4500 luminescence spectrophotometer.

Preparation of Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4

Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 was prepared through a simple soaking process: Cd-MOF-4 (10 mg) was dispersed in Eu(NO3)3·6H2O aqueous solution with different concentrations (from 0.1 mM to 0.1 M), and the mixture was stirred for 24 h at room temperature. The solid was then collected by centrifugation and washed with H2O several times to remove the attached Eu3+ ions on the surface of the crystals. Finally, the resulting sample was then dried in air.

Luminescence sensing experiment

The chemical sensing of Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 for different metal ions was performed as follows: the freshly synthesized powder of Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 was ground into powder and dispersed in aqueous solutions containing 0.01 M of M(NO3)x (M = Na+, Ag+, Ca2+, Sr2+, Mn2+, Co2+, Ni2+, Cu2+, Zn2+, Ba2+, Pb2+, Cr3+ and Fe3+) which were then treated by ultrasonication for 30 min, and then the luminescence intensities of the resulting suspensions were recorded. The luminescence spectra of Fe3+ with different concentrations were also carried out in the same way.

X-ray crystallographic study

Diffraction intensity data were collected on a Bruker SMART APEX II diffractometer equipped with a CCD area detector and a graphite-monochromated Cu Kα radiation source (λ = 1.54178 Å) for Cd-MOF-1, Cd-MOF-2 and Cd-MOF-5, and the data of the other compounds were obtained using graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). Empirical absorption corrections were applied using the SADABS program.19 The structures were solved by the direct methods and refined on F2 by the full-matrix least-squares technique using the SHELX-2014 program package.20 All non-hydrogen atoms in the structures were refined with anisotropic displacement parameters, and all the hydrogen atoms attached to C atoms were placed in calculated positions and refined isotropically using the riding model. In Cd-MOF-2, 3, 5 and 6, the final structures have a large number of residual electron density peaks, and could not be crystallographically defined satisfactorily due to the disorder, so the SQUEEZE routine within the PLATON software package21 was applied to subtract the scattering contributions of the solvent molecules to the intensity data. The final refinement gave satisfactory results, and the framework structures were uninfluenced by the routine. According to the combination of EA, FT-IR analysis and TGA (Fig. S2†), the solvent molecules were proposed to be 1.5 H2O per formula for Cd-MOF-1, 2 H2O molecules per formula for Cd-MOF-2 and 3, 2.5 H2O per formula for Cd-MOF-4, one DMF molecule per formula for Cd-MOF-5 and 3H2O molecules per formula for Cd-MOF-6. The estimated numbers of the solvent molecules were also in approximate agreement with the number of the electrons in the void given by SQUEEZE analysis. Descriptions of crystal structures

Crystal structures of {[Cd(BDC)(4-TPT)]·1.5H2O}n (Cd-MOF-1), {[Cd(NO2-BDC)(4-TPT)]·2H2O}n (Cd-MOF-2) and {[Cd(Me-BDC)(4-TPT)]·2H2O}n (Cd-MOF-3)

Cd-MOF-1, 2 and 3 were easily synthesized by the reaction of H2BDC for Cd-MOF-1, NO2-H2BDC for Cd-MOF-2 and the Me-H2BDC ligand for Cd-MOF-3, the 4-TPT ligand and Cd2+ salt under solvothermal conditions with moderate yields (Scheme 2). According to the single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis, the whole structure of Cd-MOF-1 was isostructural with Cd-MOF-2 and Cd-MOF-3, except that the H2BDC organic ligand was replaced by NO2-H2BDC and Me-H2BDC ligands. Although the substituent groups on the 5-position of the H2BDC ligand were different, the coordination modes of the H2BDC-based ligands were similar. Thus, only the structure of Cd-MOF-1 was described in detail. The network of Cd-MOF-1 was similar to one CoII-based MOF reported by our group previously.17a It crystallized in the monoclinic C2/c space group and exhibited a 3D framework based on the centrosymmetric binuclear Cd2(OCO)2 unit. The asymmetric unit consisted of one CdII ion, one BDC2− anion, one 4-TPT ligand and 1.5 lattice H2O molecules. As shown in Fig. 1, the CdII ion was in a [N2O4] octahedral coordination sphere, ligated by two chelating carboxylate oxygen atoms (O1 and O2) and two bridging carboxylate oxygen atoms (O3A and O4B) from two symmetry-related BDC2− ligands in the equatorial plane, and two pyridyl nitrogen atoms (N1 and N2C) from two different 4-TPT ligands in the axial positions. The bond lengths of Cd–O (2.234(2) Å–2.424(2) Å for Cd-MOF-1, 2.261(3) Å–2.463(4) Å for Cd-MOF-2 and 2.236(3) Å–2.399(3) Å for Cd-MOF-3) and Cd–N (2.312(3)/2.340(3) Å for Cd-MOF-1, 2.301(3)/2.321(3) Å for Cd-MOF-2 and 2.326(3)/2.352(3) Å for Cd-MOF-3) were in the normal ranges of those observed in reported CdII compounds.22 The selected bond lengths and bond angles are listed in Table S1.† Due to the chelating coordination mode of a carboxylate group, which had a small bite angle (O1–Cd1–O2 = 54.59(8)° for Cd-MOF-1, 54.39(11)° for Cd-MOF-2 and 54.26(11)° for Cd-MOF-3) and dissymmetric Cd–O bonds (2.324(3) and 2.424(2) Å for Cd-MOF-1, 2.338(3) Å and 2.463(4) Å for Cd-MOF-2, and 2.367(3) Å and 2.399(4) Å for Cd-MOF-3), the coordination geometry of the CdII center was somewhat distorted from the octahedral one. The two carboxylate groups of the BDC2− ligand adopted two coordination modes: a μ-1,3-OCO bidentate and a chelating carboxylate, and each BDC2− ligand connected three CdII ions: chelating a CdII ion at one end and binding another two CdII ions in a μ-1,3-OCO bidentate fashion at the other end.

Two symmetry equivalent CdII ions were bridged by two μ-1,3-carboxylate groups from two BDC2− ligands into a centrosymmetric dimeric Cd2(OCO)2 unit, in which the Cd⋯Cd distance was 4.006(3) Å (4.080(3) Å for Cd-MOF-2, 4.093(1) Å for Cd-MOF-3). The dimeric Cd2(OCO)2 unit was further linked by the benzoate backbone ligands to form a 1D ladder-like [Cd(BDC)]n chain (Fig. 2a). As demonstrated in Fig. 2b, the 1D chains extended along two directions: [1 1 0] and [−1 1 0], which were almost perpendicular to each other, and just due to this arrangement fashion, the 4-TPT ligands could link these 1D [Cd(BDC)]n chains into the final 3D network. Compared with two previously reported CoII-MOFs, in which 2D Co(R-BDC)n sheets (where R = NO2 and Me) were obtained through the linkage of CoII ions and R-BDC2− ligands,17a the CdII ions linked by R-BDC2− ligands (where R = NO2 for Cd-MOF-2 and Me for Cd-MOF-3) yielded 1D [Cd(R-BDC)]n, showing the affection of metal ion on the self-assembly of MOFs. From the topological point of view, if each dimeric Cd2(OCO)2 unit is connected to four neighbouring ones through four BDC2− and four 4-TPT ligands, it could be considered as a 4-connected node, and the BDC2− and 4-TPT ligands could be simplified as linear linkers. The whole structure could be reduced as a 4-connected CdS net with a point (Schläfli) symbol of 65·8 (Fig. 2c).23 In Cd-MOF-1, 2 and 3, the 4-TPT ligand acted as a V-shaped ditopic ligand to coordinate two CdII ions through two pyridyl rings to form 1D zigzag chains with the three pyridyl rings and triazine ring almost being co-planar. The remaining uncoordinated pyridyl group, as an unbound arm, may serve as the active site for the further post-synthetic modifications. The phase purity of the bulk product was confirmed by PXRD experiments. As depicted in Fig. S3 of the ESI,† the experimental PXRD patterns of bulk samples for compounds 1–3 were in good agreement with their corresponding simulated ones from the single crystal data, indicating that the phase purity of the bulk samples was obtained.

Crystal structure of {[Cd(OH-BDC)(4-TPT)]·2.5H2O}n (Cd-MOF-4)

Based on the above experimental results, the H2BDC ligand was replaced by the OH-H2BDC ligand with a hydroxyl group on the 5-position of H2BDC. Under similar reaction conditions, we separated single crystals of compound 4 (Cd-MOF-4). Cd-MOF-4 also crystallized in the monoclinic C2/c space group and exhibited a 3D framework. In the asymmetric unit, there were one CdII ion, one OH-BDC anion, one 4-TPT ligand and two and a half lattice H2O molecules. As shown in Fig. 3a, the CdII ion assumed a [N2O4] six-coordinated octahedral geometry. The equatorial positions were defined by four carboxylate oxygen atoms (O1, O2, O3A and O4B) from four different HO-BDC2− ligands with Cd–O bond lengths being in the wide range of 2.211(3) Å–2.459(3) Å, whereas the axial positions were occupied by two nitrogen atoms (N1 and N2C) from two different 4-TPT ligands with Cd–N bond lengths being 2.327(3) and 2.353(3) Å. The other selected bond lengths and bond angles are listed in Table S1.† Similar to the three Cd-MOFs above, the coordination geometry around the CdII center was highly distorted from the octahedral one owing to the chelating coordination mode of a carboxylate group (O1–C1–O2), with a small bite angle (O1–Cd1–O2 = 54.51(10)°) and dissymmetric Cd–O bonds [2.326(3) and 2.459(3) Å]. Two neighbouring CdII ions were doubly connected by two μ-1,3-carboxylate groups to give a centrosymmetric dimeric Cd2(OCO)2 unit with the Cd⋯Cd distance being 4.052(3) Å. Each dimeric Cd2(OCO)2 unit linked four neighbouring ones by four benzoate backbones of OH-BDC2− ligands to produce 2D undulated grid-like Cd(OH-BDC)n sheets, propagating along the [1 0 1] plane (Fig. 3b). Meanwhile, the 4-TPT ligand served as a V-shaped ditopic linker to coordinate two CdII ions yielding 1D Cd(4-TPT)n chains through two pyridyl nitrogen atoms (N1 and N2), by which the 2D Cd(OH-BDC)n sheets were “pillared” into the final 3D framework (Fig. 3c). Consequently, each dimeric Cd2(OCO)2 unit connected six neighbouring Cd2(OCO)2 units through four OH-BDC2− and 4-TPT ligands, and thus could be considered as a 6-connected node. If both HO-BDC2− and 4-TPT ligands could be viewed as linear linkers, the 2D undulated Cd(OH-BDC)n sheet could be considered as a 4-connected sql net, and the whole 3D structure would be reduced into a 6-connected net with a pcu topology (α-Po) and the symbol (Schläfli) point was 412·63 (Fig. 3d).23 Interestingly, the uncoordinated hydroxyl groups (–OH) on the OH-BDC2− ligand and the pyridyl groups of the 4-TPT ligand directed towards the cavities. As we all know, the –OH group was a Lewis basic site, which could not only donate its lone pair electrons during the post-synthetic modification, leading to electron transfer, but was also supposedly available for interactions with Lewis acidic metal ions.

Crystal structures of {[Cd(tBu-BDC)(4-TPT)2/3]·DMF}n (Cd-MOF-5) and {[Cd(BDC)(3-TPT)2/3]·3H2O}n (Cd-MOF-6)

When the reaction was carried out with tBu-H2BDC as a dicarboxylate ligand, in which the – tBu substituent with a larger steric hindrance and electron-donating effect on the 5-position may further impact the assembly processes and structures, we isolated single crystals of Cd-MOF-5.

X-ray structure determination revealed that Cd-MOF-5 crystallized in a trigonal crystal system with the R space group and exhibited a 3D framework constructed from the dimeric Cd2(OCO)2 unit. In Cd-MOF-5, the asymmetric unit consisted of one CdII ion, one tBu-BDC anion, and 2/3 4-TPT ligand. The selected bond distances and angles are listed in Table S1.† As shown in Fig. 4a, the CdII ion was situated in the [N2O4] coordination sphere ligated by two bridging carboxylate oxygen atoms (O1B and O2), two chelating carboxylate oxygen atoms from two different carboxylate groups (O3C and O4C) in the equatorial plane, and two pyridyl nitrogen atoms (N1 and N2) from two 4-TPT ligands in the axial positions. The coordination geometry of CdII was highly distorted from the octahedral one due to the asymmetric chelating mode (O3–C8–O4), as evidenced by the large difference in Cd–O bond lengths [2.252 (3) Å vs. 2.485(3) Å] and a small bite angle (54.84(1)°). The bond lengths of Cd–O (2.229(3) Å–2.485(3) Å) and Cd–N (2.309(3) Å/2.400(3) Å) were comparable to those of CdII-based MOFs reported in the literature.22

As presented in the compounds reported above, two equivalent CdII ions were also bridged by two carboxylate groups, giving rise to a centrosymmetric dimeric Cd2(OCO)2 unit with a Cd⋯Cd distance of 3.981 (1) Å. Each dimeric Cd2(OCO)2 unit further linked four ones through four tBu-BDC2− ligands in three directions and extended into a 3D framework. Topologically, if the dimeric Cd2(OCO)2 motif could be reduced into a 4-connected node, the resulting 3D framework would be viewed as an NbO net with a 64·82 topology.23 Similar to a reported NbO net elsewhere, there were 1D nanotubes down the c axis with radii of about 6.24 Å (the shortest distances between the cavity centers and any framework atoms), and the tBu groups protruded toward the center of the nanotubes, which offered opportunity for the accommodation of trigonal 4-TPT ligands. Different from the reported compounds above, the 4-TPT ligand in Cd-MOF-5 served as a three-way linker, and adopted the μ3-coordination mode to coordinate with three CdII centers via its three outer pyridyl donors, filled in the 1D nanotubes. The solvent accessible free volume in Cd-MOF-5 was 41.8% (5843.3 Å3 out of the 13964.8(10) Å3 unit cell volume) as obtained by PLATON calculations. Although the electronic residues in the free spaces could not be crystallographically modeled, the voids are assumed to be occupied by heavily disordered DMF guest molecules, according to the IR, TGA and EA data.21 As shown in Fig. 5, in the final 3D framework, each 4-TPT ligand connected three dimeric Cd2(OCO)2 units and each Cd2(OCO)2 unit connected four dimeric units and two pairs of 4-TPT ligands correspondingly, and thus could be treated as 3- and 8-connected nodes, respectively. Topologically, the overall 3D structure of Cd-MOF-5 could be therefore described as an interesting (3,8)-connected net with the point (Schläfli) symbol of (412·610·86)3(43)4 according to the ToposPro calculations.23

Similar to Cd-MOF-1, when the 4-TPT auxiliary ligand was replaced by 3-TPT, we obtained pale-orange single crystals of Cd-MOF-6. X-ray structure determination revealed that Cd-MOF-6 crystallized in a trigonal crystal system with the Rc space group and was isostructural with Cd-MOF-5 (Fig. 4b), so only Cd-MOF-5 was described in detail. The phase purity of compounds 5 and 6 was also confirmed by PXRD experiments. As depicted in Fig. S3,† the experimental PXRD patterns of bulk samples for compounds 5 and 6 were consistent with their simulated curves from the single crystal data, showing good phase purity.

Given that MOFs including d10 ions and conjugate ligands have interesting fluorescence properties and potential applications in the optical field,24 the solid state luminescence spectra of Cd-MOF-4 as well as the free HO-H2BDC and 4-TPT ligands were measured at room temperature. When excited at 340 nm, the free OH-H2BDC ligand revealed strong emission at 409 nm, and the 4-TPT ligand showed intense emission at 448 nm with two broad shoulder bands centered at 550 nm and 590 nm, which could be tentatively assigned to the π*–π and/or π*–n electronic transitions of the aromatic rings. Compared with free OH-H2BDC and 4-TPT ligands, Cd-MOF-4 exhibited a maximum absorption peak at 459 nm under similar excitation (Fig. 6). Considering that the CdII ion was difficult to oxidize or reduce because of its d10 configuration, the blue emission of Cd-MOF-4 might be likely ascribed to the coordination interactions to the CdII centers, which changed the rigidity, conformation and conjugation of the ligands and further restricted the deformation of the ligand and the cooperative effect of intra-ligand π–π* transition and ligand-to-metal transfer (LMCT).25 The blue color was also proved by the Commission International de I'Eclairage (CIE) chromaticity coordinate (x = 0.1881, y = 0.2224) (Table 2, Fig. 7b), which was close to the saturated blue emission with a CIE chromaticity coordinate of (0.14, 0.08), and the optical photo under laboratory UV light excitation (365 nm).26

Considering the structural feature of Cd-MOF-4, we selected it as a host to encapsulate Eu3+ ions for the preparation of a fluorescent phosphor by post-synthetic modification. As described in the Experimental section, the freshly synthesized Cd-MOF-4 was soaked in Eu(NO3)3 aqueous solution with different concentrations (from 0.1 mM to 0.1 M) for 24 h, and Eu3+ ions were introduced into the cavities of Cd-MOF-4. As expected, after post-synthetic modification with Eu(NO3)3 solution, the emission spectra of Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 exhibited characteristic transitions of Eu3+ ions when excited at 330 nm. Four sharp emission peaks located at about 580, 593, 617 and 652 nm were observed, which can be assigned to 5D0 → 57F0, 5D0 → 57F1, 5D0 → 57F2, and 5D0 → 57F3 of Eu3+ ions, respectively.27 Upon excitation with a UV lamp (365 nm), the Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 sample showed bright red light, which was easily observed by the naked eye. These facts suggested that the Eu3+ ions have successfully been incorporated into the cavities of Cd-MOF-4 after the post-synthetic modification process, forming the Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 phosphor, which was sensitized efficiently by energy transferring from organic ligands. In addition, we also investigated the effect of the Eu3+ concentration on luminescence, thus the emission spectra of xEu3+@Cd-MOF-4 (x was from 0.1 mM to 0.1 M) were collected under similar excitation. As shown in Fig. 7a, the intensity of characteristic Eu3+peaks increased with the increase of Eu3+ concentration and reached a maximum value at 0.001 M Eu3+ concentration, and correspondingly the ligand-based emission at 459 nm gradually decreased. The increase of emission intensity was mainly due to the increased number of luminescence centers, the restrained ligand-centered transition and ligand-to-metal transfer of Cd-MOF-4 and the efficient energy transfer between the electron-conjugated ligands and Eu3+ ions via the “antenna effect”.28 In the energy transfer system, Cd-MOF-4 served as an energy donor and Eu3+ acted as an energy acceptor. With the further increase of Eu3+ concentration, the emission intensity decreased owing to the concentration quenching from the energy migration between identical luminescent centers. Thus, 0.001Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 was selected as a fluorescent phosphor. Of course, as a contrast, attempts to functionalize the other Cd-MOFs with an uncoordinated pyridyl group (Cd-MOF-1, 2 and 3), only with uncoordinated pyridyl nitrogen atoms, using Eu(NO3)3 solution via post-synthetic modification failed. As shown in Fig. S5,† only emissions of Cd-MOF-1, 2 and 3 were observed, suggesting the synergistic effect of uncoordinated hydroxyl groups in the formation of the Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 phosphor. From thUV-vis spectrum, Cd-MOF-4 showed one strong absorption band centered at 230 nm and a shoulder at 339 nm, arising from the intraligand π–π* transition and ligand-to-metal transfer. After doping with Eu3+ ions, the shoulder for the Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 sample showed a significant blue-shift of about 10 nm, except the strong absorption at 230 nm (Fig. S6†). According to the structural characteristics, the uncoordinated hydroxyl group of OH-BDC2− and pyridyl group of 4-TPT, directed into the cavities of the framework, were supposedly available for interactions with Eu3+ ions. These results also confirmed that Cd-MOF-4 was an excellent chromophore for sensitization of Eu3+ ions, and 0.001Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 (hereinafter referred to as Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4) could serve as a fluorescent phosphor.

To verify further the considerable synergistic role of the uncoordinated hydroxyl groups in the formation of Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4, EDX and X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) characterization techniques were conducted. To our delight, the Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 product was composed of the C, N, O, Cd and Eu elements, and the Eu element was uniformly distributed on the surface of Cd-MOF-4 from the EDX mapping images, showing that the Eu3+ ions were successfully doped to the Cd-MOF-4 framework (Fig. 8a). Meanwhile, XPS of Cd-MOF-4 and the Eu(NO3)3 salt was also carried out for comparison. As shown in Fig. 8b, the binding energy peaks for Cd 2p1/2 and Cd 2p3/2 were mainly located at about 405 eV and 411.7 eV with a spin energy separation of 6.7 eV, which was in agreement with previous reports,29 and the Cd 2p peaks moved to higher band energies (405.3 eV and 412.0 eV) after doping with Eu3+ ions, showing the synergistic influence. Meanwhile, after the doping with Eu3+ ions, the peaks for O1s and N1s also shifted to higher band energies by about 0.4 eV,29 which illustrated the weak interaction of Cd-MOF-4 and Eu3+ ions in Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4, proving further the aforementioned synergistic effect of hydroxyl and pyridyl groups.

Detection of Fe3+ cations

In light of the excellent luminescence of the Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 phosphor, we examine the potential chemical sensing of Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 for different metal ions. The crystalline material of Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 was suspended in aqueous solutions, and then various aqueous solutions containing the same concentration of M(NO3)x·nH2O (M = Ag+, Na+, Mg2+, Ba2+, Co2+, Cr3+, Cu2+, Mn2+, Ni2+, Pb2+, Zn2+ and Fe3+) were separately dropped into the suspension above and then were treated by ultrasonication for 30 min, giving the diverse metal ion-incorporated suspensions of Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4. Then, the luminescence intensities of Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 in the presence of different metal ions were recorded at 617 nm (Fig. 9). Interestingly, the luminescence intensity of Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 largely relied on the identity of metal ions. Except for the Na+ ion, the other metal ions exhibited varying degrees of quenching effect compared with the blank Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 aqueous solution, and the Co2+, Cu2+, Cr3+ and Fe3+ ions displayed a significantly higher quenching effect, especially for the Fe3+ ions, which had the most significant effect. The result indicated the high selectivity of Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 for the detection and specific recognition of Fe3+ ions in aqueous solution by means of luminescence quenching (turn-off).

Encouraged by the excellent sensitivity of the doped Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 sensor to Fe3+ ions in aqueous solution, we explored the quenching effect of Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 for Fe3+ ions in detail, and the concentration dependent fluorescence intensities of Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 were also obtained with different concentrations of Fe3+ ions (from 1 × 10−6 M to 1 × 10−2 M). A solution of Fe3+ (0.001 M) was gradually dropped into the suspension of the selected Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 and treated by ultrasonication for 30 min, and then the luminescence intensity was recorded. As depicted in Fig. 10a, the emission intensity gradually decreased with the increase of concentration of Fe3+ ions and the quenching efficiency reached almost 100% when the Fe3+ concentration was increased to 0.005 M according to the luminescence intensity monitored at 617 nm. The relationship between the quenching efficiency and concentration of Fe3+ ions could be quantitatively evaluated by the Stern–Volmer (S–V) equation of I0/I = 1 + KSV × [Fe3+], where I0 and I are the luminescence intensity of Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 before and after the addition of Fe3+ ions, respectively, KSV is the quenching constant, and [Fe3+] represents the concentration of Fe3+ ions.30 At low concentration, there existed a good linear correlation (R = 0.9887) between the quenching efficiency and the amount of Fe3+ (Fig. 10b). The KSV value was calculated to be 3.06 × 104 M−1 based on the experimental data, indicating the strong luminescence quenching effect of Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4,31 and these results revealed that Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 could be a potential sensor with high selectivity for Fe3+ ions in aqueous environments.

In order to explore the possible application of Cd-MOF-4, the purity and stability of the material were also investigated. As shown in Fig. 11, the PXRD pattern of the as-prepared Cd-MOF-4 was in good agreement with the simulated PXRD pattern from the single crystal X-ray diffraction data, indicating the high purity of Cd-MOF-4. After introducing Eu3+ ions into Cd-MOF-4, the PXRD curve of Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 still matched well with the synthesized Cd-MOF-4, suggesting that the crystalline integrity of the material remained unchanged after the incorporation of Eu3+ ions. Meanwhile, the used Fe3+@Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 sample after being washed with H2O for several times was also tested using PXRD, and no significant difference was found in the PXRD patterns of the Cd-MOF-4 and Fe3+@Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 samples, showing its good stability and recyclable potential and that the luminescence quenching did not result from the structural collapse of Cd-MOF-4. Since the emission energy of Eu3+ was mainly transferred from the organic ligands during excitation, and the energy transfer processes may be affected by guest ions during the luminescence sensing process, the incorporation of Fe3+ ions into the cavities of Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 may perturb the electronic structure of the ligand and thus restrain the energy transfer efficiently from the aromatic ligands to the Eu3+ center, inducing the luminescence quenching. Although the mechanism was similar to those studies for sensing Cu2+ ions, the deep understanding of such quenching effects was still unclear.32

Conclusions

In summary, by regulating the substituted functional groups on the 5-position of isophthalic acid, we isolate six Cd-based MOFs with n-TPT as auxiliary ligands, and all the compounds are constructed from dimeric Cd2(OCO)2 units, connected by mixed 5-substituted isophthalic acid and n-TPT ligands. Cd-MOF-1, 2 and 3 are isostructural, in which the dimeric Cd2(OCO)2 units are bridged into 1D chains extending along two directions ([1 1 0] and [−1 1 0]), which are further interlinked by 4-TPT ligands into 3D networks with a CdS net topology. Cd-MOF-4 exhibits a 3D framework with a pcu (α-Po) topology based on the dimeric Cd2(OCO)2 unit. In Cd-MOF-5 and 6, the Cd2(OCO)2 units are firstly linked into a 3D NbO net by tBu-BDC2−/BDC2− ligands, which are further accommodated by the trigonal 3-TPT/4-TPT ligands into a novel (3,8)-connected net. Interestingly, Cd-MOF-4 with open hydroxyl groups can be functionalized by Eu3+ ions via post-synthetic modification. The resulting Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 phosphor shows a significant quenching effect upon introduction of Fe3+ ions and high selectivity for Fe3+ ions. Subsequently, Eu3+@Cd-MOF-4 can be developed as a highly selective and sensitive probe for detection and recognition of Fe3+ ions in aqueous solution.
