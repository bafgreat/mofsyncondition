Introduction

The construction of coordination polymers (CPs) or metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) from metal ions or metal clusters with multifunctional organic ligands is one of the most active areas of materials research. The intense interest in these materials is driven by their intrinsic architectural beauty as well as diverse applications such as molecular magnets, photoluminescence, adsorption, and catalysis.1 Lanthanide ions are attracting much attention due to their unique optical and magnetic properties arising from 4f electrons,2 which are much different from those of transition metal ions. Lanthanide-based MOFs can exhibit diverse structures resulting from the high coordination number and flexible coordination geometry of lanthanide ions.3 To date, lanthanide carboxylate frameworks have afforded the most diversity in this area because of their chemical stability and appropriate connectivity.4

2,5-Dihydroxy-1,4-benzenedicarboxylic acid (H4-DHBDC) ligand contains two sets of oxygen donors: the carboxylate oxygen binds strongly to the metal center, whereas the hydroxyl oxygen does so weakly. Thus it appears to be an appropriate candidate to generate extended lanthanide-based frameworks. When the two carboxylic groups are deprotonated, the dianionic H2-DHBDC2−ligand can function as a bridging ligand to two, three, four, or even six metal centers (Scheme 1a–1f). The carboxylate oxygen can form a chelating bond to a metal center with the adjacent hydroxyl oxygen. The tetraanionic DHBDC4−ligand with multifunctional coordination sites can bind to more metal ions as shown in Scheme 1g–1i. Consequently, the H4-DHBDC ligand is an effective component in the design of coordination compounds. Many frameworks have been isolated by the combination of the H4-DHBDC ligand and d-block transition metals such as Zn(ii), Ni(ii), Co(ii), and Cu(ii) or main group metals such as Mg(ii), Ca(ii), and Sr(ii).5 Very recently, we reported a series of isomorphous lanthanide frameworks based on the dianionic H2-DHBDC2−ligand synthesized under solvothermal conditions.6 These isomorphous compounds have 3D frameworks with 1D open channels that accommodate the coordinated and guest DMF molecules. In addition, the H4-DHBDC ligand has been recently used for the construction of a gadolinium–organic framework under hydrothermal conditions. The gadolinium compound is a 2D pillared-bilayer network where the organic ligand existed as dianionic and tetraanionic ions.7

In this paper, we report six novel lanthanide–organic frameworks with the H4-DHBDC ligand, namely, [Ln(H2-DHBDC)1.5(H2O)2]n (Ln = La (1) and Pr (2)), {[Nd(H2-DHBDC)1.5(H2O)3](H2O)}n (3), {[Eu(H2-DHBDC)(NO3)(H2O)4](H2O)2}n (4), and {[Ln2(H2-DHBDC)2(DHBDC)0.5(H2O)3](H2O)4}n (Ln = Gd (5) and Dy (6)). The crystal structures of all compounds have been characterized. Interestingly, the hydroxyl group acts as a secondary group to modify the structural types of theses compounds. The magnetic properties of Pr (2), Nd (3), Gd (5), and Dy (6) compounds have been studied. Additionally, IR, thermogravimetric analyses, and powder X-ray diffraction of these compounds have been carried out.

Results and discussion

Structural description

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analyses revealed three types of solid structures for compounds 1–4: 3D frameworks (La (1) and Pr (2)), a 2D layer of Nd (3), and a 1D chain of Eu (4). The four compounds exclusively contain the dianionic H2-DHBDC2−ligands with deprotonated carboxylate groups. The lanthanide ions in these four compounds are nine-coordinated. In the 3D frameworks of La (1) and Pr (2), each lanthanide ion is surrounded by five H2-DHBDC2−ligands while the lanthanide ion is coordinated to four and two H2-DHBDC2−ligands in the 2D layer of Nd (3) and 1D chain of Eu (4), respectively. Compounds Gd (5) and Dy (6) constructed from the infinite rod-shaped lanthanide–carboxylate/hydroxyl secondary building units (SBUs) display distinct structural types different from those of compounds 1–4. Compounds 1 and 2 were obtained in the absence of sodium hydroxide, while compounds 3–6 were isolated in the presence of sodium hydroxide aqueous solution for adjusting the pH value of the reaction mixture, which indicates the reaction pH value may make the structures diverse. All these structures are different from those recently reported 3D lanthanide frameworks based on the dinuclear Ln2(CO2)6 SBUs prepared under solvothermal conditions,6 and the 2D pillared-bilayer network of Gd(iii).7 Each lanthanide ion in compounds Gd (5) and Dy (6) are eight-coordinated. Compounds Gd (5) and Dy (6) contain the dianionic H2-DHBDC2− and tetraanionic DHBDC4−ligands. For these structural types, it should be noted that the hydroxyl group as a secondary group is coordinated to the lanthanide ions in the 3D frameworks, while in the 2D layer and 1D chain, the hydroxyl group is not associated with the lanthanide ion. Therefore the secondary hydroxyl group modifies the structural types of these compounds.

Crystal structures of [Ln(H2-DHBDC)1.5(H2O)2]n (Ln = La (1) and Pr (2))

Compounds 1 and 2 are isomorphous, and the structure of Pr (2) is described here representatively. The asymmetric unit of 2 contains one Pr(iii) ion, one and a half H2-DHBDC2− dianions, and two coordinated water molecules. The Pr(iii) ion is nine-coordinated by five carboxylate oxygen atoms and two hydroxyl oxygen atoms from five surrounding H2-DHBDC2−ligands, and two water molecules (Fig. 1), with Pr–O bond lengths varying from 2.435(5) to 2.641(5) Å (see Table 2). The two independent H2-DHBDC2−ligands exhibit different coordinating modes. One acts as a tridentate ligand (Scheme 1f) bridging three Pr(iii) ions with a bidentate carboxylate group and a monodentate carboxylate group, and one monodentate hydroxyl group. The other displays a tetradentate ligand with four monodentate carboxylate oxygen atoms and two monodentate hydroxyl groups (Scheme 1i), wherein the carboxylate oxygen and the adjacent hydroxyl oxygen form a chelating bond to a lanthanide center. As shown in Fig. S1 (ESI†), the tridentate H2-DHBDC2− ligands link the Pr(iii) ions to generate a 2D layer propagating along the crystallographic bc plane. The 2D layers are further connected by the tetradentate H2-DHBDC2−ligands to give a 3D framework as illustrated in Fig. 2. Careful examination indicates the 3D framework has 1D tubular channels (Fig. 3a). As depicted in Fig. 3b, a pair of praseodymium (Pr1 and Pr(1C)) ions are bridged by a pair of carboxylate groups (O(7)–C(12)–O(8)) to give a dimeric Pr2(CO2)2 unit, which is further bridged by the O(1)–C(7)–O(2) carboxylate bridges to give a 1D tubular channel. The 1D tubular channel has an approximate dimension of 8.2 × 8.9 Å2 (based on the metal centers). The coordinated water molecules point to the channels and fill the channels preventing interpenetration.

Crystal structure of {[Nd(H2-DHBDC)1.5(H2O)3](H2O)}n (3)

Compound Nd (3) features a 2D layered structure based on a dinuclear Nd2(CO2)2 unit. As shown in Fig. 4, each Nd(iii) ion is nine-coordinated by six carboxylate oxygen atoms from four surrounding H2-DHBDC2−ligands and two water molecules. The H2-DHBDC2−ligands are coordinated to the Nd(iii) centers in two distinct modes: one is using its bidentate μ2-η2:η1 and monodentate carboxylate groups to bridge three Nd(iii) ions (Scheme 1e), the other employs its two chelating carboxylate groups to bridge two Nd(iii) ions (Scheme 1b). The hydroxyl groups of H2-DHBDC2−ligands are not involved in coordination but are engaged in hydrogen bonding. As depicted in Fig. 5, two Nd(iii) ions are linked by two carboxylate oxygen atoms to form a centrosymmetric [Nd2(η2-O)2] core with Nd–O distances of 2.458(3) and 2.706(3) Å, and an O(2)–Nd(1)–O(2A) bond angle of 64.16(9)°. The Nd⋯Nd separation is 4.37 Å in the [Nd2(η2-O)2] unit. The [Nd2(η2-O)2] units are connected by the tridentate H2-DHBDC2−ligands to form a 1D ladder structure. Within the 1D ladder, the adjacent benzene rings are parallel and interact with each other in an off-set fashion with a face-to-face distance of 3.24 Å and a centre-to-centre distance of 3.75 Å, respectively. The adjacent 1D ladders are further linked by the bidentate H2-DHBDC2−ligands to give a 2D layer (Fig. 5). Extensive hydrogen bonds are observed between the 2D layers with the O⋯O distances varying from 2.569(4) to 2.949(5) Å (Table S1†), which resulted in a 3D supramolecular structure, as depicted in Fig. S2 (ESI†).

Crystal structure of {[Eu(H2-DHBDC)(NO3)(H2O)4](H2O)2}n (4)

Compound Eu (4) features a 1D zigzag chain. As shown in Fig. 6, each Eu(iii) ion is coordinated to nine oxygen atoms, four from the carboxylate groups of two H2-DHBDC2−ligands, four from four water molecules, and one from a nitrate anion. The H2-DHBDC2− ligands link the Eu(iii) ions through the carboxylate groups with chelating fashion (Scheme 1b) to form an infinite zigzag chain along the b direction, as illustrated in Fig. 7. The chain has a crystallographically imposed m symmetry, in which the Eu(iii) ion, nitrate anion, and coordinated water molecules of O(5) and O(6) are located at the mirror plane (Fig. 7). Within the chain the adjacent benzene planes are nearly perpendicular to each other with a dihedral angle of around 89.7°. The Eu–O distances range from 2.381(2) to 2.549(2) Å. The neighboring 1D chains are hydrogen bonded to each other via hydrogen bonds between the coordinated water molecules and nitrate oxygen atoms and carboxylate oxygen atoms with the O⋯O distances of 2.891(4) and 2.940(2) Å, to form a 2D corrugated layered structure along the bc plane (Fig. 7). The 2D layer is further extended into a 3D supramolecular architecture through the hydrogen-bonding interactions between the coordinated water molecule and the nitrate oxygen atom with the O⋯O distance of 2.905(3) Å, as shown in Fig. S3 (Table S1, ESI†).

Crystal structures of {[Ln2(H2-DHBDC)2(DHBDC)0.5(H2O)3](H2O)4}n (Ln = Gd (5) and Dy (6))

Compounds 5 and 6 are isomorphous, and the selected structure of Dy (6) is described here in detail. The structure of Dy (6) features infinite ⋯O–Dy–O–Dy–O⋯ chains linked by the H2-DHBDC2− and DHBDC4−ligands. There are two independent eight-coordinated Dy(iii) ions in the asymmetric unit. The coordination sphere of the Dy(1) ion consists of five carboxylate oxygen atoms from one DHBDC4− and three H2-DHBDC2−ligands, one hydroxyl oxygen (O(15)) from the second DHBDC4−ligand, and two coordinated water molecules (Fig. 8). Dy(2) ion is coordinated by six carboxylate oxygen atoms and a hydroxyl oxygen atoms O(15) from one DHBDC4− and five H2-DHBDC2−ligands, and one water oxygen atom (O(18)) (Fig. 8). The Dy–O distances vary from 2.281(3) to 2.652(3) Å (Table 2), and the longest Dy–O distance is associated with the bidentate η2 carboxylate oxygen atom (O(13)). The O–Dy–O bond angles vary from 51.45(9)° to 153.31(11)°. It is noted that in the asymmetric unit of Dy (6), there are two independent half-occupied and one full-occupied dianionic H2-DHBDC2−ligands, and one half-occupied tetraanionic DHBDC4−ligand. The ligand with coordinated hydroxyl oxygen atom (O(15)) is the tetraanionic DHBDC4−ligand. All half-occupied ligands are located inversion centers. The dianionic H2-DHBDC2−ligands display two different types of coordination modes with two bidentate carboxylate groups in bridging four metal ions (Scheme 1g), or with one chelating carboxylate group and one bidentate carboxylate group to bind three metal ions (Scheme 1d). The tetraanionic DHBDC4−ligand with the deprotonated hydroxyl groups exhibits an uncommon coordination mode using its two η2hydroxyl oxygen atoms and two μ2-η2:η1 carboxylate groups to bridge six metal ions (Scheme 1o). Dy1 and Dy2 ions are bridged by the hydroxyl oxygen O15 and two carboxylate groups to form a dimeric Dy2 unit, which is further bridged by the carboxylate groups to form a infinite metal–carboxylate/hydroxyl secondary building block (Fig. 9). This can be described as an infinite rod-shaped building block.5a The rod-shaped metal–carboxylate/hydroxyl building block contains the infinite 1D ⋯O–Dy–O–Dy–O⋯ chain built by the dysprosium ions and the η2 bridging hydroxyl oxygen (O(15)) and carboxylate oxygen (O(13)), as depicted in Fig. 9. The Dy(1)⋯Dy(2) and Dy(1)⋯Dy(2A) distances are 3.9596(3) and 4.639(1) Å, respectively. Each rod-shaped SBU is linked to four adjacent rod SBUs through the benzene linkers resulted in a 3D framework (Fig. 9) with pcu type rod packing, reminiscent of its analogues based on the terephthalate ligand.8 The lanthanide–terephthalate frameworks commonly contain infinite rod-shaped lanthanide–carboxylate SBUs, which are linked by the benzene moieties to give 3D structures. However in the present compound, hydroxyl groups sited on the benzene ring of the DHBDC4−ligand, are involved in the construction the rod-shaped lanthanide–carboxylate SBUs, which is absent in the lanthanide–terephthalate frameworks. As a result, the present 3D framework has 1D triangular channels (Fig. 9), which is much different from those 1D rhombic channels in lanthanide–terephthalate frameworks.8 The 1D triangular channels extend parallel to the crystallographic a direction, in which the coordinated molecules protrude and solvent molecules reside in and interact with the host framework through hydrogen bonds with the O⋯O distances varying from 2.582(5) to 3.149(10) Å (Table S1†).

IR spectra, thermogravimetric analysis and powder X-ray diffraction

The infrared spectra of compounds 1–6 show similar main characteristic peaks since they all contain the same organic ligand. Characteristic peaks of water and/or hydroxyl groups in organic ligands are shown broad absorption bands in the range 3400–3465 cm−1. The strong bands at 1528–1646 cm−1 and 1346–1490 cm−1 for all compounds are characteristic of the asymmetric and symmetric stretching bands of carboxylate groups, respectively.

To examine the thermal stability of these compounds and their structural variation as a function of the temperature, thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) were performed on crystalline samples of these materials (Fig. S4, ESI†). Compounds La (1) and Pr (2) will show similar thermal behavior and therefore only compound Pr (2) is discussed, as a representative for their thermal behavior. The TGA trace of Pr (2) displays a small gradual weight loss starting at 100 °C and then a sharp weight loss occurred at 170 °C. The total observed weight loss between 100 to 235 °C is 7.51%, which is attributed to the release of two coordinated water molecules (theoretical 7.68%). A small gradual mass loss continues subsequently and the main framework starts to decompose at 300 °C. Compound Nd (3) displays a continual weight loss starting from 75 °C and a sharp weight loss occurs at 100 °C, which indicates that the loss of the water molecules and decomposition of the organic ligand overlap. Compound Eu (4) exhibits two main steps of weight losses. The first step (80–220 °C) corresponds to the release of two solventwater molecules and two coordinated water molecules (weight loss: measured 13.53%, theoretical 13.9%). The second weight loss starting at 230 °C corresponds to the loss of the remaining coordinated water molecule and collapse of the framework. Compounds Gd (5) and Dy (6) are isomorphous and therefore only the TGA curve of compound Dy (6) is selected for studying. The TGA curve of compound Dy (6) exhibits the first weight loss step starting at 40 °C. The total weight loss is 12.16% between 40 and 200 °C, which corresponds to the release of all water molecules (theoretical 13.40%). The collapse of the framework occurs immediately, which indicates that we have been unable to remove the water molecules without causing the 3D framework to collapse.

The crystallinity of the bulk samples for compounds 3–6 were checked by powder X-ray diffraction. These PXRD patterns are illustrated in Fig. S5 (ESI†). Their PXRD patterns are in good agreement with the ones simulated from single crystal structural data, thus those compounds were obtained as a single phase. As described above, compounds 5 and 6 are isomorphous, thus they have similar PXRD patterns as shown in Fig. S5.† Additionally, the different structures of compounds 3, 4, 5 or 6 have also been indicated by their different PXRD patterns.

Magnetic properties

The dc magnetic susceptibilities for compounds Pr (2), Nd (3), Gd (5), and Dy (6) were measured in an applied dc field of 1 kOe and in the temperature range 2.5–300 K. For these compounds, the observed paramagnetism arises uniquely from the 4f Ln(iii) ions. The plot of χMT versus T for compound Pr (2), where χM is the molar magnetic susceptibility per Pr(iii) unit, is shown in Fig. 10. At 300 K, the χMT value of 1.44 cm3 K mol−1 is lower than the expected value of 1.6 cm3 K mol−1 for one uncoupled Pr(iii) ion in the 3H4 ground state (S = 1, L = 5, g = 4/5). χMT gradually decreases to reach 0.10 cm3 K mol−1 at 2.5 K which is probably the result of a combination of the progressive depopulation of excited Stark sublevels and the exchange interaction between the Pr(iii) ions. There is no available expression to determine the magnetic susceptibilities of such 3D systems with large anisotropy. To obtain a rough quantitative estimation of the magnetic interaction between Pr(iii) ions, as a preliminary treatment, we assumed that owing to the ligand field effects, Pr(iii) ion may exhibit a splitting of mj energy levels (Ĥ = ΔĴ 2z), resulting in a magnetic susceptibility equation previously reported by McPherson and co-workers.9 The best fitting of the susceptibility data in the temperature range 2.5–300 K gives zJ′ = −2.79 cm−1, Δ = 5.56 cm−1, g = 0.806 with R = ∑[(χMT)calc − (χMT)obs]2/∑(χMT)2obs = 7.8 × 10−4. The negative value of zJ′ indicates that an overall antiferromagnetic interaction between Pr(iii) ions is operative. The absolute value of zJ′ is similar to that in the dinuclear compound Pr2L(NO3)4·2(H2O) (zJ′ = −2.2 cm−1; L is a macrocyclic Schiff's base),9 but is smaller than that in the 2D compound [Pr2(C2O4)2(pyzc)2(H2O)2]n (zJ′ = −5.8 cm−1; pyzc is 2-pyrazinecarboxylate).10 In the [Pr2(C2O4)2(pyzc)2(H2O)2]n compound the closest Pr⋯Pr distance is 4.46 Å, while in the present compound the closest Pr⋯Pr distance is 5.81 Å.

The thermal variation of the χMT product (χM is molar magnetic susceptibility per Nd(iii) unit) for Nd (3) is shown in Fig. 11. At 300 K, χMT is equal to 1.54 cm3 K mol−1, which is slightly lower than the value expected (1.64 cm3 K mol−1) for one free Nd(iii) ion in the 4I9/2 ground state. As the temperature is lowered, χMT decreases more and more rapidly, and finally reaches a minimum of 1.07 cm3 K mol−1 at 16 K. Then χMT increases as the temperature is further lowered. The decrease in the temperature 300–16 K undoubtedly originates in the thermal depopulation of the highest Stark components resulting from the splitting of the free ion ground state 4I9/2 by the crystal field. The crystal structure shows the closest Nd⋯Nd distance is 4.37 Å in compound Nd (3). In keeping with the structural data, the increase in χMT at low temperature may be a ferromagnetic interaction between the Nd(iii) ions.

A variable temperature magnetic study on Gd (5) was carried out (Fig. 12). At 300 K, χMT (χM is the molar magnetic susceptibility per Gd2 unit) is equal to 13.89 cm3 K mol−1, which is lower than the expected value of 15.76 cm3 K mol−1 for two uncoupled Gd(iii) ion in the 8S7/2 ground state (S = 7/2, g = 2.0). Lowering the temperature causes χMT to increase. A value of 14.62 cm3 K mol−1 is reached at 2.5 K. As described above, compound Gd (5) is based on the rod-shaped metal–carboxylate/hydroxyl building block, where the Gd1 and Gd2 ions are bridged by the hydroxyl oxygen atom (O15) to give a dimeric Gd2 unit (Gd⋯Gd2 = 3.9833(6) Å). The Gd2 dimers are further linked by the carboxylate groups to form the rod-shaped metal–carboxylate/hydroxyl building block. So to obtain a rough quantitative estimation of the magnetic interaction between lanthanide ions, the magnetic data were fitted by the equation deduced from the isotropic spin Hamiltonian, H = −JSGd1SGd2, with the quantum numbers SGd1 = SGd2 = 7/2.11 The least-squares fit is given as a solid line in Fig. 12. The best-fit parameters are J = 0.0201 cm−1, g = 1.88 and R = ∑[(χMT)calc − (χMT)obs]2/∑(χMT)2obs = 4.25 × 10−6. It has been reported that the interaction between Gd(iii) ions in the oxygen-bridged dimeric Gd2 unit is either antiferromagnetic or ferromagnetic.12 An empirical formula has been suggested that the interaction for Gd–Gd coupling (J) depends on the mean Gd–O–Gd bridging angle (φ) for the compounds containing a dimeric Gd2 unit.13 It is proposed that a φ > 110.9° is a prerequisite for a Gd–O–Gd complex being ferromagnetic. Compound Gd (5) with a Gd–O–Gd bridging angle of 112.62(16)° exhibits a weak ferromagnetic behavior. The small J value is consistent with related compounds with a similar Gd–O–Gd bridging angle (φ = 115.48°, J = 0.06 cm−1; φ = 112.44°, J = 0.006 cm−1, and φ = 114.07°, J = 0.046 cm−1, respectively).14

The dc magnetic susceptibility for Dy (6) is represented in Fig. 13. At 300 K, the χMT (χM is the molar magnetic susceptibility per Dy2 unit) value is 26.76 cm3 K mol−1, which is lower than the expected value of 28.34 cm3 K mol−1 for two magnetically isolated Dy(iii) (S = 5/2, L = 5, 6H15/2, g = 4/3) ions. The χMT product decreases with lowering temperature, reaching a minimum value of 22.48 cm3 K cm−1 at about 6.5 K, which is mainly ascribed to the progressive depopulation of excited Stark sublevels.15 The χMT value then increases sharply to a maximum of 24.36 cm3 K cm−1 at 2.5 K, which probably suggests the presence of ferromagnetic interactions between the metal centers, as observed in other dysprosium compounds.16 The large amount of magnetic anisotropy for the Dy(iii) ion render the fitting of the susceptibility curve difficult, especially for such a 3D system. Therefore, there is no available expression to determine the nature and the strength of the interactions between the metal centres. To examine the spin dynamics, the temperature dependencies of the alternating-current (ac) magnetic susceptibility for compound Dy (6) were collected at zero direct-current (dc) field with an ac field of 3 Oe with oscillating frequencies ranging from 10 Hz to 1500 Hz, given in Fig. S6† as plots of χ′ and χ′′ versus T. However, no imaginary component of the ac susceptibility χ′′ was observed, which clearly exclude an SMM behavior for compound Dy (6) above 2.5 K.

Conclusions

In conclusion, six lanthanide(iii)-2,5-dihydroxy-1,4-benzenedicarboxylate frameworks with four different structural types ranging from 1D chain, 2D layer to 3D frameworks have been synthesized and their crystal structures characterized. These results demonstrate that the multifunctional 2,5-dihydroxy-1,4-benzenedicarboxylate ligand appears to be an appropriate candidate to generate diverse extended lanthanide-based MOFs and the hydroxyl groups sited on the benzene ring as secondary groups can modify the final structures. Finally, the magnetic behavior of compounds Pr (2), Nd (3), Gd (5), and Dy (6) has been discussed.

Experimental

Lanthanide nitrate and sodium hydroxide were of reagent grade and used as commercially obtained from Sinopharm Chemical Reagent Co., Ltd, China. 2,5-Dihydroxy-1,4-benzenedicarboxylic acid was obtained from Aldrich Chemical Co. Elemental analyses were carried out on an Elementar Vario EL III analyzer and IR spectra (KBr pellets) were recorded on PerkinElmer Spectrum One. Magnetic measurements were carried out on crystalline samples with a Quantum Design SQUID magnetometer MPMS-XL5. Magnetic data were corrected for diamagnetic contributions of the sample holder and diamagnetic corrections were estimated from Pascal's constants for all constituents. Powder X-ray diffraction patterns were collected on a Rigaku Miniflex Π powder diffractometer, using monochromated Cu-Kα radiation. The thermogravimetric measurements were performed with a Netzsch STA449C apparatus under a nitrogen atmosphere with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 from 25 to 700 °C.

X-ray crystallographic study

Single crystal X-ray diffraction data of compounds 1–6 were collected on a Bruker Apex II CCD diffractometer equipped with a graphite-monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). Data reduction was performed using SAINT and corrected for Lorentz and polarization effects. Adsorption corrections were applied using the SADABS routine.17 The structures were solved by the direct methods and successive Fourier difference syntheses, and refined by the full-matrix least-squares method on F2 (SHELXTL Version 5.1).18 All non-hydrogen atoms are refined with anisotropic thermal parameters. Hydrogen atoms bonded to carbon atoms were assigned to calculated positions. H atoms attached to O atoms were located from difference maps and refined with isotropic thermal parameters fixed at 1.5 times that of the respective oxygen atoms. Hydrogen atoms of one of the solvent water (O1W) in compounds 5 and 6 could not be located in the difference Fourier map. The final Fourier map had a maximum and a minimum electron density of 5.479 and −2.235 e Å−3 for compound 2 and 2.335 and −1.838 e Å−3 for compound 3, respectively. These residual peaks are within 1 Å of the lanthanide ion, which resulted from the smear effect of the lanthanide ion. The R1 values are defined as R1 = Σ||Fo| − |Fc||/Σ|Fo| and wR2 = {Σ[w(Fo2 − Fc2)2]/Σ[w(Fo2)2]}1/2.
