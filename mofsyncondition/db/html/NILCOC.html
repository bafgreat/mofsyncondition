Introduction

Owing to their enormous variety of fascinating network topologies as well as potential applications in luminescence, magnetism, gas storage and separation, catalysis and nonlinear optics, metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) or coordination polymers have recently attracted much attention.1,2 So far, the lanthanide MOFs or coordination polymers constructed from carboxylate ligands have been well studied.3 Due to the “antenna effect” or “luminescence sensitization”, lanthanide-based MOFs or coordination polymers, which have narrow emission spectrum and environmental stability in luminescent materials, have attracted great attention. However, the lanthanide MOFs or coordination polymers constructed from the semi-rigid V-shaped mulitcarboxylate ligands have been less well reported.2,4 Semi-rigid V-shaped mulitcarboxylate ligands can adopt versatile conformations according to the geometric requirements of lanthanide ions and may afford unpredictable and interesting crystal-to-crystal transformations.

The crystal-to-crystal transformation related to changes in dimensionality and physical properties is one of the most interesting topics of metal–organic framework (MOF) research.5 There are several types of crystal structural transformations that are primarily influenced by the expansion of coordination numbers, the rearrangement of bonds, the removal/exchange of solvents, and so on.6 So far, the crystal-to-crystal transformations have been observed between different dimensional coordination polymers, such as 1D to 2D, 1D to 3D, and 2D to 3D.7 Because it is usually difficult to obtain high quality transformed crystals after alteration of the coordination environment around metal centers due to collapsing of the framework, only a few crystal-to-crystal transformations have been reported. Especially, the examples of lanthanide coordination polymers with crystal-to-crystal transformation manner are relatively rare.8

In this work, one-dimensional (1D) chain architectures (series a) and three-dimensional (3D) structures (series b) consisting of lanthanide and the semi-rigid V-shaped mulitcarboxylate ligands were obtained under different hydrothermal temperatures. The structural variations from 1D to 3D are attributed to variable coordination modes of semi-rigid V-shaped multicarboxylate ligand H2CPOB. And to our surprise we found that single crystals of a2 can transform into crystals of b2 by means of heating without any significant change in crystal quality. Namely, we can enforce crystal-to-crystal transformation from series a to series b.

Experimental section

Materials and general characterization

All chemicals were purchased and used without purification. Elemental analyses for C, H, and N were measured with a Perkin-Elmer 2400 elemental analyzer. The FT-IR spectra were measured with a Vertex 70 FTIR on KBr disks (4000–400 cm−1). TGA were measured with a NETZSCH TG 209 under a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. Powder X-ray diffraction measurements were recorded on a PANalytical Empyrean X-ray diffractometer using Mo Kα radiation. The fluorescent spectra were measured on an Edinburgh FLS920 spectrophotometer.

Synthesis

Series a: [Ln(HCPOB)(CPOB)(H2O)2]n [Ln = Eu(a1), Gd(a2), Tb(a3), Dy(a4), Ho(a5), Er(a6)]. The vessel was then cooled slowly down to room temperature at 2 °C h−1. Block crystals were obtained. The yields of a1–a6 were 58, 45, 42, 38, 40, and 23% [based on Ln(NO3)3·6H2O], respectively. Crystallography

Crystallographic data of a2–a6 and b1–b6 were collected on a SuperNova single crystal diffractometer equipped with graphite-monochromatic Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). The data integration and empirical absorption correction were carried out by the SAINT program. The crystals were kept at 293(2) K during data collection. Using Olex2,9 the structures were solved with the XS structure solution program using direct methods and refined with the XL refinement package using least squares minimization. All hydrogen atoms in these coordination polymers were generated geometrically and refine isotropically using the riding model.

Results and discussion

Syntheses and crystal-to-crystal transformation

Although 2,2′-bipyridine does not embed into the structures of series a or b, the experimental results indicate that it still plays a role as a weak Lewis base to adjust the pH value of the reaction solution (detailed experiment in Fig. S1, ESI†). Hydrothermal treatment of metal nitrates and 2-(4-carboxyphenoxy)benzoic acid (H2CPOB) yields the coordination polymers of series a or b. At lower temperature condition (130 °C), only 1D compounds (series a) were obtained, while 3D compounds (series b) were synthesized at higher temperature (180 °C). Scheme 1 shows the synthetic strategy and single-crystal to single-crystal transformation for a2–a6 and b1–b6. Indeed, mixed-phase 1D and 3D compounds were obtained when we investigated the different reaction temperature at 140, 150, 160 and 170 °C, respectively. As shown in Fig. 1, temperature-dependent XRD patterns for 1D compounds indicate that the 1D coordination polymer (a2) transforms to 3D architecture (b2) on heating the crystals. Crystals of a2 transformed into crystals of b2 without any significant change in crystal quality (see Fig. S1 in ESI†). These facts provide evidence for the crystal-to-crystal transition.

Description of the crystal structures

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analyses of a2–a6 (series a) reveal that they are isostructural, possessing 1D chain structures with the general formula [Ln(HCPOB)(CPOB)(H2O)2]n [Ln = Gd(a2), Tb(a3), Dy(a4), Ho(a5), Er(a6)], and all the five compounds crystallize in the triclinic space group P (Table 1). The structures of these five isostructural complexes can be described as 1D chains constructed from Ln3+ ions and CPOB2− bridging ligands. As a representative example, only the structure of compound a2 will be discussed in detail.

In compound a2, each asymmetric unit contains one Gd3+, one HCPOB− ligand, one CPOB2− ligand and two coordinated water molecules. The Gd3+ ion is on an inversion center and HCPOB−, CPOB2− ligands and water molecules in general positions. As it is depicted in Fig. 2(A), Gd3+ is eight-coordinated with a distorted bicapped trigonal prism, which is completed by two water molecules, four bidentate-chelating carboxylic oxygen atoms and two monodentate carboxylic oxygen atoms (Fig. 2). The Gd–O distances are in the range of 2.335(5)–2.519(5) Å (See Table S2 in ESI† for selected bond lengths and angles). In series a, the HCPOB− ligand adopts the coordination modes as shown in Scheme 2A, which contains a bidentate-chelating carbonyl group and uncoordinated carbonyl groups. The CPOB2− ligand adopts diverse coordination modes as shown in Scheme 2B, which acts as a bridging ligand linking Gd3+ ions with monodentate and bidentate coordination modes forming the binuclear Gd3+ subunits. Within the binuclear Gd3+ subunit, the two Gd3+ ions are bridged by the dual carboxyl groups. The neighboring subunits are further linked by two-fold carboxyl groups, forming a 1D chain. Owing to the effect of lanthanide contraction, a steady decrease of the average bond lengths of Ln–O in a2–a6 is found with increasing lanthanide atomic number (see Table S1 in ESI†).

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analyses reveal that the 3D compounds [Ln(HCPOB)(CPOB)]n (b1–b6, series b) are isomorphous, crystallizing in the monoclinic system with space group P21/n; hence only the structure of compound b2 will be discussed in detail. In compound b2, each asymmetric unit contains one unique Gd3+, one HCPOB− and one CPOB2− ligand. The seven-coordinated Gd3+ with monocapped trigonal prism geometry is completed by seven monodentate oxygen atoms from the carboxylic acid ligands, the Gd–O distances are in the range of 2.260(8)–2.481(8) Å (see Table S2 in ESI† for selected bond lengths and angles). From b1–b6, the average bond lengths of Ln–O are also slightly decreased owing to the effect of lanthanide contraction (see Table S1 in ESI†). In series b, the HCPOB− ligand adopts C coordination mode and CPOB2− exhibits D coordination mode as shown in Scheme 2, acting as tridentate metal linker for the former and tetradentate metal linker for the latter. Within the binuclear Ln3+ subunit, the two Ln3+ ions are bridged by four-fold carboxyl groups. The neighboring subunits are further linked by two-fold carboxyl groups forming a 1D chain along the a direction. The chains are connected by the carboxylic oxygen atoms of tridentate HCPOB− ligands into a 2D plane (Fig. 3), which is further linked by the CPOB2− ligands into a 3D framework. The HCPOB− and the CPOB2− ligands can be considered as 3-connected and 4-connected nodes, respectively. The structure can be simplified as a 3,4-connected framework with the point Schlafli symbol {318·442·56}, as shown in Fig. 3.

IR spectra, powder X-ray diffraction and thermogravimetric analysis (TGA)

For the twelve compounds, very similar, sharp strong peaks ~1240 cm−1 (1236.2–1243.3 cm−1) occurred in the IR spectrum, which is attributable to the H2CPOB ligand's ether bond stretching vibrations of the CPh–O–CPh groups (Ph = benzene). In the region expected for the vas(COO) absorption, all compounds exhibit similar peak shape, sharp and strong bands at 1400–1694 cm−1, attributable to the presence of carboxyl groups with different bridging modes in the structures. For 3D compounds b1–b6, the appearance of broad band at ~3130 cm−1 indicates the presence of the vibrations of carboxyl hydroxyl groups. Along with these, the broad strong band in the range of 3130–3540 cm−1 illustrates not only the vibration of the carboxyl group hydrogen, but also the occurrence of water molecules in the structures for 1D compounds a2–a6 (See Fig. S2 in ESI†).

The phase purity of bulk products of the compounds were confirmed by PXRD measurements. The PXRD patterns for a1–a6 are well consistent with the simulated one from the single-crystal data of a2, illustrating that compounds a1–a6 are isomorphic and proving the purity of samples a1–a6. The PXRD patterns of b1–b6 have also been examined, and they are also well in conformity with the simulated one of b2, demonstrating that they are isomorphic and proving the purity of the samples (Fig. 4).

To reveal the thermal stability and further affirm the molecular formula of the compounds, TGA experiments were performed on pure single crystal samples of complexes a1–a6 and b1, b3–b5 under N2 atmosphere with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 in the range of 25–800 °C (Fig. 5). Two series of compounds exhibit two types of comparable thermal behavior. The TGA curves of a1–a6 exhibit two regions of weight loss. The first weight loss of a1–a6 between 160 and 260 °C is 5.09, 5.11, 5.09, 5.08, 5.08 and 5.09%, respectively. And they correspond to the loss of two coordinated water molecules, being identical to the calculated 5.13% for a1, 5.09% for a2, 5.08% for a3, 5.06% for a4, and 5.04% for a5, 5.02% for a6. The experimental value is slightly higher than the calculated one because of the incomplete drying of the sample. The second weight loss above 400 °C stems from the decomposition of the compounds. On the basis of the analyses mentioned above, we can conclude that, during 260–460 °C, the compounds still remain intact after removing the coordinated water molecules, and the corresponding formula of these compounds may be written as [Ln(HCPOB)(CPOB)]n. For b1, b3–b5, the first loss of weight above 400 °C stems from the partial decomposition of the 3D frameworks. Upon increasing the temperature to 480 °C, the stable structure begins to collapse.

Luminescence properties

Lanthanide ions (Ln3+), due to the gradual filling of the 4f orbitals from 4f0(La3+) to 4f14(Lu3+) and shielding effect of 4f orbitals caused by the filled 5s25p6 subshells, have well-defined and complex electronic energy levels.10 Consequently, each lanthanide ion has such a performance of narrow and characteristic 4f–4f transitions luminescence. So far, hundreds of coordination polymers (CPs) or coordination networks have been reported.11 In spite of their native luminescence properties, lanthanide ions also suffer from weak light absorption, which makes the direct excitation of lanthanide metals very inefficient. This defect can be well overcome by the “antenna effect” or “luminescence sensitization” between ligands and Ln3+ ions.10,12 Herein we synthesized 12 lanthanide complexes, some of which (a1, b1, a3, b3, a4, b4) have significant luminescence properties and will be discussed in detail as follows.

The “free” ligand is excited by the absorption band at 311 nm and exhibits a very weak emission peak in the solid state. The emission spectra of the complexes a1, a3, a4 and b1, b3, b4 in the solid state were recorded at room temperature. As shown in Fig. 6, the fluorescent intensity of the three-dimensional compounds (series b) is much stronger than that of the one-dimensional compounds (series a).

For luminescent lanthanide CPs or MOFs, the overall fluorescence intensity is determined by the efficiency of sensitization and the intrinsic nature of the lanthanide luminescence.10 The intrinsic nature, which reflects the extent of non-radiative relaxation processes occurring both in the inner- and outer-coordination spheres of the lanthanide ion, depends on the energy gap between the emissive state and the highest sublevel of the ground state of the lanthanide ion.13,14 The smaller this gap is, the easier is its closing by non-radiative transition processes through vibration of bound ligands, particularly through those having high energy O–H, N–H, and C–H vibrations.14 In other words, lanthanide luminescence is very sensitive to the quenching by high-energy vibrations, particularly O–H. For one-dimensional complexes (series a), there are water molecules attaching to lanthanide ions, while the three-dimensional compounds (series b) don't have this kind of coordination water molecules. Therefore, it can be explained very well that the fluorescence intensity of the series a is much weaker than that of the series b.

The much stronger fluorescence emission is exhibited in b1, b3 and b4, therefore we discuss the luminescence properties of the three compounds in detail. For 1b, there are five characteristic peaks of Eu(iii) shown in the emission spectrum (Fig. 6a) which are attributed to5D0→7F0 (559 nm), 5D0→7F1 (594 nm), 5D0→7F2 (612, 613, and 618 nm), 5D0→7F3 (652 nm) and 5D0→7F4 (699 nm).15 The very weak bands at 559 nm and 652 nm originated from the 5D0→7F0 and 5D0→7F3 transition, which are both electric-dipole and magnetic-dipole forbidden. As is known, the transition of 5D0→7F0 is only permissible for Cs, Cn, and Cnv(n = 1–6) site symmetries,16 while b1 crystallize in the monoclinic crystal systems, and Eu3+ belongs to the symmetry of C2v. And, the 5D0→7F3 transition is due to crystal field-induced J-mixing effect.17 Thus, it is reasonable to observe them in the emission spectra. The most intensive band assigned to the 5D0→7F2 transition splits to three bands (612, 613, and 618 nm for b1) owing to the nature of the 5D0→7F2 transition, which is hypersensitive to site symmetry and the electric dipole (ED). The bands at 594 nm for b1 may be attributed to the 5D0→7F1 magnetic dipole transition. Additionally, the average intensity of 5D0→7F2 transition in b1 is 7.86 times stronger than that of the 5D0→7F1 transition, indicating that Eu3+ ions are located at low-symmetry environment according to Judd–Ofelt theory18 and which is consistent with the crystallographic analyses. For b3, the emission spectra under excitation of 311 nm displays characteristic bands of Tb3+ and a strong turquoise luminescence, assigned to the 5D4→7FJ (J= 6, 5, 4, 3) transitions19 (Fig. 6b). The strongest emission peak at 542 nm is designated as the 5D4→7F5 transition. The peak at 488 nm is assigned to the 5D4→7F6 transitions. The peaks at 582, 589 nm are assigned to the 5D4→7F4 transitions and the peak at 620 nm is assigned to the 5D4→7F3 transitions. The spectra of compound b4 exhibit the typical bands of Dy3+ and a strong yellow luminescence upon excitation at 312 nm (Fig. 6c) These emission bands are 485 nm for 4F9/2→6F15/2 and 576 nm for 4F9/2→6F13/2 transitions.20

Conclusions

Two series of lanthanide-based coordination polymers (series a and series b) have been prepared under hydrothermal conditions by changing the temperature, leading to structural change from one-dimensional to three-dimensional. Temperature-dependent XRD patterns for 1D compounds indicate that the 1D polymers transform to the 3D coordination polymers upon heating the solid sample. Moreover, we provided evidence that single crystals of series a can be transformed into crystals of series b without any significant change in crystal quality. Solid luminescence of compounds a1 and b1, a3 and b3, and a4 and b4 display the characteristic bands of Eu3+, Tb3+, and Dy3+, respectively. As the lanthanide luminescence is very sensitive to the quenching by coordinating water molecule vibration of O–H groups, the fluorescent intensity of the three-dimensional compounds are much higher than that of the one-dimensional compounds which have water molecules incorporated in the coordination environment. Lanthanide MOFs or coordination polymers based upon single crystal-to-single crystal transformation accompanied by changes in luminescence properties will be one of the most interesting topics in coordination polymers research.
