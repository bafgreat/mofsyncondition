Introduction

Coordination polymers (CPs) have been investigated extensively for over a decade due to their unique structures and a number of potential applications such as catalysis, ion exchange, magnetism, gas storage, optical, and luminescence.1–6 Metal ions of CPs are coordinated by the bridging organic ligands to form primary components that are further linked through covalent bonds and other weak chemical bonds to construct infinitely assembled networks. It is well known that the structural types of CPs are influenced by several factors, such as the identity of counterion,7 metal-to-ligand ratio8 and solvent system,9 not to mention the coordination ability, the geometry, and relative orientation of the ligands that are also critical in constructing the framework of CPs. With the consideration of these factors, polypyridyl and polycarboxylate ligands have been used considerably as building blocks to form CPs with desired properties. Moreover, one of the common practices to prepare new types of CPs for the potential applications mentioned above is the substitution of the discrete metal centers of the CPs by the metal clusters.10,11 Aggregation of metal ions may induce interesting structural topologies and distinct properties that are quite different from those of the discrete metal ions.11

The coordination chemistry of formamidine and its anion has been extensively investigated in decades.12 Intriguing coordination modes have been found for the anions of N,N′-di(2-pyridyl)formamidine (HDpyF) and N,N′-bis(pyrimidine-2-yl)formamidine (Hpmf), resulting in dinuclear, linear trinuclear and linear tetranuclear complexes.13–19 These ligands have also been improved by changing the directions of donor sites to construct CPs with various dimensionality.20,21 For example, the N,N′-bis(pyridine-4-yl)formamidine (4-Hpyf) was utilized to react with the [Mo2(O2CCH3)4] and Hg(ii) salts to form the two-dimensional (2D) and three-dimensional (3D) heteronuclear CPs based on quadruple-bonded dimolybdenum units.20 On the other hand, one-pot solvothermal reactions of 4-aminopyridine and triethylorthoformate with copper(ii) acetate in various solvents afforded 2D and 3D CPs based on dicooper units, which display closed-shell Cu(i)–Cu(i) interactions that are supported by the 4-pyf− ligands.22

Cooper(i) halide-based CPs have attracted a great attention during recent years because of their distinct structural diversity and photophysical and photochemical properties, where the metal cores (CuxXy)x–y (X = halide) have been widely adopted to prepare CPs with the designed properties.10,23 Structural variations of these CPs are presumably due to the versatility of the ligating halide anions and the auxiliary donor ligands. However, it is difficult to predict which anions afford the similar or different structures. To prepare CPs based on the linear polynuclear copper(i) units and to investigate the role of halide anions in the structural diversity, we have conducted the one-pot solvothermal reactions of CuX (X = Br or I) with 2-aminopyridine, triethyl orthoformate and NaOH in various solvents. Herein, we reports the synthesis, structures, emission properties and structural transformations of two unique 1D CPs based on the linear tetracopper units, {[Cu4Br2(Dpyf)2]·DMF}n, 1, and [Cu4I2(Dpyf)2]n, 2.

Experimental details

General procedures

Diethyl ether, tetrahydrofuran and hexane were dried and deoxygenated by refluxing with sodium and benzophenone. Methanol and ethanol were refluxed with magnesium and iodine. Dichloromethane was purified by using phosphorus pentaoxide. Elemental analyses were obtained from a Perkin-Elmer 240 Series II analyzer. IR spectra (KBr disk) were recorded on a JASCO FT/IR-460 plus spectrometer. UV-vis spectra were obtained from a Varian Cary 100 spectrometer. Emission spectra were performed on a Hitachi F-4500 spectrometer. Powder X-ray diffraction instrument were carried out on a Bruker D2 PHASER diffractometer with CuKα (λα = 1.54 Å) radiation. EDS (energy dispersive spectroscopy) experiments were carried out on a JEOL JSM-7600F/Oxford Xmax80 spectrometer. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was performed on a Perkin-Elmer 7 Series/UNIX TGA7 analyzer. Single-phased powder samples was loaded into alumina pans and heated with a ramp rate of 5 °C min−1 from room temperature to 800 °C under a nitrogen flux.

Materials

The reagents triethyl orthoformate and copper(i) bromide were purchased from Alfa Aesar, copper(i) iodide from ACROS, 2-aminopyridine from TGI and sodium hydroxide from AENCORE.

Preparation of {[Cu4Br2(Dpyf)2]·DMF}n, 1

A mixture of CuBr (0.14 g, 1.0 mmol), 2-aminopyridine (0.38 g, 4.0 mmol), triethyl orthoformate (3.0 mL, 18 mmol), NaOH (0.08 g, 2.0 mmol) and 5 mL of DMF were placed in a Teflon stainless container, which was heated up to 120 °C for 2 days and then cooled down slowly to room temperature at a rate of 2 °C per hour. Red block crystals were collected and washed by excess of DMF and then dried under vacuum. Yield: 0.13 g (59%). Anal calcd. for C25H25Br2Cu4N9O (MW = 881.52): C, 34.06; H, 2.86; N, 14.30. Found: C, 33.73; H, 2.79; N, 14.33. IR (cm−1, KBr disk): 3029(w), 1665(m), 1578(m), 1535(s), 1476(m), 1430(m), 1350(s), 1306(m), 1258(m), 1172(m), 1160(m), 1096(m), 1001(m), 956(w), 775(m), 755(w), 531(w), 479(w), 465(w), 452(w), 425(w), 422(w), 412(w). EDS analysis reveals the existence of Cu, Br, O, N and C atoms. See EDS data for complex 1 in ESI.†

Preparation of [Cu4I2(Dpyf)2]n, 2

Compound 2 was prepared by following the procedure described for 1, except a mixture of CuI (0.19 g, 1.0 mmol), 2-aminopyridine (0.38 g, 4.0 mmol), triethyl orthoformate (3.0 mL, 18 mmol), NaOH (0.08 g, 2.0 mmol) and 5 mL of methanol was used. Yellow plate crystals were obtained. Yield: 0.18 g (20%). Anal calcd. for C22H18Cu4I2N8 (MW = 902.40): C, 29.28; H, 2.01; N, 12.41. Found: C, 29.29; H, 0.15; N, 12.48. IR (cm−1, KBr disk): 3471(w), 3409(w), 3053(w), 2361(w), 1757(w), 1638(w), 1580(w), 1534(s), 1464(s), 1430(m), 1336(s), 1296(m), 1257(m), 1240(m), 1162(m). EDS analysis reveals the existence of Cu, I, O, N and C atoms. See EDS data for complex 2 in ESI.†

X-ray crystallography

The diffraction data for complexes 1–2 were collected on a Bruker AXS SMART APEX II CCD diffractometer, which was equipped with a graphite-monochromated MoKα (λα = 0.71073 Å) radiation. Data reduction was carried out by standard methods with use of well-established computational procedures.24 The structure factors were obtained after Lorentz and polarization corrections and empirical absorption correction based on “multi-scan”. The position of some of the heavier atoms were located by using Patterson or direct method. The remaining atoms were found in a series of alternating difference Fourier maps and least-square refinements, while the hydrogen atoms were added by using the HADD command in SHELXTL 6.1012.25 Basic information pertaining to crystal parameters and structure refinement for 1–2 is summarized in Table 1.

Results and discussion

Structure of 1

The structure of 1 was solved in the space group P21/c and the asymmetric unit contains four Cu(i) ions, two Dpyf− ligands and two Br− anions. The arrangement of the tetracopper Cu4Br2(Dpyf)2 unit shown in Fig. 1(a) demonstrates that the four Cu(i) ions are positioned somewhat deviating from linearity with Cu(1)–Cu(2)–Cu(3) and Cu(2)–Cu(3)–Cu(4) angles of 178.31(3) and 172.95(3)°, respectively, while the torsion angle of the four Cu(i) ions is 175.9(9)°. The distances between copper ions are 2.4751(7), 2.4765(7) and 2.4780(7) Å, respectively, which could be indicative of the existence of cuprophilicity,26–32 considering that the van der Waal radius of copper atom is 1.40 Å.33 Furthermore, all of the four Cu(i) ions are bridged by two Dpyf− ligands through all the four nitrogen atoms. The linear tetranuclear metal units are further bridged by the bromide ions, forming a 1D extended zigzag metal chain, Fig. 1(b).

Two bonding modes for the bromide anions can be observed. While the Br(1) atom bridges two Cu(i) ions [Cu(1)–Br(1) = 2.5621(7) and Cu(4A)–Br(1) = 2.5764(7) Å], the Br(2) bridges four Cu(i) ions [Cu(1B)–Br(2) = 2.5875(7), Cu(2B)–Br(2) = 2.8448(7), Cu(3)–Br(2) = 2.9544(7) and Cu(4)–Br(2) = 2.7123(7) Å]. The Cu–Br bond distances of the bridging Br–Cu–Br linkages of 2.5621(7), 2.5764(7) and 2.58757(7) Å are longer than those reported for the dibromocuprate anion in the 1D chain (C5H8N3)CuBr2, which are 2.4975(8) and 2.5078(8) Å,34 while the other distances are significantly shorter than the sum of the van der Waal radius of Cu and Br atoms (3.25 Å, van der Waal radius of bromine atom is 1.85 Å).33 For comparison, it is noted that in the discrete monomeric complex (C5H7N3Br)3CuBr4, the Cu(i)–Br bond distances to the tetrahedral Cu(i) centers are 2.436(2), 2.491(2), 2.504(2) and 2.515(2), respectively,34 indicating that some of the bridging Cu–Br interactions in 1 [2.5621(7), 2.5764(7) and 2.58757(7) Å] are strong. Looking down the Cu–Cu–Cu–Cu metal chains, the tetracopper units show a face-to-side configuration assuming that the tetracopper unit forms a plane, Fig. 1(c).

Structure of complex 2

The structure of 2 was solved in the space group P and the asymmetric unit contains two Cu(i) ions, one Dpyf− ligands and one I− anions with the tetracopper unit Cu4I2(Dpyf)2 occupying an inversion center. Fig. 2(a) shows the coordination environments about the Cu(i) ions. The Cu–Cu distances of the adjacent copper ions are 2.5108(6) and 2.5183(9) Å, which could be indicative of cuprophilicity. The four Cu(i) ions are bridged by two Dpyf− ligands through all the four nitrogen atoms with the Cu(1)–Cu(2)–Cu(2A) angle of 138.62(4)° and the torsion angle of the four Cu(i) ions is exact 180°. The tetranuclear metal units are linked by the iodide ions to form a linear 1D chain, Fig. 2(b), forming a face-to-face configuration that is in marked to the face-to-side configuration in 1, Fig. 2(c).

While one iodide anion bridges the first and second Cu(i) ions of two adjacent tetracopper units, the other bridges the third and fourth ones with Cu–I distances of 2.6931(5) and 3.0155(6) Å for the bridging I–Cu–I linkages. The bond distance of 2.6931(5) Å is similar to those found in the [CuI(L)]n stairs,35 and that of 3.0155(6) Å is significantly shorter than the sum of the van der Waal radius of Cu and I atoms (3.38 Å, van der Waal radius of iodine atom is 1.98 Å), indicating a strong bridging Cu–I interaction.

For comparison, it is noted that the complex [Cu3(DPhIP)2](CuCl2) (HDPhIP = 2,6-diphenyliminopiperidine) composed of linear tricopper units bridged by two DPhIP− ligands has been reported,36 in which each cation has weak interactions with two adjacent CuCl2− anions, and each anion interacts with two [Cu3(DPhIP)2]+ cations, forming an infinite zigzag chain. Complexes 1 and 2 are thus the first 1D CPs that are composed of the linear tetracopper units.

Moreover, the structures of 1 and 2 can be regarded as the 1D copper(i) halide aggregates with cationic Cu4Br22+ and Cu4I22+ repeating units, which are supported by the Dpyf− ligands. As clearly shown in Fig. 3(a) and (b), the Cu4Br22+ and Cu4I22+ cations extend into a 1D zigzag chain and a looped chain, respectively, with the unique configurations for the repeating units significantly different from those reported before.10,23

Luminescent properties

The UV-visible spectra for complexes 1 and 2 were measured in solid state at room temperature, shown in Fig. S1.† There are broad bands in 450–550 nm for both compounds. The emission spectra of 1 and 2 measured in solid state at room temperature are shown in Fig. 4. While complex 1 shows an emission at 570 nm upon excitation at 341, 370, or 441 nm, complex 2 displays an emission at 549 nm upon excitations at 333, 369, or 444 nm. According to the previous report for the tetranuclear complexes based on N,N′-bis(5,7-dimethyl-1,8-naphthyridine-2-yl)amine,37 the emissions of 1 and 2 are tentatively ascribed to the 3d(Cu) → π* metal to ligand charge transfer. The red-shift of 1 compared with that of 2 may be due to the effect of halide anions as well as the impact of the structural types. The short Cu–Cu distances in the linear tetranuclear cores of 1 and 2 [2.4751(7), 2.4765(7) and 2.4780(7) Å in 1; 2.5108(6) and 2.5183(9) in 2] may also contribute to the luminescence.38

Structural transformation upon solvent exchange

Structural transformation is one of the fantastic features in CPs that may lead to the formation of versatile structures with changes in dimensionality and properties, which is an important phenomenon for the potential applications of CPs in switches and sensors.39 Structural transformation can be triggered by the exchange of guest molecules, removal and uptake of solvents and various external factors such as heat, light and mechanochemical energy.39 Complexes 1 and 2 provide a unique opportunity to investigate the structural transformations of CPs based on the linear tetranuclear chains. To investigate the structural transformation, we first checked the purities of complexes 1 and 2 by measuring their powder X-ray diffraction patterns, which are shown in Fig. S2 and S3.† The peak positions of the simulated and experimental PXRD patterns match quite well with each other, indicating the bulk purities of the complexes.

Complex 1 was first immersed into various organic solvents at room temperature and the PXRD patterns were then measured. Complex 1 was stable in most of the common organic solvents, such as tetrahydrofuran, dichloromethane, and acetonitrile, Fig. S4,† but unstable in methanol and water. As shown in Fig. 5 and 6, the PXRD patterns of 1 in methanol and water are different from that of the simulation of 1, indicating the structural transformations. Under the UV light (365 nm), the remained solids show barely no luminescence, Fig. S5(b) and (d).† Complex 1 thus can be regarded as a potential luminescent sensor for selective detection of water and methanol. Intriguingly, when the solids were immersed into dimethylformamide, yellow-orange luminescence were rejuvenated, Fig. S5(c) and (e).† Moreover, the PXRD patterns of the solvated 1 upon immersion back into DMF, measured in four days and one day, respectively, match quite well with that of the simulation of 1, Fig. 5 and 6, indicating the reversible structural transformation. The structural transformations can also be verified by recording the emission spectra. In Fig. 7 and 8, no intensity of the emission can be measured for 1 upon immersion in methanol and water, which are consistent with the pictures that were taken under the 365 nm UV lamp, Fig. S5(b) and (d).† The increasing intensities for the bands at 570 nm upon immersing into DMF demonstrate that the reversible structural transformation due to solvent exchange is foreseeable, although the structures in methanol and water remain unclear. On the other hand, complex 2 was stable in a variety of organic solvents including methanol and water, Fig. S6.†

Thermal properties

TG analysis, Fig. S7(a),† reveals that complex 1 was thermally stable up to 170 °C with the first weight loss of 6.11% (calc. 8.3%) in 170–201 °C, which can be ascribed to the removal of the DMF molecules, while the hydrated sample of 1 performed a weight loss of 11.8% (calc. 11.7%,) in 32–82 °C, corresponding to the loss of approximate six guest water molecules and demonstrating the solvent exchange of 1 in water. TG analysis, Fig. S7(b),† shows that complex 2 underwent a one-step weight loss and was thermally stable up to 295 °C. On further heating, the sample decomposed.

It is worth noting that the removal of guest water molecules of the hydrated 1 is more likely to be irreversible, evidenced by cyclic de/rehydration TG measurements accompanying with heating/cooling procedures. Fig. S8.† When the guest water molecules were completely removed at about 85 °C, nearly zero or partial weight increase was found during the cooling process under water vapor. Such heating and cooling processes were also repeated for five cycles to demonstrate the stable but irreversible water ad-/de-sorption behavior during the thermal re/dehydration processes.

The water vapor adsorption isotherm, Fig. S9,† indicates that the fully dehydrated sample of 1 adsorbs three molecules per unit in the water vapor pressure (P/P0) range of 0.35–0.6. However, attempts to measure the desorption isotherm down to the humidity range failed and no further measurement can be observed.

Feasibility of the structural transformation

As shown in the previous section, the structural transformation of 1 can only be carried out in water and methanol, while complex 2 was stable in various solvents. It is evident that complex 1 undergoes distinct structural alternations on changing the solvents from the protic solvents to the aprotic solvents. With respect to the observations on the stabilities in different solvents, it is interesting to rationalize the structural changes of 1 depending on the polarity and identity of the solvents.

The structural diversity in 1 and 2 is presumably due to the differences in the electronegativity and the size of the halide anions, while the reversible structural transformation of 1 can be ascribed to its better ionic bonding than 2. Compared to iodide anion, bromide anion is a harder base while Cu(i) ion is a softer acid. Interaction between harder base and softer acid would possess more ionic character, and interaction between softer base and softer acid would show more covalent character. Complex 1 that shows the better ionic character is therefore easier to trap polar aprotic solvents such as DMF with a polarity index of 6.4, and also tender to interact with protic solvents of high polarity such as methanol (5.1) and water (10.2),40 most probably through O–H–Br interactions. Moreover, as it can be seen in Fig. S10,† the DMF solvent molecules (with red-coloured atoms) are located between the linear chains, interacting with the tetranuclear units through weak C–H–O hydrogen bonds (H–O = 2.468–2.614 Å; ∠C–H–O = 140.55–175.47°) to the oxygen atoms of DMD molecules. For comparison, it is noted that the polarity indexes of the aprotic tetrahydrofuran, dichloromethane and acetonitrile are 4.0, 3.1 and 5.8, respectively. The selective and reversible crystal-to-crystal transformation of 1 is thus most likely triggered by the exchange between the aprotic solvent (DMF) and protic solvents (methanol and water) with high polarities.

Conclusions

Two extended metal chains based on linear tetracopper units, {[Cu4Br2(Dpyf)2]·DMF}n, 1, and [Cu4I2(Dpyf)2]n, 2, have been successfully accomplished. Their structural diversity is subject to the identity of the bromide and iodide anions, affording 1D CPs with the tetracopper units adopting a face-to-side configuration and a face-to-face configuration, respectively. Complexes 1 and 2 form the novel 1D CPs that are composed of the linear tetracopper units, which can be regarded as the copper(i) halide aggregates with the unique Cu4Br22+ and Cu4I22+ cations extending into a 1D zigzag chain and a looped chain, respectively. While complex 1 illustrates selective reversible structural transformation and luminescence change upon solvent exchange, complex 2 remains robustly stable under the same conditions, which can be ascribed to the different ionic characters of the CPs due to different halide anions that results in different diversity. This study also provides an insight into understanding the role of solvent polarity in the feasibility of the structural transformation in 1D CPs.
