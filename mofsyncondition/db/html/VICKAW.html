1. Introduction

Luminescent metal–organic frameworks (LMOFs) have recently attracted considerable research interest because of their prominent optical properties and porosity, tunable chemical functionalization, well-defined topologies, and potential sensing applications with easy operability, good repeatability, quick response time, high sensitivity, and high selectivity.1 Up to now, some LMOFs have already been reported to act as effective luminescence sensors for small molecules,2,3 cations,4,5 anions,6,7 biomolecules8 and nitroaromatic explosives,9,10 which demonstrates that MOF-based luminescent materials are promising candidates as sensors. Among the diverse LMOFs reported to date, lanthanide metal–organic frameworks (Ln-MOFs), especially Eu- and Tb-MOFs, are considered to be effective platforms for chemical probes due to their fascinating luminescence properties, such as sharp and characteristic emissions, large Stokes shifts, relatively long luminescence lifetimes and visible colors with the naked eye, which originally come from f–f transitions via the “antenna effect”.3,5,7,10,11

Extensive studies indicate that π-electron-rich aromatic polycarboxylate ligands can provide a useful method for constructing Ln-MOFs and also can efficiently promote their sensing performances.11 To increase the selectivity and sensitivity towards different molecules and metal ions, functional groups such as hydroxyl,12 carboxyl,13 amine,14 amide,15 and pyridyl16 or other N-containing groups17 were also implanted into polycarboxylate ligands in order to improve favorable analyte–MOF interactions for the recognition of small molecules as well as cations. However, the introduction of sulfide or thiophene as a functional group into lanthanide carboxylate MOFs has been less documented for sensing studies.18 Generally, Ln ions have high affinity and prefer to ligate to hard donor O atoms of the ligand, whereas the Lewis basic S atoms of the ligand can direct into the channels of the framework and thus are exposed. Thus a thiophene-carboxylate ligand can easily form sulphide-containing Ln-MOFs.18,19 Moreover, in the process of sensing, the lone pair electrons of S atoms can be spread in a highly electron-rich conjugated system and thereby realize the effective transfer of electrons and further influence the optical properties. Therefore, the introduction of sulfide or thiophene into carboxylic acid ligands may be a highly promising approach for the construction of Ln-MOFs with sensing properties. However, to the best of our knowledge, only a few thiophene-functionalized Ln-MOFs have been reported as luminescent sensors,18 and only one example has been related to thiophene-functionalized Ln-MOFs as fluorescence sensors for Fe3+.18c

The ligand 3,4-dimethylthieno[2,3-b]-thiophene-2,5-dicarboxylic acid (H2DMTDC) with bithiophene groups exhibits not only π-electron-rich conjugation characteristics, but also always leaves the thiophene S atom uncoordinated in the construction of MOFs, which is beneficial for selective luminescence sensors for small molecules and metal cations.18b,20 Herein, two new luminescent lanthanide–organic frameworks based on the H2DMTDC ligand, namely, [Eu2(DMTDC)3(DEF)4]·DEF·6H2O(1) and [Tb2(DMTDC)3(DEF)4]·DEF·6H2O (2), were synthesized under solvothermal conditions. Their structures, thermal stability, and fluorescence properties, as well as the selective detection of nitrobenzene and Fe3+, were fully studied.

2. Experimental

2.1 Materials and methods

The ligand H2DMTDC was synthesized according to a literature method.21 Other reagents and solvents are commercially available and were used without further purification. IR spectra were obtained from KBr pellets using a Bruker EQUINOX 55 FT IR spectrometer in the 400–4000 cm−1 region. Elemental analyses (C, H, N) were performed with a Vario EL elemental analyzer. The phase-purity and crystallinity of each product were checked by powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) using a Rigaku D/M-2200T automated diffractometer (CuKα, 1.5418 Å). Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was performed using a Netzsch STA 449F3 instrument. Each sample was heated from room temperature to 800 °C at a heating rate of 10.0 °C min−1 under a nitrogen atmosphere. Room-temperature photoluminescence (PL) spectra were recorded using a Hitachi F-4600 fluorescence spectrophotometer equipped with a xenon lamp. UV-visible spectra were obtained using a Hitachi 3310 UV-vis spectrophotometer at room temperature. The sorption isotherms for CO2 gas were measured using ASAP 2020M adsorption equipment. XPS data were obtained using a PHI5000 Versa probe (ULVAC-PHI, INC., Japan) instrument.

2.2 Preparation of compounds 1 and 2

2.2.1. Synthesis of [Eu2(DMTDC)3(DEF)4]·DEF·6H2O (1)

A mixture of EuCl3·6H2O (44.3 mg, 0.1 mmol), H2DMTDC (25.5 mg, 0.10 mmol), and DEF (N,N-diethylformamide, 4 mL) was placed in a Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel (15 mL) and heated at 95 °C for a constant temperature reaction of 72 h, and then cooled to room temperature at a rate of 5 °C h−1. Colorless and diamond-shaped crystals were collected by filtration, washed with ether and dried in air to afford 31.2 mg (55.6% based on the H2DMTDC ligand) of the product. Anal. calcd. for C55H85N5O23S6Eu2: C, 39.31; H, 5.10; N, 4.17. Found: C, 39.35; H, 5.18; N, 4.15. IR (cm−1, KBr): 3442(br, m), 2972(w), 2657(w), 1632(s), 1501(m), 1380(s), 1265(w), 1115(w), 824(m), 786(m), 655(w).

2.2.2. Synthesis of [Tb2(DMTDC)3(DEF)4]·DEF·6H2O (2)

The procedure is similar to that of compound 1 except that TbCl3·6H2O was used instead of EuCl3·6H2O. Colorless and diamond-shaped crystals were collected by filtration, washed with ether and dried in air to afford 29.5 mg (52.2% based on ligand H2DMTDC) of the product. Anal. calcd. for C55H85N5O23S6Tb2: C, 38.98; H, 5.06; N, 4.13. Found: C, 38.93; H, 5.09; N, 4.11. IR (cm−1, KBr): 3445(br, w), 2972(w), 2827(w), 2360(w), 1630(s), 14 450(m), 1378(s), 827(m), 807(m).

2.3 X-ray crystallography

The diffraction intensities of compound 1 were collected using a Bruker Apex CCD diffractometer and those of compound 2 were collected using a Rigaku R-AXIS SPIDER diffractometer with graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). Absorption corrections were applied by using the multi-scan program SADABS22 or the program ABSCOR.23 The structures were solved with direct methods and refined with the full-matrix least-squares technique with the SHELXTL program package.24 Anisotropic thermal parameters were applied to all non-hydrogen atoms. The reported refinement is of the guest-free structure using the *.hkp file produced using the SQUEEZE routine because the guests are highly disordered in the frameworks.25 The void volume (excluding solvent guest molecules) in the crystal cell was calculated using the program PLATON.25 CCDC 1573428 and 1573429 contain the supplementary crystallographic data for this paper. Crystal data as well as details of data collection and refinements for the compounds are summarized in Table 1, and selected bond lengths and angles are listed in Tables S1 and S2.†

2.4 Luminescence sensing experiment

Single-crystalline powder samples of compounds 1 and 2 (3 mg) without any activation were dispersed in 5 mL DMF by ultrasonication for 40 min, and were subsequently placed in a quartz cell of 1 cm width for fluorescence measurements. For all measurements, the fluorescence spectra were recorded at 298 K with the maximum emission wavelength at 613 nm for 1 (λex = 284 nm) and 544 nm for 2 (λex = 285 nm), respectively.

To check the selectivity for nitrobenzene, 200 μL solution of each analyte (10 mM) was added to 3 mL of the dispersion of 1 or 2 in DMF. To check the selectivity for Fe3+, 100 μL solution of each analyte (10 mM) was added to 3 mL of the dispersion of 1 or 2 in DMF. All the titrations were carried out by gradually adding nitrobenzene (1 × 10−2 M) and Fe3+ (6 × 10−3 M) solutions in an incremental fashion. Each titration was repeated at least three times to get a concordant value. In the time-dependent tests, a 200 μL solution of nitrobenzene (10 mM) and a 100 μL solution of Fe3+ (10 mM) were added to 3 mL of the dispersion of 1 or 2 in DMF, and the luminescence intensity was measured at a certain time interval. For the recyclability experiments, the titration maximum dose was added to the emulsion of 1 and 2 in DMF, and the suspension was shaken for ten minutes. Compounds 1 and 2 can be regenerated by filtering the solution after use and washing several times with DMF to remove nitrobenzene molecules. However, their recyclability for sensing Fe3+ was investigated by exchanging with a DMF solution of KNO3 (10 mM) and then washing several times with DMF to evacuate Fe3+. The slit widths of excitation and emission were fixed at 2.5 and 5.0 nm for 1, and 1.0 and 2.5 nm for 2, respectively.

3. Results and discussion

3.1 Crystal structure description

Single-crystal X-ray analysis reveals that compounds 1 and 2 are isostructural. Hereinafter, the crystal structure of compound 1 is described in detail as a representation. As shown in Fig. 1a, compound 1 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P, and the asymmetric unit consists of two Eu ions. All the Eu ions are eight-coordinated with two O atoms from two DEF molecules and six O atoms from the carboxyl from five DMTDC ligands. The DMTDC ligands in 1 have two different coordination modes, as shown in Fig. 1b and c. Mode I connects two Eu(iii) atoms through four O atoms from the chelating carboxylate group, while mode II links four Eu(iii) atoms through four O atoms from two μ2-η1:η1 carboxylate groups. Each Eu(iii) is linked by μ2-η1:η1 carboxylate groups in mode II to form a paddle-wheel secondary building unit (SBU, Fig. S1, ESI†). These SBUs are consequently extended by DMTDC in mode I into a three dimensional structure. The 3D framework displays three channels in different directions when the guest and coordinated DEF molecules are omitted. The cross section of 6.3 × 6.4 Å along the b axis shows a diamond channel (Fig. 2b), and when viewed along the a and c axes, the rectangle channels are 3.5 × 7.8 Å and 3.2 × 8.6 Å, respectively (Fig. 2a and c). The PLATON analysis revealed that the 3D porous framework contains large voids of 2209.5 Å3 that represent 60.2% volume per unit cell when the guest and coordinated DEF molecules are removed. From a topological point of view, the paddle-wheel SBU can be simplified as a six-connected node, and as a result, the whole framework can be regarded as a uninodal six-connected pcu α-Po topology structure (Fig. 2d).

3.2 X-ray powder diffraction (PXRD) and thermal analysis (TG)

In order to confirm the phase purity of these compounds, the powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns of 1 and 2 were obtained at room temperature. As shown in Fig. S2 and S3 in the ESI,† the peak positions of the theoretical and experimental PXRD patterns are in agreement with each other, indicating single phases of 1 and 2 are formed. The thermogravimetric curves for 1 and 2 under a N2 environment are shown in Fig. 3. The TGA curve of 1 displays a weight loss of 12.65% at 146 °C, resulting from the release of six H2O molecules and one guest DEF molecule per formula unit (calc. 12.45%). Further heating to 345 °C induces another weight loss of 24.00% (calc. 24.07%) with the release of four coordination DEF molecules, followed by decomposition of 1 at about 390 °C. Similarly, the curve of 2 shows a weight loss of 13.82% (calc. 14.21%) below 180 °C which corresponds to six H2O molecules and one guest DEF molecule. After that, the rapid weight loss of 27.01% (calc. 27.46%) at 470 °C corresponds to four coordination DEF molecules. At above 480 °C, the rapid collapse of the whole framework takes place.

3.3 Stability of frameworks

Compounds 1 and 2 are activated via soaking in methanol and dichloromethane for two days each and then outgassed at 140 °C for 4 h under vacuum to obtain the activated samples 1a and 2a. TGA analysis and IR spectra indicate that all the guests and coordinated solvents of as-synthesized 1 and 2 could be completely evacuated by this treatment (Fig. S4–S7, ESI†). Although the PXRD patterns of activated samples 1a and 2a exhibit broad, weak diffraction peaks and are slightly different from those of the as-synthesized samples, the activated samples can recover the original patterns of samples 1 and 2 after being immersed in DMF (Fig. S8 and S9, ESI†). This indicates that the porous framework maintained the crystalline integrity without guests and coordinated solvents. To examine the permanent porosity of activated 1a and 2a, N2 and CO2 gas sorption analyses were performed on the activated samples. As displayed in Fig. 4, both compounds showed no N2 adsorption at 77 K at low pressure. However, the adsorption isotherm of CO2 at 195 K displays a steep rise in the low relative pressure region and can be categorized as type I, indicating a typical physisorption process of a microporous material. It's worth mentioning that there exists a large hysteresis loop during the desorption process due to the strong affinity between the host framework and CO2.26 The Langmuir surface areas calculated from the CO2 sorption isotherm at 195 K are 125 m2 g−1 for 1a and 110 m2 g−1 for 2a, respectively. The relatively high uptake amount of CO2 compared to the nil adsorption of N2 at low pressure suggests that compounds 1a and 2a possess the capacity to selectively adsorb CO2 over N2.

3.4 Photoluminescence properties

The solid-state emission spectra of the free ligand H2DMTDC and compounds 1 and 2 were measured at room temperature, and as shown in Fig. 5, the main emission peaks of H2DMTDC were observed at 377 nm (λex = 248 nm), which may be assigned to π* → n or π* → π transitions.27 Compounds 1 and 2 show the corresponding characteristic emission bonds of Ln3+. The emission spectrum of 1 shows characteristic emission bands of the Eu3+ ion centered at 593 nm, 614 nm, 653 nm and 693 nm, which can be attributed to 5D0 → 7FJ (J = 1, 2, 3, 4) transitions of Eu3+. Under excitation at 322 nm, compound 2 exhibits typical peaks at 491, 546, 589, and 622 nm, corresponding to the electronic transition from the excited state 5D4 to the multiples 7FJ (J = 6, 5, 4, 3), respectively.

3.5 Sensing of organic solvent molecules

Recently, the highly efficient small-molecule sensing properties involving MOFs have attracted considerable attention.2,3 In order to probe the sensing properties of 1 and 2, DMF was chosen as the dispersing agent to carry out the sensing experiments because it is cheaper than DEF, which is used as the solvent for synthesizing the frameworks. Besides, the PXRD analyses verified that compounds 1 and 2 can almost retained their similar framework structures in the DMF solvent (Fig. S10 and S11, ESI†). 200 μL aliquots of each analyte (10 mM) were added to the samples of 1 and 2 dispersed in DMF. As shown in Fig. 6, the emission bands for most analytes were visible at similar positions after the addition of a small amount of analytes, while nitrobenzene completely quenches the luminescence of the framework of 1 and 2, suggesting that 1 and 2 could be used as potential luminescence sensors for detecting nitrobenzene molecules. To investigate the effect of response time on luminescence intensity, we added 200 μL of nitrobenzene to the suspension of 1 and 2 to detect their fluorescence intensity with time. The luminescence intensities of 1 and 2 are significantly rapidly quenched, even if it is immersed in nitrobenzene for less than 25 seconds (Fig. 7 and S12, ESI†), which suggests that compounds 1 and 2 can serve as fast-response, sensitive sensors for nitrobenzene.

To explore the nitrobenzene sensing ability in more detail, fluorescence quenching titrations were performed. The results show that the initial fluorescence intensity progressively decreased with the gradual addition of different concentrations of nitrobenzene to 1 or 2 dispersed in DMF (Fig. 8a and b). The maximum fluorescence intensities for the samples of 1 and 2 are reduced by 98.6% and 98.9%, upon exposure to 466.7 and 333.3 μM concentration of nitrobenzene, respectively. The fluorescence quenching efficiency was further analyzed using the Stern–Volmer (SV) equation, (I0/I) = Ksv[A] + 1, in which I0 is the initial fluorescence intensity before the addition of the analyte, I is the fluorescence intensity in the presence of the analyte, [A] is the molar concentration of the analyte, and Ksv is the quenching constant (M−1). The SV plot of NB was nearly linear at low concentrations (≤83 μM) and subsequently deviated from linearity, bending upwards at higher concentrations (Fig. 8c and d). The calculated Ksv values for nitrobenzene are 3.5 × 104 and 2.8 × 104 M−1 for 1 and 2, respectively, which are comparable to or better than those of previously reported Ln-MOFs, such as {[Tb(DMTDC)1.5(H2O)2]·DEF}n (1.7 × 104 M−1),18b [(Pr2(TATMA)2·4DMF·4H2O]n (4.1 × 103 M−1),28 [Eu2(L)2(DMA)2]n·nH2O (1.4 × 103 M−1),10b {[Eu2(pdba)3(H2O)3]·2H2O}n (3.0 × 103 M−1),29 and {[Eu3(pdba)4(H2O)4]·5H2O}n (1.6 × 103 M−1),29 for sensing nitrobenzene.

It's necessary to test the recyclability of the sensor materials which is crucial for long-term practical applications. The emissions of 1 and 2 can be regenerated with no remarkable loss of the original intensity by washing the samples at least three times with fresh DMF after being quenched (Fig. 9 and S13, ESI†). Such good reversibility can be repeated for five cycles. The PXRD experiment verified that the crystallinity of 1 and 2 could be retained after five repeated cycles (Fig. S14 and S15, ESI†), indicating that 1 and 2 have high reusability and outstanding stability as sensor materials in real-life applications.

The possible reasons for luminescence quenching of 1 and 2 by nitrobenzene could be explained using the following experimental evidence. The PXRD patterns of 1 and 2 in the presence of different analytes are almost identical to those of the as-synthesized 1 and 2 except in methanol, ethanol and DMSO, which suggests that the frameworks remain after the addition of nitrobenzene (Fig. S16 and S17, ESI†).The luminescence quenching is unlikely ascribed to the adsorption by the nitrobenzene molecule because it shows no UV adsorption bands above 400 nm (Fig. S18, ESI†). Moreover, the UV absorption band of nitrobenzene partly overlaps with the excitation bands of 1 and 2 (Fig. S19, ESI†). Thus, the fluorescence quenching response to nitrobenzene compared to other aromatic compounds can be attributed to the photoinduced electron transfer from the excited MOF to electron-deficient nitrobenzene with subsequent non-emissive energy dissipation.10c,30

3.6 Detection of metal cations

Considering that 1 and 2 contain an exposed S active basic site in their frameworks that can interact with metal ions, the fluorescent sensing of metal ions was carried out for 1 and 2.

To investigate the influence of different metal ions on the luminescence of 1 and 2, 3 mg of 1 or 2 was dispersed in 5 mL of DMF solution, forming a suspension solution by an ultrasound method, and then 100 μL of a DMF solution of M(NO3)x (10 mM; M = Na+, Mg2+, Ca2+, Pb2+, Zn2+, Cd2+, Ag+, Mn2+, Co2+, Ni2+, Cu2+, Al3+, Cr3+, Fe3+, respectively) was dropped into the above solutions to form a suspension of metal-1 and metal-2 in DMF. Interestingly, as displayed in Fig. 10, the addition of Fe3+ to the 1 or 2 emulsion can significantly quench the luminescence, whereas other metal ions show no significant effects or weaken the luminescence to some extent. The different effects on the emission indicate that 1 and 2 can be considered as promising luminescent probes of Fe3+ through fluorescent quenching. The PXRD patterns of 1 and 2 after immersing various metal ions were measured (Fig. S20 and S21, ESI†), which are approximately similar to those of 1 and 2 merely with some differences in reflection intensity and position, demonstrating that the basic frameworks remain unchanged during the sensing of Fe3+ ions. The subtle differences may be due to the different orientation of the crystals in the powder samples and the Fe3+ interaction with the corresponding complexes.31 Time-dependent fluorescence intensity was also detected. The results indicated that the luminescence intensity can also be quenched in a short time (25 s, Fig. 11 and S22, ESI†), which reveals that compounds 1 and 2 show high sensitivity for Fe3+.

In order to better understand the variation of fluorescence intensity with the concentration of Fe3+, photoluminescence titration upon the addition of Fe(NO3)3 (6 × 10−3 M) to emulsions were further conducted and showed that the emission intensity progressively decreased with the increase in the concentration of Fe3+ ions (Fig. 12). The fluorescence intensities of 1 and 2 were quenched to 97.6% and 98.3% until the concentration of Fe3+ was increased to 100 μM and 120 μM, respectively. The luminescence titration results can also be treated with the Stern–Volmer equation, (I0/I) = Ksv[A] + 1. The SV plot of Fe3+was nearly linear at low concentrations (≤20 μM) and subsequently deviated from linearity, bending upwards at higher concentrations (Fig. 12). The calculated Ksv values for Fe3+ are 4.3 × 104 and 4.6 × 104 M−1, for 1 and 2, respectively, which are comparable to or much better than those of known Ln-MOFs, such as {[Eu(L)(BPDC)1/2(NO3)]·H3O}n (5.16 × 104 M−1),32a {[Tb(L) (BPDC)1/2(NO3)]·H3O}n (4.30 × 104 M−1),32a [Tb(H2O)5(BTEC)][H(C5H6N2)] 3H2O (2.78 × 104 M−1),32b and {[Eu2L1.5(H2O)2EtOH]·DMF}n (2.94 × 103 M−1).32c The nonlinear and upward curve (Fig. 11) at higher Fe3+ ion concentrations suggests that the luminescence quenching mechanism can be attributed to interactions between Fe3+ and organic ligands because the Lewis basic S atoms of the ligand are exposed in the channels of the framework and can interact with Lewis acidic analytes.

The recyclability of 1 and 2 for sensing Fe3+ was also tested. At first, we tried to simply wash the sample after the sensing of Fe3+ with DMF, but the luminescence intensity especially for compound 2 did not recover to the original level (Fig. S23, ESI†), which is possibly ascribed to Fe3+ ions having been absorbed or bonded by the MOFs. Thus, we tried to immerse Fe3+-absorbed samples in the DMF solution of KNO3 and then washed them with DMF several times. By this treatment, compounds 1 and 2 show recyclability and reversibility after five quenching–recovery cycles without a dramatic intensity loss (Fig. 13 and S24 ESI†). The PXRD patterns after the cycle experiments are comparable to those of the as-synthesized (Fig. S25 and S26, ESI†), which demonstrates that compounds 1 and 2 are beneficial to practical applications.

To better understand the quenching effect of Fe3+ on the luminescence intensity of 1 and 2, the UV/vis spectra of Fe3+ ions in DMF solution were recorded. The UV-vis absorption band of the Fe3+ ions in DMF solution in the range of 250 nm to 400 nm (Fig. S19, ESI†) partly overlaps with the excitation bands of 1 and 2 in DMF solution. These results further indicate that the excitation energy absorbed by the organic ligand DMTDC in 1 and 2 could be transferred to the titrants further to quench the fluorescence. Besides, the color of both complexes changed from white to brown after immersing 1 and 2 in Fe(NO3)3 solutions of DMF and washing with methanol several times. This indicates that Fe3+ ions may have been absorbed or bonded by the MOFs (Fig. S27, ESI†). Moreover, the XPS results show that upon the addition of Fe3+, the S 2p peaks from free thiophene groups of 1 and 2 at 162.6 eV and 162.8 eV are shifted to 162.9 eV and 163.2 eV, respectively (Fig. S28 and S29, ESI†). Unexpectedly, the O 1s XPS of 1 and 2 also show changes before and after the addition of Fe3+ (Fig. S30 and S31, ESI†). The O 1s peaks at 529.8 eV and 530.4 eV are shifted to 530.2 eV and 530.8 eV, respectively, which indicates that Fe3+ ions not only form weak contacts with uncoordinated sulfide atoms, but also show the weak bonding of oxygen atoms from the coordinated carboxylate.33 The FT-IR spectra of 1 and 2 also display discrepancies after the addition of Fe3+ (Fig. 14). For example, the bands at 1632 for 1 and 1630 cm−1 for 2, assigned to the CO (from carboxylate group) stretching vibrations, greatly decreased. Meanwhile, the new bands which appeared in the regions of 1065–1035 cm−1 and 895–860 cm−1 may correspond to Fe–O and Fe–S stretching vibrations.33 All these results suggest that 1 and 2 exhibit weak bonding to Fe3+ ions, and this bonding reduces the antenna effect of the organic ligands and lanthanide ions, leading to the luminescence quenching.32a,34,35

Conclusions

In summary, two luminescent lanthanide–organic frameworks with uninodal six-connected pcu α-Po topology structures have been successfully constructed based on a thiophene-containing carboxylate ligand. The luminescence properties revealed that 1 and 2 showed a fast response and high sensitivity towards nitrobenzene and Fe3+ ions with favorable recyclability by drastic fluorescence quenching, indicating that they can serve as promising candidates for detecting these substances. The possible luminescence quenching mechanisms for nitrobenzene and Fe3+ ions were discussed and attributed to the electron and resonance energy transfer between the MOFs and analytes. This study provides an easy and facile route to design and yield new luminescent Ln-MOFs with thiophene groups as promising functional materials.
