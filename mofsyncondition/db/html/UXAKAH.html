Abstract

We have synthesized new luminescent coordination polymers using 4,4׳-sulfonyldibenzoic acid (H2SDA) and several lanthanide ions by open crystallization in DMF. Three different 1-D structures were obtained, as determined by single-crystal X-ray diffraction: [La(η2-NO3)(μ-SDA)(DMF)3]n (1), [Sm(η2-NO3)(μ-SDA)(DMF)2·2DMF]n (3), [Ln(η2-NO3)(μ-SDA)(DMF)2·DMF]n (Ln = Eu (4), Dy (5), Ho (6), Er (7), Tm (8) and Lu (9)). A 2-D structure, [Er2(η2-SDA)3(DMF)(H2O)3·2DMF]n (10), was also obtained. The measured photophysical properties of these compounds seem to be interesting for applications (high lifetime, narrow line width and high degree of color purity). In addition, emission spectra, spontaneous emission coefficients (Arad and Anrad), and intensity parameters (Ω2 and Ω4), from spectroscopic data of Eu3+, were used to evaluate crystal stabilities. Differences in the spectra acquired before and after DMF removal clearly reflected the changes taking place in the ion environment.

1. Introduction

Crystalline coordination polymers (CPs) have garnered scientific interest due to, among other factors, the multitude of possible structures brought about by the combination of metal ion and multifunctional ligand [1], [2]. Among the different possible ligands, flexible and semi-rigid polycarboxylic acids provide a number of coordination modes when bound to the metal ion and allow for a variety of conformations in the solid state, ensuring the stability of the crystalline structures formed [3]. These considerations are popularizing molecules such as the semi-rigid V-shaped dicarboxylates for the construction of novel coordination polymers. When featuring aromatic rings interspersed between the carboxylate groups, these ligands may promote greater supramolecular interactions in the polymer network [4], [5], [6]. Several works describe the synthesis of CPs using the V-shaped 4,4׳-sulfonyldibenzoic acid (H2SDA) as the ligand, because their donor oxygen groups enable various coordination modes according to the geometry of the metal ion. Moreover, the formation of these coordination polymers derives directly from the relative orientation between the carboxylate groups, a consequence of the distorted tetrahedral geometry of the sulfone. This gives rise to interesting crystalline structures not observed when using other rigid, more linear ligands [7], [8], [9], [10], [11]. These structural considerations allow for the perspectives for applications in catalysis, gas separation, luminescence, and others [12], [13], [14], [15], [16].

Despite the remarkable progress in the field, stability concerns still restrict the widespread application of these and several other CPs. The IRMOFs derived from the prototypical MOF-5 exemplify well this issue [17]. IRMOFs, or isoreticular metal-organic frameworks, are series of coordination polymers with the same topology. Some Zn-IRMOFs are formed by combining Zn2+ and ligands based on terephthalic acid, resulting in host-guest systems applicable in the adsorption of gas molecules such as N2, CO2, and CH4 [18], [19], [20], [21]. Their tetrahedral ZnO4 clusters have weak oxygen-zinc interaction, prone to attack by water molecules. This leads to the formation of water-containing Zn clusters that partially replace the ZnO4 units and render the IRMOFs unstable [22]. Due to the enormous potential of IRMOFs and other CPs, obtaining new compounds and understanding the many factors that determine their structure and their stability in different environments has gained prominence in the field. Intrinsic and technologically interesting optical properties, such as photostability, long lifetimes (>10−4 s), short Stokes/anti-Stokes displacements (<200 nm), and narrow, well defined lines, have stimulated the use of lanthanide ions in the synthesis of coordination compounds [23], [24], [25]. Another important feature is the influence of the ligand coordination on the energy transfer mechanisms to the lanthanide which, in some cases, can enhance its luminescence. Changes in the vicinity of the ion rule the spectroscopic properties of these compounds, i.e., symmetry operations of the coordination polyhedron are reflected in their excitation and emission spectra. Compounds with Eu3+ are particularly suited for such structural investigations, as the ion 4f6 electronic configuration allows for a more rigorous correlation between symmetry changes and the ensuing spectroscopic properties [26].

The interesting crystalline structures and the unusual magnetic and luminescent properties have motivated work on complexes of lanthanide ions and the anion of SDA2−.

The compound synthesized with Dy3+ ([Dy2(SDA)3(H2O)5]n), for example, has provided access to the 2D/2D polyrotaxane Dy3+ metal-organic framework, which shows slow magnetization relaxation of the single molecule magnet behavior [27]. Cheng et al. [28] reported an extensive research involving several CPs with lanthanides produced hydrothermally. This work has shown that some isostructural compounds can be formed (for Ln = Eu, Gd, Tb, Dy), [Ln2(SDA)3(H2O)5]n with 2D weaving structures. On the other hand, altering the metal ion (Ln = Ho, Er, Tm, Y) generate different frameworks with different composition [Ln2(SDA)3(H2O)4·H2O]n and structure, in which one water molecule is not part of first coordination sphere anymore. Another interesting system with lutetium is described in the paper, namely [Lu2(SDA)·3(H2O)3·4H2O]n, with the 1-D structure containing binuclear units connected by organic ligands. The lanthanide contraction accounted for this behavior, according to the authors. All these studies were performed in aqueous solutions and produced 2-D interpenetrated (weaving) structures, except the last one (Lu). We were interested in structural changes and modification of properties, in SDA-Ln systems caused by the use of dimethylformamide (DMF) instead of water.

In this work we describe the synthesis and characterization of ten new coordination polymers produced by combining H2SDA and the ions La3+, Ce3+, Sm3+, Eu3+, Dy3+, Ho3+, Er3+, Tm3+, and Lu3+ mediated by crystallization under mild heating in DMF. Their photophysical properties have been explored by light emission of the compounds ranging from the visible to the infrared regions, indicating that these compounds are promising in the formation of light-emitting devices. The correlation between the luminescence and structural aspects were studied using the symmetry modifications of the Eu3+ chemical environment. These changes were evaluated in terms of their emission spectrum pattern, spontaneous emission coefficients (Arad and Anrad) and intensity parameters (Ω2 and Ω4). A previously reported similar system was synthesized and compared to the one presented in this work, as to the changes promoted by structural modifications, caused by interactions before and after removal of the solvent present in the crystal structure.

2. Experimental section

2.1. Materials and general methods

All chemicals were reagent grade, and were used without purification. The syntheses took place in 10 mL penicillin vials in which DMF solutions of ligand and metal were mixed. The open vials were then kept in a silicone oil bath at 50 °C for 14 days. The resulting crystals were collected, washed several times with DMF and kept in closed penicillin vials containing small amounts of solvent. The photoluminescence properties where measured in a spectrofluorometer Horiba-Jobin Yvon Fluorolog-3 with a continuous 450 W xenon lamp and UV xenon flash tube for excitation, double-grating monochromator in the excitation and UV–vis (ultraviolet-visible) emission position, single-grating monochromator in the NIR (near infrared) emission position, R928P and H10330A-75 Hamamatsu photomultipliers respectively to UV–vis and NIR range emissions. All emission spectra were corrected by (the monochromator) spectral response of the monochromators and using silicon photodiode reference detector, to monitor and compensate for variation in the xenon lamp output, using typical correction spectra provided by the manufacturer. The lifetime experiments used the same spectroscopic apparatus, but with a 450 W xenon lamp in pulse mode. The lifetime of Eu3+ was obtained by monitoring the 5D0→7F2 (619 nm) emissions upon excitations at 395 nm. The emission quantum efficiency (η) was calculated from Eq. The powder x-rays diffraction analysis of well-ground samples was carried out with a Bruker D8 Advance diffractometer (Cu Kα radiation, λ=1.541 Å) equipped with a LynxEye detector. Typical 2θ range was 5–50°, step size of 0.01°, and acquisition time of 1.0 s per step. Data collection was carried out at room temperature 25 °C.

2.2. Synthesis of coordination polymers

The optimal metal:H2SDA molar ratio for obtaining the compounds 1–10 was 2.5:1. The methodology enabled the formation of elongated, colorless and transparent crystals with well defined faces. When the crystals were taken from their solvent (DMF), however, they gradually lost transparency and became whitish.

Synthesis of [La(η2-NO3)(μ-SDA)(DMF)3]n (1). Reaction mixture of H2SDA (0.38 mmol), La(NO3)3.6H20 (0.15 mmol), DMF (6.0 mL) was heated at 50 °C for 14 days. The colorless crystals were filtered off, washed with DMF several times and stored in an eppendorf with DMF.

Synthesis of CeSDA (2). Reaction mixture of H2SDA (0.38 mmol), Ce(NO3)3.6H20 (0.15 mmol), DMF (6.0 mL) was heated at 50 °C for 14 days. The colorless crystals were filtered off, washed with DMF several times and stored in an eppendorf with DMF.

Synthesis of [Sm(η2-NO3)(μ-SDA)(DMF)2·2DMF]n (3). Reaction mixture of H2SDA (0.38 mmol), Sm(NO3)3.6H20 (0.15 mmol), DMF (6.0 mL) was heated at 50 °C for 14 days. The colorless crystals were filtered off, washed with DMF several times and stored in an eppendorf with DMF.

Synthesis of [Ln(η2-NO3)(μ-SDA)(DMF)2 ·DMF]n (4–9). Reaction mixture of H2SDA (0.38 mmol), Ln(NO3)3.6H20 (0.15 mmol; Ln = Eu (4), Dy (5), Ho (6), Er (7), Tm (8) and Lu (9)) DMF (6.0 mL) was heated at 50 °C for 14 days. The colorless crystals were filtered off, washed with DMF several times and stored in an eppendorf with DMF.

2.3. Single crystal X-ray diffraction studies

X-ray analyses were carried out using KUMA KM4CCD or STOE IPDS instruments.

2.3.1. KUMA

Diffraction data were recorded at 293(2) K on a KUMA KM4 (Wrocław, Poland) diffractometer with graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (0.71073 Å), equipped with a Sapphire 2 CCD camera (Oxford Diffraction, Yarnton, England), Data collection was performed using CrysAlis PRO [15] (Oxford Diffraction, Yarnton, England) in the ω-scan mode. Analytical absorption correction was applied for all strongly absorbing crystals.

2.3.2. STOE

Diffraction intensity data were collected on an IPDS 2T dual-beam diffractometer (STOE & Cie GmbH, Darmstadt, Germany) at 120,0(2) K with Mo-Kα radiation of a microfocus x-ray source (GeniX 3D Mo High Flux, Xenocs, Sassenage, 50 kV, 1.0 mA, λ = 71.069 pm), The crystal was thermostated in nitrogen stream at 120 K using CryoStream-800 device (Oxford CryoSystem, UK) during the entire experiment. Data collection and data reduction were controlled by X-Area 1.75 program (STOE, 2015). An absorption correction was performed on the integrated reflections by a combination of frame scaling, reflection scaling and a spherical absorption correction. Outliers have been rejected according to Blessing׳s method.

Structures were solved using direct methods with SHELXS-13 program and refined using full-matrix least-squares refinement based on F2 by SHELXL-2013 (Sheldrick, 2008) run under control of WinGx (Farrugia, 2012). All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. C-bound hydrogen atoms were refined in their geometrically idealized positions with isotropic temperature factors 1.2 or 1.5 times the equivalent isotropic temperature factors, Ueq, of their attached atoms. The O-H hydrogen atoms, in 9, were found in the differential Fourier electron density map and were refined with constraints.

Compounds 1 and 3–9 are all formed by 1-D chains, in which two SDA2− moieties bridge two dimeric Ln clusters. The structures of 1, [La(η2-NO3)(μ-SDA)(DMF)3]n, and 3, [Sm(η2-NO3)(μ-SDA)(DMF)2·2DMF}]n, are different, but belong to the P1̅ triclinic group (No. 2). Although both compounds are coordinated by SDA2- in two modes (Figs. S1 and S2), the difference between them is evident in their coordination scheme: compound 1 has coordination number nine, and 3 has coordination number eight.

Compound 2 (Ce) also crystallizes in the tricilinic system. Its most remarkable feature is the connectivity of the SDA2- ligands, which do not connect binuclear paddle wheels. Instead, bridging is extended vertically forming infinite columns of multinuclear stacked paddle wheels with -Ce-O-Ce-O-Ce-O- chains. The structure of 2 was only approximately determined, mainly because of twinning and modulation-related problems preventing the unambiguous solution.

Compounds 4 to 9 are isostructural, and crystallize in the monoclinic system, with formula [Ln(η2-NO3)(μ-SDA)(DMF)2·DMF]n (Ln = Eu, Dy, Ho, Er, Tm, and Lu). They belong to space group I2/a (No. 15), also called C2/c or I2/c. We preferred the I2/a setting because the network translation basis vector c is collinear with the period of the coordination polymer. In other words, their 1-D chains extend along the [001] direction, as seen in Fig. S3.

A second compound with Er (10), belonging to the P1̅ triclinic group (No. 2), was obtained from ErCl3. Therefore, unlike what is observed in structures 1–9, no nitrate anion can be found in its structure, and the compound has the formula [Er2(η2-SDA)3(DMF)(H2O)3·2DMF]n. Its structure also departs from the general trend observed for the other compounds, namely the formation of 1-D coordination polymers. Instead, 1-D chains are crosslinked by SDA2− ligands, forming a 2-D coordination network (Fig. S4). Preliminary attempts to obtain similar structures using other lanthanide chlorides were not successful.

Despite the fact that some of the compounds reported here are not isostructural, a few general trends can be observed. The use of Ln(NO3)3 as the starting material and the absence of water led to structures in which a bidentate nitrate group remains bound to the metal atom, partly blocking access to the lanthanide ion. This seems to prevent higher coordination by the lignd and, as a result, only 1-D chains are formed. No interweaving takes place, contrary to the structures found in previous studies by Liu et al. [27] and Cheng et al. [28]. This less compact arrangement provides better access to the voids formed by the bent ligand. The metal ion coordination number was nine for compound 1, eight for 3–9, and seven for 10. Compound 2 also has coordination number seven.

3.2. Spectroscopic properties

The room temperature, solid state excitation and emission spectra for H2SDA (λEm = 388 nm, λEx = 344 nm) and compound 1 (λEm = 428 nm, λEx = 333 nm) were obtained. The H2SDA excitation spectrum reveals three bands centered at 326 nm, 344 nm, and 360 nm, related to π* ← π transitions. The relaxation of these transitions is evidenced by three other bands in the emission spectrum at 388 nm, 409 nm and 438 nm (Fig. 1).

The excitation spectrum of 1 exhibits a clearly different profile, with only one broad band centered at 333 nm. Because the La3+ ion has no f electrons, and consequently does not undergo f-f transitions, this signal must be related only to the ligand in the coordination polymer. The rigidity of the coordination polymer restricts the vibrational levels, and cause this effect. Furthermore, the emission of 1 also presents a single band, centered at 428 nm.

The luminescence of compounds 3, 4, 5, and 6, which emit in the visible region, were investigated by means of their emission and excitation spectra in the solid phase. The samples were kept in DMF and at room temperature at all times. The excitation spectrum for 3, monitoring at λem = 560 nm (Fig. 2A), show the H2SDA ligand band centered at 310 nm, indicating a π* ← π transition. The spectrum also reveals broad bands in the region between 325 and 550 nm, referring to 4D3/2, 6P3/2 and 4G5/2 ← 6H5/2 Sm3+ transitions. Fig. 2B shows the band structures for the emission spectrum of SDA2− (λex = 350 nm) between 370 and 650 nm, referring to the π* → π transition. Moreover, Sm3+ emission signals are observed at 560 and 704 nm, indicating the presence of 4G5/2 → 6H5/2 and 6H11/2 transitions, respectively. The chromaticity diagram shows the predominance of the blue color of compound 3 (Fig. 2C). The CIE chromaticity diagram is a 2-coordinate system used to represent the color perceived by the human eye. In the case of the emission of compound 3, the coordinates are (0.20; 0.18).

The excitation spectrum for 4, monitoring at λem = 619 nm (Fig. 3A), reveals a broad band centered at 310 nm referring to π*←π transition of the SDA2− ligand in the coordination polymer. Moreover, narrow lines between 315 and 550 nm are observed, and were assigned to the 5D4–0, 5L6, 5GJ and 5H3,6 ← 7F1,0 transitions of the Eu3+ ion, located in the region between 315 and 550 nm, were observed, which is typical for 4f6 transitions. The ligand band is less intense than the metal ion transitions, indicating that SDA2− acts as a poor sensitizer for the luminescence system. The emission spectrum of 4 (Fig. 3B) shows the lines related to the decay of 5L6 ← 7F0 transition (λex = 394 nm). We observed the emission lines related to the decay of the 5D0 emitting state of Eu3+ ion to lower 7FJ energy states (with J = 0, 1, 2, 3 and 4). The most intense transition in the spectrum is the 5D0 → 7F2; this transition is the primary contribution to the overall red color with coordinate equal the (0.68; 0.31) as shown in the chromaticity diagram (Fig. 3C). The lifetime for the 5D0 emitter level was determined by fitting the exponential decay curve with a monoexponential function, with a lifetime equal to τ = 1.47 ± 0.01 ms (Fig. S5).

The luminescence of compound 5 was studied by monitoring the 4F9/2 → 6H15/2 excitation at λem = 573 nm and by measuring the emission (exciting the 5P5/2 ← 6H15/2 transition at λex = 367 nm), respectively for the excitation and emission spectra. The excitation spectrum of 5 also has the SDA2− π* ← π transition- at 310 nm. Furthermore, the intra-4f9 transitions 4F9/2, 4G9/2, 4I13/2,15/2, and 5P5/2,7/2 ← 6H15/2 for Dy3+ takes place in the region 325–500 nm (Fig. 4A). The emission spectrum for 5 (Fig. 4B) shows two transitions, 4F9/2 → 6H13/2 and 6H15/2, which generate a blue color with color coordinates (0.32; 0.37) in the chromaticity diagram (Fig. 4C).

The luminescence of compound 6 (Fig. 5) show the excitation spectrum obtained by monitoring the 3F4, 5S2 → 5I8 transition at λem = 559 nm. Fig. 5A shows the Ho3+ f-f transitions 5F1,2,4, 5G4,6, 5S2 ← 5I8. The band SDA2− π* ← π is not observed for 6, indicating that the energy transfer between the level of ligand and the emitter level of the ion are not in resonance, making ion sensitization unfavorable. The emission spectrum shows three bands representing the relaxation of excited states for the fundamental transitions 5G5→5I7 and 5F3,5F4, 5S2 → 5I8 (λex = 452 nm) as shown in Fig. 5B. The chromaticity diagram shows a color coordinates equal to (0.39; 0.54), indicating the green color of the compound (Fig. 5C).

Of the materials studied, compounds 6, 7, and 10 emit in the near infrared region (Fig. S6). The emission spectrum of 6 (exciting the 5F1,2, 5G6 and 3K8 ← 5I8 transition at λex = 452 nm) presented the two characteristic signals of Ho3+ (Fig. S6A). These signals, located at 968 and 1192 nm correspond to the 5F5→5I7 and 5I6→5I8 transitions, respectively. For 7, exciting the 2H11/2 ← 4G11/2 transition (at λexc = 515 nm) yielded a signal centered at λem = 1559 nm, which refers to the 4I13/2→4I15/2 transition of Er3+ (Fig. S6B). The spectra of compounds 10 and 7 are very similar, and thus a detailed analysis is unnecessary.

The luminescence measurements for the other compounds were carried out, but it was not possible to observe the expected excitation and emission lines of the lanthanides. This may be due to the presence of solvent, which causes luminescence quenching. Thus, we focused this study solely on systems with observable luminescence.

3.3. Coordination environment of the europium ion

Compound 4 has only one europium ion occupying the metallic site of the coordination polymer, with coordination number 8, producing slightly distorted square antiprism EuO8 polyhedra with C4v symmetry. The photoluminescence of 4 reveals important information about its symmetry. The emission spectrum features the 5D0 → 7F0 transition, which limits the symmetry around the ion to C1, Cn, Cnv and Cs point groups consistent with the symmetry found for the coordination polyhedron [29], [30], [31]. The crystallographic data show the presence of only one Eu atom related to the second in the paddle wheel by an inversion center, and the spectroscopic data supports that, as noted by the uniqueness of its lifetime (τ = 1.47 ms ± 0.01, Fig. S5) and the presence of only one band for the 5D0 → 7F0 transition obtained by its spectral deconvolution (Fig. S7).

In a similar system, but using water instead of DMF, Cheng et al. obtained EuO8 coordination spheres and a 2D interweaving coordination polymer by hydrothermal reaction of H2SDA and europium ions [28]. The two metal centers, named Eu2 and Eu1, are very similar, albeit crystallographically distinct – both have C4V point symmetry similar to that described in compound 4. The metal centers are interconnected by O3-C11-O4, O9-C17-O8, O28-C53-O27, and O30-C55-O32, and the distance Eu1-Eu2 is equal to 4.184 Å. Its emission spectrum also shows the transition 5D0 → 7F0, indicating a good correlation with its point group (Fig. 6). The radiative decay curve presents monoexponential fit (τ = 0.36 ms; shown in Fig. S8), indicating that despite the crystallographic data informing that the Eu1 and Eu2 sites are spectroscopically distinct, they are chemically similar. In other words, it seems that the very small changes around the environment of the two ions cause no perceptible effect in their spectroscopy.

Both Cheng׳s system and compound 4 feature the same metal and ligand, but different symmetries around the metal ion (Fig. 5). The correlation between the systems containing Eu3+ was made by comparing the ratios of integrated areas (R02/01 = 5D0→7F25D0→7F1) present in the emission spectra. This estimate allowed us to assess the spectral profile and atomic interactions by the metal center intensity parameters (Ωλ) with similar point groups. We observed that Cheng׳s compound has a shorter lifetime than 4. This is related to the presence of -OH oscillators coming from coordinating water molecules that directly contribute to the high fraction of non-radiative deactivation of the system (Anrad = 2515.0 s−1), and this is reflected in the low quantum efficiency (equal to 9.4%). Compound 4 has an emission quantum efficiency equal to 73.3% and this is related mainly to the low value of Anrad (181.8 s−1) and high lifetime (τ = 1.47 ms).

Comparison of the intensity parameters (Ω׳s) should suggest a relationship with the degree of covalent interactions between the ligand and the lanthanide ion. When dynamic coupling is the dominant mechanism, as in the most cases of coordination compounds, the intensity parameter Ω4 is more appropriate for estimating the degree of covalency [33]. Compound 4 has Ω4 = 9.8×10–20 cm2 which is higher than the one we found for Chang׳s compound (Ω4 = 0.9×10–20 cm2). This suggests that coordination in 4 has a higher degree of covalency than the coordination in Cheng׳s compound.

3.4. Structural change with exposure to the atmosphere

The use of Eu3+ as a structural probe is well documented [34], [35]. Hence, one should be able to monitor coordination environment changes between the ions through variations in the photoluminescence spectrum. The excitation spectra (monitoring at λem = 619 nm) of compounds 4 and 4* (after drying for DMF removal) show a very similar behavior, in which the lines assigned to the intra-4f6 transitions are clearly present (Fig. 7A). There has been, however, an increase in the π* ← π transition of SDA2- compared to the 5L6 ← 7F0 transition in 4*, which must be related to changes in the ligand structure. We believe that removal of DMF (and probably exchange of it with water) causes loss of crystallinity and formation of new crystalline domains. The emission spectra of 4 and 4* were also compared (Fig. 7B), with excitation at 394 nm. Both spectra show the 5D0 → 7F0 transition, demonstrating the absence of an inversion center in the materials and the restriction of symmetry in the ion neighborhood to specific groups of symmetry C1, Cn, Cnv, or Cs. The 5D0 → 7F2 transition is hypersensitive to modifications in the Eu3+ chemical environment. Both 4 and 4* feature two emission lines related to these transitions, centered on 612 nm and 619 nm. However, a difference in the intensity ratios of the two bands is noticed, indicating the change in symmetry around the metal ion with the evolution of 4 of 4*.

Furthermore, the ratio of the integrated areas R02/01 showed values equal to 5.5 and 5.6 to 4 and 4* respectively. These high R02/01 values indicate that the compound 4* must possess a low symmetry for the Eu3+ environment. The intensity parameter Ω4 was 9.8×10–20 cm2 for 4 and 8.7×10–20 cm2 for 4*, indicating a decrease in the degree of covalence after removal of DMF.

The X-ray diffraction pattern of compound 4, calculated from crystallographic data, was compared to the powder pattern experimentally obtained for 4* (Fig. S10). The pattern of 4* shows most peaks found on the calculated pattern for 4 (some with slight shifts in 2θ probably due to different temperatures of single crystal and powder measurements), in addition to a few new peaks. At this moment, an unambiguous explanation for this observation cannot be made. In any case, most of the spectroscopic properties of 4 are preserved. This fact can be an attractive feature from the point of view of stability of new materials for applications in light emitting devices.

4. Conclusions

We reported the synthesis of ten new coordination polymers based on the combination of the V-shaped ligand 4,4׳-sulfonyldibenzoic acid and lanthanide ions. The methodology used has yielded three new crystalline 1-D structures: [La(η2-NO3)(μ-SDA)(DMF)3]n (1), [Sm(η2-NO3)(μ-SDA)(DMF)2·2DMF]n (3), and [Ln(η2-NO3)(μ -SDA)(DMF)2·DMF]n (Ln = Eu (4), Dy (5), Ho (6), Er (7), Tm (8) and Lu (9)), as well as a 2-D structure, [Er2(η2-SDA)3(DMF)(H2O)3·2DMF]n (10). DMF molecules were found both to coordinate the metal ion and to lie in voids within the structure. These voids were present in the 1-D and 2-D crystals alike, arising from the shape of the ligand. Loss of DMF and consequent structural collapse accounted for the air-sensitivity of the crystals.

We obtained excitation and emission spectra for all compounds, except for the La- Ce- Sm- Tm- and Lu-compounds, and carried out detailed spectroscopic studies for the Eu-compound. The coordination environment for this compound correlated very well with its spectroscopic properties.

Another europium coordination polymer, previously reported, was also synthesized. We compared its spectroscopic properties with (4), and demonstrated that the different coordination environments cause considerable changes in the luminescence profile. This allowed us to use the technique as a structural probe to discern between the different compounds, and also to follow the solvent loss observed in (4).
