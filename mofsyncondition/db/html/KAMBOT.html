Since Gomberg reported the triphenylmethyl radical over 110 years ago,1 a number of stable radical species have been characterised.2–13 Organic radicals have played significant roles in many fields on account of their importance in understanding the nature of chemical bonds, acting as intermediates in chemical reactions, and having potential applications as functional materials.6–13 Electron spin density distribution is not only the key factor to determine the physicochemical properties of radicals, but also important for the charge transport in semiconductors. For instance, the electron hopping process from a π-radical anion to the adjacent neutral molecule is responsible for charge transport in the semiconductor devices.14–17 This raises the question of whether there is charge pinning at the semiconductor interface, involving whether the electron density of the π-radical anion is delocalized on the whole molecule (A, Scheme 1) or localized at a specific space (B)? It has also been proposed that charge pinning occurring at the interface between a metal and a neutral π-molecule is more important during injection of electrons in the semiconductor device.14 These are not yet well clarified due to the absence of experimental evidence.

Tuning the electron spin density distribution is thus of great importance. Numerous efforts have been made to control the unpaired electrons in the past decades by using various strategies, including introduction of bulky substituents, extension of π-conjugated systems, and incorporation of heteroatoms.6–13 However, these strategies do not make remarkable changes of electron spin density distribution. In addition, they need variations of the molecular composition or skeleton. Alternatively, one can speculate that electrostatic attraction may readily regulate the moving of the unpaired electron, e.g. the location of the unpaired electron might be greatly affected by the alkali metal cation via electrostatic attraction in a radical anion. This does not need skeleton alteration of the molecule, but instead might lead to a switchable process due to the labile electrostatic interactions. The electrostatic approach thus has an advantage in tuning the electron spin density and further physicochemical properties of radicals. However, considerable changes of electron spin density distribution by electrostatic attraction have not been experimentally observed in the solid state.

Herein, we report a one-electron reduction of 2,7-tBu2-PTO (1, PTO = pyrene-4,5,9,10-tetraone) with potassium in the presence or absence of different macrocyclic ethers, which afforded two radical anion salts 1K(cryp) and 1K(18c6) (crypt = cryptand[2.2.2], 18c6 = 18-crown-6), a radical tetramer [1K(15c5)]4 (15c5 = 15-crown-5), and a radical polymer (1K)2n, respectively. The electron spin density distribution of radical anion 1˙− was modulated by electrostatic attraction from the whole PTO plane in 1K(cryp) to half part of the plane in 1K(18c6), both in the solid state and solution. Furthermore, reciprocal transformation can be successfully achieved between the two salts. Radical tetramer [1K(15c5)]4 and radical polymer (1K)2n exhibit strong intramolecular π–π coupling, and (1K)2n features an antiferromagnetic one-dimensional chain. This work represents the first spin density modulation of a radical anion by electrostatic attraction without skeleton alteration.

As redox-active ligands, o-semiquinone radical species serve as potential building blocks for functional materials, dynamic behavior investigation and molecular recognition.18 The PTO derivative 119 is symmetric with two carbonyl groups at each side. Its reduced radical species would be an ideal candidate to verify the conjecture on the control of the unpaired electron by electrostatic attraction. To this end, treatment of 1 with one equivalent of potassium in the presence of cryptand[2.2.2] and 18-crown-6 in THF at ambient temperature led to the formation of 1K(cryp) and 1K(18c6) in moderate yields, respectively (Scheme 2). The newly formed products were investigated by single crystal X-ray diffraction, electron paramagnetic resonance spectroscopy (EPR), and UV-Vis spectroscopy, in conjunction with theoretical calculations.

Single crystals of 1K(cryp) and 1K(18c6) suitable for X-ray crystallographic analysis20 were obtained from THF at −20 °C. Their structures with selected bond parameters are illustrated in Fig. 1 and Fig. S1 and S2 (ESI†). 1K(cryp) crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/c featuring a centrosymmetric geometry with no contacts to the K atom. 1K(18c6) crystallizes in the orthorhombic space group P212121 with two contacts to the K atom. One K⋯O distance (2.668(3) Å) is shorter than the other (2.847(3) Å), and the latter is comparable to the K⋯O distances in the (K-18c6) moiety. Both 1K(cryp) and 1K(18c6) show nearly planar PTO backbones. The C–O bonds in 1K(cryp) (1.229(3) Å and 1.238(3) Å) are elongated, compared with that (average 1.215 Å) in the neutral compound 1.19 The C–C bonds (C1–C8′ 1.490(3), C8–C1′ 1.490(3) Å) in the C2O2 moieties of 1K(cryp) are shorter than those in 1 (1.545(8) Å), showing the semiquinone character. However, the situation is remarkably different in 1K(18c6). The lengths of the C–O bonds (1.202(5) Å and 1.218(5) Å) in the C2O2 moiety non-coordinating to the K atom are comparable to those in 1, while the lengths of the C–O bonds (C2–O1 1.253(5) Å and C3–O2 1.263(5) Å) in the C2O2 moiety coordinating to the K atom are much longer than those in 1, and even longer than those in 1K(cryp). Correspondingly, the two C–C bonds in the C2O2 moieties of 1K(18c6) are pronouncedly different. The length of the C–C bond (C12–C13 1.534(6) Å) in the C2O2 moiety non-coordinating to the K atom is close to that in neutral 1, while the length of the C–C bond (C2–C3 1.458(6) Å) in the C2O2 moiety of the other side is notably shorter than that in 1, displaying the semiquinone nature of this C2O2 moiety. The discussion of bond lengths suggests that the unpaired electron is delocalized on both sides of the PTO plane in 1K(cryp), while only on one side in 1K(18c6).

Theoretical calculations were performed to study their electronic structures.21 The crystal structure of 1˙− in both salts was taken as the starting geometry for optimization at the UB3LYP/6-31G* level and the stationary points were checked by frequency calculations. The crystal structure of 1˙− in 1K(cryp) was well reproduced, while that in 1K(18c6) was reproduced only when the K atom and 18-crown-6 are added in optimization, otherwise the geometry converged to the same geometry of 1˙− in 1K(cryp) (Tables S2 and S3, ESI†). The electron spin density in 1K(cryp) (Fig. 2A) is centrosymmetric and polarized with the majority delocalized on two C2O2 moieties (0.37 e × 2) and two central C atoms (C3 and C3′, 0.114 e × 2) of the PTO backbone. Sharply different from 1K(cryp), the unpaired electron in 1K(18c6) (Fig. 2B) is mainly delocalized on one C2O2 moiety (0.78 e) coordinating to the K atom with very little negative charge on the non-coordinating C2O2 moiety, which is consistent with the above analysis on bond lengths. It is noteworthy that the values of the spin density on the C atoms at the 1, 3, 6, and 8 positions (i.e. C5, C7, C5′, and C7′ atoms) of the PTO backbone in 1K(cryp) are negative, while turn positive in 1K(18c6) (C8, C10, C15, and C17 atoms), though their absolute values are the same. The transformation of electron spin from polarized to non-polarized is induced by K coordination.

The EPR spectra of 1K(cryp) and 1K(18c6) in THF solution at 298 K both display quintet signals at g = 2.0051 for 1K(cryp) (Fig. S5, ESI†) and g = 2.0048 for 1K(18c6) (Fig. S6, ESI†), attributed to the hyperfine coupling with four 1H nuclei (I = 1/2) of the PTO moiety. The hyperfine coupling constant values for both salts are the same (a(1H) = 1.44 G), indicating that four carbon atoms of two compounds (C5, C7, C5′, and C7′ in 1K(cryp) and C8, C10, C15, and C17 in 1K(18c6)) have similar spin densities, which experimentally confirmed the theoretical prediction. The peak shapes of two salts are slightly different, perhaps related to the polarization of electron spins. Strikingly, their UV-vis absorption spectra measured in THF solutions under a nitrogen atmosphere together with DT-DFT calculations suggest that solid-state structures are maintained in solution (Fig. S12, ESI†). The maximum absorption (619 nm) of 1K(18c6) is blue-shifted compared to that of 1K(cryp) (733 nm). TD-DFT calculations at the UCAM-B3LYP/6-31+G(d) level reproduced the UV spectra and indicated that maximum absorption at 733 nm is attributed to the HOMO(α) → LUMO+1(α) transition for 1K(cryp), while that at 619 nm is mainly attributed to the HOMO−1(α) → LUMO(α) and HOMO(β) → LUMO(β) transitions for 1K(18c6) (Fig. S15, S16 and Table S5, ESI†).

Further experiments showed that 1K(cryp) and 1K(18c6) can be chemically transformed to one another (Scheme 2), as monitored by UV spectroscopy and single crystal X-ray diffraction. Upon the addition of one equivalent of cryptand[2.2.2] to the THF solution of 1K(18c6), the solution changed from green to brown in several minutes and afforded 1K(cryp) as black crystals in high yield after work-up. The opposite process occurred when one equivalent of 18-crown-6 and KI was added into the solution of 1K(cryp) in THF, which regenerated the dark green solution of 1K(18c6).

To explore the influence of macrocyclic ethers in the reaction of 1 with one equivalent of potassium, we carried out the reduction with less bulky 15-crown-5 and without any macrocyclic ethers, respectively (Scheme 3). Interestingly, a radical tetramer [1K(15c5)]4 was obtained as black needle crystals when employing 15-crown-5. By contrast, the reduction in the absence of macrocyclic ethers resulted in the formation of a radical polymer (1K)2n with a one-dimensional chain (1D chain).

Both [1K(15c5)]4 and (1K)2n crystallize in the triclinic space group P. The structure of [1K(15c5)]4 (Fig. S3, ESI†) features a double layer geometry, in which four PTO moieties are bridged by two K atoms. Similar to 1K(18c6), the C–O (1.255–1.261 Å) and C–C (1.457(4)–1.464(4) Å) bond lengths in the coordinated C2O2 moieties of the PTO planes in [1K(15c5)]4 are close to those in 1K(18c6) (C–O: 1.253(5) Å and 1.263(5) Å, C–C: 1.458(6) Å), revealing the semiquinone properties; and the C–O (1.214–1.223 Å) and C–C (1.515(7)–1.537(5) Å) bond lengths on the other side of the PTO moieties are similar to those in neutral 1, indicating that the spin density is distributed on one side of the PTO planes like 1K(18c6), as well. The structure of (1K)2n (Fig. 3, selected bond parameters are shown in Fig. S4, ESI†) is comprised of a 1D chain with a centrosymmetric geometry in each double-layer unit. Each unit consists of two staggered face-to-face PTOs and the two adjacent units are bridged with two K atoms coordinated by O atoms. In (1K)2n, C15–C16 (1.480(7) Å) is much shorter than C8–C9 (1.531(8) Å), but is comparable to that in 1K(cryp) (1.490(3) Å). Three C–O (1.240(6)–1.260(5) Å) bond lengths of each PTO moiety are comparable to those coordinated C–O bond lengths in 1K(18c6), and one C–O (1.221(6) Å) bond length is comparable to that in neutral 1, indicating uneven spin density distributions on the PTO moieties.

For [1K(15c5)]4, the two PTO planes on each side of the K bridges are not parallel from both top view and side view with the nearest distances of 2.929 Å (C2⋯C26) and 2.865 Å (C51⋯C74) (Fig. S3, ESI†), which are slightly longer than the reported long C–C bond (2.8 Å),22 indicating strong intramolecular π–π interactions.23 For (1K)2n, probably resulting from the steric repulsions of the peripheral tBu groups, all the PTO moieties in (1K)2n are bent, leading to the formation of a concave shaped side view in each unit (Fig. S4, ESI†). The nearest distance between the face-to-face PTO moieties is 2.922 Å (C16⋯C19′ and C16′⋯C19), which is comparable to that of [1K(15c5)]4, indicating π–π couplings in the molecule. Therefore, it is supposed that both [1K(15c5)]4 and (1K)2n probably possess diradical properties.

To clarify the electronic structure of [1K(15c5)]4 and (1K)2n, EPR measurements were conducted. Both EPR spectra of the two compounds in powder at 298 K (Fig. S7 and S8, ESI†) show a half-field signal of forbidden transition (Δms = ±2), verifying π–π couplings in [1K(15c5)]4 and (1K)2n. The EPR spectrum of [1K(15c5)]4 (g = 2.0054) was not simulated, because hyperfine splitting was not observed (Fig. S7, ESI†). The EPR signal of (1K)2n (Fig. S8, ESI†) was well reproduced by simulation with gx = gy = gz = 2.0056, D = 152 G and E = 0 G. From the zero-field splitting parameter D, the distance between the spin centers is calculated to be 5.68 Å, which is close to the average distance (5.26 Å) of O3 to O4′ and C16 to C15′ (Fig. S4, ESI†) in the solid state structure, suggesting the diradical character. The superconducting quantum interference device (SQUID) measurements for (1K)2n (Fig. S11, ESI†) could not be simulated, but an obvious increasing susceptibility above 250 K can be observed, attesting to its open-shell singlet ground state. The SQUID measurements together with the forbidden half-field transition of the EPR spectrum suggest that (1K)2n is an S = 0 diradical polymer featuring an antiferromagnetic interaction in each face-to-face PTO unit. The nearly signal-silent susceptibility below 250 K indicates a large singlet–triplet energy gap.

In summary, one-electron reduction of 1 with potassium generated 1K(cryp), 1K(18c6), [1K(15c5)]4 and (1K)2n with various modes of radical anion 1˙− in the presence or absence of macrocyclic ethers. From the study of 1K(cryp) and 1K(18c6), we unambiguously demonstrated an unprecedented example that the unpaired electron of an organic radical anion in the solid state can be efficiently controlled by electrostatic attraction. The work provides an alternative but versatile approach for tuning the electron spin density distribution and physicochemical properties of organic radicals. The reciprocal transformation of 1K(cryp) and 1K(18c6) indicated that charge pinning in a π-radical anion in the solid state is possible and will have a significant effect on the charge transport in the field of organic semiconductors. In addition, with less hindered 15-crown-5, the reduction led to the formation of radical tetramer [1K(15c5)]4, possessing similar crystallographic parameters (C–O and C–C bond lengths in each PTO moiety) to those of 1K(18c6). In the absence of macrocyclic ethers, 1˙− and K+ were assembled into (1K)2n featuring an antiferromagnetic one-dimensional chain. Strong intramolecular π–π interactions are evident in both [1K(15c5)]4 and (1K)2n. Reductions of PTOs with other substituents are currently in progress.
