Introduction

Polyoxometalates (POMs) have received significant attention reflecting the diverse nature of this family of inorganic clusters, which exhibit a variety of compositions and architectures as well as potential applications in catalysis, photochemistry, molecular electronics, medicine, etc.1 During the process of constructing POM-based inorganic–organic hybrid materials, the influence of POMs on the function of the hybrid materials and the relationship between the structures and properties are worth investigating and will provide reliable strategies to design and synthesize special POM-based hybrid materials.2 According to the literature, even POM anions of different charge can cause the transformation of a metal–organic unit, benefiting the assembly of new hybrid complexes.3 We proposed that a dynamic configuration of POM anions acting as a “molecular adaptor” might adjust the coordination mode of metal–organic subunits, leading to more novel assembly products of POM-based metal–organic frameworks. The octamolybdate is a good candidate due to the versatile isomers such as α, β, γ, δ, ε, ζ, η and θ evidenced in 1970.4,5 More importantly, these isomers are easily transformed into each other under mild changes to the system.4,5

In the synthesis of inorganic–organic hybrid materials, the type of organic N-donor bridging ligands is one of the important factors because the neutral organic N-donor ligands have strong coordination interactions with TM ions, which have been extensively used for constructing the POM-based metal–organic hybrid compounds. In the literature, N-donor ligands are generally classified into two types, namely chelate and bridging ligands.3,4,6 The former possess the following advantages: (i) stronger coordination ability toward transition metal (TM) ions than the bridging ligands with mono-dentate N-donor groups (for example the pyridine group); (ii) infinite coordination modes with TM ions, such as a {ML2} tetra-coordinated distorted tetrahedral geometry and a {ML3} hexa-coordinated octahedral geometry (L represents the bi-dentate chelate ligand), which is suitable for the synthesis of desirable complexes.7 If a –(CH2)n– spacer and/or rigid phenyl center is introduced between two chelate groups, a kind of organic chelate-bridging N-donor ligand can be produced and may show potential advantages for building new POM-based hybrid compounds with cage-like, helical, or even porous structural features.

Based on the aforementioned considerations, we chose a type of ligand combining both chelate and bridging functions, bis(3-(2-pyridyl)pyrazole-1-ylmethyl)benzene (bppmb) (as shown in Scheme 1), to explore new POM-based hybrid compounds.8 We employed the Cu-bppmb coordination complexes and heptamolybdate as the POM precursor to synthesize seven new POM-based inorganic–organic hybrid compounds in situ: [CuI2Lo2][Mo6O19] (1), [CuII2Lo2][β-Mo8O26]·2H2O (2), [CuII4Lo2(pypz)4][α-Mo8O26]·3H2O (3), [CuII4Lp4(H2O)4][α-Mo8O26][β-Mo8O26]·4H2O (4), [CuI4Lp3][β-Mo8O26]·H2O (5), [CuI4Lp4][α-Mo8O26] (6), and [CuI2Lp2(Emim)2][β-Mo8O26] (7) (Lo = 1,2-bppmb, Lp = 1,4-bppmb, pypz = 3-(2-pyridyl)pyrazole, Emim = 1-ethyl-3-methylimidazolium). It is noteworthy that as the heptamolybdate transformed into hexamolybdate or different isomers of octamolybdates in situ, the coordination modes of the Cu-bppmb complexes varied, leading to a series of new coordination units such as the “Möbius-ring”-type binuclear unit, “S”-type binuclear unit, butterfly-type unit, chiral helical chain and mesomeric helical chain. The photocatalytic properties of compounds 1–7 have also been investigated.

Experimental

Materials and physical measurements

All reagents and solvents for the synthesis were purchased without further purification. The bichelate-bridging ligands Lo and Lp were prepared according to literature methods.9 Elemental analyses (C, H and N) were carried out on a Perkin-Elmer 2400 CHN elemental analyzer. The FT-IR spectra were performed on a Mattson Alpha-Centauri spectrometer with KBr pellets in the range of 4000–400 cm−1. TG analyses were performed on a Pyris Diamond TG instrument in flowing N2 with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. The powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns were obtained with a Rigaku D/max-IIB X-ray diffractometer at a scanning rate of 1° per minute with 2θ ranging from 5° to 50° using Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5418 Å). Photocatalytic experiments in aqueous solutions were accomplished in a 500 mL water-cooled quartz cylindrical container. Usually, the reaction mixture in the container was kept at room temperature by a continuous flow of water through an external cooling coil. Moreover, a 125 W high-pressure mercury lamp was employed as the UV light source. A suspension of powdered catalyst (50 mg) in fresh aqueous solutions of Rhodamine-B (RhB) (400 mL, 20.0 mg L−1) was magnetically stirred in the container in the dark for 30 min before irradiation in order to establish an adsorption/desorption equilibrium of RhB on the sample surface. At given 15 min irradiation intervals, 3 mL of samples were taken out from the beaker and subsequently analyzed by an SP-1900 UV–vis spectrophotometer. The organic dye concentration was estimated by the absorbance at 550 nm.

X-ray crystallography

The crystallographic data of compounds 1–7 were collected at 150 K on the Rigaku R-axis Rapid IP diffractometer using graphite monochromatic Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) and IP techniques. A multi-scan absorption correction was applied. The crystal data of 1–7 were solved by the direct method and refined by a full-matrix least-squares method on F2 using the SHELXTL-97 crystallographic software package.10 All non-H atoms were refined anisotropically except the disordered solvent water molecules. H atoms on the C atoms were fixed in calculated positions. H atoms on the coordinated water molecules were found from the difference Fourier maps and fixed in reasonable positions. However, the H atoms on the lattice water molecules cannot be found from the residual peaks but were directly included in the final molecular formula. Furthermore, compound 2 exhibits solvent accessible voids in the final refinement but the residual peaks are too weak to be confirmed as solvent molecules.11 Based on the elemental analysis and TG analysis, the extra two solvent water molecules were directly included in the final molecular formula of 2. The detailed crystal data and structure refinement for 1–7 are given in Table 1. Selected bond lengths and angles of 1–7 are listed in Tables S1–S7,† respectively.

Results and discussion

Crystal structures

The POM anions which exist in compound 1 are the classical Lindqvist-type polyoxoanions [Mo6O19]2−, which are formed by six MoO6 octahedra connected to each other through edge-sharing oxygen atoms and thus exhibit approximate Oh symmetry (Fig. S1a and d†). The inorganic building blocks in compounds 2–7 are [α-Mo8O26]4− (α-Mo8), [β-Mo8O26]4− (β-Mo8), or both ([α/β-Mo8O26]4−, α/β-Mo8) anions. These polyoxoanions lie in the inversion centers. The α-Mo8 anion consists of six distorted {MoO6} octahedra and two distorted {MoO4} tetrahedra with three kinds of O atoms, namely six μ3-O, six μ-O, and fourteen terminal oxygen atoms (Ot) (Fig. S1b and e†). The β-Mo8 anion is composed of eight distorted {MoO6} octahedra with four kinds of O atom, including six μ-O, four μ3-O, two μ5-O, and twelve Ot (Fig. S1c and f†).

Structure description of 1

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis shows that compound 1 crystallizes in the orthorhombic space group Pbcn and the basic structural unit consists of one cationic [CuI2(Lo)2]2+ unit and one Lindqvist polyoxoanion [Mo6O19]2− (Fig. 1 and S2†). The [Mo6O19]2− moiety has crystallographically-imposed inversion symmetry, with the O6 atom on an inversion centre. In the [CuI2(Lo)2]2+ unit (Fig. 1a), each CuI center is four-coordinate with four N atoms derived from two different Lo ligands and exhibits a distorted tetragonal coordination geometry. The bond lengths of Cu–N vary from 2.020(5)–2.122(5) Å and the bond angles of N–Cu–N are in the range of 80.7(2) to 146.5(2)°. In this metal–organic unit, the Lo ligand adopts an “anti”-style configuration and acts as a tetra-dentate chelate ligand to coordinate with two Cu centers to form the binuclear Cu-cluster (Fig. 1a). Such a moiety lies about a twofold axis. Furthermore, these cationic units are extended into 2D supramolecular sheets through π⋯π interactions with distances of 3.689(1) to 4.030(1) Å between the pyridyl rings and the pyrazole rings in the ab plane (Fig. 1c and S3†). It is noteworthy that such 2D supramolecular sheets possess honeycomb-like cavities, which are occupied by the [Mo6O19]2− POM units (Fig. 1c). Based on extensive weak π⋯π interactions in the structure, these [Cu(i)2(Lo)2]2+ units form 2-D supramolecular sheets, which are well separated by the Lindqvist-type POM clusters, as shown in Fig. S4.†

Structure description of 2

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis indicates that compound 2 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/c and consists of two [CuIILo]2+ moieties and one [β-Mo8O26]4− polyoxoanion (Fig. 2 and S5†). In the crystal structure of 2, the [β-Mo8O26]4− moiety lies about an inversion centre. Each Lo ligand in 2 adopts a “syn”-style configuration, coordinating with one CuII center to form the mononuclear {CuLo} moiety (Fig. 2a). The two chelate bppmb groups in the [CuLo] unit are not in the same plane, but distort each other with a dihedral angle of 42.727(9)° (Fig. S6a†). The CuII center is five-coordinate with four N atoms derived from the same Lo ligand and one terminal O (O7) atom originating from the polyoxoanion, exhibiting a distorted tetragonal pyramid coordination geometry. The bond distances of Cu–N are in the range of 1.957(3)–2.140(3) Å and that of Cu–O is 2.063(2) Å. The bond angles of N–Cu–N range from 80.17(1) to 176.37(2)° and those of N–Cu–O from 88.51(1) to 135.07(1)°. Moreover, two mononuclear {CuLo} units are connected with the [β-Mo8O26]4− unit to form a {CuLo}-decorated POM cluster (Fig. 2c). Interestingly, the adjacent [CuLo] units are further connected in a 2-D supramolecular network in the bc plane via weak intermolecular C–H⋯π interactions between the pyrazole/pyridine rings (Fig. S6b and S7†). The typical C–H⋯π distances are 2.724(2) to 3.194(4) Å. In the packing arrangement, the 2-D supramolecular networks are well separated without obvious H-bonding or π⋯π interactions (Fig. S8†).

Structure description of 3

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that compound 3 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/n, whose basic structural unit contains one polyoxoanion [α-Mo8O26]4− and two binuclear [Cu2(pypz)2(Lo)]2+ units (Fig. 3 and S9†). The [α-Mo8O26]4− moiety lies about an inversion centre in the crystal structure of 3. In the binuclear [Cu2(pypz)2(Lo)]2+ unit, there are two different CuII centers (Fig. 3a). The Cu(2) ion shows a five-coordinate environment with two nitrogen atoms derived from the Lo ligand and three nitrogen atoms from two different pzpy ligands. Nevertheless, the Cu(1) ion possesses a six-coordinate environment, where it coordinates not only with five N atoms from the organic ligands (similar to the case of Cu(2)) but also with one terminal O atom from the polyoxoanion. The bond distances of Cu–N range from 1.955(5) to 2.312(5) Å and that of Cu–O is 2.594(0) Å. The bond angles of N–Cu–N range from 75.87(2) to 177.1(2)° and those of N–Cu–O from 81.7(8) to 169.0(5)°. Moreover, the two Cu centers are connected to each other via one Lo ligand with a “syn”-style configuration and to the pzpy ligands to form the binuclear Cu-cluster. It is noteworthy that the “syn”-style configuration of the Lo ligand in compound 3 is different to those in compounds 1 and 2, indicating that the introduction of flexible –(CH2)– spacers in the Lo ligand can provide various changes for the bridging ligand. The interesting structural feature of compound 3 is that two binuclear [Cu2(pypz)2(Lo)]2+ units are connected with one [α-Mo8O26]4− moiety, forming a dumbbell-like hybrid cluster (Fig. 3c). In the packing arrangement, these hybrid clusters possess two kinds of orientation, but no obvious intermolecular interactions among the adjacent molecules in the packing arrangement of 3 (Fig. S10†).

Structure description of 4

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis shows that compound 4 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P. The basic structural unit of 4 consists of one [α-Mo8O26]4− anion, one [β-Mo8O26]4− anion, and two cationic binuclear [CuII2Lp2(H2O)2]4+ moieties (Fig. 4 and S11†). It is noteworthy that the [α-Mo8O26]4− and [β-Mo8O26]4− moieties each lie about an inversion centre in the crystal structure of 4. In 4, all CuII ions adopt the six-coordinate environment with four nitrogen atoms derived from two different Lp ligands, one oxygen atom from the polyoxoanion, and one coordinated water molecule (Fig. 4a). The bond distances of Cu–N are in the range of 1.956(9)–2.355(1) Å and those of Cu–O vary from 2.029(8) to 2.479(7) Å. The bond angles of N–Cu–N range from 76.7(4) to 179.9(5)° and the N–Cu–O angles are in the range of 80.3(3)–167.3(1)°. Moreover, each Cu(1) ion is connected to one Cu(2) ion by two Lp ligands with a cis-configuration to form a chiral “Möbius-Ring (MR)”-type binuclear moiety.

More interestingly, such a chiral binuclear metal–organic unit is connected to a centrosymmetric [α-Mo8O26]4− anion via the Cu(1) center on one side and linked to a centrosymmetric [β-Mo8O26]4− anion via the Cu(2) center on the other side, forming a 1-D chain-like hybrid compound. (Fig. 4c). It is worth noting that the left-handed and right-handed “MR”-type moieties are alternatively arranged in the chain, leading to a mesomeric chain (Fig. 4c). To our knowledge, such a hybrid chain composed of both [α-Mo8O26]4− and [β-Mo8O26]4− units is observed for the first time. In the packing arrangement, all the 1-D chains are parallel with each other and well separated in three dimensions without obvious intermolecular interactions (Fig. S12†).

Structure description of 5

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that compound 5 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/c. The structure contains two types of chain-like building units. One is composed of the [β-Mo8O26]4− polyoxoanion and a binuclear [Cu2Lp]2+ metal–organic bridging fragment (labeled as Chain I), in which the [β-Mo8O26] moiety lies about an inversion centre in the crystal structure of 5, while the Lp ligand lies about another independent inversion centre. The other is a [Cu2Lp2]2+ metal–organic chain-like moiety (labeled as Chain II) (Fig. 5). In 5, each Cu(1) center is four-coordinate with two N atoms derived from the Lp ligand and two oxygen atoms from the polyoxoanion (Fig. 5a). The bond distances of Cu–N are 2.004(1) and 2.106(9) Å and those of Cu–O are 1.929(6) and 2.063(7) Å, respectively. The bond angle of N–Cu–N is 81.4(4)° and N–Cu–O are in the range of 92.7(3)–136.8(3)°. Two such Cu(1) ions are connected to each other by one Lp ligand with an “anti”-configuration to form a {Cu2Lp} binuclear fragment. Interestingly, these {Cu2Lp} fragments are further linked by the [β-Mo8O26]4− polyoxoanions into a “zig-zag”-type chain (Chain I) (Fig. 5c). Each Cu(2) center also exhibits a tetra-coordinate environment, wherein it is coordinated to four N atoms from two different Lp ligands (Fig. 5c). The Cu–N bond distances are in the range of 1.976(8)–2.107(1) Å and the N–Cu–N bond angles vary from 80.4(3) to 132.5(4)°. Interestingly, the neighboring Cu(2) centers are connected by the Lp ligands with an “anti”-configuration into a 1-D {Cu2Lp2} chain (Chain II) (Fig. 5d).

Although both Lp ligands in 5 exhibit the “anti”-configuration, the distances between two CuI centers are 9.523(9) and 7.639(4) Å, respectively (Fig. S14†), indicating that the Lp ligands in two types of chains possess different “anti”-configurations. Furthermore, the [Cu2Lp2]2+ metal–organic Chain II displays a chiral helical feature. Both left-handed and right-handed helical chains can be observed along the a axis (Fig. 6b). In the packing arrangement, all of the chains are parallel with each other in the ab plane (Fig. 6 and S13†). It is noteworthy that Chain I shows two kinds of orientation in the crystal structure, and the two different Chain Is are separated by the supramolecular layer composed of Chain IIs (Fig. 6 and S15†). Furthermore, all left-handed helical chains are parallel with each other on one layer of the ab plane, while all right-handed helical chains are parallel to each other on the other layer of the ab plane (Fig. 6c). The two types of layers are alternately arranged along the c axis, leading to a racemic compound 5. In the packing arrangement, all of these chains are stacked together with weak C–H⋯π intermolecular interactions between the benzene rings of two neighboring chains (Fig. S15d†).

Structure description of 6

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis shows that compound 6 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P. The basic structural unit contains one [α-Mo8O26]4− polyoxoanion and two types of 1-D [CuI2Lp2]2+ metal–organic chains (Fig. 7 and S16†). It is worth mentioning that all three components lie about independent inversion centres. In 6, one type of [CuI2Lp2]2+ chain is formed via alternate linking of the “anti”-style Lp ligands by Cu(1) centers along the c axis (Fig. 7a), where the Cu(1) centers exhibit a tetra-coordinate environment with four N atoms derived from two different Lp ligands. The bond distances of Cu–N vary from 2.023(7) to 2.086(7) Å and the bond angles of N–Cu–N from 77.4(3) to 145.8(3)°. Moreover, the Lp ligands show two types of configuration, namely Lp1 and Lp2, and the distances between two Cu(1) centers in Lp1 and Lp2 are 8.444(2) Å and 9.660(5) Å, respectively (Fig. S17a and b†). In contrast, the alternate connection of the “anti”-style Lp ligands with Cu(2) centers results in the other type of [CuI2Lp2]2+ chain along the a axis (Fig. 7b). The Lp ligands also display two types of configuration, that is, Lp3 and Lp4 (Fig. S17c and d†). The distances between two Cu(2) centers in Lp3 and Lp4 are 8.152(8) and 11.008(1) Å, respectively (Fig. S4c and d†). It is worth mentioning that both chains in compound 6 are not chiral helical chains, but show a mesomeric helical topology. In such mesomeric chains, the left-handed helical unit and the right-handed helical unit are alternately connected together, leading to a mesomeric feature.9,12 In the packing arrangement, the chains along the c axis and the those along the a axis are stacked together, forming a 3-D supramolecular open framework (Fig. 8). The adjacent chains are stabilized by the π⋯π intermolecular interactions between benzene rings from different chains. The typical distance between two adjacent benzene planes is ca. 3.487(1) Å (Fig. S18†). The [α-Mo8O26]4− polyoxoanions reside in the cavities as the guest templates (Fig. 8a).

Structure description of 7

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis shows that compound 7 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/c, whose basic structural unit consists of one cationic [CuI2Lp2]2+ metal–organic chain, one [β-Mo8O26]4− polyoxoanion and two [Emim]+ cations (Fig. 9 and S20†). In the crystal structure of 7, the [β-Mo8O26] moiety lies about an inversion centre. In the [CuI2Lp2]2+ chain of 7, each Cu(i) cation adopts a four-coordinate mode with four nitrogen atoms derived from two different Lp ligands (Fig. 9c). The bond distances of Cu–N vary from 2.006(7)–2.113(7) Å, and the bond angles of N–Cu–N are in the range of 79.4(3)° to 136.7(3)°. Interestingly, each Lp ligand in 7 adopts a similar trans-configuration, which is linked by two Cu(i) centers to form the 1-D helical chain. Furthermore, there are extensive C–H⋯π intermolecular interactions between the adjacent left-handed and right-handed helical chains in the bc plane, forming a 2-D supramolecular layer (Fig. 10 and S16†). In the packing arrangement, all of these 2-D supramolecular layers are parallel with each other and are well separated by the [Emim]+ cations and the [β-Mo8O26]4− polyoxoanions (Fig. 11 and S21†).

Synthetic and structural discussion

In the system containing chelating-bridging ligands, copper ions and heptamolybdate, the heptamolybdate [Mo7O24]6− can transform into hexamolybdate or octamolybdate in situ by changing the pH of the system. By comparing these seven compounds within a distinct pH value range, heptamolybdate preferred to form different forms of isopolyoxomolybdate: for pH < 2, the [Mo6O19]2− anion was predominant; at pH 3.0, the [α-Mo8O26]4− anion was the main form; in the pH range 3.5–4.0, [α-Mo8O26]4− and [β-Mo8O26]4− coexist at the same time; for pH > 4.0, heptamolybdate transformed to the [β-Mo8O26]4− isomer. The slight change of pH led to different forms of isopolyoxomolybdate and different forms of isopolyoxoanions influenced the assembly of the metal–organic subunits. Additionally, the different coordination ability of surface oxygen atoms on different POMs induced distinct interaction modes between POMs and metal–organic subunits so as to obtain multiple self-assembled POM-based inorganic–organic hybrid materials. In this investigation, different forms of isopolyoxomolybdates can be viewed as a “molecular adaptor” to tune the appropriate form of metal–organic subunits so that the POM anions and the metal–organic subunits have the best matching mode in the self-assembly process (Scheme 2). This may offer a new approach to construct a series of POM directed metal–organic hybrid materials.

Photocatalytic properties

The use of photocatalysts to decompose waste organic molecules so as to purify water resources is of great interest for scientific research and an important issue of concern for our society. In this research field, Rhodamine-B (RhB) is generally employed as a typical model dye contaminant to evaluate photocatalytic effectiveness in the purification of waste water.13 POMs have been proved to be one type of potential photocatalyst in the degradation of organic dyes.14 However, the typical POM salts are usually water-soluble and may induce secondary pollution if they are directly used as photocatalysts for the above purpose. Therefore, exploring heterogeneous POM-based photocatalysts is one of the key issues in this research field. POM-based inorganic–organic hybrid compounds prepared in hydrothermal or solvothermal environments usually show a very low solubility or even insolubility in water, which reveals an ideal route to develop new photocatalysts for the degradation of organic dyes in aqueous media.15 Herein, all seven compounds are insoluble in water, and therefore their heterogeneous photocatalytic performances were investigated via the experimental model of RhB photodegradation under UV irradiation. Moreover, the photocatalytic properties of the blank RhB aqueous solution without any catalyst was also checked as a control experiment. The RhB solutions were monitored by UV–vis spectra and the decolorization speeds under different conditions are displayed in Fig. 12. An obvious decrease in the absorption peaks of the RhB solution was observed under UV irradiation for compounds 1–7 and the photodegradation process of RhB without any photocatalyst has also been studied for comparison. Changes in the Ct/C0 plot of RhB solutions versus reaction time were shown in Fig. 12h. It is observed that the photocatalytic activities increase from 35.8% (without a catalyst) to 83.9% for 1, 82.3% for 2, 84.0% for 3, 84.5% for 4, 81.5% for 5, 80.0% for 6 and 79.0% for 7 after 90 min of irradiation. Compared to RhB without any photocatalyst, there is an obvious decrease in the absorption peak of compounds 1–7 upon irradiation, indicating that these compounds have good photocatalytic properties for the degradation of RhB. Moreover, since compound 4 exhibits the highest catalytic activity, it is further chosen as the representative catalyst to investigate the catalyst lifetime. In our initial experiment, the catalytic activity of compound 4 can be well sustained when it was recycled five times (Fig. S22†). Furthermore, the PXRD pattern of compound 4 after five-cycle photocatalytic tests still corresponds with the one before catalytic testing, indicating that such heterogeneous catalysts are stable during the catalytic process (Fig. 13).

Luminescent properties

The luminescent properties of compounds 1–7 have also been explored, considering that the organic ligands may induce the luminescent behavior. Actually, upon excitation at 260 nm, intense emission peaks for compounds 1–3 are located at 373 nm, 376 nm and 375 nm, respectively (Fig. 14a) and can be assigned to organic ligand-based emission (λem = 363 nm) according to the luminescence spectrum of the free Lo ligand (Fig. 14a). Meanwhile, the emission peak positions at ca. 384 nm, 378 nm, 380 nm and 392 nm for compounds 4–7 are similar to that of 1–3 (Fig. 14b), which are attributed to organic ligand-based emission (λem = 365 nm) according to the luminescence spectrum of the free Lp ligand (Fig. 14b). This emission band can be ascribed to a π–π* or π–n ligand-centered electronic transition within the aromatic systems of the L ligand.16

Conclusions

In summary, seven new organic–inorganic hybrid compounds based on the TM ions, bichelate-bridging ligands and polyoxomolybdate units have been prepared. In such a reaction system, the heptamolybdate [Mo7O24]6− can transform to hexamolybdate or octamolybdate in situ by changing pH. These POM clusters can be viewed as a “molecular adaptor” to tune the appropriate form of metal–organic subunits, such as the “Möbius-ring”-type binuclear unit, “S”-type binuclear unit, butterfly-type unit, helical chain and mesomeric helical chain. As a result, these POM anions and various metal–organic subunits have the best matching modes in the self-assembly process, leading to new hybrid compound systems. These POM-based hybrid materials display good photocatalytic activity towards the model degradation experiment of RhB under UV irradiation. Based on the present work, it can be predicted that the use of a dynamic configuration of POM anions to modulate the metal–organic coordination complex moieties may be developed as a new strategy to design and synthesise hybrid compound systems with desirable structures and functionalities in the future. This work is ongoing in our group.
