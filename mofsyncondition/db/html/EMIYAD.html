Introduction

Among various materials, metal–organic complexes (MOCs), as a type of popular crystalline material, have attracted much attention owing to their charming structural characteristics and functions as well as their applications in luminescence, magnetism, photocatalysis and gas storage.1–5 Polyoxometalates (POM), which are inorganic high-nuclear metal oxides, have gained the interest of many scholars due to their fascinating architectures and many potential applications in the fields of catalysis, electrochemistry and adsorption.6–8 Currently, many reports have focused on introducing POMs into MOCs to acquire POM-based MOC materials, which can be applied as electrochemical sensors for detecting diverse ions.9 Among these, the detection of anions, such as bromate and nitrite, is the most common. However, reports on the detection of metal cations are still rare.10,11 Nowadays, among the heavy metal ions, Cr(vi), as the representative of the most toxic ions, has been categorized as a human carcinogen because it can cross through the cell membrane easily and oxidize DNA, causing carcinogenic activity.12 Therefore, the maximum concentration of Cr(vi) in drinking water specified by the World Health Organization (WHO) is less than 0.05 mg L−1 (0.05 ppm).13 Furthermore, Fe(iii) is a common metal in our everyday environment, and it also plays a vital role in the human body and in other organisms. However, excessive Fe(iii) in drinking water may cause many problems related to human health.14 In this view, it is very imperative to exploit efficient, accurate and low-cost sensors for detecting these ions in water.

On the other hand, due to the rapid advancement of industrial printing and aquaculture, water contamination caused by dyes has become increasingly serious. The multiplex structures of dye molecules make it hard to degrade under natural conditions, which could seriously affect human health.15–17 Therefore, from the environmental perspective, it is imperative to remove organic dyes from wastewater. At present, photocatalytic degradation, physical adsorption and biodegradation are common methods that can effectively remove dye molecules from wastewater.18–20 Physical adsorption is considered promising technology that can remove organic dyes from wastewater economically and environmentally,21 thus, the research on adsorbent materials for water treatment has gained wide attention throughout the world. As adsorption materials, POM-based MOCs seem to be a good option to eliminate dye molecules from wastewater due to their efficiency and simplicity.22–24 Therefore, the design and assembly of novel POM-based MOCs with multifunctional performance is an imperative issue. Polyoxymolybdates are also excellent building units to construct POM-based MOCs, including {Mo4}, {Mo6}, {Mo8}, {Mo36}, {Mo154}, {Mo176}, and {Mo368}, of which the octamolybdate {Mo8} is considered to have the most varied structures and is usually chosen to generate diverse POM-based MOCs.25 In addition, it is generally recognized that the organic ligands exert a significant function on the design and formation of POM-based MOCs. Thus, we need to design suitable organic ligands purposefully to form POM-based MOCs with attractive architectures and outstanding properties.

Hence, based on the above consideration, we devised four bis(pyrazine)–bis(amide) ligands with flexible –(CH2)n– (n = 2, 3, 4) spacers and different substituents [N,N′-bis(2-pyrazinecarboxamide)-1,2-ethane (bpae), N,N′-bis(2-pyrazinecarboxamide)-1,4-butane (bpab), N,N′-bis(5-methyl-2-pyrazinecarboxamide)-1,3-propane (bmpap), and N,N′-bis(5-methyl-2-pyrazinecarboxamide)-1,4-butane (bmpab)] (Scheme 1), and introduced them into the CuII–POM systems, trying to assemble the POM-based MOCs with varied architectures and outstanding properties. This kind of flexible bis(pyrazine)–bis(amide) ligands possess the following advantages: (i) pyrazine and amide groups supply more potential coordination sites. (ii) Pyrazine and amide groups possess strong coordination abilities, and they may chelate with central metals. (iii) Flexible –(CH2)n– groups can twist freely to fulfill the coordination demands of metal centers and ligands, which may contribute to the construction of high-dimensional architectures. (iv) The CH2 substituent may affect the structure of target complexes and influence their properties. As expected, four new octamolybdate-based MOCs with various structures: [Cu2(bpae)2(β-Mo8O26)]·6H2O (1), {H2Cu(bpab)(β-Mo8O26)(H2O)2}·2H2O (2), [Cu2(bmpap)(β-Mo8O26)(H2O)4]·3H2O (3), and {H2Cu(bmpab)(β-Mo8O26)(H2O)2}·2H2O (4) were prepared under hydrothermal conditions. To our knowledge, the reports on bis(pyrazine)–bis(amide) ligands are extremely limited, and bmpap and bmpab are used for the first time. The distinct coordinated networks of 1–4 are discussed. Furthermore, the electrocatalytic and amperometric sensing performance and the dye adsorption abilities of 1–4 were examined in detail.

Experimental

Materials and characterization

All chemicals with reagent-grade quality were obtained from commercial sources without further purification. The bis(pyrazine)–bis(amide) ligands were synthesized based on the literature.26 FT-IR was performed on a Varian-640 spectrometer using KBr pellets. Powder X-ray diffraction experiments were examined on an Ultima IV using a D/teXUltra diffractometer. Electrochemical behaviors were determined with a CHI 760 electrochemical workstation. The ultraviolet-visible absorption spectroscopy was carried out by making use of a SP-1901 UV-vis spectrophotometer at room temperature.

Preparation of complexes 1–4

Synthesis of [Cu2(bpae)2(β-Mo8O26)]·6H2O (1)

A mixture of (NH4)6Mo7O24·4H2O (0.1 mmol), Cu(NO3)2·3H2O (0.50 mmol), bpae (0.10 mmol) and H2O (10 mL) was dissolved for 30 min at room temperature. Then, 1.0 M NaOH was added dropwise to adjust the pH value of the hybrid solution to 4.28. The aqueous solution was sealed in a Teflon-lined autoclave at 120 °C for 4 days. After slowly cooling to room temperature, blue block crystals of 1 were obtained and washed with distilled water. Yield: 49% based on Mo. Elemental analysis (%) calcd. for C24H36Cu2Mo8N12O36: C 14.68, H 1.85, N 8.56. Found: C 14.61, H 1.89, N 8.47%. IR (KBr, cm−1): 3466 (s), 3073 (w), 1635 (s), 1477 (m), 1439 (m), 1353 (w), 1295 (w), 1175 (m), 1099 (m), 1051 (m), 940 (s), 920 (m), 854 (s), 720 (s), 557 (w).

Synthesis of {H2Cu(bpab)(β-Mo8O26)(H2O)2}·2H2O (2)

The synthesis method of 2 is similar to that of 1 apart from bpab (0.10 mmol), which was used to substitute bpae. The pH value was adjusted to 4.35 with 1.0 M NaOH. Blue block crystals of 2 were harvested (37% yield based on Mo). Elemental analysis (%) calcd. for C14H30CuMo8N6O34: C 10.15, H 1.82, N 5.07. Found: C 10.24, H 1.87, N 5.13%. IR cm−1 (KBr): 3313 (s), 1649 (s), 1544 (w), 1400 (m), 1181 (w), 1066 (m), 966 (s), 902 (s), 837 (s), 687 (s), 538 (w).

Synthesis of [Cu2(bmpap)(β-Mo8O26)(H2O)4]·3H2O (3)

The synthetic procedure for 3 was similar to that for 1, except that bmpap (0.10 mmol) was employed instead of bpae. 1.0 M HNO3 was added dropwise to adjust the pH value of the hybrid solution to 2.37. Green block crystals of 3 were harvested (41% yield based on Mo). Elemental analysis (%) calcd. for C15H32Cu2Mo8N6O35: C 10.29, H 1.84, N 4.80. Found: C 10.36, H 1.89, N 4.72%. Synthesis of {H2Cu(bmpab)(β-Mo8O26)(H2O)2}·2H2O (4)

Green crystals of 4 were acquired by employing a similar approach to that for 1 apart from the adoption of bmpab (0.10 mmol) instead of bpae. 1.0 M HNO3 was added dropwise to adjust the pH value of the hybrid solution to 2.24. Structural description of 1

Complex 1 reveals a 3D metal–organic framework based on the [Mo8O26]4− anions (abbreviated to Mo8) and the bpae ligands, and crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/c. The asymmetric unit of 1 is comprised of two CuII ions, two bpae ligands, one Mo8 anion and six lattice water molecules. Cu1 coordinates with two O atoms (O2 and O2#2) and two N atoms (N1 and N1#2) from two bpae ligands, two terminal O atoms (O4 and O4#2) from two Mo8 anions, with Cu–N distance of 1.996(4) Å and Cu–O distances of 1.922(3) and 2.534(4) Å, respectively (Fig. 1a). Cu2 presents a distorted octahedral coordination geometry, which is surrounded by four N atoms (N2, N2#3, N4#4 and N4#5) and two O atoms (O1 and O1#3) from four L ligands (Cu–O distance: 2.152(4) Å, Cu–N distances: 2.084(5)–2.157(4) Å).

In 1, bpae links neighboring Cu2 ions to form a [Cu(bpae)]n2n+ helical chain along the a axis (Fig. 1b). At the same time, bpae connects the adjacent Cu2 ions along the b axis to outspread a 2D layer (Fig. 1c). Most strikingly, in 1, the 2D layer was constructed only by the μ3-bridging bpae ligands and Cu2 ions. The Cu1 ion coordinates with two Mo8 anions and two bpae ligands, connecting the adjacent 2D layers to establish the final 3D metal–inorganic framework (Fig. 1d). To acquire further insight into the structure of 1, Mo8 anions act as a bidentate linear connector. The bpae ligand coordinated with three CuII ions can be regarded as a 3-connected node. Both Cu1 and Cu2 can act as 4-connected nodes with distinct coordination environments. Hence, the entire structure of 1 can be reduced to a 3,4,4-connected 3D topological architecture, as presented in Fig. 1e.

Structural description of 2

Complexes 2 and 4 display isomorphous structure, so 2 is depicted in detail as a representative (Fig. 2 and S1†). Complex 2 is constituted by one Mo8 anion, one CuII ion, one bpab ligand, two coordinated water molecules and two lattice water molecules (Fig. 2a). To keep the charge in balance, two hydrogen protons were introduced in the formula,27–29 so the final molecular formula is defined as {H2Cu(bpab)(β-Mo8O26)(H2O)2}·2H2O. Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis demonstrates that 2 belongs to the P space group in the triclinic system. CuII ion adopts a distorted octahedral coordination geometry with two O atoms and two N atoms from two bpab ligands, and two O atoms from two coordinated water molecules. The Mo8 anion acts as a non-coordinated template in 2. The Cu–O bond distances range between 1.937(6) and 2.431(6) Å, while the Cu–N distance is 1.992(7) Å. Each bpab acts as a μ2-bridging ligand linking two neighboring CuII ions to produce a 1D [Cu(bpab)]n2n+ chain (Fig. 2b). The adjacent [Cu(bpab)]n2n+ metal–organic chains connect with the discrete Mo8 anions through hydrogen bonds [N(4)–H(4)⋯O(8), 2.927(9)Å] and further produce a 2D supramolecular network (Fig. 2c).

Structural description of 3

Complex 3 is constructed from one Mo8 anion, two CuII ions, one bmpap, four coordinated water molecules and three lattice water molecules (Fig. 3a). Each Cu1 ion is connected to one Mo8 anion, two bmpap ligands, and two coordinated water molecules in an octahedral geometry, with Cu–O bond lengths ranging from 1.965(3) to 2.659(3) Å, and Cu–N bond lengths ranging from 1.997(3) to 2.442(3) Å. Each Cu2 ion adopts a similar coordination environment to Cu1, with Cu–O bond lengths ranging from 1.953(3) to 2.592(3) Å, and Cu–N bond lengths ranging from 2.009(3) to 2.372(4) Å.

Different from complexes 1, 2 and 4, the bmpap ligand adopts a μ4-bridging coordination mode by its four pyrazine N and two amide O atoms in 3 (Table 2). The neighboring CuII ions are connected by the μ4-bridging bmpap ligands to construct a 2D metal–organic framework (Fig. 3b). Each Mo8 anion with a bidentate coordinated mode is embedded in the metal–organic framework (Fig. 3c). The structure of 3 is predigested for a more intuitive understanding. Each Mo8 anion can be treated as a linear connector. The Cu1 and Cu2, which connect two bmpap ligands and one Mo8 anion, can be simplified 3-connected nodes. The bmpap ligand can be regarded as a 4-connected node, comprising two connected Cu1 ions and two Cu2 ions. So the architecture of 3 can be predigested to a 3,3,4-connected 2D structure (Fig. 3d).

Effects of different bis(pyrazine)–bis(amide) ligands on the structures of 1–4

In order to explore the influences of length and substituents of ligands on the architectures of the target complexes, four new isopolymolybdate-based complexes were constituted successfully by regulating the spacer lengths and substituents of the bis(pyrazine)–bis(amide) ligands under hydrothermal conditions. As shown in Table 2, the ligands in 1–4 display three types of coordination modes. In 1, bpae exhibits a μ3-bridging mode, with three N atoms from two pyrazine groups and two O atoms from two amide groups coordinating with three CuII ions. In 2 and 4, two bis(pyrazine)–bis(amide) ligands with different substituents, bpab and bmpab, adopt the same μ2-bridging coordination modes. In 3, bmpap displays a μ4-bridging mode by its four N atoms from two pyrazine groups and two O atoms from two amide groups coordinating with four CuII ions. Therefore, the distinct ligands lead to the varied structures of the title complexes. Moreover, we found that the spacer lengths and the substitution group of the ligands also have a pivotal effect on the construction of 1–4. 1 and 2 are obtained by selecting the bis(pyrazine)–bis(amide) ligands with distinct spacer lengths. With increasing spacer lengths, the structures of 1–2 change from 3D to 1D. Similarly, when the spacer length of ligands was extended in 3 and 4, their structures change from 2D to 1D. In addition, the introduction of substitution groups also leads to different structures. In our recent study, we reported a Mo8-based complex [CuL1(Mo8O26)0.5]·H2O [L1 = N,N′-bis(2-pyrazinecarboxamide)-1,3-propane].30 The spacer length of ligand L1 is the same as bmpap in 3, but bmpap has two extra methyl groups. The ligand L1 connected CuII ions to construct a 1D Cu–L1 chain in [CuL1(Mo8O26)0.5]·H2O, which is different from the 2D Cu-bmpap network in 3. They present different structures, illustrating that the methyl group has a significant influence on the construction of the architectures. The above results indicate that by adjusting the spacer length and substituents of the ligands, various architectures can be obtained.

Powder X-ray diffraction, infrared spectra, and TG analyses of 1–4

The PXRD patterns of 1–4 are consistent with the theoretical spectra simulated by single crystal data, indicating that the acquired crystals are pure phase (Fig. S2†). The IR spectra of 1–4 are presented in Fig. S3.† The presence of the characteristic bands at 557–1051 cm−1 for 1, 538–1066 cm−1 for 2, 558–1051 cm−1 for 3, and 682–1053 cm−1 for 4 can be ascribed to the ν (Mo–O) and ν (Mo–O–Mo) of Mo8 anions.31 The characteristic bands at 1635 cm−1 for 1, 1649 cm−1 for 2, 1629 cm−1 for 3, and 1627 cm−1 for 4 could be attributed to the stretching vibrations of CO group.32

Thermogravimetric (TG) analyses of 1–4 were carried out under a flowing N2 atmosphere with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 from room temperature to 700 °C (Fig. S4†). The TG curves of 1–4 display two distinct steps of weight losses, respectively. The first weight losses of 5.21% for 1, 4.74% for 2, 7.31% for 3 and 4.57% for 4 below the temperature of 135, 252, 159 and 257 °C are consistent with the removal of coordinated/lattice water molecules (calc. 5.50%, 4.35%, 7.20% and 4.28%), respectively. The TG curves of 1–4 indicated that the host networks remained stable at 419, 356, 478 and 424 °C, respectively. The second significant weight losses are due to the collapse of the frameworks and loss of organic ligands.

Electrochemical behaviors of carbon paste electrodes bulk-modified with complexes 1–4 (1-, 2-, 3-, 4-CPEs)

The POMs can undergo reversible multi-electron redox processes, which make them quite prevalent in electrocatalytic research and chemically modified electrodes. So as to research the electrochemical behaviors of 1–4, the cyclic voltammograms of 1–4 bulk-modified carbon paste electrodes (1-, 2-, 3-, 4-CPEs) were measured in a mixed solution of Na2SO4 (0.5 M) + H2SO4 (0.1 M) at different scan rates in the potential range of +500 to −300 mV, as presented in Fig. 4. The average peak potentials E1/2 = (Epa + Epc)/2 (scan rate: 60 mV s−1) of 1–4-CPEs are listed in Table S3.† For 1–4-CPEs, their three reversible redox peaks are related to the redox processes of the Mo8 anion.33 Furthermore, when the scan rates were varied from 20 to 500 mV s−1, the peak potentials varied progressively: the anodic peak potentials shift to the positive direction. Accordingly, the cathodic peak potentials divert towards the negative direction. The peak currents are almost proportional to the scan rates, demonstrating that 1–4-CPEs adopt a surface-controlled process.34 The results reveal that 1–4 could preserve the redox activities of the parent polyoxoanions, making them promising to be employed in electrochemistry as outstanding inorganic–organic hybrid materials.

The bifunctional electrocatalytic activities were also studied for 1–4-CPEs. As seen from Fig. 5 (Fig. S5–S7†), with increasing concentration of bromate, hydrogen peroxide and nitrite, the reduction peak currents increased, and the oxidation peak currents were reduced. Furthermore, the oxidation of ascorbic acid (AA) was likewise examined; with the increase of AA concentration, the anodic peak current enhanced significantly, indicating that all the CPEs possessed the ability to catalyze the oxidation of AA.

The amperometric detection of Cr(vi), Fe(iii), BrO3−, and NO2− ions

Cr(vi) has been named as the seventeenth most dangerous toxic substance by the US Environmental Protection Agency.35 Excessive or insufficient Fe(iii) can cause serious human system diseases and can even be life-threatening.36 Nitrate and nitrite are found widely in the environment, but at the same time, they are also carcinogens and threaten human health.37 Thus, the detection of these harmful ions is very important. In this work, we employ 1–4-CPEs as amperometric sensors for detecting Cr(vi), Fe(iii), BrO3− and NO2−. Potassium dichromate and ferric chloride in ultrapure water were used as Cr(vi) and Fe(iii) sources, respectively. Fig. 6 reveals that 1-CPE displays a good current response at the optimal voltage with the successive additions of Cr(vi), Fe(iii), BrO3− and NO2− at 30 s intervals. At the same time, within the corresponding concentration range, the relation between the response currents of 1–4-CPEs and the substrate concentration shows a good linear relationship. For 2-, 3- and 4-CPE, the detection of Cr(vi), Fe(iii), BrO3− and NO2− are presented in Fig. S8–S10.† The experimental data for detecting the above four substances using 1–4-CPEs are summarized in Table S4.† Most strikingly, the limit of detection (LOD) (S/N = 3) of 1-CPE for Cr(vi) reaches 0.51 μM, with a linear regression equation of I (μA) = −0.146 C [Cr(vi)] (μM) − 0.42 (R2 = 0.996). Compared with other previous reports, we find that 1 has excellent sensing performance toward Cr(vi) with a low detection limit, lower than that of most electrochemical sensors reported in the literature (Table S5†).38–42

For electrochemical sensors, it is important to distinguish the target analytes from other potential interfering substances.43 To prove the anti-interference abilities of complexes 1–4, we choose Cl−, Br−, SO42− and CO32− as interfering ions in the detection of BrO3− and NO2−. Meanwhile, Zn2+, Co2+, Cd2+, Ni2+ and K+ cations were chosen as interferents in the detection of Cr(vi) and Fe(iii) using 1–4-CPEs (Fig. 7 and S11–S13†). Adding the same concentration of Cr(vi), Fe(iii), BrO3− and NO2− solution to the electrolyte causes a significant current response of 1–4-CPEs, while after adding the same concentration of the interfering ions, the current response remained unchanged. The results illustrate that 1–4-CPEs possess outstanding selectivity for Cr(vi), Fe(iii), BrO3− and NO2−.

Adsorption properties of complexes 1–4 toward organic dyes

POM-based MOCs have been regarded as a type of effective adsorbent material to remove some organic dye molecules.44 In order to explore the performance of the title complexes in dye adsorption, we evaluate their adsorption capacities towards gentian violet (GV) and methylene blue (MB) in the dark, using a classic approach (Scheme S1†): 40 mg samples of 1–4 were suspended in 80 mL GV and MB solution (10 mg L−1) and stirred evenly. Then, the supernatants of GV and MB solutions were recorded by UV-vis spectroscopy to test the organic dye concentration at a predetermined time. With increasing adsorption time, we observed apparent variations in the color and concentration of MB and GV aqueous solutions in the presence of 1–4. As displayed in Fig. 8 and 9, we observed that complexes 1–4 have different adsorption capacities for GV and MB. The calculation revealed that the adsorption ratio of GV was 98.18% for 1 after 50 minutes, and 90.07% for 2, 71.86% for 3 and 63.15% for 4 after 90 minutes. For MB, the adsorption ratios were 95.09% for 1, 81.60% for 2, 61.16% for 3 and 60.61% for 4; the adsorption capacities of 1 and 2 are better than 3 and 4. The adsorption abilities of 1–4 may result from the hydrogen bonding interactions and/or π–π stacking interactions between the ligands and organic dye molecules. We speculate that the methyl group presents a certain hindrance to the adsorption capacity towards organic dyes.

Conclusions

Four new octamolybdate-based complexes, 1–4, were obtained through the use of bis(pyrazine)–bis(amide) ligands with distinct substituents and spacer lengths, resulting in diverse structures. The length of the ligand has a considerable effect on the architectures and adsorption performances of 1–4. All the complexes possess electrocatalytic abilities and can be employed as amperometric sensors to detect Cr(vi), Fe(iii), BrO3−, and NO2− ions. Furthermore, it is worth mentioning that 1 exhibits the most significant current response to Cr(vi), with a lower detection limit. In addition, 1–4 can act as adsorbents to adsorb the organic dyes GV and MB. This work provides a basis for the development of efficient polyoxometalate-based electrochemical sensors, which is of great significance in the field of environmental monitoring.
