Introduction

The field of molecule-based magnetic materials has attracted intense interest, specially since the last decades of the 20th century.1 The discovery of single-molecule magnets (SMMs), i.e. molecules that retain their magnetization after removal of an applied field, thus, displaying magnetization hysteresis, along with the observation of macroscopic tunneling of the magnetization in these species revived the field.2 Along with the rapid development of zero-dimensional (0D) SMMs, Glauber's prediction of the possibility of observing the freezing of the magnetization in 1D chains3a was realized on a new class of compounds, called single-chain magnets (SCMs). These systems require high uniaxial anisotropy, strong intrachain interactions between the high-spin magnetic units, and weak interchain interactions. In the last decade, many examples of SCMs have been reported.3 Gatteschi's group reported the first CoIISCM which opened new perspectives of storing information in a single magnetic polymer.3b Ligands such as cyano, carboxylato, hydroxylo and azido are widely used as bridging ligands in coordination chemistry. Amongst them, the azido ligand is often used in the assembly of coordination complexes with targeted ferromagnetic coupling, since it can bridge two or more metals using only one terminal N atom in what is called the end-on (EO) fashion, providing a very efficient pathway for ferromagnetic coupling.4 However, there are few examples of SCMs containing both azido and CoII ions.5 Usually the synthetic strategies to achieve SCMs are those commonly used in coordination chemistry and involve self-assembly either using building blocks or from the metal and ligands,6 and tuning the magnetic interactions.7

Herein we present two examples in which the dimensionality and magnetic properties have been modulated by using different solvent media and ratio of reactants, 1, [Co(N3)(L)·H2O]n and 2, [CoNa(N3)2(L)]n (HL = Pyrazine-2-carboxylato). 1 is a ladder-like 1D chain constructed by ferromagnetically coupled dinuclear CoII entities while compound 2 is a 2D framework of 1D CoII-azido SCMs.

Experimental

Materials and synthesis

All the chemicals used for synthesis of the compounds were of analytical grade and commercially available. Co(NO3)2·6H2O, Pyrazine-2-carboxylato (HL) and sodium azide were purchased from commercial sources and used as received.

Caution: Azide-metal complexes are potentially explosive, only a small amount of material should be prepared, and it should be prepared and handled with care.

Synthesis of [Co(N3)(L)·H2O]n (1)

A mixture of Co(NO3)2·6H2O (1.5 mmol), NaN3 (1.5 mmol) and HL (0.5 mmol) in 15 mL H2O was sealed in a Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel (25 mL), heated at 140 °C for 2 days under autogenous pressure, and then cooled to room temperature. Red block crystals of 1 were harvested in 30% yield based on HL. FT-IR (KBr): 2085 cm−1 (N3−). Elemental analysis (%) for C5H5CoN5O3 (1): Calcd.: C 24.81, H 2.08 N 28.93; Found: C 24.45, H 2.13, N 28.59.

Synthesis of [CoNa(N3)2(L)]n (2)

A mixture of Co(NO3)2·6H2O (0.5 mmol), NaN3 (2 mmol) and HL (0.5 mmol) in 15 mL MeOH was sealed in a Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel (25 mL), heated at 140 °C for 2 days under autogenous pressure, and then cooled to room temperature. Dark red block crystals of 2 were harvested in 20% yield based on HL. FT-IR (KBr): 2079 cm−1 (N3−). Elemental analysis (%) for C5H3CoN8NaO2 (2): Calcd. C 20.78, H 1.05 N 38.77; Found: C 20.59, H 1.30, N 38.41.

Physical measurements

Elemental analyses (C, H, N) were performed on a Perkin-Elmer 240C analyzer. IR spectra were measured on a Tensor 27 OPUS (Bruker) FT-IR spectrometer with KBr pellets. The X-ray powder diffraction (XRPD) was recorded on a Rigaku D/Max-2500 diffractometer at 40 kV, 100 mA for a Cu-target tube and a graphitemonochromator. Simulation of the XRPDspectra was carried out by the single-crystal data and diffraction-crystal module of the Mercury (Hg) program available free of charge via the Internet at http://www.iucr.org

Magnetic data were collected using crushed crystals of the sample on a Quantum Design MPMS-XL SQUID magnetometer equipped with a 5 T magnet. The data were corrected using Pascal's constants to calculate the diamagnetic susceptibility; an experimental correction for the sample holder was applied.

X-ray data collection and structure determinations

X-ray single-crystal diffraction data for complexes 1 and 2 were collected on a Bruker Smart 1000 CCD diffractometer at 293(2) K with Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) by the ω scan mode. The program SAINT8 was used for integration of the diffraction profiles. All the structures were solved by direct methods using the SHELXS program of the SHELXTL package and refined by full-matrix least-squares methods with SHELXL (semi-empirical absorption corrections were applied using the SADABS program).9 Metal atoms in each complex were located from the E-maps and other non-hydrogen atoms were located in successive difference Fourier syntheses and refined with anisotropic thermal parameters on F2. The hydrogen atoms of the ligands were generated theoretically onto the specific atoms and refined isotropically with fixed thermal factors. Detailed crystallographic data are summarized in Table 1.

Results and discussion

Synthesis and crystal structure

Solvothermal synthesis has been extensively used in the synthesis of coordination compounds and 1D, 2D and 3D systems. In this case, we have successfully used solvothermal synthesis in Teflon autoclaves under autogenous pressure to obtain two CoII-azido species with competition from two kinds of negative ligands. Solvent conditions and the ratio of N3− are the main factors effecting the construction of complexes 1 and 2. In complex 1, CoII ions are hexacoordinated with one coordinated water molecule, showing a disordered octahedral fashion. Complex 2 synthesized from methanol by solvothermal conditions resulted in rare five-coordinated CoII ions without coordinated water molecules, bridged solely by end-on azide to form 1D chains. The use of excess NaN3 in the synthesis of 2 makes new chains in the layers and diamagnetic Na+ ions make the chains nearly isolated. The CoII : N3− ratio to obtain compound 1 is 1 : 1, which is what is found in the final product. To obtain compound 2, the CoII : N3− ratio is 1 : 4, and the CoII : N3− ratio in the final product 2 is 1 : 2. The excess of NaN3 in the reaction media that leads to the isolation of 2 also facilitates the incorporation of Na+ ions into the product during the self-assembly process. In both cases, the nature of the final product could not be predicted beforehand, as neither could the structural role of the Na+ ions be anticipated. However, these reactions build up our knowledge on how the Co : N3 : L reaction system works and will certainly allow us in the future to have greater control over the reaction products, and even provide access to targeted frameworks.

Data collection and crystallographic parameters for 1 and 2 are listed in Table 1. Selected bond lengths and angles of 1 and 2 are given in Table 2.

[Co(N3)(L)·H2O]n (1)

Complex 1 crystallizes in the triclinic space groupP. The asymmetric unit of complex 1 consists of one CoII ion, one azido anion, one L ligand and one coordinated water molecule. The CoII ion displays a distorted octahedral coordination environment (CoO2N4) (Fig. 1S in ESI†). The CoII coordination sphere is occupied by one H2O ligand (Co1–O1W = 2.070 Å), two nitrogen atoms from EO azido anions (Co1–N1 = 2.162 Å and Co1–N1A = 2.122 Å), one oxygen atom from the carboxylato group of L (Co1–O1 = 2.091 Å) and two nitrogen atoms of the pyrazinegroups of two different L ligands (Co1–N4 = 2.115 Å and Co1–N5B = 2.188 Å). The EO azido ligands link two symmetry-related CoII ions to form dinuclear CoII units, and these [Co2] units are linked by the pyrazinegroups of the pyrazinecarboxylato ligands in a ladder like 1D ribbon (Fig. 1a). Hydrogen bonds (O1W⋯O2 = 2.693 Å) connect the 1D chains into a 3D framework (Fig. 1b).

[CoNa(N3)2(L)]n (2)

Complex 2 crystallizes in the monoclinic space groupC2/c. The asymmetric unit of 2 consists of one five-coordinated CoII ions, one Na+ ion, two azido ligands and one L. The CoII ion is pentacoordinated in a distorted square-base pyramidal fashion, with τ = 0.285 (τ = 1 for trigonal bipyramid and 0 for square-based pyramid). The CoII ions are bridged by EO azido ligands into 1D chains along the c axis of the unit cell (Fig. 2a), it is important to point out that the EO azido ligand is the only bridge between the CoII ions in 2. The coordination sphere of each CoII ion is comprised by two nitrogen atoms from EO azido ligands (Co1–N1A = 2.062 Å and Co1–N1 = 2.092 Å), one μ1,1,3azido (or end-to-end azido ligand that bridges the CoII ions to Na1 (Co1–N4 = 2.137 Å) and one chelating L ligand in a κN,O mode, using both its pyrazinic N donor and one oxygen atom of the carboxylato group to bind to Co1 (Co1–N7 = 2.152 Å and Co1–O2 = 2.037 Å) (Fig. 2S†). The 1D chains formed by Co1 and N1 are bridged by μ1,1,3 azides with Na1 to form a 2D network (Fig. 2b).

XRPD results

In order to confirm the phase purity of the bulk materials, X-ray powder diffraction (XRPD) experiments were carried out for complexes 1 and 2. The experimental and computer-simulated patterns of 1 and 2 are shown in Fig. 3S.† The excellent agreement between the experimental XRPD pattern of the bulk samples and the simulated from the single-crystal model for both 1 and 2 confirm the phase purity of the bulk materials.

Magnetic studies

[Co(N3)(L)·H2O]n (1)

Even though the crystallographic asymmetric unit of 1 consists of only one CoII ion, the magnetic structure is best described as ladder of [Co2] units linked by the pyrazinegroups of the pyrazinecarboxylato ligands, as shown in Fig. 1a. Magnetization vs. field data were collected at 2 K, and are shown in the inset of Fig. 3. The magnetization tends to saturation and is in agreement with the expected for two CoII ions with spin–orbit coupling. DC magnetic susceptibility data were collected for a crushed crystalline sample of 1 in the 2 to 300 K temperature range at applied field of 0.3 T (Fig. 3). The χT product per [Co2] unit at 300 K has a value of 6.45 cm3K mol−1, larger than the spin-only value expected for two high-spin ions with S = 3/2. The value is in good agreement with the observed values for hexacoordinated CoII with strong spin–orbit coupling (usual χT values per CoII ion are in the 2.3 to 3.4 cm3K mol−1 range).10 As temperature decreases the χT product increases, until a maximum is reached at 30 K, indicating predominant ferromagnetic interactions. Below this temperature, a sudden drop of χT is observed. Due to the unquenched spin–orbit coupling, a quantitative analysis of the magnetic data for the ladder-like 1D chain of CoII ions of 1 is not possible. Currently, the available software for treating magnetic complexes with spin–orbit coupling like VPMAG11 are limited to mononuclear and dinuclear complexes. In the case of complex 1, if the coupling through the pyrazine is not taken into account and the dimers are considered as ‘isolated’ the magnetic data can be modelled using VPMAG. This is a very reasonable approximation, since it is known that pyrazine bridges lead to extremely weak (less than 1 K or 1.4 cm−1) magnetic coupling.12 For years, the only available theory to quantitatively study the exchange coupling in dinuclear and trinuclear CoII complexes has been the one proposed by Lines.13 In this theory, a perfect octahedral high-spin CoII complex is assumed, in which the single-ion excited states are well separated (> 8000 cm−1) from the ground state and can be neglected. The 4T1 ground state is split into a sextet, a quartet and a Kramers' doublet by spin–orbit coupling. The eigenvalues and eigenfunctions are found by diagonalizing the energy matrix built using the operator Ĥ = −3/2 κλL·Ŝ where κ and λ are the orbital reduction factor and the spin–orbit coupling parameters respectively. The occurrence of the factor −3/2 is due to the analogy between the matrix elements of L within the 4T1 state and that of the −3/2L between the p functions. Unfortunately, when trying to fit the magnetic data for dinuclear CoII complexes, applying Lines' formula the fit is always rather poor due to the distortion with regard to the perfect octahedral geometry. Recently, Lloret14 and Cano11 have revisited this problem in dinuclear systems assuming axial distortion. In this case, the triplet orbital 4T1g ground state splits into a singlet 4A2 and a doublet 4E levels with a D energy gap. The one-centre operator responsible for an axial distortion is expressed by eqn (1). Max = D [Lz2 − 1/3 L(L+1)]. (1)

The Hamiltonian involving the magnetic exchange, spin–orbit coupling, axial distortion and Zeeman interaction is given by eqn (2). In the weak crystal field limit (B ≫ Dq), A = 1.5 whereas in the strong crystal field limit (B ≪ Dq), A = 1. In this model ge is the free electron g value, ge = 2.0. (2)

An analytical expression for the magnetic susceptibility as a function of J, A, κ, λ and D cannot be derived. The values of these parameters have to be determined through numerical matrix diagonalization. The VPMAG package developed by Cano11 has been used to fit the experimental data. Even though the VPMAG software performs a fitting with a large number of parameters which is admittedly not ideal, it is the most reliable software currently available to model magnetic data including spin–orbit coupling. This software has been tested by the authors and successfully used to obtain reliable values for a large number of CoII dinuclear complexes.11 The Aκ, D and λ parameters are obtained from the high temperature data, while the exchange parameter J is obtained from fitting all the data (from 2 to 300 K) and using the Aκ, D and λ values obtained from the high temperature fitting. The best fit is shown in Fig. 3 as a solid line. The best fit values were: Aκ = 1.2, D = 306 cm−1; λ = −84 cm−1 (λ for the free ion is −175 cm−1); J = 42 cm−1 and the electron g factor, ge, fixed to 2.0; the agreement factor was F = 1.53 ×10−4 (F is defined as Σ(χmTexp − χmTtheo)2)/Σ(χmTexp)2. AC magnetic susceptibility data collected for complex 1 at various frequencies (not shown here) reveal no out-of-phase signal.

[CoNa(N3)2(L)]n (2)

Magnetic susceptibility data were collected for a crushed crystalline sample of 2 (Fig. 4). The χT product per CoII ion in 2 has a value at 300 K of 2.82 cm3K mol−1, larger than the expected value of 1.875 cm3K mol−1. The CoII ions in complex 2 are pentacoordinated in distorted square-pyramidal fashion thus having S = 3/2, an axial geometry.15 As temperature decreases, the χT product increases until it reaches a maximum value of 10.49 cm3K mol−1 at 7 K. This evidences ferromagnetic coupling between the CoII ions of 2. The susceptibility data at T > 70 K obeys the Curie–Weiss law (Fig. 4, inset) with C = 2.6 cm3K mol−1 and Θ = 23.8 K, which indicates ferromagnetic interactions between the CoII ions in 2. As a very rough estimate, molecular field theory16 can be used to extract an approximate value for the ferromagnetic exchange interaction between the CoII ions in complex 2 using the obtained value of Θ = 2zJS(S + 1)/3kB: for S = 3/2 and average z = 2 for each CoII with two nearest neighbours along the chain the magnetic interaction J/kB = 5 K, this value is in agreement with exchange constant values reported for EO azido-CoII dimers and chains.17

The magnetic structure of 2 consists of chains of pentacoordinated CoII ions bridged solely by EO azido ligands that are organized in 2D layers by the ligands and diamagnetic Na+ ions which play a structural role, but do not provide any efficient pathways for magnetic exchange; in any one 2D layer, two adjacent chains are 7.6 Å apart. The end-on azido ligands mediate ferromagnetic interaction between orthogonal dx2-y2 and dz2 magnetic orbitals on neighbouring CoII ions along the 1D chains. The 1D nature of the magnetic chains that form complex 2 can be corroborated by following the correlation length of the system, which is proportional to the χ′T product at zero-field for a one dimensional problem (Fig. 4S†). In ferromagnetic chains of anisotropic Heisenberg spins the correlation length exponentially increases on decreasing temperature and χ′T = Ceffexp(Δς/T).18 By plotting ln(χ′T) vs. 1/T, where χ′T is the AC in-phase susceptibility at 11 Hz and 3 Oe, one can extract the values of Ceff = 1.37 cm3K mol−1 and Δς = 60 K at the temperature regime between 30 and 15 K, where the linear relationship holds. The Δς value is the energy to form a domain wall within the 1D chain.17 The effective Curie constant obtained is in good agreement with the expected value for a pure Ising S = 3/2 spin, Cising = Ng2μB2S2/3kB = 1.125 cm3K mol−1, as expected for pentacoordinated CoII with S = 3/2. Several authors have reported finite-size effects on hexacoordinated CoII SCMs, like Gatteschi and co-workers.19 As claimed by Clérac and co-workers,20 the exponential increase of the χ′T product above 15 K clearly establishes the 1D nature of compound 2. However, at lower temperatures the very weak intrachain interactions within each 2D layer must be taken into account. Below 7 K the χ′T product reaches a maximum and then decreases slowly, this is usually attributed to the anisotropy of the system and as observed for a number of other SCMs, to the fact that the correlation length becomes larger than the average distance between two defects.21 Complex 2 shows strong irreversibility as evidenced by the opening of magnetization vs. field hysteresis loops at 2 and 3 K without the onset of 3D ordering (Fig. 5) until 2 K, below this temperature the zero-field cool/field cool measurement (ZFC/FC) reveals a small divergence, which could indicate a long-range order phenomenon below this temperature (Fig. 5S†).

AC magnetic susceptibility data at different frequencies were collected for 2 (Fig. 6). These data are very complex. Below 15 K, as temperature decreases an out-of-phase (χ′′) signal appears as the in-phase (χ′) signal decreases. The observed signal is clearly frequency-dependent: along a range of frequencies of 10 to 1500 Hz and the temperature range of the observed maxima is of 4.2 K. Indeed, if the thermal energy is of the order of the effective energy barrier for the relaxation process, then a maximum in χ′′ is seen when the frequency equals the rate of the relaxation process. This is diagnostic of either spin-glassiness or SCM behaviour. Since the properties of ferromagnetic anisotropic chains are dictated by the thermal excitation of domain-walls, ferromagnetic segments of opposite magnetization direction (kinks or solitons) are formed within the chains. When the interchain exchange coupling is extremely weak or negligible like in 2, the establishment of complete 3D order between the chains can become a delicate process readily disturbed by the presence of lattice defects that act as pinning centres preventing the free movements of the domain-walls along the chains. In such a situation, depending on the initial conditions of field and temperature, a large amount of disorder may be easily frozen-in during the interchain ordering process. In this case, a ferromagnetic 1D system arrayed in a crystal by Na+ ions, the clearest possibility is that of having a rare example of 3D array of SCMs. The possibility of a spin-glass was ruled out by calculating the frequency shift parameter Φ introduced by Mydosh.22 The obtained Φ value for the process centered at T2 was 0.04, while the value for the process centered at T1 was 0.14, both of them orders of magnitude above the expected for a spin-glass and in good agreement with the expected values for a disordered system and superparamagnetic behavior (as for example that of a SCM) respectively. The out-of-phase signal has two sets of frequency-dependent peaks centered at T1 = 9.8 K and T2 = 11.8 K, respectively. In the semi-logarithmic plot of the relaxation times (Fig. 6S†), the straight lines are the fit to an Arrhenius law which provides the values of U1 = 138 K and U2 = 304 K for the activation energies, and τ2 = 3.5e − 15 s and τ1 = 5.5e − 10 s for the pre-exponential factors. If the ac susceptibility is studied vs. the frequency, and the Arrhenius law applied (Fig. 7), the obtained values are U = 140 K and τ(f) = 2.5e − 10 s. The activation energies and relaxation times calculated for 2 using both the frequency and temperature dependence of the out-of-phase magnetic susceptibility fall in the range of the reported values for SCMs. The two relaxation processes could be associated with single-kink and kink-pair activations within the ferromagnetic chains, as already reported for other ferromagnetic Ising chains.23 The change in the pre-exponential value reflects the different dynamics (e.g. energies and available states) of the two processes. One has to consider that single-kink excitations have energies J and are created at the ends of the chains, whereas kink-pair excitations have energies 2J. From the Arrhenius fit to T1 and T2, the activation energies U1 = 138 K and U2 = 304 K comply with the condition that one is (nearly) half the other, respectively (Fig. 6S†). Since U1 = 4JS2, we finally obtain J = 15 K or 10 cm−1, which does not differ substantially from the above-estimated value using simpler molecular-field approximation, on the basis of the S = 3/2 formalism for the CoII ions. The above scenario strongly indicates the single-chain magnet nature of the azido-CoII chains of 2. A more detailed analysis of the dynamics of each of the relaxation processes taking part in 2 is clearly precluded by the close proximity of the peaks in the ACvs.T measurement, which leads to overlapping Cole–Cole curves (Fig. 7). On a final note, we report that, as temperature decreases below 5 K, a new slow relaxation process takes place, its frequency dependence cannot be unambiguously assessed since the maximum is not clearly observed down to 2 K (Fig. 6).

The possibility of long range order was clearly ruled out by studying the heat capacity of complex 2. No sign of long-range magnetic order is in fact detected from heat capacity measurements of 2 down to T = 0.35 K (Fig. 7S†), that show a combination of phonon contributions and 1D magnetic fluctuations within the chains. In general for a quasi-1D magnetic system one expects a broad rounded anomaly in the magnetic specific heat at a temperature of the order of kBT/J, with J/kB the intrachain exchange constant, as a consequence of the development of the 1D magnetic correlations. This broad anomaly is then followed by a small peak at lower temperature, when 3D magnetic order is established. For well-isolated magnetic chains (like in 2), the entropy content of the 3D ordering peak can become very small, and the presence of lattice defects along the chains may smear it out very fast. In the present case, indeed, no anomaly is detected likely to be due to the segmentation of the chains and crystallographic defects. We furthermore note that the heat capacity at the lowest temperatures is clearly sensitive to the applied magnetic field (Fig. 7S†). The magnetic contribution in this temperature range is one–two orders of magnitude smaller than that expected for isolated and field-split magnetic ions. The observed behaviour is consistent with the excitation of randomly-formed magnetic domains along the 1D chains, with relaxation times longer than the time needed for a single measurement.

Conclusion

By tuning the CoII : N3− ratio and the solvent in the CoII–L reaction system, two new different species have been synthesized. The ladder-like polymer1 is formed by ferromagnetically coupled [Co2] units. The magnetic properties of 2 are complex: it is a new example of a 2D crystalline array of CoII-azido SCMs. The CoII : N3− : L reaction system has proved to be versatile and the results presented here indicate that our further efforts in exploring these reaction systems could lead to new examples of CoII : N3− compounds with SMM or SCM properties.
