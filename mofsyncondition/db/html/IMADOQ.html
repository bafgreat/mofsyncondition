Introduction

Investigations of the synthesis, structures and properties of coordination polymers or metal–organic frameworks are currently a frontier area of research.1 The major goal in this area comprised the development of strategies for a more rational design of their structures, in order to design new materials with desired physical properties in a more directed way. This approach, called “crystal engineering”, must include a special knowledge of the interactions in crystals, as well as investigations of structure–property relationships in solids.2

However, for any characterization or application, large quantities of a desired compound must be prepared in a pure form, which, especially in the case of coordination compounds, is sometimes difficult to achieve. Most of such compounds are prepared in the liquid phase, in which different species are commonly in equilibrium. Therefore, frequently mixtures are obtained in the end, or in some cases compounds are overlooked or cannot be prepared in solution e.g. if they are metastable.

One alternative approach for the development and pure preparation of such compounds can be thermal decomposition reactions, in which ligand-rich precursor compounds with additional neutral co-ligands are heated. In most cases this leads to a stepwise removal of these ligands and to the formation of ligand-deficient intermediates. If the ligand-rich precursors consist of paramagnetic transition metals and small-sized non-bridging magnetically active anionic ligands, this method frequently leads to the formation of new, ligand-deficient intermediates with a more condensed coordination network, in which the small-sized ligands bridge the metal centres. This structure transformation is accompanied with a dramatic change in their magnetic properties. Whereas most of the ligand-rich compounds exhibit only Curie–Weiss paramagnetism, cooperative magnetic phenomena, like e.g. ferro- or antiferromagnetism, are frequently observed in the ligand-deficient intermediates. It must be noted that such ligand-deficient compounds are in most cases not accessible by reaction of their educts in solution, but they can always be prepared phase pure by thermal decomposition of their corresponding ligand-rich precursors.

Systematic investigations on the utility of this method for the preparation of new coordination polymers were performed by our group recently. We showed that a series of new ligand-deficient compounds with cooperative magnetic exchange interactions based on transition metal thiocyanates,3–6 selenocyanates,7 or formates8 containing different organic spacer ligands like pyrazine,3,7pyrimidine,4pyridazine5 or 4,4′-bipyridine6,8 can be prepared by this elegant route.

In the course of these systematic investigations we explored whether this method can generally be applied to the preparation of condensed networks of a wide range of different compounds. In this context dicyanamido (dca) compounds are of special interest.9 The dicyanamido (dca) anion is a versatile ligand, with coordination modes ranging from monodentate10 to μ4 bridging.11 The most frequently observed coordination mode is the bidentate μ-1,5 bridging mode, for which several coordination polymers of composition [M(μ-1,5-dca)2(L)x]n with L = different neutral N- and O-donor co-ligands, like e.g.pyridine12,13 or methanol13,14 for x = 2 and e.g.pyrazine15,16 or 4,4-bipyridine17 for x = 1, exist. All these compounds have in common that they exhibit only weak magnetic exchange interactions due to their long five-atomic exchange pathway of the μ-1,5 dca bridges, and cooperative phenomena are not observed in any of these compounds. In contrast, the tridentate μ-1,3,5 dca bridges in the transition metal dicyanamides [M(dca)2]n lead to cooperative magnetic exchange interactions at lower temperatures,13,18,19 demonstrating that the three-atom pathway of such bridges are of significant importance for a magnetic ordering. However, according to a search in the Cambridge Structure Database only a few compounds with paramagnetic transition metals and μ-1,3,5 dca bridges have been reported.20,21

Coming back to the fact that thermal decomposition reactions of precursor compounds with extended structures like e.g., μ-1,5 dca bridges, lead to new intermediate compounds with highly condensed networks like e.g., μ-1,3,5 dca bridges, we investigated the ligand-rich 1 : 2 μ-1,5 dca bridged compound [Mn(dca)2(pyridazine)2]n (1-Mn) reported recently22 for its thermal properties in order to obtain a new ligand-deficient compound with a more condensed structure. Furthermore, for a more extensive investigation of their magneto-structural correlations, we also prepared the corresponding ligand-rich 1 : 2 precursor compounds with iron(ii) 1-Fe, cobalt(ii) 1-Co and nickel(ii) 1-Ni. Here we report on these investigations.

Mn(NO3)2·4H2O, FeSO4·7H2O, Co(NO3)2·6H2O, NiCl2·6H2O, Na(dca) and pyridazine as well as all solvents were obtained from Alfa Aesar and used without further purification.

Single-crystal structure analysis

Data collection was performed with an imaging plate diffraction system (IPDS-1 for 1-Fe, 2-Mn and 2-Fe; IPDS-2 for 1-Mn, 1-Co and 2-Co) with Mo-Kα-radiation from STOE & CIE. The structure solutions were done with direct methods using SHELXS-97 and structure refinements were performed against |F|2 using SHELXL-97.23 A numerical absorption correction was applied using X-Red Version 1.31 and X-Shape Version 2.11 of the Program Package X-Area.24 All non-hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic displacement parameters. All hydrogen atoms were positioned with idealized geometry and were refined with fixed isotropic displacement parameters [Ueq(H) = −1.2·Ueq(C)] using a riding model with dC–H = 0.93 Å for 1-Mn, 1-Fe, 1-Co, 2-Fe and 2-Co as well as dC–H = 0.95 Å for 2-Mn. XRPD experiments were performed using a PANalytical X'Pert Pro MPD Reflection Powder Diffraction System with Cu-Kα-radiation (λ = 154.0598 pm) that is equipped with a PIXcel semiconductor detector from PANalytical.

Differential thermal analysis, thermogravimetry, and mass spectroscopy (DTA–TG–MS)

The DTA–TG measurements were performed in a nitrogen atmosphere (purity: 5.0) in Al2O3 crucibles using a STA-409CD instrument from Netzsch. The DTA–TG–MS measurements were performed with the same instrument, which is connected to a quadrupole mass spectrometer from Balzers via Skimmer coupling from Netzsch. The MS measurements were performed in analogue and trend scan mode in Al2O3 crucibles in a dynamic nitrogen atmosphere (purity: 5.0) using heating rates of 4 °C min−1. All measurements were performed with a flow rate of 75 mL min−1. The instrument was calibrated using standard reference materials.

Results and discussion

Crystal structures of the ligand-rich 1 : 2 compounds

The crystal structures of the 1 : 2 compounds [Mn(dca)2(pydz)2]n (1-Mn), [Fe(dca)2(pydz)2]n (1-Fe) and [Co(dca)2(pydz)2]n (1-Co) were determined by single crystal X-ray diffraction. The structure of 1-Mn was redetermined at room temperature because significant differences between the calculated and experimental powder patterns are observed based on the structure determined at 87 K, published before.22 For [Ni(dca)2(pydz)2]n (1-Ni) no single crystals were obtained, but XRPD investigations show that this compound is isotypic to 1-Fe and 1-Co (see Table 1 for lattice parameters and Fig. S10† in the ESI for powder pattern). 1-Mn crystallizes in the centrosymmetric triclinic space groupP-1 with one formula unit in the unit cell whereas 1-Fe and 1-Co are an isotypic pair and crystallize in the centrosymmetric monoclinic space groupC2/m with two formula units in the unit cell (Table 1). The asymmetric unit of 1-Mn consists of one metal cation located on a centre of inversion as well as one pyridazineligand and one dicyanamido anion in general positions. The asymmetric unit of 1-Fe and 1-Co consist of one metal cation located in position 2/m as well as one pyridazineligand and one dicyanamido anion situated on a crystallographic mirror plane. The topology of the coordination network in the crystal structures of 1-Mn and 1-Fe/1-Co are identical, while differences are found in their geometrical parameters and the orientation of the building units. In the crystal structure the metal cations are N-coordinated by four bridging dicyanamido anions and two terminal pyridazineligands in a slightly distorted octahedral coordination (Fig. 1). The metal cations are each μ-1,5 bridged by four dicyanamido anions into linear polymeric M-(dca)2-M chains, in which the pyridazineligands are terminally N-bonded (Fig. 2: top). These chains stretch along the direction of the crystallographic b-axis and are stacked in the direction of the crystallographic a-axis involving weak π–π-interactions of intermolecular adjacent pyridazineligands (Fig. 2: bottom).

The MN6 octahedron is markedly compressed, with four short M–Ndca distances in the range of 2.2150(13)–2.1095(14) Å and two long M–Npydz distances in the range of 2.2870(13)–2.166(2) Å, while the N–M–N bond angles are in the range of 87.94(14)–92.04(5)° (for ‘cis’ N–M–N bonds) and 180° (for ‘trans’ N–M–N bonds) (Table 2). The metal–metal separation through the bridging dicyanamido anions is 7.4686(5) Å for 1-Mn, 7.4195(9) Å for 1-Fe and 7.3409(5) Å for 1-Co, whereas the shortest inter-chain separation of two adjacent metal cations is 6.3864(5) Å for 1-Mn, 6.378(1) Å for 1-Fe and 6.3272(5) Å for 1-Co.

Investigations on the thermal decomposition reactions

The ligand-rich 1 : 2 compounds [M(dca)2(pydz)2]n with M = Mn (1-Mn), Fe (1-Fe), Co (1-Co) and Ni (1-Ni) can be easily prepared by reaction of their educts in solution (see Experimental). Due to their extended crystal structure they seem to be good candidates for thermal decomposition reactions in order to obtain new ligand-deficient compounds with higher condensed coordination networks. Thus, their thermal properties were investigated.

On heating, [Mn(dca)2(pydz)2]n (1-Mn) in a thermobalance to 300 °C, two mass steps are observed in the TG curve that are accompanied by endothermic events in the DTA curve (Fig. 3). From the MS trend scan curve, it is proven that only pyridazine (m/z = 80) is emitted during these mass steps. The DTG curve shows that the first event is not well resolved and resolution cannot be improved by heating rate dependent measurements. However, the experimental mass losses for 1-Mn of Δmexp (1st step) = 22.7% and Δmexp (2nd step) = 23.1% are in good agreement with that calculated for the loss of half of the ligands in each step [Δmcalc (pydz) = 23.1%]. A similar thermal behavior is also found for its analogues 1-Fe, 1-Co and 1-Ni (see Fig. S4–S6† in the ESI).

Based on the experimental mass losses, it can be assumed that in the first step ligand-deficient 1 : 1 intermediates of composition [M(dca)2(pydz)]n with M = Mn (2-Mn), Fe (2-Fe), Co (2-Co) and Ni (2-Ni) are formed, which lose the remaining ligands in the second step leading to the formation of M(dca)2. The final products of these reactions were identified as the corresponding metal dicyanamides by X-ray powder diffraction.

To investigate the nature of intermediates formed in the thermal decomposition reaction of the ligand-rich 1 : 2 compounds, additional TG measurements were performed and stopped after the first TG step. The residues obtained were investigated by elemental analysis, infrared spectroscopy and X-ray powder diffraction. Elemental analysis yielded a composition which is in agreement with that calculated for ligand-deficient 1 : 1 compounds (see Experimental).

Taking into account that the octahedral coordination mode of the metal cations must be retained if some of the co-ligands are removed, it can be expected that a part of the dicyanamido anions must become μ-1,3,5 bridging. This can easily be proved by IR spectroscopic investigations.

In general the dicyanamido anion exhibits several ν(CN) bands in the infrared spectra. These include νas(C–N) + νs(C–N), νas(CN), νs(CN), νas(C–N) and νs(C–N). In the IR spectra of the ligand-rich 1 : 2 compounds these bands are shifted to higher frequencies compared to those in free anion, which can be traced back to the bridging coordination mode of the dicyanamido anions (Table 3).26 In comparison to the ligand-rich 1 : 2 compounds, the vibration bands of the ligand-deficient 1 : 1 compounds are split and especially νas(CN) and νs(CN) are somewhat shifted to higher frequencies (Table 3). The shifting can be assigned to μ-1,3,5 bridging dicyanamido anions and the splitting is attributed to the coexistence of μ-1,5 and μ-1,3,5 bridging dicyanamido anions in the ligand-deficient 1 : 1 compounds.

Investigations of the 1 : 1 intermediates 2-Mn, 2-Fe and 2-Co by XRPD measurements show clearly that all compounds are isotypic (Fig. 4). All powder patterns can be indexed based on an orthorhombic unit cell, which further supports that the intermediates are obtained phase pure. However, the intermediate 2-Ni cannot be obtained phase pure by thermal decomposition, and all isolated residues were always contaminated with large amounts of the precursor 1-Ni (see Fig S14† in the ESI). Therefore, no further investigations were performed.

To prove unambiguously the above proposed coordination mode of the 1 : 1 ligand-deficient intermediates, we tried to prepare single crystals of 2-Mn, 2-Fe, 2-Co and 2-Ni in solution. This can be done by the reaction of pyridazine with sodium dicyanamide and the transition metal halides, but all batches were always contaminated with either the metal dicyanamides or other compounds which cannot be identified (see Experimental). However, single crystal structure analysis clearly proved the coexistence of μ-1,5 and μ-1,3,5 bridged dca anions, in agreement with our spectroscopic investigations (see below). Furthermore, the experimental XRPD patterns match with those calculated from single crystal data, which show that the 1 : 1 intermediates 2-Mn and 2-Fe are formed phase pure (see Fig. S11 and S12† in the ESI). Only compound 2-Co is always contaminated with traces of α-Co(dca)2 regardless of the temperature at which 2-Co was isolated (see Fig. S13† in the ESI). It must be noted that this contamination is also observed if the ligand-deficient 1 : 1 compounds [Co(dca)2(L)]n with L = pyrazine-1,4-dioxide and 2-methyl-pyrazine-1,4-dioxide are prepared, which was published recently.20

Crystal structures of the ligand-deficient 1 : 1 compounds

[M(dca)2(pydz)2]n with M = Mn (2-Mn), Fe (2-Fe) and Co (2-Co) crystallizes in the centrosymmetric orthorhombic space groupPnma with four formula units in the unit cell. The compounds are isotypic (Table 1). The asymmetric unit consists of one metal cation, one pyridazineligand and two dicyanamido anions located on a crystallographic mirror plane. In the crystal structure the metal cations are N-coordinated by five dicyanamido anions and one pyridazineligand in a slightly distorted octahedral coordination mode (Fig. 5). The overall crystal structure motif exhibits linear M–(dca)2–M chains, which consist of equal parts of μ-1,5 and μ-1,3,5 bridging dicyanamido anions. The central N atom of the μ-1,3,5 bridging dicyanamido anions connects adjacent chains into layers, in which every metal cation is coordinated by two μ-1,5 and three μ-1,3,5 bridging dicyanamido anions. The pyridazineligand is terminally bonded to the metal cation and oriented to the outside of the layers (Fig. 6). The layers are stacked perpendicular to the crystallographic a-axis and half of the pyridazineligands of each layer are eclipsed with those of the next layer, indicating weak π–π-stacking interactions.

The MN6 octahedron is markedly compressed, with four short M–Ndca distances in the range of 2.088(2)–2.2183(15) Å as well as one long M–Npydz distance in the range of 2.140(3)–2.258(2) Å and one long M–Ndca distance in the range of 2.344(2)–2.231(3) Å, while the N–M–N bond angles are in the range of 86.44(6)–93.94(14)° and 175.65(6)–179.39(8)° (Table 4). The metal–metal separation through the μ-1,5 bridging dicyanamido anions is 7.5143(4) Å for 2-Mn, 7.4044(7) Å for 2-Fe and 7.3474(2) Å for 2-Co and the metal–metal separation through the μ-1,3,5 bridging dicyanamido anions is 6.2196(4) Å for 2-Mn, 6.1139(6) Å for 2-Fe and 6.0490(6) Å for 2-Co, whereas the shortest interlayer separation of two adjacent metal cations is 9.1712(4) Å for 2-Mn, 9.1658(8) Å for 2-Fe and 9.1204(6) Å for 2-Co.

Magnetic investigations

In order to investigate all compounds for magneto-structural correlations, the temperature dependence of their susceptibility was investigated by applying a magnetic field of H = 0.1 T in the temperature range of 300–1.8 K (Fig. 7–10). Please note that magnetic measurements for compound 1-Mn were already reported, but for comparison this compound was also investigated. However, our results are in accordance with those found in the literature.22

In the ligand-rich 1 : 2 compounds 1-Mn, 1-Fe, 1-Co and 1-Ni the metal centers are separated by the five atoms of the μ-1,5 bridging dicyanamido anions. It can be assumed that only minor exchange interactions can be mediated by these bridges, because the exchange pathway will be too long, which is in accordance with previously published experimental data and MO calculations.12,16,27 In contrast, in the ligand-deficient 1 : 1 compounds 2-Mn, 2-Fe and 2-Co the metal centers are separated by μ-1,3,5 bridging dicyanamido anions, leading to a much shorter exchange pathway of only three atoms. Therefore, in contrast to their corresponding ligand-rich 1 : 2 compounds, long-range magnetic ordering is expected for the ligand-deficient 1 : 1 intermediates .

As expected for the ligand-rich 1 : 2 compounds 1-Mn, 1-Fe, 1-Co and 1-Ni, only Curie–Weiss paramagnetism is found, indicated by linear χM−1versus T curves, following the Curie–Weiss law χM = C/(T − θ) in the temperature range investigated (Fig. 7: top). A negative Weiss constant θ fitted with the Curie–Weiss law for 1-Mn (−3.7 K), 1-Fe (−1.4 K) and 1-Ni (−0.4 K) indicates only weak and for 1-Co (−20.5 K) strong net antiferromagnetic interactions (Table 4), which is consistent with decreasing χMT values at lower temperatures upon cooling (Fig. 7: bottom). Interchain interactions are expected to be negligible due to large distances between the chains, with the shortest metal to metal separations of 6.3864(5), 6.378(1) and 6.3272(5) Å for 1-Mn, 1-Fe and 1-Co, respectively. The effective magnetic moments μeff for 1-Mn (6.10 μB), 1-Fe (5.55 μB) and 1-Ni (3.11 μB) are in reasonable agreement with the spin-only values for their high spin states (S = 5/2 for Mn2+, 2 for Fe2+ and 1 for Ni2+, g = 2.00). In contrast, for compound 1-Co (5.26 μB) μeff is quite a lot larger than the spin-only value (3.87 μB) for a high spin Co2+ ion (S = 3/2, g = 2.00) (Table 5). However, it is well documented that a significant spin–orbit coupling/zero-field splitting, with g-values strongly deviating from 2.00, yields such an effective magnetic moment.28

For the ligand-deficient 1 : 1 compounds 2-Mn, 2-Fe and 2-Co, no consistent magnetic behavior is found. Compound 2-Mn shows a sharp maximum in the χMvs. T curve, which indicates a long-range antiferromagnetic ordering at lower temperatures, with TN = 5.2 K. Above this temperature Curie–Weiss behavior is present, which is obvious due to the linear χM−1 curve shape (Fig. 8: top). The negative Weiss constant θ of −14.8 K (Table 6) and the decrease of the χMT values upon cooling (Fig. 8: bottom) confirm the antiferromagnetic behavior. The effective magnetic moment of 6.14 μB is in reasonable agreement with the calculated spin-only value for a high spin Mn2+ ion (S = 5/2, g = 2.00) (Table 6). The antiferromagnetic ordering must be mediated by the tridentate dicyanamido bridges, as was observed for the related tridentate structure of the dicyanamido compound α-Mn(dca)2 (spin-canted antiferromagnet with TN = 16 K).13,29,30

At first glance the magnetic susceptibility of compound 2-Fe shows only Curie–Weiss behavior (Fig. 9A). The magnetic data in the range of 300–70 K were fitted with the Curie–Weiss law yielding a negative Weiss constant θ = −23.8 K (Table 6), indicating net antiferromagnetic interactions between the Fe(ii) centers on cooling. This is also obvious by the decrease of the χMT values upon cooling to 20 K (Fig. 9C). The effective magnetic moment μeff of 4.76 μB is in good agreement with the spin-only value of 4.90 μB for a high spin Fe2+ ion (S = 2, g = 2.0) (Table 6). However, below 20 K, χMT values increase rapidly with a maximum around 14.5 K. Below the maximum, χMT the value decreases again, which means that below 14.5 K antiferromagnetic interactions seem to dominate (Fig. 9C). But the increase, and the presence of the peak of the χMT values, suggest a transition to a ferromagnetic state. To investigate this behavior in detail, field-cooled (FC) and zero-field-cooled (ZFC) magnetization at low applied field of Hdc = 0.01 T were measured (Fig. 9B). The FC magnetization was obtained on cooling within the field and the ZFC magnetization was obtained by cooling below the transition temperature in zero-field and then applying the field on warming. The FC and ZFC magnetizations curves exhibit a steep increase at around TC = 18.5 K and 2 K, indicating the occurrence of two long-range ferromagnetic transitions. The difference between the FC and ZFC curves below the transition temperature is another hint for ferromagnetic long-range order. Note that the FC and ZFC curves match perfectly above TC. Furthermore, the presence of ferromagnetic properties are provided by magnetization saturation experiment performed at T = 2 K. Please note that the saturated value of the magnetization at 1.42 Nβ per Fe(ii) ion is not completely reached at 9 T and shows no characteristic hysteresis (Fig. 9D). Further evidence for the ferromagnetic phase transitions is supplied by alternating current (ac) susceptibility measurements. However, both real (χΜ′, in-phase) and imaginary (χΜ′′, out-of-phase) components of the susceptibility show a rather complicated behavior, with hints of three ferromagnetic transition states. At around TC = 2 K a strong signal is observed, followed by two maxima of very weak intensity at around TC = 5.5 K and 18.5 K (Fig. 9E and 9F). The signal at 18.5 K can definitely be assigned to a very small contamination with α-Fe(dca)2 (TC = 18.8 K),9,19,30 and similar observations were made for [Co(dca)2(L)2]n (L = pyrazine dioxide, 2-methylpyrazine dioxide) (see below).20 The origin of the very low signal at 5.5 K is unclear, and it can be traced back to the presence of an unknown phase. However, because of its high intensity the ferromagnetic transition at TC = 2 K can be definitely assigned to be an intrinsic property of compound 2-Fe. Please note that elemental analysis, IR spectroscopic investigations and XRPD measurements of all batches used for the magnetic measurements gave no hints of any contamination (see Experimental). Thus, only traces of contamination must be present.

The magnetic behavior of compound 2-Co is very similar to that of 2-Fe. According to the Curie–Weiss law θ = −18.1 K, which indicates net antiferromagnetic interactions between the adjacent Co(ii) ions (Table 6). This behavior is consistent with the decrease of χMT values on cooling to approx. 25 K. The following steep increase, with a maximum at 6.8 K, is followed by short range of decreasing values to 3.2 K, and increasing values to 2 K (Fig. 10C). Therefore, two ferromagnetic phase transitions might be involved. To investigate this in detail, ZFC and ac measurements were performed. ZFC magnetization measurements at low field of 0.01 T show two maxima at TC = 9 and 2 K (Fig. 10B). Also, ac measurements show two sharp maxima at the same temperatures in the in-phase (Fig. 10E) and out-of-phase (Fig. 10F) ac magnetic susceptibilities. Both these observations support the existence of two ferromagnetic phase transition states. Further evidence for the presence of ferromagnetic ordering is provided by field dependence magnetization experiments performed at T = 2 K. The rapid increase in the saturation at low temperatures and the occurrence of a hysteresis at a relatively low value for the coercitive field of Hc = 0.03 T and a remanent magnetization of Mr = 0.31 Nβ mol−1 are quite typical for long-range ferromagnetic ordering with soft ferromagnetic behaviour (Fig. 10D). It must be noted that only the ferromagnetic transition at TC = 2 K is an intrinsic property of compound 2-Co, whereas the transition state at TC = 9 K must be caused by contamination with α-Co(dca)2 (TC = 9 K).18,19,29,30

Please note that similar observations were made for [Co(dca)2(L)2]n (L = pyrazine dioxide, 2-methylpyrazine dioxide) that were also contaminated with small amounts of α-Co(dca)2.20 Obviously the latter compound is extremely stable and easily formed under the circumstances given.

Conclusion

In this contribution we reported on investigations on the thermal and magnetic properties of seven new coordination polymers based on transition metal dicyanamides and pyridazine as co-ligand. In these investigations we tried to prove whether more condensed dicyanamide networks with modified magnetic interactions could be synthesized in a directed way by thermal decomposition reactions. On controlled heating of ligand-rich 1 : 2 compounds of composition [M(dca)2(pydz)2]n that contain μ-1,5 coordinating dicyanamide anions, ligand deficient 1 : 1 compounds of composition [M(dca)2(pydz)]n are formed, in which half of the anions become μ-1,3,5 bridging. This structural transformation is accompanied with a pronounced change in their magnetic properties. Whereas in the ligand-rich precursors only Curie–Weiss behavior is found, in all ligand-deficient intermediates cooperative magnetic exchange interactions are formed, which are mediated by the tridentate dicyanamide anions.

Although some intermediates are contaminated with traces of the transition metal dicyanamides our results clearly show the great potential of thermal decomposition reactions for the development and preparation of ligand-deficient intermediates with more condensed coordination networks and cooperative magnetic exchange properties. In this context it must be mentioned that the contaminations in 2-Fe are manifested only as an unusual behavior in the magnetic measurements and were not detected by routine analytical tools. Thus, especially if magnetic properties are interpreted, this has to be performed with utmost care.

However, in 2-Mn antiferromagnetic, in 2-Fe and 2-Co ferromagnetic ordering is observed, which shows that the overall situation is more complex. Thus, not only the coordination behavior of the small-sized anionic ligands has to be taken into account but also the magnetic properties and geometry of the transition metals involved must be considered. Therefore, for the investigations on the magneto-structural correlations in such systems, more systematic investigations are needed.
