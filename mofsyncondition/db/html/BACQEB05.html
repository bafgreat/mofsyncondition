Introduction

In recent decades, the generation of metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) has gained considerable interest because of their variety of topological architectures1 and diverse tunable, fascinating and desired functionalities for potential applications.2 An emerging subset of MOFs, chiral metal–organic frameworks (CMOFs) draw the attention beyond the fundamental search for new topologies due to their particular potential applications, such as non-linear optical behaviour3 (i.e., SHG), electro-optical switching,4 chiral recognition,5 enantiomerically selective catalysts,6 separations7 and other areas.8 The possible structural landscape of CMOFs and their racemic analogue is vast. One of the chiral forms of nickel aspartate,9 for example, contains a single helix, while its racemic analogue contains both hands of the same helix.

Thus, the formation of CMOFs becomes one of the major challenges in crystal engineering.10 Essentially, we can devise three strategies for the synthesis of CMOFs: (i) the utilization of a chiral connector,11 (ii) the exploitation of both chiral and achiral ligands to break the symmetry12 and (iii) the use of molecules with spontaneous chiral resolution occurs.13 Out of the three, much interest has been focused on the architectures of helical coordination polymers using chiral connectors as many important biopolymers such as DNA and peptides possess a helical motif with chirality.14 The use of peptides and chiral dicarboxylic acids are particularly intriguing because flexible nature of both offers a virtually unlimited structural diversity.

With this aim, chiral dicaroxylate ligand, l-tartaric acid with two chiral carbon atoms used to synthesize CMOFs. The acid is flexible and has rich stereochemistry and its two carboxylic and two alcoholic groups are chemically unequal, with the 1- and 4-carboxyl groups more acidic than the 2- and 3-alcoholic groups. Therefore, it also has potential to change the direction of partial carboxylic or alcoholic groups to adopt other chiral configurations. So we expect that under appropriate synthetic conditions with appropriate pH values, the protons located on the alcoholic and carboxylic oxygen atoms of tartrate may be kept deactivated to form different CMOFs. At a higher pH (4.95), all the carboxylic and alcoholic groups of the ligand are deprotonated and a supramolecular CMOF, {[Cu2(1,10-phen)2(tartrate)H2O]·8H2O}n (1) results. As pH decreases from 4.95 to 4.10, the alcoholic groups become deprotonated and carboxylic groups stay protonated generating a different CMOF {[Cu(1,10-phen)(tartrate)]·6H2O}n (2). These chiral complexes are characterized by single-crystal X-ray diffraction, IR and CD spectra studies. Interestingly, complex 1 contains scarce 2D water sheet and complex 2 contains 1D helical water chain. Non linear optical, luminescence and guest desorption and re-adsorption properties were also studied.

Experimental

General

Copper(ii) nitrate, hexahydrate; copper(ii) carbonate; sodium tartrate and 1,10- phenanthroline were purchased from Merck chemical company. And all other chemicals used were AR grade. Elemental analysis (C, H, N) were carried out using a Perkin-Elmer 240C elemental analyzer. The thermal analysis was carried out using a Mettler Toledo TGA-DTA 85 thermal analyzer under a flow of dinitrogen (30 mL min−1). The sample was heated at a rate of 10 °C min−1 with inert alumina as a reference. IR spectroscopy was measured on Nicolet Impact 410 spectrometer between 400 and 4000 cm−1, using the KBr pellet method. Photoluminescence spectra were collected on a Shimadzu RF-5301PC spectrophotometer. The solid-state (KBr pellets) circular dichroism (CD) spectra were recorded on a JASCO J-810 spectropolarimeter. Powder XRD pattern of the compounds were recorded by using Cu-Kα radiation (Bruker D8; 40 kV, 40 mA)).

Second harmonic measurements

Q-switched Nd:YAG laser of fundamental wavelength 1064 nm (Spectra Physics, PROLAB 170, pulse width 10 ns and repetition rate 10 Hz) was used as the source of light for powder second harmonic generation (SHG) measurements. The beam from the laser was passed through a couple of high energy laser mirrors and a long pass colored glass filter before being focused onto a glass capillary using a converging lens of 200 mm focal length. The incoherently scattered SH photons were collected in the transverse direction using a combination of a monochromator and a photomultiplier tube. The second harmonic signal was then sampled, averaged over 512 shots and recorded in a digital storage oscilloscope. KDP powder was used as the reference material.

Synthesis of complex (1) {[Cu2(1,10-phen)2(tartrate)H2O]·8H2O}n

Copper(ii) nitrate, hexahydrate (0.1222 gm, 0.5 mmol) dissolved in 5 mL distilled water was allowed to react with 5 mL of an aqueous solution of disodium tartrate (0.1173 gm, 0.5 mmol). After 5 min 15 mL methanolic solution of 1,10-phenanthroline (0.0512 gm, 0.25 mmol) was added to the above solution. The whole mixture was stirred for 30 min and the pH was monitored at 4.95 through dropwise addition of triethylamine. Now the bluish solution was refluxed at 85 °C for 4 h. Then it was allowed to cool and filtered, the blue filtrate was kept in desiccator. After 48 h whisker type sky blue crystals suitable for X-ray structural study were obtained, these were separated and dried. Yield 70%. Anal. calcd for C28H36N4O15Cu2: C 42.2%, H 4.5%, N 7.0%. Found: C 42.1%, H 4.5%, N 7.0%. IR (KBr pellet, cm−1): 3425 (s), 1606 (s), 1518 (w), 1428 (s), 1370 (s), 1220 (w), 1136 (w). UV-vis: λmax 225, 271, 295 and 668 nm.

Synthesis of complex (2) {[Cu(1,10-phen)(H2tartrate)]·6H2O}n

Copper(ii) perchlorate (0.1838 gm, 0.5 mmol) dissolved in 5 mL distilled water was allowed to react with 5 mL of an aqueous solution of disodium tartrate (0.1158 gm, 0.5 mmol). After 5 min 15 mL methanolic solution of 1,10-phenanthroline (0.0508 gm, 0.25 mmol) was added to the above solution. The whole mixture was stirred for 4 h at room temperature and the pH was monitored at 4.10. Then it was filtered, the blue filtrate was kept in desiccator. After 96 h sky blue parallelepiped crystals suitable for X-ray structural study were obtained, these were separated and dried. Yield 80%. Anal. calcd for C16H24N2O12Cu: C 38.4%, H 4.8%, N 5.6%. Found: C 38.3%, H 4.8%, N 5.7%. IR (KBr pellet, cm−1): 3396 (s), 1620 (s), 1519 (w), 1430 (s), 1371 (s), 1220 (w), 1139 (w). UV-vis: λmax 226, 271, 295 and 657 nm.

Crystallographic data collection and refinement

Suitable single crystals of each complexes were mounted on a Bruker SMART diffractometer equipped with a graphite monochromator and Mo-Kα (λ = 0.71073 Å) radiation. The structures were solved using Patterson method by using the SHELXS-97. Subsequent difference Fourier synthesis and least-square refinement revealed the positions of the remaining non hydrogen atoms. Non-hydrogen atoms were refined with independent anisotropic displacement parameters. Hydrogen atoms were placed in idealized positions and their displacement parameters were fixed to be 1.2 times larger than those of the attached non-hydrogen atom. Successful convergence was indicated by the maximum shift/error of 0.001 for the last cycle of the least squares refinement. All calculations were carried out using SHELXS-97,15 SHELXL-97,16PLATON-99,17 ORTEP-3218 and WinGX system Ver-1.64.19 Data collection and structure refinement parameters and crystallographic data for the two complexes are given in Table 1. The selected bond lengths, bond angles and non-covalent interaction parameters are summarized in Table 2–7.

Result and discussion

Crystal structure of complex 1

Complex 1 crystallizes in the chiral P21 space group. X-Ray crystal structure reveals that the complex 1 is dimeric complex connected by tartrate ligand. Each asymmetric unit contains two Cu2+ ions, two phenanthroline (phen) rings and one tartrate4−ligand, one coordinated water molecule and eight uncoordinated water molecules. The ORTEP diagram of the asymmetric unit without uncoordinated water molecules is shown in Fig. 1. The selected bond lengths and angles are given in Table 2. Both the Cu atoms (Cu1 and Cu2) are penta-coordinated with distorted square pyramidal geometry [τ = 0.13 for Cu1 and 0.03 for Cu2] but their coordination environments are different. For the Cu1 atom, N1 and N2 atoms of 1,10-phenanthroline ring, O1 (carboxylic oxygen) and O3 (alcoholic oxygen) atoms of tartrate bridge form the basal plane, whereas for Cu2 atom, N3 and N4 of the other 1,10-phenanthroline ring, O4 (alcoholic oxygen) and O5 (carboxylic oxygen) atoms form the basal plane. The main difference is in the fifth coordination position, for Cu1, fifth coordination position is occupied by O4 (alcoholic oxygen) atom whereas for Cu2 atom, one water molecule (O1W) resides at this position. Two carboxylic oxygen atoms (O2 and O6) of the tartrate bridge remain inert towards coordination mode with metal ion, and IR study agrees with the result. The Cu1 atom is located towards the apical O4 by 0.077(1) Å above the mean plane formed by basal N1, N2, O1 and O3 atoms and the Cu2 atom is located towards the apical O1W by 0.245(1) Å above the mean plane formed by basal N2, N4, O4 and O5, which reflects the degree of distortion of metal coordination environment.

The crystal packing reveals that Cu(ii) dimeric units are linked by O1W–H1W1⋯O3ahydrogen bonding interaction (Table 3) to form a 1D supramolecular chain (Fig. 2). The phen rings bonded to the Cu(ii) centers are just like lateral arms from adjacent dimers and are paired in a parallel fashion. This arrangement facilitates in a face-to-face π⋯π interactions. The pyridyl rings N1–C1 → C5 [R(1)], N2–C6 → C12 [R(2)] and the aryl ring C4 → C9 [R(5)] interact with pyridyl ring N3–C13 → C17 [R(3)], N4–C18 → C24 [R(4)] and aryl ring C16 → C21 [R(6)] in the same molecule at (x, y, z). The distances between their centroids are 3.752 Å, 3.829 Å and 3.858 Å, and the dihedral angles are 5.0°, 4.1° and 4.2°, respectively (Table 4). Similarly, the pyridyl rings R(1), R(2) and the aryl ring R(5) experiences π⋯π interaction with R(3), R(4) and R(6) rings of neighbouring phen molecule at (−1 + x, y, z). The distances between their centroids are 4.975, 4.908 and 4.845 Å and dihedral angles are 5.0, 4.1 and 4.2°, respectively. The π⋯π interactions provide the stability to the 1D chain and aid to form a 2D water sheet.

Crystal structure of complex 2

Complex 2 crystallizes also in the chiral P212121 space group. The single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that the complex 2 is a 1D coordination polymer. Each asymmetric unit contains one Cu2+ ion, one tartrate2−ligand, one phenanthroline (phen) ring and six uncoordinated water molecules. The ORTEP diagram of the asymmetric unit without uncoordinated water molecules is shown in the Fig. 3. The selected bond length and bond angles are given in the Table 5. Cu1 atom in 2 locates itself in a distorted octahedral geometry. N1,N2 atom of the phen ring and O2 and O5* (* = x − 1, y, z) carboxylic atoms of two different tartrate ligands form the basal plane for Cu atom. O1 and O4* of two tartrate ligands are at the axial positions. Distortion from perfect octahedral geometry is reflected in cisoid (73.4(1)–108.8(2)°) and transoid (155.8(1)–157.2(2)°) angles. The distance between two Cu atoms connected by bridging tartrate ligand is 6.706 Å. Like complex 1, two oxygen atoms (O3 and O6) of carboxylate groups remain inert towards coordination with metal ions. The conformational flexibility of the tartrateligand depends upon the behaviour of oxygen atoms in these two complexes. In complex 1, one oxygen atom (O4) binds two metal centers and thus the two Cu atoms come close (distance = 3.545 Å) to each other, but this type binding is absent in complex 2 and thus the distance between the two Cu atoms is 6.706 Å which is too much larger than former.

Each tartrate anion bridges Cu atoms in bis-dentate fashion to form a 1D infinite chain. The chains are running in anti-parallel fashion which recognized each other to inter-digitated 1D + 1D structure via π⋯π stacking interactions (Fig. 4). The pyridyl rings N1–C1 → C5 [R(1)] and N2–C6 → C12 [R(2)] experiences π⋯π interactions with R(2) rings and the aryl C4 → C9 [R(3)] rings of the neighbouring 1D chain at both −1/2 + x, 3/2 − y, −z and 1/2 + x, 3/2 − y, −z positions. The distances between centroids are 4.321 and 4.134 Å and the dihedral angles are 4.1° for R(1)⋯R(2) and 3.632 and 3.578 Å and the dihedral angles are 1.7° for R(2)⋯R(3) interactions (Table 7). Similarly, the aryl ring R(3) faces π⋯π stacking interactions with the R(3) rings of the neighbouring phen moieties. The distances between centroids are 3.575 and 3.574 Å, respectively, and the dihedral angles between the rings are 2° for both interactions. These dimensions are ideal for moderately strong π⋯π interactions. π woven chains run parallel to a-axis such that the hydrophobic phen is buried and non-coordinating oxygen atoms protrude from two sides of the chain in a parallel fashion. These arrangement forms a hydrophilic channel along a-axis and 1D chain of water similar to channel symmetry is formed as discussed later.

Formation of water cluster and helices in the chiral complexes

The dynamic nature of hydrogen-bonding interactions and their fluctuations pose a major hurdle to evaluate the anomalous properties of bulk water in terms of its structure. This has led to an intense experimental and theoretical work focusing on unravelling structures of hydrogen-bonded water clusters.20 The past few years have seen an unprecedented number of papers reporting the occurrence of hydrogen-bonded water clusters as tetramers, pentamers, hexamers, octamers, decamers, etc., in crystalline organic, inorganic, or metal–organic hosts.21 In selected cases, extended water interactions containing 1D infinite water chains22 consisting of cyclic tetramers, with regularly ordered columnar structures with large 12- or 18-membered water rings,23 but 2D water sheets24 relatively scarce. However, the novelty of these water structures reported in several crystal hydrates still remain a puzzle as questioned by Mascal et al.25 in a recent article.

Complex 1

It is noteworthy that the 1D supramolecular chains are running parallel to a-axis and line up in such a way that all the non-coordinated oxygen atoms (O2 and O6) lie in ab-plane making a hydrophilic plane. This arrangement aids to form 2D water sheet. The O–H⋯O interactions between lattice water molecules in complex1 lead to the formation of cyclic tetramers (O3W, O4W, O5W, O9W) (Table 3). The average O⋯O distance in the tetramer is 2.75 Å. This distance is shorter than those observed in liquid water (2.85 Å) and comparable to the corresponding value in hexagonal ice (Ih) (2.76 Å). Moreover, the average O⋯O⋯O angle is nearly 90°, too much shorter than the corresponding value of 109.3° in Ih. Each tetrameric water cluster is linked to other six identical tetramers by hydrogen bonding (ESI, Fig. S1).† In contrast to the common “vertex-connected” and “edge-sharing” association mode, two of the six tetramers share a single water molecule (O6W) whereas other four tetramers share three water molecules (O6W, O2W, O8W) with the tetramer. Thus a repeating unit …Tetramer…O6w…Tetramer… runs along a-axis whereas …Tetramer… (O6W⋯O2W⋯O8W)…Tetramer… runs along c-axis. This aggregation mode leads to formation of a corrugated 2D sheet of water molecules in the ac-plane and the water sheets enclose a large rectangular holes with (H2O)14 topology (Fig. 5). The sheets stack along the b-axis with the stacking distance of nearly 12.4 Å. In so doing, channels are formed along b-axis with dimensions ∼5.79 × 10.68 Å taking into account van der Waals radii. The supramolecular [Cu2(Phen)2(tart)(H2O)]n chains are disposed in the space between two water sheets (ESI, Fig. S2).† The O4W atom of the sheet links to ligated O1W atom viaO7W atom. The planes of the water sheet and the blocker ligand are disposed at an angle of ∼90°. All the O atoms of the tartrateligand act as hydrogen bond acceptor to donor atoms of the sheet in such a way that the organization of 2D sheet and [Cu(tart)(H2O)] part of the complex generates a channel along a-axis. The blocker ligand phen interwoven by π⋯π interaction fills the channel. Consequently, the overall structure could be considered as a 3D metallosupramolecular network (ESI, Fig. S3).†

Interestingly, hydrogen bonding interactions among O2W, O4W, O6W and O7W water molecules lead to the formation supramolecular 1D helical chain along b-axis shown in Fig. 6.

Complex 2

In this complex, O1W, O2W, O3W and O4W molecules are connected with each other viahydrogen bonds (Table 6) into tetrameric water cluster. The average O⋯O distance in the tetramer is 2.81 Å. This distance is shorter than those observed in liquid water (2.85 Å) and comparable to the corresponding value in hexagonal ice (Ih) (2.76 Å). The O1w and O2w is a double hydrogen-bond donor and a single acceptor in a planar geometry, while the O3w and O4w molecules act as double hydrogen-bond acceptors and donors resulting in tetrahedron geometries. Each water tetramer is connected to other two identical tetramers below and above by hydrogen bonding. The inter-tetramer water–water connections have O⋯O distance of ∼2.750 Å. The aggregation of the tetramers is controlled by “vertex-connected” association mode, in contrast with previously reported water associations where clusters are always fused by “edge-sharing” or “vertex-sharing” mode. This novel cluster aggregation mode leads to formation of a 1D helical chain of water molecules along the a-axis (ESI, Fig. S4).†

Four neighbouring 1D polymeric coordination chains are π woven and run parallel to a axis such that the hydrophobic phen is buried and non-coordinating oxygen atoms (O3 and O6) protrude from two sides of the chain in a parallel fashion. These arrangement forms a hydrophilic channel along a axis and stabilizes the 1D helical chain of water. These channels are filled by 1D water chains, shown in Fig. 7. The 1D coordination chains are extended by π-interaction in the bc plane. Consequently, the overall structure could be considered as a 3D metallosupramolecular network (ESI, Fig. S5).†

It is also worth noting that the complex 2 contains three helices along three 21-crystallographic axes shown in Fig. 8. The 1D helical chain along the a axis described above. In the formation of 1D helical chain along b axis, only O5w water molecule connects the coordination framework and the helix follows the O4⋯O5w⋯O6 hydrogen bonding chain. In the formation of 1D helical chain along c axis, O1w and O2w water molecules connect the coordination framework and the helix follows the O1⋯O1w⋯O2w⋯O3 hydrogen bonding chain.

pH Dependent deprotonation alcoholic oxygen

During the reaction of disodiumtartrate, dihydrate, 1,10-phenanthroline with Cu(NO3)2,6H2O and Cu(ClO4)2,6H2O, respectively, the ligand disodiumtartrate showed totally different behaviour. In complex 1, the tartrateligand is fully deprotonated and the metal ion is coordinated through the entire deprotonated oxygen atom whereas in complex 2 the metal atom coordinated only through deprotonated alcoholic oxygen and deprotonated oxygen (CO) of carboxylate end and the protonated oxygen atom.

The pH measurement study reveals that complex 1 was formed at pH = 4.95 and the complex 2 was formed at pH = 4.10. If we look into the reaction condition, the complex 1 is formed in refluxing condition at about 85 °C where as the complex 2 is formed only through stirring of the reaction mixture at room temperature. In more drastic condition (for complex 1) all the hydrogen atoms are removed. At lower pH, the metal ion favored coordination to the ligand through deprotonated alcoholic oxygen atoms. In solution at pH = 4.10 medium disodiumtartrate is in equilibrium among several intermediates as shown below.

Among all the six intermediates, 1st and 4th forms are more symmetric and hence more stable. So, these two forms are more suitable for complex formation. Between these two forms, 1st intermediate is expected to form complex with metal ions generating a four membered ring while 4th intermediate is expected to form complex with metal ions with more stable five membered ring. Hence at low temperature 4th intermediate is more favorable for complex formation. Now, acids can form stronger hydrogen bonds than alcohols because their O–H bonds are more strongly polarized as Oδ−–Hδ+. During equilibrium condition the hydrogen atom moves to the carboxylic end (i.e. 4th intermediate) and then the hydrogen atom at carboxylic O3 atom form hydrogen bond with O2W and O4W water molecules and hydrogen atom at O6 form hydrogen bond with O5W. The stability of five membered ring coupled with strongly polarized Oδ−–Hδ+ on the carboxylic group favouring weak interactions help to deprotonate the alcoholic oxygen in place of carboxylic oxygen.

CD spectra

The results of CD measurements of complex 1 and 2 confirm the chiral nature of these two stereoisomers. As shown in Fig. 9 and Fig. 10, complex 1 shows two negative Cotton effects at 240 and 289 nm, respectively, while complex 2 show Cotton effects at 249 and 289 nm wavelengths, respectively. The small difference in the absorption peak may be due to difference in the conformation of the ligand within two complexes. Two peaks arise for the presence of two optically active centers. The ligand tartrate (disodium tartrate, dihydrate l(+) conformation) contains two chiral centers and due to its R,R-type of binding with the metal ions, the chirality transforms from the ligand to the metal–ligand complex.

Thermal analysis

The thermal analysis for coordination polymers and supramolecular compounds are very important due to their thermal stability. In TG curve of complex 1 shows that (ESI, Fig. S6)† mass loss started at 50 °C. Subsequently, a large mass loss took place at about 120 °C. It is clear from TG curve that the mass loss of complex 1 up to 130 °C is 15.84% (15.83% in theory) corresponds to seven lattice water molecules and at 180 °C one lattice water and one coordinated water molecules are released. While in complex 2, total mass loss of 14.10% (14.40% in theory) at 130 °C corresponds to four lattice water molecules and at 180 °C rest two lattice water molecules are released. The TG-DTA curve of complex 1 and 2 are shown in supplementary (ESI, Fig. S6 and S7).†

The dehydration pattern of both the complexes is consistent with DTAthermogram. Results of DTA analysis of the both complexes show a broad exothermic peak at 50 °C and a small endothermic peak at 120 °C. These exothermic and endothermic dip in the DTA curve correspond to the removal of the lattice water molecules in the crystal. The exothermic peak of complex 1 at 210 °C indicates the conversion of the crystals into the anhydrous form and partial decomposition of the molecule into CO2 whereas the same peak at same temperature of complex 2 corresponds to decomposition of the complex into carbon dioxide. The residual product is CuO in both cases.

Removal and reintroduction of guest solvates

Metal–organic coordination polymers with specific channels or cavities have attracted considerable interest because of their potential applications in catalysis, adsorption, and ion exchange. The thermal analysis of complex 1 showed that the complex lost solvated water molecules at 180 °C. The complex 1 was heated at 180 °C for three hours. The X-ray powder diffraction (XRPD) pattern of the complex after heating as usual was done. The XRPD data reveals that due to loss of the water molecules the crystal structure of the complex was destroyed and it become amorphous. After 24 h again X-ray powder diffraction was done and it reveals that it remains in amorphous form. The weight change of the compound was also studied. At first the complex has a weight 21.2 mg, after heating 17.0 mg and after 24 h it becomes 19.6 mg. Thus due to heating the weight loss is 4.2 mg and after 24 h 2.6 mg weight was recovered. The complex 2 was also heated at 180 °C for 3 h and XRPD pattern was taken as usual after heating and another XRPD pattern was taken after 24 h. These indicate that though the water molecules were removed from the crystal structure the crystalline nature was not lost like complex 1. For compound 2, similar weight change was also studied. At first the weight was 28.2 mg, after heating it became 22.4 mg and after 24 h 24.2 mg. Thus due to heating weight lost is 5.8 mg and after 24 h weight recovered 1.8 mg. All the XRPD patterns for complex 2 were shown in the Fig. 11.

The pattern indicates that after heating the 1st peak arises at higher angle (9.26°) in comparison to as synthesized (7.18°), which further shifted to 9.095° at 24 h after heating. Due to heating all water molecules are lost and the peak at 12.4° in as synthesized disappears in the pattern after heating. And 24 h after heat a new peak arises at 12.67° may be due to re-absorption of water.

Non-linear optical properties

Non-centrosymmetric MOFs have attracted much attention for their SHG non-linear optical activity.26 Due to their high thermal stability, neutral non-centrosymmetric frameworks could be considered as potential NLO-active materials. For evaluation of their NLO activity, we have performed second harmonic generation (SHG) measurements on microcrystalline powder samples of complex 1 and 2 using the method adopted by Kurtz and Perry.27 Complex 1 and 2 crystallize in non-centrosymmetric space groupsP21 and P212121, respectively. We find that both complexes are SHG-active and display powder SHG efficiency 0.8 and 0.9 times of KDP for complex 1 and 2, respectively. Thus complex 1 and 2 can be used as a NLO active metal–organic framework.

Photoluminescence

The emission spectra of both complexes were studied in solid state at room temperature. Both the two complexes show similar blue emission spectra at the same region although structures are different. The complex 1 shows emission maxima at ca. 436 nm and ca. 384 nm emission whereas complex 2 at ca. 437 nm and ca. 382 nm (Fig. 12 and Fig. 13). In complex 1, the fluorescence arises from π–π* transition between the two intramolecular aromatic π rings, while for complex 2 the π–π* transition occurs between two intermolecular aromatic π rings. But the distance between the two rings are nearly same for both complex (for complex 1 = ∼3.80 Å and for complex 2 = ∼3.57 Å), hence both two complexes have nearly same emission spectra. The complexes 1 and 2 may be used as an advanced material for a light emitting diode device owing to their strong short-wavelength emission.

Conclusion

In summary, reflux at 85 °C, Complex 1 {[Cu2(1,10-phen)2(tartrate)H2O]·8H2O}n having deprotonated –COO– and –O−group (pH = 4.95) crystallizes in P21 producing a helical supramolecular polymer. Following our design strategy to retain the alcoholic/carboxylic hydrogen, stirring at room temperature (pH = 4.10), complex 2 {[Cu2(1,10-phen)2(H2tartrate)H2O]·6H2O}n crystallizes in P212121 space group producing a 1D coordination polymer. From a comparison of complex 1 and 2 interesting changes in the supramolecular self-assembly are noticed. Thus, the self-assembly/disassembly of the O-bridged complex is rationally controlled by the protonation/deprotonation of the tartarte carboxylate group. Our studies indicate that l(+)-tartrate is a robust ligand for the formation of homo chiral metal–organic frameworks with helicity and it is promising to build up unusual architectures via a different reaction pH, thus opening up a new field in the preparation of chiral metal coordination polymers with potential NLO activity and luminescent properties. Our investigations also suggest that the higher nuclearity structures obtained from synthesis in water rely upon the heavily upon the hydrogen bonding of the guest H2O molecules for overall framework stability. The compact nature of tartrate renders CMOFs made solely from it limited in porosity, however the retention of chirality under the forcing conditions and the robustness of the resulting structures have implications for its use in more open chiral frameworks in combination with other spacer ligands. We are engaged now to design and synthesize open and helical CMOF.
