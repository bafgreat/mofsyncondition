Abstract

[Cu2(C6H4N3(C3H5))2(H2O)2SiF6]·2H2O (I) and [Cu2(C6H4N3(C3H5))2(CH3OH)2(H2O)2]SiF6 (II) π-compounds were obtained by alternating current electrochemical synthesis, starting from ethanol–methanol solution containing 1-allylbenzotriazole, H2SiF6, CuSiF6·4H2O and copper wire electrodes. Both compounds were X-ray structurally investigated. In complex I a mid-point of the CC bond of one 1-allylbenzotriazole (L) molecule, the nitrogen atom from another L moiety, the oxygen atom from a water molecule and the fluorine atom from a SiF62- anion form the trigonal-pyramidal environment of the CuI center. One more water molecule is fixed in the crystal space by strong O–H⋯F and O–H⋯O hydrogen bonds. The bridging function of the L, Cu+ and SiF62- units results in a formation of infinite metal-organic layers. The metal surrounding in II differs from that in I by a presence of oxygen atom from the methanol molecule instead of fluorine atom from the SiF62- anion. An appearance of ⋯SiF62-–CH3OH–Cu+–L–Cu+–H2O⋯ infinite layers in II instead of ⋯SiF62-–Cu+–L–Cu+⋯ in I leads to a noticeable elongation of the unit cell of II along [0 1 0] direction in comparison with I. The structure I represents the first known example of the Cu+–SiF62- bond.

Graphical abstract

I and II π-compounds were obtained by alternating current electrochemical synthesis. Compound I represents the first known example of the Cu+–SiF62- bond. Strongly related I and II compounds crystallize in the same P21/a space group with similar unit cells parameters.

Highlights

1. Introduction

π-Complexes of copper(I) hexafluorosilicate have not been widely investigated. The fact that no direct Cu–F contacts have been found earlier [1], [2], [3] indicates that the SiF62- moiety behaves as a weakly bonded anion, making the metal cation easily accessible to ligands. However, this anion demonstrates high affinity to water, resulting frequently in the involvement of water, or other solvent molecules in the crystal structure. To continue our study in this direction, two π-complexes of copper(I) hexafluorosilicate with N-allylbenzotriazolium hexafluorosilicate of [Cu2(C6H4N3(C3H5))2(H2O)2SiF6]·2H2O (I) and [Cu2(C6H4N3(C3H5))2(CH3OH)2(H2O)2]SiF6 (II) composition have been obtained and structurally investigated. Unexpectedly, a CuI–FSiF52- bond has been observed in the structure of compound I that to our knowledge represents the first known example of such interaction. These results are represented in the article.

2. Experimental section

2.1. Synthesis

2.1.1. N-Allylbenzotriazole

It was obtained by refluxing on an oil bath and magnetic stirring the equimolar mixture of benzotriazole, fresh-distilled allyl chloride and NaHCO3 in 95% ethanol during 10 days (with breaks for nights), total time of heating and mixing was about 120 h. A weight of a precipitated NaCl confirmed a practically quantitative yield of the product. After distilling the excess of ethanol concentrated solution of N-allylbenzotriazole (L) (presumably mixed 1-allyl and 2-allylbenzotriazoles [4]) was used.

2.1.2. Compound I

0.002 M of L in 1.5 ml of ethanol was titrated by 19% H2SiF6 to pH 3. To this solution 0.002 M CuSiF6·4H2O in 4 ml of CH3OH was added. After that copper-wire electrodes in cork were inserted and alternating current of 0.45 V (the initial current was 0.75 mA) was applied. After 23 days colourless extremely thin elongated plates of complex I appeared. Crystals are rather sensitive to a humid atmosphere.

2.1.3. Compound II

0.0015 M of the L in 2 ml of C2H5OH titrated by 19% H2SiF6 to pH 5 were mixed with 0.002 M of CuSiF6·4H2O in 4 ml of CH3OH. Single crystals of II were also obtained by alternating current technique [5] during 6 days. Although the crystals, appeared at the copper electrodes in a “hedgehog” manner in the shape of elongated prisms, were somewhat more stable compared with I, they decompose in humid atmosphere during 3 days.

2.2. Crystallography

Single-crystal data were collected on a Rigaku AFC7 diffractometer equipped with a Mercury CCD area detector, using graphite monochromatized Mo Kα radiation. Data were treated using the Rigaku CrystalClear software suite program package [6]. Both structures were solved by direct methods using SIR-92 [7] program (teXan crystallographic software package of Molecular Structure Corporation [8]) and refined on F2 with Shelxl-97 [9] software, implemented in program package WinGX [10]. Hydrogen atoms were included on idealized positions and refined with geometrical restrictions. The figures were prepared using Diamond 3.1 software [11]. 3. Results and discussion

In the structure of complex I the CuI atom demonstrates a unique surrounding. The basal plane of the metal coordination sphere involves a mid-point of the CC bond of the allyl group of one N-allylbenzotriazole molecule, the nitrogen atom from another ligand moiety and the oxygen atom from a water molecule. The fluorine atom from a SiF62- anion, located at the apical position, completes the copper coordination sphere to trigonal-pyramidal (Fig. 1). To our knowledge, it is a first known compound with the direct Cu+–SiF62- bond. The Cu–F bond length of 2.439(2) Å is noticeably longer than all other contacts in the copper environment (Table 2), but still much shorter than the sum of respective Van-der-Waal radii 2.87 Å [12]. The ligand moiety acts as a bridge, being bonded to one metal atom through the nitrogen atom of the triazole core, and to another one via the CC bond of the allyl group. The bridging role of L moiety, SiF62- anions and Cu+ cations results in the infinite (SiF62-–Cu+–L–Cu+⋯)n corrugated layers lying in (0 1 1) plane (Fig. 2). These layers, in turn, are interconnected via rather strong O–H⋯O hydrogen bonds (Table 4).

All six fluorine atoms in SiF62- unit act as bridges: two of them, located at trans-positions, are bound to two metal atoms, the other four form strong O–H⋯F (O⋯F 2.680(5) Å) hydrogen bonds. There are two crystallographically independent H2O molecules in structure I. One of them is bonded through the O atom with the metal center, and both hydrogen atoms are involved in O–H⋯F hydrogen bonds (H⋯F 1.94–2.15 Å). Another water molecule is fixed in the crystal space by two O–H⋯F and two O–H⋯O bonds only, resulting in slightly greater thermal motion parameters of the O1 atom than those of O2.

The CuI–(CC) interaction in I demonstrates a moderate effectiveness. The C–Cu–C angle value of 39.2(3)° is rather high and the Cu–m (m is a mid-point of the CC bond) distance of 1.920(5) Å appears to be moderately short. The CC bond coordinated to the copper atom is elongated to 1.368(6) Å, in contrast to 1.33 Å value in free olefins. The metal atom is slightly shifted (by 0.112(2) Å) from the base of the trigonal pyramid, as is typical for effective CuI–(CC) interaction.

In the structure of compound II the basal plane of the metal surrounding comprises, similarly to that in I, from the mid-point of the CC group of one L molecule, the nitrogen atom of benzotriazole core from another L unit and the water molecule. The oxygen atom from a methanol molecule is located at the apical position in the copper coordination polyhedron, thus “replacing” the fluorine atom. (Fig. 3). Such a difference noticeably affected on the shape of the metal surrounding. The CuI ion is removed from the basal plane of coordination polyhedron on 0.384(2) Å, the distance to the apical oxygen atom is shortened and is practically equal to those between copper and axial ligands (Table 3). The coordinated CC bond (Cu–m 1.935(5) Å, CC 1.366(6) Å, C–Cu–C 38.9(2)°) is slightly tilted relative to the basal plane of coordination polyhedron. All fluorine atoms from the SiF62- anion play bridging role. Two of them are bound to H(O) atoms from two methanol molecules, and four other ones are connected to hydrogen atoms from four water molecules. Likewise to I, bridging moieties (Cu+, SiF62-, L) form infinite layers lying in (0 1 1) plane (Fig. 4). Further association these layers into 3D structure carrying by non-valence interactions.

It is easy to explain a similarity in lattice parameters for both structures. In I and II a parameters arises from the distance between two SiF62- units connected via H2O molecule. Also the c parameter in both structures relates to the distance between two anions. The longest parameter b depends in the case of I on the length of ⋯SiF62-–Cu+–L–Cu+⋯ chains, which can be marked out from the above mentioned layers. Contrary to that respective chains in II have a formula SiF62-–CH3OH–Cu+–L–Cu+–H2O⋯ resulting in a noticeable unit cell elongation in comparison with I.

An absence of the direct Cu–F bonding in earlier studied Cu2SiF6 π-complexes [Cu2(C3H5)2NCN(H2O)3CH3OH][SIF6] [1], [CuOOCH(C3H5NH3)]2SiF6 [2], [Cu2Cl3(C3H5NH3)2]2SiF6 [2], [Cu(H2O)2.5{μ-C4H8N2(H)2(C3H5)2}0.5]SiF6·H2O [3], [Cu2(μ-atu)2(atu)4]SiF6 [13] (atu – allylthiourea) and [Cu(C4H8ONH(C3H5))(H2O)2]SiF6·H2O [14] was attributed to the soft-hard Lewis acids and bases concept. The soft Lewis acid Cu+ preferably coordinates oxygen atoms from water molecules, whereas the hard base SiF62- is preferentially bonded with harder than Cu+ Lewis acids, i.e. hydrogen atoms. The structure of I appears to be an exception from the general tendency. One may note two additional points. At first, the weakness of Cu–F bond reveals in a regular trigonal-pyramidal shape of the copper surrounding with markedly removed apical fluorine atom. One more point is that the small change of the acidity of the reaction mixture appears to be enough to allow the SiF62- anion interacts with the hydroxyl-group of the methanol molecule instead of CuI center. All these facts confirm the weakness of the CuI–FSiF52- bond and emphasize that the existence of compound I is a very unusual phenomenon.
