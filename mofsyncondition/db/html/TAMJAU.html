Porous coordination polymers (PCPs) that exhibit a degree of flexibility or dynamic behaviour have been extensively studied in the last decade.1,2 The dynamic properties of these materials make them useful for applications which are not easily accessible by rigid frameworks.1b,2g In this regard, the ability of these soft materials to optimise their pore size in accordance with the type of guest molecule, whilst retaining high regularity, is an advantageous feature. This can result in high selectivity or gated adsorption/desorption behaviour for guests.1,2f Examples of dynamic behaviour include drastic solid-state expansion/contraction (swelling),2a,3,4 as well as reversible and non-reversible crystal-to-crystal transformations.5 These dynamic properties of PCPs have been studied in the context of gas storage and separation, where crystal-to-crystal transformations can be utilised to reversibly enhance or diminish a PCPs capacity for gas uptake.1 The ability to alter the size of pore apertures or channels is also relevant to supramolecular catalysis in PCPs.1 New materials are sought to extend the types of dynamic behaviour that could be utilised for these applications.

Coordination polymers employing silver(i) as a node represent a vibrant research area6–9 where the silver(i) is utilised for its variable coordination number, flexible geometry and properties.6,10 While coordination polymers of pyrazine and 2,2′-dipyrazine with silver(i) are well known,11 the coordination chemistry of di-pyrazinyl compounds separated by a spacer12 has only recently begun to be explored. Di-2-pyrazinylmethane (dpzm)13 is novel in this class of compounds in that the spacer is not able to engage in further coordination of a metal centre. Silver(i) coordination polymers are known for their ability to exchange anions which often results in network transformations.8,11 However, there are limited examples7b of porous materials based on silver(i) that display marked, reversible dynamic behaviour based on interactions with guest molecules. In fact, most silver(i) coordination polymers have non-porous structures or are not robust enough for further study and manipulation.6

Herein, we report the differential dynamic behaviour of a 3-D PCP (1) formed from dpzm and AgClO4. The as-synthesised PCP (1asas) has a flexible structure with a sodalite topology, which can facilitate either guest-induced expansion and contraction in the solid-state, or a transformation of 1 to a 2-D framework upon complete desolvation. To our best knowledge, this work represents an unprecedented dynamic behaviour in the context of 3D silver(i) coordination polymers, and the very first example of a silver(i) PCP that displays solid-state breathing.

Large crystals of {[Ag(dpzm)]ClO4·1.1(DMSO)·0.9(EtOAc)}∞ (1asas, Fig. 1) were grown by slow vapour diffusion of ethyl acetate into a dimethyl sulfoxide solution containing a 1 : 1 mixture of AgClO4 and dpzm.†X-ray crystallography revealed the 3-D PCP adopts a 4264sodalite topology, containing 1-D channels directed along the c-axis with a honeycomb-like arrangement (Fig. 1). The apertures of the microporous channels are formed from six silver(i) centres arranged in a chair conformation spanning ∼14.3 Å in diameter. The distorted rectangular windows in the walls of the channels are occupied by anions and ordered guest molecules.

The asymmetric unit of 1asas contains a distorted trigonal bipyramidal five-coordinate silver(i) centre chelated by one molecule of dpzm. The silver is in turn coordinated to a pyrazinenitrogen donor from two additional symmetry-related molecules of dpzm. One weakly coordinated DMSO molecule completes the coordination environment of 1asas.

Solution-state 1H NMR spectroscopy of dissolved crystals confirms the presence of 1.1 molecules of DMSO and ∼0.9 equivalents of ethyl acetate solvate in 1asas. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) of 1asas also supports this, showing an initial mass loss (3.9%) of volatile EtOAc solvate molecules located in the channels, followed by a plateau and then a sharp two-step loss of 19.1% (18.5% calc. for 1.1 DMSO). Thereafter no mass is lost until the experiment was stopped at 280 °C.

Solvent exchange, by submersing crystals of 1asas in dichloromethane, resulted in a crystal-to-crystal transformation to give 1dcmdcm (Fig. 2) that is complete within 4-5 min.‡ While there was some cracking and disintegration of the very large crystals (dimensions of 2–3 mm), they maintain their crystallinity. X-ray crystallography revealed that the sodalite structure was preserved, however the unit cell volume had decreased dramatically; from 10155.0(13) Å3 (1asas) to 8296(1) Å3 (1dcmdcm).† This unit cell volume decrease is due to a crystal-to-crystal contraction of the 3-D PCP along the c-axis, corresponding to a decrease of 3.09 Å, or 18% of the unit cell volume, while the b and c axes increase by ∼0.4 Å. During this process, the guests in the microporous channels (ethyl acetate/DMSO) and the walls of the channels (DMSO) are exchanged for CH2Cl2. This was confirmed for the bulk sample by analysis of digested crystals using solution-state 1H NMR spectroscopy. The reduction in cell volume is driven by replacement of the coordinated DMSO ligand with a CH2Cl2 solvate molecule in the sodalite structure.

The contracted sodalite structure, 1dcmdcm, could be expanded by a soaking the same batch of crystals in acetone for 2 days to give 1aceace. X-ray crystallography showed an increase in the unit cell volume to 9815(20) Å3, which corresponds closely to the acetone solvated structure obtained directly from 1asas.† In this transformation the dichloromethane in the 1-D channels is completely exchanged for acetone, and notably, the disordered dichloromethane solvates located in the walls of 1dcmdcm are replaced by a fully occupied and weakly coordinated acetone molecule, regenerating a five-coordinate silver centre. Again, this crystal-to-crystal transformation‡ is accompanied by an elongation along the c axis of 2.23 Å, amounting to a 18.3% volume increase from 1dcmdcm. In addition to studies of the expanded material by solution 1H NMR spectroscopy, FT-IR also confirmed solvent exchange. Moreover, the reversibility of shrinking and expanding was demonstrated over another cycle (1dcm(2)dcm(2), 1ace(2)ace(2)), which was followed by 1H NMR spectra of digested samples and unit-cell determinations.† The smaller unit cell volume for 1dcmdcm is a consequence of the reduced steric requirement of having only one DCM guest molecule in each distorted rectangular window of the wall structure (and a four coordinate silver(i) centre). In contrast, introducing a coordinating guest (1aceace) results in a five coordinate silver(i) centre equating to two solvate molecules in each window (Fig. 2) and correspondingly a larger unit cell volume.

Crystals of 1asas are not stable to solvent loss and upon standing at room temperature in paratone-N oil undergo a visual single crystal to multiple crystal transformation over a period of 1–2 weeks.‡ These stable crystals were suitable for single crystal X-ray crystallography which revealed the transformed sample to be a non-porous 2-D coordination polymer (2, Fig. 3) that can also be obtained directly from silver perchlorate and dpzm.† This 2-D polymer contains no coordinated DMSO molecules, which indicates that loss of the solvent plays an integral part in the transformation process. Differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) supports this assertion; when all DMSO is lost from 1asas a pronounced exothermic peak is observed (184 °C), and afterwards there is no weight loss until decomposition (320 °C). The stability of the resulting material after solvent loss supports a close-packed structure. Both solvent-exchanged materials 1dcmdcm and 1aceace also exhibit an exothermic peak (143 °C and 193 °C respectively) in the DSC trace, and after this point, no further weight loss. Notably, neither of the solvent exchanged materials displayed the structural transformation to 2 in N-paratone.

Examination of the three structures using the Accelrys Materials Studio14 indicates that when the solvent molecules are removed from 1asas, the resulting free volume is the largest of all three forms of 1 (free volume 1606 Å3 per unit cell when using a 1.84 Å probe). This compares to free volumes of 754 Å3 and 1269 Å3 for 1dcmdcm and 1aceace respectively, when the guest molecules are removed. The free volume available in 1asas appears to be enough to facilitate a facile 3-D to 2-D transformation, while 1dcmdcm and 1aceace are too compact to undergo this process at room temperature.

In summary we have, for the first time, demonstrated a reversible guest-induced breathing behaviour for a silver(i) PCP. This is driven by the coordinating nature of the solvent guest and its attendant steric bulk. The crystal-to-crystal solvent exchange occurs in a facile manner whilst retaining structural regularity, however upon desolvation we observed a 3-D to 2-D structural transformation whereby 1asas is converted to 2. This observation provides insight as to why permanent porosity may be a challenge to achieve for 1, although we are continuing to explore combinations of guests and anions (e.g.carborane anions) that may stabilise this structure. We anticipate this material may be applicable to the separation of particular solvents or gases, principally those capable of coordinating silver(i), from a mixture of compounds.
