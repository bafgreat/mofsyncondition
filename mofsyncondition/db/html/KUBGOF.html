A new mixed N-/O-donor for constructing functional CuII-coordination polymer: Crystal structure, photocatalysis and magnetic properties

Abstract

A first transition–metal coordination polymer [CuH2L(H2O)]n (1) based on a new mixed N-/O-donors 5-[2-carboxy-3-(1H-tetrazol-5-yl)-phenoxy]-isophthalic acid (H4L), which exhibits a 2D 3-connected polymeric network, has been presented and characterized by single-crystal X-ray diffraction, elemental analysis, IR and TGA. It has prominently displayed highly photocatalytic degradation activity for dye degradation as well as antiferromagnetic interactions between the adjacent CuII ions.

Graphical abstract

A first 2D 3-connected copper(II) coordination polymer [CuH2L(H2O)]n (1) based on a new mixed N-/O-donors has been presented. It exhibits highly photocatalytic degradation activity for dye degradation as well as antiferromagnetic interactions between the adjacent CuII ions.

Coordination polymers (CPs) and the related metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) are constructed using a modular approach, from organic links and metal ion units, controlling both their architecture and chemical functionality. These frameworks have received great scientific attention during the last decade, not only because of their intriguing varieties of architectures and topologies, but also due to their potential CP's applications in carbon dioxide capture, gas storage, separation and catalysis [1], [2], [3], [4], [5], [6], [7]. The design and use of new organic ligands as a basic and most important synthetic strategy, which can add flexibility and diversity to the chemical structures and functions of these materials, have permitted the successful assembly of functional CPs [8]. With the development of functional CPs, diverse N- or O-donor ligands have been designed [8], [9]. However, sometimes just one ligand containing only one kind of N- or O-donor cannot realize the specific functionality of CPs. Clearly, if the multiple N-donor-only and O-donor-only ligands are mixed, the final structure will be hard to predict. Alternatively, it is possible to access sophisticated functionalized CPs with a designed ligand containing N- and O-donors simultaneously. Potentially, the study of this area will help us to establish the correlations between the ligands–structures–functional properties of CPs and to synthesize target materials with desired structures and properties.

In this context, more and more multi-ligand CPs and mixed donor ligand CPs have been used to construct functional CPs, which have well been reviewed recently [9]. Compared with multi-ligand CPs, those based on a single ligand with mixed N-/O-donors are less abundant [9]. In our previous work, a series of CPs based on the aromatic polycarboxylic acid and secondary N-donor co-ligands have been incorporated in order to explore the combination of significant structural diversification with the different functional properties produced [10], [11], [12]. Also, we utilized N-heterocyclic dicarboxylate ligands, as building blocks to investigate the constructions of CPs, which show unique topological motifs and interesting properties [13], [14]. As an extension of our work, an undeveloped 5-[2-carboxy-3-(1H-tetrazol-5-yl)-phenoxy]-isophthalic acid (H4L) linker, containing one tetrazolium and three carboxyl groups, is employed to build the functional CPs. Herein, we report a novel 2D transition–metal compound [CuH2L(H2O)]n (1) based on H4L, which shows a 3-connected hcb net, high photocatalytic activity for dye degradation and antiferromagnetic interactions.

All chemicals were purchased commercially and used without further purification. Under hydrothermal conditions, the reaction of H4L with CuCl2·2H2O at 140 °C for 3 days yields the title compound 1 [15]. Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis [16] reveals that compound 1 crystallizes in the orthorhombic system with space group Pca21, and the independent asymmetric unit of 1 consists of only one CuII ion, one H2L2 − linker, and one coordinated water molecule (see Fig. 1a). The Cu1 center is five-coordinated by two nitrogen atom (N1, N4B) and three oxygen atoms (O1, O7A) from three H2L2 − linkers and one oxygen atom (O8) from water, respectively, displaying distorted tetragonal pyramidal geometry. The Cu–O and Cu–N bond lengths vary from 1.970(3) to 2.310(4) Å, and the O–Cu–O, O–Cu–N and N–Cu–N are in the range of 83.74(15)–173.15(16)° (see Table S1).

In 1, each crystallographically independent H2L2 − linker adopts μ3-η1O7:η1N1:η1N4 coordination mode to bridge three Cu(II) centers into a 2D layer (see Fig. 1b), in which both H2L2 − and Cu1 serve as 3-connected nodes. Thus, the structure of 1 can be defined as a 3-connected hcb net (see Fig. 1c). Adjacent 2D layers are packed through the O–H⋯O hydrogen bonds whose distances are in the range of 2.65–2.81 Å (see Table S2), involving the aqua oxygen O8, the carboxyl O7 and O2 of same layer H-bond to the carboxyl O6, O4 and O5 of an adjacent 2D layer [O2–H⋯O5, 2.808(6), 144(5)°; O7–H⋯O4, 2.656(4), 158°; O8–H⋯O6, 2.733(3) 151(6)°], resulting in an extended 3D supramolecular network (see Fig. 1d).

To confirm that the crystal structures are truly representative of the bulk materials, powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) experiments were carried out for compound 1. As shown in Fig. 2a, the PXRD measured and simulated (black line) patterns of compound 1 show that the synthesized bulk material is the same as the single crystal. TGA analysis of 1 (see Fig. S1) demonstrates a curve gradient phase about the weight loss of 4.6% in the temperature range of 25–310 °C corresponding to the loss of one coordinated water molecule per formula unit (Calcd. 4.0%). After that, a sharp weight loss of 77.4% rapidly occurs from 310 to 500 °C where the H2L2 − anion is destroyed (Calcd. 78.3%). The percentage of residual weight obtained from the graph (18.0%) is in well agreement with the calculated value of 17.7% indicating that the final product obtained is copper oxide (CuO).

The band-gap size of compound 1 was investigated by the UV–vis diffuse reflection measurement method at room temperature. The results give an Eg (band gap energy) value of 2.95 eV (see Fig. S2), and it has an absorption response to the visible light due to its narrow band-gap size. Additionally, we investigated the photocatalytic performances of compound 1 for the photodegradations of rhodamine B (RhB), which is a model for dye contamination in aqueous media to evaluate the photocatalytic effectiveness under xenon arc lamp illumination. The experiments were carried out in typical fashion [17] and the photocatalytic activity of 1 is shown in Fig. 2b and c. At first, for the degradation of RhB, the characteristic absorption of RhB at approximately 550 nm was selected to monitor the adsorption and photocatalytic degradation process. The photocatalytic activity of 1 was gradually enhanced with time increased to 120 min. When the light application time increases to 120 min, the degradation ratio of RhB reaches nearly 96%. Meanwhile, the formed hydroxyl radicals (OH) on the surface of visible-illuminated compound 1 could cleave H2O2 effectively to complete the photocatalytic process, and the proposed photocatalytic degradation mechanism was very similar to those of the previously reported CPs [17], [18]. In order to investigate the stability of compound 1 as visible-light photocatalyst, we repeated the photocatalytic degradation of RhB three times (Fig. S3). Interestingly, it maintains similar photocatalytic efficiency. In addition, powder residues of 1 were obtained by filtration after photocatalytic reaction, and the PXRD patterns of each powder were basically identical to those of the parent compounds, indicating that compound 1 is stable during photocatalysis (Fig. 2a). Compared with other N, O containing complexes [19], 1 has exhibited a relatively high photocatalytic degradation activity for the photodegradations of RhB.

The temperature-dependent molar magnetic susceptibility of compound 1 was measured using a collection of some single crystals in an applied field of 1000 Oe from 300 to 2 K as shown in Fig. 3. At room temperature, χMT value for 1 is 0.35 cm3 kmol− 1, which is a little smaller than the expected value (0.375 cm3 kmol− 1) for an isolated S = 1/2 CuII ion [20]. As the temperature is lowered, the χMT value decreases monotonically to reach a minimum of 0.029 cm3 kmol− 1 at 2 K. The magnetic susceptibility data (1/χM value) in the range of 50–300 K can be fitted well to the Curie–Weiss law [χ = C/(T − θ)], with a Curie constant C = 0.41 cm3 kmol− 1 and a Weiss constant θ = − 50.59 K. The negative θ value demonstrates the presence of an antiferromagnetic interaction between the adjacent CuII ions mediated through the carboxylate and tetrazolium bridges; which is comparable to those reported for other similar Cu(II) compounds [21].

In summary, the first transition–metal coordination polymer based on 5-[2-carboxy-3-(1H-tetrazol-5-yl)-phenoxy]-isophthalic acid (H4L) and the Cu(II) ion has been hydrothermally prepared. It exhibits a 2D 3-connected hcb net, high photocatalytic degradation activity for organic dye, and antiferromagnetic interactions of the copper (II) ions. Following this line of research, additional efforts on the H4L-based coordination frameworks with other transition metals are currently underway in our lab, for developing new crystalline materials with desirable structures and properties.

    A new Cu(II) coordination polymer, namely, [Cu(NPT)2(NO2-BDC)]n (1) (NO2-H2BDC = 5-nitro-1,3-benzenedicarboxylic acid, NPT = 4-(4-nitrophenyl)-1,2,4-triazole) has been synthesized under hydrothermal condition and characterized by elemental analysis, and single-crystal X-ray diffraction. Single-crystal X-ray diffraction study reveals that complex 1 features one-dimensional chain structure. The magnetic studies reveal that the antiferromagnetic interactions exist between the adjacent CuII ions. Moreover, complex 1 displays highly photocatalytic degradation activity for the degradation of rhodamine B, methylene blue and methyl orange.
