Formation of cobalt(II)–piperazine supramolecular systems under different organic acid mediums: synthesis, characterization and crystal structures

Author links open overlay panel
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.molstruc.2004.01.025Get rights and content

Abstract

The reactions of CoCl2 salt and piperazine (L) in the presence of different organic acid mediums afford a series of cobalt(II) complexes, the solid structures (determined by X-ray diffraction techniques) of which are controlled by the nature of the specific acid. When terephthalic acid was used, a novel mononuclear molecule [Co(HL)2Cl3]·Cl·(H2O) (1) was obtained [orthorhombic, Pnma, a=24.604(16) Å, b=9.882(7) Å, c=6.609(4) Å, Z=4], in which the CoII center takes the unusual compressed trigonal-bipyramidal coordination geometry (CoN2Cl3) and multiple hydrogen-bonding interactions extend this structure into a three-dimensional supramolecular network. However, with the replacement of terephthalic acid with malonic acid in the above reaction, a one-dimensional coordination polymer [CoLCl2]n (2) was obtained [monoclinic, P21/n, a=6.363(3) Å, b=10.244(5) Å, c=12.124(5) Å, β=103.560(8)°, Z=4], in which the tetrahedral CoII centers (CoCl2N2) are linked by the bidentate-coordinated piperazine molecules to form a zigzag chain array and these coordination chains are further expanded to a three-dimensional hydrogen-bonding architecture. In addition, a mononuclear complex [Co(gly)3]·(H2O) (3) [monoclinic, P21/c, a=6.261(5) Å, b=14.280(9) Å, c=12.182(8) Å, β=101.450(14)°, Z=4] was yielded when glycine was used in the similar procedure. A three-dimensional framework is also observed through the hydrogen-bonding interactions between [Co(gly)3] moieties, in which the guest water molecules are included. These results unequivocally indicate that the nature of the organic acid templates play the key role in formation of these complexes.

1. Introduction

Self-assembly processes involving metal ions and organic ligands directed by either metal coordination or hydrogen bonds have attracted much attention in the field of supramolecular chemistry and crystal engineering from the viewpoints of the development of novel functional materials with desired properties [1], [2], [3], [4], [5]. Although the general and precise principles for controlling the solid structures of the target products are still needed to be classified and established, many rational synthetic strategies have been brought forward and proved significant in the approach of the design of the metal-based coordination supramolecules. The selection of proper ligands as ‘building blocks’, such as the most common 4,4′-bipyridyl-based ligands, is undoubtedly a key point in manipulating the network structures [6], and other factors such as metal ion, counter anion, solvent, metal/ligand ratio and even pH value have also been found to highly influence the structural topologies of such coordination frameworks [7].

Recently, a series interesting coordination supramolecules, [{M(BTEC)(H2O)4}]·{H2L}·4H2O]n (M=CoII, NiII and ZnII; BTEC=1,2,4,5-benzenetetracarboxylate), were reported by Murugavel et al., [8], which exhibit the anionic metal-organic and cationic organic layer alternated architecture. In these compounds, the metal centers coordinate to the BTEC molecules, and the protonated piperazine moieties act as the cation templates in the formation of the final products. Additionally, Tran Qui and Palacios have reported the crystal structure of [H2L]·[CoCl4]·4H2O prepared by CoCl2 and L in the presence of HCl [9], which consists of a bis-protonated H2L cation, CoCl4 mononuclear anionic unit and solvents, and a hydrogen-bonding three-dimensional supramolecular network is observed. To further develop such metal-based supramolecular systems, we select terephthalic acid, malonic acid or glycine (see Chart 1) instead of BTEC or HCl to assemble with CoII salt and piperazine. In each case, other reacting conditions are kept constant, and the only varied parameter during these processes is the organic acid medium. Our results indicate that the nature of the organic acid templates play the key role in formation of the complexes [Co(HL)2Cl3]·Cl·(H2O) (1), [CoLCl2]n (2) and [Co(gly)3]·(H2O) (3) with different supramolecular architectures, as described in the present paper.

2. Experimental

2.1. Materials and general methods

All reagents for synthesis and analysis were obtained commercially with analytical grade and used as received. FT-IR spectra (KBr pellets) were taken on a FT-IR 170SX (Nicolet) spectrometer. Carbon, hydrogen and nitrogen analyses were performed on a Perkin–Elmer 240C analyzer.

2.2. Synthesis of complexes 1–3

2.2.1. [Co(HL)2Cl3]·Cl·(H2O) (1)

A dilute aqueous solution of CoCl2 (87 mg, 0.2 mmol) was added to a solution of terephthalic acid (33 mg, 0.4 mmol) in DMF (20 ml). The mixture was heated to 80 °C under stirring for ca. 30 min to obtain a light purple solution. Then, piperazine hexahydrate (116 mg, 0.6 mmol) in water (10 ml) was added slowly to the above hot solution with further stirring for 30 min, and the resultant purple solution was filtered after cooling to room temperature. Prism purple singe-crystals suitable for X-ray diffraction were obtained by slow evaporation of the solvents within two weeks in 40% yield. Found: C, 24.3; H, 6.1; N, 14.4%. Calc. for C8H24Cl4CoN4O: C, 24.5; H, 6.2; N, 14.3%. FT-IR (KBr pellet, cm−1): 3485b, 3238s, 3212m, 3114m, 3005w, 2982w, 2954w, 2816m, 2793m, 2723m, 2643m, 2486w, 2315w, 2192w, 1959w, 1614vs, 1595vs, 1490w, 1450vs, 1434s, 1377s, 1315w, 1228w, 1205m, 1100s, 1087m, 1068m, 1007vs, 953w, 871s, 816m, 738m, 600w, 572w, 478m.

2.2.2. [CoLCl2]n (2)

The same synthetic procedure as for 1 was used except that terephthalic acid was replaced by malonic acid, resulting in prism violet single-crystals suitable for X-ray diffraction in 65% yield. Found: C, 22.4; H, 4.8; N, 13.4%. Calc. for C4H10Cl2CoN2: C, 22.2; H, 4.7; N, 13.0%. FT-IR (KBr pellet, cm−1): 3247m, 3031m, 2872w, 2805w, 2762w, 2663w, 2464w, 1685m, 1636vs, 1581vs, 1490m, 1431s, 1397s, 1369s, 1342w, 1269s, 1161w, 1122w, 1087m, 982m, 956s, 866m, 774s, 734s, 677w, 582m, 488w.

2.2.3. [Co(gly)3]·(H2O) (3)

The same synthetic procedure as for 1 was used except that terephthalic acid was replaced by glycine, giving block pink single-crystals suitable for X-ray diffraction in 50% yield. Found: C, 23.7; H, 4.3; N, 14.3%. Calc. for C6H14CoN3O7: C, 24.1; H, 4.7; N, 14.1%. 2.3. Crystallographic data collection and structure determination

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction study of complexes 1–3 were performed on a BRUKER SMART 1000 CCD diffractometer equipped with a graphite crystal monochromator situated in the incident beam for data collection. The determination of unit cell parameters and data collections were performed with Mo Kα radiation (λ=0.71073 Å) by ϖ scan mode in the range of 2.64<θ<25.68° (for 1), 2.63<θ<25.64° (for 2) and 3.32<θ<20.35° (for 3) at 293(2) K. All data were corrected by semi-empirical method using SADABS program. The program SAINT [10] was used for integration of the diffraction profiles.

All structures were solved by direct methods using SHELXS program of the SHELXTL-97 package and refined with SHELXL [11]. CoII centers were located from the E-maps and other non-hydrogen atoms were located in successive difference Fourier syntheses. The final refinements were performed by full matrix least-squares methods with anisotropic thermal parameters for non-hydrogen atoms on F2. All the hydrogen atoms were first found in difference electron density maps, and then placed in the calculated sites and included in the final refinement in the riding model approximation with displacement parameters derived from the parent atoms to which they were bonded. The refinements converged to final R=0.0385, wR=0.0799, S=1.045, (Δ/σ)max=0.002, (Δ/σ)min=0.000 (for 1); R=0.0420, wR=0.0791, S=1.027, (Δ/σ)max=(Δ/σ)min=0.000 (for 2); and R=0.0575, wR=0.0907, S=0.991, (Δ/σ)max=(Δ/σ)min=0.000 (for 3). The ratios of minimum to maximum apparent transmission are 0.7305 (for 1), 0.7278 (for 2) and 0.7843 (for 3), respectively. Molecular graphics were drawn with the program package XP. Further crystallographic data and experimental details for structural analyses of the three complexes are summarized in Table 1, and selected bond lengths and angles with their estimated standard deviations for complexes 1–3 in Table 2, Table 3, Table 4, respectively.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Description of the crystal structures of complexes 1–3

3.1.1. [Co(HL)2Cl3]·Cl·(H2O) (1)

The crystal structure of 1 consists of a discrete mononuclear [Co(HL)2Cl3]+ cation with a symmetry mirror through Co(1)–N(1)–N(3)–Cl(1) plane, a chloride anion and a lattice water molecule. The ORTEP view of the coordination cation in complex 1 with atom labeling is shown in Fig. 1a. The coordination geometry around the CoII center could be described as a compressed trigonal-bipyramidal generated by N(1)–N(3)–Cl(1)–Cl(2)–Cl(2A) with τ value of 0.88, which is used to describe the degree of distortion for penta-coordinated complexes (τ=1 for an ideal trigonal-bipyramid and τ=0 for an ideal square-pyramid) [12], [13]. Two nitrogen atoms of distinct piperazine rings act as the axis with the mean Co–N distance of 2.195(5) Å, and three chloride anions are composed of the basal plane with the mean Co–N distance of 2.320(2) Å (CoII ion is deviated from this mean coordination plane by only 0.0078 Å). Two protonated piperazine rings are in normal chair conformation, which are both slightly distorted from ideal (the ring torsion angles lying in the range of 51.8(6)–59.9(6)°), and take the monodendate coordination mode with the central CoII ion due to the protonation of one nitrogen donor. The average C–C and C–N bond lengths of the piperazine rings are 1.507(6) and 1.472(5) Å, respectively.

Analysis of the crystal packing of complex 1 in the unit cell shows that there exist multiple hydrogen-bonding interactions, resulting in a three-dimensional supramolecular network. As shown in Fig. 1b, a pair of N(2)–H(2B)–Cl(2)i,ii (i=x,y,z−1; ii=x,−y+1/2,z−1) hydrogen bonds, together with the N(3)–H(3C)–Cl(1)iii (iii=x,y,z+1), between one protonated piperazine ring and the adjacent coordinated chloride anion link the [Co(HL)2Cl3]+ cations to form a one-dimensional chain motif along [001] direction. Meantime, the uncoordinated Cl(3) anion acts as a three-connected node through N(1)–H(1C)–Cl(3)iv (iv=−x+1/2,−y+1,z+1/2), N(2)–H(2A)–Cl(3)v (v=−x+1/2,−y+1,z−1/2) and N(4)–H(4B)–Cl(3)vi (vi=−x,−y+1,−z+2) interactions, linked the adjacent chains to form a two-dimensional layered architecture along (101) plane (Fig. 1b). In addition, the lattice water molecules in each layer form intra-layered N(4)–H(4A)–O(1)vii (vii=−x,−y+1,−z+1) bonds with the adjacent piperazine rings, and further expand these layers through [010] direction to form a three-dimensional framework via two types inter-layered O(1)–H(1)–Cl(2)ii and O(1)–H(2)–Cl(2)viii (viii=x,y+1,z−1) interactions. For all the hydrogen bonds stated above, the N⋯Cl separations are in 3.056–3.402 Å range, the N⋯O and O⋯Cl separations are 2.739 and 3.126 Å, respectively; and the smallest bond angle is 135.7°, falling into the normal range of such weak hydrogen-bonding interactions [14].

3.1.2. [CoLCl2]n (2)

X-ray single crystal structure determination reveals that complex 2 is a neutral one-dimensional infinite zigzag coordination chain as shown in Fig. 2a. Each CoII center coordinates to two N donors of distinct piperazine rings and two chloride anions, forming a tetrahedral coordination sphere. The CoCl2 moiety is preserved within this complex: Co(1)–Cl(1) and Co(1)–Cl(2) bond distances are 2.243(2) and 2.242(2) Å, respectively, accompanied by a Cl(1)–Co(1)–Cl(2) angle of 105.38(6)°. The piperazine rings in this structure also take the usual chair conformation, which are both slightly distorted from ideal (the ring torsion angles lying in the range of 52.5(5)–55.5(6)°), bridging the CoII centers via two nitrogen donors to form a one-dimensional zigzag coordination polymer along [001] direction. The average C–C and C–N bond lengths of the piperazine rings are 1.510(6) and 1.481(5) Å, respectively, agreeing with the values in 1. In this 1:1 ligand/metal polymeric chain, the neighboring Co(1)⋯Co(1A) separation (bridged by piperazine ring) is 6.862 Å and the Co(1)⋯Co(1B) distance is 12.124 Å. In addition, the shortest Co⋯Co (symmetry codes: −x+1/2, y−1/2, −z−1/2; −x+1/2, y+1/2, −z−1/2) distance between the adjacent chains is only 5.425 Å.

Analysis of the crystal packing of 2 in the unit cell indicates that four independent inter-chain N–H⋯Cl hydrogen bonds link these one-dimensional coordination chains to result in a three-dimensional architecture as depicted in Fig. 2b. The N(1)–H(1C) group in one piperazine ring of each coordination chain forms two N–H⋯Cl hydrogen bonds with chloride anions of two adjacent distinct coordination chains: N(1)–H(1C)⋯Cl(2)i (i=x−1,y,z) and N(1)–H(1C)⋯Cl(1)ii (ii=−x−1/2, y−1/2, −z+3/2). Meantime, the N(2)–H(2C) group in the other piperazine ring of each coordination chain also forms two N–H⋯Cl hydrogen bonds, however, with chloride anions of one adjacent coordination chain: N(2)–H(2C)⋯Cl(2)iii and N(2)–H(2C)⋯Cl(1)iii (iii=−x+1/2,y−1/2,−z+3/2). All the N⋯Cl separations are in 3.436–3.627 Å range with H⋯Cl separations in 2.701–2.939 Å region, respectively; and the smallest bond angle is 133.5°, falling into the normal range of such weak hydrogen-bonding interactions [14].

3.1.3. [Co(gly)3]·(H2O) (3)

The crystal structure of compound 3 consists of one isolated neutral [Co(gly)3] unit and a lattice water molecule. Two crystallography isomers (monoclinic, P21/n, a=13.860(3) Å, b=13.153(3) Å, c=13.996(3) Å, β=112.88(1)°, Z=8; orthorhombic, Pna21, a=15.331(2) Å, b=13.205(2) Å, c=11.606(2) Å, Z=8) of a very similar compound [Co(gly)3]·(H2O)2 were reported previously [15], [16]. The [Co(gly)3] molecule with atom numbering scheme is presented in Fig. 3a. The CoII center coordinates to three chelated gly ligands (each with one N donor of the –NH2 group and one O atom of the monodendate –COO− group), forming a distorted octahedral stereochemistry. The average Co–N and Co–O distances are 1.935(5) and 1.916(4) Å, respectively, which are comparable to other similar compounds [15], [16], [17].

Analysis of the crystal packing of 3 in the unit cell indicates the existence of six intermolecular N–H⋯O hydrogen bonds between the –NH2 and –COO− groups of the gly molecules: N(1)–H(1A)⋯O(5)i (i=x+1,y,z), N(1)–H(1A)⋯O(2)ii (ii=x,−y+1/2,z−1/2), N(1)–H(1B)⋯O(4)iii (ii=−x+1,−y+1,−z+1), N(2)–H(2A)⋯O(1)ii, N(2)–H(2B)⋯O(2)iv (iv=x−1,−y+1/2,z−1/2) and N(3)–H(3B)⋯O(6)i, resulting in a three-dimensional supramolecular network with cavities along [100] direction as depicted in Fig. 3b. The lattice water molecules are included in these channels with the formation of N(3)–H(3A)⋯O(7)ii, O(7)–H(7A)⋯O(3) and O(7)–H(7B)⋯O(6)v (v=−x,y+1/2,−z+3/2) hydrogen bonds with the host framework, which further stabilize this structure. All the N(O)⋯O separations are in 2.774–3.055 Å range with H⋯O separations in 2.074–2.310 Å region, respectively; and the smallest bond angle is 134.3°, falling into the normal range of such weak hydrogen-bonding interactions [14].

3.2. Role of the organic acid in the self-assembly processes of complexes 1–3

From above descriptions, the choice of different organic acids is clearly critical in determining the molecular structures of the resultant complexes 1–3. As stated in Section 1, when 1,2,4,5-benzenetetracarboxylate or HCl was used in the self-assemblies process, the piperazine rings are bis-protonated and not involve in the coordination to the metal center. In this study, the nature of the acids is the underlying reason behind the differences in the structures of this series of CoII complexes. In complex 1, the terephthalic acid causes the mono-protonation of the piperazine rings, and thus, prevents their further coordination with the CoII center to form a mononuclear complex. For complex 2, the free piperazine rings bridge the CoII centers to form a one-dimensional coordination polymer. For compound 3, the glycine moieties directly chelated with the CoII center to form a mononuclear molecule and the piperazine ring does not take part in the coordination. Additionally, a mononuclear CoII complex with N-methylpiperazine was reported recently [18]. Thus, direct coordination to metal centers is not the only role of the acids, and indeed extremely subtle templating effects to change the coordination modes of piperazine have also been observed in this study.

4. Conclusion and comments

A series of CoII complexes 1–3 have been prepared by means of self-assemblies of CoCl2, piperazine and different organic acids, and structurally characterized by X-ray diffraction analyses. The resultant structures of these complexes are profoundly influenced by the organic acids, and different hydrogen-bonding supramoleclar architectures are observed, which can be utilized to advantage in supramolecular chemistry and crystal engineering. Further studies of the self-assemblies of such systems with other organic acids are under way in our laboratory.

The CQU-Chen-Gly-Co-3-1-based electrode exhibited a specific discharge capacity as high as 879.1 C g−1 at 1 A g−1 and even 547.7 C g−1 at a high rate of 20 A g−1 through a voltage range of 0.35 V, and average discharge capacitance of 2511.6 and 1564.8 F g−1 at 1 and 20 A g−1, respectively. The materials also exhibited superior charge–discharge stability and maintained 99.5% of its initial capacitance after 2000 continuous charge–discharge cycles at a current density of 10 A g−1. This simple design and facile synthesis provides an effective strategy to construct SCs electrode materials with enhanced battery-type of capacitive performance and shows promising potential for a wide range of applications in energy storage.

  • Copper(II)-saccharinato complexes with piperazine and N-(2-aminoethyl)piperazine ligands

    2008, Polyhedron

    Two copper(II) complexes of the saccharinate anion (sac) with piperazine (ppz) and N-(2-aminoethyl)piperazine (aeppz), namely [Cu(sac)2(ppz)(H2O)]n (1) and trans-[Cu(sac)2(aeppz)2] (2), have been synthesized and characterized by elemental analyses, UV–Vis, FT-IR, TGA/DTA, X-ray diffraction and magnetic measurements. The ppz ligands in 1 bridge the copper(II) centers through both nitrogen atoms to form a 1D helical chain structure and the distorted trigonal-bipyramidal coordination geometry at each copper center is completed by an aqua and a pair of N-bonded sac ligands. The helical chains are linked by Ow–H⋯O hydrogen bonds to build a 2-D network. In complex 2, copper(II) ions are octahedrally coordinated by two sac anions and two neutral aeppz ligands, displaying a distorted octahedral coordination. Sac is O-bonded via the carbonyl group, while ppzea acts as a N,N′-bidentate chelating ligand. The molecules are connected by N–H⋯N and N–H⋯O hydrogen bonds, forming a linear chain. In the thermal decomposition of both complexes, the removal of the aqua and ppz or aeppz ligand takes place endothermically in the first stages and the sac moiety undergoes highly exothermic decomposition at higher temperatures to give CuO.
