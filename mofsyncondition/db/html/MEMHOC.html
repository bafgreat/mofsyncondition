One-dimensional coordination polymer of N-(phosphonomethyl)iminodiacetic acid with iron(II)

Author links open overlay panel
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.molstruc.2006.01.003Get rights and content

Abstract

A one-dimensional coordination polymer containing Fe2+ centres and N-(phosphonomethyl)iminodiacetate residues (H4pmida), [Fe(H2pmida)(H2O)2], was synthesised via the hydrothermal method and structurally characterised by single-crystal and powder X-ray diffraction, elemental analysis, infrared spectroscopy, thermal analysis and magnetic measurements. The crystal structure is formed by the close packing of one-dimensional ∞1[Fe(H2pmida)(H2O)2] neutral coordination polymers, which run along the [0 0 1] direction of the unit cell. Interactions between neighbouring polymers are assured by several strong and highly directional O–H⋯O hydrogen bonds, described by a C54(11) graph set motif, involving the water molecules, the carboxylic acid group and the protonated phosphonate moiety.

1. Introduction

In the early 1990s, Hoskins and Robson [1] synthesised the first multi-dimensional diamondoid-type metal–organic frameworks (known as MOF, coordination polymers or coordination frameworks), making the first use of highly directional and strong coordinative bonds in the field of crystal engineering, but they probably never imagined the quantity of world-wide research which was to follow. Indeed, the development of this field is reflected in exponential growth of the number of publications in international journals in recent years, which has been largely motivated by the variety of peculiar topologies of MOF frameworks, together with their interesting properties and potential applications as functional materials [2], [3], [4], [5], [6].

Following our continuing interest in the synthesis (using optimised hydrothermal synthetic approaches [7]) and structural characterisation of such types of MOFs [7], [8], [9], [10], [11], [12], [13], we recently focused our attention on the multidentate chelating ligand N-(phosphonomethyl)iminodiacetic acid (H4pmida; Scheme 1) to construct multi-dimensional frameworks using the [V2O2(pmida)2]4− anionic unit [8], [14], [15]. Apart from the two flexible chelating arms containing carboxylic acid groups, the molecule also has a central N-donor atom plus a phosphonate group with additional flexible coordination properties (mainly due to the presence of three tetrahedrally-distributed oxygen atoms). Interestingly, searches in the available literature [16], [17], [18], [19], [20], [21], [22], [23] and in the Cambridge Structural Database [24], [25], revealed only a few materials containing this organic molecule. Although iron phosphonates have been investigated [26], [27], we report here the synthesis, structural characterisation and magnetic properties of the first example of a one-dimensional coordination polymer containing H4pmida residues and Fe2+ centres.

2. Experimental section

2.1. Synthesis of [Fe(H2pmida)(H2O)2]

Chemicals were readily available from commercial sources and were used as received without further purification: N-(phosphonomethyl)iminodiacetic acid hydrate (H4pmida, C5H10NO7P, 97% Fluka) and ammonia ferrous sulphate hexahydrate ((NH4)2Fe(SO4)2·6H2O, 99% Merck).

Syntheses were typically carried out in PTFE-lined stainless steel reaction vessels (ca. 40 cm3), under autogeneous pressure and static conditions in a preheated oven at 120 °C. Reactions took place over a period of 4 days after which the vessels were removed from the oven and left to cool to ambient temperature before opening.

The title compound was synthesised from a mixture containing 0.78 g of (NH4)2Fe(SO4)2·6H2O and 0.30 g of H4pmida in ca. 16 g of distilled water. This mixture was stirred through roughly at ambient temperature for 30 min yielding a suspension with a molar composition of 1.5: 1.0: 673, respectively, which was transferred to the reaction vessel. After reacting, a large quantity of colourless crystals (0.20 g, 48% yield) was isolated as a pure phase by vacuum filtering, washed with copious amounts of distilled water (ca. three times 50 ml), and then air-dried at ambient temperature. The pH values of the initial and final solutions were 1.8 and 1.6, respectively.

2.2. Instrumentation

Powder X-ray diffraction patterns were recorded using a Philips X'Pert diffractometer, operating with a monochromatic Cu–Kα radiation source at 40 kV and 50 mA. Simulated powder patterns were based on single-crystal data, and calculated using the STOE Win XPOW software package [28].

Elemental analyses for C, H and N were performed in the Microanalysis Laboratory of the University of Aveiro using a CHNS-932 Elemental Analyser.

FT-IR spectra were measured from KBr disks (Aldrich, 99%+, FT-IR grade) on a Matson 7000 FT-IR spectrometer. FT-Raman spectra were measured on a Bruker RFS 100 with a Nd:YAG coherent laser (λ=1064 nm).

Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were carried out using a Shimadzu TGA-50, with a heating rate of 5 °C/min under a continuous flow of air with a rate of 20 cm3/min.

SEM (Scanning Electron Microscopy) and EDS (Energy Dispersive analysis of X-rays Spectroscopy) were performed using a Hitachi S-4100 field emission gun tungsten filament instrument working at 25 kV.

Magnetic measurements were performed using a Quantum Design SQUID (Superconducting Quantum Interference Device) magnetometer MPMS at the University of Porto (Portugal).

2.3. Single-crystal X-ray diffraction

A suitable single-crystal of [Fe(H2pmida)(H2O)2] was mounted on a glass fibre using perfluoropolyether oil [29]. Data were collected at 180(2) K on a Nonius Kappa charge coupled device (CCD) area-detector diffractometer (Mo Kα graphite-monochromated radiation, λ=0.7107 Å), equipped with an Oxford Cryosystems cryostream and controlled by the Collect software package [30]. Images were processed using the software packages of Denzo and Scalepack [31], and data were corrected for absorption by the empirical method employed in Sortav [32], [33]. The structure was solved by the direct methods of shelxs-97 [34], and refined by full-matrix least squares on F2 using shelxl-97 [35].

All non-hydrogen atoms were directly located from difference Fourier maps and refined with anisotropic displacement parameters. Hydrogen atoms associated with the O(1W) and O(2W) water molecules were directly located from successive difference Fourier maps, and refined with the O–H and H⋯H distances restrained to 0.95(1) and 1.55(1) Å, respectively (in order to ensure a chemically reasonable geometry for these molecules), and using a riding model with an isotropic displacement parameter fixed at 1.5 times Ueq of the atom to which they are attached. The hydrogen atom from the uncoordinated carboxylic acid group was also directly located from difference Fourier maps and refined in successive least-squares cycles with the O–H distance restrained to 1.00(1) Å. Even though the hydrogen atom associated with the phosphonate group was also located in difference Fourier maps, its position was constrained using the AFIX 147 instruction in shelxl [35] and the isotropic thermal displacement parameter fixed at 1.5 times Ueq of O(7). Hydrogen atoms attached to carbon were located at their idealised positions using the HFIX 23 instruction in shelxl [35], and included in the refinement in riding-motion approximation with an isotropic thermal displacement parameter fixed at 1.2 times Ueq of the carbon atom to which they are attached. The last difference Fourier map synthesis showed the highest peak (0.572 eÅ−3) at 1.18 Å from H(2C), and the deepest hole (−0.764 eÅ−3) at 0.69 Å from Fe(1).

2.4. Cambridge structural database survey

The Cambridge Structural Database (CSD) (Version 5.25, November 2003) contains structural data for 298097 organic and organometallic compounds [24], [25], providing a valuable research tool to create electronic libraries of structural knowledge (e.g. geometrical aspects of coordination environments), and the ability to investigate the probability of occurrence of individual supramolecular motifs (e.g. hydrogen bonding and close contacts patterns). A handful of extensive studies of the database are already available in the literature [25], [36], [37], [38].

We have used the CSD to collect geometrical information on the Fe–O and Fe–N bonds. Searches to collect the intended structural information (bond distances, number of hits, refcodes, bibliographic information and data collection details) were performed using the graphical interface ConQuest [24], [39]. It is important to note that the vast majority of the available crystal data in the database were collected at ambient temperature (72% of the found structures were collected above 270 K). Thus, the statistical bond lengths forthcoming from the CSD searches are, in general, slightly longer than those obtained from diffraction data collected at lower temperatures. However, for the comparative study intended here it is fair to assume that these differences are very small.

For an iron coordination sphere containing water molecules, N- and O-donor atoms, there are a total of 92 entries in the CSD, of which only 54 have the three-dimensional coordinates fully determined [40]. Statistical analyses of the bond length distributions are summarised in Fig. 1 as box plots, and in Table 4.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Crystal structure of [Fe(H2pmida)(H2O)2]

The hydrothermal reaction, under mild conditions, between ammonium iron(II) sulfate and H4pmida yielded a highly crystalline compound (see Section 2) which was studied by SEM and EDS analyses (P: Fe ratio of ca. 1: 1), and formulated as [Fe(H2pmida)(H2O)2] on the basis of single-crystal X-ray diffraction (Table 1) and elemental analysis. Phase purity and homogeneity of the bulk sample were confirmed by powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) which also revealed the presence of some strong preferential orientation of the crystallites (not shown).

The crystal structure contains only one crystallographically unique Fe2+ metal centre, which is coordinated to two water molecules [O(1W) and O(2W), average bond distance ca. 2.12 Å], and to three O- and one N-donor atoms from H2pmida2− residues, leading to a six-coordination environment, {FeNO5}, resembling a distorted octahedron, with the cis and trans angles within the 74.19(8)–100.68(9)° and 167.21(9)–173.73(8)° ranges, respectively (Fig. 2 and Table 2). Interestingly, the coordination geometry of Fe2+ can also be described as a more regular square pyramidal environment [bond distances and angles found within the 2.051(2)–2.182(2) Å and 89.81(1)–96.36(1)° ranges, respectively], with the longer Fe–N bond to the H2pmida2− residue [2.311(2) Å] capping the base of the polyhedron (Fig. 2 and Table 2). The latter description is further supported by the results of the search of the Cambridge Structural Database (see above), which shows that the average Fe–N bond length in related compounds is only ca. 2.22 Å, significantly lower than the 2.311(2) Å registered for the title compound. Moreover, this bond length is significantly close to the statistical maximum (Fig. 1 and Table 4). Interestingly, there are only twelve structures (out of 54) [41] with Fe–N bond lengths comparable to that of ∞1[Fe(H2pmida)(H2O)2], all having the iron centre coordinated to multidentate chelating organic ligands similar to H4pmida. While the average Fe–Owater bond distance (ca. 2.12 Å; Table 2) is well within the expected range of related compounds (Fig. 1 and Table 4), the average Fe–O bond length (ca. 2.12 Å) is slightly longer than expected, being located in the upper 25th percentile of the statistical distribution. This deviation is due to the very long Fe(1)–O(4) bond length (Fig. 2 and Table 2), caused by the strong hydrogen bonding interaction between the coordinated carboxylate group and the O(2W) water molecule of a neighbouring moiety (see below; Table 3).

The H2pmida2− anion appears in [Fe(H2pmida)(H2O)2] as a polydentate organic ligand, which traps the Fe(1) centres inside two five-membered chelate rings, formed by one carboxylate group and the phosphonate moiety, both connected in the usually observed anti-unidentate coordinative fashion (Fig. 2). Interestingly, the two bite angles of ∞1[Fe(H2pmida)(H2O)2] are significantly different, a relatively uncommon feature among the known crystal structures containing H4pmida residues [16], [17], [18], [19], [20], [21], [22], [23]. On the one hand, the bite angle formed with the coordinated carboxylic acid group, 74.19(8)° (Fig. 2 and Table 2), is very close to the angles usually observed for the anionic centrosymmetric [V2O2(pmida)2]4− unit (ca. 76.2–77.0°) [8], [15], [16], [42], with the small difference being attributed to the slightly higher effective ionic radius of the Fe2+ centre (for a typical six-coordination geometry: V4+0.58 Å; Fe2+0.61 Å and 0.78 Å, for low- and high-spin complexes, respectively) [43], [44]. On the other hand, the bite angle formed with the phosphonate group, 83.51(9)° (Fig. 2 and Table 2), is also very similar to the angles observed in similar structures in which this group bridges two adjacent metal centres as, for example, in the cyclic [C30H30Co6N6O42P6]12- hexameric unit (ca. 82.3°) [19], and the polymeric structure [Co2(pmida)(H2O)5]n (ca. 81.4°) [17].

Another interesting feature of [Fe(H2pmida)(H2O)2], not observed in any other structure with H4pmida or its residues, is that the single H2pmida2- anion appears with one uncoordinated (and protonated) carboxylic acid group (Fig. 2), which is involved in a very strong O–H⋯O hydrogen bonding interaction with the coordinated carboxylate group of a neighbouring moiety (see below; Table 3). Furthermore, while for the coordinated carboxylic acid the C–O bonds are equivalent [Δ(C–O) of 0.016 Å], the uncoordinated group exhibits clear differences between the C–OH and the CO bonds [Δ(C–O) of 0.123 Å], even though this moiety is engaged in the hydrogen bonds mentioned above (Table 2).

As usually observed in structures containing H4pmida residues, the phosphonate functional group acts as an anchoring harbour due to the presence of the three coordinating oxygen atoms. Indeed, because of the translational symmetry intrinsically present in the crystal structure, this functional group establishes physical bridges between adjacent Fe2+ metal centres, imposing Fe(1)⋯Fe(1)i separations of 6.325(1) Å [symmetry code: (i) x, y, 1+z], leading to the formation of a one-dimensional ∞1[Fe(H2pmida)(H2O)2] neutral coordination polymer which runs along the [0 0 1] direction of the unit cell (Fig. 3). Individual and neighbouring ∞1[Fe(H2pmida)(H2O)2] polymers close-pack to form pseudo-layers in the ac plane, in which the coordinated water molecules all face each other and are involved in strong and highly directional [<(DHA) are all above 160°] O–H⋯O hydrogen bonding interactions (Fig. 4 and Table 3). It is interesting to note that the intra-layer hydrogen bonds, all involving water molecules, are distributed in a chain-like fashion describing two identical (related by inversion) C54(11) graph set motifs [45] running in opposite directions along [0 0 1] (Fig. 5, which also depicts the connectivity within the hydrogen bonding chain). Inter-layer connectivity is also assured by hydrogen bonds: the uncoordinated and pendant carboxylic acid groups interact with O(5) from the coordinated carboxylate of a neighbouring ∞1[Fe(H2pmida)(H2O)2] polymer (Table 3).

3.2. Thermogravimetry

Thermogravimetric studies under a continuous flow of air showed that the title compound is thermally stable up to ca. 170 °C. Between 170 and 340 °C a total weight loss of ca. 38.5%, in a multi-step process, is attributed to the release of all water molecules (calculated weight loss of ca. 11.4% for two molecules per formula unit), along with the partial oxidation of the H4pmida residues (ca. 27.1%, most probably corresponding to the release of two CO2 molecules arising from carboxylic acid groups (calculated for two CO2 residues ca. 27.8%). The second weight loss in the 340–600 °C temperature range is ascribed to the oxidation of the remnant organic component along with oxidation of the iron centres, leading to the formation of the stoichiometric amount of FePO4, as the mass of the residue (ca. 48.4%) is in good agreement with the expected value (ca. 47.6%). Powder XRD confirmed FePO4 as the main phase in the final residue.

3.3. Infrared spectroscopy

Infrared spectra are particularly informative, exhibiting the most important and diagnostic bands associated with the primary building blocks of the title compound and giving structural information on how these individual units interact in the solid state (see Section 2) [46], [47], [48]. In particular, several characteristic vibrational modes arise from the H2pmida2- anion [e.g. ν(C–H in –CH2–); δ(–CH2–); δ(C–C–N, amines); ν(P–O); ν(N–C), ν(P–C), δ(C–N–C, amines)], particularly those associated with protonated phosphonate groups [ν and δ(PO–H)] and carboxylic acid groups [ν(CO) and δ(O–H)] involved in hydrogen bonds.

The spectra are very informative about the extensive hydrogen bonding sub-network present in the crystal structure, which was previously described in detail (Table 3). Indeed, the broad band associated with hydrogen-bonded water molecules (and also carboxylic acid groups) which usually arises in the 3600–3200 cm−1 spectral region, appears in the title compound split into several broad bands (see Section 2) which is clear evidence of well-defined, very strong and highly directional O–H⋯O interactions involving the functional groups of H2pmida2− and water molecules (see Table 3). The presence of these hydrogen bonds is further supported by the typical vibrational bands associated with the PO–H stretching (ν, in the 2700–2500 cm−1 spectral region) and the in-plane deformation (δ) modes, and also to the in-plane (δ) and out-of-plane (γ) O–H⋯O deformations.

The presence of coordinated and uncoordinated carboxylic acid groups is clear from the vibrational spectra of [Fe(H2pmida)(H2O)2]. The typical antisymmetric and symmetric stretching bands for coordinated carboxylate anions are present, with the corresponding Δ[νasym(–CO2−) – νsym(–CO2−)] value calculated to ca. 159 cm−1, which agrees well with the presence of carboxylate functional groups coordinated in a typical anti-unidentate coordination fashion [49], [50]. Moreover, the stretching (ν) vibrational mode of CO from protonated carboxylic acid groups is evident in the 1740 cm−1 spectral region which, as usual, appears overlapped with the in-plane (δ) H–O–H deformation of water molecules.

3.4. Magnetic properties

At high temperature the compound shows typical Curie-Weiss behaviour (Weiss constant θ=0.9 K) with an effective magnetic moment of 5.8 μB/Fe ion and g-factor g=2.46, as reported for other high-spin iron (II) (S=2) complexes exhibiting a distorted octahedral geometry. The magnetic susceptibility of [Fe(H2pmida)(H2O)2] was determined over the temperature range 4–300 K and 100 Oe magnetic field (Fig. 6). At high temperature the compound shows typical Curie-Weiss behaviour with a magnetic moment of 5.8 μB/Fe ion, as reported for other high-spin iron (II) (S=2) complexes exhibiting a distorted octahedral geometry [51]. Below 50 K the effective magnetic moment is reduced through first order spin-orbit coupling and the influence of the crystal field. High-spin Fe (II) has a 5T2g ground state and thus the spin-orbit coupling does not quench the orbital moment, leading to large deviations of the g-factor from the free electron value and to large zero-field splitting.

The data was analysed according to existing models that describe the magnetic susceptibility. We have used the models described by Griffith [51] for 5T2g term arising from a free ion 5D term (transition ion in octahedral crystal fields, where the quenching of the orbital contribution is not complete) and as an alternative, the model of Figgis [52], [53] for large axial distortion that considers, besides the spin–orbit coupling, the effect of ligand field and electron delocalisation. All the attempts to describe the experimental data with those models did not provide a good fit.

4. Conclusion

In summary, we have reported the synthesis of the compound [Fe(H2pmida)(H2O)2] which is the first example of a 1D polymer containing the H4pmida ligand and Fe2+ centres. This coordination polymer contains a distorted octahedron {FeNO5} as the basic structural unit, in which the ferrous ion has an high-spin configuration. The crystal structure of the compound consists of a close packing of such 1D coordination polymers via strong hydrogen bonds. The possibility of assembling these 1D units into hierarchical more complex structures will be further investigated by introducing diverging ligands into the octahedron basic unit.

  • Synthesis and characterization of two manganese tert-butylphosphonate complexes

    Two manganese(II) phosphonate complexes, Mn2(t-BuPO3H)4(bipy)2 1 (bipy = 2,2′-bipyridine) and [Mn(H2O)6][t-BuPO3H]2·6H2O 2 have been synthesized with identical procedure from similar system containing tert-butylphosphonic acid and bipy ligands in different ratio at different temperature.   • Synthesis and characterization of two cobalt complexes derived from the system containing phenylphosphonic acid

    2008, Journal of Molecular Structure

    A single-chain cobalt phosphonate complex [Co(phen)(4,4′-bpy)(PhPO3H)2]n·0.5H2O 1 (phen = 1,10-phenanthroline, 4,4′-bpy = 4,4′-bipyridine) and a mononuclear cobalt complex [Co(pic)2(H2O)2]·0.5H2O 2 (picH = picolinic acid) have been synthesized with identical procedure from similar system containing phenylphosphonic acid and N-containing ligands. The magnetic measurement reveals overall weak antiferromagnetic exchange interactions between the cobalt ions in both complexes 1 and 2.
