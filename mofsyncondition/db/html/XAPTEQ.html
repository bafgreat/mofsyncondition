Introduction

Persistent organic pollutants (POPs), which can permanently persist in living environments, have attracted significant attention from governmental bodies and scientific community.1 They can enter the human body via the food chain, causing permanent damage to human health.2 Organochlorine chemicals, especially polychlorinated benzenes, are widely used in industry as solvents and materials for the synthesis of different types of dyes and phenols. They are also used as pesticides and fungicides in agriculture.3a Unfortunately, polychlorinated benzenes are one of the representative POPs that exist in living environments such as in soil, water, and industrial waste.3b They tend to bioaccumulate in living organisms, particularly in those residing at higher trophic levels and are resistant to biological degradation.3a,b To date, polychlorinated benzenes are generally identified by high performance thin layer chromatography (HPTLC), high performance liquid chromatography (HPLC), liquid secondary ion mass spectrometry (LSIMS), gas chromatography (GC), gas chromatography mass spectrometry (GC/MS), and so on. These methods can provide reliable analytical data but generally require complicated instruments and large amounts of cryogen and solvents.3c–g Therefore, there is an urgency to develop new methods that are highly efficient and easy to perform for detecting polychlorinated benzenes.

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have recently received significant attention in small-molecule sensing.4 MOFs are crystalline materials constructed via the self-assembly of metal ions/clusters and organic linkers.5 They have shown a range of different applications such as in gas storage and separation,6 heterogeneous catalysis,7 chemical sensing,8 drug delivery,9 and so on. As a subfamily of MOFs, lanthanide MOFs (Ln-MOFs) exhibit remarkable luminescence properties resulting from the intrinsic characteristics of lanthanide ions and the energy transfer between ligands and emission centers.10 Considering their applications as luminescent sensors, it is of high interest to utilize the signal transduction on luminescence readout and/or the colour change observed by the naked eye as induced by the targeted compounds. In this regard, Eu3+ and Tb3+ ions are widely used to synthesize luminescent sensing materials because of their intense characteristic red and green emissions when excited by the energy transfer from the ligands to the lanthanide ions.11

In this contribution, as part of an ongoing research on luminescent sensors based on lanthanide MOFs (Ln-MOFs),8l,m,10b,h–k we report a new Ln-MOF, {[Eu2(L)3(DMF)2]·DMF·MeOH}n (Ln-MOF 1, H2L = 5-(4H-1,2,4-triazol-4-yl)benzene-1,3-dicarboxylic acid, MeOH = methanol, DMF = N,N-dimethylformamide), obtained via a coordination symmetry approach,5f and its application as a highly efficient luminescent sensor to detect polychlorinated benzenes including 1,2,4-trichlorobenzene (1,2,4-TCB), 1,2,3,4-tetrachlorobenzene (1,2,3,4-TCB), 1,2,4,5-tetrachlorobenzene (1,2,4,5-TCB), pentachlorobenzene (PeCB), and hexachlorobenzene (HCB). To the best of our knowledge, Ln-MOF 1 is the first highly efficient luminescent sensor for detecting polychlorinated benzenes.

Experimental

Materials and methods

All the materials and solvents were purchased from commercial suppliers and used without further purification. H2L was synthesized following a reported method.12 Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) measurements were performed using a Rigaku D/Max-2500 X-ray diffractometer equipped with a Cu-target tube and a graphite monochromator scanning over the range of 5–50° at the rate of 0.2° s−1. The calculated patterns were generated using the Mercury software. Elemental analyses for C, H, and N were carried out using a Perkin-Elmer 240 CHN elemental analyzer. The Fourier transform infrared (FT-IR) spectrum was obtained using a Bruker TENOR 27 spectrophotometer with KBr pellets in the range of 4000 cm−1 to 400 cm−1. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) curves were obtained using a Labsys NETZSCH TG 209 Setaram apparatus under a nitrogen atmosphere at the heating rate of 10 °C min−1. UV-vis spectroscopic studies were carried out using a Shimadzu UV-3600 spectrophotometer. All luminescence measurements were performed using an Agilent Cary Eclipse fluorescence spectrophotometer at room temperature.

Synthesis

Synthesis of {[Eu2(L)3(DMF)2]·DMF·MeOH}n (Ln-MOF 1). A solution containing H2L (0.020 g, 0.1 mmol), EuCl3·6H2O (0.073 g, 0.2 mmol), and DMF/MeOH (9 : 4, 13 mL) was stirred for 5 min, then transferred to a thick-walled pressure vessel, and heated to 80 °C. After 8 h, colorless crystalline sheets were obtained. These were filtered off, washed with DMF, and dried in the air to afford the final product in a yield of 72% (based on H2L). Elemental analysis for Eu2C40N12O16H40 calcd: C, 38.47; H, 3.23; N, 13.46; found: C, 38.43; H, 2.94; N, 13.70. FT-IR (KBr, cm−1): 3302(w), 3110(m), 3067(m), 2975(m), 1666(s), 1623(s), 1590(s), 1552(s), 1430(s), 1382(s), 1294(s), 1237(s), 1206(s), 1156(m), 1098(s), 1050(s), 991(m), 862(m), 779(s), 742(s), 712(s), 670(s), 600(s), 560(s), 520(m) (Fig. S2†).

X-ray structure determination

Diffraction data for single crystal of Ln-MOF 1 was obtained using an Agilent Technologies SuperNova Single Crystal Diffractometer at 122 K equipped with graphite-monochromatic Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). The structure was solved by a standard direct method using the SHELXS program. All non-hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic thermal parameters by the least-squares method on F2 using the SHELXTL program, and hydrogen atoms on the organic ligands were generated by the riding mode.13 A summary of the crystallographic data and refinement parameters is provided in the ESI (Table S1†). The Cambridge Crystallographic Data Center (CCDC) contains supplementary crystallographic data for this study, CCDC 1505031.†

Results and discussion

Crystal structure

Single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis revealed that Ln-MOF 1 crystallized in the monoclinic space group I2/a. Ln-MOF 1 contains one crystallographically independent Eu3+ ion, one and a half L2− ligands, one coordinated DMF molecule, a half non-coordinated DMF molecule, and a half MeOH molecule per asymmetric unit. Each Eu3+ ion is eight-coordinated and adopts a square antiprism coordination geometry (Fig. 1a): six oxygen atoms from five carboxylate groups of five L2− ligands and one oxygen atom from one coordinated DMF, with Eu–O bond lengths ranging from 2.297(5) Å to 2.501(4) Å, and one nitrogen atom from the triazole of L2− ligand with the Eu–N bond length of 2.581(5) Å (Table S2†). Two eight-coordinated Eu3+ ions are bridged by four μ2-η1:η1 carboxylate groups from four L2− ligands to form a binuclear core with a Eu–Eu distance of 4.316(6) Å (Fig. 1b). The binuclear units are further bridged by carboxylate groups of different L2− ligands to form a 2D network structure (Fig. 1c). The 2D network structure is further connected by these bridging carboxylate groups into a 3D framework (Fig. 1d). The network topology was analysed by the freely available computer program TOPOS to obtain a simplified framework. If each Eu2 binuclear unit is considered as an eight-connected node, and L2− ligands are considered as three-connected nodes that link the Eu2 binuclear units, the framework can be viewed as a 3,8-connected tfz-d net with a short (Schläfli) vertex symbol of {43}2{46·618·84} (Fig. S3†). This topology is not common and has been reported in our previous study.10h

Thermogravimetric analyses

Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) curves were obtained in the temperature range of 30–800 °C (Fig. S4†). The TGA curve for Ln-MOF 1 shows no obvious plateau due to the strong interaction of DMF molecules with the framework. Ln-MOF 1 shows a weight loss of 20% from 100 to 350 °C, which is attributed to the loss of one uncoordinated MeOH, one uncoordinated DMF, and two coordinated DMF molecules. Further heating leads to a sharp weight loss, indicating the decomposition of the framework.

Luminescence properties

Luminescence spectra were obtained in both solid state and suspensions at room temperature. The excitation spectrum of Ln-MOF 1 exhibits a broad band with a maxima at 294 nm. When excited at 294 nm, the emission spectrum of Ln-MOF 1 reveals well-resolved peaks due to luminescence of f–f transitions, which are attributed to the energy transfer from the H2L ligands to Eu3+ ions (Fig. S5 and S6†). The emission spectrum of Ln-MOF 1 displays four characteristic peaks of Eu3+ ions, with a brilliant red luminescence at 592, 616, 652, and 704 nm, which are attributed to 5D0 → 7FJ (J = 1, 2, 3, and 4) transitions, respectively.14–17 Ln-MOF 1 shows a bright red luminescence with the main contribution from the 5D0 → 7F2 transition at 616 nm under 254 nm UV light.

To investigate the sensing ability of Ln-MOF 1 for polychlorinated benzenes, a series of luminescence measurements were conducted with samples in suspension state. Because Ln-MOF 1 is unstable in water, the luminescence spectra of Ln-MOF 1 in different organic solvents were obtained first. The stability of Ln-MOF 1 dissolved in different solvents was confirmed by the powder X-ray diffraction patterns (Fig. S7†). The standard suspensions were prepared by adding 3 mg of a ground sample into 3 mL of DMF and then treating by ultrasonication for 30 min since Ln-MOF 1 was synthesized with DMF as the dispersion medium to provide fine stability. Different organic solvents were added to the standard suspension with the same quantity to examine the potential of Ln-MOF 1 for the recognition of small organic molecules. As shown in Fig. S8,† organic solvents such as acetonitrile, formaldehyde, trichloromethane, and toluene could enhance the luminescence intensity. On the other hand, adding organic solvents such as methanol, ethanol, ethylene glycol, 1,4-dioxane, hexane, 1,2-dimethylbenzene, chlorobenzene, 1,2-dichlorobenzene, or 1,2,4-TCB into the standard suspension resulted in an obvious quenching effect, especially for polychlorinated benzenes. The results show that Ln-MOF 1 has a highly selective and sensitive sensing ability for polychlorinated benzenes, especially for 1,2,4-TCB. To investigate the sensitivity towards polychlorinated benzenes, further measurements were conducted. Suspensions of Ln-MOF 1 were prepared by the same abovementioned method but with gradually increasing amount of polychlorinated benzenes. As shown in Fig. S9–S11,† the luminescence intensity distinctly weakened with the gradually increasing concentration of polychlorinated benzenes. With the increase in the number of chlorine atoms on the benzene ring, the decrease of luminescent intensity became more and more significant. As for 1,2,4-TCB (Fig. 2), when the concentration was 0.8 mM, the luminescent intensity of Ln-MOF 1 showed a quenching efficiency as high as 33% (quenching efficiency = (I0 − I)/I0 × 100%, I0 is the maximum primary luminescence intensity without the addition of 1,2,4-TCB and I is the maximum luminescence intensity after exposure to 1,2,4-TCB). At the concentration of 100 mM, the luminescence intensity was almost completely quenched, with a remarkable quenching efficiency of 95%. After the addition of 1,2,4-TCB, the distinct red luminescence almost disappeared under illumination with UV light (254 nm). Fig. 2b shows the 5D0 → 7F2 transition intensities between 0 and 1000 mM. The curve shows a good agreement with the first-order exponential equation at lower concentrations (0–20 mM), which indicates a diffusion-controlled quenching process for the 1,2,4-TCB molecule.18

To understand the luminescence quenching mechanism, the quenching curve was quantitatively analyzed by the Stern–Volmer (S–V) equation, I0/I = KSV[C] + 1 (1) where I0 is the initial luminescence intensity before the addition of analyte, I is the luminescence intensity in the presence of analyte, [C] is the molar concentration of analyte, and KSV is the quenching constant (M−1).19 To make a thorough investigation of the luminescence quenching mechanism, we utilized an exponential quenching equation to fit the Stern–Volmer (S–V) equation, I0/I = a exp(k[C]) + b (2) where I0 is the maximum primary luminescence intensity without the addition of 1,2,4-TCB, I is the maximum luminescence intensity after exposure to 1,2,4-TCB, [C] is the molar concentration of 1,2,4-TCB, and a, b, and k are constants.20 The result shows that the curve can be perfectly fitted to I0/I = 306.28 exp(0.001[C]) − 316.31, with a correlation coefficient (R2) of 0.987 (Fig. 3b). As shown in the inset of Fig. 3b, a linear curve was obtained at lower concentrations (0–20 mM). Then, the fitting curve gradually deviated from linearity to nonlinearity with the increasing concentration. This result indicates that the quenching phenomenon induced by 1,2,4-TCB is due to a self-absorption or energy transfer process.10h–j

To further investigate the sensing properties for other polychlorinated benzenes such as 1,2,3,4-TCB, 1,2,4,5-TCB, PeCB, and HCB, which are typical POPs, a series of experiments were conducted as follows: 1,2,3,4-TCB, 1,2,4,5-TCB, and PeCB were separately dissolved in MeOH, whereas HCB was dissolved in CHCl3. The standard suspensions were prepared by adding a well-ground sample (3 mg) into MeOH or CHCl3 (3 mL) followed by ultrasonication for 30 min, forming MeOH standard suspensions and a CHCl3 standard suspension. The corresponding MeOH or CHCl3 solutions with polychlorinated benzenes were added to the standard suspensions. After ultrasonication for 30 min, a series of suspension-state luminescence spectra were obtained. The intensity versus the concentration of 1,2,3,4-TCB, 1,2,4,5-TCB, PeCB, and HCB are shown in Fig. 4 and 5. When the concentration was 0.02 mM, the luminescence intensity of Ln-MOF 1 treated with 1,2,3,4-TCB decreased, with the quenching efficiency of 7%. At the concentration of 12.5 mM, the luminescence quenching efficiency was 82% (Fig. 4a). Similar to 1,2,3,4-TCB, with four chlorine atoms, 1,2,4,5-TCB displayed similar property but with a higher quenching efficiency of 13% (at 0.02 mM) and 86% (at 12.5 mM) (Fig. 4b). As for PeCB, when the concentration was 0.02 mM, the luminescence intensity decreased with a quenching efficiency of 13%. When the concentration was 12.5 mM, the luminescence intensity was quenched by 98% (Fig. 5a). For HCB, the luminescence intensity weakened with the gradually increasing concentration of HCB (Fig. 5b). At the concentration of 0.02 mM, the luminescence intensity decreased with a quenching efficiency of 14%. At the concentration of 12.5 mM, the luminescence intensity was almost completely quenched by 98%. Fig. 4c and d and 5c and d show the intensities of 5D0 → 7F2 transition between 0 and 12.5 mM for 1,2,3,4-TCB, 1,2,4,5-TCB, PeCB, and HCB, respectively. From the insets of these figures, we can see that at lower concentrations (0–0.32 mM), the curves show a good agreement with the first-order exponential equation, which indicates a diffusion-controlled quenching process for these molecules at these concentrations.

The Stern–Volmer plots of Ln-MOF 1 with different concentrations of 1,2,3,4-TCB, 1,2,4,5-TCB, PeCB, and HCB are displayed in Fig. 6a. PeCB and HCB clearly exhibit a higher quenching efficiency than 1,2,3,4-TCB and 1,2,4,5-TCB. To further explore the luminescence quenching efficiency of 1,2,3,4-TCB, 1,2,4,5-TCB, PeCB, and HCB, we used the abovementioned exponential quenching equation to fit the Stern–Volmer (S–V) equation as well. The results show that the curve of 1,2,3,4-TCB can be fitted to I0/I = 5.27 exp(0.046[C]) − 4.87 at higher concentrations (0.16–12.5 mM) (Fig. 6b). The fitting curve for 1,2,4,5-TCB is I0/I = 0.61 exp(0.163[C]) + 1.33 at higher concentrations (1.0–12.5 mM) (Fig. 6c). For PeCB, the curve can be perfectly fitted to I0/I = 15.4 exp(0.105[C]) − 14.998 with the correlation coefficient (R2) of 0.989 (0–12.5 mM) (Fig. 6d). With regards to HCB, the fitting curve can be well-fitted to I0/I = 10.46 exp(0.13[C]) − 9.996 with the correlation coefficient (R2) of 0.999 (0–12.5 mM) (Fig. 6e). To the best of our knowledge, Ln-MOF 1 is the first specialized and efficient luminescence sensor for polychlorinated benzenes, especially for detecting PeCB and HCB, which is of great significance for living environments and human health.

We also carried out a recyclability study of Ln-MOF 1 based on the detection of 1,2,4-TCB. The results revealed that Ln-MOF 1 can be easily reactivated by a simple filtration, then washing several times with DMF, followed by centrifugation to be reused for many cycles. The initial luminescence intensity in the first five cycles was mostly maintained (100%, 99.3%, 98.6%, 97.3%, and 94.7%), based on the luminescence intensity of standard suspensions. Powder X-ray diffraction of the samples after five cycles revealed that the structure was highly stable, with no loss of crystallinity (Fig. S16†).

The mechanism of luminescent response

To better understand the high quenching efficiency of Ln-MOF 1 towards polychlorinated benzenes, we studied the quenching mechanism in detail. The kinetic sizes of polychlorinated benzenes (Fig. S17†) are larger than the size of the Ln-MOF 1 channel (2.36 × 9.82 Å2) along the a-axis, which results in the inability of polychlorinated benzene molecules to enter the Ln-MOF 1 channel, except on the surface. It is known that the influence of guest solvent molecules on the ligand–metal energy transfer (LMET) definitely plays an important role since there are physical intermolecular interactions between the ligands and guest solvent molecules. If the energy from the excited p-states of the ligand is transferred to the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) of the analyte, a fluorescence quenching of the lanthanide centers may occur.18,21,22d Thus, the energies of HOMOs and LUMOs of polychlorinated benzenes and the ligand in Ln-MOF 1 were calculated by density functional theory (DFT) (Fig. S18†).22,23 Based on the results, the LUMO of the ligand had a lower energy than the LUMO of polychlorinated benzenes, suggesting that there may not be any energy transfer between the guest polychlorinated benzenes and the ligands, which will not cause the quenching effect.23d,g

The mechanism of the quenching effect was further investigated by UV-vis absorption spectroscopy. It was hypothesized that the Ln-MOF 1 luminescence quenching after the addition of polychlorinated benzenes could be induced by a competition for the absorption of irradiated light between the analyte and the ligand. When polychlorinated benzenes were added to the standard suspensions of Ln-MOF 1, the polychlorinated benzenes speedily filtered the light absorbed by the ligand, thus decreasing the total energy transfer from the ligand to the Eu3+ centers, and consequently quenching the luminescence.11k,23g To further confirm this hypothesis, luminescence studies with the exciting light with wavelength longer than the absorption of the guest solvents were conducted. Based on the absorption band of chlorobenzene, 1,2-dichlorobenzene, and 1,2,4-TCB (Fig. S19a†), when excited at 320 nm, which is not in the UV-vis absorption region of 1,2,4-TCB, the luminescence intensities of Ln-MOF 1 and that of the mixture of Ln-MOF 1 and 1,2,4-TCB were almost identical (Fig. S19b†). Fig. S19c† shows that the absorption spectrum of PeCB and HCB have a larger overlap with the spectrum of H2L than those of 1,2,3,4-TCB and 1,2,4,5-TCB. When excited at 350 nm, the luminescence intensities remained the same for Ln-MOF 1 and the mixture of Ln-MOF 1 and guest molecules (Fig. S19d†). This phenomenon indicate that the competition for the absorption of radiation between polychlorinated benzenes (chlorobenzene, 1,2-dichlorobenzene, 1,2,4-TCB, 1,2,3,4-TCB, 1,2,4,5-TCB, PeCB, HCB) and H2L can significantly affect the quenching process. In summary, the quenching mechanism is in agreement with the abovementioned quenching behavior and is consistent with previous studies.11k,23a,g

Conclusions

In summary, an effective luminescence sensor for polychlorinated benzenes based on a new lanthanide metal–organic framework with a macroscopic red-emission signal was successfully developed. Systematical luminescence studies showed that Ln-MOF 1 represents a high efficiency quenching phenomenon on detecting polychlorinated benzenes including 1,2,4-TCB, 1,2,3,4-TCB, 1,2,4,5-TCB, PeCB, and HCB, which belong to the persistent organic pollutants. With the increase of number of chlorine atoms on benzene ring, the decrease of luminescent intensity became more evident. The high-sensitivity sensing function could be attributed to the competition for the absorption of photons between polychlorinated benzenes and ligands in the system. The well-defined correlation between the luminescence intensity of Ln-MOF 1 and the concentration of polychlorinated benzenes provides a reliable relationship that makes Ln-MOF 1 a low-cost and user-friendly luminescence sensor to determine the concentration of different target species. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first specialized and efficient luminescence sensor for polychlorinated benzenes. The present study highlights the practical applications of luminescent MOFs as sensors and provides a novel Ln-MOF-based sensor for persistent organic pollutants, which is of great importance for living environments and human health.
