Abstract

The catena-poly[di-μ-aqua-(μ-maleic hydrazidato-O)sodium] hydrate, [Na(MH) (H2O)2]n·H2O is examined using single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis. The crystals are monoclinic, space group C2/c, with a = 14.321(4), b = 16.114(5), c = 6.547(1) Å, β = 104.11(2) and Z = 4. In title complex, each sodium ion is coordinated by two oxygen atoms of two water molecules, one oxygen atom of the maleic hydrazidato, two oxygen atoms of two water molecules from an adjacent [Na(MH)(H2O)2] unit and one oxygen atom of the maleic hydrazidato from an adjacent [Na(MH)(H2O)2] unit too. It is interesting that the sodium ion is not bonded by O-deprotonated oxygen atom but by carbonyl oxygen atom of maleic hydrazidato. The infrared and Raman spectra for maleic hydrazide (MH), its deuterated derivative (MD) and [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O are reported. The theoretical wavenumbers, infrared intensities and Raman scattering activities have been calculated using density functional (B3LYP) method with the 6-311++G(d,p) for MH and MD and 6-311++G(d,p)/LanL2DZ for [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O basis sets. The calculated potential energy distribution has proved to be of great help in assigning the infrared and Raman spectra maleic hydrazide, its deuterated derivative and [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O. The isotope replacements were very helpful in correlation of observed bands and of the theoretically calculated normal vibrations. The results from natural bond orbital (NBO) analyses for keto-hydroxy, diketo and dihydroxy forms of MH as well as for the theoretical model of [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O are compared.

1. Introduction

Maleic hydrazide (MH) is applied as the herbicide in agriculture [1] and acts as a plant-growth [2]. Moreover, the molecules of MH can act either as a purine analogue forming base pairs with uracil and thymine, or as a pyrimidine analogue forming base pairs with adenine and guanine [3], [4].

Crystals of MH were investigated by X-rays [5], [6], [7]. The keto-hydroxy form has been found in the solid state of MH [5], [6], [7]. There are the OH⋯O and the NH⋯O hydrogen bonds in the crystals of MH [5], [6], [7]. The semiempirical AM1, MNDO-PM3, MNDO, and ab initio calculations of relative stabilities, dipole moments, structures, and ionization potentials of the different tautomers of maleic hydrazide are reported [8], [9], [10]. The calculations indicate that the keto-hydroxy form and the diketo form are the most stable tautomers of the plant-growth inhibitor maleic hydrazide both in the gas phase and in solution [10]. Fritz et al. [11] investigated of Ca(II), Mn(II), Fe(II), Co(II), Ni(II), Cu(II), Zn(II), and Pt(II) metal complexes with maleic hydrazide and their physiologic action on Yoshida-ascites-sarcoma cells. Basing on the infrared, Raman and H-NMR spectroscopic methods, the authors reported that maleic hydrazide predominantly coordinates to metals vie the two oxygen atoms (mainly Zn(II) ions) or via the oxygen and nitrogen atoms (another metal ions) and additionally, in the platinum(II) complex O-deprotonated oxygen atom of maleic hydrazide is not bonded by Pt(II) ions. It should be noted, that the frequencies of these complexes in the range 4000–300 cm−1 were been measured but were not been published and their assignments were not been determined. Moreover, no single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis has been performed for the metal complexes of maleic hydrazide, as yet. The crystal structure of sodium ions with triethanolamine triacetic acid has been reported [12]. In this complex the sodium ions are not bonded by O-deprotonated oxygen atom of carboxylic group but by carbonyl oxygen atom of carboxylic group [12].

In the present study, the [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O is examined using single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis. I have performed the comprehensive theoretical and experimental studies of the infrared and Raman spectra of MH its DO- and DN-deuterated derivative and of the novel sodium complex, [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O.

2. Experimental

2.1. Preparation of crystals of [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O and MD

Crystals of [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O were prepared by mixing a water solution of maleic hydrazide (Aldrich) (0.11 g in 150 cm3) with 2 cm3 the solution of 1 M NaOH. The mixture was heated to 318 K until maleic hydrazide dissolved completely. The obtained solution was left for crystallization. After a two weeks the transparent crystals of [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O were formed.

The deuterated derivative MD was prepared by repeated crystallization from D2O until no bands due to the N–H and O–H vibrations were detected in the spectra.

2.2. X-ray analysis

The X-ray diffraction data were collected at 290(2) K, using a XCALIBUR 3™ CCD diffractometer with graphite-monochromated MoKα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). The structure was solved by direct methods using SHELXS-97 program [13] and refined by full-matrix least-squares on F2 with SHELXL-97 [13]. The hydrogen atoms bonded to C and N atoms were introduced at calculated positions as riding atoms, with C–H and N–H distances of 0.93 and 0.86 Å, respectively, while the O-bound H atoms were located from difference maps. The O–H distances were restrained with a DFIX command in SHELXL-97 [13]. For all H atoms, Uiso (H) values were constrained to be 1.2Ueq (C, N) or 1.5Ueq (O).

The crystal data together with the refinement details are given in Table 1. Atomic coordinates have been deposited at the Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre and allocated under the deposition number CCDC 605856.

2.3. Spectroscopic measurements

The infrared spectra of solid MH, MD, and [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O in the region 4000–400 cm−1 were measured at 2 cm−1 resolution on a Nicolet–Nexus spectrometer using the KBr pellets. The far infrared spectra in the frequency range 700–50 cm−1 were measured at 2 cm−1 resolution on a Perkin Elmer 2000 spectrometer. Samples were prepared as Nujol mulls placed between polyethylene windows.

The Raman spectrum of solid MH was recorded on Nicolet 2000 spectrometer at 2 cm−1 resolution and by a laser operating at 1064 nm. The Raman spectra of solid MD and [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O were recorded on Nicolet Almega spectrometer at 2 cm−1 resolution and by a laser operating at 532 nm.

2.4. Theoretical methods

The theoretical studies have been performed using the density functional three-parameter hybrid, B3LYP [15], [16] method with the LanL2DZ [17], [18] for the sodium and 6-311++G(d,p) [19], [20] basis sets for the remaining atoms. According to the results obtained by B3LYP calculations the keto-hydroxy form of MH (shown in Scheme 1) is more stable than the diketo form of MH by about 1.49 kJ/mol. The diketo form of MH is slightly more stable than the dihydroxy form of MH only by about 4.3 J/mol. The harmonic wavenumbers, infrared intensities and Raman scattering activities were calculated for the optimized planar keto-hydroxy form of the MH and these were compared with the experimental data of MH in solid state (Table 4). The harmonic wavenumbers, infrared intensities were calculated for the same form of the MD and these were compared with the experimental data of MD in solid state (Table 4). For the [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O, the theoretical model was limited to coordinating bridging O8-deprotonated maleic hydrazidato to two sodium ions via oxygen atom O7 and one bridging aqua molecule (shown in Scheme 2). Prior to normal mode computations, the optimization of molecular geometry was carried out starting from structural parameters determined by the X-ray method. Basing on the optimized structure, the normal vibration wavenumbers, IR intensities and Raman activities were calculated and these were compared with the experimental data of [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O in solid state (Table 5).

The scaling of the wavenumbers computed by the B3LYP method deserves some comments. The calculated by B3LYP method wavenumbers of the p-fluorophenol and its OD deuterated derivative [21], [22] and of the 5-bromo-2-nitropyridine [23] were scaled by the factor 0.953 for the stretching modes OH and OD and by the factor of 0.958 for the stretching modes CH and by 0.983 for the remaining vibrational modes. In this work all the wavenumbers calculated by the B3LYP method were scaled by 0.93 for stretching modes OH, NH, OD, and ND, by 0.96 for stretching modes CH and by 0.98 for the remaining modes, similarly as in our earlier studies [24], [25], [26]. This approach aimed at using the least possible number of scaling factors, which can yield frequencies in better agreement with the experiment. The overall agreement between the theoretical and experimental wavenumbers is good with the exception of modes which are involved in the hydrogen bonds.

The normal coordinate analyses for the theoretical models of MH, MD, and [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O have been carried out, including the calculation of the potential energy distribution (PED) for each normal mode, according to the procedures described in ref. [27] (BALGA program). The non-redundant set of internal coordinates has been derived, as recommended by Fogarasi and Pulay [28], [29], [30].

The computations were carried out with the Gaussian 03 programs [31] on multiprocessor SGI Origin 2400 supercomputer.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Description of the structure of [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O

The overall view and labeling of the atoms and the coordination environment around the sodium ion in the [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O are shown in Fig. 1. The each sodium ion is six-coordinated by two oxygen atoms O(1) and O(2) of two water molecules, one oxygen atom O(7) of the maleic hydrazidato, two oxygen atoms O(1) and O(2) of two water molecules from an adjacent [Na(MH)(H2O)2] units at (x, −y, z − 1/2) and (x, −y, z + 1/2) as well as one oxygen atom O(7) of the maleic hydrazidato belonging to the unit at (x, −y, z + 1/2). Table 2 presents the selected bond lengths and angles in [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O. The Na(1)–O(1) bond is longer by about 0.061 Å than the Na(1)–O(2) bond. The Na(1)–O(1) bond is shorter and the Na(1)–O(2) bond is longer than in an adjacent [Na(MH)(H2O)2] unit only by about 0.033 and 0.008 Å, respectively. The Na(1)–O(7) bond is shorter than in an adjacent [Na(MH)(H2O)2] unit by about 0.054 Å. Fig. 2 illustrates part of the crystal structure of [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O, showing the O(1)–H(1A)⋯N(1)#3 and N(2)–H(2)⋯O(7)#5 (symmetry codes: #3 1 − x, y, 3/2 − z; #5 1 − x, −y, 1 − z) hydrogen bonds denoted as k and n, respectively, as well as the intermolecular π⋯π stacking contacts between the maleic hydrazidato ring in the molecules at (x, y, z) and those in the molecules at (1 − x, y, 3/2 − z) (A) and (1 − x, y, 1/2 − z) (B). These maleic hydrazidato rings are nearly parallel, with an interplanar angle between rings of only 0.23°. The centroid Cg⋯Cg (Cg is the centroid of maleic hydrazidato ring) separations are 3.361(1) and 3.381(1) Å, respectively, while the interplanar separations are 3.33 and 3.24 Å, respectively. As it can be seen from Fig. 2 the k and n hydrogen bonds together with the A and B π⋯π contact link the two polymeric chains into the infinite chain running parallel to the c axis. Further, the adjacent chains are connected by four hydrogen bonds type O–H⋯O (l, m, o, and p in Fig. 3 and in Table 3) into the three-dimensional network.

3.2. Natural Atomic Charges of MH

In Scheme 1 the natural atomic charges calculated from the NBO analysis for the keto-hydroxy, the diketo and the dihydroxy forms of the MH are illustrated. Calculated charge on the carbon C(3) atom of the keto-hydroxy form is less positive in comparison to the charges on the carbon C(3) and C(6) atoms of the diketo form. Calculated charges on the carbon C(6) atom of the keto-hydroxy form and on the carbon C(3) and C(6) atoms of the dihydroxy form are practically unaffected. Calculated charges on the nitrogen N(1) atom of the keto-hydroxy form and on the nitrogen N(1) and N(2) atoms of the dihydroxy form are also practically unaffected. Calculated charge on the nitrogen N(2) atom of the keto-hydroxy form is less negative in comparison to the charges on the nitrogen N(1) and N(2) atoms of the diketo form. The charge on the carbonyl oxygen O(7) atom of the keto-hydroxy form is more negative than charges on the both carbonyl oxygen O(7) and O(8) atoms in diketo form. The charges on the oxygen O(8) atoms of the keto-hydroxy and dihydroxy forms are practically unaffected.

In Scheme 2 the natural atomic charges calculated by NBO method for the theoretical model of [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O are presented. The removal of proton from the oxygen O(8) atom of the hydroxy group and the coordination to the oxygen O(7) atom two sodium ions leads to an accommodation of the negative charge (of about −1.04e) on the oxygen O(7) atom. The positive charge on the sodium ions leads to coordination bridging water molecules. An increase in the complex the negative charge on the oxygen O(7) atom is accompanied by a degrease the negative charge on the oxygen O(8) atom in comparison to the keto-hydroxy form of MH. It should be emphasized that coordination to the oxygen O(7) atom two sodium ions and deprotonation of the oxygen O(8) atom leads to a redistribution of electron density also in the maleic hydrozidato ring. The calculated charges on the nitrogen N(1) and carbon C(4) atoms of the complex are more negative, while those on the nitrogen N(2) and carbon C(5) are less negative, meanwhile on the carbon C(3) is less positive and on the carbon C(6) is more positive, in comparison to keto-hydroxy form of MH.

It has been concluded that the polarities of the bonds for the keto-hydroxy form of MH and for the [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O are different. According to those results the wavenumbers of bands of the same bonds in these molecules can be observed in the different wavenumbers.

3.3. Theoretical studies and vibrational spectra of MH and its deuterated derivative

The NH and OH stretching and bending vibrations of the MH can be considered as N–H⋯O and of O–H⋯O intermolecular hydrogen bonds, respectively [5], [6], [7]. The theoretical calculations were performed for isolated molecule without intermolecular interactions and could not be used for assignment of bands derived from vibrations of hydrogen bonds. The observed N- and O-deuteration effects have been used for attribution of NH and OH group vibrations. The infrared and Raman spectra of MH and of MD in the range from 3700 to 1800 cm−1, from 1800 to 600 cm−1, and from 600 to 50 cm−1 are shown in Fig. 4, Fig. 5, Fig. 6, respectively.

The strong band at 3167 cm−1 in the infrared spectrum of MH may be assigned to νN2H mode in the solid state. On the other hand according to PED analysis the band at 3350 cm−1 was assigned to the stretching vibration of this group. During deuteration this band is shifted to 2322 cm−1. The isotopic ratio is equal to 1.36. The theoretical N2D stretching wavenumber is predicted at 2457 cm−1.

The strong band at 1410 and weak band at 1424 cm−1 in infrared and Raman spectra, respectively, may be assigned to the in-plane deformation of N2H group. After deuteration it moves to 1114 cm−1 in infrared spectrum (isotopic ratio = 1.27). As follows from Table 4, the scaling factor of 0.98 employed for the B3LYP-calculated wavenumber of the N2H in-plane bending mode, reproduces very well the experimental wavenumber. Examination of Table 4 shows that the N2D in-plane bending mode is coupled with the other modes and contributes to the three modes with the predominant contribution at theoretical wavenumber 908 cm−1.

According to the B3LYP calculations, the wavenumber of the C3O7 stretching mode for MH and MD contributed to two modes with predominant contribution at 1716 and 1708 cm−1, respectively. In the C=O stretching region strong band at 1661 cm−1 in the infrared spectrum and weak band at 1663 cm−1 in the Raman spectrum of MH and medium band at 1653 cm−1 in the infrared spectrum and weak band at 1647 cm−1 in the Raman spectrum of MD are observed. It should be underlined that anharmonic in the solid state wavenumbers of MH and MD are observed in lower wavenumbers than harmonic predicted by B3LYP calculations. This can be explained by the fact that in the crystal, MH molecules joined by O8-H⋯O7=C3 and N2-H⋯O7=C3 hydrogen bonds.

According to the calculations at the B3LYP level the out-of-plane bending C3O7 mode in MH and in MD contributed to four and three modes, respectively with predominant contribution at 722 cm−1 for both. It should be noted that B3LYP calculations shows that this mode has very small Raman activity. In the infrared and Raman spectra of MD corresponding bands are observed as weak at 333 cm−1 and medium at 334 cm−1, respectively.

As revealed by PED, one mode is associated mainly with the CH out-of-plane bending vibration but the mode has zero infrared intensity and very small Raman activity.

The bands at 2872, 2789, 2723, 2585, 2524, 2408, 1965, 1849, and 1820 cm−1 in the infrared spectrum of MH and at 3099, 2869, 2767, 2704, 2581, 2512, 2420, and 1963 cm−1 in the infrared spectrum of MD are not enumerated in Table 4. These bands can be assigned to overtones or combination modes.

3.4. Theoretical studies and vibrational spectra of [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O

The infrared and Raman spectra of crystalline [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O are shown in Fig. 7, Fig. 8.

A comparison of the vibrational spectra of the [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O with that of the MH indicates that all bands assigned to O8H vibrations in the ligand disappear in the spectra of the complex, which confirms deprotonation of the oxygen atom O8. The N2H modes in the MH disappear not in the spectra of the [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O, which acknowledges that the N2H group is not deprotonate.

The bands for the N2H vibrations in the [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O were assigned by analogy to the NH modes in the MH. The stretching and bending modes of the N(2)H group in the title complex can be assigned as vibration of N–H⋯O hydrogen bands in the crystals of [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O.

According to DFT result, the three modes calculated at 1497, 1454, and 1307 cm−1 have a contribution of about 41–13% from the N2H in-plane bending vibration, with predominant contribution at 1454 cm−1. The strong band at 1419 cm−1 in the infrared spectrum and the weak band at 1427 cm−1 in the Raman spectrum can be assigned to δN2H vibration.

The medium band at 908 cm−1 in [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O can be result of out-of-plane N2H vibration. The calculations predict that normal mode at 683 cm−1 has contribution from γN2H.

According to the calculations at the B3LYP level the C3O7 and C6O8 stretching vibrations generates three and two modes, respectively. The calculated PED has revealed that the νC3O7 vibration, contributes to 1307, 1189, and 796 cm−1 modes. The νC3O7 mode overlap with other modes and have not predominant contribution. Examination of the Raman spectrum of [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O revealed the presence of the two new strong and very strong bands at 1314 and 1308 cm−1, respectively which can be assigned to the stretching C3O7 mode. A lowering of the C3O7 stretching wavenumber in the [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O as compared to those in the spectra of MH confirms the coordination to two sodium ions. The νC6O8 vibration contributes to modes at 1629 and 1497 cm−1 with the predominant contribution at 1629 cm−1. The C6O8 bond participates in the hydrogen bonding in the [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O crystal. Therefore in the vibrational spectra the νC6O8 vibration moves to lower wavenumbers. The strong band at 1522 cm−1 in the infrared spectrum and weak band at 1523 cm−1 in the Raman spectrum, may arise from the stretching vibration of the C6O8.

According to the B3LYP calculations, the δC3O7 and γC3O7 vibrations are coupled with other vibrations and contributes to four (562, 413, 381, and 366 cm−1) and to three (622, 525, and 262 cm−1) normal modes, respectively. The δC3O7 and γC3O7 modes have not predominant contribution. The δC6O8 and γC6O8 modes also are coupled with other vibrations and contributes to five (562, 479, 413, 381, and 366 cm−1) and to three (818, 622, and 525 cm−1) modes, but the γC6O8 vibration has predominant contribution at theoretical wavenumber 525 cm−1.

In additional to the fundamental modes (the antisymmetric and symmertic OH stretchings, the in-plane bending HOH and librational modes) of the free water molecule coordinated water exhibits other rocking HOH, wagging HOH and stretching metal-oxygen modes [38], [39], [40].

In the [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O the hydrate and the bridging coordinated water molecules are present. From the infrared spectrum of [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O it is evident that the water molecules in the crystals are involved in hydrogen bonding. A few new broad bands in the infrared spectrum of [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O from 3618 to 2775 cm−1 can be assigned to stretching water vibrations. The theoretical model was limited to coordination only one bridging water molecule. The calculations show that the antisymmetric and symmetric stretching OH modes of bridging water molecule are predicted theoretically at 3571 and 3501 cm−1. The water in-plane bending mode is calculated at 1597 cm−1. The strong band in the infrared spectrum at 1595 cm−1 and the weak band in the Raman spectrum at 1586 cm−1 can be assigned to in-plane bending H2O mode. The theoretical calculations predicted that the wagging mode of the bridging water molecule of the [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O is higher than the rocking mode. The wagging HOH mode at 530 cm−1 is not coupled with other modes. However the rocking HOH mode predicted theoretically at 381 and 366 cm−1 are coupled with other modes. In aqua-halogeno complexes of various types the wagging H2O modes are observed higher in the infrared spectra than the rocking H2O modes [41]. The band lattice water expected around 670 cm−1 in the infrared spectrum of [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O cannot be assigned because presence of other bands in the region this makes impossible.

In the Raman spectrum of the diclofenac sodium the νNaO mode is assigned to the shoulder at 417 cm−1 [42]. According to the calculated PED the stretching νNa1O7 and νNa1aO7 vibrations are strongly mixed between themselves and overlap with other modes with predominant contributions at 398 and 246 cm−1. The νNa1O2 and νNa1aO2 stretching modes are strongly mixed between themselves at 148 and 61 cm−1 theoretical wavenumbers. The medium at 430 cm−1 in the infrared spectrum and the weak band at 424 cm−1 in the Raman spectrum of [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O can be assigned to stretching Na–O7 modes. A broad and asymmetric shape of the band at 190 cm−1 can be attributed to the presence of several Na–H2O absorptions.

According to the calculated PEDs the νC5H mode has predicted at theoretical wavenumber and scaled by 0.96 factor at 3066 cm−1 and the νC4H mode at 3032 cm−1. The C4H and C5H modes are not coupled themselves in [Na(MH)(H2O)2]n·H2O in contrast to MH and MD. The medium bands at 3064 and 3063 cm−1 in the infrared and Raman spectra, respectively can be assigned to the stretching C5H vibration. The band at 2956 cm−1 can be assigned to the stretching C4H. However, the calculations performed for MH and its deuterated derivative predicted that the predominant contribution for the C5H stretching mode is observed in lower wavenumber range than the C4H stretching mode.

According to PED, the in-plane and out-of-plane bending CH modes have contribution to four and three bands, respectively. The δCH and γCH modes are observed in the range 1217–1014 cm−1 and below 855 cm−1, respectively. As revealed by PED one mode is associated mainly with the CH out-of-plane bending vibration which can be assigned to band at 855 cm−1 in the infrared spectrum.

In conclusion, the results obtained will aid considerably in further investigation of the intermolecular interactions of maleic hydrazide with other biomolecules or metal ions, by using vibrational spectroscopic methods. The detailed examination of the spectra provided additional data for the sodium–ligand bonding.
