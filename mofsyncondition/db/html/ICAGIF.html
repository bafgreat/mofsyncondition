Abstract

In this paper, a series of new complexes, [Zn2(HL1)2(H2O)4]·H2O (1), [Co2(HL1)2]·TEA (2), [Co3(HL1)2(H2L1)2(H2O)4]n (3), [Cu(HL1)(H2O)2]n (4), {[Cu5(HL2)2(OH)4(ClO4)2]·4H2O}n (5) and [Cu2(L3)]n (6) were successfully prepared by utilizing three bis(tetrazole) ligands [bis-(1H-tetrazol-5-ylmethyl)-amine (H3L1), bis-(1H-tetrazol-5-ylethyl)-amine (H3L2) and 1,5-bis(5-tetrazolo)-3-thiapentane (H2L3)], all of which have been characterized by elemental analyses, FT-IR spectroscopy, powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD), thermogravimetric analyses as well as single-crystal X-ray diffraction analyses showing different dimensionalities (0D, 1D and 3D). Complexes 1 and 2 are 0D structures, 1 shows a dinuclear structure, 2 displays two crystallographically different mononuclear structures, 1 and 2 are further assembled to form 3D supramolecular framework and 2D supramolecular network by hydrogen-bonding interactions, respectively. Complexes 3, 4 and 5 are 1D structures, 3 features a mononuclear unit and a 1D chain, which are arranged into 3D supramolecular architecture by hydrogen-bonding interactions, 4 presents a zigzag chain, 5 shows an infinite chain structure constructed from pentanuclear Cu(II) subunits and ClO4− anions. Complex 6 exhibits a 3D coordination framework based on cyclic [Cu4(L3)2] dimmer subunits as nodes possessing an 8-connected network topology with the point symbol {424·64}. Further, semiconductor behaviors, the solid-state luminescent properties of the complexes 1–3 and 6 were measured and studied seriously at room temperature.

1. Introduction

Over the past two decades, rational design and assembly of multitudinous solid coordination frameworks (SCFs) that have multiple and interesting topological architectures, have evoked considerable attention and SCFs are always applied to versatile applications in the areas of gas storage [1], magnetism [2], sensing [3], catalysis [4], and drug delivery [5] as functional materials. Lots of novel self-assembly SCFs have been synthesized continuously, recently [6]. Exploiting appropriate strategies in the configuration of intriguing SCFs with predictable motifs and good properties has been among the most compelling challenges up to now, which is closely related to versatile chemical environments, such as the choice of organic ligands, the counter ions, temperature, pH values, solvents and metal-to-ligand ratio [7], [8]. Therefore, the selection of organic ligands with appropriate sites capable of coordinating with metal centers is very crucial to manipulate SCFs with ordered diversified architectures and exciting functional properties [9], [10].

It's worth noting that much more interest has been paid to the tetrazole-based ligands as versatile building sticks, not only due to their variety of coordination modes and excellent coordination abilities but also because of their designable properties and potential applications, and the synthesis of 5-substituted 1H-tetrazoles is one of the hottest fields [11], [12]. It is well-known that these flexible ligands can bend or rotate freely and feature various coordination conformations during the assembly with metal centers, which usually result in unpredictable diversified frameworks with useful properties [13], [14], although it is very difficult to stabilize to construct porous SCFs with flexible ligands [15]. The bis(tetrazole) ligands in which N-donor rings are separated by alkyl (CH2)n spacers and N/S atoms are a good choice of flexible bridging ligands, which produce a large variety of possible bonding modes with nine coordination sites of two tetrazolate rings and one N/S donor, and may be good candidates to exhibit new SCFs possessing fascinating structures, such as novel helical and versatile interpenetrating structures [16], [17].

With this background information, our team have reported that we choose bis-(1H-tetrazol-5-ylmethyl)-amine (H3L1), bis-(1H-tetrazol-5-ylethyl)-amine (H3L2) and 1,5-bis(5-tetrazolo)-3-thiapentane (H2L3) (Chart 1) as flexible ligands to coordinate with zinc, cadmium, manganese and copper salts [18], [19]. In this work, we also focused our attention on utilizing the three ligands with zinc, cobalt and copper sources to prepare a series of coordination complexes in different system, namely, [Zn2(HL1)2(H2O)4]·H2O (1), [Co2(HL1)2]·TEA (2), [Co3(HL1)2(H2L1)2(H2O)4]n (3), [Cu(HL1)(H2O)2]n (4), {[Cu5(HL2)2(OH)4(ClO4)2]·4H2O}n (5) and [Cu2(L3)]n (6). These complexes have been structurally characterized by single-crystal X-ray diffraction, elemental analyses, FT-IR spectroscopy, powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) and thermogravimetric analyses (TGA). Interestingly, there exist hydrogen bonding interactions that play a significant role in offering the possibility for the construction of high-dimensional supramolecular frameworks in complexes 1–3. In addition, semiconductor behaviors, the solid-state luminescent properties of the complexes 1–3 and 6 were studied in detail.

2. Experimental section

2.1. Materials and general methods

All reagents used for the syntheses were commercially available and employed without further purification. The ligands H3L1, H3L2 and H2L3 used in this work were synthesized in a safe, convenient and environmentally friendly synthetic method according to the literature procedure [20]. Elemental analyses of C, H and N were determined with a Thermo Flash EA 1112-NCHS-O analyzer. IR spectra were measured on a Brucker Equinox 55 FT-IR spectrometer with KBr pellets in the range of 4000–400 cm−1. The X-ray powder diffraction (PXRD) was recorded on a Rigaku D/Max-2500 diffractometer at 40 kV, 100 mA for a Cu-target tube and a graphite monochromator. Thermogravimetric (TG) measurements were carried out on a Perkin-Elmer TG-7 thermal analyzer from 25 °C to 800 °C at the rate of 10 °C min−1 under the protection of argon atmosphere at the rate of 10 °C min−1. UV–vis absorption spectra were collected using a Hitachi UV-3010 UV–Vis Spectrophotometer. The fluorescence behavior of ligands H3L1 and H2L3 as well as complexes 1–3 and 6 were studied using a Hitachi F-4500 Fluorescence Spectrophotometer with a Xe arc lamp as the light source and bandwidths of 2.5 nm at room temperature. Fluorescence lifetime data were conducted on Fluorolog-3 Spectrophotometer.

2.3. X-ray crystallography

The single-crystal diffraction data of complexes 1–6 were recorded on a Bruker SMART APEX II CCD detector equipped with a graphite monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 A) by using a ω-φ scan technique at 293(2) K. The structures of 1–6 were solved by direct methods and refined anisotropically on F2 by the full-matrix least-squares technique using the SHELXTL program and SHELXL-97 [21]. The program SAINT [22] was used for integration of the diffraction profiles. The data for complexes were modified for absorption by using the program SADABS [23]. Non-hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic thermal parameters. Metal atoms in each complex were located from the E-maps. Most of hydrogen atoms were assigned to calculate positions and refined with fixed geometry with respect to their carrier atoms, and the water hydrogen atoms were located from difference maps. Crystallographic data and experimental details for structural analyses are summarized in Table 1.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Syntheses and general characterization

For syntheses of the three ligands, we used here a safer and exceptionally efficient process for transforming nitriles into tetrazoles in water with sodium azide and zinc salts. Complexes 1 and 2 are 0D structures, complexes 3, 4 and 5 are 1D structures, complex 6 features 3D network. So the result demonstrates that the rational selection of organic ligands with specific conformation and geometry is an effective approach to the construction of entangled motifs. Complexes 1–4 were synthesized by the evaporation of the solvent at room temperature, complexes 5 and 6 were prepared based on the solvothermal reaction under different conditions in this article. The systematical studies disclose that 1 and 4 in similar solvent system feature the different structures due to the coordination morphologies of the ligands and the radius of center metal ions. Complexes 2 and 3 having the same ligands and metal centers present different structures and coordination modes because of different synthetic conditions. Therefore many factors can affect the structural configuration of the complexes.

In the IR spectra, the broad absorption bands at 3246 cm−1, 3366 cm−1, 3393 cm−1 and 3368 cm−1 are assigned to the coordinated or free H2O molecules, respectively. The absorption bands appearing at 1079 cm−1 and 663 cm−1 are characteristic of the coordinated ClO4− anions. Complexes 1–6 all exhibit absorptions corresponding to the framework vibrations of tetrazole rings at about 1400–1500 cm−1. The bands about 3100 cm−1 are assignable to the stretching vibrations of the NH in coordinated ligands.

3.2. Crystal structures

3.2.1. Crystal structure of [Zn2(HL1)2(H2O)4]·H2O (1)

Complex 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic system P21/n, and selected bond lengths and bond angles are listed in Table S1 of Supplementary 1. There is one crystallographically independent Zn(II) ion, one HL1 ligand, two coordinated water molecules and one lattice water molecule in the asymmetric unit. As shown in Fig. 1a, in each distorted octahedral ZnN4O2 core, four nitrogen atoms (N1, N5, N6 and N7) are from two distinct HL1 ligands in the equatorial plane and two oxygen atoms (O1 and O2) are from two water molecules at the axial positions with τ = 0.16. The ZnO bond distances are 2.332(13) Å and 2.343(11) Å, while the bond lengths of ZnN are in the range of 2.284(11)–2.462(12) Å. The HL1 ligands adopt a μ2-κN1, N5, N6: κN7 mode to link two Zn(II) ions with the distance of 4.513(21) Å to form a dinuclear structure. Then, such units are interconnected via the O2H4W⋯N4#5 hydrogen bonding interactions to give an infinite 1D chain along the c-axis as seen in Fig. 1b, and the chains are further expanded by the O(1)–H(1W)⋯O(3)#2, O(1)–H(2W)⋯N(8)#3, O(2)–H(3W)⋯O(3)#4 and O(3)–H(5W)⋯N(2)#5 hydrogen-bonding interactions to generate a two-dimensional supramolecular sheet (Fig. 1c). Finally, the 3D supramolecular entangled net is an assembly of the sheets by means of the O3H6W⋯N9#6 hydrogen-bonding interactions (Fig. 1d). To better understand the nature of the intricate framework, the 3D supramolecular structure can be seen in a simplified way as a net with point symbol of {336·448·57} by considering the dinuclear structure as a 14-connected node and hydrogen-bonding as a linker (Fig. 1e). The hydrogen-bonding parameters were placed in Table S2 of Supplementary 1. Complex 1 and the complex Cd2(HL1)2(H2O)4·2H2O we have reported in this article [18] are isostructural with different metal ions, different number of lattice water molecules and different synthesis conditions.

3.2.2. Crystal structure of [Co2(HL1)2]·TEA (2)

Complex 2 which crystallizes in the triclinic system with the P-1 space group consists of two crystallographically distinct Co(II) ions, two unique HL1 ligands and one lattice triethylamine molecule. As shown in Fig. 2a, the six-coordinated Co1 ion adopts a distorted [CoN6] octahedral geometry, filled by six nitrogen atoms from two individual HL1 ligands. The Co2 ion is also six-coordinated by six nitrogen atoms from two independent HL1 ligands in Fig. 2b. The bond CoN distances around the Co1 ions are 1.899(13) Å–1.999(13) Å and around Co2 ions are 1.897(13)–1.981(13) Å, the NCoN bond angles are 83.4(5)–96.6(5)° for Co1 and 83.1(6)–96.9(6)° for Co2, respectively. HL2 ligands act as tridentate chelating ligands linking Co(II) center with the dihedral angle of tetrazolate rings in Co1 ions and Co2 ions being 72.9° and 84.9°, respectively. A detailed structural analysis reveals that the two crystallographically distinct Co(II) center units are alternately connected by via N5H1N⋯N13#3 hydrogen bonds to form a 2D supramolecular layer, having the shortest distance of 7.747(10) Å between the neighboring Co1 and Co2 ions (Fig. 2c).

3.2.3. Crystal structure of [Co3(HL1)2(H2L1)2(H2O)4]n (3)

Structural analysis reveals that complex 3 belongs to the triclinic system with the P-1 space group and features a mononuclear unit and 1D chain structure. There are three Co(II) ions, one HL1 ligand, one H2L1 ligand and two coordinated water molecules in the asymmetric unit of 3. Co1 ion is six-coordinated by six nitrogen atoms from two different HL1 ligands, exhibiting a distorted octahedron geometry (Fig. 3a). Co3 ion is located in a similar octahedron [CoN6] coordination environment (Fig. 3b). The octahedral coordination sphere around the Co2 ion is completed by four oxygen atoms from four associated water molecules, two nitrogen atoms from two HL1 ligands (Fig. 3b). All the CoO/N bond lengths fall in the range of 1.891(5)–2.120(6) Å. Moreover, there are two different ligands that have two different coordination modes: a μ1-κN1, N5, N9 chelating mode and a μ2-κN13, N14, N18: κN10 bridging mode. The ligands associate with Co2 ions and Co3 ions to form an infinite 1D polymetallic chain with the Co(2)⋯Co(3) distance of 5.957(45) Å. The mononuclear unit and 1D chain (Fig. 3c) are interconnected via the N14H14⋯N6#3 hydrogen-bonding interactions to give a 2D layer as seen in Fig. 3d. It should be noted that the 2D layer can form 3D supramolecular structure, which is constructed through interconnection of two kinds of hydrogen-bondings (N5H5⋯N15#5 and N3H3⋯O2#4) in Fig. 3e. With the Co1 ions and Co3 ions being as 6-connected nodes at the same time Co2 ions acting as 4-connected nodes for this framework, the whole supramolecular of complex 3 can be described as a binodal (4,6)-connected net with a point symbol of {32·44·54·64·7}2{32·62·72} by denoting hydrogen-bondings and ligands as connectors in Fig. 3f.

3.2.4. Crystal structure of [Cu(HL1)(H2O)2]n (4)

X-ray structural analysis reveals that 4 crystallizes in the orthorhombic space group Pbca, and the asymmetric unit consists of one Cu(II) ion, one HL1 ligand and two coordinated water molecules. As shown in Fig. 4a, the Cu(II) center displays a distorted octahedral sphere surrounded by two oxygen atoms (O1 and O2) occupying axial positions from two coordinated water molecules and four nitrogen atoms (N4, N5, N6, N9) locating the equatorial plane from two different ligands, of which, N4, N5, N6 that adopt the chelating coordination mode come from one HL1 ligand, nevertheless the remaining N9 is from another crystallographically independent ligand. The CuO bond lengths are in the normal range of 2.486(21)–2.715(25) Å. The CuN bond lengths are in the range of 1.961(18)–2.070(19) Å in the normal range slightly shorter than the values of related complexes [24]. In 4, the ligands adopt a μ2-κN4, N5, N6: κN9 coordination mode to link Cu(II) ions to form a zigzag chain with the adjacent Cu⋯Cu distance of 5.8914 Å along the b direction (Fig. 4b). The Cu⋯Cu⋯Cu angle through the ligand is 110.6 (6)°. The topology structure are shown in Fig. 4c, the Cu(II) centers representing the nodes and the ligands showing connectivity.

3.2.5. Crystal structure of {[Cu5(HL2)2(OH)4(ClO4)2]·4H2O}n (5)

Complex 5 crystallizes in the monoclinic P2(1)/n space group and the single crystal X-ray diffraction study reveals 5 has a 1D structure built by μ3-OH– anions and ClO4− anions linking Cu(II) ions. The asymmetric unit of 5 consists of three crystallographically independent Cu(II) ions, one HL2 ligand, one ClO4− anion, two OH− anions and two dissociative water molecules. As illustrated in Fig. 5a, Cu1 is coordinated by two nitrogen atoms from HL2 ligands, two oxygen atoms from ClO4− anions and two oxygen atoms from two μ3-OH– anions in a octahedral coordination environment. The six-coordinated Cu2 possesses a distorted octahedral [CuN2O4] environment similar to Cu1 except for one oxygen atom being from one μ3-OH– anion and another oxygen atom being from one μ1-OH– anion in Fig. 5b. Cu3 lying in square pyramid coordination geometry is coordinated by two nitrogen atoms from one ligand HL2, one oxygen atom from ClO4− anion and final oxygen atom from one μ3-OH– anion in Fig. 5c. The CuO distances are between 1.919(9) Å and 2.426(7) Å, and the lengths of CuO bond are slightly elongated compared with that of other similar complexes. The CuN distances are between 1.900(9) Å and 2.077(8) Å in the normal range [25], [19]. The ligand exhibits a μ4-κN1, N5, N6: κN2: κN7: κN8 mode to coordinate with one Cu1, two Cu2 and one Cu3. Simultaneously, the five adjacent Cu(II) ions (a pair of Cu1 and Cu2 ions and one Cu3 center) are connected with each other by μ3-OH– anions, μ3-ClO4– anions and the ligands to give a pentanuclear Cu(II) subunit in Fig. 5d. The connectivity of the subunits via Cu2ClO4– coordination interactions results in the formation of a chain structure along the a-axis as seen in Fig. 5e. Complex 5 and the complex {[Cu5(HL2)2(SO4)2(OH)2(H2O)2]·5H2O}n we have reported in that article [19] belong to the same crystal system and space group possessing different coordination anions and different number of lattice water molecules under different synthesis conditions.

3.2.6. Crystal structure of [Cu2(L3)]n (6)

Crystal structure determination reveals that complex 6 displays a 3D framework constructed from organic L3 ligands and Cu(I) ions in the orthorhombic crystal system of space group Cmca. In 6, there are two crystallographically independent Cu(I) ions (Cu1 and Cu2), which exhibit one kind of coordination geometry. The Cu1 ion adopts a four-coordinated distorted trigonal-pyramidal geometry of [CuN2S2] by two nitrogen atoms of one L3 ligand and two sulfur atoms of two L3 ligands (Fig. 6a). The four-coordinated Cu2 ion connects with four nitrogen atoms from four individual L3 ligands (Fig. 6b). The CuS bond distances are 2.316(11) Å and 2.394(10) Å, and the CuN bond lengths are in the range of 2.020(2)−2.086(2) Å. The bond angles around Cu(I) ions vary from 96.6(6)° to 115.2(9)°. In 6, there is only one type of L3 ligand, The L3 ligands adopt a μ6-κN4,N4#1,S1:κN2:κN2#3:κN1#4:κN1#5:κS1 bridging mode to link six Cu(I) ions. Interestingly, the N1 and N4 atoms of the tetrazole rings bridge four metal ions to construct a cyclic [Cu4(L3)2] dimmer subunit (Fig. 6c). The dimer subunits are linked together by Cu2 ions and nitrogen atoms of the ligands interactions to give an infinite 1D chain along the a-axis as seen in Fig. 6d and ulteriorly to afford a 2D layer structure in Fig. 6e. At the same time, the Cu2 centers are connected by the coordinated nitrogen atoms of the ligands to form one dimensional beaded chains, with the shorter Cu⋯Cu separation of 3.764 (4) Å along the b-axis. Then, the 2D layers induce a porous complicated 3D network through interactional Cu1 ions and sulfur atoms of ligands in Fig. 6f. If the dimmer subunits are assigned as eight-connected nodes, the overall framework can be viewed as a 3D 8-connected topology with the point symbol {424·64} (Fig. 6g). The calculation by PLATON demonstrates a total void volume of 176.8 Å3 (8.1% of the unit cell volume).

3.3. Powder X-ray diffraction and thermal stabilities of the complexes

Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) experiments have been carried out for 1–6 at room temperature in order to check the phase purity of these complexes. As shown in Fig. S2 of Supplementary 2, the peak positions of the experimental and simulated PXRD patterns are in good agreement with each other, indicating that the crystal structures are truly representative of the bulk crystal products. The difference in intensity of some diffraction peaks may be attributed to the preferred orientation of the crystalline powder samples.

To further estimate the thermal stability of these complexes, their thermal behaviors were studied by thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) under argon environment with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 from room temperature to 800 °C as shown in Fig. S3 of Supplementary 2. The TG analyses of 1 and 4 demonstrate a curve gradient phase about the weight loss of 14.37% in the temperature range of 100–250 °C for 1 and loss of 12.93% in the temperature range of 110–200 °C for 4 corresponding to the loss of coordinated water molecules and lattice water molecules respectively (Calcd. 15.55% for 1 and 12.13% for 4). After that, a weight loss rapidly occurs from 325 °C to 600 °C for 1 and from 270 °C to 650 °C for 4 where the ligands are destroyed. The percentages of residual weight obtained from the graph (27.21% for 1 and 32.93% for 4) are in well agreement with the calculated value of 28.10% for 1 and 28.54% for 4 indicating that the final products obtained are zinc oxide and copper oxide (ZnO and CuO), respectively. The complex 2 experiences almost one-step mass loss from 150 °C to 600 °C, which can be dued to the thermal decomposition of the ligands and triethylamine molecules. The remaining fraction is 17.37% of the initial one, which is well consistent with the expected value for the formation of a stoichiometric quantity of Co (Calcd. 20.42%). Under heating from room temperature, the compositions of the framework 3 and 5 begin to collapse and the final mass remnant of 27.84% at 700 °C and 37.50% at 700 °C represent the deposition of CoO and CuO compared with the calculated value of 23.24% and 37.13%, respectively. The framework structure of 6 maintains stability up to 300 °C, then as the temperature increases, a series of the decomposition of organic ligands commences, expressing one-step weight loss. The experiment result illustrates that the complex 6 exhibits the highest thermal stability among 1–6, perhaps arising from 3D SCFs normally showing higher robustness than the 0D, 1D and 2D ones.

3.4. Semiconductor behaviors

The solid state UV–vis absorption spectra of 1−3 and 6 were measured at room temperature (in Fig. S4 of Supplementary 2). 1–3 and 6 manifest stronger absorption bands at 299, 255, 260 and 292 nm in the scale of 200–400 nm, respectively, ascribed to the intraligand charge transitions. Moreover, the band gap energy of these materials can be calculated according to the formula: Eg = 1240/λg (eV), where Eg is the band gap energy (eV) and λg is the absorption edge wavelength (nm). As depicted in Fig. S5 of Supplementary 2, the Eg values are 3.60 eV for 1, 3.50 eV for 2, 3.69 eV for 3 and 3.47 eV for 6, singly, which may be beneficial to promising semiconductors.

3.5. Fluorescent properties of the complexes1-3 and 6

It is universally acknowledged that metal coordination frameworks possess excellent luminescence properties [26] with potential applications such as chemical sensors, photochemistry, electroluminescence display, and so on. For the above reason, the solid-state photoluminescence properties of complexes 1–3 and 6 as well as the corresponding ligands H3L1 and H2L3 have been investigated at room temperature. As shown in Fig. S6 of Supplementary 2, excitation of the solid samples of H3L1 ligand at 340 nm produces blue luminescence peak with a maximum at 417 nm, which can be attributed to the n → π* or π → π* transition in ligand-centered electronic transitions [27], [18]. Excitations of the samples of 1, 2 and 3 at 340 nm, 275 nm and 275 nm produce luminescence with the maxima peaks at 414 nm, 395 nm and 390 nm, respectively. This research shows the emission spectra of complex 1 and H3L1 ligand exhibit similar shapes. Since the d10 metal ions are difficult to oxidize or to reduce due to their configurations, the emission band of 1 is neither metal-to-ligand charge transfer (MLCT) nor ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT) in nature. It can probably be assigned to the intraligand (IL) π → π* transitions [28], [18]. Compared with the maximum emission of the ligand, the obvious blue-shift of 2 and 3 can be considered to mainly originate from the change of the rigidity by the metal coordination. The emission spectrum of H2L3 ligand excited at 330 nm displays emission maxima at 399 nm covering the blue region, and complex 6 shows fluorescent emission maxima peak at 510 nm under excitation with 330 nm light. The obvious red-shift in complex 6 compared to the band of the precursor probably results from the fact that the coordination of the precursor to the Cu(I) ions. The decrement of luminescence of the four complexes can be attributed to the binding of ligands to the metal centers, which reduces the rigidity of the ligand and increases the loss of energy by radiationless decay. In addition, organic ligands, their coordination modes and different metals, evidently affect the emission wavelength and luminescence performance [29].

The time-resolved emission measurements of 1−3, 6 and H2L3 ligand were used to probe further at room temperature because of the fluorescence lifetime of H3L1 ligand we have reported in this paper [18]. The curves of luminescent lifetimes reveal triple exponential decays for 1–3, H2L3 ligand and 6 (in Fig. S7 of Supplementary 2), and the corresponding lifetimes are 0.0513, 1.280 and 1.713 ns for 1, 0.600, 5.402 and 0.345 ns for 2, 0.723, 0.314 and 5.812 ns for 3, 0.0194, 0.444 and 3.459 ns for H3L1 ligand as well as 0.181, 0.487 and 0.466 ns for 6, respectively. The above observations suggest that these polymeric complexes may be suitable candidates for potential photoactive materials.

4. Conclusion

As a part of our ongoing investigation, we successfully obtained six complexes by using Zn(II), Cu(II), Co(II) and Cu(I) ions and three ligands with distinct structures possessing different dimensionalities. Complex 1 displays a dinuclear structure, and 2 features two crystallographically different mononuclear structures, 3 shows a mononuclear unit and a 1D chain, complexes 4 and 5 present an infinite chain. Complex 6 exhibits a complicated 3D framework with the point symbol {424·64}. So the spacer length of bis(tetrazole) flexible ligands and synthetic system of these complexes are important influence factors in the construction of complexes. Complexes 1–3 can give rise to the formation of the complex H-bonded nets by strong intermolecular hydrogen bonds. Therefore, hydrogen-bonding interactions have significant influence on the construction of supramolecular structure. The TG results exhibit that normally the 3D complex is more stable than the 0D or 1D complex. Furthermore, the semiconductor behaviors were detailedly investigated, which may demonstrate they may be promising semiconductor materials. The research of the solid-state luminescent properties for 1–3 and 6 indicates that the complexes were the potential candidates in the optical area.
