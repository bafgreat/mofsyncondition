Influence of the solvent in the formation of different 1D and 2D coordination polymers from the reaction of copper(II) phthalate with pyrazole

Abstract

From the reaction of Cu(Pht)(H2O) (Pht = o-benzene dicarboxylate) with pyrazole (Hpz), four different 1D or 2D Coordination Polymers (CPs), [Cu(Pht)(Hpz)2]·CH2Cl2, 1, [{Cu(Pht)(Hpz)4}3]·3.5Hpz·H2O, 2, [{Cu(Pht)(Hpz)2}2·0.5H2O], 3, and [Cu(Pht)(Hpz)2], 4, where Pht dianions bridge two or three mononuclear Cu(Hpz)x moieties (x = 2, 4), as well as the compound [Cu(Pht)(Hpz)4]·MeOH, 5, have been obtained, depending on the solvent employed. All compounds are air stable and have been characterized through elemental analyses and spectral determinations (IR, ESI-MS, magnetic susceptibility) and 1–4 also by single crystal X-ray diffraction studies. The self-assemblies of compounds 1–4 in different solvents generate different 1D and 2D CPs as a consequence of the presence of different crystallization solvent molecules in the supramolecular cavities.

1. Introduction

In the past century, at the end of the eighties, Robson and Hoskins [1] put in evidence the possibility to obtain solid polymeric materials by linking metal centers with polytopic ligands, thus starting the coordination polymers (CPs) age. From that moment the number of studies on these materials, in some cases referred to as metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) [2], strongly increased, due to the fact that these compounds are promising materials for applications in gas storage, gas purification, anion exchange, heterogeneous catalysis, etc. [3].

In recent years, we have studied the interaction of some transition metal ions with carboxylates and pyrazole (Hpz), synthesizing different CPs. Besides zinc(II), cadmium(II) and mercury(II) carboxylates [4] we have mainly studied the reactions of copper(II) carboxylates with Hpz, carried out in protic solvents (water, alcohols) or in MeCN, obtaining CPs based on the trinuclear triangular [Cu3(μ3-OH)(μ-pz)3] [5] or Cu(pz)2 [6] Secondary Building Units, (SBUs) [7]. In numerous cases we observed that reaction conditions (solvent, reaction or crystallization temperature, reagents molar ratios, etc.) can largely influence, often serendipitously, the results, in terms both of stoichiometry and topology [8]. Moreover, we have also employed some bicarboxylate anions such as fumarate, 2-methylfumarate [9] and succinate [10], obtaining CPs based on the above mentioned trinuclear triangular CuII clusters and/or mononuclear Cu(Hpz)x SBUs.

Here we report the synthesis and crystallographic characterization of a series of CPs obtained by treating, in different solvents, Cu(Pht)(H2O) [Pht = o-benzene dicarboxylate, (phthalate) anion] with Hpz.

2. Experimental

2.1. Materials and methods

All chemicals were purchased from Aldrich and used without further purification. Cu(Pht)(H2O) was prepared according to the reported procedure [11]. All the reactions and manipulations were carried out in the air. Elemental analyses (C, H, N) were performed with a Fisons Instruments 1108 CHNS-O elemental analyzer. Infrared spectra were recorded with a Nicolet A-VATAR 320 FT-IR spectrometer. Positive electrospray mass spectra were obtained with a Series 1100 MSI detector HP spectrometer using MeOH as mobile phase. Solutions for electrospray ionization mass spectrometry (ESI-MS) were prepared using reagent grade methanol, water and/or acetonitrile, and obtained data (m/z and intensities) were compared with those calculated by using the IsoPro isotopic abundance simulator [12]. The reported m/z values correspond to the most intense signal of the isotopic clusters. The magnetic susceptibilities were measured at room temperature (20–28 °C) by the Gouy method with a Sherwood Scientific magnetic balance MSB-Auto, using HgCo(NCS)4 as calibrant and corrected for diamagnetism with the appropriate Pascal constants. The magnetic moments (in μB) were calculated from the equation μeff = 2.84(XmcorrT)1/2.

2.2. Synthesis of [Cu(Pht)(Hpz)2]·CH2Cl2, 1

To a suspension of 628 mg of Cu(Pht)(H2O) (2.6 mmol) in 20 mL of CH2Cl2 a large excess of Hpz (1.575 g, 23.1 mmol) dissolved in 10 mL of CH2Cl2 was added. The mixture was stirred for ca. 30 min and filtered obtaining a blue solution that was let to evaporate slowly in the air. In a few days pale-blue hexagonal platelets, suitable for a single-crystal XRD determination, were obtained, which were separated from mother liquors, washed with CH2Cl2 and dried in the air.

2.7. Crystallographic data collection and structure determination

The X-ray intensity data of compounds 1–4 were measured on a Bruker Apex II CCD diffractometer. Cell dimensions and the orientation matrix were initially determined from a least-squares refinement on reflections measured in three sets of 20 exposures, collected in three different ω regions, and eventually refined against all data. A full sphere of reciprocal space was scanned by 0.3°ω steps. The software smart [13] was used for collecting frames of data, indexing reflections and determination of lattice parameters. The collected frames were then processed for integration by the saint program [13], and an empirical absorption correction was applied using sadabs [14]. The structures were solved by direct methods (sir 97) [15] and subsequent Fourier syntheses and refined by full-matrix least-squares on F2 (shelxtl) [16] using anisotropic thermal parameters for all non-hydrogen atoms. In the asymmetric unit of compound 1 a CH2Cl2 solvent molecule is present whereas in compound 3 half water molecule is found. In the asymmetric unit of 2 in addition to one crystallization water molecule also 3.5 Hpz molecules are observed. The aromatic hydrogen atoms were placed in calculated positions, constrained to ride on their parent atoms and refined isotropically with Uiso(H) = 1.2 Uiso(parent atom). The water hydrogen atoms in 3 were located in difference Fourier maps and were refined with constraints on their positions and Uiso values. The molecular graphics were generated by using Mercury 3.1 program [17] Crystal data and details of the data collections for compounds 1–4 are reported in Table 1.

3. Results and discussion

In the course of our studies on the interaction of copper(II) bicarboxylate with Hpz we succeeded in the synthesis of CPs based on the trinuclear triangular [Cu3(μ3-OH)(μ-pz)3] fragment (Chart 1a), whose double positive charge is balanced by rigid [9] and flexible [10] dianions, provided that the COO groups are placed on the opposite sites of the spacers (Chart 1b–d). Here we report the results obtained by using instead the phthalate dianion (Chart 1e) for which, due to steric reasons, the formation of CPs based on the same trinuclear triangular moiety is highly improbable. Actually, by reacting Cu(Pht)(H2O) with Hpz, we obtained, depending on the solvent employed, four CPs, where Pht dianions bridge two or three mononuclear Cu(Hpz)x moieties (x = 2, 4), as well as the compound [Cu(Pht)(Hpz)4]·MeOH.

In detail, by treating a suspension of Cu(Pht)(H2O) in dichloromethane (DCM) with a large excess of Hpz (Cu:Hpz ∼1:8), a blue solution was obtained, that was let to evaporate slowly in the air yielding, by fractional crystallization, two different compounds. The first one, [Cu(Pht)(Hpz)2]·CH2Cl2, 1, formed as pale blue hexagonal platelets in low yield (ca. 9%), while from the mother liquors, dark blue needles of a second compound, [{Cu(Pht)(Hpz)4}3]·3.5Hpz·H2O, 2, were obtained in good yield and SC-XRD determination were carried out on both compounds.

In Fig. 1 (left) the asymmetric unit of compound 1 is shown, from which the different coordinative behavior of the two COO moieties of the Pht dianion is evident. While the O3–C8–O4 carboxylate coordinates in a monoatomic way to Cu1ii [O3–Cu1ii 2.028(3) Å, symmetry code (ii) −x, y − 1/2, −z − 1/2],1 the oxygens of the second carboxylate group bridges in a syn-syn fashion two symmetry related Cu1 ions, thus generating an 8-membered metallacycle (Fig. 1 right), having an inversion center located in its center. The slightly distorted TBP coordination geometry of the two Cu1 ions is completed by the pyrazole nitrogens N1 and N3 in axial positions. A crystallization DCM molecule, which does not show any particular interaction with the complex, is also present in the asymmetric unit. Finally, a N-H···OH hydrogen bond involving N2 and O4 likely reinforces the assembly. Incidentally, O4 (as well as the symmetrically related O4i) is also engaged into another weaker N-H···O hydrogen bond with N4 of the same moiety (see Fig. S1 of Supplementary material). All the other geometrical features are quite normal and the relevant bond distances and angles are reported in Table S1 of Supplementary material.

As a consequence of the different coordinative behavior of the two carboxylates of each Pht dianion, compound 1 acts as a SBU, since the 8-membered metallacycles, that are almost perpendicular each other (see Fig. S2), are joined through the C6H4 phthalate ring, leading to the formation of the 1D CP shown in Fig. 2.

On the other hand, the complete self-assembly produces also 28-membered macrocycles that, together with the 8-membered metallacycles, give rise to 2D CPs lying parallel to the bc plane (Fig. 3). From the topological point of view, we can consider the Cu and Pht ions as 3-connected nodes building a uninodal 2-periodic fes net (point symbol {4.82}) [18].

Finally, the above defined sheets pack together generating channels running parallel to the crystallographic b axis, that are occupied by guest DCM molecules (Fig. 4).

The mother liquors obtained upon separation of 1 were let to evaporate and blue crystals of [{Cu(Pht)(Hpz)4}3]·3.5Hpz·H2O, 2, separated in fairly good yield. A deep examination of the solid state structure of 2 shows that all three Cu(II) centres adopt a distorted octahedral geometry being coordinated by four pyrazoles and two oxygens pertaining to different Pht ligands but there are small differences among the three Cu(Pht)(Hpz)4 moieties constituting 2 arising from strong non equivalent intramolecular N-H···O hydrogen bonds. In fact the N-bound protons of the pyrazoles coordinated to Cu2 are engaged in five intramolecular N-H···O hydrogen bonds with five oxygens of the two Pht ligands [N16-H16A···O2, N16-H16A···O3, N10-H10A···O6, N12-H12A···O5, N14-H14A···O4] whereas all four N-bound protons of the pyrazoles coordinated to Cu3 establish intramolecular N-H···O hydrogen bonds [N20-H20A···O8, N18-H18A···O10, N24-H24A···O10, N22-H22A···O11] with three oxygens of the two Pht ligands and only two N-bound protons coordinated to Cu1 are involved in intramolecular N-H···O hydrogen bonds with two oxygens of two Pht ligands [N6-H6A···O2, N8-H8···O12″]. In Fig. 5 the asymmetric unit of 2 is reported, while the above indicated intramolecular H-bonds are shown in Fig. S3.

Anyhow, the bond distances and angles are quite normal for this kind of structures and are reported in Fig.S4 and in Table S2. This linear trinuclear assembly behaves as a SBU generating 1D CPs, one of which is partially shown in Fig. 6.

In compound 2 in addition to the already mentioned strong, intramolecular N-H···O hydrogen bonds involving coordinated pyrazoles and Pht ligands, also free crystallization pyrazoles (some of which display partial occupancy or orientational disorder) and water molecules are engaged in intermolecular N-H···O and N-H···N bonds (see Fig. S5). A further H-bond likely connects crystallization pyrazole and water [O13···N30 2.61(3) Å].2 The 1D CPs further self-assemble through N–H⋯N hydrogen bonds involving a pyrazole coordinated to Cu1 and a guest Hpz pertaining to another 1D chain [N4⋯N25i 2.977(9) Å, N4–H4⋯N25i 139°; symmetry code (i) x + 3/2, -y + 3/2, z + 1/2] (Fig. S6). By this way 2D networks whose stacking produces the final crystal packing of 2, formed (see Figs. S7–S8).

When the reaction of Cu(Pht)(H2O) with a large excess of Hpz (Cu:Hpz ∼1:8) was carried out in EtOH a blue solution was obtained, from which, by slow evaporation in the air, the compound [{Cu(Pht)(Hpz)2}{Cu(Pht)(Hpz)2}]·0.5H2O,·3, crystallized in good yield.

At a first sight, the molecular structure of 3 seems almost identical to that of 1, but a deep examination reveals instead that the structure reported in Fig. 7 represents the asymmetric unit of 3, due to the fact that the two Cu(Pht)(Hpz)2 units are slightly different, as witnessed by the bond lengths and angles reported in Table S3, and by the fact that only one half crystallization water molecule is present in the dinuclear structure.

Nevertheless, the connections generating the 8-membered metallacycle are almost identical to those present in compound 1, as well as the coordination schemes of Cu1 and Cu2, that have both a slightly distorted TBP coordination geometry. O1–C1–O2 and O5–C9–O6 generate the 8-membered metallacycle joining Cu1 and Cu2 through two slightly different syn–syn bridges. Pyrazole nitrogens occupy the axial positions in the TBP of each copper ion and the coordination schemes are completed by the bonds of Cu1 to O3 and Cu2 to O7 of two distinct dinuclear assemblies [Cu1–O3iii 2.011 (3) Å, symmetry code (iii) −x + 1/2, y − 1/2, −z − 3/2, Cu2−O7iv 2.020(2) Å, symmetry code (iv) −x + 1/2, y + 1/2, −z − 5/2].3 Four intramolecular N–H⋯O bonds involving the pyrazole N atoms are also present, a feature very similar to that observed in compound 1. Finally, the crystallization water molecule is involved into an O–H⋯O hydrogen bond with the carboxylate oxygen O3 [O9⋯O3 2.855(7) Å, O9–H9A⋯O3 138(9)°]. Compound 3 self-assembles forming a 1D CP by joining the above described 8-membered metallacycles (Fig. 8), that are almost perpendicular each other, adopting a motif already observed in compound 1.

Moreover, a series of these parallel 1D CPs further self-assembles generating 2D CPs sheets, one of which is depicted in Fig. 9, with the same underlying fes net found in 1. In this case, when looking along the crystallographic a axis, two independent nets are arranged in an alternate stacking.

Finally, similarly to 1, the above defined planes pack closely together, but in this case channels are not present, even though the guest water molecules are fictionally removed as in Fig. S9.

Contrarily to the case of compound 1, from the mother liquors of the synthesis of 3 we were unable to obtain any other pure crystalline phase.

When the reaction of Cu(Pht)(H2O) with Hpz (Cu:Hpz ∼1:8), was carried out in MeOH it was possible to isolate some crystals suitable for a SC-XRD determination. The molecular structure of this compound, [{Cu(Pht)(Hpz)2}{Cu(Pht)(Hpz)2}], 4, is shown in Fig. 10. It consists of two non-equivalent Cu(Pht)(Hpz)2 moieties bridged through one Pht dianion. Both Cu1 and Cu2 exhibit distorted square planar geometries. Besides the coordination by the pyrazolate nitrogens N1 and N3, Cu1 is involved in the monoatomic coordination by O1 and O7 [O1–Cu1 1.929(4), O7–Cu1 1.952(4) Å]4 pertaining, respectively, to the O1–C1–O2 and O7–C16–O8 carboxylate groups of two different Pht dianions. In the case of Cu2, the distorted square planar geometry is due to the coordination by N5 and N7 pyrazolate nitrogens, to the monoatomic coordination of O5 pertaining to the O5–C9–O6 carboxylate group and to one oxygen (O3i) pertaining to another dinuclear unit [O3i–Cu2 1.942(4) Å, symmetry code (i) x + 1, y, z]5. All the other geometrical features are quite normal and the relevant bond distances and angles are reported in Table S4.

Noteworthy, the dinuclear assembly is likely reinforced by the presence of four intramolecular N-H···O hydrogen bonds involving the carboxylate oxygens O2, O4, O6 and O8 and the four NH groups (see Fig. S10). The dinuclear {Cu(Pht)(Hpz)2}{Cu(Pht)(Hpz)2} assembly acts as a SBU, being Cu2 and the O3–C8–O4 carboxylate the starting points for the generation of a waved 1D CP, running parallel to the crystallographic a axis, as partially sketched in Fig. 11.

Besides the already mentioned “intrachain” N–H⋯O interactions with O6 and O2, the N8–H8N and N4–H4N groups are also involved into two “interchain” N–H⋯O hydrogen bonds, with the O6 and O2 atoms pertaining to the two adjacent 1D CPs (see Fig. S11) thus generating the supramolecular 2D network of parallel waved chains shown in Fig. 12.

Finally, these parallel, H-bonds based, networks pack together generating the crystal packing shown in Fig. S12.

Solid-state IR spectra of compounds 1 and 3 are very similar each other (see Experimental) and are in agreement with their crystal structures, showing in both cases the presence of carboxylate ions that are differently coordinated to Cu ions. In particular, taking into account that the carboxylates can coordinate to the metal ions in a bidentate or monodentate fashion, on the basis of the Δ criterion [Δ = difference between νasym(COO) and νsym(COO)]19 we can confirm that in compounds 1 and 3 the two carboxylate moieties of the Pht adopt bridging mono- and bidentate coordination modes with Δ values falling around ca. 170 cm−1.

The absorptions at 1549, 1522, and 1480 cm−1 for compound 2, and 1576, 1554, 1486 cm−1 for compound 5, can be assigned to the CO stretching vibrations of the carboxylate ions, which in these cases act as unidentate unsymmetric ligands and, for compound 2, confirm X-ray crystal structural features. Moreover, the IR spectra of 1–3 and 5 all show signals at 1610 cm−1 [ν(CC), ν(CN)] and 760 cm−1 due to the pyrazole ligand. The presence of an N–H stretching vibration at 3200 cm−1 suggests the coordination of neutral Hpz. A broad absorption centered at 3400 cm−1, due to the H-bonded OH group of methanol, or water, is observed in 3 and 5.

The positive ESI mass spectra performed in methanol/acetonitrile solutions of compounds 1–3 and 5 show the same pattern for the main signals. Even though it is well known that in ESI-MS conditions aggregation phenomena, leading to oligomeric species, may occur [20], in these cases only isotopic clusters corresponding to mononuclear copper species are present. The most intense signal at 364.0 m/z is a cluster exactly simulated by [Cu(Pht)(HPz)2 + H]+, suggesting that the Cu(Pht)(HPz)2 moiety is particularly stable. Moreover, signals deriving from the free Hpz ligand are present. An additional signal at 258.0 m/z, always present in compounds 1–3 and 5, is likely due to [Cu(HPz)2(MeCN)(H2O)]+ species, where copper(II) is reduced at copper(I) maybe inside the ESI instrument, a feature already observed before [21].

The magnetic susceptibility recorded at room temperature for the complexes 1 and 5 (2.30 and 2.33 μB, respectively) are close to the range expected for paramagnetic mononuclear CuII species. Magnetic susceptibility value of 2 (4.28 μB) and 3 (3.13 μB) corresponds to three and two independent copper(II) ions respectively, in agreement with the X-ray structure, that show a linear trinuclear assembly for compound 2 and dinuclear assembly for compound 3.

The thermal behavior of compounds 1–4 is shown in Figs. S13–16. Complex 1 undergoes a mass loss of ca. 18% between 100 and 120 °C, which corresponds to the elimination of the not-coordinated dichloromethane molecule (calcd 18.9%). This process is accompanied by a significant lowering of the temperature in the furnace, likely due to a strong endothermic desolvation process. A second mass loss of ca. 32% occurs between 150 and 210 °C, which seems to correspond to a loss of the phthalate group, immediately followed (or accompanied) by a third decomposition step likely due to the two pyrazole loss. An analogous mechanism seems to be present in the decomposition pattern of compound 4. In this case the compound likely loses the phthalate and one pyrazolyl groups at ca. 200 °C, accompanied from the formation of the polynuclear species [Cu(pz)(OH)] that likely converts to CuO at ca. 280 °C. In the case of 3, the first step, occurring at 100 °C is due to the loss of the water molecule. However, in this case the second step seems due to the two pyrazoles loss (ca. 35% with respect to a calculated 36.5%), also in this cases immediately followed at ca. 200 °C by the phthalate loss (43%). The pattern observed for compound 2 is the more complicated one. After a first step that clearly can be assigned to the loss of the uncoordinated pyrazole and water molecules, occurring at ca 100 °C, six different decomposition steps are found, likely due to progressive pyrazole and phthalate loosing, not always clearly identified, since some steps indicate a loss of 3.5% corresponding to ca. one Hpz, while some others to two or more pyrazoles and one or two phthalate in the trinuclear unit. On the other hand, these differences may be justified by the different H-bond interactions involving coordinated pyrazoles, reported in the structure description of 2.

These data demonstrate that compounds 1–4 are not thermally stable and also that when solvent molecules are present decomposition steps are found also at ca 100 °C. Really, the more stable complex is compound 4, where crystallization solvent molecules are not present, decomposing only at 200 °C and showing the simplest decomposition pattern.

4. Conclusion

The reaction between copper(II) phthalate and Hpz leads to different crystal structures, depending on the different solvent employed, though not necessarily incorporating the solvents as crystallization molecules. Despite the rigid ortho structure of phthalate anion, mono- and bi-dimensional CPs have been obtained. In all the cases the reactions were carried out at room temperature with the same Hpz:Cu ratio, (ca. 8), thus the different results are reasonably due only to the different solvents employed. In compounds 1 [Cu(Pht)(Hpz)2]·CH2Cl2, 3 [{Cu(Pht)(Hpz)2}2·0.5H2O] and 4 [Cu(Pht)(Hpz)2], obtained as first products in the reactions carried out in DCM, EtOH, and MeOH respectively, the species Cu(Pht)(Hpz)2 is always formed. While 4 self-assembles generating a mono-dimensional CP, 1 and 3, forming 8-membered metallacycles, self-assemble as different solvates of the same 2D CP. Interestingly, the different decoration of the isostructural 2D networks by DCM or H2O molecules, respectively, leads to the formation of a different polymorphic packing. Finally, from mother liquors of 1 and 4, compounds 2 [{Cu(Pht)(Hpz)4}3]·3.5Hpz·H2O, and 5 [Cu(Pht)(Hpz)4]·MeOH, both containing the species Cu(Pht)(Hpz)4, are obtained, in accordance with a higher Hpz content in the mother liquors after the formation of 1 and 4. While elemental analysis, IR and ESI–MS data pertaining to compound 5 are in agreement to the proposed formulation, the lack of its crystal structure does not let to compare its structure with that of the mono-dimensional CP 2. In conclusion, these results evidence that, as expected, it is likely impossible, or at least quite difficult, to obtain trinuclear triangular CuII pyrazolate derivatives, analogous to those previously synthesized by us [5], [8], [9], [10] when the bicarboxylate ion employed is the sterically demanding phthalate. On the other hand, by using different solvents it is possible to address the synthesis toward the formation of different compounds, even though it is difficult to forecast, a priori, the final structure of the obtained compounds and their assembly to generate CPs, as well as the different supramolecular packing.
