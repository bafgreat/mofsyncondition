Introduction

In recent decades, crystal engineering in the basis of metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) has caused considerable attention, not only for their elegant framework topologies but also due to their applications in practical production, such as molecular magnetism, catalysis, host–guest chemistry, and optoelectronic devices, etc.1–3 In this regard, the rational design and construction of new materials with specific properties has become a particularly important and topical subject.4,5 In order to assemble these molecular structures, carboxylate ligands are frequently employed as bridging ligands to construct coordination polymers. They fit the synthetic flexibility and variation of the metal centers due to their versatile coordination properties and high structural stability.6–8 Moreover, some carboxylate ligands, especially those with polycarboxylate ends acting as bridging mediators between the divalent transition metal centers can transfer or separate the magnetic communications.9–11 These polycarboxylates may exhibit versatile coupling to mediate magnetic interactions owing to their different coordination modes, i.e., syn-syn, syn-anti, anti-anti, etc., accomplishing the transmission of magnetic coupling to different degrees.12

On the other hand, a fairly effective way to construct desirable networks is the introduction of multidentate flexible N-donor ligands, such as 1,2-bis(imidazol-1-yl)ethane, 1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)-ethane, 1,3-bis(4-pyridyl)-propane and so on. Their flexibility can result not only in gauche and anti conformations in coordination, but also in free rotation to meet the requirements of the coordination geometry of the metal ions in the assembly process. Thus, they might favor the further minimization of intercluster or interchain magnetic exchanges joined by carboxylates and the elongation of the metal building units into higher-dimensional frameworks.13,14 Combining chiral dicarboxylates and bis(imidazole) ligands with mixed-metal ions, Gao at. al.3h recently reported a series of chiral molecular magnetic materials. Zeng and his group3i reported a 2D pillared bilayer coordination polymer by the reaction of 5-aminoisophthalate and 4,4′-bipyridyl with a Co salt, which exhibits reversible single-crystal-to-single-crystal transformations upon removing and rebinding the coordinated waters as well as replacing them with MeOH and EtOH from the solvent. Considering all the aspects stated above, our idea in this work is elaborately to select two kinds of ligands (Scheme 1) to construct CoII and NiII carboxylate complexes exhibiting interesting magnetic properties. Herein, four complexes, {[Co(mip)(bpa)]}n (1), {[Co(mip)(bpp)]}n (2), {[Ni(mip)(bpa)]}n (3) and {[Ni(mip)(bpp)(H2O)]}n (4), have been synthesized from the reactions of the Co(ii) or Ni(ii) with 5-methylisophthalic acid in the presence of flexible N-donor auxiliary ligands. Their magnetic properties have also been investigated.

Experimental

Materials and general methods

All reagents used in the syntheses were of analytical grade. The 5-methylisophthalic acid, 1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)-ethane and 1,3-bis(4-pyridyl)-propane were purchased from Aldrich. All other reagents were commercially available and were used as received without further purification. Elemental analyses for carbon, hydrogen, and nitrogen atoms were performed on a Vario EL III elemental analyzer. The infrared spectra (4000 ∼ 600 cm−1) were recorded by using KBr pellet on an Avatar 360 E. S. P. IR spectrometer. The powder X-ray diffraction patterns were recorded with a Rigaku D/Max 3III diffractometer with a scanning rate of four degrees per minute. Variable-temperature magnetic susceptibilities were measured using a MPMS-7 SQUID magnetometer. Diamagnetic corrections were made with Pascal's constants for all constituent atoms.

Preparation of 1–4

{[Co(mip)(bpa)]}n (1)

A mixture of 5-methylisophthalic acid (0.1 mmol, 18 mg), 1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)-ethane (0.1 mmol, 18 mg) and Co(OAc)2·4H2O (0.1 mmol, 24 mg) in distilled water (15 mL) was placed in a Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel, heated to 120 °C for 3 d, and then cooled to room temperature over 24 h. Red block crystals of 1 were obtained in 35% yield (based on Co). Elemental analysis (%): calcd for C21H18CoN2O4 (Mr = 421.3): C 59.87, H 4.31, N 6.65, found: C 59.81, H 4.40, N 6.56. IR (cm−1): 3417 (m), 2933 (m), 1615 (s), 1580 (s), 1428 (s), 1385 (s), 1116 (w), 1016 (m), 824 (s), 782 (s).

{[Co(mip)(bpp)]}n (2)

2 was synthesized in the similar way as that described for 1, except that 1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)-ethane was replaced by 1,3-bis(4-pyridyl)-propane. Yield: 46% (based on Co). Elemental analysis (%): calcd for C22H20CoN2O4 (Mr = 435.3): C 60.70, H 4.63, N 6.43, found: C 60.61, H 4.69, N 6.37. IR (cm−1): 3423 (s), 2925 (m), 1615 (s), 1547 (w), 1420 (s), 1389 (s), 1110 (w), 1014 (m), 818 (m), 778 (s).

Synthesis of {[Ni(mip)(bpa)]}n (3)

3 was synthesized in the similar way as that described for 1, except that Co(OAc)·4H2O was replaced by Ni(OAc)2·4H2O. Yield: 42% (based on Ni). Elemental analysis (%): calcd for C21H18NiN2O4 (Mr = 421.08): C 59.90, H 4.31, N 6.65, found: C 60.01, H 4.29, N 6.57. X-Ray crystallographic data collection and structural determination

Single crystal X-ray diffraction analyses of 1–4 were carried out on a Bruker SMART APEX II CCD diffractometer equipped with a graphite monochromated MoRα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) by using φ/ω scan technique at room temperature. The structures were solved by direct methods with SHELXS-97. The hydrogen atoms were assigned with common isotropic displacement factors and included in the final refinement by use of geometrical restrains. A full-matrix least-squares refinement on F2 was carried out using SHELXL-97. Hydrogen atoms of water molecules were located by Fourier map, and then refined by riding mode. The crystallographic data and selected bond lengths and angles for 1–4 are listed in Table 1 and Tables S1–S4, shown in Electronic Supplementary Information.†

Results and discussion

Syntheses

As is known, many transition metal carboxylate compounds have been successfully prepared under hydrothermal conditions, which might be due to the poor solubility of carboxylate ligands. In this work, compounds 1–4 were isolated under the same hydrothermal conditions. Compounds 1–4 are air stable, insoluble in common organic solvents, and can retain their crystalline integrity at ambient temperature for a long time. No characteristic absorption bands of the –COOH group at 1700–1750 cm−1 are observed from the IR data which indicates that all the carboxylic groups of H2mip are deprotonated in 1–4.

Description of crystal structures

{[Co(mip)(bpa)]}n (1)

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction study reveals that 1 has a 2D bilayer linked by both mip and bpa molecules. The asymmetric unit of 1 consists of one Co(ii) ion, one mip2− anion and one bpa ligand (as shown in Fig. 1a). Each Co(ii) ion is coordinated by two bpa nitrogen atoms (Co1–N1 = 2.176(3) and Co1–N2A = 2.184(3) Å) and four oxygen atoms from three different mip ligands (Co1–O1 = 2.033(3), Co1–O2A = 2.007(3), Co1–O3B = 2.120(3), Co1–O4B = 2.262(3) Å), in a distorted octahedral geometry {CoN2O4}. It should be noted that the mip ligand in 1–3 shows the same coordination modes, bis-monodentate and bidentate-chelating modes for the two carboxyl groups, and a pair of Co(ii)/Ni(ii) atoms are connected by two bis-monodentate carboxyl groups to form a binuclear unit, then to a 1D polymeric ribbon by the connection of mip ligands (Fig. 1b). In 1, the Co⋯Co distances separated by the carboxyl group and mip ligand are 4.518 and 10.109 Å, respectively. Furthermore, the bpa acts as a bridging ligand with the dihedral angle between the two pyridyl rings of 49.76° to connect two Co(ii) ions, giving rise to an infinite 2D bilayer structure (Fig. 1c), and the Co⋯Co distance through the bpa ligand is 13.606 Å.

{[Co(mip)(bpp)]}n (2)

When a more flexible bpp ligand was used instead of bpa, a novel structure of 2 was obtained. The asymmetric unit of 2 (see Fig. 2a) contains one Co(ii) ion with a {CoN2O4} distorted octahedral geometry, one mip2− anion and one bpp ligand. The Co(ii) atom is coordinated with two nitrogen atoms from two bpp ligands and four oxygen atoms from three different mip ligands. The Co–N bond lengths are 2.1571(19) and 2.175(2) Å, which are similar to those found in the other octahedral cobalt complexes of bpp (Co–N: 2.136–2.140 Å)15 and are longer than those found in tetrahedral bpp complexes (2.047–2.048 Å).16 The Co–O bond lengths are in the range of 2.0373(14)–2.2247(15) Å, longer than the range reported for octahedral environments (2.047(3)–2.176(3) Å).17 Based on the same bridging modes as in 1, the mip ligands join Co(ii) atoms into a similar 1D polymeric ribbon containing the binuclear units (see Fig. 2b). The Co⋯Co distances through carboxyl group and mip ligand are 4.235 and 10.168 Å, respectively. Bearing a longer methylene (–CH2–)3 skeleton compared to bpa, the bpp ligand tends to exhibit more flexible conformations. In 2, the bpp ligand adopts a rare GG conformation14b dangling on two sides of the 1D polymeric ribbon. Although the assistant bpp ligand does not have a remarkable function in the extension of the complex's space structure, it should be emphasized that the bpp ligand has a significant effect on enhancing the stability of the architecture. Meanwhile, there are weak C–H⋯π interactions in the structure. The C–H⋯π interactions (C16–H1B⋯π, H⋯π separations 2.509 Å, and C–H⋯π angles 168.60°) are present between bpp and phenyl rings from adjacent ribbons resulting in a 2D supramolecular layer (Fig. 2c).

{[Ni(mip)(bpa)]}n (3)

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that 3 is a two-dimensional bilayer similar to that of 1, except that the central Co(ii) ion is replaced by Ni(ii) ion (as shown in Fig. 3a). The asymmetric unit of 3 contains one central Ni(ii) ion, one mip2− anion and one bpa molecule. The Ni(ii) ion locates in a distorted octahedral geometry, with four carboxylate oxygen atoms occupying the equatorial basal plane and two nitrogen atoms from bpa at the apical sites. The Ni–O and Ni–N bond lengths are in the range of 2.0307(12)–2.1865(13) and 2.1157(15)–2.1197(18) Å, respectively, which are all similar to other Ni(ii) complexes in the references.18,19 As described in 1, the extended 2D bilayer of 3 (Fig. 3b) is formed by the bridge of mip2− anions and bpp ligands linking the Ni(ii) ions. The Ni⋯Ni separation through the carboxyl group, mip, and bpa ligand are 4.662, 10.120 Å, and 13.504 Å, respectively.

{[Ni(mip)(bpp)(H2O)]}n (4)

As shown in Fig. 4a, the asymmetric unit of 4 consists of one Ni(ii) ion, one mip2− anion, one bpp ligand and one coordinated water molecule. Each Ni(ii) ion is six-coordinated with two nitrogen atoms from two bpp molecules (Ni–N = 2.0908(14), and 2.0817(14) Å), three oxygen atoms from two mip ligands and one oxygen atom from one water molecule (the Ni–O bond lengths are in the range of 2.0095(12) to 2.1949(12) Å), with the {NiN2O4} distorted octahedral geometry. Different from those in 1–3, the mip ligand in 4 shows a monodentate and bidentate-chelating coordination modes for the two carboxyl groups, bridging the neighbouring Ni(ii) ions to yield a 1D straight chain. The adjacent chains are further bridged by bpp ligands to form a 2D (4,4) layer (Fig. 4b), showing a rectangular window with the dimensions 10.142–12.831 Å. Moreover, the C–H⋯π interactions between the –CH3 of the mip and the pyridyl rings (N2 containing) of the bpp ligands (C9–H9A⋯π, H⋯π separations 2.941 Å, and C–H⋯π angles 138.58°) led the 2D layer to a 3D supramolecular structure (Fig. 4c). If the C–H⋯π interaction is considered, then the mip ligands are 3-connecting and the Ni[N2O3(H2O)] moieties act as the 5-connected nodes. Thus a (3,5)-connected network is formed, which has the rarely reported (63)(69.8)-hms topology (Fig. S1, ESI†).20 Because of the porous nature of the single network, two equivalent 3D networks mutually interpenetrated with each other to generate a two-fold interpenetrating 3D network (Fig. 4d).

When a Zn salt is used in the synthetic procedure, two different complexes, [Zn(mip)(bpa)]n (5) and [Zn(mip)(bpp)]n (6) were obtained.21 Complex 5 is a 3-fold interpenetrating three-dimensional (3D) network with CdSO4 topology constructed from alternately left-handed and right-handed helical channels. Complexes 6 is a 4-fold interpenetrated diamondoid-like network. When a Mn salt is used in the synthetic procedure, another two complexes, [Mn(mip)(bpa)]n (7) and {[Mn3(mip)2(Hmip)2(bpp)2(H2O)2]·2H2O}n (8) were obtained.22 The structure of 7 is similar to that of compounds 1 and 3. However, the structure of 8 possesses a 1D ladder-like framework composed of the linear trimetallic cluster containing two five-coordinated and one six-coordinated manganese centers. In 8, the bpp ligand adopts the unidentate coordination mode and the unligated N2 atom acts as the hydrogen-bonding acceptor.

XRPD

In order to check the phase purity of these compounds, the X-ray powder diffraction (XRPD) patterns of compounds 1–4 were checked at room temperature. As shown in Fig. S2, ESI,† the peak positions of the simulated and experimental XRPD patterns are in agreement with each other, demonstrating the good phase purity of the compounds. The differences in intensity may be due to the preferred orientation of the crystalline powder samples.

Magnetic properties

The magnetic susceptibilities, χM, of 1–4 were measured in the 2–300 K region at 2000 G, and are shown as χMT and χMvs. T plots in Fig. 5–8. The experimental χMT value of 1 at room temperature is 4.32 cm3 K mol−1, which is slightly larger than the spin value expected for two uncoupled high-spin Co(ii) ions (3.75 cm3 K mol−1). The χMT value of 1 steadily increases with decreasing temperature to reach a maximum value of 4.61 cm3 K mol−1 at 92 K. Upon further cooling, the χMT value decreases to a minimum of 3.97 cm3 K mol−1 at 12 K and then increases to a maximum value of 4.47 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K. The increase of χMT at high temperature is due to the ferromagnetic coupling within the carboxylate bridged binuclear Co(ii) ions. The decrease of χMT between 92 to 12 K maybe due to the antiferromagnetic coupling within the mip bridged binuclear Co(ii) ions. Further, the increase of χMT between 12 to 2 K maybe due to the weak ferromagnetic coupling within the bpa bridged Co(ii) ions between the adjacent chains. Considering the complicated facts of the two-dimensional structure and the addition of the spin–orbit coupling, as well as zero-field splitting effects in the Co(ii) ions, further attempts to fit the χMT data were unsuccessful.

The experimental χMT values of 2 at room temperature are 3.98 cm3 K mol−1, which are slightly larger than the spin value expected for two uncoupled high-spin Co(ii) ions (3.75 cm3 K mol−1). The temperature dependence of reciprocal susceptibility (χM−1) above 2 K follows the Curie–Weiss Law (χM = C/(T-θ)) with a weiss constant θ = −7.79 K and Curie constant of C = 4.52 cm3 K mol−1 for 2. The negative Weiss constant indicates an obvious intramolecular antiferromagnetic interaction between adjacent Co(ii) ions through the carboxyl group. The strength of the antiferromagnetic exchange interaction and the spin–orbit coupling for Co2+ were estimated based on eqn (a). (a) χT = Aexp(-E1/kT) + Bexp(-E2/kT), where A + B equals the curie constant. The E1 and E2 are the spin–orbit coupling constant and activation energy of antiferromagnetic interactions, respectively. The best fitting gave: E1/k = 27.73 K, E2/k = 2.23 K, A = 1.45 cm3 K mol−1, B = 3.14 cm3 K mol−1 (see Fig. 6). The value for A + B (4.59 cm3 K mol−1) is consistent with the Curie constant of C = 4.52 according to the Curie–Weiss Law.23 The value for E1/k is consistent with those given in the literature for both the effects of spin–orbit coupling and site distortion, and the value E2/k ∝ −2J corresponding to spin coupling between the Co2+ ions, shows the distinct anti-ferromagnetic exchange mediated between Co2+ through the carboxyl group.

The experimental χMT value of 3 at 300 K is 2.67 cm3 K mol−1, which is slightly larger than the spin-only value (2.0 cm3 K mol−1) expected for two uncoupled high-spin Ni(ii) ions (See Fig. 7). According to the structure of 3, it could be presumed that the main magnetic interactions between the Ni(ii) centers might happen between the carboxylate group bridged Ni(ii) ions whereas the superexchange interactions between Ni(ii) ions through the mip and bpa bridge can be ignored because of the long distances of the Ni/Ni separations. The magnetic susceptibility data were fitted assuming that the carboxylate group bridges between Ni(ii) ions form a binuclear unit with an exchange constant J and then mip and bpa connect the binuclear units to form a 2D network with an exchange constant zJ′. The susceptibility data were thus approximately analyzed by an isotropic dimer mode of spin S = 1.24 H = −2JS1·S2

The least-squares analysis of the magnetic susceptibility data led to J = 3.57 cm−1, g = 2.29, zJ′ = −0.21 cm−1 and R = 3.85 × 10−3. The J value indicates a weakly ferromagnetic interaction between the two Ni(ii) ions bridged by the carboxylate group. The smaller negative zJ′ value can be attributed to a very weak antiferromagnetic interaction between the Ni(ii) ions through mip and bpa bridges.

Although magneto-structural studies on carboxylate-bridged nickel(ii) with the carboxylate adopting the syn-anti bridging mode are rare, ferromagnetic interactions through this exchange pathway are known.25 In any case, independently of the nickel(ii) ion, the syn-anti configuration reduces drastically the overlap between the magnetic orbitals, thus diminishing the antiferromagnetic coupling and enhancing the ferromagnetic pathway. This exchange pathway is most likely responsible for the ferromagnetic coupling observed in 3.

As described in Fig. 8, at room temperature, the value of χMT of complex 4 is equal to 1.28 cm3 K mol−1, larger than the spin-only value for an isolated S = 1 center (1.0 cm3 K mol−1). As the temperature is lowered, the χMT value increases to a maximum at 8 K of 1.33 cm3 K mol−1, and then decreases rapidly upon further cooling, indicating ferromagnetic coupling interactions between the NiII centers. Complex 4 can be considered as a 1D chain, in which each NiII ion is linked by mip bridges since couplings through bpp are almost negligible. In order to evaluate the exchange interactions between neighboring nickel(ii) ions, the susceptibility measurements were fitted using the following ferromagnetic Ni(ii) chain equation induced from the Hamiltonian: .26

; A = −0.920563α3 + 2.14971α2 − 1.62319α + 0.541133, B = −0.42654α3 + 1.33776α2 − 1.57569α − 0.124497, C = 0.891021α3 − 1.62232α2 + 1.9104α − 0.312675, D = −1.12551α3 + 2.48341α2−1.84808α + 0.586753, E = 0.748354α3 − 1.2567α2 + 0.771037α − 0.88762.

The least-squares analysis of the magnetic susceptibility data led to J = 2.46 cm−1, g = 2.26 and R = 3 × 10−4 for 4 (see Fig. 8). The J value indicates a weak ferromagnetic interaction between the two NiII ions bridged by mip bridges. The crystal structure of the present compound 4 indicates that the exchange pathways between neighbouring magnetic centres involve the dicarboxylate and bpp ligands. Both are known to promote weak exchange interactions. The low efficiency of these exchange pathways explains the weak ferromagnetic behaviour observed for 4. It is worth noting that very weak exchange interactions have indeed been reported for other terephthalato- or isophthalato-bridged nickel(ii) complexes.27

Conclusion

In summary, four divalent transition metal dicarboxylate complexes with length-modulated N-containing ligands have been synthesized and characterized. Though these products have been synthesized under the same hydrothermal conditions and consist of similar compositions, they display a high degree of structural diversity. The magnetic properties of 1–4 were studied in the 2–300 K region at 2000 G. The structures and magnetic properties diversity could be due to the subtle differences in metal ions and ligands choices in the hybrid synthesis and further investigation is ongoing in our lab. It's believed that the results shown in this work will provide an effective way towards the design of novel MOFs with specific properties in the future.
