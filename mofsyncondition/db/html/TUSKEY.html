Dihydroxyacetone phosphate, DHAP, in the crystalline state: monomeric and dimeric forms

Abstract

It was shown that dihydroxyacetone phosphate may exist in both monomeric DHAP (C3H7O6P) and dimeric DHAP-dimer (C6H14O12P2) form. Monomeric DHAP was obtained in the form of four crystalline salts: CaCl(DHAP)·2.9H2O (7a), Ca2Cl3(DHAP)·5H2O (7b), CaCl(DHAP)·2H2O (7c), and CaBr(DHAP)·5H2O (7d) by crystallization from aqueous solutions containing DHAP acid and CaCl2 or CaBr2, or by direct crystallization from a solution containing DHAP precursor and CaCl2. At least one of the salts is stable and may be stored in the crystalline state at room temperature for several months. The dimeric form was obtained by slow saturation of free DHAP syrup with ammonia at −18 °C and isolated in the form of its hydrated diammonium salt (NH4)2(DHAP-dimer)·4H2O (8). The synthesis of the compounds, their crystallization, and crystal structures determined by X-ray crystallography are described. In all 7a–d monomeric DHAP exists in the monoanionic form in an extended (in-plane) cisoid conformation, with both hydroxyl and ester oxygen atoms being synperiplanar to the carbonyl O atom. The crucial structural feature is the coordination manner, in which the terminal phosphate oxygen atoms act as chelating as well as bridging atoms for the calcium cations. Additionally, the DHAP monoanions chelate another Ca2+ by the α-hydroxycarbonyl moiety, in a manner observed previously in dihydroxyacetone (DHA) calcium chloride complexes. In dimeric 8 the anion is a trans isomer with the dioxane ring in a chair conformation with the hydroxyl groups in axial positions and the phosphomethyl group in an equatorial position.

1. Introduction

The synthesis of dihydroxyacetone phosphate (DHAP, 7) and its isolation in the form of a stable, easy-to-store compound, as well as the structural investigations of DHAP in monomeric (7) as well as in dimeric form (8), was the main aim of the project dealing with the chemical conversion of the simplest ketose, dihydroxyacetone (DHA, 2), into its phosphate ester, DHAP (7). It is also a final part of the series of our previous papers on the structural characterization of the intermediates on this chemical pathway (Scheme 1).1, 2, 3, 4, 5

In living cells sugars are primed for metabolic transformations by phosphorylation. In case of DHAP, there are multiple pathways to it, mainly DHA phosphorylation and l-glycerol 3-phosphate (G-3P) oxidation in the mitochondrial glycerophosphate shuttle. DHAP is also produced in the glycolytic pathway by the cleavage of d-fructose 1,6-bisphosphate (Fru-1,6-BP) (Scheme 2).6, 7

DHAP (7) is one of the most important biochemical intermediates of high importance for all living cells. As a phosphate ester of a ketotriose (DHA), it plays a crucial role in energy-producing processes and in many biosyntheses. It acts as a substrate for at least 18 different enzymes, including triosephosphate isomerase, glycerol-3-phosphate dehydrogenase, dihydroxyacetone phosphate acyltransferase, and several types of aldolases. DHAP is involved in 25 different enzymatic reactions (most of which are shown in Scheme 2) and 9 different metabolic pathways, mainly in the area of carbohydrate metabolism, but also that of cofactors and vitamin metabolism.6, 7 Thus, dihydroxyacetone phosphate plays a crucial role in, for example, gluconeogenesis, fructose and mannose metabolism, glycerol and fatty acid metabolism, pentose phosphate pathway, the Calvin cycle, biosynthesis of triacylglycerols and (ether)phospholipids, glycerophosphate shuttle, and finally, in glycolysis. What is interesting is the fact that only three from eleven glycolytic intermediates have not been yet crystallized. Two of them are just DHAP and its isomer (glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate) GAP.8

Reversible reactions involving DHAP-dependent aldolases have been commonly used in the synthesis of synthetic sugars and related chiral compounds on a preparative scale. They accept a broad spectrum of aldehydes, but all of them are very specific to DHAP (for review of the application of DHAP-dependent aldolases in the asymmetric organic synthesis see Refs. 9, 10, 11, 12, 13, 14). The use of aldolases (EC 4.1.2) as catalysts for the aldol condensation helps to avoid difficulties in controlling the stereo- and regioselectivity of the reaction by numerous blocking/unblocking steps, the use of chemical catalysts activating the acceptor, donor or both, the use of non-aqueous solvents and other difficulties. Instead, the aldolase-catalyzed reactions may be carried out in aqueous solutions at room temperature, and they lead to the enantiomerically pure products from achiral or racemic substrates.

It is known that DHAP, similar to DHA, exists in water as a mixture of two monomeric forms: the free carbonyl, ketone (K), and the gem-diol (hydrate, H).15, 16, 17, 18 The ketone/hydrate ratio was found to be both temperature and pH dependent. The amount of the keto form is increased at higher temperatures,15, 16, 18 and its proportion in neutral aqueous solution was established by Gray and Barker as 55% at 25 °C and 63% at 37 °C18 and by Reynolds et al., as 15% at 4 °C, 55% at 20 °C, and even 83% at 37 °C.16 Additionally, Reynolds et al.16 stated that DHAP aqueous solution at 20 °C and neutral pH (fully ionized DHAP anion) consists of free keto (K), hydrated keto (H, gem-diol), and enol forms in proportions 55:44:1. One percent of the enolic species in DHAP water solution was also observed at 30 °C, and it is rather huge proportion in comparison with 0.02% for dihydroxyacetone (DHA), 0.04% for monohydroxyacetone, and 0.17% for monohydroxyacetone phosphate under the same conditions.16 The overall pH dependence of ketone/hydrate ratio reveals that the amount of the keto form is increased with pH increase at temperatures below 23 °C, and it is increased with pH decrease at temperatures above 23 °C.16 All in all, it was also shown that the keto form of DHAP is the form of the substrate handled by the enzyme (α-glycerophosphate dehydrogenase, aldolase, and triosephosphate isomerase).15, 16 It is to be mentioned that the existence of the dimeric form of dihydroxyacetone phosphate, DHAP-dimer, has been postulated but probably never observed experimentally.16, 18 Moreover, its possible role in the biochemical processes has not been even taken into consideration. For comparison, it is known that commercial, solid dihydroxyacetone, which is 100% in the dimeric form, DHA-dimer, dissociates in water solution into a mixture of two monomeric forms: the free ketone and the hydrate in a ratio 4:1.19 It has also been shown that the crystalline DHA monomer kept at room temperature undergoes dimerization, which is complete in 25–30 days, and then polymerization within several months.20 Additionally, it has been shown that the DHA-dimer to DHA interconversion process in aqueous solutions is reversible and temperature dependent; increasing the temperature of dihydroxyacetone aqueous solutions favors the formation of monomeric DHA from DHA-dimer.21 Taking this into consideration, a similar process should not be excluded in the case of DHAP.

It has been shown that DHA in water may undergo enolization and interconversion into dl-glyceraldehyde,21, 22 even though the enolization reactions are known to require the acid–base catalysis. Probably because of enolization, DHAP is unstable in the whole pH range, and its stability is the lowest under alkaline conditions. For example, 0.1 M NaOH solution at room temperature was shown to be the conditions that degrade DHAP with a halftime of about 2 min.23 It was also shown that in 1 N hydroxide solution at room temperature DHAP fully hydrolyzes just in 20 min.24 However, DHAP enolization does not lead to the respective aldehyde. Instead, the hydrolysis of DHAP occurs, resulting in inorganic phosphate and 2-oxo-propanal (also called pyruvaldehyde or methylglyoxal, MG), instead of DHA, as shown in the scheme below:

DHAP enolization is the first step of its nonenzymatic elimination (hydrolysis) and possible isomerization.23 In an aqueous solution, the endiolate intermediate undergoes an immediate elimination to produce MG. (The elimination reaction is ∼100 times faster than the isomerization.) This cannot take place in vivo, because DHAP must be converted into GAP for glycolysis, and the synthesis of toxic MG should be under control. Therefore, in living organisms these two processes (reversible isomerization and irreversible elimination) are controlled by two different enzymes: triosephosphate isomerase, TIM, EC 5.3.1.1, and methylglyoxal synthase, MGS, EC 4.2.3.3 (Scheme 2). It is known that the first step of the reactions catalyzed by both TIM and MGS is the formation of the common endiol(ate) intermediate, and that the active sites of the two enzymes are very similar (for the respective X-ray and NMR structures see the Protein Data Bank25).

One of two postulated mechanisms to prevent toxic MG formation from DHAP in the TIM-catalyzed reaction, besides the conformational changes of the enzyme with the isolation of the substrate bound in the active site, is keeping the enzyme-bound substrate in an extended (in-plane) conformation. Such geometry of DHAP, with the phosphate group lying in the carbonyl plane, makes the phosphate elimination and methylglyoxal formation difficult.26 Actually, the structures of TIM and MGS in complexes with the endiolate intermediate analogs, phosphoglycolohydroxamate (PGH) and phosphoglycolate (PG), deposited at PDB, show that the same analog may exist in a different conformation in the TIM and MGS active sites. PG complexed with TIM is usually bound in more extended conformation than in MGS (see, e.g., PDB ID codes 2V2H, 2J27, 2VXN, and 1EGH).25 PG complexed with MGS exists in a bent conformation (PDB ID code 1EGH),25, 27 but it has been shown that it may be bound in different conformations in TIM active site (PDB ID code 1N55).25, 28 However, it is possible and theoretically predicted that the planar substrate conformation is crucial for its stability, but it may not be the only factor. A huge role may also be played by the hydrogen contacts with the enzyme active site.29 Indeed, the crystal structure of the TIM–DHAP complex at near-atomic resolution (1.2 Å) reveals DHAP bound in the enzyme active site in the bent (out-of-plane) conformation. Both the phosphate and hydroxyl groups are displaced from the plane defined by the rest of the anion, with the O(P)–C–CO and O(H)–C–CO torsion angles being ∼70° and ∼40°, respectively (PDB ID code 1NEY).30

In aqueous solutions, DHAP was found to form complexes with different M2+ ions (M = Mg, Ca, Sr, Ba, Mn, Co, Ni, Cu, Zn, Cd), which are coordinated by the phosphate group only. What is interesting is the fact that there are no indications for the participation of the oxygen atom of the carbonyl group in complex formation, even though it would be possible on steric grounds. However, measurements with Cu2+ and DHAP in water containing 30% or 50% (v/v) 1,4-dioxane proved that to some extent cyclic seven-membered chelates involving both the phosphate group and the carbonyl oxygen atoms may be formed.31, 32

Because DHAP itself is unstable and expensive,† there are two general strategies for the synthesis: (1) enzymatic preparation of DHAP solution for immediate use and (2) the chemical synthesis of a stable DHAP precursor (with the ketone group protected), which may be stored indefinitely and may yield DHAP just by a simple hydrolysis. Since the enzymatic methods have some limitations (they are often too expensive to use on a preparative scale and give impure and difficult to isolate products), chemical synthesis is the best way of producing DHAP in the laboratory. Direct DHA phosphorylation has also been tried, but the method does not lead to DHAP isolation.33 Several stable DHAP precursors have been reported in the literature (I–V in Scheme 3), but the main factors determining the precursor choice are (1) the overall costs and yield of its synthesis and (2) the yield of its hydrolysis to give DHAP. In this regard, DHAP dimethyl acetal, (MeO)2DHAP (I in Scheme 3), seems to be the most attractive: its hydrolysis gives DHAP in nearly quantitative yield, its synthesis does not involve enzymatic steps and expensive Pd- or Pt-catalyzed hydrogenolysis. In 1999 Ferroni et al. described a very convenient, three-step preparation of this stable DHAP precursor.44 A slight modification introduced by us in the first step of the original synthetic method improved the yield of the least efficient reaction from 41% to 70%, and the overall yield of the precursor synthesis increased from 19% to 44%.1, 3

An interest in structural investigations of DHAP is the consequence of its biological function and wide applications as a C3 building block in enzymatic organic synthesis. As a result, the demand for the stable and inexpensive DHAP salt has induced us to undertake the chemical synthesis of DHAP and to develop and optimize the synthesis of such a derivative, which may be stored indefinitely (even at room temperature) without decomposition.

It is to be pointed out that the structures and applications of biologically important compounds like sugars and their phosphate esters have been studied for a long time, but short (C3 or C4) sugars have been rarely crystallized. Until several years ago, the only exception was d-glyceraldehyde, whose dimeric form was determined by X-ray crystallography. Therefore we have undertaken the structural investigations on the chemical pathway leading from dihydroxyacetone to dihydroxyacetone phosphate (Scheme 1), and we present herein the final part of this project. Previously, we reported the crystal and molecular structures of the series of the compounds occurring on the first six steps of the investigated pathway (1a–c, 2a–c, 2e, 2f, 3, 4, 5a–e, 5e′–h, and 6a–e in Scheme 1).1, 2, 3, 4, 5 Among other things the results of the structural investigations on three polymorphs of dihydroxyacetone in dimeric form, DHA-dimer (1a–c), as well as in monomeric form, DHA (2c), along with its four complexes with CaCl2, CaBr2, and CdCl2, and its dimethyl acetal, (MeO)2DHA, have been reported in recent years.1, 5 Also the crystal and molecular structures of four salts of DHAP stable precursor, its dimethyl acetal, (MeO)2DHAP (6a–d), along with the salt of its phenyl ester, (MeO)2DHAP(Ph) (6e) has been published.3 Recently the X-ray structures of nine different salts of (MeO)2cDHAP (5a–e, 5e′–h)4 along with its phenyl derivative, (MeO)2cDHAP (Ph) (4)2, the cyclic form of compounds 6 and 6e, respectively, have been reported.

The present paper concerns the synthesis and the structure of four crystalline derivatives of monomeric dihydroxyacetone phosphate, three of which are its calcium chloride salts: CaCl(DHAP)·2.9H2O (7a), Ca2Cl3(DHAP)·5H2O (7b), and CaCl(DHAP)·2H2O (7c), and one the calcium bromide salt: CaBr(DHAP)·5H2O (7d), as well as one crystalline compound of dimeric dihydroxyacetone phosphate isolated in the form of its hydrated diammonium salt (NH4)2(DHAP-dimer)·4H2O (8). All of 7a–d crystals contain calcium cations coordinated by DHAP− monoanions. Thus, the geometry of the DHAP− anions, along with the probable influence of their coordination manner and the polymeric architecture of the crystals on DHAP stability, will be discussed. Additionally, the molecular geometry of DHAP− and (DHAP-dimer)2− anions will be compared with that of sugar analogs: DHA and DHA-dimer, respectively.

2. Results and discussion

2.1. DHAP monomer

The crystalline monomeric dihydroxyacetone phosphate (DHAP) salts described in this paper contain calcium cations. The asymmetric units of the crystals of CaCl(DHAP)·2.9H2O (7a), Ca2Cl3(DHAP)·5H2O (7b), CaCl(DHAP)·2H2O (7c), and CaBr(DHAP)·5H2O (7d) consist of DHAP− monoanions, Ca2+ cations, Cl− (in 7a–c) or Br− (in 7d) anions and water molecules in amounts relevant to their molecular formulas. Both the DHAP conformation and its close environment pattern are almost identical in all the crystals.

2.1.1. Molecular geometry of the DHAP− monoanions in 7a–d

The overall structure of the DHAP− monoanions reveals great similarities. As shown in Figure 1, the anions exist in an extended (in-plane) conformation with the geometry of the sugar moiety being the same as that observed in monomeric dihydroxyacetone (DHA) molecules.1, 5 Then, the O3 hydroxyl group and the ester O1 atom are coplanar with the rest of the sugar moiety and are synperiplanarly oriented in relation to the carbonyl O2 atom. Also the phosphorus atom, along with one of the terminal phosphate oxygen atoms, O5, is antiperiplanar to the C2 and C1 atoms, respectively, and therefore also lies in the plane defined by the sugar moiety. Two remaining terminal phosphate O4 and O6 atoms are oriented synclinal in relation to C1. Such orientation of the terminal O atoms in the phosphate group is rather typical for the organic phosphate monoesters.8 The hydrogen atom in the phosphate group of 7a, 7c, and 7d is located at the antiperiplanar O5 atom, which is not often observed in the monoanionic phosphate esters.8 Instead, in most of the phosphates deposited in the Cambridge Structural Database,8 this H atom is located at the synclinal atoms, as it is observed in 7b. Actually, the antiperiplanar location of the hydrogen atom has only been observed in few cases, for example, in some salts of phosphoenolpyruvic acid (PEP). The geometry of the phosphate group is significantly different from the ideal tetrahedral shape. In 7a,c,d the O–P–O angle formed by the ester O atom and the terminal antiperiplanar hydroxyl O atom (O1–P–O5) is the smallest from all the phosphate angles (96.4(2)° in 7a, 99.9(1)° in 7c, and 96.7(2)° in 7d). The remaining O–P–O angles are of similar values (from 110.0(2)° to 114.4(2)°). On the whole, the values of Oester–P–Oap(H) angles in 7a,c,d are correlated with the respective P–O bond lengths. In 7a,c,d DHAP− anions P–O1ester and P–O5ap(H) bonds are significantly longer than the other P–O4 and P–O6 (Table 1). Nevertheless, it is to be noted here that values below 100° have rarely been observed for O–P–O angles in phosphate monoesters.8 A quite different situation is observed in 7b, where the H atom is located at the phosphate synclinal O4 atom of the DHAP− monoanion. Here, the P–O1ester and P–O4sc(H) bonds are the longest, but neither the Oester–P–O(H) (O1–P–O4) nor the Oester–P–Oap (O1–P–O5) angle is the smallest. Instead, three lower values (from 102.1(1)° to 105.6(1)°) and three higher values (from 110.7(1)° to 119.3(1)°) are observed for O–P–O angles in the phosphate group. In general it may be stated that in 7a–d different deformation in the phosphate group is observed depending on the phosphate H atom location, but other effects (like the coordination of DHAP− to Ca2+ or hydrogen bonds) may also be of importance.

In all DHAP− anions in 7a–d the ester P–O1 bond is the longest of all the P–O distances (from 1.589(3) Å in 7a to 1.601(2) Å in 7b) and may be considered as rather short P–O ester bonds. The values of the remaining P–O and the ester O1–C1 bonds are in a typical range for the phosphate monoesters with the monoionized phosphate group. As could be expected, the P–O(H) bonds in all 7a–d are intermediate in value between the ester P–O1 and the remaining P–O bonds.

The recently reported, atomic resolution X-ray crystal structure of triosephosphate isomerase, TIM in complex with DHAP (TIM/DHAP) revealed the geometrical parameters of the DHAP2− dianion bonded in the enzyme active site (PDB ID code 1NEY).30 A comparison of the molecular structures of DHAP− ions observed in the presented crystal salts 7a–d with that of the DHAP2− dianion observed in the TIM active site (Fig. 2) reveals significant differences in DHAP conformation, mainly in the orientation of the phosphate group in relation to the sugar moiety. The differences in the location of the ester O1 and hydroxyl O3 atoms (out-of-plane conformation) may be the result of both the interactions of the dianion with the active site amino acids and the phosphate group ionization state.

2.1.2. Coordination of DHAP− to Ca2+ cations in 7a–d. Crystal packing and intermolecular interactions

In the presented crystals of DHAP salts the coordination manner shows a number of common features, but in 7b it is somewhat different than in 7a, 7c, and 7d, where the coordination of DHAP monoanions to the calcium cations is the same. Two different regions involved in the coordination could be distinguished in DHAP anions. These are the monoionized phosphate group and the α-hydroxycarbonyl moiety. Both regions in 7a–d bind two different Ca2+ ions in a bidentate, chelating manner (via O4 and O6 atoms from one side and via O2 and O3 from the other side). The phosphate group in 7a–d is involved in an additional monodentate coordination to two different calcium cations, thereby bridging two adjacent Ca2+ ions. The same O4 and O6 are engaged in this additional coordination in 7a,c,d, whereas in 7b the bridging atoms are O6 and O5. In this way, every DHAP monoanion is surrounded by four Ca2+ ions as shown in Figure 3.

It is to be pointed out here that the same mode of coordination of the α-hydroxycarbonyl moiety to Ca2+ cations is observed in all the monomeric dihydroxyacetone (DHA) complexes,1, 5 as well as in hydroxyacetophenone complexed with CaCl2.45 Such bidentate coordination with the carbonyl and hydroxyl O atoms involved is typical for the α-chelation mode, a characteristic of the interactions of hydroxyacids with Ca2+ ions.46 Like in DHA complexes, also in DHAP salts there is a strong tendency for the calcium cation to lie in the plane of the α-hydroxycarbonyl group. The Ca2+ cations are restricted to a narrow region, with Ca⋯OC angles of about 120°, which are in the range of 110–130°, which is typical for the α-chelation mode.

The coordination environment of the Ca2+ ions in the crystals 7a, 7c, and 7d is very similar, while that in 7b differs slightly as shown in Figure 4. Each calcium cation in 7a, 7c, and 7d (coordination number = 8) is coordinated by four symmetry-related DHAP− anions and two crystallographically independent water molecules. In 7b three symmetry-related DHAP− and three independent water molecules are coordinated to Ca2+ ions denoted as Ca1. In all 7a–d complexes two of the DHAP− anions chelate the metal center (one of them via the phosphate O4 and O6 atoms, and the other via the O2 and O3 form an α-hydroxycarbonyl group), and the remaining DHAP− anions (one in 7b and two in 7a,c,d) are coordinated in a monodentate manner (by O4 or O6 phosphate atom). The coordination sphere of Ca2 labeled atoms in 7b (of a geometry of distorted octahedron) is shown in Figure 5 and is composed of three crystallographically independent Cl− ions, two different water molecules and one phosphate O5 atom. Two adjacent Ca1 ions are coordinated by four symmetry-related DHAP− monoanions (two of which act as bridging ligands) and six water molecules to form centrosymmetric dimeric units with a Ca⋯Ca distance of 3.876(2) Å, as shown in Figure 5a. Each DHAP− ion is coordinated to two adjacent dimeric units simultaneously: with its phosphate group to one of them, and with its α-hydroxycarbonyl group to the other, resulting in polymeric ribbons built along the a-axis (Fig. 5b). Every phosphate group is additionally coordinated to a Ca2 cation surrounded also by two water molecules and three Cl− ions each, as shown in Figure 5. The Ca2 octahedrons, located in the interlayer exterior, play a crucial role in the crystal lattice of 7b formation, mainly by O–H⋯Cl, but also by O–H⋯O inter-ribbon interactions of the following types: H2O⋯Cl, DHAP−⋯Cl, H2O⋯DHAP− and H2O⋯H2O.

The architecture of polymeric layers observed in 7a, 7c, and 7d crystals is very similar. In these cases DHAP− anions and water molecules coordinate to the calcium cations in a manner as shown in Figure 6, Figure 7, to form ribbons along the b-axis in 7a (Fig. 6), and along the a-axis in 7c and 7d (Fig. 7). In the ribbon, every second DHAP− chelates one of the Ca2+ cations with its phosphate O4 and O6 atoms and serves as a bridge to two other calcium cations with the same O atoms. The other DHAP− ions chelate Ca2+ with their α-hydroxycarbonyl moiety via O2 and O3 atoms. Thus, every two adjacent Ca2+ ions within the ribbon are joined with each other through two bridging phosphate O atoms (O4 and/or O6) from two different DHAP− monoanions. The ribbons in 7a and 7d are formed by the action of a 21 screw axis, and therefore, between the calcium cations, two different O atoms (O4 and O6) are located and every Ca⋯Ca distance within the ribbon is equal and amounts to 4.037(2) Å for Ca⋯Caii,iii in 7a and 3.971(2) Å for Ca⋯Caxi,xii in 7d. The ribbons in 7c are centrosymmetric, and hence two different kinds of bridges between the calcium cations are present, one involving two O4 atoms and one involving two O6 atoms (Fig. 7). Therefore, the Ca⋯Cav distance (of 4.034(2) Å) is different from the Ca⋯Caix distance (of 4.006(2) Å).

Every DHAP− monoanion coordinates to one ribbon with its phosphate group and to another with its α-hydroxycarbonyl moiety, which results in corrugated layers of a common structure shown for 7a in Figure 8. The layers are parallel to (101¯), (010) and (001) plane in 7a, 7c, and 7d, respectively, as shown in Figure 9. The inter-ribbon distance within the plane is similar in the respective crystals, that is, about 9.22 Å = a half of the sum of a and c basal vectors in 7a, 9.190(3) Å = c-axis in 7c, and 9.341(2) Å = b-axis in 7d. Halogen anions (Cl− in 7a and 7c, Br− in 7d) and water molecules (O3W in 7a and O3W, O4W, O5W in 7d) non-coordinating to the calcium cations, are located in the space between the positively charged layers (Fig. 9) and serve as linkers between the layers by being involved in a variety of hydrogen bonds, that is, O–H⋯O, O–H⋯Cl/Br, C–H⋯O (only in 7a and 7d) and C–H⋯Cl (only in 7c). DHAP− anions in all 7a–d complexes involve hydroxyl groups from the phosphate and sugar moieties in hydrogen bonds, mainly to water molecules and/or halogen atoms. Furthermore, in 7b and 7d, the O1 and O2 atoms accept additional hydrogen bonds from water molecules. Interestingly in 7c, DHAP− anions from the adjacent layers interact with each other through direct O3–H3⋯O5xviii contacts (Table 3). The interlayer distance is similar in 7a (about 8.54 Å) and 7c (8.707(3) Å equals b-axis) and is much smaller than in 7d, where it equals 1/2 c-axis, that is, 10.586(4) Å. Solutions of several DHAP salts at different pH values have been subjected to NMR studies in order to determine the influence of pH and a salt type on the stability of DHAP, as well as on the equilibrium state between two monomeric DHAP forms, the free ketone (K) and the gem-diol (hydrate, H), shown in the scheme below:

The samples for NMR experiment were prepared as follows: To a freshly prepared DHAP solution, inorganic salt (Li2CO3, NaHCO3 or KHCO3) was added in a suitable molar ratio (1:1 or 1:2) or with pH control. The solutions were then frozen and lyophilized. Next, the sample was dissolved in ca. 0.5 mL of D2O or MeOH-d4. D2O solutions obtained by dissolving crystalline calcium chloride salts of DHAP were also measured. NMR experiments were performed at room or lower temperature. The samples after the NMR experiments were frozen and stored at −18 °C for the subsequent determination of DHAP stability under the storing conditions.

In the 1H, 31P, and 13C NMR spectra, signals of both ketone (K) and hydrate (H) DHAP forms are always present. The signals in the spectra were ascribed (see Section 4, and Fig. 10a) on the basis of the heteronuclear correlation 2D NMR experiments (1H–31P and 1H–13C). It is to be noted here that similar δ values were observed in the spectra of other DHAP solutions at pH 4.5 (i.e., equimolar LiHCO3–, NaHCO3–, KHCO3–DHAP systems) or at slightly lower pH 3.5–4 (CaCl2–DHAP solution obtained by dissolving crystals of 7c). However, in 1H NMR spectra of lithium, sodium, and potassium salts at neutral pH, the changes in relative positions of the respective signals are observed, as shown in Figure 10b. For the gem-diol form the changes are negligible, but for the ketone form at pH 7.0, the upfield shift of the doublet and simultaneous downfield shift of the singlet is observed, resulting in the change of the relative positions of these signals (with the singlet and doublet being partly superimposed in the case of DHAP/KHCO3 1:2 solution). It has to be noted that the same is not observed in the case of calcium salt solution. In 31P NMR, the downfield shifts of the singlets are observed with the pH increase, and for the potassium and calcium salts at pH 7.0, one broaden signal is observed.

The equilibrium state between the ketone and gem-diol (hydrate) forms is only slightly sensitive to pH changes. On the basis of the respective signals integrations, the K/H ratio at room temperature (295 K) has been established as about 1:1.2 for the free DHAP acid solution, as 1:0.9 for the DHAP salts solution of pH 4.5, and as 1:0.8 for the DHAP salt solutions of pH 7.0, which is consistent with the previous reports.16, 18 It is noted here that the rate of DHAP decomposition is the lowest when it is stored frozen at −18 °C at pH 4.5. However, the best way of storing DHAP is to keep it in the form of its crystalline calcium salt: CaCl(DHAP)·2.9H2O (7a), which is easy to prepare and may be kept even at room temperature for months without decomposition.

2.2. DHAP-dimer

As it could be predicted, dihydroxyacetone phosphate may also exist in the dimeric form, (DHAP-dimer), in the solid state. Actually, we isolated this form as the tetrahydrated diammonium salt of 2,5-dihydroxy-2,5-bis(hydroxymethyl)-1,4-dioxane bisphosphate, (NH4)2(DHAP-dimer)·4H2O (8). The crystals are built up from (DHAP-dimer)2− dianions, ammonium cations and water molecules in a molar ratio of 1:2:4.

2.2.2. Crystal packing and intermolecular interactions in the crystal of (NH4)2(DHAP-dimer)·4H2O (8)

The crystal structure of 8 is stabilized by the extensive network of O–H⋯O and N–H⋯O hydrogen bonds. Besides the water molecules and ammonium cations mainly involved in these interactions, all the O atoms from the (DHAP-dimer)2− anion, that is, all the phosphate, hydroxyl O2 and endocyclic O3, play a role. As shown in Figure 12, every (DHAP-dimer)2− is caged by the solvent molecules and ammonium cations, forming close contact with four NH4+ ions and eight water molecules. The other NH4+ ions and H2O molecules are located in the next coordination spheres, thereby separating the adjacent (DHAP-dimer)2− anions and minimizing direct interactions between them. Thus in the crystal lattice of 8, the adjacent anions of the DHAP-dimer interact with each other mainly through the indirect interactions of the following type: anion⋯H2O⋯anion, anion⋯NH4+⋯anion, anion⋯H2O⋯H2O⋯anion, anion⋯NH4+⋯H2O⋯anion and anion⋯H2O⋯H2O⋯NH4+⋯anion.

Besides the extensive network of indirect interactions observed in the crystal structure of 8, some direct interdimeric contacts are also present. The adjacent (DHAP-dimer)2− anions are joined to each other by O2–H2⋯O6ii hydrogen bonds, as shown in Figure 13. Each anion interacts with four others through both its hydroxyl and phosphate groups, to form layers parallel to the (102¯) plane, the structure of which is additionally stabilized by weak C1–H1B⋯O5ii interactions. All that gives rise to the extensive three-dimensional network of hydrogen contacts in the crystal of 8.

3. Conclusions

Dihydroxyacetone phosphate was synthesized by the almost quantitative acid hydrolysis of its dimethyl acetal, (MeO)2DHAP, which is the best known, stable DHAP precursor. DHAP was obtained in an improved synthesis1, 3, 44 in an overall yield of 33%.

Dihydroxyacetone phosphate was crystallized in the monomeric, DHAP (C3H7O6P), as well as dimeric, DHAP-dimer (C6H14O12P2) form. Monomeric DHAP had been isolated in the form of four crystalline salts: CaCl(DHAP)·2.9H2O (7a), Ca2Cl3(DHAP)·5H2O (7b), CaCl(DHAP)·2H2O (7c), and CaBr(DHAP)·5H2O (7d). This allowed discussing in detail the geometry and coordination manner of the DHAP− anions, which revealed a great similarity to DHA molecules. The characteristic of both DHA and DHAP is the extended (in-plane) conformation, with non-hydrogen atoms (all in DHA and most of them in DHAP) lying in one plane. Dimeric dihydroxyacetone phosphate, whose possible role in biochemical processes has never been mentioned and taken into consideration in the literature so far, was isolated in the form of its tetrahydrated diammonium salt (NH4)2(DHAP-dimer)·4H2O (8).

That which is of great practical importance, is that the method of preparation of a stable DHAP salt was worked out. At least one of the crystalline forms of monomeric DHAP salts, CaCl(DHAP)·2.9H2O (7a), is stable and may be successfully stored even at room temperature for several months without any decomposition symptoms. It may also be easily used for biochemical purposes and for enzymatic organic synthesis without any special preparation. It needs to be mentioned here that the crystals of other calcium chloride DHAP salts 7b,c decompose in fact, but DHAP itself does not undergo decomposition, and the sample may be recrystallized to give form 7a. (The chemical stability of DHAP was determined by ESIMS and NMR experiments.)

We can conclude from the X-ray analyses that both the stability of the presented DHAP salt(s) and the conformation of the DHAP− anions may result from the manner in which DHAP⋯Ca2+ coordinates. DHAP− in the extended (in-plane) cisoid conformation (with both hydroxyl and ester O atoms being synperiplanar to the carbonyl O atom) acts as a chelating ligand as well as a bridging ligand for several calcium cations simultaneously. DHAP− monoanions chelate two different metal centers via both the phosphate group and α-hydroxycarbonyl moiety. Additionally, the terminal phosphate O atoms are involved in the monodentate coordination to two other Ca2+ ions. The manner in which α-hydroxycarbonyl⋯Ca2+ coordinates in the DHAP salts is almost identical to that observed previously in dihydroxyacetone (DHA) calcium chloride complexes,1, 5 and additionally it is typical for the α-chelation mode, which is a characteristic of the interactions of hydroxyacids with Ca2+ ions46 (Scheme 4).

It is to be noted here that the DHAP hydrolysis process, enzymatic as well as nonenzymatic (which goes through the formation of an endiol transition state in the first stage) involves mainly the α-hydroxycarbonyl group of the anion. In the crystals 7a–d presented here, the α-hydroxycarbonyl moiety is ‘blocked’ by the coordination to the Ca2+ cations. Additionally the coordination of the phosphate group to three other calcium cations contributes to keeping the extended in-plane conformation by the DHAP− anions and to forming 1D or 2D polymeric structures.

In summary, we showed that dihydroxyacetone phosphate, which is similar to its analog, dihydroxyacetone, may exist at least in two monomeric forms, [which are the ketone (K) and the hydrate (H)], as well as in the dimeric form. Two of these forms, the free carbonyl (2a–f for DHA1, 5 and 7a–d for DHAP) and the dimeric form (1a–c for DHA1 and 8 for DHAP), were crystallized, and their structures were confirmed by of X-ray crystallography (Scheme 5). It is to note that the dimeric form of dihydroxyacetone phosphate, DHAP-dimer, has been postulated but probably never observed experimentally.16, 18 Moreover, its possible role in biochemical processes has not been even taken into consideration. The pH-dependent equilibrium between two monomeric forms (K and H) in DHAP aqueous solutions was characterized by NMR techniques.

4. Experimental

4.1. General experimental procedures

Dowex 50 × 2–100 (H+, Serva) was regenerated after use by washing it with about 100 mL of 3–4 M HCl and then with water until the pH was neutral. NMR spectra were recorded using a Bruker Avance 500 spectrometer. High-resolution mass spectra were recorded on a Bruker micrOTOF-Q spectrometer (Bruker Daltonic, Bremen Germany). Crystals of DHAP salts were initially grown from the freshly prepared and lyophilized DHAP solutions. After taking notice of the fact that the calcium salt is stable, the next crystallizations with CaCl2 were carried on from freshly prepared, but not lyophilized DHAP solutions.

4.2. Synthesis of the compounds

Dihydroxyacetone phosphate dimethyl acetal, (MeO)2DHAP (6), stable DHAP precursor, was synthesized according to the previously described procedure.44, 1, 3 A different DHAP precursor, the barium salt of 2,5-diethoxy-2,5-bis(phosphomethyl)-1,4-dioxane was synthesized from dihydroxyacetone based on a combined synthetic procedure.41, 42 Dihydroxyacetone phosphate (DHAP, 7) for most crystallizations was synthesized by the acidic hydrolysis of its dimethyl acetal, (MeO)2DHAP (6), in nearly quantitative yield (Scheme 6). A water solution of the cyclohexylammonium salt of the precursor (cha)2[(MeO)2DHAP]·H2O (6a)3 (100 mg 0.23 mmol in a minimal quantity of water) was passed through an ion-exchange column [Dowex 50(H+)], and the solution of the acid (MeO)2DHAP obtained in this way (∼0.025 M) was heated for 4 h at 40 °C. 4.3. Preparation of the compounds. Crystallization

4.3.1. CaCl(DHAP)·2.9H2O (7a)

To a freshly prepared DHAP solution (19.6 mg, 0.12 mmol in ca. 5 mL of distilled water) CaCl2·6H2O was added in a molar ratio of 1:1 (25.2 mg, 0.12 mmol). The solution was stored at 4 °C allowing slow evaporation. Very small, needle-like, stable crystals so obtained were then recrystallized, which after six weeks gave larger needles of 7a suitable for X-ray experiments. The crystals of 7a are stable even at room temperature and may be stored without decomposition for months. The same crystals were obtained when crystallized from water solution of DHAP/Ca in a molar ratio of 2:1 (12.6 mg, 0.06 mmol of CaCl2·6H2O).

The same crystals of 7a were also obtained directly from the other DHAP precursor, the barium salt of 2,5-diethoxy-2,5-bis(phosphomethyl)-1,4-dioxane, as shown in Scheme 6. A water solution of the compound was passed through an ion-exchange column [Dowex 50(H+)], and to the acidic solution CaCl2·6H2O was added in a molar ratio of 1:2. The crystals of 7a were obtained by the slow evaporation of the solution at 4 °C.

4.3.2. Ca2Cl3(DHAP)·5H2O (7b) and CaCl(DHAP)·2H2O (7c)

To a freshly prepared DHAP solution (19.6 mg, 0.12 mmol in ca. 5 mL of distilled water) CaCl2·6H2O was added in a molar ratio of 1:1 (25.2 mg, 0.12 mmol). The solution was frozen and then evaporated under a nitrogen stream at room temperature. Colorless, large, very hygroscopic, unidentified plates (maybe of calcium chloride) were grown first, and then, after a few hours, small plates of 7b appeared. (The addition of water to the hygroscopic crystals causes formation of the crystals of 7a straight away.) The crystals of 7b are not stable and they decompose just after the low-temperature X-ray measurement.

Rather large, relatively stable crystalline plates of 7c were obtained from 7b stored at room temperature after a dozen of days.

4.3.3. CaBr(DHAP)·5H2O (7d)

The compound was obtained accidentally during an attempted crystallization of DHAP dimethyl acetal, (MeO)2DHAP (6), and its calcium salt. (cha)2[(MeO)2DHAP]·H2O (6a)1 (50 mg 0.12 mmol in a minimal quantity of water) was passed through an ion-exchange column [Dowex 50(H+)]. The solution (ca. 5 mL) of the acid (MeO)2DHAP so obtained was frozen and lyophilized yielding a syrup that was stored at −18 °C. After two months CaBr2·2H2O was added (55 mg, 0.23 mmol; 1:2 molar ratio) to the vessel that was kept at −18 °C. After several days large, very hygroscopic twinned columns of 7d were grown, which, after the structural analysis, were shown to be the salt of DHAP, instead of (MeO)2DHAP.

4.3.4. (NH4)2(DHAP-dimer)·4H2O (8)

A freshly prepared DHAP solution (19.6 mg, 0.12 mmol in ca. 5 mL of distilled water) was frozen and lyophilized yielding a syrup that was stored at −18 °C and was subjected to very slowly saturation with ammonia vapor of low concentration. After about six weeks large, hygroscopic blocks of 8 grew.

4.4. X-ray crystal structure determinations

The crystallographic measurements for 7a–d and 8 were performed on a Kuma KM4CCD automated four-circle diffractometers with the graphite-monochromatized Mo Kα radiation. The data for the crystals were collected at 100(2) K using an Oxford Cryosystems cooler. Data collection, cell refinement, and data reduction and analysis were carried out with the km4ccd software (Oxford Diffraction Poland): crysalisccd and crysalisred, respectively.47 Analytical or multi–scan absorption correction was applied to the data of 7a–d, with the use of crysalisred. All structures were solved by direct methods using shelxs-9748 and refined by a full-matrix least-squares technique using shelxl-9748 with anisotropic thermal parameters for all non-H atoms, except for low occupied Cl1 chloride position in 7a and O30W water position in 7b. The chloride atom in 7a and one of the water molecules in 7b and 8 are disordered into two positions with the respective site-occupation-factors (s.o.f.): 0.87(1) and 0.13(1) for Cl and Cl1 in 7a, 0.78(3) and 0.22(3) for O3W and O30W in 7b and 0.60(2) and 0.40(2) for O1W and O10W (with the H atoms in common positions) in 8. One of three water molecules in 7a (O3W) is not fully occupied, and in the final model it was refined with the s.o.f. = 0.87(1), assuming a correlation between O3W water occupancy and chloride disorder. Owing to the low occupancy of Cl1 in 7a and O30W in 7b, they were not discussed. The positions of H atoms on the low occupied O30W water molecule in 7b have not been found. The remaining H atoms in 7a–d and 8 were found in difference-Fourier maps and were refined isotropically, but in the final refinement cycles, the hydroxyl H3 atom in 7a and H5 in 7d, as well as all methylene atoms in 7d, were treated as riding atoms, with O–H distances of 0.84 Å and C–H distances of 0.99 Å and with Uiso values of 1.5Ueq(O) or 1.2Ueq(C). Atoms H3A, H6W in 7a and H4, H1A, H5W in 7b were refined with Uiso = Ueq of the parent atom, and atoms H1W, H3W, H4W in 7a and H1W, H7W in 7d were refined with Uiso(H) values set at 1.5Ueq(O). Some of the water molecules: O3W in 7a and O1W in 7d were refined with the O–H distances restrained to 0.84 Å. O1W in 7d was refined with H⋯H distance set at 1.37 Å. The structure of 7d was refined as a racemic twin with TWIN and BASF (refined to 0.40(2)) instructions applied.
