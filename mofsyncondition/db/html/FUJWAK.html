Owing to the virtues affording an environmentally-friendly approach to stereospecific cyclobutane derivatives and the revealing of a solid-to-solid transformation mechanism, solid-state photochemical [2 + 2] cycloaddition has captured intense attention in modern synthetic and physical chemistry.1 Thus far, topical studies have achieved the planned orientation of olefins by making use of supramolecular interactions, such as hydrogen bonding and π–π stacking.2 More recently, an emphasis has been placed on projecting the [2 + 2] photodimerization reaction within discrete coordination complexes and coordination polymers (CPs), thereupon showing single-crystal-to-single-crystal (SCSC) transformations.3 The metal–ligand strategy introduced a novel pathway to valuable organic molecules and desirable optical properties.4 However, it is noticeable that these olefin ligands are almost exclusively functionalized with pyridine and carboxylate groups, and their photoreactions have never been kinetically probed by in situ fluorescence techniques.

As part of a clear design toward the introduction of the tetrazolate group to olefin ligands by the in situ [2 + 3] cycloaddition of cyano and azide groups (Scheme 1),5 herein we report the construction of four Lene-containing CPs varied from 1D to 3D {Lene = 1-(4-pyridyl)-2-[4-(5-tetrazolyl)phenyl]ethane}, and their SCSC transformations to four Lane-containing CPs with the same dimensions through the photochemical [2 + 2] cycloaddition of CC bonds {Lane = 1,3-bis(4-pyridyl)-2,4-bis[4-(5-tetrazolyl)phenyl]cyclobutane}. The tetrazolyl olefin Lene was chosen as the ligand since its diverse bonding modes promise variable dimensions of its CPs and its elongated body allows the relaxation of stress originating from atom displacements in the crystal.

The solvothermal reaction of the 4-(4-cyanostyryl)pyridine precursor with sodium azide and ZnCl2 or ZnBr2 in a 1 : 1 : 1 molar ratio in an isopropanol–water solvent at 160 °C results in the formation of CPs [Zn2(Lene)2(OH)(Cl)] (1-Cl) and [Zn2(Lene)2(OH)(Br)] (1-Br), respectively. Single-crystal X-ray analyses reveal that CPs 1-Cl and 1-Br are isostructural. Without altering the solvothermal conditions, the reaction of 4-(4-cyanostyryl)pyridine with sodium azide and Zn(NO3)2·6H2O in a 1 : 3 : 1.5 molar ratio results in CP [Zn2(Lene)2(OH)(N3)] (2), and the reaction of 4-(4-cyanostyryl)pyridine with sodium azide and ZnBr2 in a 1 : 1 : 4 molar ratio results in CP [Zn2(Lene)(OH)2(Br)] (3). In all CPs, the Zn(ii) ion adopts a tetrahedral coordination geometry. After irradiation by UV light, CPs 1-Cl, 1-Br, 2 and 3 were transformed into CPs [Zn4(Lane)(Lene)2(OH)2(Cl)2] (1-Cl′), [Zn4(Lane)(Lene)2(OH)2(Br)2] (1-Br′), [Zn4(Lane)(Lene)2(OH)2(N3)2] (2′), and [Zn4(Lane)(OH)4(Br)2] (3′), respectively, which were identified by single-crystal X-ray diffraction (Table 1).

Fig. 1 to 3 depict these photo-induced structural transformations. The skeletons of CPs 1–3 have 1D tube, 2D layer, and 3D frameworks, respectively, but they are all made up of Zn(ii), Lene, and OH elements, revealing the influence of terminal anions and reaction stoichiometry on the polymeric structures. In these structures, the Lene ligand displays various coordination modes. It serves as both a μ2 linker and a μ3 linker in 1 and 2, but acts solely as a μ3 linker in 3. Moreover, the tetrazolate ring of μ3-Lene in 1 and 3 coordinates through the 1,3-nitrogen atoms, while in 2 it coordinates through the 1,4-nitrogen atoms. Most importantly, the CC bonds of the μ3-Lene ligands in CPs 1–3 are oriented parallel and close to each other (distances: 3.827 Å in 1-Cl, 3.691 Å in 2, and 3.697 Å in 3), but those of the μ2-Lene ligands in 1 and 2 are remote from each other (Fig. S13†). This thus suggests that the photoreaction extent could be controllable. Thermogravimetric analyses (TG) of CPs 1–3 under nitrogen atmosphere show that all the skeletons are stable up to ∼300 °C (Fig.S1 to S3†).

It is the suitable alignment of the μ3-Lene ligands that meets the Schmidt's criteria (CC bonds aligned parallel and at a distance less than 4.2 Å from each other). When exposed to UV light, CPs 1–3 transform into CPs 1′–3′ in a SCSC manner through the [2 + 2] cycloaddition of partial or full CC bonds. This reaction occurs uniformly within the molecule (e.g., intra-tube, intra-layer, and intra-framework). In all cases, each pair of μ3-Lene ligands is arranged head-to-tail, thus giving the anti Lane product.2b The dimerization reaction changes the bond parameters around the Zn(ii) ions little (Tables S1 to S3†), showing that the elongated ligand is prone to retain the single-crystalline nature of the compound during this reaction. Although the reaction proceeds easily through local atomic relocation,6 it is of great significance for first illustrating the SCSC phenomenons of kindred CPs across all dimensions. This example of such a system highlights the excellent ability of the tetrazolate moiety in the orientation of olefins.

We examined the solid-state photoluminescent properties using bulk crystals at room temperature. Fig. 4 presents the emission spectra of CPs 1–3 before and after 24 hours of irradiation. CP 2 and the HLene molecule (as solids obtained from the acid hydrolysis of 2) give a broad band emission from 450 to 650 nm, attributable to the ligand-based π*→π and π*→n transitions. Due to the presence of halide-to-metal charge transfer (XMCT),7 the emission bands of CPs 1 and 3 (around 400–600 nm) are a little blue-shifted and relatively weak. After conversion to 1′–3′, the emission bands are changed remarkably. Those of 1′ and 2′ mainly show a decrease in intensity, but that of 3′ also exhibits a large blue shift (∼90 nm). The luminosities of 1′ and 2′ are dominated by the emission from Lene, as the Lene has a larger π-conjugation system than Lane.8 The emission from 3′ implies the exhaustion of Lene, giving no band around 400–600 nm; instead, one around 375–525 nm belonging to Lane was present. These observations of 3 make it a good candidate for molecular memory or switches.9

The large decrease in the luminescence of 2 provides a chance for the study of its photoreaction kinetics, as the emission intensity is only a function of time (Fig. S9†). The plot of intensity versus time is presented in Fig. 5. It can be seen that the reaction undergoes two steps, with the first phase at around 0–10 min and the second phase at around 10–120 min. Both phases fit first-order kinetics well, with kI = 0.205 min−1 (R2 = 0.998) and kII = 0.020 min−1 (R2 = 0.999). The turning point at ∼10 min may be attributed to the phase transition to an intermediate with a high stress level.10 Previous work has shown that the reaction of a dispersed sample in KBr pellets is fast and complete.11 We here propose that this is not only because of the increased surface area but also because of the decreased stress in the ultrafine grains. As we know, the single crystal often appears cracked after the reaction.

In summary, we succeeded in the synthesis of 1D–3D Zn(ii) CPs with a tetrazolyl olefin ligand and realized SCSC [2 + 2] photodimerization. This is the first time for the introduction of a tetrazolate group into an olefin ligand to construct photoreactive coordination polymers. After the reaction, complexes 1 and 2 show a decrease in luminescence, but complex 3 also exhibits a luminescence blue shift. The kinetics study by in situ fluorescence experiments reveals that the photodimerization reaction undergoes two steps with a phase transition point.

Experimental section

Syntheses of 1 and 1′

A mixture of 4-(4-cyanostyryl)pyridine (0.014 g, 0.07 mmol), NaN3 (0.005 g, 0.07 mmol), and ZnCl2 or ZnBr2 (0.07 mol) in a mixed i-PrOH–H2O (2 mL/4 mL) solvent was sealed in a 12 mL Teflon-lined steel reactor and heated at 160 °C for 48 hours, followed by cooling to room temperature at a rate of 5 °C per hour. Yellow block crystals of 1 were isolated, washed with ethanol and then dried in air. Yields were 83% based on 4-(4-cyanostyryl)pyridine. Crystals of 1 were irradiated with a Hg lamp (24 W) for 24 hours to generate crystals of 1′.

Syntheses of 2 and 2′

A mixture of 4-(4-cyanostyryl)pyridine (0.014 g, 0.07 mmol), NaN3 (0.014 g, 0.21 mmol), and Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (0.031 g, 0.105 mol) in a mixed i-PrOH–H2O (2 mL/4 mL) solvent was sealed in a 12 mL Teflon-lined steel reactor and heated at 160 °C for 48 hours, followed by cooling to room temperature at a rate of 5 °C per hour. Yellow block crystals were isolated, washed with ethanol and then dried in air. Yields were 81% based on 4-(4-cyanostyryl)pyridine. Crystals of 2 were irradiated with a Hg lamp (24 W) for 24 hours to generate crystals of 2′.

Syntheses of 3 and 3′

A mixture of 4-(4-cyanostyryl)pyridine (0.014 g, 0.07 mmol), NaN3 (0.005 g, 0.07 mmol), and ZnBr2 (0.063 g, 0.28 mol) in a mixed i-PrOH–H2O (2 mL/4 mL) solvent was sealed in a 12 mL Teflon-lined steel reactor and heated at 160 °C for 48 hours, followed by cooling to room temperature at a rate of 5 °C per hour. Yellow block crystals were isolated, washed with ethanol and then dried in air. Yields were 65% based on 4-(4-cyanostyryl)pyridine. Crystals of 3 were irradiated with a Hg lamp (24 W) for 24 hours to generate crystals of 3′.

Single-crystal X-ray crystallography

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction data were collected on an Agilent Technologies Gemini A System (Mo Kα, λ = 0.71073 Å) at 293 K. The data were processed using CrysAlis. All structures were solved by direct methods and refined by full-matrix least-squares cycles on F2. CCDC reference numbers 841881–841886.
