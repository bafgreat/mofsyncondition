1 Introduction

The oxovanadium phosphate (V–P–O) system is a structurally diverse1 subclass of the family of inorganic oxides, whose chemistry has been extensively explored in relation to efficacy of vanadyl pyrophosphate (VO)2P2O72,3 and vanadyl phosphite4,5 in the selective air oxidation of butane to maleic anhydride. The expansive literature on these oxides includes not only studies directed at the catalytic mechanism of such processes, but also the design of related materials. The compositional range and structural diversity of the V–P–O system reflects the variable oxidation states and coordination polyhedra available to the vanadium center, the possibility of aggregation through condensation of vanadium polyhedra into oligomeric aggregates or chain or layered vanadium oxide substructures, and the variations in vanadium and phosphorus polyhedral connectivities, as well as protonation of oxygen sites and coordination of aqua ligands.

Further structural expansion may be achieved by introducing organic components to provide hybrid materials which combine the unique characteristics of the organic and inorganic components.6,7 While organic components are commonly introduced as structure-directing and charge compensating subunits in imitation of zeolite chemistry,8,9 the tetrahedral phosphate component may be directly derivatized to achieve incorporation of an organic moiety.10 The resultant oxovanadium–organophosphonate chemistry provides a variety of solid state materials11–27 as well as molecular species of considerable structural diversity.14

The structural chemistry of the solid oxides of the oxovanadium–organophosphonate class is influenced both by the identity of the organic substituent of the anionic {VxOy(O3PR)3}n− subunit, in the sense of bulk or additional functionality on the monophosphonate ligands, {O3P(RX)}2−, and of spacer length in the tethered diphosphonate subunits, {O3P–R–PO3}4−, and by the nature of the charge-compensating cation. Thus, with organoammonium or alkali metal cations, anionic V–P–O layers, buttressed by the organic tether of the diphosphonate, are a recurrent structural theme (Scheme 1).18,25

However, the chemistry of the cationic component could be further elaborated by replacing the simple charge separated organoammonium or alkali metal cations with a transition metal coordination complex cation, {ML}n+,28 so as to exploit the coordination preferences of this secondary metal site and the geometric variations attainable at the ligand site. Copper organoimine complex cations have been demonstrated to be particularly effective as charge-compensating components, which in addition coordinate to the oxygen atoms of the V–P–O component.28–33 In previous investigations, we have shown that, in general, the structural motif of V–P–O layers buttressed by the organodiphosphonate tethers is maintained for {Cu(bpy)}2+ (bpy = 2,2′-bipyridine) and {Cu(o-phen)}2+ (o-phen = ortho-phenanthroline) as the secondary-metal ligand components. In these materials, the copper(ii)–organoimine components serve to decorate the surfaces of the V–P–O layers.28,33 In contrast, when {Cu(terpy)}2+ (terpy = 2,2′:6′,2″-terpyridine) is introduced as the cationic component, one-, two- and three-dimensional structures are obtained, depending on the identity of the spaces of the organodiphosphonate ligand.34

The profound structural influence of the {Cu(terpy)}2+ subunit in affecting surface modification of the V–P–O substructure encouraged us to investigate the role of bisterpy (2,2′:4′:2″,2‴-quarterpyridyl-6′,6″-di-2-pyridine) in providing a rigid dipodal rod, {Cu2(bisterpy)}4+,35 in the construction of Cu–organoimine/VxOy/organodiphosphonate phases (Scheme 2). Consequently, the transmission of structural information through this rigid linker, while introducing variations in the tether length of the diphosphonate, {O3P(CH2)nPO3}4−, the flexible dipodal connector, could be exploited. Hydrothermal synthesis provided seven novel hybrid materials of the {Cu2(bisterpy)}4+/VxOyn−/{O3P(CH2)nPO3}4− family:

[{Cu2(bisterpy)}V2O4{O3PCH2PO2(OH)}2] (1), [{Cu2(bisterpy)(H2O)2}V2O4{O3P(CH2)2PO3}{HO3P(CH2)2PO3H}] (2), [{Cu2(bisterpy)}V4O8(PO3(CH2)3PO3)2]·4H2O (3·4H2O), [{Cu2(bisterpy)(H2O)}VO2{O3P(CH2)3PO3}{HO3P(CH2)3PO3H2}] (4), [{Cu2(bisterpy)}V2O4(OH)2{O3P(CH2)4PO3}]·4H2O (5·4H2O), [{Cu2(bisterpy)}V4O4{O3P(CH2)5PO3H}4]·7.3H2O (6·7.3H2O), and [{Cu2(bisterpy)}V2O4{O3P(CH2)6PO3H}2]·H2O (7·2H2O).

2 Experimental

2.1 General considerations

All chemicals were used as obtained without further purification. Copper(ii) acetate monohydrate, copper(ii) oxide, vanadium(v) oxide, sodium orthovanadate, and hydrofluoric acid (48–51%) were purchased from Aldrich; methylenediphosphonic acid was purchased from Alfa Aesar, and 1,2-ethylenediphosphonic acid was purchased from Lancaster. Bisterpy was prepared according to the literature method.36 The diphosphonate ligands1,3-propylenediphosphonic acid, 1,4-butylenediphosphonic acid, 1,5-pentylenediphosphonic acid, and 1,6-hexylene diphosphonic acid were prepared according to the literature methods.37,38 All syntheses were carried out in 23 ml poly(tetrafluoroethylene) lined stainless steel containers under autogenous pressure. The reactants were stirred briefly and initial pH measured before heating. Water was distilled above 3.0 MΩ in-house using a Barnstead Model 525 Biopure Distilled Water Center. The initial and final pH of the reactions were measured using Hydrion pH sticks.

2.8 X-Ray crystallography

Crystallographic data for all compounds were collected with a Brüker P4 diffractometer equipped with a SMART CCD system39 and using Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). The data were collected at 90 K and corrected for Lorentz and polarization effects.40 Absorption corrections were made using SADABS.41 The structure solutions and refinements were carried out using the SHELXTL42 crystallographic software package. The structures were solved using direct methods, and all of the non-hydrogen atoms were located from the initial solution. After locating all of the non-hydrogen atoms in each structure, the model was refined against F2, initially using isotropic then anisotropic thermal displacement parameters, until the final value of Δ/σmax was less than 0.001. Hydrogen atoms with the exception of those associated with water molecules of crystallization which were not included either at observed or calculated positions, were located at calculated positions and included in the final cycles of refinement using a riding model. 3 Results and discussion

3.1 Syntheses and structures

Conventional hydrothermal techniques44 provide a convenient synthetic method for the preparation and crystallization of organic/inorganic hybrid materials. The reactions of Na3VO3, a Cu(ii) salt, the appropriate diphosphonic acid and bisterpy in H2O at temperatures in the range 120–180 °C for 72–114 h produced crystalline samples of 1–7 in moderate to good yields. While compounds 1, 2 and 5–7 were obtained as monophasic materials, oxides3 and 4 could only be obtained as a mixture. The reported synthesis is optimized; variations in stoichiometry, pH, temperature and fill volume resulted in reduced yields or amorphous mixtures.

It is noteworthy that in the case of compound 6 the vanadium sites have been reduced to the V(iv) state, although the reaction conditions are not significantly different from those employed to isolate the V(v) species. Such reduction of V(v) to V(iv) is not uncommon in the hydrothermal chemistry in the presence of nitrogenous ligands. The interplay of relative redox potentials, temperature and stoichiometry has been shown to result in reduction to the V(iv) and even the V(iii) oxidation states. Of course, the yields are not quantitative, and some V(v) species may be present on by-products of the reaction.

The IR spectra of 1–7 are characterized by medium to strong intensity bands in the 900–945 cm−1 range attributed to ν(VO). In addition, a series of three or four bands in the 1000–1600 cm−1 range are attributed to the diphosphonate ligands.

As shown in Fig. 1, the structure of [{Cu2(bisterpy)}V2O4{O3PCH2PO3H}2] (1) is one-dimensional. The structure may be described as [V2O4{O3PCH2PO3H}2]4− clusters linked through the rigid dipodal {Cu2(bisterpy)}4+ subunits into a chain. The V(v) sites exhibit distorted trigonal bipyramidal geometry, defined by two terminal oxo-groups and an oxygen donor from a methylenediphosphonate group in the equatorial plane and two oxygen atoms from different diphosphonate ligands in the axial positions. Consequently, each methylenediphosphonate ligand contributes three oxygen donors to the vanadium centers. One {PO3} terminus of the ligand engages in µ2-O,O′ bridging between the two V(v) sites of the cluster, while the second contribute a single oxygen vertex to one vanadium center. In addition, each methylenediphosphonate group chelates through oxygen donors from each phosphorus terminus to the Cu(ii) site to generate a six-membered {Cu–O–P–C–P–O} ring. One oxygen atom of the methylenediphosphonate is pendant and protonated. The polyhedral connectivity within the [V2O4{O3PCH2PO3H}2]4− cluster produces a twelve-membered {V–O–P–C–P–O}2 ring.

The square pyramidal Cu(ii) sites enjoy coordination to the three nitrogen donors of one terpyridine terminus of the bisterpy ligand and to one diphosphonate oxygen donor in the basal plane and to a second diphosphonate donor in the apical position. The bisterpy ligand is planar and the two linked Cu(ii) square pyramids adopt an anti-orientation of apical oxygen atoms with respect to the plane.

In contrast to the structure of 1, [{Cu2(bisterpy)(H2O)2}V2O4{O3P(CH2)2PO3}{HO3P(CH2)2PO3H}] (2) is two-dimensional, as shown in Fig. 2. The structure consists of [V2O4{O3P(CH2)2PO3}{HO3P(CH2)2PO3H}] 4n−n chains linked through {Cu(bisterpy)(H2O)2}4+ rods. The V(v) sites exhibit distorted tetrahedral geometry defined by two terminal oxo-groups and two oxygen donors from adjacent ethylenediphosphonate ligands. There are two distinct diphosphonate environments. One exhibits corner-sharing of each phosphate tetrahedron to an adjacent vanadium site and to an adjacent Cu(ii) site; consequently this ethylenediphosphonate group links two vanadium and two copper sites. One oxygen donor on each phosphorus terminus is pendant and unprotonated as suggested by the average PO bond distance of 1.504(2) Å, compared to an average P–O distance of 1.553(2) Å for those bonded to vanadium sites. The second diphosphonate subunit links adjacent V(v) sites through as oxygen donor of each phosphorus terminus. Each {PO3} group exhibits two pendant oxygen atoms, one protonated with an average P–O(H) distance of 1.552(2) Å and the second displaying significant PO multiple bond character, as suggested by the P–O distance of 1.488(2) Å.

The square pyramidal Cu(ii) sites exhibit three nitrogen donors of one terpy terminus of the bisterpy and a diphosphonate oxygen donor in the basal plane and aqua ligand occupying the apical position. The bisterpy is characteristically planar with the Cu(ii) polyhedra in the anti-configuration with respect to the plane.

With propylenediphosphonate as the flexible linker, two distinct structures are observed, the two-dimensional [{Cu2(bisterpy)}V4O8{O3P(CH2)3PO3}2]·4H2O (3·4H2O) and the one-dimensional [{Cu2(bisterpy)(H2O)}VO2{O3P(CH2)3PO3}{HO3P(CH2)3PO3H}] (4), shown in Figs. 3 and 4, respectively. The layer structure of 3 is constructed from [V4O8{O3P(CH2)3PO3}2] 4n−n chains linked through {Cu2(bisterpy)}4+ rods. The chain substructure may be described as [V4O8{O3PR}4]4− clusters of corner-sharing V(v) square pyramids and phosphorus tetrahedra, linked through the propylene chains of the diphosphonate ligands. There are two distinct V(v) sites: one exhibits an apical terminal oxo-group and a terminal oxo-group defining a basal vertex, with three oxygen donors, two from one phosphorus terminus of one propylenediphosphonate ligand and one from a second, in the remaining basal positions; the second site is defined by an oxo-group bridging to a Cu(ii) site in the apical position, a terminal oxo-group in one basal site and oxygen donors from three diphosphonate ligands in the remaining basal sites. The [V4O8{O3PR)4]4− clusters are characterized by the presence of the common {V–O–P–O}2 eight-membered ring which is fused on either end to {V–O–V–O–P–O} six-membered rings, as a consequence of one diphosphonate oxygen atom adopting a µ3-(V2,P) bonding mode.

The propylenediphosphonate ligand bonds in an unsymmetrical fashion. One terminus shares oxygen vertices with three V(v) sites, while the other shares two vertices with vanadium sites (one bridging two sites, the other in the more common singly bridging mode) and one with the Cu(ii) site.

The Cu(ii) sites exhibit the common square pyramidal geometry with a vanadium oxo-group at the apex and three nitrogen donors and a phosphonate oxygen in the basal plane. While the {Cu2(bisterpy)}4+ geometry is unexceptional, it is noteworthy that the Cu⋯Cu axis of the {Cu2(bisterpy)}4+ rod makes an angle of ca. 25° with respect to the V–P–O–C chain axis, in contrast to the angle of ca. 90° observed in the case of compound 2.

The one-dimensional structure of 4, shown in Fig. 4, exhibits a number of unanticipated features. The chain may be described as [V2O4{O3P(CH2)3PO3}]2− clusters linked through {Cu(bisterpy)}4+ rods, decorated by {HO3P(CH2CH2CH2)PO3H2}− groups. The clusters are composed of sixteen-membered {V–O–P–C–C–C–P–O}2 heterocyclic rings or alternatively twelve edge-sharing vanadium, phosphorus and carbon tetrahedra. Each tetrahedral vanadium site exhibits two terminal oxo-groups and two oxygen donors from the diphosphonate groups of the ring.

Each {PO3} terminus of the diphosphonate groups of the ring employs one oxygen donor in bonding to a vanadate site and one in bridging to a Cu(ii) site, leaving a pendant multiply bonded {PO} unit (P–O distance, 1.504(2) Å (av.)). The second propylenediphosphonate group acts as a monodentate ligand to the copper site. Consequently, the ligand is triply protonated [HO3P(CH2)3PO3H2]− with the (–PO3H2) unit pendant. The protonation sites are clearly established by the bond lengths: P–O(H), 1.559(2) Å (av.); PO, 1.506(2) Å (av); P–O(Cu), 1.498(2) Å.

There are also two distinct square pyramidal Cu(ii) sites. The first coordinates to three nitrogen donors of the bisterpy ligand and oxygen donors from two propylenediphosphonate groups. The oxygen donor of the pendant [HO3P(CH2)3PO3H2]− group occupies the apical position. The second copper center is characterized by three nitrogen donors and a phosphonate oxygen in the basal plane and an apical aqua ligand. In contrast to structures 1–3, the {Cu2(bisterpy)(H2O)}4+ subunit is not planar but rather shows an angle of 47° between the planes of the terpyridine groups.

While the structure of [{Cu2(bisterpy)}V2O4(OH)2{O3P(CH2)4PO3}]·4H2O (5·4H2O), shown in Fig. 5, is two-dimensional, the oxovanadium-diphosphonate substructure is a simple [V2O4(OH)2{O3P(CH2)4PO3}]4− molecular fragment. Each tetrahedral V(v) site bonds to an oxygen donor of the butyldiphosphonate ligand, two terminal oxo-groups (V–O, 1.659(2) Å (av.)) and a terminal hydroxy group (V–O(H), 1.736(1) Å). Each {–PO3} terminus of the diphosphonate ligand bonds to the V(v) site and to two Cu(ii) sites to produce an assembly, [Cu2V2O4(OH)2{O3P(CH2)4PO3}], of edge-sharing tetrahedra and square pyramids. The Cu(ii) coordination geometry is composed of three nitrogen donors from the bisterpy ligand and oxygen donors from two diphosphonate subunits. The structure may be described as [Cu2{O3P(CH2)4PO3}]n chains, decorated with pendant {VO2(OH)} groups, and connected through the bisterpy scaffold into a two-dimensional network. The {Cu2(bisterpy)}4+ exhibits the unexceptional planarity and anti-configurations of the Cu(ii) square pyramids.

The oxide [{Cu2(bisterpy)}V4O4{O3P(CH2)5PO3H}4]·7.3H2O (6·7.3H2O) adopts the complex three-dimensional structure shown in Fig. 6. The structure is best described as [V4O4{O3P–}4{HO3P–}4] 4n−n chains, each chain linked through the pentyl tethers to four adjacent chains to produce the overall 3-D framework of the oxovanadium–pentyldiphosphonate substructure. There are two distinct V(iv) square pyramidal environments. The first exhibits an apical multiply bonded oxo-group which bridges to the Cu(ii) site, with the basal plane defined by four oxygen donors from four different diphosphonate ligands. The second V(iv) site is characterized by a terminal oxo-group in the apical position and four oxygen donors from four different diphosphonate ligands in the basal plane.

There are also two unique pentyldiphosphonate environments. One {–PO3} terminus of the first ligand engages in corner-sharing with two vanadium sites and a Cu(ii) site, while the other terminus links to two vanadium centers and projects a pendant –OH group. The second diphosphonate ligand employs one {–PO3} terminus to connect three adjacent vanadium sites, while the other phosphorus tetrahedron engages in bonding to a single vanadium center, projecting pendant {PO} and {P–OH} groups (P–O distances: 1.500(3) Å and 1.572(3) Å, respectively).

The connectivity of vanadium and phosphorus polyhedra produces ruffled V–P–O chains with the characteristic di-µ-(O,O′) phosphate bridges.1 Consequently, the chain may be described as an assembly of fused eight-membered {V2P2O4} rings.

The protonation of an oxygen atom of each unique diphosphonate ligand identifies the ligands as the triply-deprotonated {O3P(CH2)5PO3H}3− subunits, consequently requiring that the vanadium sites be in the +4 oxidation state. This observation is confirmed by valence sum calculations45 and by the magnetic susceptibility studies (vide infra).

The Cu(ii) sites exhibit the unremarkable square pyramidal geometry, defined by an apical oxo-group bridging to a vanadium site and three nitrogen donors and a phosphonate oxygen in the basal plane. The {Cu2(bisterpy)}4+ rods exhibit the characteristic planarity and anti-disposition of the Cu polyhedra. The {Cu2(bisterpy)}4+ subunits are embedded within the oxovanadium pentyldiphosphonate framework and occupy channels running parallel to the crystallographic b-axis.

The one-dimensional structure of [{Cu2(bisterpy)}V2O4{O3P(CH2)6PO3H}2]·2H2O (7·2H2O) is shown in Fig. 7. The chain is constructed from [V2O4{O3P(CH2)6PO3H}2]4− molecular fragments, linked through {Cu2(bisterpy)}4+ subunits. The V(v) sites exhibit distorted five coordination, conforming somewhat more closely to the trigonal bipyramidal rather than the square pyramidal idealized geometry.46 The vanadium coordination is defined by two terminal oxo-groups and three oxygen donors from two different diphosphonate ligands. One phosphorus terminus of the diphosphonate ligands shares vertices with the vanadium center and the copper site and exhibits a pendant –OH group (P–O, 1.555(2) Å). The second terminus participates in edge-sharing to the vanadium site through two oxygen atoms, one of which also bonds to the copper site; the third oxygen is pendant and multiply bonded (PO, 1.490(2) Å). Consequently, the structure may be alternatively described as {CuVO2(O3P–)(HO3P–)} clusters bridged in an alternating pattern by bisterpy and pentyl tethers.

3.2 General structural observations

A curious feature of the structural chemistry of the Cu2(bisterpy)/oxovanadium/organodiphosphonate system of this study is the absence of a persistent V–P–O building block, as revealed in Table 3. One consequence of this observation is an unusual structural variability reflected in seven unique structures as the organic spacer group –(CH2)n– is varied from n = 1 to n = 6.

In contrast, the Cu(ii)-ligand/oxomolybdenum/organodiphosphonate system is derived from a prototypical molecular building block {Mo5O15(O3PR)2}4−, which is observed in the molecular chemistry of the polyoxometalates and which persists in the solid state chemistry as an integral building block.47–52 While the oxovanadium organophosphonate system also exhibits a rich cluster chemistry, under both conventional and hydrothermal conditions,14 the cluster chemistry does not persist in the solid state, a characteristic noted previously for the Cu(ii)–L/oxomolybdenum/organophosphonate systems with L = 2,2′-bipyridine, o-phenanthroline and terpyridine.18,53,54

Not only does the {Cu2(bisterpy)}4+/oxovanadium/diphosphonate system not exhibit embedded, well-defined clusters in the structural chemistry, but there are also no instances of small oligomeric subunits such as binuclear or trinuclear {VxOy}n− aggregates with V–O–V bonds. In contrast to the Cu(ii)–L/oxovanadium/organophosphonate materials (L = bpy, phen and terpy) which exhibit several examples of binuclear, trinuclear and even tetranuclear oxovanadium building blocks, the structures of this study are constructed exclusively from isolated vanadium polyhedra, i.e., no V–O–V bonds.

On the other hand, the materials of this study do possess a range of vanadophosphonate substructures, including clusters, chains and a three-dimensional framework. Curiously, there are no two-dimensional vanadophosphonate motifs, which are a relatively common feature of the Cu(ii)–L/oxovanadium/organophosphonate systems with L = bpy, phen and terpy. In fact, in view of the persistence of two-dimensional motifs based on the {V2O2(µ-O,O′-phosphate/phosphonate)2} subunit in the V–P–O structural chemistry, the absence of two-dimensional V–P–O substructures in the materials of this study is quite unexpected. Furthermore, oxide6 presents a unique example of a material of this class constructed from a three-dimensional vanadophosphonate substructure, an observation which may be related to the spatial extension provided by the pentyl spacer of the diphosphonate ligand.

The subsequent decrease of the magnetic moment with decreasing temperature is associated with long range antiferromagnetic interactions.

4 Conclusions

Hydrothermal synthesis provides a convenient preparative method for the isolation of a novel series of organic-inorganic hybrid materials, incorporating both rigid rod (bisterpy) and flexible (alkyl tether of the diphosphonate) organic spacers. Variations in alkyl tether length provided the one-dimensional oxides [{Cu2(bisterpy)}V2O4{O3PCH2PO3H}2] (1), [{Cu2(bisterpy)(H2O)}VO2{O3P(CH2)3PO3}{HO3P(CH2)3PO3H2}] (4) and [{Cu2(bisterpy)}V2O4{O3P(CH2)6PO3H}2]·2H2O (7·2H2O), the two-dimensional materials [{Cu2(bisterpy)(H2O)2}V2O4{O3P(CH2)2PO3}{HO3P(CH2)2PO3H}2] (2), [{Cu2(bisterpy)}V4O8{O3P(CH2)3PO3}2]·4H2O (3·4H2O) and [{Cu2(bisterpy)}V2O4(OH)2{O3P(CH2)4PO3}]·4H2O (5·4H2O) and the three-dimensional framework [{Cu2(bisterpy)}V4O4{O3P(CH2)5PO3H}4]·7.3H2O (6·7.3H2O). A variety of vanadophosphonate substructures based on embedded clusters, chains and frameworks are encountered. However the vanadium building blocks are in all cases “isolated” {VOx} polyhedra; that is, there are no V–O–V bonds.

The absence of clear structural systematics may reflect the accessibility of different vanadium polyhedral geometries and oxidation states, as well as the variability available in polyhedral connectivity between copper, phosphorus and vanadium subunits. Furthermore, variable protonation of phosphonate oxygen donors and even of oxovanadium sites enhances the structural complexity. The tether length and flexibility of the diphosphonate ligand also results in unanticipated structural consequences. Combine these factors with the inherent dynamism of the hydrothermal reaction domain, and it is no wonder that structural predictability remains elusive. The systematization of this structural chemistry, in order to achieve rational design of solid organic–inorganic hybrids, requires continued elaboration of the crystal data base so as to define the structural possibilities.
