Introduction

Coordination polymers (CPs), metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) or metal-cluster based organic frameworks (MCOFs) have attracted great interest in inorganic chemistry because of their fascinating topologies and their potential applications as functional materials.1,2 The metal ions and organic ligands such as N-donor and polycarboxylate ligands are the key factors for assembling CPs, MOFs or MCOFS.3–5 However, many other factors, such as solvent and reaction temperature also play an important role in assembly of CPs, MOFs or MCOFS.6,7

For example, Tzeng and coworkers obtained distinct coordination polymers which show 1D, 2D, and 3D network structures by the reactions of 4,4′-dipyridylsulfide (Py2S) with Cd(ClO4)2 in a variety of mixed solvents (DMF–CH2Cl2, MeCN–CH2Cl2, and MeOH–CH2Cl2).6d Chen and coworkers synthesized two mononuclear complexes, [Mn(dpt24)2(MeOH)2] and [Mn(dpt24)2(H2O)2]·6H2O, and three isomeric two dimensional (2D) coordination polymers α-[Mn(dpt24)2], β-[Mn(dpt24)2]·g (g = DMF and H2O), and γ-[Mn-(dpt24)2]·g (g = toluene and MeOH) with the reactions of 3-(2-pyridyl)-5-(4-pyridyl)-1,2,4-triazole (Hdpt24) with Mn(OAc)2 under different conditions.6e Duan and coworkers synthesized two porous coordination polymers (PCPs), [Cu6(L)4(H2O)6]·10DMA·4EtOH and [Cu5(L)2(OH)2(H2O)2(DMA)2]·2DMA·EtOH·2H2O which show an unusual non-crystallographic (NC) net to a (4,6)-connected framework with an fsh topology in DMA/EtOH and DMA/EtOH/H2O solvent systems (H6L = 1,3,5-tris(3-carboxy-4-hydroxylphenyl)benzene).6f The hydrothermal method is very effective for the synthesis of CPs, MOFs or MCOFS which are difficult to obtain by the traditional methods.8

Organic dyes such as methyl orange (MO), methylene blue (MB) and rhodamine B (RhB) which are resistant to biodegradation are extensively used in the textile industry. Decomposition of these dye molecules into simple molecules can diminish the environment pollution of organic dyes. Recently, coordination polymers (CPs), metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) and metal-cluster based organic frameworks (MCOFs) have demonstrated good photocatalytic activity for the degradation of organic dyes.9–11

Multicopper oxidases contain a trinuclear cluster core [Cu3(OH)] or [Cu3(O)] and thus have generated great interest in the synthesis of copper complexes containing a [Cu3(μ3-OH)] core.12–14 Several compounds containing [Cu4(μ3-OH)2] tetranuclear copper(ii) clusters in which two [Cu3(μ3-OH)] cores share two same copper(ii) atoms were also obtained.14 In a previous work, we synthesized many MOFs using flexible bis(triazole) ligands.15,16

Polythreaded nets, one of entanglement, can be considered as extended periodic analogues of molecular rotaxanes and pseudorotaxanes.17–21 There are only a few polythreaded structures with 2D components such as 1D chains and 2D sheets (1D + 2D → 2D) or (1D + 2D → 3D)18 and 2D sheets (2D → 3D).19–21

The bis(imidazole) and bis(triazole) N-donor ligands have been widely used for construction of novel CPs, MOFs or MCOFs.4,5 The designation of new ligands is very important for the development of CPs, MOFs or MCOFS. In the present work, we designed and synthesized a new ligand, 1-imidazol-1-yl-3-(1,2,4-triazol-4-yl)propane (itp), which has never been reported in the literature, as the main ligand to construct new CPs, MOFs or MCOFs based mostly on the following considerations: (1) the asymmetric itp ligand containing both imidazole and triazole rings should yield different CPs, MOFs or MCOFs, compared with the widely used symmetric ligands such as bis(imidazole) and bis(triazole) N-donor ligands. (2) The imidazole and triazole rings can freely twist around three –CH2– groups with different angles to generate different conformations and coordination modes. (3) The 4-substituted 1,2,4-triazole ring can act in N1/N2 bidentate coordination mode and easily construct MCOFs.

In our effort to synthesize unusual coordination polymers with intriguing structures and high and efficient photocatalytic activity for the degradation of the organic dyes (MO, MB and RhB), we fortunately synthesized two intriguing coordination polymers {[Cu4(OH)2(itp)2(btc)2(H2O)4][Cu(H2O)4(Hbtc)]2·4H2O}n (1) and {[Cu4(OH)2(itp)2(btc)2]·2EtOH·2H2O}n (2) by the hydrothermal reaction of 1-imidazol-1-yl-3-(1,2,4-triazol-4-yl)propane (itp) and 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylate (btc) ligands and CuCl2 in H2O or EtOH/H2O solvent. {[Cu4(OH)2(itp)2(sip)2(H2O)4]·4H2O}n (3) was also obtained by the hydrothermal reaction of itp, 5-sulfoisophthalate (sip) and CuCl2 in H2O solvent. 1–3 show diverse structures based on the tetranuclear copper(ii) cluster [Cu4(μ3-OH)2]. The photocatalytic experiment shows that 1–3 are universal and highly efficient photocatalysts for the degradation of the organic dyes MO, MB and RhB under UV irradiation, are very stable and easily separated from the reaction system for reuse.

Experimental section

Materials and physical measurements

1-Imidazol-1-yl-3-(1,2,4-triazol-4-yl)propane (itp) was synthesized according to the literature method using 3-imidazol-1-yl-propylamine.22 All reagents were of analytical grade and used without further purification. Elemental analyses for C, H and N were performed on a Perkin-Elmer 240C analyser. IR spectra were obtained for KBr pellets on a Nicolet 170SX FT-IR spectrophotometer in the 4000–400 cm−1 region. PXRD was performed on a D/MAX-3C diffractometer with CuKα radiation (λ = 1.5406 Å) at room temperature. Diffuse reflectance spectra of the solid samples were collected using a Cary 500 spectrophotometer equipped with a 110 mm diameter integration sphere, and measured from 200 nm to 800 nm using barium sulfate (BaSO4) as a standard with 100% reflectance. TGA was carried out using a Thermal Analyst 2100 TA Instrument and an SDT 2960 Simultaneous TGA-DTA Instrument in flowing dinitrogen at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1.

A mixture of itp (0.1 mmol), H3btc (0.1 mmol), CuCl2·2H2O (0.2 mmol), NaOH (0.28 mmol) and H2O (10 mL) was placed in a Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel, heated to 110 °C for three days, then cooled to room temperature over 24 h. Blue block crystals of 1 were obtained. Yield: 43% based on btc. Anal. calc. for C52H62Cu6N10O38 (1): C, 34.39; H, 3.44; N, 7.71; found: C, 34.32; H, 3.41; N, 7.65%. A mixture of itp (0.1 mmol), H3btc (0.1 mmol), CuCl2·2H2O (0.2 mmol), NaOH (0.28 mmol), EtOH (6 mL) and H2O (6 mL) was placed in a Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel, heated to 110 °C for three days, then cooled to room temperature over 24 h. A mixture of itp (0.1 mmol), NaH2sip (0.1 mmol), CuCl2·2H2O (0.2 mmol), NaOH (0.20 mmol) and H2O (10 mL) was placed in a Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel, heated to 110 °C for three days, then cooled to room temperature over 24 h. X-ray crystallography

Single crystals of 1–3 suitable for X-ray analysis were carefully selected under an optical microscope and glued to thin glass fibers. The diffraction data were collected on an Agilent Gemini Atlas CCD diffractometer with graphite monochromated Cu Kα radiation. Intensities were collected using the ω scan technique. The structures were solved by direct methods and refined with the full-matrix least-squares technique (SHELXTL-97).23 The parameters of the crystal data collection and refinement of 1–3 are given in Table 1. Selected bond lengths and bond angles are listed in Table S1 in the ESI.†

Photocatalytic reactions

The experiment was carried out in a typical process. 1 (0.064 g), 2 (0.064 g) or 3 (0.064 g) and 1 mL of 30% H2O2 were added into 200 mL of a methyl orange (MO) solution (10 mg L−1), methylene blue (MB) solution (10 mg L−1), or rhodamine B (RhB) solution (10 mg L−1). The suspension solution was stirred in the dark for about 30 min. Then, the mixture was stirred continuously under UV irradiation from a 175 W high pressure mercury vapour lamp (main output 365 nm). At a given interval, aliquots of the reaction mixture were periodically taken and analyzed with a UV-vis spectrophotometer at an absorption wavelength of 465 nm for MO, 664 nm for MB and 552 nm for RhB. This procedure was repeated in the absence of catalyst as a blank comparison experiment.

Results and discussion

Crystal structure of {[Cu4(OH)2(itp)2(btc)2(H2O)4][Cu(H2O)4(Hbtc)]2·4H2O}n (1)

The single-crystal analysis revealed that 1 consists of an unusual two-dimensional network based on the tetranuclear copper(ii) clusters [Cu4(μ3-OH)2] and Cu1 cores (Cu3). In the [Cu4(μ3-OH)2] moiety, the Cu1 ion has a square-pyramidal geometry with a CuO4N chromophore and is coordinated by one carboxylate oxygen atom (O1) from one btc, two μ3-OH (O13, O13A), one coordinated water molecule (O14) and one triazole nitrogen atom (N1) from one itp ligand (Fig. S1 in the ESI†). The Cu1 ion lies 0.168(2) Å over the plane defined by the basal donor atoms (O1/O13/O13A/N1). The apical site is occupied by one coordinated water molecule (O14) with an elongated Cu–O bond length of 2.245(3) Å. The square-pyramidal coordination geometry of the Cu2 ion is due to the coordination of two carboxylate oxygen atoms (O2A and O3B), one μ3-OH (O13) and one triazole nitrogen atom from one itp ligand (N2) in the equatorial plane and one coordination water molecule (O15) in the apical position with an elongated Cu–O bond length of 2.280(2) Å. The Cu2 ion lies 0.161(2) Å over the plane defined by their basal donor atoms (Fig. S2 in the ESI†). One μ3-OH group (O13) connects three Cu(ii) ions (Cu1, Cu2, and Cu1A) and deviates from the Cu atom plane over 0.509(2) Å (Fig. 1a). Two μ3-OH groups (O13 and O13A) separate the Cu(ii) ions and generate a tetranuclear [Cu4(μ3-OH)2] moiety with a chair arrangement.

One carboxylate group (O1O2) of the btc ligand shows a bidentate coordination mode bridging two Cu(ii) ions (Cu1 and Cu2A) of the first tetranuclear Cu(ii) cluster. The other carboxylate group (O3O4) shows a monodentate coordination mode linking one Cu(ii) ion (Cu2D) of the second tetranuclear Cu(ii) cluster. The third carboxylate group (O5O6) is free and does not coordinate any Cu(ii) atom. Each [Cu4(μ3-OH)2] tetranuclear copper(ii) cluster connects two double btc and two itp ligands (Fig. 1a). The [Cu4(μ3-OH)2] tetranuclear copper(ii) clusters are bridged by double btc ligands to form the one-dimensional chain [Cu4(OH)2(btc)2]n.

In the Cu1 core, the Cu3 atom has a square-pyramidal geometry with a CuO4N chromophore and is coordinated by two carboxylate oxygen atoms (O7 and O10C) from two Hbtc, one coordinated water (O16) and one triazole nitrogen atom (N4) from one itp ligand in the equatorial plane and one coordination water (O18) in the apical position with an elongated Cu–O bond length of 2.530(3) Å (Fig. S1 in the ESI†). Two carboxylate groups (O7O8 and O9O10) of one Hbtc ligand act as monodentate nodes. The COOH group (O11O12) is free and does not coordinate any Cu(ii) atom. The Hbtc ligands exhibit 2-connected nodes and connect the Cu3 atoms to form the one-dimensional chain [Cu(Hbtc)]n.

Each itp ligand shows the anti–gauche conformation and connects two kinds of one-dimensional chains ([Cu4(OH)2(btc)2]n and [Cu(Hbtc)]n) to construct an unusual 2D network (Fig. 1b and Fig. S2 in the ESI†). Topologically, the tetranuclear copper(ii) cluster [Cu4(μ3-OH)2] is simplified as one node which are 4-connected (Fig. 1a). The double btc, Hbtc and itp ligands are 2-connected nodes. The Cu1 core (Cu3 atom) is a 3-connected node. The 2D network in 1 can be described as a (3,4)-connected 2D network based on tetranuclear copper(ii) cluster [Cu4(μ3-OH)2] and the Cu1 core (Cu3 atom) with a Schläfli symbol of (62·8)2(6·84·10) analyzed by the TOPOS program (Fig. 1c, Fig. S3 in the ESI†).24 This (3,4)-connected 2D network in 1 is unprecedented, to the best of our knowledge.

The most interesting structural feature of 1 is that the COOH groups of one (3,4)-connected 2D network insert into the lateral voids of two adjacent networks, resulting in an unusual 2D → 3D polythreaded motif (Fig. 1d and e). So far, only a few 2D → 3D polythreaded motifs are reported.17,19–21 Most of these 3D polythreaded frameworks are constructed from simple 2D 44-sql layers with dangling side arms or undulated 2D networks containing part blocks or frames which are located up and down each 2D array, and insert into the lateral voids of the adjacent networks to produce a 2D → 3D polythreaded motif. [Cd2(tp)(bpp)2Br2]n (H2tp = terephthalic acid, bpp = 1,3-bis(4-pyridyl)propane) forms a 2D → 3D polythreaded network based on undulated (6,3) sheets with dangling bpp arms.21a [Zn(CH3O-ip)(bime)0.5] (CH3O-ip = 5-methoxyisophthalate, bime = 1,2-bis(imidazol-1-yl) ethane) features an extended 3D polythreaded network based on 2D coordination frameworks with a (3,4)-connected (42·6)(42·63·8) topology.21b {[Zn(btre)0.5(OH-bdc)(H2O)2]·1.5H2O}n (btre = 1,2-bis(1,2,4-triazol-4-yl)ethane, (OH-bdc = 5-hydroxy-1,3-benzenedicarboxylate) shows parallel polythreading of two-fold polycatenated (6,3) layers to form a 2D → 3D polythreaded network.21c {[Cd2(m-pdoa)2(dpa)3(H2O)]}n (m-H2pdoa = 1,3-phenylenedioxy diacetic acid, dpa = 4,4′-dipyridylamine) shows mutually embedded bilayers originating from two reverse 2D 44-sql layers with 1D single side arms in an alternating fashion, and a 2D → 3D polythreaded structure.21d The 2D → 3D polythreaded motif based on this (3,4)-connected 2D network in 1 is unprecedented.

There are versatile hydrogen bonds between the COOH and lattice water (O11⋯O17 (−x + 1, −y + 2, −z)), the μ3-OH group and lattice water (O13⋯O16 (−x, −y + 1, −z)), the coordination water or lattice water and COO groups (O14⋯O19 (−x + 1, −y + 1, −z), O15⋯O5 (−x + 1, −y + 2, −z + 1), O15⋯O3 (x − 1, y − 1, z), O16⋯O4 (−x + 1, −y + 2, −z), O16⋯O6 (−x, −y + 2, −z), O17⋯O8, O18⋯O8 (−x + 1, −y + 2, −z), O19⋯O9 (x, y + 1, z), O19⋯O12), the coordination and lattice water (O14⋯O18 (−x, −y + 1, −z)), and lattice water and lattice water (O17⋯O19 (x, y−1, z)) (Table S2 in the ESI†). These hydrogen bonds sustain the stability of the 3D supramolecular architecture in 1.

Crystal structure of {[Cu4(OH)2(itp)2(btc)2]·2EtOH·2H2O}n (2)

2 shows an unusual (3,10)-connected three-dimensional metal–organic framework based on the tetranuclear copper(ii) cluster [Cu4(μ3-OH)2]. In the [Cu4(μ3-OH)2] moiety, the Cu1 atom has a distorted square-pyramidal geometry with a CuO3N2 chromophore and is coordinated by one carboxylate oxygen atom (O1) from one btc, one μ3-OH (O7A) and two nitrogen atoms (N1, N5B) from two itp ligands in the equatorial plane and one μ3-OH (O7) in the apical position with an elongated Cu–O bond length of 2.434(2) Å (Fig. S4 in the ESI†). The Cu2 atom is in a square-pyramidal coordination geometry, coordinated by two carboxylate oxygen atoms from two btc (O3C, O5D), one μ3-OH (O7) and one nitrogen atom from one itp ligand (N2A) in the equatorial plane and one carboxylate oxygen atom in the apical position (O2) with an elongated Cu–O bond length of 2.245(2) Å.

One μ3-OH group (O7) connects three Cu(ii) atoms (Cu1, Cu2 and Cu1A) and deviates from the Cu(ii) atom plane over 0.632(2) Å. Two μ3-OH groups (O7 and O7A) bridge the Cu(ii) atoms and generate a tetranuclear [Cu4(μ3-OH)2] moiety with a chair arrangement. There is one kind of btc ligand in 2. One carboxylate group (O1O2) of the btc ligand shows a bidentate coordination mode bridging two Cu(ii) atoms (Cu1 and Cu2) of one tetranuclear copper(ii) cluster. Two carboxylate groups (O3O4 and O5O6) exhibit a monodentate coordination mode linking one Cu(ii) atom of one tetranuclear copper(ii) cluster. Each btc ligand connects three [Cu4(μ3-OH)2] tetranuclear copper(ii) clusters (Fig. 2a). Each itp ligand shows the anti–anti conformation and connects three Cu(ii) atoms from two tetranuclear copper(ii) clusters (Fig. 2b). Each [Cu4(μ3-OH)2] tetranuclear copper(ii) cluster connects six btc and four itp ligands (Fig. 2c). The tetranuclear copper(ii) clusters are bridged by the btc and itp ligands to construct an unusual three-dimensional metal–organic framework (Fig. 2d). There are versatile hydrogen bonds between the μ3-OH group and COO group (O7⋯O6 (x, −y + 1/2, z + 1/2)), the lattice water and COO groups (O8⋯O1 (−x, y − 1/2, −z + 1/2), O8⋯O3), and the OH group of EtOH and COO group (O9⋯O3 (−x + 1, y − 1/2, −z + 1/2)) in 2 (Table S2 in the ESI†).

Topologically, the tetranuclear copper(ii) clusters [Cu4(μ3-OH)2] in 2 are simplified as one node which are 10-connected (Fig. 2c). The btc ligands are 3-connected nodes. The itp ligands are shown as 2-connected nodes. The 3D network in 2 can be described as a (3,10)-connected 3D network based on a tetranuclear copper(ii) cluster with the Schläfli symbol of (3·4·5)2(34·46·510·615·710) analyzed by the TOPOS program (Fig. 2e).24

Crystal structure of {[Cu4(OH)2(itp)2(sip)2(H2O)4]·4H2O}n (3)

Syntheses, PXRD and solid-state diffuse-reflectance study

1 and 2 were synthesized using the same ratio of reactants (Cu(ii), itp, btc) and temperature in different solvents (H2O for 1, EtOH/H2O for 2). No new coordination polymer other than 1 and 2 was obtained by changing the ratio of reactants (Cu(ii) : itp : btc = 2 : 1 : 1, or 1 : 1 : 1), pH (changing the reactant NaOH (0.20 mmol, 0.24 mmol or 0.28 mmol)) and temperature (90 or 110 °C). This result shows that the structures of coordination polymers can be adjusted by the solvent. 3 was obtained by the hydrothermal reaction of itp, 5-sulfoisophthalate (sip) and CuCl2 in H2O. No new coordination polymer other than 3 was synthesized by changing the ratio of reactants (Cu(ii) : itp : btc = 2 : 1 : 1, or 1 : 1 : 1), pH (changing the reactant NaOH (0.10 mmol, 0.15 mmol or 0.20 mmol)), solvent (H2O or H2O/EtOH) and temperature (90, 110 or 130 °C). The measured and simulated PXRDs confirm the purity of 1–3 (Fig. S5–S7 in the ESI†). The optical diffuse reflectance spectra of crystalline solids 1–3 were measured at room temperature. The absorption data were calculated from the reflectance using the Kubelka–Munk function.9f,10c The energy band gaps (Eg) obtained by extrapolation of the linear portion of the absorption edges were estimated to be 2.29 eV (1), 2.27 eV (2) and 2.39 eV (3), indicating their semiconducting nature (Fig. 4).

Catalytic activity for the degradation of methyl orange (MO), methylene blue (MB) and rhodamine B (RhB)

To study the photocatalytic activity of 1–3, we selected the organic dyes methyl orange (MO), methylene blue (MB) and rhodamine B (RhB) as the model dye contaminants to evaluate the photocatalytic effectiveness in the purification of wastewater in the presence of H2O2. It was found that the degradation rates of MO are 94.0%, 61.2%, 53.8% and 7.8% at 150 min in the presence of 1–3 or the blank experiment (Fig. 5a, 6, S6a and S7a in the ESI†). The degradation rates of MB are 96.4%, 86.3%, 73.8% and 25.2% at 150 min in the presence of 1–3 or the blank experiment (Fig. 5b, 7, S6b and S7b in the ESI†). The degradation rates of RhB are 92.6%, 91.7%, 87.7% and 5.2% at 150 min in the presence of 1–3 or the blank experiment (Fig. 5c, 8, S6c and S7c in the ESI†). The catalytic efficiency for the degradation of MO and MB is 1 > 2 > 3. 1–3 are good photocatalytic catalysts for the degradation of MO and MB, and excellent photocatalysts for the degradation of RhB.

After the photocatalytic degradation of the MO, MB and RhB solutions, the photocatalysts (1–3) can be separated by simple centrifugation as they are undissolved in water. After the photocatalysis, the PXRD patterns of 1–3 are in good agreement with that of the original compounds, implying that 1–3 maintain their structural integrity after the photocatalysis reaction, which confirmed that their stability toward the photocatalysis is good. The photocatalysts (1–3) can be recovered from the catalytic system and reused for five catalytic cycles (Fig. S9–S11 in the ESI†). The photocatalytic experiment shows that 1–3 are universal and highly efficient photocatalysts for the degradation of the organic dyes MO, MB and RhB (Fig. S8 in the ESI†).

1–3 show very low photocatalytic activity for the photocatalytic degradation of the organic dyes MO, MB and RhB under the same experiment conditions except the absence of H2O2 (the detail is shown in the ESI†).

H2O2 is the precursor of hydroxyl radicals, which are effective and highly active oxidizing species. During the photocatalytic process of coordination polymers 1–3, UV-vis light may induce the coordination polymers to produce oxygen and/or nitrogen–metal charge transfer by promoting an electron from the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) to the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO). The HOMO strongly needs one electron to return to its stable state. Thus, one electron was captured from H2O2 or water molecules, which was oxygenated to generate ˙OH radicals. Then the ˙OH active species could decompose the organic dyes (MO, MB or RhB) effectively to complete the photocatalytic process.9–11,25

To study the photocatalytic activity of photocatalysts, researchers usually select one dye such as methyl orange (MO), methylene blue (MB) or rhodamine B (RhB) as the model dye. Only a few researchers select two or three model dyes. The photocatalysts usually show high catalytic efficiency toward one model dye. Universal and highly efficient photocatalysts are rare.9–11,25 The syntheses of the universal and highly efficient photocatalysts 1–3 are very difficult and very unusual.

Thermogravimetric analysis

To characterize the coordination polymers more fully in terms of thermal stability, the thermal behaviours of 1–3 were examined by TGA. The experiments were performed on samples consisting of numerous single crystals with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 (Fig. S12 in the ESI†). In the TG curve of 1, the lattice and coordination water molecules were completely lost at 150 °C (calcd.: 15.87%, found: 16.02%). The anhydrous matter was thermally stable upon heating to 275 °C. Then the decomposition happened and did not end until 800 °C. The main residue of 1 should be CuO (calcd.: 26.28%, found: 26.16%). The lattice EtOH and water molecules of 2 were completely lost at 160 °C (calcd.: 10.82%, found: 10.69%. The network of 2 was thermally stable upon heating to 260 °C. Then the weight loss continuously occurred. The residue at 800 °C of 2 was CuO (calcd.: 26.85%, found: 27.01%). The lattice and coordination water molecules of 3 were completely lost at 150 °C (calcd.: 11.32%, found: 11.54%. The anhydrous matter of 3 was thermally stable upon heating to 278 °C. Then the weight loss continuously occurred. The residue at 800 °C of 3 should be CuO (calcd.: 24.99%, found: 25.18%).

Conclusions

In summary, three coordination polymers based on the tetranuclear copper(ii) cluster [Cu4(μ3-OH)2] were synthesized by a hydrothermal method. 1 consists of an unprecedented (3,4)-connected 2D network based on the tetranuclear copper(ii) clusters [Cu4(μ3-OH)2] and Cu1 cores, and showed an unprecedented 2D → 3D polythreaded motif. 2 shows an unusual (3,10)-connected 3D network based on the tetranuclear copper(ii) cluster [Cu4(μ3-OH)2]. 3 exhibits a 2D (4,4) network based on tetranuclear copper(ii) clusters [Cu4(μ3-OH)2]. The diverse structures demonstrate that the multicarboxylate ligand and solvent can adjust the coordination polymers with the N-coligand. 1–3 are universal and highly efficient photocatalysts for the degradation of organic dyes such as methyl orange (MO), methylene blue (MB) and rhodamine B (RhB) under UV irradiation, are very stable and easily separated from the reaction system for reuse. This work provides not only new information regarding polynuclear metal(ii) chemistry but also new photocatalytic catalysts.
