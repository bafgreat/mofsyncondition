The effect of reaction conditions on the nature of cadmium 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylate metal–organic frameworks

Author links open overlay panel
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ica.2010.11.025Get rights and content

Abstract

The product from the reaction between Cd(NO3)2·4H2O and 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylic acid (H3btc) in DMF at 95 °C depends on the reaction time, with [Cd(Hbtc)(H2O)2] 1 and [Cd(Hbtc)(DMF)2] 2 isolated after heating for 10 min, the latter after standing the solution for 1–2 weeks at room temperature. [Cd3(btc)2(H2O)9]·4H2O 3 was isolated after heating for 1 h, whereas [Cd12(btc)8(DMF)14(OH2)2]·1.5DMF 4 was isolated after heating for 2 days. Compounds 1 and 3 have been previously reported, whereas 2 and 4 are both new. Compound 2 adopts a two-dimensional sheet structure, with the coordinated DMF ligands projecting from both sides of the sheets, whereas 4 has a complex three-dimensional structure related to the fsc net. When the reaction was repeated in the presence of pyrazine (pyz), the product [Cd(Hbtc)(pyz)(DMF)]·DMF 5 was isolated as a minor compound. Compound 5 has a two-dimensional structure, with Cd-Hbtc zig-zag chains linked into sheets through the pyrazine ligands.

Graphical abstract

The product from the reaction between Cd(II) and 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylic acid in DMF at 95 °C depends on the reaction time, with new two- and three-dimensional metal–organic frameworks characterised, whereas carrying out the reaction in the presence of pyrazine leads to a two-dimensional structure in which the pyrazine molecules do not pillar between sheets.

1. Introduction

Coordination polymers, often referred to as metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), are currently attracting a high level of attention in inorganic and materials chemistry [1], [2], [3], [4]. These compounds have extended structures and typically contain either metal centres or polynuclear metal aggregates interconnected by bridging ligands into one-, two- or three-dimensional networks. The interest in these materials arises in part from their structural variety, and the ability to control the structure through use of secondary building units (SBUs) [5] and linkers in which the number and orientation of the donor groups is pre-determined. The open framework structure of many MOFs gives them the potential for porosity. Although elegant structural chemistry has been undertaken on coordination polymers for many years [6], [7], the recent upsurge of interest in these materials has resulted largely because some are sufficiently robust for the structure to be maintained on loss of included solvent molecules from the pores. The resultant ‘activated’ materials have been investigated for a range of applications including hydrogen storage [8], [9], catalysis [10], [11] and drug delivery [12], [13].

Zinc MOFs, in which polycarboxylate ligands link di-, tri- and tetranuclear SBUs, are amongst the most well-studied of this class of material [14], [15], [16]. MOFs containing the heavier congener cadmium have received less attention, with the majority of reported work focussing on systems containing polypyridine co-ligands [17], [18]. We have recently reported the syntheses, structures and properties of cadmium MOFs containing 1,4- and 1,3-benzenedicarboxylate linkers [19]. In the current paper, we extend these studies to the ligand 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylate (btc).

Previous work on the reaction between Cd(II) and H3btc has yielded a number of MOF structures, with the majority of these formed under hydrothermal conditions. Thus, the network compounds Na3[Cd3(btc)3(H2O)2]·6H2O [20], [Cd3(btc)2(H2O)2]·2H2O [21], Na[Cd(btc)(H2O)2] [22], [Cd3(btc)2(H2O)9]·4H2O [23] and [Cd3(btc)2]·8H2O [23] were formed from the reaction of a Cd(II) salt with H3btc in water between 150 °C and 200 °C, in some cases in the presence of an added base. Two MOFs were formed on milder heating in mixed solvents – [Cd(Hbtc)(H2O)2] [24], [25], [26] and [Cd4(btc)3(H2O)2(DMF)2]·1.5H2O [27] – whereas another – [Cd3(btc)2(H2O)9]·2H2O [28] – was crystallised at room temperature using a silica gel method.

We have shown that heating Cd(NO3)2·4H2O and H2bdc (bdc = 1,4-benzenedicarboxylate) in DMF below the boiling point of the solvent can afford access to new MOF materials, and that the structures of these can often be controlled by careful variation of the reaction conditions [19], [29]. Systematic variation of the reaction conditions can allow a more complete exploration of a particular metal–ligand system, in an attempt to ensure that as many as possible of the attainable MOFs are isolated and, if possible, structurally characterised. In this way, those with the most interesting structures can be studied further. We sought to continue this approach by investigating the reaction of Cd(NO3)2·4H2O with H3btc in DMF. Despite some structures from this system having been described previously, we show that systematic variation of the reaction time leads to two new phases at 95 °C. In addition, we show that attempts to generate three-dimensional MOFs by pillaring between two-dimensional sheets can lead to unexpected results.

2. Experimental

2.1. Materials and methods

Chemicals were purchased from Fisher Scientific and Aldrich Chemical Co. and used without further purification. Microanalysis (C, H and N) were carried out by Mr. Alan Carver (University of Bath Microanalytical Service). Thermogravimetric analyses were recorded on a Perkin–Elmer TGA 7 analyser. Powder diffraction data were recorded on a Bruker D8 diffractometer fitted with Goebel mirrors and a 0.2 mm beam slit, and in each case, unless noted below, the experimental pattern showed a good match with that simulated from the single crystal analysis.

2.2. Synthesis of [Cd(Hbtc)(H2O)2] 1 and [Cd(Hbtc)(DMF)2] 2

Cd(NO3)2·4H2O (0.500 g, 1.62 mmol) and H3btc (0.340 g, 1.62 mmol) were dissolved in DMF (60 cm3) and the solution heated for 10 min at 95 °C then allowed to cool to room temperature over 1 h. During the reaction a colourless solid formed, which was identified by X-ray powder diffraction as 1. Crystals of 2 deposited on top of the powder after 1–2 weeks as a minor product. Combined yield 0.22 g (38% based on 1 as product).

2.3. Synthesis of [Cd3(btc)2(H2O)9]·4H2O 3

Cd(NO3)2·4H2O (0.500 g, 1.62 mmol) and H3btc (0.340 g, 1.62 mmol) were dissolved in DMF (60 cm3) and the solution heated at 95 °C for 1 h. A crystalline powder of 3 was present at the end of the reaction. This was separated by filtration, washed with fresh DMF and allowed to air dry. Yield 0.208 g (39%). The compound was identified as 3 by comparison of its powder X-ray diffraction pattern with that simulated from the crystal structure [23].

2.4. Synthesis of [Cd12(btc)8(DMF)14(OH2)2]·1.5DMF 4

Cd(NO3)2·4H2O (0.500 g, 1.62 mmol) and H3btc (0.340 g, 1.62 mmol) were dissolved in DMF (30 cm3) and the solution heated at 95 °C for 2 days. Crystals of 4 were present at the end of the reaction and these were separated by filtration, washed with fresh DMF and allowed to air dry. Yield 0.231 g (41%). Anal. Calc. for C118.5H136.5Cd12N15.5O65.5: C, 34.1; H, 3.30; N, 5.20. Found: C, 34.1; H, 3.28; N, 5.38%. νmax/cm−1: 1655s, 1606s, 1552s, 1430s, 1358s, 1251s.

2.5. Synthesis of [Cd(Hbtc)(pyz)(DMF)]·DMF 5

Cd(NO3)2·4H2O (0.500 g, 1.62 mmol), H3btc (0.340 g, 1.62 mmol) and pyrazine (0.130 g, 1.62 mmol) were dissolved in DMF (30 cm3) and the solution heated for 2 days at 95 °C. Crystals of 5 formed together with a powder on cooling the sample slowly to room temperature. The major product was identified by powder X-ray diffraction as 4. Combined yield 0.42 g.

2.6. X-ray crystallography

X-ray data for compounds 2 and 5 were collected on a Nonius Kappa CCD diffractometer using Mo Kα radiation, whereas X-ray data for compound 4 were collected at Daresbury Station 16.2 SMX using synchrotron radiation. Unless noted below, all non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically in the final least squares run, and hydrogen atoms were included at calculated positions. The structures were solved using shelxs-97 and refined using full-matrix least squares in shelxl-97 [30].

The absolute structure parameter refinement for 2 (0.51(5)) indicates racemic twinning of the crystal. This, coupled with pseudosymmetry in the asymmetric unit, suggested that perhaps the incorrect space group had been assigned. Analysis with platon adsymm indicated that space group Pbcm might be a feasible alternative, however with 1160 systematic absence violations and poor convergence, this was not viable.

The large asymmetric unit of 4, coupled with difficulties resolving the solvent and disorder led to slow convergence of the structure. Partial occupancy atoms arising from disorder were treated isotropically throughout. The DMF molecule coordinated to Cd(1) is disordered over two sites. One half of this – based on partial occupancy oxygen atom O(11) – was resolved but the remainder – based on O(11C) – could not be. It is clear that there is residual electron density in this area (difference Fourier map maximum located in this region), but no plausible model that could be refined was obtained. All bond distances in the refined DMF fractions were restrained to ideal values, and the partial occupancy atoms therein were restrained to being co-planar. The water hydrogen atoms on O(11A) and O(23) could not be located and consequently were omitted from the refinement. The DMF molecules containing O(19) and O(110) were, with the exception of the oxygen atoms, located evenly over two sites. Four C–N distances in the two fragments of DMF containing O(19) were restrained to ideal values, as were all bond distances in the refined fractions of DMF based on O(110). The partial occupancy atoms in each of these fragments were also restrained to being co-planar. The atomic displacement parameters in the DMF molecule based on O(111) were refined subject to similarity restraints. In the partial occupancy lattice solvent based on O(1S1), the O–C and C–N distances were restrained to ideal values, and the partial occupancy atoms therein were restrained to being co-planar.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. The reaction between Cd(NO3)2·4H2O and H3btc

The product isolated from the reaction between Cd(NO3)2·4H2O and H3btc in DMF at 95 °C depends on how long the solution is maintained at this temperature. When the solution was heated for 10 min, the reaction produced a powder as the major product, upon which a small quantity of crystals were deposited after 1–2 weeks. The powder was identified on the basis of X-ray powder diffraction as [Cd(Hbtc)(H2O)2] 1, which has been previously reported [24], [25], [26]. In contrast, the crystals were identified by a single crystal X-ray analysis as the hitherto unreported compound [Cd(Hbtc)(DMF)2] 2. When the reaction mixture was heated for 1 h, a colourless crystalline material was formed. X-ray powder diffraction studies showed no evidence for the formation of 1 or 2, and confirmed the identity of this product as [Cd3(btc)2(H2O)9]·4H2O 3, which has previously been reported, and shown to have a bilayer structure [23]. When the reaction mixture was heated for 2 days, the composition of the solid was different again, and a single crystal X-ray analysis confirmed the product to be [Cd12(btc)8(DMF)14(OH2)2]·1.5DMF 4. Crystals of 4 decompose rapidly due to loss of solvent, but X-ray powder diffraction confirms that this compound is the only crystalline product after 2 days.

3.2. The structure of [Cd(Hbtc)(DMF)2] 2

The asymmetric unit of compound 2 consists of a cadmium centre, a Hbtc ligand and two coordinated DMF molecules. The cadmium centre is 7-coordinate, with a distorted pentagonal bipyramidal coordination geometry. The pentagonal plane comprises O(1) and O(2) from a bidentate (κ2) carboxylate group of the Hbtc ligand, O(3) and O(4) of a bidentate carboxylate group of a symmetry-related anion and O(6) of the carboxylic acid functionality of another symmetry-related anion. The DMF ligands occupy the axial positions. A short hydrogen bond reinforces the monodentate coordination mode of the carboxylic acid group [O(5)⋯O(2) 2.538 Å, H(5)⋯O(2) 1.70 Å, O(5)–H(5)⋯O(2) 175°].

Both the cadmium centre and the Hbtc ligand act as 3-connecting linkers, so overall 2 adopts the (6, 3) two-dimensional sheet structure shown in Fig. 1a. The DMF ligands from neighbouring layers interdigitate (Fig. 1b) reducing the void space between sheets and limiting the distance between parallel layers to 7.1 Å. The structures of [Cd(Hbtc)(H2O)2] 1 and [Cd(Hbtc)(DMF)2] 2 are related, despite the difference in the axial ligands. Both form similar two-dimensional sheet structures, though the smaller size of the water ligands ensures that interlayer separation is considerably less in 1, only 3.3 Å [24], [25], [26].

3.3. The structure of [Cd12(btc)8(DMF)14(OH2)2]·1.5DMF 4

The asymmetric unit of 4 contains 12 cadmium centres, 8 btc ligands, 14 DMF ligands, 2 aqua ligands and 1.5 included DMF solvent molecules. Compound 4 has a complex three-dimensional structure as shown in Fig. 2a. It contains four crystallographically distinct SBUs. Those containing Cd(1)–Cd(2) and Cd(3)–Cd(4) are ‘paddle wheels’, whereas those containing Cd(5)–Cd(8) and Cd(9)–Cd(12) are infinite tubular SBUs. The two pairs of SBUs are pseudosymmetrically related.

One of the infinite tubular SBUs is shown in Fig. 3, with views both along and perpendicular to the crystallographic a-axis. Each cadmium centre is coordinated to six oxygen atoms, with a bidentate carboxylate, three monodentate carboxylates and a DMF molecule comprising the coordination sphere. The cadmium centres within the SBU are linked by pairs of bridging carboxylate groups, one bridging through a single oxygen atom (chelating/bridging bidentate) and the other through two (bridging bidentate). The cadmium-carboxylate chains are further reinforced by the bridging btc ligands, such that when one carboxylate group is coordinated to two cadmium centres, a second is coordinated to two further cadmium centres along the chain with one cadmium centre in-between these two pairs. The coordination in the two independent tubular SBUs is similar, though they differ in the orientation of the btc ligands. Only two of the three available carboxylate groups per ligand are involved in formation of these infinite SBUs.

The tubular SBUs are not linked directly to each other in the gross structure. Instead, the remaining carboxylate group on each btc ligand is coordinated to a Cd2(O2CR)4(DMF)3(OH2) paddle wheel SBU in which each cadmium centre is coordinated to six oxygen atoms, four from carboxylate groups and two from the terminal DMF or water ligands.

By regarding each Cd4(btc)4 section of the infinite SBU as an octahedral node, which is linked to four Cd2 paddle wheels and two further Cd4(btc)4 units, the structure can be described as a fsc net (Fig. 2b) [31], [32].

3.4. The cadmium-btc system

The structural analyses have revealed that the dimensionality of the isolated product increases with increasing reaction time. Thus, heating for 10 min yields the two-dimensional networks 1 and 2, heating for 1 h gives the bilayer network 3 and heating for 2 days gives the three-dimensional network 4. Although compounds 1–4 crystallise from DMF, the reactions observed do not involve solid-state transformations, as the precipitates of 1 and 3 are digested as the reaction proceeds. Compounds 1–3 are networks in which single cadmium centres act as nodes, whereas 4 contains more complex SBUs. Given this, it is likely that formation of 4 cannot occur until the concentration of the polynuclear building blocks in solution reaches a sufficiently high concentration, which is consistent with the longer reaction time in this case.

It is notable that none of the compounds 2–4 have direct zinc analogues, though the crystal structure of [Zn(Hbtc)(H2O)2], isostructural to 1, has been reported [33], [34], [35]. Zinc and cadmium have some MOF architectures in common, such as the sheet structures based on linear M3(O2CR)6 SBUs, [16], [19], [29] but in the majority of cases the two metals form MOFs of different topologies with the same dicarboxylate linker. One reason for this is the prevalence in zinc chemistry of the Zn4O(O2CR)6 and Zn2(O2CR)4 SBUs, which are observed as discrete compounds in addition to being present in MOFs. The tetranuclear analogue is unknown in cadmium chemistry, having been observed in neither a discrete compound nor a MOF, whereas the Cd2(O2CR)4 dimer has only been observed in a few discrete compounds [36], [37]. While a number of factors may combine to lead to these differences in chemistry, the greater size of the cadmium(II) centre is likely to be most significant, as this leads to higher coordination numbers. This, in turn, favours other SBUs.

3.5. The reaction between Cd(NO3)2·4H2O and H3btc in the presence of potential pillaring ligands and the structure of [Cd(Hbtc)(pyz)(DMF)]·DMF 5

The observation of flat Cd(Hbtc) sheets in the crystal structures of 1 and 2 led to the consideration of replacing the terminal axial ligands (H2O and DMF) with linear bridging ligands such as 4,4′-bipyridyl or 1,4-diazacyclo[2.2.2]octane (dabco). This type of ligand has the potential to act as pillars between the sheets and thus interconnect the two-dimensional layers of 1 or 2 into a three-dimensional network [38], [39], [40].

In order to investigate this possibility, the reaction between Cd(NO3)2·4H2O and H3btc was undertaken in the presence of a range of potential bridging ligands. In the majority of cases, no crystalline products were obtained, but with pyrazine, the reaction at 95 °C gave 4 as the major product after 2 days, but crystals of a second compound 5 were obtained as a minor product. An X-ray single crystal analysis confirmed the identity of 5 as [Cd(Hbtc)(pyz)(DMF)]·DMF.

The asymmetric unit of 5 consists of a cadmium centre, a Hbtc ligand, a pyz ligand, and two DMF molecules, one of which is coordinated. The cadmium centre is 7-coordinate, being coordinated to the Hbtc ligand through O(1) and O(2) in a bidentate coordination mode, a second symmetry-related Hbtc ligand which is bidentate coordinated through O(5) and O(6), a pyrazine nitrogen atom N(1), a second pyrazine nitrogen atom N(2) from a symmetry-related ligand, and a DMF molecule, coordinated through O(7).

The Hbtc ligands link the cadmium centres into zig-zag chains, shown in Fig. 4a. These chains are cross-linked into corrugated (4, 4) sheets via the pyrazine ligands, as shown in Fig. 4b. Coordination of the DMF molecule instead of the carboxylic acid group prevents these sheets from being interconnected into a three-dimensional network, so the Hbtc ligand acts as a two-connected linker, rather than the three-connected linker observed in 1 and 2. The uncoordinated carboxylic acid group is involved in a short hydrogen bond with O(8) of the included DMF molecule acting as acceptor [O(3)⋯O(8) 2.559, H(3)⋯O(8) 1.73 Å, O(3)–H(3)⋯O(8) 169°]. The layer structure of 5 contrasts with the three-dimensional networks present in compounds such as [Zn2(bdc)2(dabco)] [40] in which the N,N′-ditopic ligand adopts the anticipated pillaring role between sheets. The structure of 5 clearly indicates that pillaring is not always a successfully strategy to increase the dimensionality of network structures.

3.6. Thermal properties

Information about the thermal stability of 4 and 5 was obtained through thermogravimetric analysis (TGA). Compound 4 loses 24.5% mass between 20 °C and 100 °C, then slowly loses mass (33% total) between 100 °C and 400 °C. There is no plateau in the TGA trace, which suggests a desolvated network cannot be isolated.

Compound 5 loses 26% of its mass between 80 °C and 330 °C, which corresponds to the loss of both the coordinated and included DMF molecules (calc. 26.7%). Although this mass loss occurs in three steps, it does not appear that the included DMF can be removed without disruption of the coordination network.

4. Conclusions

The reaction time has been shown to be a major factor in the product isolated from the reaction between Cd(NO3)2·4H2O and H3btc in DMF at 95 °C. Four products have been isolated from this reaction. Two of these, [Cd(Hbtc)(H2O)2] 1 and [Cd3(btc)2(H2O)9]·4H2O 3, have been previously reported, though formed under different conditions, whereas [Cd(Hbtc)(DMF)2] 2 and [Cd12(btc)8(DMF)14(OH2)2]·1.5DMF 4 are new, and have been shown crystallographically to have two-dimensional and three-dimensional structures, respectively.

Notably, in this system at least, the dimensionality of the structure increases with increasing reaction time. Thus, heating for 10 min yields the two-dimensional networks 1 and 2, heating for 1 h gives the bilayer network 3 and heating for 2 days gives the three-dimensional network 4.

Attempts to create three-dimensional networks by replacing the coordinated DMF with N,N′-ditopic pillaring ligands were unsuccessful, though reaction with pyrazine gave a two-dimensional network structure [Cd(Hbtc)(pyz)(DMF)]·DMF 5, in which the Hbtc ligands are ditopic, and contain an uncoordinated carboxylic acid group.

  • Metal-organic frameworks for food applications: A review

    2021, Food Chemistry

    Metal-organic frameworks (MOFs) are high surface-to-volume ratio crystalline hybrid porous coordination materials composed of metal ions as nodes and organic linkers. The goal of this paper was to provide an updated and comprehensive state-of-the-art review of MOFs for different food applications such as active food contact materials, antimicrobial nanocarriers, controlled release nanosystems for active compounds, nanofillers for food packaging materials, food nanoreactors, food substance nanosensors, stabilizers and immobilizers for active compounds and enzymes, and extractors of food contaminants. Extraction and sensing of several food contaminants have been the main food applications of MOFs. The other applications listed above require further investigation, as they are at an early stage. However, interesting results are being reported for these other fields. Finally, an important limitation of MOFs has been the use of non-renewable feedstocks for their synthesis, but this has recently been solved through the manufacture and use of γ-cyclodextrin-based MOFs.

  • Dependence of solvents, pH, molar ratio and temperature in tuning metal organic framework architecture

    2019, Arabian Journal of Chemistry

    One of the strategic goals in crystal engineering is to synthesize the crystals with predictable structures and valuable properties. Metal Organic Frameworks (MOFs), a new class of crystalline porous materials, is a recent upsurge of interest in materials research. The challenges in the area encompass the deliberate control of structure, and therefore the properties and function, as many synthetic factors play a subtle role in the crystallization of MOFs. Among many influencing factors, nature of solvents, pH, molar ratio of reactants and temperature are four crucial parameters that determine the overall architectures of MOFs. This review presents the effect of these major parameters on the synthesis of several MOFs to clearly understand their influence on the nature of binding and formation of different MOF structures.
