Introduction

Entangled systems have received much attention because of their properties, indisputable aesthetic as well as intricate structures and topologies.1 Many topologically entangled structures have been discussed in the comprehensive reviews by Batten and Robson as well as Ciani and coworkers.2 Their significant potential applications as molecular materials for drug delivery vehicles, molecular machines and sensor devices have been studied owing to their special entangled modes.3,4 Thus, it is necessary to fabricate more structural paradigms to further prove the nature of such entangled systems.5 Nevertheless, the ingenious design and synthesis of original entangled structures still remains a tremendously difficult and long-term challenge.6 Recently, there has been a growing trend in using flexible ligands to construct abundant and intriguing network architectures.7 Long flexible ligands have emerged with the ability to produce unique entangled structures thanks to their varied conformations and geometries.8 To obtain splendid entangled structures, a bis(methylbenzimidazole) ligand with a –(CH2)6– spacer are a good choice of flexible ligands. The characteristics of the flexible bis(methylbenzimidazole) ligand are as follows: (a) the longer alkyl spacers –(CH2)6– allows the ligands to bend and rotate when they coordinate to metal centers, which often causes structural diversities9–11 and may afford the formation of interpenetrating networks. (b) The 2-position methyl substituent effectively boosts the donated electron ability of hbmb, which is conducive to enrich the coordination ability of hbmb with metal ions. Besides, steric hindrance could be identified as a tool for maneuvering different interpenetrating dimensions.12–14 In addition, polycarboxylate ligands, as a crucial family of multidentate O-donor ligands,15 are of particular interest and have been widely used to fabricate various coordination complexes owing to their fascinating characteristics, including a strong coordination ability, diverse coordination modes and different organic skeletons of carboxylate linkers.16,17 In particular, flexible carboxylate ligands, in view of their flexibility and conformational freedom, may provide more possibilities for the production of diverse frameworks.18 Thus, the effect of flexible carboxylates as auxiliary ligands cannot be neglected in the construction of different kinds of entangled structures.

Taking the aforementioned idea into consideration, the reactions of zinc(ii) and cadmium(ii) salts, long-chain 1,1′-(1,6-hexane)bis-(2-methylbenzimidazole)) (hbmb) (Scheme S1 in the ESI†) together with a series of flexible dicarboxylate coligands (Scheme S2 in the ESI†) were carried out under different hydrothermal conditions. Eight new coordination complexes, namely, [M(hbmb)0.5(o-phda)]n [M = Zn (1), Cd (2)], {[M(hbmb)(p-phda)]·H2O}n [M = Zn (3), Cd (4)], [M(hbmb)0.5(oba)]n [M = Zn (5), Cd (6)], {[M(hbmb)(sdba)]m}n [M = Zn, m = 2 (7); m = 1, Cd (8)] (H2phda = phenylenediacetic acid, H2oba = 4,4′-oxybis(benzoic acid), H2sdba = 4,4′-sulfonyldibenzoic acid), respectively, were successfully obtained. Four pairs of complexes represent different entangled structures. The chemical stability, thermal stability and photoluminescence of complexes 1–8 were also reported.

Experimental section

Materials and physical measurements

All reagents and solvents were commercially available except for hbmb, which was synthesized according to the literature.19 FT-IR spectra were recorded on a Bruker-ALPHA spectrophotometer with KBr pellets in the 400–4000 cm−1 region. Elemental analyses (C, H, and N) were carried out on a FLASH EA 1112 elemental analyzer. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns were recorded using Cu–Kα1 radiation on a PANalytical X'Pert PRO diffractometer. Thermal analyses were performed on a Netzsch STA 449C thermal analyzer from room temperature at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 in air. The luminescence spectra for the powdered solid samples were measured at room temperature on a Hitachi 850 fluorescence spectrophotometer. The excitation slit and emission slit were both 2.0 nm.

Synthesis

Synthesis of [Zn(hbmb)0.5(o-phda)]n (1)

A mixture of Zn(Ac)2·2H2O (0.2 mmol), hbmb (0.1 mmol), o-H2phda (0.2 mmol) and NaOH (0.2 mmol) in H2O (10 mL) was kept in a 25 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel at 130 °C for three days. After the mixture was cooled to room temperature at a rate of 5 °C h−1, colorless crystals suitable for X-ray diffraction were obtained with a yield of 68% (based on Zn). Anal. calcd for C21H21N2O4Zn (%): C, 58.55; H, 4.91; N, 6.50. Found: C, 58.45; H, 5.21; N, 6.53. IR (KBr, cm−1): 3441(w), 3131(s), 1613(w), 1558(m), 1398(s), 1282(w), 1163(w), 1137(w), 932(w), 766(w).

Crystal data collection and refinement

The data of 1–8 were collected on a Rigaku Saturn 724 CCD diffractomer (Mo–Kα, λ = 0.71073 Å) at a temperature of 20 ± 1 °C. Absorption corrections were applied using a multi-scan program. The data were corrected for Lorentz and polarization effects. The structures were solved by direct methods and refined with a full-matrix least-squares technique based on F2 with the SHELXL-97 crystallographic software package.20 The hydrogen atoms were placed at calculated positions and refined as riding atoms with isotropic displacement parameters. The crystallographic data and structure processing parameters for 1–8 and parts of the complexes under specific conditions are summarized in Tables 1 and S1 (ESI†), severally. Selected bond lengths and bond angles for 1–8 and parts of the complexes under specific conditions are listed in Tables S3 and S4 (ESI†), respectively. The crystallographic data for 1–8 have been deposited at the Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre with CCDC reference numbers 917704–917711. For 1-DMF, 3-MeCN, 4-MeCN, 5-MeOH and 6-DMF: CCDC reference numbers 946074–946078.

Results and discussion

Crystal structures of [Zn(hbmb)0.5(o-phda)]n (1) and [Cd(hbmb)0.5(o-phda)]n (2)

Similar cell parameters with the same space group P21/c and the results of the crystallographic analyses confirm that 1 and 2 are isostructural. Thus, only the structure of 1 is described in detail as a typical example. As depicted in Fig. 1a, the Zn(ii) ion is five coordinate with four oxygen atoms (O1, O2C, O3D, O4B) from four different o-phda2− anions and one nitrogen atom (N1) from one hbmb, taking an ideal square-pyramidal geometry (τ ≈ 0.00).21

The Zn–O distances are in the range of 2.049(3)–2.077(3) Å, while the Zn–N bond length is 2.021(4) Å, which are in the normal range.22 The hbmb ligand adopts an asymmetric trans-conformation with two different Ndonor⋯N–Csp3⋯Csp3 torsion angles of 95.621° and 95.620°. The o-phda2−ligand also adopts an asymmetric trans-conformation and the angles of two –CH2– are 108.829° and 116.367°.

In 1, the “V” shape ligando-phda2− adopts (κ1-κ1)-(κ1-κ1)-μ4 coordination information. Each pair of Zn(ii) ions is bridged by four such o-phda2− anions to generate well-known paddle-wheel binuclear clusters with a Zn⋯Zn separation of 3.002 Å. The binuclear clusters [Zn2(CO2)4], serving as secondary building units (SBUs), are further extended by o-phda2− anions into a 2D net with alternately arranged left- and right-handed helical chains (Fig. 1b). Both the helical pitches are 10.887(8) Å, corresponding to the length of the b-axis. The Zn(ii)/o-phda2− nets are pillared by a hbmb ligand in a trans-conformation via Zn–N connections to generate a pillar-layered three-dimensional (3D) framework (Fig. 1c). Furthermore, topological analysis was carried out to get an insight of the structure of 1. If the [Zn2(CO2)4] dimeric unit is taken as a 6-connected node and the ligands are considered as linkers, the 3D framework of 1 can be described as a 6-connected topology with a point symbol of 44·610·8, showing a new net topology. Markedly, the 3D architecture again reveals a self-penetrating pattern.23e As shown in Fig. 1d, each six-membered shortest circuit is catenated by two rods of the same network.23 Self-penetration is an uncommon form of topological entanglement.23d Thus, 1 can be regarded as another example of self-penetrated coordination complexes.23d,24 Remarkably, no solvent accessible volume is found in the structure because of the interpenetration.

Crystal structures of {[Zn(hbmb)(p-phda)]·H2O}n (3) and {[Cd(hbmb)(p-phda)]·H2O}n (4)

X-ray single crystal diffraction reveals that complexes 3 and 4 are essentially isostructural. Herein, only the structure of 3 is given in the ensuing discussion, whereas the coordination environment of the Cd(ii) ions in 4 is presented in Fig. S1 (in the ESI†). 3 crystallizes in the monoclinic system, space group P2/c. Each asymmetric unit of 3 consists of one crystallographically independent Zn(ii) ion, one hbmb ligand, one p-phda2−ligand and one guest water molecule. The Zn(ii) ion adopts a distorted tetrahedral coordination environment, ligated by two nitrogen atoms from two separate hbmb ligands as well as two oxygen atoms from two p-phda2− anions (Fig. 2a). The Zn–O bond length is 1.9537(19) Å and the Zn–N bond length is 2.046(2) Å. They are reasonable when compared with those in reported works. The distortion of the tetrahedron can be indicated by the calculated value of the τ4 parameter introduced by Houser (τ4 ≈ 0.89 in 3).25 The hbmb ligand adopts a symmetric trans-conformation with a Ndonor⋯N–Csp3⋯Csp3 torsion angle of 76.516°. The p-phda2− anions also adopt a symmetric trans-conformation and the angle of –CH2– is 115.146°.

In 3, each p-phda2− anion bridges two Zn(ii) ions in a bis-monodentate coordination mode to give rise to a 1D infinite chain (Zn(ii)/p-phda2− chain) along the c-axis with a Zn⋯Zn separation of 12.9513 Å (Fig. 2b). The 1D zigzag chain (Zn(ii)/hbmb chain) along the a-axis is also formed by the trans-conformational hbmb ligands and Zn(ii) ions with a Zn⋯Zn distance of 14.3863 Å (Fig. 2c). The combination of the 1D chains of Zn(ii)/p-phda2− and Zn(ii)/hbmb generates the 3D structure of 3. Notably, tetrahedral nodes of Zn(ii) are connected by six hbmb ligands and six p-phda2− anions into an adamantanoid cage containing four cyclohexane-like windows, each in a chair conformation (Fig. 2d). The elongated [Zn10(hbmb)6(p-phda2−)6] subunit has a top-to-bottom length of 41.112(8) Å, equivalent to four times the crystallographic b-axis length. This large superadamantane gives the single diamond network a huge chamber, which is filled via mutual interpenetration of three independent equivalent networks, leading to the formation of a 4-fold interpenetrating 3D architecture with a 66-dia topology (Fig. 2e and 2f).25b,26

Crystal structures of [Zn(hbmb)0.5(oba)]n (5) and [Cd(hbmb)0.5(oba)]n (6)

Single crystal X-ray analysis reveals that 5 and 6 are isostructural. For the convenience of depiction, the structure of 5 is described in detail here as a representative example. As illustrated in Fig. 3a, the crystallographically independent Zn(ii) ion exhibits a five-coordinate environment with one nitrogen atom (N1) deriving from one hbmb ligand and four oxygen atoms (O1, O2, O3, O4) originating from four different oba2− anions. The structural index parameter (τ)27 is close to 0.00, indicating that the geometry around Zn(ii) can be described as an ideal square-pyramidal geometry. The lengths of the Zn–O bonds are in the range of 2.021(3)–2.083(3) Å and the Zn–N bond length is 2.029(3) Å.

The flexible hbmb ligand adopts a symmetrical trans-conformation with an Ndonor⋯N–Csp3⋯Csp3 torsion angle of 84.574°. The ligand oba2− exhibits a (κ1-κ1)-(κ1-κ1)-μ4 coordination mode and the dihedral angle between two benzene rings is 61.657°. Each pair of Zn(ii) ions is bridged by four such oba2− anions to form well-known paddle-wheel binuclear clusters with a Zn⋯Zn separation of 3.072 Å, which is a little longer than that in 1. The binuclear clusters are further extended by oba2− anions into a 2D net with alternately arranged left- and right-handed helical chains (Fig. 3b). Both the helical pitches are 14.355(3) Å, corresponding to the length of b-axis. The hbmb ligands act as pillars, linking the adjacent Zn(ii)/oba2− nets into a 3D framework (Fig. 3c). To further understand the structure of the complex, topological analysis was performed on 5. If the [Zn2(CO2)4] dimeric unit is taken as a 6-connected node and the ligands are considered as linkers, 5 represents a 6-connected 3D topological net (Fig. 3d). The Schläfli symbol for 5 is 412·63. Careful inspection of the structure of complex 5 suggests that it is a 3D 2-fold interpenetrating framework, as shown in Fig. 3e. Intriguingly, a void volume of 158.1 Å3 is also left after interpenetration and the pore volume ratio was calculated to be 6.8% by the PLATON program.

Crystal structures of [Zn2(hbmb)2(sdba)2]n (7) and [Cd(hbmb)(sdba)]n (8)

7 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P and features a parallel interpenetrated 2D → 3D network motif. The asymmetric unit consists of two crystallographically unique Zn ions, two deprotonated sdba2− anions and two hbmb ligands. As illustrated in Fig. 4a, both Zn ions display distorted tetrahedral geometry, defined by two carboxyl oxygen atoms from two sdba2− anions and two nitrogen atoms from two hbmb ligands. The τ4 parameters introduced by Houser25a of Zn1 and Zn2 are 0.85 and 0.87. The Zn–O bond distances vary from 1.953(5) to 1.997(5) Å, while the Zn–N bond lengths are in the range of 2.009(6)–2.054(6) Å.

In 7, there are two kinds of dihedral angle between the two benzene rings of the sdba2−ligand. One of the dihedral angles is 84.778°. The other is 89.682°. The two types of sdba2− anions alternately bridge two neighboring Zn ions in a bis-monodentate coordination mode to form a one-dimensional (1D) chain (Fig. 4b). The Zn⋯Zn distances across the sdba2−ligands are 12.913 Å and 13.383 Å, separately. There are two kinds of independent hbmb ligands, both of which exhibit an asymmetrical cis-conformation. The Ndonor⋯N–Csp3⋯Csp3 torsion angles of hbmb-I are 72.010° and 121.781°, respectively, while the Ndonor⋯N–Csp3⋯Csp3 torsion angles of hbmb-II are 86.346° and 126.446°, respectively. The two types of hbmb ligand act as a bidentate mode to link two adjacent Zn(ii) ions alternately to give rise to a “lemniscate” shape pseudo meso-helix along the c-axis, as portrayed in Fig. 4c. The Zn⋯Zn distances across the hbmb bridges are 14.435 (hbmb-I) and 14.354 (hbmb-II) Å, separately. Interestingly, every two hbmb ligands and two sdba2− anions are alternately linked by adjacent Zn(ii) ions, resulting in pseudo left- and right-handed helical chains along the c-axis (Fig. 4d). Notably, the hbmb and sdba2−ligands serve as connectors, linking four-connected Zn atoms into an undulating or corrugated 2D (4,4) net,28 which is a common network with (44·62)2 topology (Fig. 4e). There are four types of parallelograms in this 2D network. The dimensions of the different polygons are 12.913 × 14.435 Å with angles of 53.86° and 112.39°, 12.913 × 14.354 Å with angles of 66.05° and 113.95°, 13.383 × 14.435 Å with angles of 65.68° and 114.32° as well as 13.383 × 14.354 Å with angles of 57.69° and 122.31° (defined by the Zn⋯Zn separation and Zn⋯Zn⋯Zn angles, respectively), separately.29 The undulating layer is further connected to the adjacent layers in a parallel fashion to give an overall 2D → 3D polycatenated network (Fig. 4f).28h In spite of the polycatenation,28h7 still possesses a solvent-accessible volume in the channels of about 290.2 Å3 in the unit cell, which accounts for 8.6% of the total cell volume, as calculated by the PLATON program. As we know, only a limited number of examples of 2D → 3D parallel polycatenation with (4,4) nets have been reported to date.30

Similar to 7, 8 as a 3D coordination complex is also constructed through uncommon 2D → 3D parallel interpenetration of deeply corrugated 2D nets. The crystal structure of 8 was solved in the space group C2/c. Fig. 5a illustrates the coordination environment of the Cd(ii) ions. Each Cd(ii) ion is surrounded by two nitrogen atoms from two hbmb ligands and four oxygen atoms from two sdba2− anions. The lengths of the Cd–O bonds are in the range of 2.304(6)–2.485(7) Å, whereas the lengths of the Cd1–N1 and Cd1–N3 bonds are 2.244(7) Å and 2.276(7), separately. They are in conformity with those found in reported corresponding cadmium complexes.31 Unlike complex 7, two carboxylate groups of the sdba2− anions both adopt a bisdentate chelated coordination mode. The hbmb ligand adopts an asymmetric cis-conformation with two different Ndonor⋯N–Csp3⋯Csp3 torsion angles of 68.980° and 111.121°.

In 8, the sdba2− anions connect two adjacent Cd(ii) ions using the two carboxylate groups resulting in a 1D infinite chain with a dihedral angle between the two benzene rings of 89.512° (Fig. 5b). The Cd⋯Cd distances across the sdba2−ligands are 13.342 Å. Similar to 7, each hbmb ligand bridges two Cd(ii) ions to generate a pseudo meso-helix with a Cd⋯Cd distance of 14.619 Å along the c-axis (Fig. 5c). The interlaced connection of the Cd/hbmb and Cd/sdba2− chains constructs the corrugated 2D (4,4) network by sharing Cd(ii) ions (Fig. 5d). Intriguingly, in sharp contrast to the structure of 7, two types of parallelograms are present in the 2D structure. The dimensions of the two types of polygons are 13.341 × 14.619 Å with angles of 64.04° and 117.96° and 13.341 × 14.619 Å with angles of 56.88° and 123.12°, respectively. The degree of fluctuation can be reflected from the dihedral angle between these two types of polygons,7b which is 81.97°. The topology of 8 was analyzed to get a better insight into the structure. The Cd(ii) ions can be considered as a 4-connected node and the hbmb ligands and sdba2− anions serve as linkers. On the basis of this simplification, 8 exhibits a 4-connected topology with a point symbol of 44·62. The solvent-accessible volume is ∼535.0 Å3 per unit cell and the pore volume ratio was calculated to be 7.9%.

Effect of hbmb and dicarboxylate coligands on the structures of complexes 1–8

Based on the character of the flexible ligand, hbmb can present a cis/trans-conformation and symmetric (with the same Ndonor⋯N–Csp3⋯Csp3 torsion angles of two methylbenzimidazol arms)/asymmetric (with different Ndonor⋯N–Csp3⋯Csp3 torsion angles of two methylbenzimidazol arms) conformations. From the structural descriptions above, hbmb exhibits an asymmetric trans-conformation in 1 and 2, a symmetric trans-conformation in 3–6 as well as an asymmetric cis-conformation in 7 and 8. All of the Ndonor⋯N–Csp3⋯Csp3 torsion angles are different in 1–8. The free conformations of the –(CH2)– spacer greatly enrich the structures of complexes 1–8. In 1, 2, 5 and 6, the hbmb ligands serve as bridging pillars, which means they show 3D pillar-layered frameworks. In 3 and 4, hbmb bridges adjacent Zn(ii)/Cd(ii) ions, leading to the formation of a 1D infinite chain. In 7 and 8, hbmb acts as a bidentate mode to link two adjacent Zn(ii)/Cd(ii) ions alternately to give rise to pseudo meso-helices. Two bis(methylbenzimidazole) groups of hbmb can rotate freely when coordinating with central metal ions because of the flexible nature of the –(CH2)– spacers, which can greatly beautify and enrich the structure of complexes.

We have selected four dicarboxylates as coligands. The carboxylate anions have an important influence on the construction of the resultant 3D structures. In this work, we first selected two phenylenediacetate (phda2−) isomers as coligands. In 1 and 2, o-phda2− bridges Zn(ii)/Cd(ii) ions to generate well-known paddle-wheel binuclear clusters and the binuclear clusters are further extended by o-phda2− anions into 2D nets with alternately arranged left- and right-handed helical chains. The 2D Zn(ii)/Cd(ii)–H2phda layer is pillared by hbmb to generate a pillar-layered 3D framework with self-penetrating topology. In 3 and 4, p-phda2− anions adopting a symmetric trans-conformation connect two adjoining Zn(ii)/Cd(ii) ions to generate a 1D Zn(ii)/Cd(ii)–p-phda2− chain. The combination of a Zn(ii)/Cd(ii)–p-phda2− chain and Zn(ii)/Cd(ii)–hbmb chain leads to the 3D structures of 3 and 4, which exhibit 4-connected 66-dia topologies. Obviously, the different coordination fashions and angles of the two carboxylate groups of phda2− bring about varieties of structures and topologies in complexes 1–4. By introducing the “V” shape ligand H2oba, 3D 2-fold interpenetrating 6-connected (412·63) networks in 5 and 6 are obtained. oba2− also bridges Zn(ii)/Cd(ii) ions to generate well-known paddle-wheel binuclear clusters and the binuclear clusters are further extended by oba2− anions into 2D nets with alternately arranged left- and right-handed helical chains. When H2sdba is introduced into the synthetic procedure of 7 and 8, two 2D → 3D interpenetrating networks with distinct topologies are built. For 7, the sdba2− anions alternately bridge two neighboring Zn(ii) ions in a bis-monodentate coordination mode to form a one-dimensional (1D) chain, while in 8, two carboxylate groups of the sdba2− anions, which adopt a bisdentate chelated coordination mode, connect two adjacent Cd(ii) ions resulting in a 1D infinite chain. Comparing 7 with 8, we found that their structural differences are mainly attributed to the effect of the dihedral angle between the two benzene rings of the sdba2−ligand and the different coordination modes of the carboxylate groups. The appreciable structural differences of 5 and 7 demonstrate that the various coordination modes of the carboxylate groups play key roles in the construction of the complexes. These results show that diverse dicarboxylate coligands exert a profound influence on the features and overall structures of the resultant complexes.

Analyses of the thermal and chemical stability

To evaluate the stability of the coordination architectures with entangled structures, thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were carried out, as shown in Fig. S4 (ESI†). The TGA curve of complex 1 shows that it is stable up to 305 °C and then loses weight from 305 to 645 °C, corresponding to the decomposition of organic components. The remaining weight corresponds to the formation of ZnO (obsd, 18.53%; calcd, 18.89%). For anhydrous complex 2, the removal of the framework occurs in the range of 302–617 °C. The remaining weight corresponds to the formation of CdO (obsd, 27.73%; calcd, 26.87%). For 3, the TGA shows a weight loss of 3.09% from 72 to 199 °C, corresponding to the release of lattice water molecules (calcd, 2.89%), and then a plateau region is observed. The overall framework of 3 begins to decompose from 289 °C and the ZnO residue of 12.50% (calcd, 13.08%) is observed at 579 °C. Complex 4 shows that the first weight loss of 2.85% in the range of 84–177 °C is related to the loss of one lattice water molecule (calcd, 2.69%). The overall framework of 4 begins to decompose from 284 to 572 °C, corresponding to the decomposition of hbmb and p-phda2−, and the CdO residue of 20.71% (calcd, 19.19%) is observed. Complex 5 displays a high thermal stability, which is stable up to 392 °C and then keeps losing weight until to 564 °C, corresponding to the losses of hbmb and the decomposition of oba2−. Finally, the white ZnO residue of 14.84% (calcd, 16.45%) is observed. The framework of 6 remains intact until it is heated to 344 °C and the CdO residue of 22.77% (calcd, 23.69%) is obtained at 584 °C. For complex 7, there is no obvious weight loss until the framework begins to decompose at 339 °C. The ZnO residue of 12.42% (calcd, 11.36%) is obtained at 642 °C. The TGA curve of 8 shows a one step weight loss process from 359 to 602 °C, corresponding to the decomposition of hbmb and sdba2−. A residue of CdO (obsd, 17.49%, calcd, 16.82%) is observed.

Moreover, the chemical stability of complexes 1–8 was also investigated by suspending samples in boiling water, methanol, ethanol, acetonitrile and DMF for 48 h. During this process, the samples were periodically observed under an optical microscope. Complexes 1–8 were found to have kept their original shapes under boiling water, methanol, ethanol, acetonitrile and DMF. Single-crystal X-ray diffraction showed that the unit-cell parameters basically did not change (Table S2, ESI†). The crystal data of parts of the complexes in the boiling solvents mentioned above have also been collected (Table S1, ESI†). The PXRD patterns were also recorded for these samples (Fig. S3, ESI†). The high chemical stability of the eight entangled complexes in boiling water and organic solvents may mainly be ascribed to their dense structures.32 Besides, in 1 and 2, 5 and 6, the existence of the binuclear clusters [M2(CO2)4] (M = Zn/Cd) might also be conducive to the stability of the skeletons. In 3 and 4, diamond networks and intermolecular hydrogen bonds between uncoordinated water molecules and oxygen atoms from p-phda2− anions likely prevents the dissolution of the frameworks. In 7 and 8, the generation of helical chains is also possibly an important factor to maintain the frameworks.

Photoluminescence properties

The fluorescence properties of coordination complexes with d10 metal centers have attracted more interest owing to their potential applications in chemical sensors, photochemistry and electroluminescent displays.33 Hence, the solid-state photoluminescent spectra of Zn(ii)/Cd(ii) complexes 1–8, the free hbmb ligand and all of the dicarboxylic acids were investigated at ambient temperature, as depicted in Fig. S5 (ESI†). The free ligand hbmb displays a relatively weak emission band at 306 nm (λex = 275 nm). The photoluminescent spectra of o-H2phda, p-H2phda, H2oba and H2sdba show different emission peaks at 393 nm (λex = 330 nm), 482 nm (λex = 400 nm), 315 nm (λex = 296 nm) and 329 nm (λex = 277 nm), respectively. Complexes 1–8 exhibit an emission maxima at 445 nm (λex = 370 nm) for 1, 470 nm (λex = 365 nm) for 2, 443 nm (λex = 362 nm) for 3, 442 nm (λex = 366 nm) for 4, 445 nm (λex = 367 nm) for 5, 453 nm (λex = 392 nm) for 6, 436 nm (λex = 329 nm) for 7, 435 nm (λex = 333 nm) for 8, respectively. For 1 and 2, the emission bands are red-shifted (52 nm and 77 nm) compared with that of the free ligando-H2phda, which may be assigned to the intraligand emission of the carboxylate ligando-H2phda. It is possible that the coordination of the carboxylate groups to the Zn(ii)/Cd(ii) ion decreases the π* → n gap of the carboxylate ligand, leading to the red-shift of the emission peak.34 Notably, the emission spectra for 3 and 4 are blue-shifted (39 nm and 40 nm) with respect to the free carboxylic acid p-H2phda. The hypsochromic shift of the emission peaks of 3 and 4, related to p-H2phda, indicates that the coordination of the p-H2phda ligand to Zn(ii)/Cd(ii) ions increases the rigidity, leading to less vibrations of the skeleton and reducing the loss of energy by radiationless decay of the intraligand emission excited state.35 Obviously, the emission bands of 5 and 6 present larger red shifts (130 and 138 nm) in contrast to that of the free ligand H2oba. Interestingly, the emission bands of 7 and 8 are also highly red-shifted (107 and 106 nm) compared to that of the free ligand H2sdba. This result suggests that the fluorescence of 5, 6, 7 and 8 may be ascribed to a mixture characteristic of intraligand and ligand-to-ligand charge transition (LLCT).36 The strong fluorescence emissions of these complexes indicate that they may be excellent candidates for use as photoluminescent materials.37 Nevertheless, more elaborate theoretical and spectroscopic researches may be essential for a better understanding of the luminescent mechanism.37

Conclusions

In conclusion, by the introduction of different flexible dicarboxylate coligands, various 3D self-penetrating/interpenetrating architectures can be constructed from Zn(ii)/Cd(ii) salts and the flexible bis(methylbenzimidazole) ligand. The successful fabrication of complexes 1–8 may furnish meritorious insights into the construction of frameworks with wonderful entanglements. Moreover, 1–8 exhibit excellent chemical resistance to boiling water and usual organic solvents. Currently, further research is underway to prepare other new coordination complexes with intriguing entangled structures and useful properties.
