Polymeric and mixed ligand complex of cobalt(II) and 4-hydroxybenzhydrazide: Crystal structure and vibrational spectroscopy

Author links open overlay panel
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.molstruc.2010.06.027Get rights and content

Abstract

Reaction of CoSO4 and 4-hydroxybenzhydrazide in water/methanol solution resulted in polymeric complex with CO and NH2 groups of organic ligand, two water molecules and two oxygen atoms of two sulfate anions in metal coordination sphere. The X-ray analysis revealed that the complex is in the form of polymer chain of the helical structure formed due to sulfate bridge geometry and intra-chain hydrogen bonds. The variety of coordinated ligands is reflected in the vibrational spectra where six different metal–ligand modes were found. The bands related to stretching and bending vibrations of NH2, NH, OH groups as well as water molecules were located with help of hydrogen–deuterium substitution. For proposed band assignment the animation of DFT calculated normal modes was employed.

1. Introduction

A considerable number of coordination compounds is formed with the elements belonging to the VIII-th group of the periodic table. In the set of ferrous metals, the chemistry of the cobalt complexes is particularly advanced and reflects the cobalt ion capability of binding a wide range of organic as well as inorganic ligands, including solvent molecules. The metal itself is relatively uncommon but its compounds have been in use for centuries, mostly as pigments (cobalt blue) in glass and porcelain. The properties of that element are notable for the ease with which complexes are formed and complex formation stabilizes the +2 and +3 metal oxidation states. Usually, the vicinity of cobalt in coordination compounds is characterized by the octahedral, rarely by the tetrahedral geometry, where that former has been found in cobalamin (vitamin B12). The vitamin B12 consists of central Co atom, which occurs in the unusual range of +1 to +3 oxidation stage, flat spacious corrine ring in the equatorial and two ligands in the parallel positions. One of the coordination bonds is the type of Co–C, what makes the cobalamin the only known naturally occurring metaloorganic compound [1].

The diversity of molecules, which can bind the cobalt ion and form new compounds of interesting properties, allowed us to concern the 4-hydroxybenzhydrazide (4hbah) as a ligand in reactions with cobalt salts. As described in previous articles [2], [3], 4hbah belongs to a class of hydrazine derivatives, well known for different biological, analytical and agricultural applications. The most famous hydrazide is the isonicotinic acid hydrazide (isoniazide, antituberculosis drug) and it is different from 4hbah only by the presence of pyridine ring instead of 4-hydroxybenzene group. However, 4hbah shows moderate antimicrobial activity [4], it has been found that it increases the resistance against the tomato wilt disease [5] and can be attractive subject of further chemical and biological investigations.

In the literature a few examples of 4hbah complexes with transition metals have been reported. The research has been mainly focused on the copper(II) [6], [7], [8], [9], [10], [11], [12], [13], [14] and nickel(II) [6], [7], [11], [14] complexes. Only two communications have taken into account the 4hbah complexes with dithionate cobalt salts [7], [14]. Based on the infrared spectroscopic measurements, the authors have proposed the general formula of the complexes (MeLnS2O6·mH2O where n = 2, 3; m = 0, 1, 2; L – various benzhydrazides) and coordination mode through the N-amino and O-carbonyl atoms without any deeper consideration of the cobalt coordination environment, which frequently is completed by the solvent molecules.

Herein was described the preparation and crystal structure of a new 4hbah complex with cobalt(II) sulfate(VI) where the 4-hydroxybenzhydrazide, SO4 groups and solvent molecules are involved in metal binding. Additionally, the detailed analysis of vibrational spectra was performed, enhanced by the O,N-deuteration and DFT calculations.

2. Experimental

2.1. Synthesis and physical measurements

The [Co(H2O)2(4hbah)(μ-SO4)]n complex was synthesized in the reaction of 0.1 mmol commercially available 4-hydroxybenzhydrazide (Aldrich) in 5 cm3 warm CH3OH/H2O (3:2) solution with 0.1 mmol cobalt(II) sulfate(VI) (Aldrich) in 3 cm3 water solution. After 1 day incubation in room temperature the pink crystals suitable for X-ray investigation were obtained. Calculated for C7H12N2O8SCo (%): C, 24.50; H, 3.52; N, 8.16; S, 9.34. Found: C, 24.60; H, 3.38; N, 8.09; S, 9.27.

The preparation of O,N-deuterated isotopologue was preceded by twofold recrystallization of 4-hydroxybenzhydrazide from CH3OD/D2O solution and further carried out as for natural complex. The deuteration efficiency of more than 90% was estimated from the ratio of the integrated intensities of the absorption at 1648 cm−1.

The IR spectra have been measured on Perkin–Elmer FT-IR 2000 spectrometer with 2 cm−1 resolution, applying 64 scans and KBr pellet technique for middle-IR and 300 scans and sample immersed in Nujol mull for far-IR region.

Raman spectrum has been recorded on Horiba Jobin Yvon HR800 dispersive Raman spectrometer equipped with a microscope, laser diode (785 nm), operating in Sol–Gel Nanotechnology Materials Laboratory of Lower Silesian Centre for Advanced Technologies in Wroclaw. Final spectrum was computed with 1.5 cm−1 resolution from 16 scans measured at the back-scattering geometry.

2.2. X-ray analysis

The [Co(H2O)2(4hbah)(μ-SO4)]n crystal was measured on a KUMA KM4CCD diffractometer with the Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Ǻ) at 100 K with applying Lorentz and polarization corrections. The crystal structure was solved by direct methods option in SHELXS97 program [15] and refined by the full-matrix least-squares method on all F2 data using the Sheldrick’s SHELXL97 software [16]. The positions of heavy atoms were refined with anisotropic thermal parameters, whereas the hydrogen atoms were located in the difference Fourier syntheses and refined with isotropic thermal parameters. Other additional details and crystal data are included in Table 1. Molecular graphics were prepared using the ORTEP application [17] whereas the selected crystal parameters were calculated employing the PARST97 program [18].

2.3. Theoretical DFT calculations

The quantum calculations were performed applying the GAUSSIAN09 package [19], at DFT level, using the B3LYP functional [20] and LanL2DZ basis set [21]. For the structure optimization followed by computation of normal vibrations, the [Co2(H2O)2(4hbah)(μ-SO4)2] molecular fragment the same as shown in Fig. 1 was extracted from complex polymeric chain. The starting structural parameters were taken from the X-ray measurements.

In the optimized structure of the [Co2(H2O)2(4hbah)(μ-SO4)2], the quasi-octahedral structure of cobalt(II) coordination sphere and sulfate bridging mode were preserved.

Using the optimized structural parameters, the wavenumbers, IR intensities, Raman activities and atom displacements for all normal vibrations were computed with harmonic approximation. The computed frequencies were not scaled. Molden application was applied for the visual animation of normal vibrations and generation of both theoretical IR and Raman spectra [22].

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Description of the [Co(H2O)2(4hbah)(μ-SO4)]n structure

The arrangement of the simplest structural unit [Co(H2O)2(4hbah)(μ-SO4)] with adjacent sulfate group belonging to the next unit is shown in Fig. 1. In the metal coordination, the 4hbah acts as a bidentate ligand which employs carbonyl oxygen and a terminal nitrogen atom of the hydrazide, contributing in the five-membered chelate ring. Such coordination mode is typical for hydrazides [23]. The organic ligand, together with two water molecules and two SO42- groups occupy six positions of Co(II) coordination polyhedron. Each sulfate group links two neighbor metal atoms causing the formation of an infinite polymeric chain. The cis position of sulfate groups bonded to the same Co atom results in helical shape of the whole coordination polymer (Fig. 2).

Selected structural parameters of the metal environment are compiled in Table 2. It was assumed that three oxygen (O1w, O2, O3) and one nitrogen (N2) atoms form the quasi-planar base of the coordination square-bipyramid, meanwhile the apical positions are occupied by the oxygen atoms of water molecule (O2w) and sulfate anion (O4). The position of O2w is almost perpendicular to this basal plane (deviation is 2.08°) whilst the O4 atom is slightly (4.60°) displaced from the normal to that plane towards to O1w and O3 atoms. The shortest CoO bonds are formed between central atom and O1w and O2w oxygen atoms of water molecules with respective distances equal 2.076(1) and 2.057(1) Å. The lengths of bonds involving cobalt(II) and SO4 oxygen atoms are 2.112(1) and 2.143(1) Å, which are in agreement with the literature data, where the similar CoO bond lengths of 2.105 and 2.153 Å [24], [25] are reported.

The distances and angles in the SO4 group are in accordance with those generally observed in sulfates(VI) [1], [24], [26]. The participation of O3 and O4 atoms in metal coordination should influence the lengths of S–O3, S–O4 bonds, but surprisingly, only the S–O3 bond is slightly longer than three remaining bonds (Table 2). This untypical behavior could be explained by the O2w–H22⋯O3 hydrogen bond, which can cause some displacement of the O3 atom and elongation of the Co–O3 bond, in comparison with Co–O4 bond where the oxygen atom does not participate in any H-bonding.

The review of all hydrogen bonds indicates that [Co(H2O)2(4hbah)(μ-SO4)] takes advantage of the HOH, OH, and NH groups as donors and simultaneously the OH and SO4 groups as acceptors. Lengths and angles of hydrogen bonds are collected and numbered in Table 3. The hydrogen bonds could be classified into two sets: intra- and inter- polymeric chain. Two intra-chain hydrogen bonds involve the NH group, water molecule (H2O1w) and O5, O6 atoms of the sulfate ion. The N1–H1n···O6 bond number 1 is responsible for holding two structural units in twisted position, represented by 66.8(1)° dihedral angle between the best planes of the respective chelate rings. Second bond O1w–H12⋯O5 links two units separated by another unit, completing one full helical turn of the polymeric chain (Fig. 2). The chains expand along the b-axis, whereat two neighboring helices are twisted in opposite directions.

The chains are joined by four interchain H-bonds, numbered from 3 to 6 in Table 3 and shown in Fig. 3. Two of these interactions (numbers 3 and 4) involve the OH substituent of aromatic ring. The nearest benzene rings are inclined of 50.75° and the edge-to-face interactions between them are observed. The distance between the respective ring centroid and H3 atom of the next ring is 2.95 Ǻ, whereas the separation of both ring centroids is 4.87 Ǻ which is comparable with typical distances of discussed interactions [27].

3.2. Vibrational spectroscopy

The assignment of observed vibrational transitions of studied complex was carried out on the basis of theoretically calculated normal modes and simulated IR and Raman spectra. The fragment of polymeric chain (Fig. 1), selected for calculations, provides a good and complete modeling of metal coordination environment and bridging character of sulfate groups. For the last purpose, the vibrations of sulfate group without second Co atom (Fig. 1) were not taken under consideration. The theoretical IR spectrum of O,N-deuterated isotopologue was also calculated as a counterpart for the respective observed spectrum.

The band assignment, proposed in Table 4, describes the bands in terms of normal vibrations predominantly contributing in respective theoretical bands. Descriptions of the calculated normal modes have been based on their computer animations.

3.2.1. Stretching vibrations of the cobalt coordination sphere

The metal coordination sphere of the investigated complex is not homogenous and consists of three types of ligands: 4-hbah, H2O and SO4. Consequently, more than three stretching modes, characteristic for an ideal octahedral symmetry, should be observed. The review of the normal mode animations reveals that it is possible to differentiate six stretching Co-ligand vibrations and corresponding observed bands. Most of these vibrations are not characteristic, but the vibrational coupling is usually observed between stretching vibrations of bonds in trans positions with respect to the metal atom.

The vibrations of bonds formed between cobalt and organic ligand contribute significantly in five theoretical bands at 582, 495, 430, 415 and 304 cm−1. In the highest wavenumber vibration, the ν(Co–O2) vibration couple with the opposite CoOSO3 bond stretching which in turn couples with SO4 group deformation mode. The first vibration is predominant in 415 cm−1 mode, where some deformations of metal surrounding also weakly contribute. The coupling between ν(Co–N) and ν(Co–O1w) vibrations is more evident and appears in 430 and 304 cm−1 bands resulting respectively from out-of-phase and in-phase stretching vibrations of discussed bonds. Second vibration shows typical for ν(M–NH2) energy lowering upon deuteration, which was also observed for corresponding 320 cm−1 band shifted to 303 cm−1. The stretching vibrations of third pair of the opposite coordination bonds between CoO4SO3 and Co–O2w were calculated at 311 and 269 cm−1. According to computed normal mode animations, both vibrations are out-of-phase, but with different contributions of ν(CoO4SO3) and ν(Co–O2w) what was pointed out by placing the predominant vibration on the first position in the band assignment (Table 4). The observed counterparts for both theoretical bands were detected at 309 and 258 cm−1.

3.2.2. Vibrations of the bridging sulfate group

The bridging character of the sulfate group causes that two free OSO and two coordinated O–S–O oxygen atoms could be distinguished. Despite small differences between S–O bond length (Table 2), the vibrational behavior of mentioned OSO and O–S–O groups is much more divergent. In a consequence, the asymmetric and symmetric stretching vibrations of the OSO bonds were assigned to two very strong and broad absorptions at 1115 and 1047 cm−1. Similar vibrations of the O–S–O bonds were attributed to the band of medium intensity at 978 cm−1 observed in the infrared spectrum and to its 980 cm−1 counterpart in Raman spectrum. Presented wavenumbers are relatively similar to stretching vibrations of uncoordinated sulfate, present at 1105 (F2) and 983 cm−1 (Ag) [28].

The corresponding calculated frequencies of sulfate group vibrations, given in the Table 4, are considerably lower in relation to those observed. Probably, it results from differences of SO4 group interactions (via hydrogen bonds and with cobalt(II) atoms) which occurs in crystal lattice, but were not modeled in theoretical calculations. The comparison of calculated and observed lengths of cobalt-sulfate bonds testifies that the former are shorter (1.97 and 1.81 Ǻ) than the latter (2.143(1) and 2.112(1) Ǻ). The geometry of the Co–O–S subunit forces the vibrational coupling between stretching of cobalt–sulfate oxygen bond and bending of the respective SO4 bonds. Such coupling was well reflected in calculations, where the ν(Co–OSO3) mode contributes in the 582 and 507 cm−1 normal modes of the predominant δ(SO4) character.

3.2.3. The chelate ring vibrations

In the set of atoms, excluding cobalt, which build the chelate ring, there are oxygen, carbon and nitrogen atoms linked by the CO, C–N and N–N bonds.

According to calculations, the stretching vibration of coordinated carbonyl group is not characteristic but couples with other vibrations as ν(CN), ν(CC) as well as with the vibration of the aromatic ring numbered 19a (in the Wilson’s notation [29]). Due to this coupling, the ν(CO) occurs in three theoretical bands at 1581, 1527 and 1518 cm−1, attributed respectively to 1578, 1506 and 1539 cm−1 strong IR bands. The listed couplings also cause that these theoretical transitions, especially those with significant ν(C-N) contribution, are affected by deuteration, what was taken into account during their assignment to bands observed in the spectra of natural and deuterium labeled complex (Fig. 4). The most noticeable effect of that kind was observed for the normal mode at 1527 cm−1 (1472 cm−1 after deuteration) attributed not to the nearest 1539 cm−1 band, but to the absorption at 1506 cm−1, for which the adequate band was found after deuteration at 1466 cm−1. In normal modes at 1527 and 1518 cm−1 the coupling between ν(CN) and ν(CO) vibrations is different, consisting in the symmetric (1518 cm−1) or asymmetric (1527 cm−1) stretching of both bonds.

Unfortunately, the band characteristic for the ν(NN) vibrations was not detected in observed spectra, however the calculation predicted the ν(NN) contributions in three theoretical bands at 1050, 1137, and 1161 cm−1. These wavenumbers were not listed in Table 4 because in the similar IR region, two wide and intensive absorptions were already attributed to SO4 normal modes, what makes impossible to observe the weaker bands related to the discussed ν(NN) vibration.

3.2.4. The NH2, NH and OH vibrations

The positions of absorption bands resulting from the NH2, NH or OH vibrations may vary in some extent due to several interactions in which these groups may be engaged in the condensed state. Therefore, their typical and characteristic wavenumbers cannot always be directly applied; some additional information should be experimentally collected to detect these bands among others, lying at similar positions. In order to localize the bands originating from the vibrational stretching and bending motions of the NH2, NH and OH groups (including water molecules), the spectrum of O,N-deuterated complex was recorded. The comparison of both, natural and deuterated compound spectra (Fig. 4) unambiguously indicates that a very complicated absorption in the region of 3600–3000 cm−1 corresponds to stretching vibrations of mentioned groups. Within the confines of this absorption, five maxima at 3483, 3331, 3296, 3225 i 3153 cm−1 are present. As it results from the calculations, one maximum, at the highest wavenumber, should be attributed to the stretching vibration of the OH group present in organic ligand and asymmetric vibrations of water molecules, occurring in the cobalt coordination sphere. The symmetric stretching vibration of H2O molecules was assigned to maximum at 3296 cm−1. According to calculations, the energy of ν(NH) vibration is between those recently discussed and was attributed to the 3331 cm−1 band. Two remaining absorptions with the smallest wavenumbers were associated with asymmetric and symmetric vibrations of NH2 group.

In the wavenumber range of bending modes of discussed groups, the deuteration effect and performed calculations, show that scissoring normal mode of NH2 group should be assigned to the intense band at 1648 cm−1 which disappeared after deuteration (Fig. 4). This band is probably also due to the scissoring vibrations of water molecules, calculated at 1651 and 1591 cm−1. Such assignment is proposed because the close positioned bands observed at 1615 and 1578 are not sensitive to deuteration and the 1648 cm−1 band remains the only possible candidate for scissoring vibration of HN2 and both H2O groups.

The remaining NH2 bending vibrations have the contributions in normal modes at 1286 (ρt(NH2)), 1255 (ρw(NH2)) and 785 cm−1 (ρr(NH2)). The listed wavenumbers were assigned to bands which are sensitive to deuteration, in following order: ρt(NH2) – 1316, ρw(NH2) – 1243 and ρr(NH2) – 720 cm−1. Applying similar criterion, the counterpart bands of the deuterated complex were localized at: ρt(ND2) – 957, ρw(ND2) – 911 i ρr(ND2) – 580 cm−1.

The NH group bending vibrations can be described as movements of H atom in relation to the plane composed of vicinal heavy atoms. In the case of discussed complex, that plane is the plane of chelate ring. The vibration in which the inclination of the hydrogen atom is parallel to mentioned plane, δ(NH) was calculated at 1348 cm−1 and attributed to weak absorption at 1370 cm−1. As results from the calculations, the corresponding δ(ND) vibration has about 300 cm−1 lower frequency, and therefore it has been assigned to the band at 1018 cm−1. The second deformation of NH group, normal to chelate ring, π(NH) has lower energy and appears markedly in the set of three normal modes located in the scope of 530–495 cm−1. In observed IR spectrum, near to this wavenumbers broad absorption with the maximum at approximately 550 cm−1 was found and assigned to discussed π(NH) vibration. The predominant contribution of π(ND) vibration was found in theoretical band at 393 cm−1, correlated with the observed 425 cm−1 band.

For each water molecule coordinated to cobalt atom, three deformation modes are expected: scissoring, wagging and twisting. However, in the literature there are some discrepancies in the assignments of these vibrations, even in their frequency order [30], [31]. The structural researches indicate, that water molecules are engaged in numerous hydrogen bonds in the [Co(H2O)2(4hbah)(μ-SO4)]n crystal. This kind of interactions was not taken into consideration in calculations, what causes, that correlating both calculated and observed H2O related bands should be carried out with a special attention. More, the animations of the respective normal modes show that pure scissoring, wagging and twisting modes do not appear, but are mixed in different extent. Thus, in the assignment proposed in Table 4, six normal modes showing significant contributions of discussed deformations were only generally characterized as ρ(H2O). In the observed spectra, the deuteration experiment pointed out only four (H) or five (D) bands positioned in the same wavenumber regions as respective calculated modes.

3.2.5. The vibrations of aromatic ring

The analysis of the IR intensities calculated for the aromatic ring vibrations indicates that among twelve skeletal modes only seven of them have the intensities strong enough to be practically observed. Applying the Wilson’s notation [29], the ν(8b), ν(19b), ν(1), τ(4), δ(6a), δ(6b) and τ(16) vibrations were attributed to the IR bands observed at 1615, 1446, 893, 765, 634, 668 and 550 cm−1. The assignment of 893 cm−1 band to the breathing ν(1) mode is additionally confirmed by strong intensity of the corresponding Raman band.

The stretching and bending vibrations of CH groups are typical and those detected in the recorded spectra are listed in Table 4.

4. Conclusions

The performed normal mode calculations and measured deuteration effects lead to the following conclusions: (1) The stretching vibrations of metal–ligand bonds are not characteristic, but tend to couple between themselves, especially if bonds are in opposite positions in respect to the metal atom. (2) The computer animations of respective normal modes show that the deformation vibrations of coordinated water molecules cannot be characterized in terms of pure rocking, twisting and wagging modes, because of their partial mixing. (3) The lower number of observed bands sensitive to deuteration than predicted by calculations suggests that some vibrations of H/D replaced groups and water molecules have similar wavenumbers and overlap in the IR spectrum.
