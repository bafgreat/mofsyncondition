1 Introduction

With the requirements of national security and environmental friendliness, finding an efficient and cheap material to detect highly explosive compounds such as nitrobenzene and its derivatives is becoming more and more urgent. Fluorescence sensing of chemicals is a recently emerging research area and holds great promise due to its operability, sensitivity, high selectivity, rapid response and long-term stability.1 Since the last century, lanthanide metal–organic frameworks (Ln-MOFs) have attracted extensive attention for their potential applications such as in sensor devices, gas separation and storage due to their diverse structures, permanent channels/holes, large specific surface areas, specific functional groups, characteristic luminescence and fast response.2 Due to the oxygen-philic characteristics of rare earth cations, the design and synthesis of Ln-MOFs with excellent specific performance using aromatic carboxylic acids as ligands have been the focus of research by scientists.3 In particular, a Ln-MOF-based sensor built with π-electron-rich ligands usually shows a sensitive response to electron deficient analytes, such as nitroaromatic compounds, due to the redox quenching mechanism.4 The organic ligands in Ln-MOFs contain aromatic or conjugated π moieties, which not only endow the Ln-MOFs with strong photoluminescence properties, but also make the frontier orbitals of the compounds susceptible to the interference of analytes. There are few Ln-MOFs with aromatic carboxylic acids as ligands for selective sensing of nitroaromatic compounds reported in the literature.5 Meanwhile, the stability, selectivity and photoluminescence efficiency are still challenges for current Ln-MOF-based sensors. Based on the considerations that (i) a multidentate carboxylate ligand might be one of the most promising linkers due to the rigidity of its structure, the thermal stability of the chelated compounds and versatile coordination modes in one compound; (ii) the extension of Lewis' original concept from atoms to molecules and further to MOFs adds pore space into the realm of synthetic chemists' ability to control matter, and the accessible Lewis-base sites in the host MOFs can display excellent monofunctional sensing of single organic molecules or metal cations;6 (iii) Tb-MOFs, which are the most useful lanthanide complexes, having sharp typical emission bands and millisecond lifetimes, are regarded to be promising potential optical sensing materials,7 we are especially interested in Tb-MOFs with ligands containing multiple aromatic rings and open Lewis basic triazolyl active sites, i.e., H3DBPT (3-(3,5-dicarboxylphenyl)-5-(4-carboxylphenyl)-1-H-1,2,4-triazole). Such a π-electron rich ligand would achieve high sensitivity and high selectivity for sensing electron deficient nitro explosives.

On the other hand, the introduction of several different functional groups within the ligands will be helpful to yield multivariate frameworks, in which the varying arrangement of functionalities gives rise to materials that offer a synergistic combination of properties.8 Ln-MOFs with ligands that have multiple aromatic rings and open Lewis basic triazolyl active sites exhibit a certain porosity and high stability, which also makes the Ln-MOFs have adsorption and selectivity properties for certain gases, and they can be used as potential materials for gas separation. Meanwhile, to date, there have been only a few reports on Ln-MOF materials for potential application in mixed gas separation.9 The work herein reports the synthesis and crystal structure of four porous anionic Ln-MOFs {[Me2NH2][Ln3L3(HCOO)]·7DMF}n (Ln-MOF, Ln = Gd, Tb, Ho, Dy, L = H3DBPT) under solvothermal reaction conditions. The Gd-MOF after desolvation exhibits potential for industrially important C2H2/CH4 and CO2/CH4 separation. The luminescence intensity of the Tb-MOF can be modulated using small molecule organic solvents, and the fluorescence of the Tb-MOF exhibits significant quenching in nitrobenzene solvent.

2 Results and discussion

2.1 Crystal structure

The four anionic Ln-MOFs are isostructural. The Tb-MOF was taken as an example to give the structural description. X-ray single crystal structure analysis shows that the Tb-MOF belongs to the monoclinic crystal system, with the C2/m space group. In addition to solvent molecules and counterions, the smallest asymmetric unit contains 1.5 Tb(iii) ions, 1.5 L3− ligands and 0.5 formate ions. As shown in Fig. 1, the central metal ion Tb1 coordinates with eight carboxyl oxygen atoms from six ligands, forming a twisted double-cap triangular prismatic configuration. Tb2 coordinates with seven oxygen atoms from five carboxyl groups and two statistically distributed oxygen atoms of formic acid (50%) or two statistically distributed oxygen atoms of benzoic acid (50%) to form a single-cap tetragonal antiprism configuration. Tb2–Tb1–Tb2 ions form [Tb3(COO)10] secondary building units (SBUs) through ten bridge carboxyl ions, and the [Tb3(COO)10] SBUs are interconnected to form a one-dimensional inorganic chain parallel to the a-axis (Fig. 2a and b). The chains are further connected by the ligand L3− to form a three-dimensional framework with one-dimensional tunnels parallel to the a-axis (Fig. 2c). Calculated using the program PLATON, the pore occupancy is 44.93%, and the disordered DMF solvent molecules and Me2NH2+ counterions fill the pores. The aperture of one-dimensional tunnels is 1.5 nm × 1.2 nm in size as shown in Fig. S3.†

2.2 Fluorescence properties and detection of small molecules in organic solvents

Tb-MOFs are regarded to be promising potential optical sensing materials among lanthanide complexes for their sharp typical emission bands and millisecond lifetimes.7 Here, the Tb-MOF is taken to study its fluorescence properties. At room temperature, the free ligand H3L is excited at a wavelength of 351 nm and exhibits emission peaks at 408, 468, and 520 nm (Fig. 3a), which may be attributed to the π–π* transition of the ligand. The solid sample of the Tb-MOF is excited at a wavelength of 314 nm, and the characteristic green fluorescence of the Tb(iii) ion can be observed (Fig. 3b). The strongest emission peak is at 543 nm, which is attributed to the 5D4 → 7F5 transition. The two weak emission peaks at 584 nm and 621 nm are attributed to the 5D4 → 7F4 and 5D4 → 7F3 transitions, respectively. In order to further explore the fluorescence properties of the Tb-MOF, the exchange of small molecules in different organic solvents were carried out. 3.0 mg of Tb-MOF was put in a 5 mL volumetric flask, washed 2–3 times with the appropriate solvents respectively after being soaked in methanol (MeOH), ethanol (EtOH), methylene chloride (CH2Cl2), chloroform (CHCl3), carbon tetrachloride (CCl4), acetonitrile (MeCN), N,N-dimethyl formamide (DMF), tetrahydrofuran (THF), toluene (TO), chlorobenzene (Cl-BZ) and nitrobenzene (NB), then oscillated with an ultrasonic instrument for 30 min and rested at room temperature for 24 hours, and finally, a stable suspension was made for the liquid fluorescence test.

As shown in Fig. 3c, the Tb-MOF has different luminous intensities in different organic solvents: Tb-MOF-DMF > Tb-MOF-THF > Tb-MOF-MeCN > Tb-MOF-CCl4 > Tb-MOF-CH2Cl2 > Tb-MOF-EtOH > Tb-MOF-MeOH > Tb-MOF-Cl-BZ > Tb-MOF-TO > Tb-MOF-CHCl3 > Tb-MOF-NB. The results show that the Tb-MOF displays a fluorescence quenching phenomenon in nitrobenzene solvent. Nitro aromatic compounds can quench the fluorescence of the Tb-MOF, which is mainly attributed to a similar absorption of light by NB and H3L. The energy of the H3L absorbed light is transferred to the nitroaromatic compounds, which weakens the possibility of energy transfer from H3L to Tb(iii), and finally results in the fluorescence quenching of the Tb(iii).10 For further study of the fluorescence quenching of the Tb-MOF in different concentrations of NB solution, DMF suspensions with different concentrations of 1-nitrobenzene were prepared and their fluorescence properties were studied. Fig. 3d shows the change of fluorescence intensity of the Tb-MOF in DMF and DMF suspensions with different concentrations of NB. It can be seen that the Tb-MOF has high sensitivity to NB. Only 5 mg L−1 NB solution can quench the fluorescence of the Tb-MOF significantly, and with the increasing concentration of NB, the quenching phenomenon will become more and more obvious until the fluorescence is completely quenched. Therefore, the characteristics of the change of fluorescence intensity can be used to study the potential application of Tb-MOFs in the detection of nitro explosives.

2.3 Adsorption properties

Gd(iii) is the lightest ion among the four lanthanides used in this study. Here, the Gd-MOF is taken to study its adsorption properties. The sample was activated before gas adsorption measurement. At first, the sample was washed with DMF and then immersed in anhydrous acetone at 373 K for 2 h; after that acetone was sucked out and then fresh acetone was added to the sample. The above process was repeated seven times to achieve the complete replacement of the guest molecules in the sample with acetone. Finally, the sample was activated under vacuum at 373 K until the degassing rate reached 2 μMHg min−1. The nitrogen adsorption behavior of the activated samples was determined. As shown in Fig. 4a, the adsorption curve doesn't coincide with the desorption curve of nitrogen, but shows an obvious hysteresis phenomenon. At 77 K and one standard atmosphere, the maximum adsorption of the Gd-MOF for nitrogen is 99.48 cm3 g−1. The BET and Langmuir specific surface areas of the Gd-MOF are 238 m2 g−1 and 453 m2 g−1, respectively, calculated from the adsorption isotherm curve. The pore volume is 0.154 cm3 g−1.

Considering the presence of triazolyl groups and the microporous characteristics of the material, we studied the adsorption and separation of CO2, CH4 and C2H2 by the Gd-MOF at different temperatures. As shown in Fig. 4b–d, at 298 K and one standard atmospheric pressure, the maximum adsorption of the Gd-MOF for CO2 is 19.88 cm3 g−1, for C2H2 is 32.36 cm3 g−1 and for CH4 is 6.48 cm3 g−1. When the temperature drops to 278 K, the adsorption of CO2, C2H2 and CH4 by the Gd-MOF is 25.59, 39.04 and 8.67 cm3 g−1, respectively. It can be seen from the adsorption curves that compared with CO2 and C2H2, only a limited amount of CH4 is adsorbed by the Gd-MOF. Such a difference in the adsorption capacity makes the separation of the C2H2–CH4 and CO2–CH4 gases by the Gd-MOF possible. In addition, it can be seen from the trend of the isotherms that the adsorption and desorption curves of the Gd-MOF for CO2 gas almost completely coincide without an obvious hysteresis phenomenon, which indicates that the adsorption and desorption of the Gd-MOF for CO2 gas are completely reversible. However, the adsorption and desorption curves of the Gd-MOF for CH4 and C2H2 show an obvious hysteresis phenomenon. The reason may be that the tunnels of the Gd-MOF will be narrowed after activation, and the kinetic radius of CH4 (3.8 Å) is larger than those of CO2 (3.3 Å) and C2H2 (3.3 Å), which hinders the escape of CH4. Moreover, due to the high polarizability and the strong interaction between C2H2 and open metal active sites on the surface of the tunnels, C2H2 is not easy to desorb. Meanwhile CO2 only has a weak interaction with nitrogen atoms of L3−, which makes CO2 easier to desorb. The higher selective adsorption can be attributed to the synergistic effect of tunnels, open metal active sites and Lewis basic nitrogen atoms.

3 Conclusion

Four Ln-MOFs were synthesized by the reaction of lanthanide nitrate with 3-(3,5-dicarboxylphenyl)-5-(4-carboxylphenyl)-1,2,4-triazole under solvothermal conditions. Single crystal diffraction analysis shows that the four Ln-MOFs are isostructural and have 3D frameworks with one-dimensional tunnels. The luminescence intensity of the Tb-MOF can be modulated using small molecule organic solvents, and the Tb-MOF has high sensitivity to NB which shows its potential application in the detection of nitro explosives. Furthermore, the Gd-MOF after desolvation exhibits its potential for industrially important C2H2/CH4 and CO2/CH4 separation. Its highly selective adsorption can be attributed to the synergistic effect of tunnels, open metal active sites and Lewis basic nitrogen atoms.
