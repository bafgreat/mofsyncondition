Reactions of Cd(OAc)2·2H2O with variously substituted pyridines. Efforts to unravel the factors that determine structure/nuclearity of the products

Abstract

The reactions of Cd(OAc)2·2H2O with variously substituted pyridines in methanol afforded unique one-dimensional coordination polymers (1D CPs), [Cd2(μ2-κ2:κ1-OAc)2(μ2-κ1:κ1-OAc)2L2] (L = NC5H5 (1), NC5H4Me-3 (2), and NC5H3Me2-3,5 (3)) and [Cd3(μ3-κ1:κ2-OAc)3(μ2-κ2-OAc)(μ2-κ2:κ1-OAc)2(NC5H3Me2-3,4)2] (4), and discrete and bimolecular complexes, [(Cd(OAc)2(NC5H3Me2-3,4)2(H2O)2] (5), [Cd(κ2-OAc)2(NC5H4Me-4)2(H2O)]·[Cd(κ2-OAc)2(H2O)2)] (6), [Cd(κ2-OAc)2L2L′]·xH2O (x = 0, L′ = H2O, L = NC5H4(OMe)-4 (7); NC5H4tBu-4 (8); x = 2, L = L′ = NC5H4(NMe2)-4 (9·2H2O)). The products were characterised by elemental analysis, IR, solution NMR (1H and 13C), solid-state CP-MAS 13C{1H} and 113Cd NMR, TGA/DTA analyses, and single crystal X-ray diffraction. Phase purity of 1–4 was verified by powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD). Plausible mechanisms of formation of the products are proposed based on a point zero charge model. 4 represents the first cadmium containing 1D CP that possesses a tridentate bridging (μ3-κ1:κ2) acetate coordination mode and 6 represents the first structurally characterised bimolecular cadmium(II) complex containing two different neutral cadmium(II) coordination species per formula unit. 9·2H2O was calcined at 500 °C to afford CdO as confirmed by PXRD and the morphology of CdO was studied by scanning electron microscopy.

1. Introduction

Lewis base coordinated cadmium(II) carboxylate complexes are known to exist in a variety of forms ranging from mononuclear, dinuclear, and multinuclear complexes to coordination polymers (CPs) [1], [2], [3], [4]. These complexes have been widely studied over the past few decades due to their interesting structural aspects and photoluminescent properties [1], [2], [3], [4], as a surrogate metallo-bioprobe for the active sites of Zn(II), Ca(II), and Mg(II) containing metalloproteins through 113Cd NMR spectroscopy [5], [6], and as pre-catalysts in copolymerization of epoxides and CO2 [7]. Moreover, this class of complexes are shown to be useful substrates [8] and key intermediates [9] in the formation of cadmium chalcogenide nanocrystals. The structure and nuclearity of Lewis base coordinated group 12 metal carboxylate complexes in general and those of cadmium in particular are difficult to predict due to the presence of a completely filled d10 electronic configuration of the metal ion and the flexible coordination modes exhibited by the carboxylate moiety. Various coordination modes of the carboxylate moiety towards the metal ion are illustrated in Fig. 1 [10], [11], [12]. We believe that steric and electronic properties of both the Lewis base and the carboxylate moiety in Lewis base coordinated cadmium(II) carboxylate complexes dictate the structure/nuclearity of the products when other experimental conditions are identical.

In the past, we studied the reactions of Zn(OAc)2·2H2O with structural isomers of lutidine in methanol at ambient condition and from these reactions two types of lutidine coordinated trinuclear complexes and a 1D CP were isolated [13]. We now want to investigate the reactions of Cd(OAc)2·2H2O with pyridine and variously substituted pyridines (Ls) in methanol at ambient condition with a view aimed at understanding how the differences in the intrinsic properties of Ls in conjunction with the greater ionic radius of cadmium than zinc (1.09 versus 0.88 Å) [14], and hence propensity of the former metal ion to adopt higher coordination numbers and a variety of geometries [1](b), [15], [16] influence the structures and nuclearity of the resulting products. From this endeavour, we have isolated two types of CPs namely, [Cd2(μ2-κ2:κ1-OAc)2(μ2-κ1:κ1-OAc)2L2] (L = NC5H5 (1), NC5H4Me-3 (2), and NC5H3Me2-3,5 (3)) and [Cd3(μ3-κ1:κ2-OAc)3(μ2-κ2-OAc)(μ2-κ2:κ1-OAc)2(NC5H3Me2-3,4)2] (4) and discrete and bimolecular complexes namely, [(Cd(OAc)2(NC5H3Me2-3,4)2(H2O)2] (5), [Cd(κ2-OAc)2(NC5H4Me-4)2(H2O)]·[Cd(κ2-OAc)2(H2O)2)] (6), [Cd(κ2-OAc)2L2L′]·xH2O (x = 0, L′ = H2O, L = NC5H4(OMe)-4 (7); NC5H4tBu-4 (8); x = 2, L = L′ = NC5H4(NMe2)-4 (9·2H2O)) in moderate to good yields. The products have been characterised by elemental analysis, IR, solution NMR (1H and 13C), solid-state CP-MAS 13C{1H} and 113Cd NMR, TGA/DTA analyses, and single crystal X-ray diffraction. Plausible mechanisms of formation of products prepared in the present investigation are proposed based on a point zero charge (pzc) model [17]. To the best of our knowledge, 4 represents the first structurally characterised cadmium(II) carboxylate 1D CP that contains a tridentate bridging (μ3-κ1:κ2) acetate coordination mode while 6 represents the first structurally characterised bimolecular cadmium(II) complex containing two different neutral cadmium(II) coordination species per formula unit to be reported in the literature.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Syntheses

Cd(OAc)2·2H2O was treated with various Ls in methanol at ambient condition in 1:1, 1:2, and 1:3 Cd:L ratios that led to the formation of a variety of products as outlined in Scheme 1. The Ls employed in the present investigation and their pKa values are listed in Fig. 2 [18], [19]. Cd(OAc)2·2H2O was separately treated with one equiv of pyridine, 3-picoline, and 3,5-lutidine in methanol at ambient condition for 24 h to afford 1D CPs, 1–3 in 94%, 89%, and 70% yields, respectively while the aforementioned reactions carried out with two equiv. of the respective Ls afforded 1–3 in comparable yields. Cd(OAc)2·2H2O was separately treated with one and two equiv. of 3,4-lutidine in methanol at ambient condition for 24 h to afford a 1D CP, 4 in 62% yield and a mononuclear complex, 5 in 90% yield, respectively. Cd(OAc)2·2H2O was separately treated with one and two equiv. of 4-picoline in methanol at ambient condition for 24 h to afford a bimolecular complex, 6 in 92% yield from both the reactions. Cd(OAc)2·2H2O was separately treated with two equiv. of 4-methoxypyridine and 4-tert-butylpyridine in methanol at ambient condition to afford mononuclear complexes, 7 and 8 in 91% and 95% yields, respectively. Complexes 7 and 8 were also obtained as the exclusive products albeit in lower yields of 40% and 42%, respectively even when one equiv of L per cadmium was used. Similarly, the reactions of Cd(OAc)2·2H2O with one, two, and three equiv of 4-dimethylaminopyridine (DMAP) in methanol at ambient condition afforded a mononuclear complex, 9·2H2O in 30%, 62%, and 94% yields, respectively. Thus, the level of aggregation of the products depends upon Cd:L ratio in the formation of 4 and 5 and does not depend upon Cd:L ratio in the formation of other products.

Remarkably, the reactions of Cd(OAc)2·2H2O with one or two equiv. of sterically more hindered Ls namely, 2-picoline, 2,3-, 2,4- and 2,6-lutidines in methanol at ambient condition did not afford any new products. The pKa value of 2-picoline is comparable with that of 4-picoline but greater than that of 3-picoline (Fig. 2). Analogously, pKa values of 2,3-, 2,4- and 2,6-lutidines are greater than those of 3,4- and 3,5-lutidines. Hence, steric factor rather than basicity/nucleophilicity appears to be the reason for the non-reactive nature of sterically more hindered Ls towards Cd(OAc)2·2H2O. The reactions of Zn(OAc)2·2H2O with 2,3- and 2,4-lutidine carried out separately in methanol at ambient condition resulted in the formation of trinuclear complexes, [Zn3(μ2-κ1:κ1-OAc)4(μ2-κ2-OAc)2L2] (L = 2,3-lutidine and 2,4-lutidine) [13]. Thus, the difference in reactivity pattern of M(OAc)2·2H2O (M = Zn and Cd) pair towards 2,3- and 2,4-lutidines is attributed to greater ionic radius of cadmium than zinc in conjunction with the greater steric bulk of these lutidines. Due to greater ionic radius, cadmium tends to adopt higher coordination numbers in its complexes but such complexes would be destabilised when the L is sterically more hindered.

3.2. X-ray diffraction studies

ORTEP representations of 1–3 are illustrated in Fig. 3 and selected bond distances and bond angles are listed in Table S1 in the Supplementary material. 1–3 possess an identical 1D CP framework. There are two types of chemically distinct cadmium atoms in a repeat unit and in one of these units, there are two crystallographically distinct cadmium atoms. The crystallographically distinct cadmium atoms, Cd1 and Cd3 are surrounded by a pair of oxygen atoms from a pair of bidentate bridging (μ2-κ1:κ1) acetate moieties, a pair of oxygen atoms from a pair of tridentate chelating bridging (μ2-κ2:κ1) acetate moieties and the nitrogen atom from a pair of Lewis bases and thus attain a coordination number of six. The two pairs of oxygen atoms and the pair of nitrogen atoms are centrosymmetrically related. On the other hand, the central cadmium atom, Cd2 is surrounded by a pair of oxygen atoms from a pair of bidentate bridging acetate moieties, and two pairs of oxygen atoms from a pair of tridentate chelating bridging acetate moieties and thus attains a coordination number of six. The cadmium atoms Cd1 and Cd3 revealed a distorted octahedral geometry while the cadmium, Cd2 revealed a distorted trigonal prismatic geometry. Thus, two adjacent cadmium atoms in a repeat unit are linked by one bidentate bridging and one tridentate chelating bridging acetate moieties and thus lead to the growth of a 1D CP. The N1–Cd1–N1 and N2–Cd3–N2 units are non-coplanar with the angle between these units being 76.46(4)°, 78.39(3)°, and 77.22(4)° in 1–3, respectively.

The formation of a 1D CP, 3 as against the formation of a discrete open framework trinuclear complex, [Zn3(OAc)2(μ2-κ2:κ1-OAc)2(μ2-κ1:κ1-OAc)2(H2O)2(NC5H3Me2-3,5)2] reported earlier by one of us [13] from the reactions of M(OAc)2·2H2O (M = Cd and Zn) with 3,5-lutidine under identical condition confirms the findings that cadmium(II) salts are prone to form polymeric complexes while the respective zinc(II) salts are prone to form discrete complexes. It is to be noted that both Zn(OAc)2€·2H2O [20] and (Imidazole)2Zn(OAc)2 [21] are monomers while Cd(OAc)2·2H2O [16] and (Imidazole)2Cd(OAc)2 [2] are 1D CPs aggregated through the acetate moiety.

The molecular structure of 4 is illustrated in Fig. 4 and selected bond distances and bond angles are listed in Table S2 in the Supplementary material. 4 is a 1D CP consisting of three distinct cadmium atoms namely, Cd1, Cd2, and Cd3. A pair of tridentate bridging (μ3-κ1:κ2) acetate moieties simultaneously bridges not only the Cd1/Cd3 pair through two pairs of oxygen atoms, O3, O4 and O9, O10 but also two adjacent Cd1/Cd2 and Cd2/Cd3 pairs of atoms through the oxygen atoms, O4 and O9, respectively. A tridentate bridging acetate moiety simultaneously bridges not only the Cd2/Cd3 pair through the oxygen atoms O7 and O8 but also the Cd1/Cd2 pair through the oxygen atom, O7. Each of the aforementioned pairs of cadmium atoms is also bridged by a tridentate chelating bridging (μ2-κ2:κ1) acetate moiety. One 3,4-lutidine is coordinated to each Cd1 and Cd3 and thus only two lutidines are present in a repeat unit. 3,4-lutidine bound to Cd1 and that bound to Cd3 are significantly twisted by forming an angle of 64.40(6)° between the two Cd-L planes. The Cd–O distances pertaining to the acetate that contains O1, O2, C8, and C9 atoms around Cd1 in 4 differ greatly (2.262(4) and 2.733(6) Å) and this pattern is reminiscent to that observed for the acetate moiety in a 1D CP, [Cd(OC(O)Me)(OC(O)Ph)(H2O)2] (2.279(4) and 2.728(5) Å [22]). The acetate moiety in [Cd(OC(O)Me)(OC(O)Ph)(H2O)2] was suggested to possess a tridentate chelating bridging (μ2-κ2:κ1) coordination mode with the coordination number of the cadmium being seven. However, the acetate containing O1, O2, C8, and C9 atoms in 4 is assigned as monoatomic bridging (μ2-κ2) as one of the δ(113Cd) values for 4 supports 5O,1 N environment for the cadmium atoms ligated by 3,4-lutidine (see later). The cadmium atom which is devoid of lutidine (i.e. Cd2) possesses 7O environment.

The presence of a total of three types of acetate coordination modes in 4 as against two in 1–3 is likely ascribed to the fact that the L in the former CP is more basic than the Ls present in the latter CPs. It has been shown that syn lone pair of the carboxylate moiety in metal carboxylate complexes is more basic than anti lone pair [23] and the presence of more basic 3,4-lutidine in 4 than the Ls in 1–3 is anticipated to increase the basicity of even anti lone pair in the former and thus appears to encourage aggregation through the tridentate bridging acetate coordination mode. Cadmium(II) acetate CPs wherein the two adjacent cadmium atoms are linked exclusively by the acetate moiety or the Lewis base or both the acetate moiety and the Lewis base are known in the literature [2], [3], [4]. However, cadmium(II) acetate CPs aggregated exclusively through the acetate moiety [2], [3] are rarer than those aggregated exclusively through the Lewis base or both the acetate moiety and the Lewis base. Hence, molecular structures of 1–4 add to the rare class of CPs aggregated exclusively through the acetate moiety. One dimensional (1D) CPs are an important class of compounds due to their interesting magnetic, electrical, mechanical, and optical properties [24].

1D CPs 1–3 are linear while the CP 4 is syndiotic and as a representative example, the space fill models of 3 and 4 are illustrated in Fig. 5. CPs 1–4 form a 2D sheet like structures through C–H⋯O, C–H⋯π, and π⋯π interactions as illustrated in Figs. S1–S6 in the Supplementary material. The phase purity of the bulk material of CPs 1–4 was confirmed by powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) of the ground single crystals of each CP. The powder X-ray pattern of all CPs agreed well with the respective simulated patterns generated from single crystal X-ray diffraction data [25] of each CP (Figs. S7–S15, Supplementary material).

The ORTEP representation of 5 with the relevant bond parameters is shown in Fig. 6. The cadmium atom is surrounded by the oxygen atom of monodentate acetate, that of water and nitrogen atom of 3,4-lutidine and their inversion related atoms. Thus, the cadmium atom attains a distorted octahedral environment. The Cd–O(water) 2.368(2) Å distance is greater but Cd–N 2.345(2) Å and Cd–O(acetate) 2.253(2) distances are shorter as compared to the respective distances reported for structurally related [Cd(OAc)2(diethylnicotinamide)2(H2O)2] (Cd–O(water) 2.2988(4) Å, Cd–N 2.3756(3) Å and Cd–O(acetate) 2.2814(4)) [26]. Complex 5 is stabilised through C–H⋯O and O–H⋯O hydrogen bonds in the crystal lattice that leads to the formation of a sheet like structure (Figs. S16–S18, Supplementary material).

ORTEP representations of 6, 7, 8, and 9·2H2O with the relevant bond parameters are shown in Fig. 7, Fig. 8, Fig. 9. Complex 6 is a bimolecular species that consists of two chemically distinct neutral species (units 1 and 2) in an asymmetric unit. The unit 1 consists of [Cd(κ2-OAc)2(NC5H4Me-4)2(H2O)] moiety and the unit 2 consists of [Cd(κ2-OAc)2(H2O)2)] moiety. The unit 1 of 6, 7 and 8 are similar with same coordination environment (5O, 2N) around the cadmium atom. The structure of 9·2H2O is similar to that found in unit 1 of 6, 7, and 8 with the difference being that there are three DMAP per cadmium in the former complex without any coordinated water molecule. The geometry of the cadmium atom in unit 1 of 6 and that in 7, 8, and 9·2H2O is best described as a distorted pentagonal bipyramidal wherein the equatorial positions are occupied by two pairs of oxygen atoms with each pair arising from a κ2 chelating acetate moiety and one oxygen atom from a water molecule (6 (unit 1), 7 and 8) or the nitrogen atom from DMAP (9·2H2O) (Mean deviation of the atoms from the equatorial plane 0.0135 Å (6 (unit 1)), 0.1341 Å (7), 0.1389 Å (8) and 0.0097 Å (9·2H2O)) while the apical positions are occupied by the nitrogen atom of two respective Ls. The bond distances around the cadmium atom in unit 1 of 6 are comparable with the respective bond distances known for the structurally related [Cd(κ2-OAc)2(2-(pyridin-4-yl)benzo[d]thiazole)2(H2O)] [27] except the Cd-O(water) distance which is longer in the former complex (2.331(3) versus 2.278(3) Å). The asymmetry in chelation of the acetates in 7, 8, and 9·2H2O increases in the following order: Δd(Cd–O) = 0.003(4), 0.024(4) Å (8) < 0.044(3), 0.072(3) Å (7) << 0.231(4) Å (9·2H2O). This trend is ascribed to the greater basicity of L in 7 than that in 8 and greater number of more basic DMAP in 9·2H2O. It is to be noted that the coordination mode of the carbamate in [(N,N)Zn(OC(O)NR2)2] (N,N= tetramethylethylenediamine, R = Et [28] and N,N = (1,1,3,3-tetramethylguanidine)2, R = iBu [29]) is chelating in the former complex and monodentate in the latter, primarily due to higher basicity of the guanidine in the latter complex. In the crystal lattice of 6–8, intermolecular C–H···O and O–H···O hydrogen bonds result in the formation of sheets in various planes (Figs. S19–S24, Supplementary material).

Two water molecules are present per cadmium in the crystal lattice of 9·2H2O as solvent of crystallisation and form a cyclic tetramer through an intermolecular O–H···O hydrogen bond with the crystallographically related adjacent water molecules and this tetramer is further connected to four oxygen atoms of four acetates and four hydrogen atoms of four DMAP through O–H···O and C–H···O hydrogen bonds, respectively (Figs. S25 and S26, Supplementary material). An analogous water tetramer formed through O–H···O hydrogen bond was also observed in the crystal lattice of [Cd(OAc)2(2-aminobenzimidazole)2]·3H2O [30].

The cadmium atom in unit 2 of 6 is surrounded by two κ2 chelating acetate moieties and two water molecules and revealed a distorted trigonal prismatic geometry. The unit 2 in 6 is isostructural with [Cd(κ2-OC(O)R)2(H2O)2] (R = CH2iPr [31a] and (CH2)4(CH(CH2)2S2) [31b]). However, the coordination mode of acetate is asymmetrical in unit 2 of 6 (Δd(Cd-O(acetate)) = 0.081(4) and 0.053(4) Å) while it is symmetrical in the latter two complexes (Δd(Cd-O(acetate)) = 0.0(0) and 0.006(4) Å, respectively). Cd(OAc)2·2H2O is a 1D CP wherein the cadmium atom is seven coordinated and is surrounded by one κ2 chelating and one tridentate chelating bridging (μ2-κ2:κ1) acetate moieties and two water molecules [13]. The differences in Cd–O distances of two acetate moieties in unit 2 of 6 are significantly smaller than the corresponding difference of 0.242(4) Å observed for the κ2 chelating acetate moiety in Cd(OAc)2·2H2O. Moreover, Cd–O distances of 2.206(4) and 2.218(4) Å associated with two water molecules in unit 2 of 6 are significantly shorter than the corresponding distances of 2.325(4), and 2.299(3) Å observed in Cd(OAc)2·2H2O. The differences in bond parameters between unit 2 of 6 and Cd(OAc)2·2H2O reflect the type of constraints imposed on these units by acetate coordination mode, coordination number and geometry of the cadmium, and intermolecular packing forces. To the best of our knowledge, 6 represents the first crystallographically characterised bimolecular cadmium(II) complex containing two different neutral cadmium(II) coordination species per formula unit to be reported in the literature.

3.3. 13C{1H} and 113Cd NMR Spectroscopy

The CPs/complexes prepared in the present investigation revealed simpler 1H and 13C{1H} NMR patterns in solution than one would anticipate based on their solid-state structures. This anomalous behaviour is ascribed to sample dissociation [13] and proclivity of the acetate moiety to undergo a low energy ‘carboxylate shift’ process [32] in solution. The solid state Cross Polarisation Magic Angle Spinning (CP-MAS) 13C{1H} NMR spectroscopy is an important tool to identify the chemically and sometimes even crystallographically distinct carboxylate moieties in metal carboxylate complexes as the sample remains intact during NMR acquisition [13], [15](b), [33], [34], [35]. Moreover, one can qualitatively assign the coordination modes of the carboxylate moiety from the chemical shift range that the carbonyl carbon of a metal carboxylate covers in its 13C{1H} NMR spectrum. The deshielding of δ(13C) of the carbonyl carbon of metal acetate complexes decreases in the order shown below: bidentate chelating > bidentate bridging > monoatomic bridging > monodentate [13], [33](b), [34], [35].

3.3. Thermal studies

CPs/complexes were subjected to TGA/DTA analyses to understand their mode of decomposition. The TGA/DTA thermograms are illustrated in Figs. S61–S67 in the Supplementary material. The mode of decomposition of each complex determined from the weight loss in TGA pattern is outlined in Fig. S68 in the Supplementary material. Decarboxylation is one of the pathways of decomposition of metal carboxylates [43], [44] and all complexes except 4 undergo decarboxylation in at least one step. Decarbonylation is also observed during the thermolysis events of 4, 8 and 9·2H2O. The TGA weight loss pattern of 1–3 is identical with the successive loss of L, CO2 and ethane. Metallic cadmium was the end product in the thermolyses of 1–3 and 5–7. On the other hand, 4 decomposed to cadmium(II) methoxide while 8 and 9·2H2O decomposed to CdO. As a representative example, 9·2H2O was calcined at 500 °C for 4 h under N2 atmosphere to confirm the formation of CdO as the end product in the thermolysis event. The brown coloured end product obtained from calcination experiment was confirmed as CdO through PXRD pattern (Fig. S69, Supplementary material). All the peaks in the pattern can be indexed to a pure cubic phase (space group Fm3m, No. 225) of CdO (JCPDS 65–2908). The morphology and size of the CdO were observed through scanning electron microscopy (SEM), which revealed the presence of pyramidal particles of μm size (Fig. S70, Supplementary material).

3.4. Mechanistic aspects

A plausible mechanism of formation of 1–8 and 9·2H2O is suggested based on a point zero charge (pzc) model proposed by Ramanan and Whittingham for the formation of MOFs and CPs from the metal salt and organic ligands [17]. Cd(OAc)2·2H2O upon dissolution in methanol disassembles leading to the formation of pzc intermediate A as shown in Scheme S1 in the Supplementary material. The pzc species A transforms either to pzc species C via pzc species B or pzc species E via pzc species D depending upon the basic, nucleophilic properties, and substitution pattern of L. CPs, 1–3 are formed through a reassembly process involving pzc intermediates A and E through the formation of A⋯E⋯A⋯E⋯A ensemble as shown in Scheme 2. Analogously, the CP 4 is formed through a reassembly process involving pzc intermediates A and D through the formation of D⋯D⋯A⋯D⋯D ensemble as shown in Scheme 3. The driving force for the formation of the aforementioned ensembles is believed to be steric and electronic complementarity between hydrogen bond donor and acceptor functionalities in the pzc intermediates. Complex 5 may be considered as a desolvated version of E while the bimolecular complex 6 may be considered as an equilibrating mixture of desolvated A, and B. Complexes 7 and 8 represent pzc intermediate B and 9·2H2O represents dihydrated pzc intermediate C.

It is remarkable that the bimolecular complex 6 is the sole product of the reaction of Cd(OAc)2·2H2O with one or two equiv. of 4-picoline and this complex upon reflux in methanol even after two days did not transform into any new product. This observation suggests that complex 6 is stabilised so effectively through intermolecular forces in the solid state, that even forcing condition such as the aforementioned one does not lead to the formation of any new product. The fact that 9·2H2O is the sole product irrespective of employing one, two or three equivalent of DMAP per cadmium suggests that the course the reaction is mainly driven by high nucleophilicity in conjunction with high basicity of DMAP.

4. Conclusions

The reactions of Cd(OAc)2·2H2O with pyridine and substituted pyridines in methanol at ambient temperature allowed us to isolate a series of products with distinct structures/nuclearity. Plausible mechanisms of formation of new CPs and complexes were discussed with the aid of a pzc model. The product formation was found to be independent of the Cd:L ratio in all reactions except in those that involve 3,4-lutidine. The basicity, nucleophilicity and substitution pattern of the Lewis base appear to be critical factors in determining the nature of pzc species and hence structure/nuclearity of the products. The frameworks present in CPs 1–4 and the presence of two distinct units together in the crystal lattice of 6 are unprecedented in the literature. The solid state CP-MAS 13C{1H} NMR and 113Cd NMR techniques were used to identify the coordination modes of the acetate and coordination environment around the cadmium atom, respectively in the products. The outcome of the present investigation is anticipated to be useful to those who work in the field of carboxylate based metal complexes/CPs and metal organic frameworks (MOFs).
