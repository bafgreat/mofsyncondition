Introduction

In the past two decades, the prosperous domain of crystal engineering has paved the way for a sound junction between the target coordination solids and their potential applications, mainly in the aspects of gas adsorption/separation, catalysis, ion-exchange, luminescence and magnetism, etc.1–4 In essence, the structural and functional diversification for such crystalline materials significantly rests with the choice of building units (metal ions and organic ligands) and the synthetic pathways.5–7 Under this direction, a wide range of organic tectons with different functional groups (such as pyridyl,8carboxylate,9 and thioether10) have been designed for assemblies of various coordination systems, from discrete molecules or clusters, to infinite 1D, 2D, and 3D polymeric frameworks.

In this context, aromatic polycarboxyl compounds have been extensively applied to design coordination networks, due to their versatile coordination capability and sensitivity to acidity of the reaction medium.11 Among them, terephthalic acid, as one of the most familiar rod-like building blocks, has been well demonstrated to prepare functional coordination frameworks.12 However, the relevant investigation of R-substituted terephthalic acids (R being coordination-unfavourable groups) with different electronic and/or steric effect, has been rarely known thus far.13,14 On the other hand, we have revealed that the bent dipyridyl tecton 2,5-bis(4-pyridyl)-1,3,4-oxadiazole (4-bpo) can be used for assembling diverse metallosupramolecular materials with unusual structures and properties, mainly arising from its specific backbone geometry as well as the versatile building function for coordination and/or secondary interactions.15

As a continuation of our work, we will describe herein a series of novel CoII, NiII, ZnII, AgI, CdII, CuII, and PbII complexes based on tetrabromoterephthalic acid (H2tbta) and 4-bpo. Most of these complexes were synthesized under similar conditions and their structural diversification, ranging from the mononuclear pattern to 3D coordination network, can be properly considered as a result of metal-directed assembly. In addition, a variation of the reaction pathway also produces different ZnII complexes. Solid-state fluorescent properties of the ZnII, AgI, CdII, and PbII (d10 metals) complexes will also be presented.

Experimental

Materials and general methods

With the exception of the ligand 4-bpo that was prepared according to the literature method,16 all reagents and solvents were commercially available. Elemental analyses were carried out on a CE-440 (Leemanlabs) analyzer. Fourier transform (FT) IR spectra (KBr pellets) were taken on an AVATAR-370 (Nicolet) spectrometer. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns were recorded on a Rigaku D/Max-2500 diffractometer at 40 kV and 100 mA for a Cu-target tube (λ = 1.5406 Å). The calculated PXRD patterns were obtained from the single-crystal diffraction data using the PLATON software. Fluorescence spectra of the solid samples were measured on a Cary Eclipse spectrofluorimeter (Varian) at room temperature.

Single-crystal X-ray crystallography

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction data for 1–8 were collected on a Bruker Apex II CCD diffractometer with Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). There was no evidence of crystal decay during data collection. The semi-empirical absorption correction was applied (SADABS) and the program SAINT was used for integration of the diffraction profiles for each case.17 All structures were solved by direct methods using the SHELXS program of SHELXTL and refined with SHELXL.18 The final refinements were performed by full-matrix least-squares methods with anisotropic thermal parameters for all non-H atoms on F2. In general, C-bound H atoms were placed geometrically and refined as riding, whereas O- and N-bound H atoms were first determined in difference Fourier syntheses and then fixed at the calculated positions. Isotropic displacement parameters of hydrogen were derived from the parent atoms.

For 1, the CH3CN ligand was refined by using standard geometry constraint (DFIX) to improve the refinement stability as well as similar Uij (SIMU) restraint. For 2, standard geometry constraint was also applied for the lattice CH3OH molecule. The lattice water molecule of O6 is disordered over two sites with the occupancy factor of 0.5 for each, whereas the O7 molecule was assigned to half occupancy to obtain the appropriate thermal parameter. Thus, their affiliated H atoms were not located. For 6, standard geometry constraint was applied for the lattice CH3OH molecule, and soft constraint (SADI) was imposed on CuII ion and the around H atoms of water ligand. For 7, the lattice water molecule of O8 was located at the special position with 1/4 occupancy, and the other lattice water moieties (O9, O10, and O11) were also assigned to 1/4 occupancy to achieve the appropriate thermal parameters. All these oxygen atoms were refined with pseudo-isotropic (ISOR) restraint, as the free refinement will lead to unrealistic anisotropic displacement parameters. Their affiliated H atoms were not determined. For 8, the 4-bpo molecule was properly treated by using a disorder model with all atoms adopting two opposite locations, on which multiple constraints (DFIX and FLAT) were imposed to improve the refinement stability and restrain the corresponding atoms on their respective aromatic planes. Results and discussion

Synthesis and general characterization

In general, the complexes were synthesized using diffusion method at ambient conditions, by layering the CH3OH–CH3CN solution of metal salt to the DMF–H2O solution of mixed-ligands. Notably, for the case of ZnII, a distinct crystalline product 7 (in comparison with 3) can be obtained by directly mixing the above-mentioned solutions of Zn(NO3)2·6H2O and 4-bpo/H2tbta. Notably, for the cases of other metal salts, the products are unchanged by using such two different synthetic routes. All complexes were characterized by IR spectra and elemental analysis. For each case, phase purity of the bulk crystalline sample was also confirmed by PXRD technique, that is, the experimental pattern agrees well with the calculated one (see Fig. S1, ESI).† In the IR spectra of 1–7, the broad peaks centered at ca. 3400 cm−1 can be assigned to the O–H characteristic stretching vibration of carboxyl, water, or methanol. For the IR spectra of 2–8, the absence of characteristic absorption band of carboxyl (at. ca. 1700 cm−1) reveals a complete deprotonation of H2tbta, whereas for 1, a strong peak at 1711 cm−1 clearly indicates the presence of carboxylgroup (for Htbta−). In addition, a very strong band observed at 1701 cm−1 in the IR spectrum of 7 corresponds to the lattice DMF molecule.

Crystal structures of complexes 1–8

0D complex

Co(Htbta)2(4-bpo)2(CH3CN)2 (1)

Complex 1 is a neutral mononuclear coordination molecule with centrosymmetry (see Fig. 1a), in which the CoII ion lying on an inversion center of (1/2, 1, 1) is six-coordinated by two carboxylateoxygen donors from a pair of Htbta− anions and four nitrogen atoms from two 4-bpo and two CH3CN ligands. The C20–O4 distance (1.302(6) Å) is significantly longer than that of C20–O3 (1.208(6) Å), indicating the structural feature of carboxylgroup. As expected, such carboxylgroups are hydrogen bonded to the uncoordinated pyridyl rings of 4-bpo (O4–H4⋯N4, see Table S2 in ESI for details),† producing a 2D grid-like layered network with (4,4) topology (see Fig. 1b), in which the adjacent Co⋯Co distance is 23.403(3) Å. Notably, three symmetry-related such 2D layers are entangled in a parallel mode to form a 3-fold interpenetrating array (see Fig. 1c). Furthermore, the twisty character of such layers facilitates the formation of π⋯π stacking interactions between the phenyl groups of Htbta and the central oxadiazole rings of 4-bpo (centroid-to-centroid distance: 3.837(3) Å; dihedral angle: 8.6°). As a result, the interpenetrating array can be regarded as a self-interpenetrating prototype (see Fig. 1d) if the aromatic stacking interactions are also considered. Such self-interpenetrating motifs have a parallel arrangement in the 3D lattice, without other significant secondary interaction.

1D coordination chains

{[Ni(tbta)(4-bpo)2(H2O)2](CH3OH)2(H2O)3}n (2) and {[Zn(tbta)(4-bpo)2(H2O)2](H2O)2}n (3)

Structural analysis of complexes 2 and 3 shows that they have similar 1D coordination chains and 3D extended lattices (but not isomorphous), and their slight structural discrepancy may arise from the different lattice solvents. Thus, only the structure of 2 is described herein. As shown in Fig. 2a, the NiII ion is located at an inversion center of (0, 1/2, 0) and adopts the MN2O4 octahedral sphere, which is provided by paired tbta, 4-bpo, and water ligands. The tbta ligand also lies about an inversion center of (0,1,0). As a result, the adjacent NiII ions are connected by the tbta dianionic bridges to afford a 1D linear chain along the [010] axis with the adjacent Ni⋯Ni distance of 11.3444(5) Å, which is also decorated with the unidentate 4-bpo ligands at both sides to result in a fishbone-like motif (see Fig. 2b). Intrachain O4–H4A⋯O3 bond between the water ligand and adjacent uncoordinated carboxylateoxygen is found. Furthermore, the lattice CH3OH molecules serve as both H-bonding acceptors (O4–H4B⋯O5) and donors (O5–H5A⋯N3) to extend such 1D arrays into a 2D layered supramolecular network (see Fig. 2c), within which the lattice water molecules are included. Further investigation of the crystal packing reveals that the 2D layers take a parallel arrangement with no significant interlayer interaction.

[Ag(tbta)(4-bpo)(H-4-bpo)(H2O)]n (4) and [Cd(tbta)(4-bpo)2(H2O)]n (5)

X-Ray single crystal diffraction analysis reveals that complexes 4 and 5 are isomorphous and thus, only the structure of 4 is discussed herein.19 The AgI ion has a distorted pentagonal-bipyramidal geometry (AgN2O5) composed of one 4-bpo, one H-4-bpo, two tbta, and one water molecule (see Fig. 3a). The two tbta ligands lie on independent inversion centers of (0, 1, 1/2) and (1/2, 1/2, 1), respectively. The adjacent AgI centers are bridged by the bis-chelated tbta ligands to afford a zigzag chain array along [11] with the neighbouring Ag⋯Ag distance of 11.360(2)/11.454(2) Å. The unidentate 4-bpo and H-4-bpo ligands are located at both sides of this 1D array (see Fig. 3b). Interchain O7–H7B⋯O6 hydrogen bonds are observed between the aqua ligands and carboxylategroups, resulting in a 2D supramolecular layer (see Fig. 3c). Furthermore, interlayer O7–H7A⋯N7 bonds occurring between the water ligands and oxadiazole rings of 4-bpo extend such 2D layers into a 3D network (see Fig. 3d). Additionally, rich aromatic stacking interactions involving the oxadiazole, protonated pyridyl, and phenyl groups are found, with the centroid-to-centroid distances and dihedral angles in the range of 3.697(4)–3.825(6) Å and 3.9–9.0°, respectively.

2D coordination layers

{[Cu(tbta)(4-bpo)(H2O)](CH3OH)}n (6)

In the structure of 6, the CuII center takes a distorted square-pyramidal sphere (τ = 0.36),20 which is defined by two carboxylateoxygen atoms of tbta, two pyridylnitrogen donors of 4-bpo, and one water ligand (see Fig. 4a). The CuII ion lies approximately on the least-squares plane of O2–N1–O5A–N4B with the deviation of 0.20 Å and the axial position is occupied by the H2O ligand, which is H-bonded to the neighbouring carboxylategroup (O6–H6A⋯O3). Both the 4-bpo and tbta ligands serve as the bidentate spacers to bridge the CuII centers into a (4,4) coordination layer (see Fig. 4b), in which the Cu⋯Cu distances separated by 4-bpo and tbta are 14.072(1) and 11.196(1) Å, respectively. In addition, each lattice CH3OH acts as both H-bonding acceptor and donor (O6–H6B⋯O7 and O7–H7⋯O4), interlinking the parallel layers to result in a 3D supramolecular framework (see Fig. 4c) with pcu topology.21 Notably, a pair of such 3D nets are intertangled to effectively fill the voids, affording a 2-fold interpenetrating lattice (see Fig. 4d).

{[Zn(tbta)(4-bpo)(H2O)](DMF)(H2O)}n (7)

The ZnII center in 7 is also penta-coordinated by two tbta, two 4-bpo, and one water ligand, which is similar to that of CuII in 6. However, in this case, the coordination polyhedron is properly considered as a distorted trigonal bipyramid with the τ parameter of 0.78,20 in which two carboxylateoxygen atoms occupy the axial positions (see Fig. 5a). And also, the 4-bpo and tbta linkers bridge the ZnII ions to form a 2D (4,4) coordination network (see Fig. 5b), in which the Zn⋯Zn distances separated by 4-bpo and tbta are 13.580(3) and 11.398(4) Å. Notably, a pair of centrosymmetric 2D nets are combined via interlayer O6–H6B⋯O1 bonds to afford a 2D bilayer, and such bilayer motifs show a parallel stacking (see Fig. 5c), being completely different from the crystal structure of 6. Additionally, O6–H6A⋯O7 and O6–H6B⋯O5 bonds are observed within the 2D coordination layer.

3D coordination network

[Pb(tbta)(4-bpo)]n (8)

When post-transition metal ion PbII with an identifiable void for ligand disposition (namely, inert-pair effect22) is used, an interesting 3D polymeric framework 8 is obtained. The PbII center lies on a 2-fold axis and shows a distorted tetrahedral coordination sphere, which is defined by two carboxylateoxygen atoms of tbta, lying on an inversion center of (5/4, 3/4, 1), and two pyridylnitrogen donors of 4-bpo (see Fig. 6a). Both 4-bpo and tbta ligands act as the bidentate spacers to extend the PbII ions into a 3D coordination network with dia topology21 (see Fig. 6b), which shows 1D channels along the b axis with the dimension of ca. 14.1 × 11.6 Å2. Generally, the large void space will facilitate the formation of interpenetrating crystalline lattices,23 and as a consequence, each 3D porous framework in 8 is filled via catenation of two other nets, affording a 3-fold interpenetrating structural prototype (see Fig. 6c).

Diversity of structural assembly of 1–8

For complexes 1–8, obvious structural diversification is observed from the discrete mononuclear motif to infinite 1D, 2D, and 3D coordination frameworks, which should be mainly assigned to the nature of metal coordination as well as the synthetic approach (for ZnII complexes 3 and 7). For further demonstrating the role of each building unit on structural assembly, it will be beneficial to perform a detailed comparison of their structural features (see Table 2). In 1–8, the metal centers show diverse coordination numbers and geometries. Comparing with the rich binding modes and linking functions of tbta in metal–tbta coordination polymers,14d,e the tbta component in 2–8 uniformly acts as the spacer to bridge two metal ions and the Htbta ligand in 1 is unidentate. This can be attributed to the intervention of 4-bpo, which offers additional coordination sites. In all structures, the carboxylate or carboxylgroup is almost perpendicular to the central aromatic plane due to the significant steric effect of Br-substituent.13d As for 4-bpo, it may serve as either a unidentate terminal or a bridging spacer when binding to different metal centers. That is to say, the metal–ligand synergistic effect is essential to modulate the resulting crystalline materials. This may be further illustrated by the ZnII complexes 3 and 7 that are obtained under different synthetic conditions, in which both the 4-bpo ligands and ZnII ions play different roles in structural construction. Notably, although the ligand binding modes in 2/3 and 4/5 are very similar, different 1D linear and zigzag coordination motifs are formed due to the discrepancy of metal spheres. This is also observed for the structures of 6/7 (2D layer) and 8 (3D dia network). In addition, different solvent molecules are found in these complexes although they were synthesized under similar conditions, which is closely related to their dissimilar roles in the resulting crystal structures, i.e. to meet the local requirement of metal coordination (as ligand) or to stabilize the overall crystalline lattice (as supramolecular agent, usually being involved in weak interactions). Another important point is the weak secondary interactions such as H-bonding and aromatic stacking, which are also critical to direct the final supramolecular architectures.

Photoluminescence properties

Notably, the 1,3,4-oxadiazole moiety shows good electron affinity and electron-transmission ability for improvement of luminescent efficiency, which has been widely applied as the essential component of photoluminescent, electroluminescent and non-linear optical materials.24 Thus, the emission spectra of complexes 3–5, 7, and 8 (with d10 metals) were measured in the solid state to explore their potential for hybrid luminescent materials (see Fig. 7). Excitation of the microcrystalline samples leads to the formation of different fluorescent emissions, with the maximum peaks occurring at 362 nm (λex = 320 nm) for 3, 482 nm (λex = 342 nm) for 4, 490 nm (λex = 344 nm) for 5, 358 nm (λex = 320 nm) for 7, and 502 nm (λex = 342 nm) for 8. The photoluminescence property of free 4-bpo ligand was also studied, displaying a comparable emission peak at 417 nm (λex = 370 nm). The very similar profiles of their emission peaks indicate that the photoluminescent mechanism of these coordination complexes may be tentatively attributed to intraligand transitions. Additionally, the obvious blue-shift for 3 and 7 (ZnII complexes) as well as red-shift for 4, 5, and 8 in comparison with that of 4-bpo should result from the incorporation of metal–ligand coordination interactions.25

Conclusion

In this work, we have presented a new family of coordination complexes assembled from the mixed ligands H2tbta and 4-bpo as well as different metal nitrates for CoII, NiII, ZnII, AgI, CdII, CuII, and PbII. The diverse structures of such complexes largely depend on the metal ions with different radii and coordination geometries, which readily interact with the adaptable organic ligands to result in a variety of mononuclear pattern, 1D fishbone-like linear or zigzag chains, 2D grid-like layers, and 3D interpenetrating framework. Furthermore, the extended supramolecular lattices of the lower-dimensional coordination motifs can be well regulated by the secondary interactions such as H-bonding and aromatic stacking. And also, the assembling condition will significantly affect the resulting crystalline products for ZnII. The current results promote us to make a further systematic investigation of the metallosupramolecular solids based on bent dipyridyl tectons and substituted aromatic acids, which may develop new crystalline materials with desired structures and properties.
