Luminescent materials are promising candidates used in light-emitting diodes,1 bio-imaging agents2 and other electronic devices.3 Among various luminescent materials, those with circularly polarized luminescence (CPL) or two-photon excited fluorescence (TPEF) are becoming hotspots. CPL is the differential emission of right- and left-circularly polarized light by chiral luminescent systems, reflecting the excited-state structural information of chiral molecules,4 and has been intensively studied and gained increasing interest in 3D optical imaging,5 information storage and processing,6,7 and molecular switches.8 On the other hand, TPEF is one of the third-order nonlinear optical (NLO) properties, referring to a single molecule that can absorb two photons simultaneously and emit one photon. In the past few years, designing and synthesizing TPEF materials have attracted much attention, not only because of their applications in three-dimensional data storage,9 three-dimensional fluorescence imaging10 and two-photon pumping,11 but also because of their great fundamental research significance. In general, TPEF properties are usually found in donor–π–acceptor (D–π–A) systems.12,13 It is well known that the D–π–A system has large ground-state dipole moments and exhibits fluorescence properties, because donor–π–acceptor molecules contain both electron donor (D) and acceptor (A) groups linked by π-conjugated bridges. From the donor to the acceptor moiety in the D–π–A structures, intramolecular charge transfer (ICT) can cause strong photo absorption and emission properties.14 This is the structural basis of its application in two-photon excited fluorescence. The above two properties have been easily observed in solutions,15,16 but the coexistence of them in a single material in a solid state has not been observed. One of the most possible reasons that the coexistence does not occur may be that many luminophores usually suffer from the aggregation-caused quenching (ACQ) effect, which is induced by the formation of excimers and exciplexes among aromatic aggregates in the condensed phase.17,18 Another reason may be the special requirement of a crystal structure that makes the target product difficult to synthesize.

In recent years, the design and synthesis of new fluorescent coordination polymers (CPs, and metal–organic frameworks) have witnessed significant progresses.19–21 Unlike traditional inorganic or organic materials, luminescent CPs are more easily prepared under mild conditions to feature high crystallinity, and their crystal structures can be determined by a common X-ray diffractometer, thus the relationships between structures and properties are feasibly uncovered.22 In addition, the luminescent properties of CP materials can be pre-designed and tuned by modifying organic linkers with multiple photonic units (MPUs)23 and/or by introducing photo-responsive metal ions/clusters. Therefore, based on the advantages of CP platforms, we tried to use the pre-design method and hoped to facilely combine CPL and TPEF properties in single-crystal materials.

CPL are observed in chiral systems,24 while TPEF properties are usually found in donor–π–acceptor (D–π–A) systems12,13 We noticed that the 6,6′-diacceptor-substituted binaphthyl (BN) specimen was axially chiral and had been widely used as a chiral luminophore to construct CPL-active materials due to its easy modification in solution.8,25 Moreover, BN is a bis-dipole molecule consisting of two D–π–A systems,26,27 which would satisfy the criteria of TPEF. Using ligands containing such components and d10-configuration Cd2+ ions, we may be able to combine the two properties in a single material. We use Cd(ii) ions because they are bigger in size than the Zn2+ ion and can be seven-coordinated, so it is easier to form a loop-like one-dimensional structure (see below). For Zn(ii) ions, the same structure cannot be achieved, and the most likely possibility is that the Zn(ii) ion is four-coordinated under the same circumstances.

Here, we report for the first time that solid homochiral CP materials exhibit CPL and TPEF properties. The optically active linkers (R-L and S-L, Fig. 1, L is R- or S-2,2′-bis(methoxymethoxy)-6,6′-bis(4-pyridyl)-1,1′-binaphthyl) consist of a BN core, two methoxymethoxy (MOMO) groups (donor, D) and two pyridine groups (acceptor, A), showing double D–π–A motifs. We have assembled R/S-L with cadmium(ii) nitrate to construct chiral CPs, namely R-L-Cd and S-L-Cd, respectively, with the general formula [Cd(NO3)2L2]·THF. The resulting compounds (R/S)-L-Cd, in contrast to the low fluorescent activity of L, show extraordinary emission and strong CPL signals with |glum| = 2.8 × 10−3 (glum was defined as 2(IL − IR)/(IL + IR)). In addition, TPEF is also observed for these compounds and is accompanied by second harmonics generation (SHG).

The enantiomerically pure R/S-L were prepared through three-step syntheses from optically active BN precursors with high yield (Fig. S1, ESI†). To synthesize R-L-Cd or S-L-Cd, R-L or S-L (2 equiv.) were mixed with cadmium(ii) nitrate (1 equiv.) in tetrahydrofuran (THF) at ambient temperature. Vapor diffusion of diethyl ether into the solution afforded colorless block crystals of R-L-Cd or S-L-Cd. Single-crystal X-ray structural determinations revealed that (R/S)-L-Cd crystalized in the enantiomorphic polar space group P1 with one complete formula in the asymmetric unit, [Cd(NO3)2L2]·THF, in which a seven-coordinate Cd(ii) ion was coordinated by four pyridyl groups from four L ligands and two NO3− groups with chelating and monodentate coordination modes, respectively (Fig. S7, ESI†). The {Cd(NO3)2} units are bridged into a one-dimensional (1D) structure by the chiral V-shape ligands, and adjacent 1D CPs are stacked into the 3D supramolecular structure through hydrogen bonds and C–H⋯π interactions (Fig. 2 and Fig. S9 and S10, ESI†). The packing structure of (R or S)-L-Cd has opening channels with uniform size of 1.8 nm × 1.2 nm along the c axis, which contain guest THF molecules. Because of the steric hindrance within the BN core, L shows a highly twisted conformation in which the two naphthalene planes are twisted from each other with a torsion angle of 72.8°. In addition, the pyridyl groups also form various angles with the naphthalene planes (Fig. S11, ESI†). Such highly twisted conformation of L can significantly prevent the 1D structure from forming inter-chain π–π interactions and the non-radiation decay of the luminophores; therefore, the fluorescence intensity of L-Cd should be enhanced compared with L.

To analyze the solid circular dichroism (CD) spectra, crystals of R-L-Cd and S-L-Cd were pressed respectively with KBr to form a pellet in the weight ratio of 1 : 400. The CD spectrum of R-L-Cd exhibits a negative Cotton effect at 200–400 nm (Fig. 3a), which affiliates to the π–π* transition band of the BN moiety located at 300–350 nm28 and confirms the homochiral nature of R-L-Cd, whereas S-L-Cd features a positive mirror-image spectrum, corresponding to the enantiomer of R-L-Cd. In contrast to the weak emission of L in the solid state, strong blue luminescence was observed in the (R/S)-L-Cd crystals under UV irradiation (365 nm, Fig. 3b). The quantum yield (φPL) of L was measured to be 4.38% only, while the crystalline powder of L-Cd showed a quantum yield of 10.87% (±0.5%) (Fig. S13 and S14, ESI†). Because of the dual chirality and fluorescent properties, we measured CPL properties of the two compounds to investigate their structural chirality in the excited state. The crystals of R-L-Cd or S-L-Cd were placed respectively between two quartz plates and were excited with 360 nm light, and obvious mirror-image CPL signals of R-L-Cd (negative) and S-L-Cd (positive) were observed at 480 nm (Fig. 3c), which are in accordance with the fluorescent spectra of (R/S)-L-Cd. The results revealed that the complexes well hold the chiral characteristics when the ligands are assembled with metals, showing CPL activity that has a relatively large CPL dissymmetric factor of |glum| = 2.8 × 10−3. To the best of our knowledge, (R/S)-L-Cd is the first solid example of CPs that exhibits CPL properties.

More interestingly, the powder samples of homochiral R-L-Cd and R-L were subjected to the excitation of an 800 nm femtosecond laser, and the two-photon images were obtained, as shown in Fig. 4b–d, respectively. Since the absorption wavelength of homochiral L-Cd is less than 500 nm, the 800 nm excitation is a typical two-photon excited process. As expected, L itself displays low TPEF activity due to the ACQ effect, whereas homochiral L-Cd exhibits a strong blue emission peak at 480 nm under the excitation of 800 nm light. Common with the single-photon excited fluorescence, the TPEF spectrum of L-Cd also exhibits the same emission peak at 480 nm, indicating that TPEF of L-Cd originates from the π*–π transition of L.29 Meanwhile, the bright TPEF signals are stable for at least 30 min after laser irradiation, suggesting a weak photobleaching ability of NIR light on L-Cd. It's remarkable that the second harmonic generation (SHG) signals at 400 nm are also observed in the TPEF spectra, and in which the split peaks can be rationalized by the reabsorption effect.30,31

SHG is a second-order NLO process in which the frequency of emergent light is two times that of the incident light. SHG materials have been widely used in light modulators32 and switchable NLO devices.33 However, SHG can only occur in non-centrosymmetric materials.34R-L-Cd and S-L-Cd crystalize in non-centrosymmetric space group P1 due to chirality, which satisfies the requirement of SHG. Therefore, we measured the SHG properties of enantiopure L-Cd by irradiating 1064 nm laser light on its crystalline sample and using potassium dihydrogen phosphate (KDP) as a reference. The results showed that L-Cd exhibits SHG efficiency of approximately 0.9 times of KDP (χ(2) KDP = 0.39 pm V−1) (Fig. 4a).

In conclusion, we have adopted chiral ligands possessing multiple optical active R/S-BN bis-pyridyl groups to construct a pair of homochiral CPs. Due to the existence of axial chirality and dimeric D–π–A systems, the two CPs for the first-time exhibit CPL (|glum| = 2.8 × 10−3) and TPEF properties with blue light synchronously. Besides, this material also exhibits SHG activity. This combination of three special functions (CPL, TPEF and SHG) in a single material holds great potential for the development of new optical functional materials and devices. This study may provide insights into the rational design of solid-state multifunctional luminescent materials by using chiral linkers with D–π–A systems.
