Chirality is an essential feature of life and spans many research fields over pharmaceutics, chemical industry, and biology, etc.1 During the past decade, chiral porous metal–organic frameworks (CPMOFs) have attracted great interest due to their potential applications in asymmetric heterogeneous catalysis, highly selective separation, gas storage and nonlinear optics.2 The most effective strategy to prepare chiral MOFs is directly using enantiopure ligands as starting materials.3 Recently, chiral influences, such as chiral templates, ionic liquids with chiral anions or catalytic amounts of chiral spectators, were utilized to induce and achieve the controllable symmetry breaking crystallization of chiral MOFs.4,5 Such strategies undoubtedly offer the great advantage to isolate a single enantiomer because one-pot spontaneous crystallization without involving any chiral components usually results in a mixture of racemic twins.6 Spontaneous symmetry breaking, however, still remains as a seldom achieved approach and rarely occurs in most reactions. Previous reports on symmetry breaking have the following two features: (1) they are random, as a batch of reactions between a ligand and a number of M2+ ions usually generate one rare example of chiral compounds; (2) as they are one-pot, it is hard to figure out the symmetry breaking mechanism from intermediate species. Very recently, stepwise assembly as a powerful technique has been used to fabricate new MOFs with high-connectivity metal complexes and extra-large channels.7 This synthetic method may be a good choice to rationally design chiral porous MOFs only using achiral components. To the best of our knowledge, such a process has never been made to succeed. To realize a stepwise synthetic scheme for the construction of targeted CPMOFs, three possible requirements are proposed: (1) the selected ligands should have a synergistic effect on the formation of chiral MOFs and different functions in the coordination directionality; (2) precursors with potential conformational chirality are preferable; and (3) the reaction intermediate should be in a meta-stable state or have specific coordination sites with weak interactions that could be further replaced by a secondary ligand.

Bearing the aforementioned ideas in mind, we have selected a semi-rigid ligand hexa[4-(carboxyphenyl)oxamethyl]-3-oxapentane acid (H6L, Scheme 1) as the building block to construct new structures. H6L features flexible backbones and multi-coordination sites and thus can be considered as an ideal ligand to assemble porous three-dimensional (3D) networks. The ligand (H6L) is also a potential source of conformational chirality and is basal for chiral generation and symmetry breaking. Reactions of H6L and M2+ (M = Cd, Co) in a mixed solvent system, dimethylacetamide (DMA)/H2O, at 85 °C resulted in two new porous compounds 1 (P-1) and 2 (C2/c). The secondary building units (SBUs) in the two compounds are shown in Fig. 1a–c. It is worth noticing that 1 contains two distinct SBUs, one with two DMA ligands and one with four aqua ligands, 2 contains one type of SBU with two terminal DMA ligands. The terminal M2+ ions are coordinated by solvent molecules or aqua ligands. In principle, these weak coordinated sites could be substituted by pyridineligands or their corresponding derivatives. Additional reactions of compounds 1 and 2 with a rigid ligand 4,6-tris(4-pyridyl)-1,3,5-triazine (tpt, C3 symmetry) at 100 °C gave two new chiral compounds 3 and 4, respectively. When the solvent was changed to DMF/H2O, compound 5 (C2) was isolated between the reactions of 2 and tpt (Scheme 1). The phase purities of bulk samples 1–5 were confirmed by powder XRD (see ESI†). It is noteworthy here that direct one-pot reactions of H6L, tpt and M2+ salts usually lead to a mixture of achiral intermediates (1 or 2) and chiral products. Attempts were made to synthesize the corresponding compounds with other M2+ ions (including Mn2+, Ni2+, Cu2+ and Zn2+), but these failed. Compounds 3–5 are the only compounds obtained from the above reaction system.

The SBUs among all five compounds are trinuclear clusters, and compounds 1 and 2 are composed of linear ones, whereas 3, 4 and 5 feature triangle shapes (Fig. 1, S1, S3, ESI†). The SBUs in 1 and 2 are further linked by the carboxylic ligands, resulting in a [4, 4] layered net and a binodal pts 3D porous net with a point symbol of {42.84} and vertex symbol of [4.4.87.87.87.87] [4.4.82.82.88.88] (Fig. S2, S3, ESI†).

Compound 3 [Cd3(L)(tpt)(H2O)] crystallizes in chiral space group P1. The asymmetric unit contains three crystallographically independent Cd2+ ions, all of which are octahedrally coordinated (Fig. 1d, S4, ESI†). Polyhedra containing Cd(1) and Cd(2) are edge-sharing and further link Cd(3) via vertex-sharing oxygen atoms to form one-dimensional (1D) chains along the a-axis (Fig. S5, ESI†). These 1D Cd chains are further linked by the carboxylic ligands to form a 3D porous framework (Fig. S5, ESI†). The tptligand serves as a two-coordinated node bridging two Cd atoms. The whole structure can be simplified as a sqc33 net after omitting the 2-coordinated node (Fig. S5b, ESI†). There are two types of 1D channels (15.6 × 9.2 Å2 and 16.6 × 11.6 Å2) along the c-axis. The solvent-accessible volume is approximately 806.9 Å3 per unit cell volume, and the pore volume ratio is calculated to be 36.1% using a PLATON program.7 The measurement of second harmonic generation (SHG) shows that complex 3 exhibits a SHG efficiency ∼2.0 times that of potassium dihydrogen phosphate (KDP).

Compounds 4 [Co3(OH)(L)(tpt)(H2O)·H2NMe2] and 5 [Co3(OH)(L)(tpt)·H2NMe2] crystallize in chiral space groups P21 and C2, respectively. Their structures are only slightly different, and once the aqua ligand (O1W) in 4 is replaced by a carboxylic oxygen atom, the higher symmetry compound 5 is generated (Fig. 1e and f, S6, ESI†). The asymmetric unit in compound 4 contains Co atoms, carboxylic ligands, tpt molecules, hydroxyl anions as well as aqua ligands (Fig. S6, ESI†). Every Co2+ is octahedrally coordinated and shares an OH− anion with the other two cobalt atoms, resulting in a trinuclear cluster, which is further linked by tpt and carboxylate groups to afford a highly porous anionic 3D framework with large channels (16.4 × 15.1 Å2) along the c-axis (Fig. 2b, S7, ESI†). To keep the charge balance and structure stabilities of 4 and 5, protonated dimethylamines fill the channels. Different from that in compound 3, the tptligand is tridentate and links three Co3 clusters forming a 2D honeycomb layer (Fig. S7c, ESI†). The solvent-accessible volume is approximately 4835.8 Å3 per unit cell volume, and the pore volume void ratio is 67.5% calculated by the PLATON program.8 The framework can be simplified as a 3-connected MnD.65 net (Fig. S8, ESI†).

Some crystals were randomly picked from the batch of compounds 3–5 and their structural determinations and Flack absolute structure parameters are listed in Tables S2–S4 (ESI†). Solid-state circular dichroism (CD) spectra for several batches of these compounds have also been displayed (Fig. S10, ESI†). Both the crystal refinements and the Cotton effect strongly indicate that the bulk samples of all three compounds are in enantiomeric excess.

Induction of the rigid tptligand clearly is a key factor to such unusual symmetry breaking. Further structure analyses indicates that the cooperative effect of the dual-ligands contributes a lot to such unusual symmetry breaking. In compounds 1 and 2, a pair of H6Lligands coordinate to metal centers in an end-to-end fashion and an inversion center is formed (Fig. S11, ESI†). Metal centers are linked into two types of trimeric Cd3 SBUs (Fig. 1a and b) in 1, and trimeric Co3 SBUs in 2 (Fig. 1c), all of which are linear with inversion centers. These achiral SBUs contain coordinated small molecules, such as aqua ligands or solvent molecules. As is well known, nitrogen has stronger coordination ability than oxygen to Cd2+ and Co2+. Once the rigid tptligand was introduced into the reaction material, all coordinated solvent molecules and most aqua ligands were replaced by the nitrogenligands as well as some coordination sites of the carboxylate groups were rearranged, resulting into more stable chiral compounds.

As shown in Fig. 3, the H6Lligand is locked in a twisted chiral conformation, and the coordination modes of the carboxylic ligands to the metal centers exhibit mirror-related conformations. The resultant trinuclear metal building unit is non-symmetrical (Fig. 1d–e) or with two-fold rotational axis (Fig. 1f). Neither bidentate nor tridentate tptligands participate in the formation of 3D frameworks, but they direct the metal coordination and strengthen the whole 3D porous frameworks. These results confirm that the tptligands exhibit a remarkable chirality induction effect and control the absolute chirality of the 3D porous framework by direct binding to the frameworks, which is an essential driving force for chiral generation.

Thermogravimetric analysis indicates that the framework of 3 is stable up to 300 °C, whereas 4 and 5 smoothly decompose starting from 100 °C (Fig. S12, ESI†). To confirm and evaluate the porosity of highly stable 3, N2 and H2 isotherms on the guest-free samples were measured. Compound 3 exhibits type I sorption isotherms and takes up 158 cm3 g−1 of N2 at 77 K and 1 atm. The calculated Langmuir and BET surface areas are 692 and 475 m2 g−1, respectively. The H2 isotherm shows that it adsorbs 1.13 wt% at 77 K in 1 atm (Fig. S13, ESI†).

In conclusion, we demonstrate here an unusual structural transformation from achiral MOFs to chiral MOFs induced by an achiral precursor via a two-step process. Structure analyses and experimental procedure confirm the induction effect of the rigid tptligand in this transformation. This work may provide a new strategy to rationally design targeted chiral MOFs and may shed new light on the symmetry breaking mechanism.
