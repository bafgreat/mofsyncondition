Structures and spectroscopic properties of Ni(II) and Mn(II) complexes based on 5-(3′, 5′-dicarboxylphenyl) picolinic acid ligand

Abstract

Two novel complexes including [Ni(Hdcppa)(H2O)4] (1) and {[Mn3(dcppa)2(H2O)6]·2H2O}n (2) have been synthesized and characterized by single crystal X-ray structure analysis and elemental analysis. Results show that 1 is a mononuclear nickel(II) compound with octahedron coordination geometry, while 2 is a stairs-like 2D layer structure consisting of the trinuclear MnII units linked through dcppa3−. Spectroscopic and electrochemical properties of the complexes 1–2 have also been studied in dimethyl sulfoxide solution at room temperature.

1. Introduction

The design and synthesis of coordination polymers (CPs) are of great interest, owing to their intriguing esthetic structures as well as their tremendous potential applications in nonlinear optics, gas storage, luminescence, magnetism and catalysis [1], [2]. Various CPs built up from polynuclear transition metal clusters and multicarboxylate building blocks have been extensively studied [3], [4]. In the past decade, the researches in this field have largely focused on high-symmetry structures ligands to prepare MOFs or coordination polymers with well-regulated network structures [5], [6]. In recent years, ligands with coordination groups distributed in an unsymmetrical direction have been extensively used to self-assemble irregular CPs. Yan's group synthesized ten Co(II) complexes using positional isomeric dipyridyl ligands and the phenyl dicarboxylate, and systematically investigate the influence of the positional isomeric ligands on the structures and magnetic properties of their complexes [7a]. Wang's group studied significant positional isomeric effect on structural assemblies of Zn(II) and Cd(II) coordination polymers based on bromoisophthalic acids and various dipyridyl-type coligands, the results revealed that the diverse coordination networks of complexes can be adjusted by the different dispositions of the -Br substituent of isophthalate building blocks, as well as the metal ions and the dipyridyl coligands [7b]. In these ligands, biphenyl-3,4′,5-tricarboxylic acid(H3BPT) has been also started to engage research and a recognized structural component constructing metal-organic frameworks. For example, a unique independent one-dimensional green luminescent coordination polymer nanotube based on an unsymmetric tricarboxylate linker was synthesized by Hong and coworker [8a]. The first near-infrared luminescent ytterbium metal–organic framework has been acquired successfully by Zhang's group and realized for the highly selective and sensitive sensing of small molecules [8b]. Wang's group reported four compounds with four kinds of different topologies based on H3BPT ligand [8c]. These research works showed that the frameworks based on unsymmetrical linkers may exhibit many novel structures and unique properties. The existence of carboxylate groups in different orientations on the aromatic backbone of some multicarboxylate acids may have significant effects on the assembly of coordination networks [9], [9](a), [9](b), [9](c), [9](d), [9](e), [9](f).

5-3′,5′-dicarboxylphenyl picolinic acid (H3dcppa) is unsymmetrical substituted linkers with three carboxylates and N–O chelator. H3dcppa can be partially or fully deprotonated to generate H2dcppa−, Hdcppa2− or dcppa3− at different pH values. As shown in Scheme 1, the tricarboxylate anions can display more versatile coordination modes than biphenyl-3,4′,5-tricarboxylic acid. Therefore, it can coordinate with metal centers in multicoordinated ways to form a series of coordination polymers with interesting topologies (1D helical chain, 2D layer and 3D MOFs) and properties.

Herein, we report the syntheses and features of the two new structures containing H3dcppa with different metal centers, namely [Ni(Hdcppa)(H2O)4] and {[Mn3 (dcppa)2 (H2O)6]·2H2O}n.

2. Experimental

2.1. Materials and instrumentation

H3dcppa was received from Jinan Camolai Trading Company, China. Other reagents and solvents were of analytical reagent and directly used without further purification. Elemental analyses (C, H and N) were performed on a Perkin–Elmer 240 CHN elemental analyzer. UV–vis spectra were recorded on a Puxi TU-1901 double-beam spectrophotometer (Beijing, China). Luminescence spectrum was recorded on a CARY Eclipse fluorescence spectrophotometer at room temperature. A CHI 660E electrochemical workstation (Shanghai CH Instrument Company, China) was used for all of the electrochemical measurements. A glassy carbon electrode (GCE) of 3-mm diameter was used as working electrode; a platinum wire and a saturated calomel electrode (SCE) were used as counter and reference electrodes, respectively.

2.2. X-ray diffraction study

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction data for the complexes 1–2 were collected on a Bruker Apex CCD area-detector diffractometer (Cu Kα, λ = 1.54184 Å). Absorption corrections were applied by using multiscan program SADABS. The structures were solved with direct methods and refined with a full-matrix least-squares technique with the SHELXTL program package [10], [10](a), [10](b), [10](c), [10](d), [11]. All non-H atoms were refined anisotropically, then hydrogen atoms attached to C atoms were placed geometrically and refined using a riding model approximation, with C–H = 0.93 Å and Uiso(H) = 1.2Ueq(C). H atoms of water molecules were located from the difference Fourier map and placed in the structural model with the O–H distances restraint to O–H distances of 0.80–0.85 Å. Crystallographic data of the complexes are shown in Table 1. Selected bond lengths and bond angles were given in Table S1. The geometrical parameters of the hydrogen bonds are listed in Table S2.

2.3. Syntheses of 1 and 2

The complexes 1–2 were prepared by self-assembly of NiCl2·6H2O, MnCl2·4H2O and H3dcppa in mixture solvent (Scheme 2). As the results of the self-assembly of two metal ions (Ni(II), Mn(II)) with H3dcppa using the same metal-to-ligand ratios in aqueous-DMF solution, two complexes give rise to complexes with mononuclear compound and two-dimensional molecular stairs. These coordination results may relate to crystal and ionic radii (Å, C.N.6) of metal ions, (Mn(II) and Ni(II)).

2.4. Syntheses of [Ni (Hdcppa) (H2O)4] (1)

A mixture of NiCl2·6H2O (0.024 g, 0.1 mmol), H3dcppa (0.029 g, 0.1 mmol) and 12 mL of H2O/DMF (V/V = 6:1) was placed in 20 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel, then stirred at room temperature for 30 min and heated at 130 °C for 72 h. The autoclave was then cooled to room temperature, and blue block crystals of 1 were collected by filtration, washed with distilled water, and dried in air. The final yields were 60% based on M for 1. Elemental analysis For C28H30N2Ni2O20, Calcd (%): C 40.42, H 3.63, N 3.37. Found (%): C 40.32, H 3.53, N 3.33.

2.5. Syntheses of {[Mn3 (dcppa)2 (H2O)6]·2H2O}n (2)

Compound 2 was prepared in the same way as for 1, only using MnCl2·4H2O instead of NiCl2·6H2O. Colorless block crystals of 2 were obtained, yield 65%. Elemental analysis For C28H24MnN2O20, Calcd (%): C 44.05, H 3.17, N 3.67. Found (%): C 44.14, H 3.15, N 3.52.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Description of structures of the compound (1)

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that the title compound 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic system, with P21/c space group (Table 1). The asymmetric unit contains one Hdcppa2−, a Ni(II) ion, and four water molecules. As is shown in Fig. 1, the structure of 1 consists of one crystallographically independent coordinated Ni(II) ion, the Ni1 atom is coordinated by one nitrogen atom and one oxygen atom [N1 & O1] from one Hdcppa2− ligand, and four terminal water molecules [O7, O8, O9, & O10] to present a six-coordination environment [NiNO5], which can be described as slightly distorted octahedral coordination geometry.

The H2dcppa− ligand is partly deprotonated and adopts μ1-O1 coordination mode (Scheme 1b). There are rich and strong O–H···O interactions originating from all the coordination water molecules (Table S2). In the structure of (1), the water molecules form hydrogen bonds to each other, with O–H···O angles in the range 112–173° and O···O distances between 2.5335(5) and 3.264(6) Å (Table S2). Two molecular structural units were connected through O9–H9B···O2iii and O9–H9A···O4iv hydrogen bond, which are further linked to form an infinite 1D chain structure by O10–H10A···O8i and O10–H10B···O7ii hydrogen bond in the [001] direction (Fig. 2 and Table S2). Moreover, the chains are connected by O8–H8A···O2vi and O8–H8B···O4v hydrogen bond along the a and b axes to build a three-dimensional framework (Fig. 3). Additional numerous intermolecular O–H···O hydrogen bonds (Table S2) occur in the structure, and play an important role in stabilizing the structure.

3.2. Description of structures of the compound (2)

Compound 2 is a 2D coordination polymer built up from the trinuclear manganese (II) subunit, which is composed of three Mn ions, two dcppa3− ligands and six water coordination molecules. The fundamental structure unit with the partial atom labeling is shown in Fig. 4. Within the noncentrosymmetric trinuclear unit, there are two crystallography independent Mn(II) sites, Mn(1) and Mn(2), the Mn(1) and Mn(2) coordination geometries are distorted octahedral. Mn(2) are surrounded by one N atom, four O atoms coming from carboxygen groups and one water molecule. Mn(1) is coordinated by six O atoms from four water molecules and two ligands carboxylates. Three carboxylate groups of each H3dcppa anion exhibit three coordination fashions(Scheme 1a): O(3) and O(4) from -COO- employ a bidentate chelation coordination mode and O(1) and O(2) from -COO- employ a syn-anti coordination mode, while O(5) from –COO– adopts a monatomic coordination mode, which results in a linear trinuclear Mn(II) cluster with Mn2 and Mn2a are connected by two dcppa3− ligands, Mn2···Mn2a distance of 9.2892(14) Å. The bond distances around the Mn(II) ions are typical of those found in six-coordinate manganese(II) complexes with O- and N-chelation, which are also comparable with the reported ones [12], [13] (Tables S1 and S2).

The trinuclear subunit is connected by μ1-O1 atom forming one dimensional chain along the a direction (Fig. 5). Each infinite 1D chain is linked via μ1-O5 of the dcppa3− ligands to form the stairs 2D layer structure. It is noteworthy that the π···π stacking interactions are observed between benzene rings and the pyridyl rings from neighboring layers with the centroid-to-centroid distances of 3.681 Å (Fig. 6).

3.3. Electronic spectra

Fig. 7 shows the UV–Vis absorption spectra of the ligand as well as complexes 1 and 2 in dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO). The ligand exhibits a strong absorption peak at 263 nm attributed to π–π* transitions of the ligands and a weak shoulder peak about 282 nm originated from n–π* transitions [14]. Absorption spectrum of complex 1 is basically similar to that of the ligand. It is noteworthy is that the enhancement of peak at 263 nm is higher compared with the enhancement of shoulder peak at 282 nm, probably arising from coordination environment of the ligand in complex 1. But for complex 2, it is observed that the shoulder peak at 282 nm becomes more apparent, which can be assigned to ligand-centered (LC) transitions [15].

3.4. Fluorescence property

The emission spectra of free ligand and complexes 1–2 in DMSO solution at room temperature are depicted in Fig. 8. Upon excitation at 282 nm, the emission spectrum of the ligand exhibits a main peak at 333 nm and a weak peak at 382 nm. These peaks may be assigned to the intra-ligand electronic transition [16]. However, it is noteworthy that complex 2 shows obviously stronger fluorescence compared with the ligand and complex 1, the enhancement for the emission peak of (2) is probably due to the unique coordination of the ligands to the Mn(II) center, which effectively increases the rigidity of the ligand and reduces the loss of energy by radiation-less decay [17].

3.5. Cyclic voltammetry

The electrochemical behaviors of the ligand and complexes 1–2 in DMSO solution are further investigated by cyclic voltammetry. As shown in Fig. 9, the free ligand shows a reduction peak at Epc = − 0.941 V owing to the reduction of the carboxyl group [18]. For Complex 1, the reduction peak is negatively shifted to −1.085 V, implying that the reduction of carboxyl becomes difficult because of the formation of the complex. But for Complex 2, three carboxyl of the ligand are further restricted due to the formation of the complex, as a result, the reduction peak of the carboxyl further negatively shifted to −1.352 V. Moreover, a new couple of redox peaks at Epa = −0.657 V and Epc = −0.945 V was observed, which can be assigned to Mn3+/Mn2+ transformation. Above these phenomena suggest that the formation of the complexes seriously influences the redox process of the ligand [19].

4. Conclusions

A mononuclear compound [Ni(Hdcppa)(H2O)4] and a 2D stairs coordination polymer {[Mn3(dcppa)2(H2O)6]·2H2O}n have been synthesized and characterized by single crystal X-ray diffraction. As the results of the self-assembly of two metal ions (Ni(II), Mn(II)) with H3dcppa using the same preparation condition, the structure of two complexes were different. These coordination results should relate to crystal and ionic radii (Å, C.N.6) of metal ions (Mn(II) and Ni(II)). Spectroscopic and electrochemical properties show that absorption spectra of complex 1 is similar to that of the ligand, but complex 2 displays an apparent different behavior. This is probably attributed to the different coordination environment of the ligand in the two complexes.
