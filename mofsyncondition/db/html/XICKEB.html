Lanthanide–radical linear chain compounds based on 2,4,4,5,5-pentamethylimidazoline-1-oxyl-3-oxide: Structure and magnetic properties

Author links open overlay panel
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ica.2012.12.025Get rights and content

Abstract

Two one-dimensional nitronyl nitroxide–lanthanide complexes [Ln(hfac)3(NITMe)]n (Ln(III) = Y (1), Dy(2), hfac = hexafluoroacetylacetonate; NITMe = 2,4,4,5,5-pentamethylimidazolyl-1-oxyl-3-oxide) have been successfully prepared and characterized. Single crystal X-ray crystallographic analysis reveals that complexes 1 and 2 show similar linear chain structures composed of Ln(hfac)3 units bridged by NITMe radicals through their NO groups. Variable-temperature magnetic susceptibilities of Y complex show that there exists weak antiferromagnetic coupling between the radical ligands through the diamagnetic Y(III) ions. Alternating current (ac) susceptibilities of Dy complex reveal frequency dependent out-of-phase signals, indicating that it behaves as single-chain magnet.

Graphical abstract

Two new one-dimensional Ln–nitronyl nitroxide complexes with Ln = Y, Dy have been successfully synthesized and characterized. Single crystal X-ray crystallographic analysis reveals that two complexes show similar linear chain structures composed of Ln(hfac)3 units bridged by NITMe radicals through their NO groups. AC magnetic susceptibility studies of Dy complex show slow clear frequency-dependent out-of-phase signals, suggesting a SCM behavior.

1. Introduction

In recent decades, design and synthesis of molecular magnetic compounds have attracted significant attention due to both fundamental interest and potential application in high-density information storage [1], [2], [3]. Among the various chemical routes investigated to obtain and improve the properties of those molecular magnetic compounds, a strategy involving metal ions with organic radicals as ligands has proven very successful [4], [5], [6]. As well known, the radical ligands can generate typically stronger intramolecular magnetic exchange coupling, which is beneficial to construct molecular magnetic materials. A variety of radical ligands such as nitronyl nitroxide, verdazyl, thiazyl, thioaminyl and their corresponding metal–radical compounds have been reported [7], [8], [9], [10], [11], [12], [13], [14]. Research so far is focused in particular on the nitronyl nitroxide (NIT) radicals, because they are stable and easy to be functionalized [15], [16]. However NIT radicals are poorly donating ligands, so the use of strongly electron-withdrawing coligands such as hexafluoroacetylacetonate (hfac) is required. The steric demand of the coligands restricts the dimensionality of the resulting metal–radical compounds, therefore most resulting metal–radical compounds are zero- or one-dimensional structures [17], [18], [19], [20], [21], [22]. Some of them recently have received significant attention due to the discovery of the first single-chain magnet [Co(hfac)2(NITPhOMe)](NITPhOMe = 4-methoxyphenyl-4,4,5,5-tetramethyl-imidazoline-1-oxyl-3-oxide) by Gatteschi’s group [23]. To observe SCM behavior, two main requirements are strong uniaxial magnetic anisotropy (Ising-type) and negligible magnetic interactions between the chains. In addition, a substantial intrachain exchange interaction is desirable for raising the blocking temperature. The second requirement is to avoid 3D magnetic order which is believed to suppress the slow magnetic dynamics of SCM system [24], [25]. However, recently SCM dynamics has been observed and theoretically demonstrated in the ferromagnetic and antiferromagnetic ordered phases of some compounds. Ishida co-workers have reported CoII–nitronyl nitroxide radical chain compound with the observation of slow magnetization relaxation in an ordered ferromagnetic [26]. Coulon and co-workers have shown experimentally and theoretically that antiferromagnetic ordering does not hinder the phenomena of slow relaxation of the magnetization in MnIIINiII chain compounds [27], [28].

Lanthanide–nitronyl nitroxide based one-dimensional complexes are good candidates for the construction of SCMs, because the lanthanide ions, especially heavy lanthanide ions such as terbium(III) and dysprosium(III), have a large anisotropy and the coligands such as hexafluoroacetylacetonate around the lanthanide ions can decrease interchain interactions. Recently Gatteschi co-workers have prepared a family of lanthanide-nitronyl nitroxide based one-dimensional complexes [Ln(hfac)3(NITPhOPh)] (Ln(III) = Dy, Tb, Ho, Er, Tm and NITPhOPh = 2,4′-benzoxo-4,4,5,5-tetramethyl-imidazoline-1-oxyl-3-oxide) by using a radical ligand with bulk substitution PhOPh. These compounds exhibit SCM behaviors [29], [30]. Recently our group has prepared this type of one-dimensional lanthanide complex [Tb3(hfac)9(NIT-2thien)3]n (NIT-2thien = 2-(2-thienyl)-4,4,5,5-tetramethyl-imidazoline-1-oxyl-3-oxide), by using a thiophene substituted nitronyl nitroxide radical [31]. This complex shows the concomitant existence of slow magnetic relaxation and three-dimensional magnetic ordering. So far, the reported one-dimensional lanthanide–radical compounds behave as SCMs are still very scarce. In order to further explore the relationship between the magnetic properties and substitution of the radical ligand, we decided to employ radical ligand NITMe (NITMe = 2,4,4,5,5-pentamethyimidazoline-1-oxyl-3-oxide), which has small substitution, to construct new one-dimensional lanthanide–radical compound [32]. Herein we report two new one-dimensional complexes [Ln(hfac)3(NITMe)]n [Ln = Y(1), Dy (2)]. Magnetic studies show that complex 1 exhibits a weak antiferromagnetic coupling. Complex 2 reveals clear frequency-dependent out-of-phase signals, indicating SCM behavior.

2. Experimental

2.1. Materials and physical measurements

All the starting chemicals were of AR grade and used as received. The radical ligand NITMe was prepared according to literature method [33]. Elemental analyses (C, H, N) were carried out with a Perkin–Elmer 240 elemental analyzer. The infrared spectra were recorded from KBr pellets in the range 4000–400 cm−1 with a Bruker Tensor 27 IR spectrometer. The magnetic measurements were performed with an mpmsxl-7 squid magnetometer. Diamagnetic corrections were made with Pascal’s constants for all the constituent atoms.

2.2. Synthesis of [Y(hfac)3(NITMe)]n (1)

A solution of Y(hfac)3·2H2O (0.0797 g 0.1 mmol) in 30 mL dry boiling heptane was heated to reflux for 2 h. Then the solution was cooled to 60 °C, a solution of NITMe (0.0171 g 0.1 mmol) in 3 mL of CH2Cl2 was added. The resulting solution was stirred for 5 min and then cooled it to room temperature. The filtrate was allowed to stand at room temperature for slow evaporation. After two days, the red crystals were collected; yield 50%. Anal. Calc. for C23H18F18N2O8Y (881.27): C, 31.35; H, 2.06; N, 3.18. Found: C, 31.71; H, 1.61; N, 3.34%. Yield 53%. FT-IR (KBr): 1653 (s), 1617 (s) 1559 (s), 1532 (s), 1258 (s) 1206 (s), 1147 (s), 801 (m), 662 (m) cm−1.

2.3. Synthesis of [Dy(hfac)3(NITMe)]n (2)

Complex 2 was synthesized using the same procedure for complex 1 but with Dy(hfac)3·2H2O instead of Y(hfac)3·2H2O. Anal. Calc. for C23H18F18N2O8Dy (954.87): C, 28.93; H, 1.90; N, 2.93. Found C, 29.51; H, 1.51; N, 2.81%. Yield 48%. FT-IR (KBr): 1653 (s), 1617 (s) 1559 (s), 1532 (s), 1258 (s) 1206 (s), 1147 (s), 801 (m), 662 (m) cm−1.

2.4. X-ray crystal structure determinations

X-ray single-crystal diffraction data for complexes 1 and 2 were collected using a Rigaku Saturn CCD diffractometer at 113(2) K with Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). The structure was solved by direct methods by using the program shelxs-97 [34] and refined by full-matrix least-squares methods on F2 with the use of the shelxl-97 [34] program package. Anisotropic thermal parameters were assigned to all non-hydrogen atoms. The hydrogen atoms were set in calculated positions and refined as riding atoms with a common fixed isotropic thermal parameter. Disordered carbon atoms were observed in the hfac ligands for both compounds and disorders were also observed for some fluorine atoms. The pertinent crystallographic data and structure refinement parameters for complexes 1 and 2 were listed in Table 1, Table 2.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Structural descriptions

Single crystal X-ray crystallographic analysis reveals that complex 1 and 2 are isomorphous. Therefore, only the structure of complex 2 will be described in detail. Complex 2 crystallizes in the monoclinic P21/n space group and the oxygen atoms of the radicals bind to adjacent Dy atoms thus bridging the Dy(hfac)3 units yielding a one-dimensional polymer (Fig. 1).

Each Dy atom is eight-coordinated with three didentate hfac ligands and the coordination sphere of the Dy atom is completed by two oxygen atoms from two nitroxide groups. The Dy–O(radical) bond lengths are 2.353(4) and 2.355(4) Å, which are comparable to those of the reported Ln(hfac)3 with nitronyl nitroxides [4], [15], [18]. The angle formed by the two bridging nitronyl nitroxides and Dy is 135.22(14)° for O(7)–Dy–O(8). The intrachain Dy–Dy distance is 8.1156(25) Å. Packing arrangement of complex 2 is shown in Fig. 2. The nearest interchain Dy–Dy contact is 10.775(3) Å.

3.2. Magnetic properties

Variable-temperature magnetic susceptibilities of complex 1 and 2 were measured from 300 to 2.0 K in an applied field of 2 kOe. The plots of χMT versus T of the two compounds are shown in Fig. 3, Fig. 4, respectively. For compound 1 the χMT value is 0.367 cm3 K mol−1, which corresponds well with one uncoupled nitronyl nitroxide radical value (0.375 cm3 K mol−1). Upon cooling, the χMT value decreases slowly until 45 K, then it decreases rapidly to 0.065 cm3 K mol−1 at 2.0 K. According to the crystal structure, the complex 1 can be treated as one-dimensional chain with S = 1/2, thus the magnetic data were analyzed by a theoretical expression deduced from the spin Hamiltonian Eq. The best fitting leads to g = 2.01, J = −4.67 cm−1, with an agreement factor R = 3.87 × 10−3 (R = ∑[(χMT)obs − (χMT)calc]2/∑[(χMT)obs]2) The negative J proves the antiferromagnetic intramolecular interaction. The observed the antiferromagnetic coupling between the neighboring radicals mediated by the Y(III) ion is attributed to a superexchange interaction through the empty metal orbitals (5s or 4d). The found J value is comparable with value found previously for the reported compound involving diamagnetic Y(III) with its nitroxide radical ligands [35].

For complex 2, at 300 K, the χMT value is 14.67 cm3 K mol−1. This value is close to the expected value of 14.45 cm3 K mol−1 for an uncoupled system for one Ln(III) ion (Dy(III): 6H15/2, S = 5/2, L = 5, g = 4/3, C = 14.17 cm3 K mol−1) plus one radical (S = 1/2, 0.375 cm3 K mol−1). As the temperature is lowered, the χMT value remains almost unchanged down to 70 K. Below this temperature, the χMT product decreases markedly and reaches a value of 8.39 cm3 K mol−1 at 2.0 K. As well known, there is the presence of both nearest-neighbor (NN) metal–radical magnetic coupling and next-nearest-neighbor (NNN) metal–metal or radical–radical magnetic interactions in lanthanide–radical chain, and the first one is ferromagnetic and these last ones are antiferromagnetic in nature [29], [30], [35]. The magnetic behavior of compound 2 can be ascribed to the combination of possible magnetic interactions and the depopulation of the Dy ions Stark levels. A strictly theoretical treatment of magnetic properties for this system is still a difficult task due to the large anisotropy of these Ln(III) ions. The field dependence of the magnetization (M) for compound 2 has been determined at 2 K in the range of 0–70 kOe (shown in Fig. S3). The initial increase of magnetization is relatively rapid, which implies the presence of the ferromagnetic interactions, and then increases continuously to 6.58 Nβ at 70 kOe without reaching saturation, indicating the presence of a magnetic anisotropy and/or low-lying excited states in the system.

Alternating current (ac) susceptibility measurements for complex 2 were carried out in low temperature regime under a zero dc field to investigate the dynamics of the magnetization. As shown in Fig. 5, compound 2 exhibits frequency dependent out-of-phase signals, indicating that it behaves as SCM.

Because no peaks in χ″ are observed for complex 2, the energy barrier Δ and τ0 via the conventional Arrhenius plot method cannot be determined. To get an estimation of the energy for the reversal barrier and of the characteristic relaxation time in the absence of such maxima in the χ″ versus T, recently alternatively method was proposed by Bartolom et al. [36]. Assuming that there is only one characteristic relaxation process of the Debye type, one obtains the following relation Eq. (2).(2)ln(x″/x′)=ln(ωτ0)+Δ/kBT

From this expression, and by plotting ln(χ″/χ′) versus 1/T at the different frequencies, one can perform linear regressions to obtain the gradients Δ/kB and intercepts [ln(ωτ0)] and then extract a rough evaluation of the activation energy and the characteristic time. For complex 2, as shown in Fig. 6, the linear correlations were observed in plotting ln(χ″/χ′) versus 1/T at the different frequencies. Best linear fit to the experimental data yielded Δ/kB = 21.2 K and τ0 = 1.22 × 10−8s. The observed τ0 value is consistent with a superparamagnetic-like character of the relaxation dynamics. The obtained Δ value is comparable to those of reported lanthanide–radical chain showing SCM behavior [29], [30], [31]. The dynamic of the magnetization relaxation of 2 suggests classical SCM behavior.

4. Conclusions

In summary, we have successfully obtained two new one-dimensional lanthanide–radical complexes. Complex 1 shows antiferromagnetic behavior, which is important to understand the magnetic exchange interactions of the one-dimensional lanthanide–radical system. The frequency dependence of the ac susceptibility justifies that complex 2 shows the existence of slow magnetic relaxation indicating single-chain magnet behavior and no magnetic ordering is observed. Our results suggest that fine tuning of the magnetic behavior of one-dimensional lanthanide–radical systems can be achieved by choosing the substitution of the nitronyl nitroxide radical.

    A novel DyIII-NaI one-dimensional chain, namely [DyIIINaI(L)(CH3CO2)2]n·2CH3CN (1, H2L = N1,N3‑bis(3‑methoxysalicylidene)diethylenetriamine), was synthesized by treating H2L, di-2-pyridyl ketone (dpk), and NaHCO3 with Dy(CH3CO2)2·6H2O in acetonitrile. Complex 1 was characterized by elemental analyses, IR spectroscopy, single-crystal X-ray diffraction analyses and magnetic properties. Structural analyses revealed that 1 consists of a neutral one-dimensional (1D) chain. In each [DyNa] unit, one DyIII and one NaI ions are bridged by two phenoxido O atoms from one L2− ligand and one carboxyl O atom from one CH3CO2−. Moreover, the [DyNa] units were further connected by two carboxyl O atoms from another CH3CO2−. The magnetic studies revealed that 1 exhibits the field-induced single molecule magnet behavior with an effective energy of 13.21 K. The orientation of the anisotropy axes for the DyIII in 1 was estimated by Magellan program, indicating that the directionality of the anisotropic axes is almost collinear to the Dy1-O8 bond, with an angle of 3.085°.

  • Magnetic relaxation in two 1D Tb-nitronyl nitroxide complexes

    2018, Inorganica Chimica Acta
    Citation Excerpt :

    As well known, the first reported single-chain magnet is Co(II)-nitronyl nitoxide chain [1]. Recently, SCM behavior has been observed in some nitronyl nitroxide-Co(II)/Ln(III) one-dimensional compounds, and some of them show a relatively high energy barrier [20–32]. It’s worth noting that these reported SCMs are based on aromatic group substituted nitronyl nitroxides [16,17,25–32].

    Reaction of the nitronyl nitroxide radical NIT-CH3 and NIT-C3H5 with Tb(hfac)3 affords two one-dimensional lanthanide–nitronyl nitroxide compounds: [TbIII(hfac)3(NIT-CH3)]n (1), and [TbIII(hfac)3(NIT-C3H5)]n (2) (NIT-CH3 = 2,4,4,5,5-pentamethylimidazolyl-1-oxyl-3-oxide; NIT-C3H5 = 2-cyclopropyl-4,4,5,5-tetramethylimidazoline-1-oxyl-3-oxide). Single crystal X-ray crystallographic analysis reveals that these two compounds are one-dimensional chains built up by Tb(hfac)3 units bridged by radicals through their NO groups. Magnetic studies show that both Tb complexes exhibit frequency-dependent out-of-phase signals.

  • Syntheses, structures and magnetic properties of four-spin Mn-Imino nitroxide radical complexes

    2017, Journal of Molecular Structure
    Citation Excerpt :

    In the building of novel magnetic metal–organic framework architectures, the combination of metal ions and nitronyl nitroxides (metal radical approach) has proved to be particularly efficient for making molecular magnetic materials in the last few years [1,2].

    Based on the nitroxide radicals, four-spin complexes [Mn(hfac)2(IMpPhCl)]2·NITpPhCl (1) and [Mn(hfac)2(IMmPhCl)]2·NITmPhCl (2) (IMpPhCl = 2-(4'-chlorophenyl)-4,4,5,5-tetramethylimidazoline-1-oxyl, IMmPhCl = 2-(3'-chlorophenyl)-4,4,5,5-tetramethylimidazoline-1-oxyl, hfac = hexafluoroacetylacetonate) have been synthesized and characterized by single-crystal X-ray diffraction. The X-ray crystal structure analyses show that the structures of the two compounds are similar and the imino nitroxide radical ligand acts as a bridge ligand linking two Mn(II) ions through the oxygen atom of the N–O group to form a four-spin system. Two kinds of nitroxide radicals: nitronyl nitroxide and imino nitroxide radicals coexist in the cyclic Mn(II) complexes. The magnetic studies show that there exists an antiferromagnetic interaction between Mn(II) ions and the imino nitroxide radical ligands, which is explained by spin polarization mechanism. The antiferromagnetic interaction of Mn−Rad in complex 2 (J1 = −9.36 cm−1) is stronger than that in complex 1 (J1 = −9.19 cm−1), which is consistent with crystal structure of complexes (The bond length of the shortest Mn-O in complex 2 (2.1625 Å) is smaller than complex 1 (2.1898 Å)).

  • Three lanthanide–nitronyl nitroxide complexes: Syntheses, crystal structures and magnetic properties

    2017, Polyhedron
    Citation Excerpt :

    Six oxygen atoms from three hfacs with the Gd–O bond lengths in the range of 2.343(3)–2.407(3) Å while two oxygen atoms from two radical molecules with the Gd(1)–O(3) bond length of 2.338(3) Å and Gd(1)–O(5) 2.361(3) Å. Tb complex displays frequency-dependent signals at low temperature suggesting that it behaves as SMM.

  • Two cobalt complexes containing different nitronyl nitroxide radicals: Structure and magnetic properties

    2015, Inorganic Chemistry Communications

    Two Co(II) complexes based on different nitronyl nitroxide radicals were synthesized and characterized structurally and magnetically: [Co(hfac)2(NITPhp-Cl)(phenol)](1) and [(Co(hfac)2(NITPh-(OCH3)3)2)(Co(hfac)2(H2O)2)] (2) [hfac = hexafluoroacetylacetonate, NITPh-p-Cl = 2-(4′-chlorophenyl)-4,4,5,5-tetramethylimida-zoline-1-oxyl-3-oxide, NITPh(OCH3)3 = 2-(3′,4′,5′-trimethoxyphenyl) -4,4,5,5-tetramethylimida-zoline-1-oxyl-3-oxide]. X-ray crystal structure analysis revealed that the Co(II) ions in complexes 1 and 2 are six-coordinated with slightly distorted octahedral geometry. The packing diagram of complex 2 shows a 3D supermolecular structure formed by intermolecular hydrogen bond interactions. Variable temperature magnetic susceptibility measurement studies revealed that complexes 1 and 2 exhibit antiferromagnetic interactions between Co(II) and radicals with J = − 0.1 cm− 1 (1) and J = − 3.43 cm− 1 (2), respectively.
