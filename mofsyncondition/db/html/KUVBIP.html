Metal–organic coordination polymers bearing anilate ligands – derivatives of 2,5-dihydroxy-1,4-benzoquinone – are studied intensively due to their perspective redox and magnetic properties.1–11 Various substituents at positions 3 and 6 in quinone ligands may generate considerable steric strain and may influence electron delocalization in the quinoid ring. Dihydroxy-benzoquinones most commonly used in the crystal engineering of coordination polymers are unsubstituted 2,5-dihydroxy-1,4-benzoquinone,1,9,11–17 chloranilic acid,1,4,6,7,10,14,18–32 bromanilic acid,1,7,8,14,20,28,33,34 fluoranilic acid,23,35 nitranilate benzoquinone2,36 and asymmetric chlorocyananilato ligands.1,20,33,37,38 The deprotonated forms of these types of ligands potentially may be involved in metal complexes in four different redox states.5 The unique optical and magnetic properties afforded by lanthanide ions in combination with the redox-active dioxolene bridging ligands may yield interesting materials for electro-optical, data storage, and sensing applications.15,18,20,33,34,37–39

In the present work, the dioxolene bridging ligand which has been involved in the coordination with lanthanides is 2,5-dihydroxy-3,6-di-tert-butyl-para-quinone (H2pQ).40 The two electron-donating t-Bu groups give rise to a large steric effect in this ligand and change the redox properties of the latter significantly in comparison with its halogen substituted analogues. The tert-butyl-substituted anilic ligand is quite rarely used in coordination chemistry. To date only a few examples of monomeric binuclear compounds of 3d-metals (Ni, Co, Fe) were prepared and investigated.41,42 The iron complex bridged with 2,5-dihydroxy-3,6-di-tert-butyl-1,4-benzoquinone exhibits spin crossover behavior around room temperature41,42 while the cobalt compound demonstrates the phenomenon of redox-induced electron transfer.42 No attempt was made to produce metal–organic frameworks bearing H2pQ before this investigation.

Compounds [(M2(pQ)3)·4DMAA]n (M3+ = La3+ (1), Pr3+ (2), Nd3+ (3); pQ2− – the dianionic form of 2,5-dihydroxy-3,6-di-tert-butyl-para-quinone; DMAA = N,N′-dimethylacetamide) were synthesized by a solvothermal method from lanthanide chloride and H2pQ in N,N′-dimethylacetamide (Scheme 1).

The structures of 1–3 have been examined by single crystal X-ray diffraction. Metal–organic coordination polymers 1–3 have quite similar structures and crystallize in the triclinic P space group. The compounds demonstrate hexagonal honeycomb (6,3)-lattices (Fig. 1), and the topologies of the underlying nets are ≪hcb≫ in standard representation of valence-bonded MOFs.43 The simplest unit of [(M2(pQ)3)·4DMAA]n comprises two M(iii) cations, three pQ2− dianions and four coordinated N,N′-dimethylacetamide ligands (Fig. 2).

As a rule, the coordination number of the metal ion in lanthanide compounds based on anilate type ligands is 9 or 8.33,34,38 Most of the octacoordinated compounds have a square antiprism coordination environment.33,38 Systematic analysis of the coordination geometries around the Ln(iii) centers using SHAPE 2.1 software44,45 (see the ESI†) reveals that the octacoordinated lanthanide centers of complexes 1–3 adopt a geometry that is best described as a triangular dodecahedron (TDD, minimum CShM values of 1.953, 1.849, and 1.821 for 1–3, respectively). We used a user-defined reference polyhedron – one-capped pentagonal bipyramid (ocPB) (Fig. 2, right view, see also the ESI†) additionally. This shape was found to give even closer results (CShM values of 1.612, 1.612, and 1.629 for 1–3, respectively). The structures can be more precisely described as being along the minimal distortion pathway between the ocPB and TDD shapes. Lanthanide atoms are near the midpoint of the interconversion pathway (45.9, 43.8, and 45.0% for 1–3, respectively) between TDD and ocPB. The dianions of 2,5-dihydroxy-3,6-di-tert-butyl-para-quinone exhibit different coordination modes. Two of them consist of two delocalised π-electron systems connected by single C–C bonds. They include almost identical C(8)–C(9) and C(9)–C(10); C(8)–O(3) and C(10)–O(4′); C(15)–C(16) and C(16)–C(17); C(15)–O(5) and C(17)–O(6′) bonds. Such delocalisation of electron density in the quinoid ring is characteristic for the bridging mode of anilate derivatives.46,47 The third ligand demonstrates rather p-quinoid bond alternation with quite different C(1)–C(2) and C(2)–C(3) or C(1)–O(1) and C(3)–O(2′) bond lengths. It is also reflected in the difference of Ln–O distances. The greatest variation is observed for the chelate cycle Nd(1)O(1)C(1)C(3′)O(2) (Nd(1)–O(1) and Nd(1)–O(2) are 2.385(2) and 2.459(2), respectively). The full crystallographic data and details of the structure refinements are summarized in Table S1 (ESI†). Selected bond lengths (Å) for compounds 1–3 are presented in Table S2 (ESI†).

Temperature dependences of the effective magnetic moments (μeff) for complexes 2 and 3 are similar and presented in Fig. 3. The value of μeff at 300 K is 3.40 and 3.46 μB for compounds 2 and 3, respectively, which is slightly less than theoretical values of 3.58 μB and 3.62 μB expected for the corresponding isolated Pr(iii) (3H4 ground state, gJ = 4/5) and Nd(iii) (4I11/2 ground state, gJ = 8/11) ions, respectively. The μeff values show a progressive decrease to the value of 1.03 μB for 2 and 2.08 for 3 at 2 K. The observed behavior indicates that both compounds are essentially paramagnetic. A decrease of μeff with lowering temperature is caused by anisotropy effects and by weak antiferromagnetic interaction between metal paramagnetic ions via dioxolene bridges. This is in good agreement with results recently observed for transition metal14,25,36 or lanthanide34,38,48 ions bridged with anilate-type ligands.

Thermal stability of the obtained compounds was examined by TGA and DTA (Fig. 4). Compounds 1–3 start to decompose above 200 °C and show a steady mass decrease upon heating to 300 °C due to a continuous loss of coordination solvent molecules. The observed mass loss was 14% which corresponds to one DMAA molecule. Decomposition of the framework occurs at 347 °C for 1, 349 °C for 2, and 360 °C for 3.

Electronic absorption spectra of nujol mulls for complexes 1–3 are presented in Fig. 5 and show intense bands at 350–355 nm and broad unresolved absorption bands at λ > 520 nm. They can be assigned to spin- and dipole-allowed intraligand or ligand-to-ligand charge transfers from HOMO to LUMO π-orbitals of dioxolane species. The HOMO–LUMO gap for coordination polymers 1–3 can be tentatively estimated as close to 2.3 eV according to the low-energy absorption band.

An analysis of the porous structure was performed by a carbon dioxide adsorption technique at 195 K. Initially compounds 1–3 were activated in a dynamic vacuum using the standard ‘outgas’ option of the equipment at 453 K for 6 h. The measured adsorption isotherms are shown in Fig. 6. All compounds under investigation demonstrated low porosity and both pore volumes and specific surface areas. The calculated parameters of the porous structure are given in Table S3 (ESI†). Pore size distributions (PSD) in 1 and 3 were calculated using the DFT approach which gives good agreement between measured and calculated isotherms. All isotherms are typical for microporous materials with slit pores which are additionally proven by DFT PSD calculations. We could not find good fit for the two-step isotherm of 2 and therefore did not calculate the PSD. Nevertheless, the CO2 desorption curve of 2 is quite close to the adsorption curve of 3. This is evidence of porous structure similarity for these two compounds.

The redox properties of compounds 1–3 in solid-state have been investigated by voltammetry using carbon paste electrodes. The cyclic voltammograms (CVs) are shown in Fig. S1–S6 (ESI†). The electrochemical data for complexes 1–3 are summarized in Tables 1 and 2.

The electrochemical reduction of 1–3 is characterized by two one-electron peaks, reversible for 1 and 3 and irreversible for 2. Obviously, multistage reduction is associated with electron transfers to dioxolene ligands which form a pQ3− trianion state (Scheme 2). It is necessary to note that the first reduction potentials are slightly shifted to the negative values in comparison with known electrochemically active anionic cadmium 2D (6,3) [Cd2(dhbq)3]2− networks based on unsubstituted anilate ligands.16 Three irreversible low-observable waves appear in the anodic region and correspond to the oxidation of three anilate-ligands to the monoanion state (Scheme 2). The HOMO and LUMO energies (Table S4†) were calculated based on the electrochemical data using previously proposed formulas.49 The HOMO–LUMO gap for 2 and 3 is 2.24 and 2.28 eV, respectively. These values are in good accordance with the data obtained by electronic absorption spectroscopy (2.3 eV).

In summary we have reported the first examples of the coordination polymers of lanthanides based on 2,5-dihydroxy-3,6-di-tert-butyl-para-quinone bridging ligands. These compounds consist of hexagonal honeycomb (6,3)-2D-lattices and are microporous materials. Compounds 1–3 give a unique opportunity to investigate the redox behavior of coordination polymers in solid state. CV experiments demonstrate that compounds 1–3 can be involved in five redox processes due to the activity of dioxolane bridges.
