Divalent copper and zinc complex based on a new 4,4′-bis-[(dicarboxymethyl)aminomethyl]biphenyl and N-donor ligands: Syntheses, structures and photoluminescence

Abstract

Seven new coordination compounds, namely, [Cu2(L)(H2O)4]·4H2O (1), [Cu4(L)2(phen)4]·15H2O (2), [Cu2(L)(Him)4]·4H2O (3), [Cu4(L)2(pbib)2(H2O)4]·8H2O (4), [Zn2(L)(pbib)2]·8H2O (5), [Zn2(L)(biim-4)2]·5H2O (6) and [Zn2(L)(biim-4)]·3H2O (7), where biim-4 = 1,1′-(1,4-butanediyl)bis(imidazole), pbib = 1,4-bis(imidazol-1-ylmethyl)benzene, phen = 1,10-phenanthroline, Him = imidazole and H4L = 4,4′-bis-[(dicarboxymethyl)aminomethyl]biphenyl, have been synthesized by Cu(II) and Zn(II) salts with L anions and N-donor ligands. These compounds were characterized by single-crystal X-ray diffraction analyses and further identified by infrared spectra (IR), elemental analyses, powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) analyses and thermogravimetric analyses (TG). Compounds 1–3 show zero-dimensional (0D) structures. In compounds 1 and 2, the discrete structures are further linked by intermolecular hydrogen-bonding and π–π interactions to form a two-dimensional (2D) supramolecular layer, respectively. Moreover, in compound 3, the discrete structure is further extended by intermolecular hydrogen-bonding interactions to yield a 3D supramolecular architecture. Compound 4 displays a tetranuclear structure, which is further connected by intermolecular hydrogen-bonding interactions to form a 3D supramolecular structure. Compound 5 shows a 3D 5-fold interpenetrating diamond framework. Compound 6 reveals a 2D 63-hcb network. Compound 7 shows a 3D 6-connected net with the (411·64) topology. In addition, the luminescent properties of 5–7 have also been investigated in details.

1. Introduction

In recent years, the construction of coordination compounds based on organic building blocks as well as metals has attracted much attention owing to the novel structure topologies and potential applications in the fields of catalysis, sorption, host–guest chemistry, optical materials, luminescence, and so on [1]. Although much progress has been made on the design and synthesis of fascinating coordination compounds, the precise prediction of the outcome of the crystallization process still remains a long term challenge [2]. In general, the structures of coordination compounds are influenced by several factors in the self-assembly process, such as the solvents, the coordination geometry of the central metal ions, pH value, reaction temperatures, N-donor ligands, organic anions etc. [3], [4], [5]. Among them, the organic ligands play an important role in the structures of coordination compounds due to their multiple coordination modes [6]. Up to now polycarboxylate ligands, such as 1,3,5-benzentriacetic acid and 1,3,5-benzenmethyltriiminodiacetic, have been extensively used to assembly coordination compounds with fascinating structures and functions [7]. Among these reported structures, flexible carboxylate ligands are particularly interesting because of their various coordination modes with metal ions [8]. On the other hand, the flexible N-bridging ligands, such as 4,4′-bis(1,2,4-triazol-4-yl)ethane [9a], N,N′-terephthaloyl-bis(S-aminocarboxylato) [9b], and 1,2-Bis(1,2,4-triazol-4-yl)ethane [9c] have been investigated as auxiliary ligands for the construction of novel structures. Through using these mixed flexible ligands, a variety of frameworks with versatile topologies have been obtained [10]. However, the coordination chemistry of N-heteroaromatic carboxylates has received relatively less attention [11]. Furthermore, the N-heteroaromatic ligand as one of the most important biological ligand has potential applications in the surface recognition of protein [12], [13].

On the basis of the above consideration, we synthesized a new flexible ligand 4,4′-bis-[(dicarboxymethyl)aminomethyl]-biphenyl (H4L). In this ligand, four iminodiacetic groups can show various coordination modes when coordinating to metal cations. Herein, seven new compounds based on the H4L and different N-donor ligands have been synthesized, namely, [Cu2(L)(H2O)4]·4H2O (1), [Cu4(L)2(phen)4]·15H2O (2), [Cu2(L)(Him)4]·4H2O (3), [Cu4(L)2(pbib)2(H2O)4]·8H2O (4), [Zn2(L)(pbib)2]·8H2O (5), [Zn2(L)(biim-4)2]·5H2O (6) and [Zn2(L)(biim-4)]·3H2O (7), where biim-4 = 1,1′-(1,4-butanediyl)bis(imidazole), pbib = 1,4-bis(imidazol-1′-ylmethyl)benzene, and phen = 1,10-phenanthroline. These compounds have been characterized by powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD), infrared spectra (IR) and thermogravimetric analyses (TG). In addition, photoluminescent properties of 5–7 have been investigated in detail Scheme 1.

2. Experimental section

2.1. Materials and methods

The N-donor ligands biim-4 and pbib were synthesized according to the reported procedures [14]. Other reagents were obtained from commercial suppliers and used without further purification.

2.2. Physical measurements

The C, H and N elemental analysis was carried out on a Perkin-Elmer 240C elemental analyzer. The FT-IR spectra were recorded from KBr pellets in the range 4000–400 cm−1 on a Mattson Alpha-Centauri spectrometer. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was performed on a Perkin-Elmer TG-7 analyzer heated from 40 to 600 °C under nitrogen gas. The powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) data of the samples were collected on a Rigaku Dmax 2000 X-ray diffractometer with graphite monochromatized Cu Kα radiation (λ = 0.154 nm) and 2θ ranging from 5 to 50°. The experimental PXRD patterns are in good agreement with the corresponding simulated ones (Fig. S1 in the Supporting Information). The photoluminescent properties of ligands and compounds 5–7 were measured on a FLSP920 Edinburgh Fluorescence Spectrometer at room temperature.

2.3. Synthesis

2.3.1. Synthesis of H4L

The flexible H4L ligand was synthesized according to a reported procedure [14]. A mixture of 4,4′-Bis-chloromethyl-biphenyl (7.53 g, 30 mmol), diethyliminodiacetate (11.35 g, 60 mmol), anhydrous K2CO3 (8.28 g, 60 mmol) and CH3CN (100 ml) were heated at water bath for 12 h and then most of the solvent was removed in water bath. After that, ethanol (100 mL), water (200 mL) and sodium hydroxide (9.6 g, 240 mmol) were added to the isolated products and then the mixture was heated and refluxed for 8 h. The solvent was evaporated by water bath to 20 mL, and then 300 mL water was added. The mixture was adjusted to pH = 1–2 with HCl (1.0 mol L−1), and an off-white solid of H4L formed immediately. The white solid was filtered, washed with water and recrystallized by ethanol. The target H4L ligand was obtained (yield 83%). Anal. Calc. for C22H24N2O8 (Mr = 444.43): C, 59.45; H, 5.44; N, 6.30. 2.4. Crystal structure determination

Crystallographic diffraction data for compounds 1–7 were recorded on an Oxford Diffraction Gemini R Ultra diffractometer with graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at 293 K. Absorption corrections were applied using a multi-scan technique. All the structures were solved by Direct Method of shelxs-97 and refined by full-matrix least-squares techniques using the shelxl-97 program within wingx [15]. All non-hydrogen atoms of 1–7 were refined with anisotropic temperature parameters. The hydrogen atoms attached to carbons were generated geometrically. Some aqua hydrogen atoms were located in a difference Fourier map and refined as riding atoms with d(O–H) = 0.89–0.91 Å and Uiso = 1.5 Ueq(O). The disordered C atoms in compounds 6 (C16, C16′) and 7 (C16, C16′) are split over two sites with a total occupancy of 1. Water molecule O1W in compound 7 is split over two sites (O1W, O1W′) with equal site occupation factors of 0.25. Some aqua hydrogen atoms of compounds 1, 2, 4, 5 and 7 could not positioned with difference Fourier maps. Other hydrogen atoms of water molecules were located from difference Fourier maps and refined with isotropic displacement parameters. The topologies of compounds 5 and 7 were analyzed by using olex program [16]. The detailed crystallographic data and structure refinement parameters for these compounds are summarized in Table 1. Selected bond distances and angles and hydrogen bond parameters for them are listed in Tables S1 and S2 (see the Supporting Information).

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Crystal structures

3.1.1. Structure of [Cu2(L)(H2O)4]·4H2O (1)

Single-crystal X-ray crystallography reveals that the asymmetric unit of 1 consists of one Cu(II) ion, half a L anion, two coordination water molecules and two lattice water molecules. As shown in Fig. 1a, each Cu(II) ion is five-coordinated by two carboxylate oxygen atoms and one nitrogen atom from one L anion (Cu1–O1 = 1.949(3), Cu1–O3 = 1.979(3), and Cu1–N1 = 2.001(3) Å), and two oxygen atoms from two water molecules (Cu1–O1W = 2.403(3) and Cu1–O2 W = 1.988(4) Å). The structural index τ of Cu(II) atom is 0.016, indicating that is in a slightly distorted tetragonal pyramidal geometry (τ = 0 and 1 for perfect tetragonal pyramidal and trigonal bipyramid geometries, respectively) [17]. Each L anion bridges two Cu(II) atoms in a μ2-η0:η1:η1:η0:η0:η1:η1:η0 coordination mode (mode I, Scheme 2). In this way, two Cu(II) ions are linked by one L anion to furnish a discrete structure. There are charge-assisted intermolecular hydrogen-bonding interactions between coordination water molecules and carboxylate groups (O1W–H1A⋯O4#2, O1W–H1B⋯O1#3, O1W–H1B⋯O2#3 and O2W–H2B⋯O2#4), which extend the discrete structure into a 2D supramolecular layer (Fig. 1b) [18].

3.1.2. Structure of [Cu4(L)2(phen)4]·15H2O (2)

As shown in Fig. 2a, both Cu1 and Cu2 atoms are five-coordinated in a distorted tetragonal pyramid coordination geometries (with τ = 0.275 and 0.295 for Cu1 and Cu2, respectively). Each of them is surrounded by two carboxylate oxygen atoms (Cu1–O1 = 2.194(3), Cu1–O3 = 1.947(3), Cu2–O5 = 1.918(4) and Cu2–O7 = 1.981(5) Å) from one L anion, and three nitrogen atoms from one phen ligand and one L ligand (Cu1–N1 = 2.061(3), Cu1–N2 = 1.975(3), Cu1–N3 = 2.035(3), Cu2–N4 = 2.330(4), Cu2–N5 = 2.022(5) and Cu2–N6 = 1.989(5) Å). The L anion in a μ2-η0:η1:η1:η0:η0:η1:η1:η0 coordination mode (mode I, Scheme 2) links two Cu(II) ions to form a discrete structure. The phen ligands are attached to both sides of the discrete structure. In addition, there are four kinds of π–π interactions in 2. The former two are between the phenyl rings from L and the phen ligands (the face-to-face distances of 3.48 and 3.24 Å; the centroid-to-centroid distances of 3.57 and 3.80 Å) [19]. The latter two are between the phenyl rings from phen ligands (the face-to-face distances of 3.48 and 3.43 Å; the centroid-to-centroid distances of 3.72 and 3.64 Å). The former two π–π interactions link the discrete structures into a 1D supramolecular chain. Adjacent parallel chains are further extended into 2D supramolecular sheets by the latter two π–π interactions between phen ligands (Fig. 2b). There are also intermolecular hydrogen-bonding interactions (three hydrogen bonds between water molecules and one stronger charge-assisted hydrogen bonds, O3W–H3A⋯O7W, O6W–H6A⋯O5W#2, O9W–H9A⋯O8W and O3W–H3B⋯O2#1), which further stabilize the 2D supramolecular layers.

3.1.3. Structure of [Cu2(L)(Him)4]·4H2O (3)

When im ligands were introduced into the reaction system of 1, a quite different structure of 3 was obtained. As shown in Fig. 3a, the Cu(II) ion of 3 is five-coordinated (τ = 0.318) by two carboxylate oxygen atoms from one L anion (Cu1–O1 = 1.970(2) and Cu1–O3 = 2.151(3) Å), and three nitrogen atoms from one L anion and two im ligands (Cu1–N2 = 1.968(3), Cu1–N4 = 2.003(2) and Cu1–N5 = 2.071(2) Å) in a distorted tetragonal pyramidal coordination environment. Each L anion bridges two Cu(II) atoms in a μ2-η0:η1:η1:η0:η0:η1:η1:η0 coordination mode (mode I, Scheme 2). In this coordination mode, two Cu(II) ions are linked by one L anion to furnish a discrete structure. Two im ligands are attached to both sides of the discrete structure. Furthermore, the intermolecular hydrogen-bonding interaction (C1–H1⋯O2#6) among carboxylate oxygen atom of L anion and carbon atom of im ligand links the discrete structure into a wavelike chain (Fig. 3b). The chains are extended into 2D supramolecular layers by intermolecular hydrogen-bonding interaction (C5–H5⋯O2#7) between carboxylate oxygen atom of L anion and carbon atom of im ligand (Fig. 3c). The 2D supramolecular layers are further linked by two hydrogen bonds between water molecules (O1W–H1A⋯O2W#2 and O2W–H2B⋯O1W), and three stronger charge-assisted hydrogen bonds, O1W–H1B⋯O4, O2W–H2A⋯O2 and N3–H3A⋯O4 to give a final 3D supramolecular architecture (Fig. 3d).

3.1.4. Structure of [Cu4(L)2(pbib)2(H2O)4]·8H2O (4)

X-ray crystallography reveals that the asymmetric unit of 4 consists four Cu(II) ions,two L anions, two pbib ligands, four coordinated water molecules, and eight lattice water molecules. As shown in Fig. 4a, Cu1 and Cu2 atoms are five-coordinated in distorted tetragonal pyramid coordination geometries (with τ = 0.115 and 0.005 for Cu1 and Cu2, respectively). Each of them is surrounded by three oxygen atoms from one L anion and one coordinated water molecule (Cu1–O5 = 1.952(5), Cu1–O7 = 1.962(5), Cu1–O1W = 2.392(7), Cu2–O2 = 1.936(6), Cu2–O3 = 1.945(5), Cu2–O2W = 2.413(6) Å), and two nitrogen atoms from one pbib ligand and one L ligand (Cu1–N5 = 1.941(6), Cu1–N6 = 2.005(6), Cu2–N1 = 1.983(5) and Cu2–N2 = 1.934(5) Å). The L anion adopts a μ2-η0:η1:η1:η0:η0:η1:η1:η0 coordination mode (mode I, Scheme 2). In this manner, two L anions and two pbib ligands bridge four Cu(II) atoms to form a tetranuclear compound [Cu4(L)2(pbib)2(H2O)4] (Fig. 4b). In addition, one charge-assisted hydrogen bond (O1W–H1B⋯O6#1) links the tetranuclear units into a supramolecular chain (Fig. 4c). The chains are further extended by another charge-assisted hydrogen bond (O2W–H2A⋯O8#2) to give a 3D supramolecular architecture (Fig. 4d).

3.1.5. Structure of [Zn2(L)(pbib)2]·8H2O (5)

When Cu(II) ions were replaced by Zn(II) ions, a quite different structure of 4 was obtained. As shown in Fig. 5a, the asymmetric unit of 5 has two Zn(II) ions, one L anion, two pbib ligands and eight lattice water molecules. Zn1 and Zn2 atoms are both five-coordinated exhibiting distorted tetragonal pyramidal coordination geometries (with τ = 0.349 and 0.300 for Zn1 and Zn2, respectively). Both of them are surrounded by two carboxylate oxygen atoms (Zn1–O6#1 = 2.072(4), Zn1–O8#1 = 2.080(4), Zn2–O1 = 2.066(4) and Zn2–O3 = 2.071(4) Å) from one L anion and three nitrogen atoms from two pbib ligands and one L ligand (Zn1–N2#1 = 2.133(4), Zn1–N6 = 1.993(4), Zn1–N7 = 1.986(4), Zn2–N1 = 2.137(4), Zn2–N3 = 2.004(4) and Zn2–N9 = 1.988(4) Å). The pbib ligands linked the adjacent Zn(II) ions to generate a wavelike chain (Fig. 5b). Neighboring chains are further extended by L anions in μ2-η0:η1:η1:η0:η0:η1:η1:η0 coordination modes (mode I, Scheme 2) to yield a 3D framework (Fig. 5c). From a topological perspective, if both the Zn1 ions and Zn2 ions can be considered as 3-connected nodes, the whole 3D structure can be regarded as a 3-connected framework. Five identical frameworks interpenetrate each other, giving rise to a 5-fold interpenetrating framework with the (102·12) topology (Fig. 5d).

3.1.6. Structure of [Zn2(L)(biim-4)2]·5H2O (6)

The asymmetric unit of 6 contains one Zn(II) ion, half a L anion, two halves biim-4 ligands, two and a half lattice water molecules. Zn1 is five-coordinated (τ = 0.431) by two carboxylate oxygen atoms from one L ligand (Zn1–O2 = 2.067(2) and Zn1–O3 = 2.086(2) Å), and three nitrogen atoms from one L anion and two biim-4 ligands (Zn1–N1 = 2.026(3), Zn1–N3 = 1.986(3) and Zn1–N5 = 2.125(3) Å) (Fig. 6a). As shown in Fig. 6b, adjacent Zn(II) ions are bridged by biim-4 ligands to generate a wavelike chain. The chains are further linked by L anions in μ2-η0:η1:η1:η0:η0:η1:η1:η0 coordination modes (mode I, Scheme 2) to generate a layer (Fig. 6c). From the topological view, if Zn(II) ions are viewed as 3-connected nodes, and the biim-4 ligands and L anions are considered as linkers, the framework of 6 can be described as a 3-connected 63-hcb net (Fig. 6d).

3.1.7. Structure of [Zn2(L)(biim-4)]·3H2O (7)

As illustrated in Fig. 7a, the asymmetric unit of 7 has one Zn(II) cation, half a L anion, half a biim-4 ligand, one and a half lattice water molecules. Zn1 is six-coordinated by four carboxylate oxygen atoms from two L anions (Zn1–O3#1 = 2.094(4), Zn1–O4 = 2.013(4), Zn1–O2#1 = 2.030(4) Å), and two nitrogen atoms from one L anion and one biim-4 ligand (Zn1–N1#1 = 2.155(4) and Zn1–N2 = 1.997(5) Å) in a distorted octahedral coordination geometry. As shown in Fig. 7b, adjacent Zn(II) cations are bridged by L anions in μ4-η1:η1:η0:η1:η0:η1 coordination modes (mode II, Scheme 2) to yield a 1D double-chain. The chains are further linked by the biim-4 ligands to generate a 3D framework (Fig. 7c).

Topologically, if the Zn(II) ions are reduced to 6-connected nodes, and the L anions and biim-4 ligands are considered as linkers, the structure of 7 can be classified as a 3D 6-connected net with (411·64) topology.

3.2. PXRD results and thermal analysis

In order to substantiate the purities of the as-synthesized compounds 1–7, their PXRD experiments were carried out. The experimental PXRD patterns match well with the corresponding computer-simulated ones except for the relative intensity variation as shown in the Supporting Information (Fig. S1).

Thermogravimetric analyses were carried out for compounds 1–7 in order to characterize the compounds more fully in terms of thermal stability. The experiments were performed under N2 atmosphere with a heating rate of 10 °C/min from 25 to 600 °C, as shown in Fig. S2 (see the Supporting Information). TGA data are summarized in Table 3. Compound 1 shows two steps of losing weights. The first step from 36 to 123 °C can be attributed to the removal of four lattice water molecules and four coordinated water molecules (obsd 21.3%, calcd 20.24%). The second step corresponding to the decomposition of the anhydrous compound occurs from 235 to 356 °C. The final residue is assigned to the formation of CuO (obsd 22.1%, calcd 22.5%). For compound 2, the weight loss from 25 to 109 °C can be attributed to the loss of seven and a half free water molecules (obsd 13.9%, calcd 12.7%). The second weight loss from 203 to 417 °C can be attributed to the collapse of the framework. The remaining residue is 15.8%, which can be assigned to the formation of CuO (calcd 15.1%). Compound 3 loses its lattice water in the range of 96–193 °C (obsd 9.3%, calcd 7.9%). In the temperature range of 193–406 °C, the weight loss can be attributed to the decomposition of the organic ligand. The remaining weight is assigned to the formation of CuO (obsd 16.1%, calcd 17.6%). For compound 4, the weight loss between 36 and 108 °C is ascribed to the release of four free water molecules and two coordinated water molecule (obsd 11.46%, calcd 11.81%). After removal of the water molecules, the remaining solid is stable up to 150 °C. Decomposition of the residual composition occurs from 150 to 436 °C, leading to the formation of CuO as the residue (obsd 18.3%, calcd 17.5%). For 5, the first weight loss before 103 °C can be attributed to the removal of eight water molecules (obsd 14.0%, calcd 12.1%). The departure of the organic components occurs from 280 to 496 °C (obsd 14.3%, calcd 13.6%). For 6, the weight loss corresponding to the release of five lattice water molecules is observed from 36 to 151 °C (obsd 9.3%, calcd 8.6%). After removal of the water molecules, the remaining solid is stable up to 263 °C. In the temperature range of 263–506 °C, the weight loss can be attributed to the decomposition of the organic ligand. The remaining weight corresponds to the formation of ZnO (obsd 16.3%, calcd 15.5%). Compound 7 undergoes dehydration in the temperature range from 25 to 75 °C (obsd 7.3%, calcd 6.2%). After removal of the water molecules, the remaining solid is stable up to 263 °C. Decomposition of the organic ligand occurs from 263 to 506 °C, leading to the formation of ZnO as the residue (obsd 19.6%, calcd 18.5%).

3.3. Luminescent properties

Solid-state luminescent properties were examined for ligands H4L, biim-4, pbib, and compounds 5–7 at room temperature (Fig. 3s and Table 2). The main emission peaks of biim-4, and pbib are at 438 (λex = 354 nm) and 423 nm (λex = 416 nm), respectively [20]. The H4L shows an emission peak at 423 nm (λex = 354 nm). The emission peaks of the free ligands are probably attributable to the π* → n or π* → π transitions [21].

The emission peaks of Zn(II)-containing compounds occur at 413 nm (λex = 265 nm) for 5, 422 nm (λex = 352 nm) for 6, and 425 nm (λex = 350 nm) for 7. The emission peaks of compounds of 5–7 are similar to that of the L ligand. Since the Zn(II) with d10 configuration is difficult to oxidize or reduce, the emissions of compounds 5–7 are neither metal-to-ligand charge transfer (MLCT) nor ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT) in nature. So the emission bands of these compounds can be attributed to the intraligand fluorescent emission of L anions [22].

4. Conclusion

Seven new coordination compounds have been synthesized by a new H4L ligand and different N-donor ligand. These compounds display fascinating 0D, 2D and 3D structures. The structural differences of the compounds demonstrate that the H4L ligand is a good candidate for the construction of coordination compounds with aesthetic structures. Furthermore, the hydrogen-bonding interactions and the π–π stacking interactions contributed to the formation of the supramolecular architectures. The photoluminescent emissions of compounds 5–7 imply that they may be good candidates for optical materials.

    There are only three zinc complexes described by Vittal and co-workers that contain thiolate residue as additional ligands and metallic centers bridged by 4,4′-bipyridine [19]. Among complexes synthesized by us so far, we have obtained some dinuclear Cd(II), Zn(II) and Co(II) silanethiolates with 4,4′-bypiridine and [Co2{SSi(OtBu)3}4(μ-C4H4N2)] with pyrazine [6–10]. Our last investigations have concerned further introduction of pyrazine (pz) and new ligand 1,2-bis(2-pyridyl)ethylene (bpe) into the Co(II), Cd(II) and Zn(II) silanethiolates.

    A series of Co(II), Zn(II) and Cd(II) complexes have been synthesized in reaction of respective metal tri-tert-butoxysilanethiolates with pyrazine 1 and 2 and 1,2-bis(2-pyridyl)ethylene 3–5. All compounds have been characterized by single-crystal X-ray structure determination, elemental analysis, FT-IR and thermogravimetry. Obtained complexes are bimetallic with the formula of [M{SSi(OtBu)3}2(μ-L)]2 and metal ions bridged by N,N′-spacer. The penta-coordinated spheres of the metallic atoms are completed by tri-tert-butoxysilanethiolate residue playing the role of O,S-chelating ligand. Luminescent properties of 1, 2, 4 and 5 complexes have also been investigated in solid state.
