Syntheses, structures and luminescence of lanthanide coordination polymers based on 5-(3-pyridylmethoxy)isophthalate ligand

Abstract

Eight new lanthanide coordination polymers, {[LnL(Bdc)0.5(H2O)2]·H2O}n [Ln = Ce(1), Pr(2), Nd(3), Sm(4), Eu(5), Gd(6), Tb(7), Dy(8)] (H2L = 5-(3-pyridylmethoxy)isophthalic acid, H2Bdc = terephthalic acid), have been hydrothermally synthesized and structurally characterized by elemental analyses, IR spectra, PXRD and single crystal X-ray diffraction. Complexes 1–8 display isostructural 3D metal-organic frameworks (MOFs) constructed from 2D [LnL(H2O)2]n networks pillared by Bdc2− spacers. Tetradentate 5-(3-pyridylmethoxy)isophthalate ligand coordinates four Ln(III) ions via two bridging carboxyl groups. The 3D MOFs can be simplified as a (4,5)-connected tcs topological network with the Schläfli notation of {44.62}{44.66}. Thermal stabilities (TG-DSC) of these complexes are determined. Eu(5) and Tb(7) complexes emitting strong red and green luminescences demonstrate that the ligand-to-lanthanide energy transfer is efficient.

1. Introduction

Coordination polymers and metal-organic frameworks have attracted wide attention owing to their intriguing structural and topological motifs as well as distinctive physical properties [1], [2], [3], [4]. Much effort has been focused on the rational design and controlled synthesis of coordination polymers using multidentate ligands such as polycarboxylate and N-heterocyclic ligands [5], [6], [7], [8]. Lanthanide-organic frameworks are of specially interest because of their unique luminescent properties [9], [10], [11]. The 4f orbitals of the lanthanide ions (Ln3+) are gradually filled, from 4f0 (La3+) to 4f14 (Lu3+). These electronic [Xe] 4fn configurations (n = 0–14) generate a variety of electronic energy levels, resulting in the intricate optical properties [12], [13]. Because of the Ln3+ characteristic, Ln3+ complexes are used as functional probes in biological systems and have wide applications in laser, sensor, electro-luminescence display, and light emitting diode [14], [15]. So it is of great interesting to study the luminescence of Ln3+ coordination polymers with aromatic ligands.

In this work, 5-(3-pyridylmethoxy)isophthalic acid (H2L, Scheme 1) selected as multifunctional ligand is based on the following considerations: (1) H2L contains a rigid isophthalic piece and one freely rotating pyridyl arm combined by intervening methoxy group, which is a semirigid multidentate ligand. (2) H2L possesses a pyridyl nitrogen atom and four carboxyl oxygen atoms giving rise to a variety of coordination modes. (3) The collaboration of 5-(3-pyridylmethoxy)isophthalate primary ligand and terephthalate coligand might significantly affect the emission wavelength of lanthanide coordination polymers. Moreover, terephthalate is also an excellent spacer in constructing porous metal–organic frameworks such as MOF-5 [16], [17]. Previously, only one paper concerning 5-(3-pyridylmethoxy)isophthalate complex was documented [18]. Two papers concerning similar 5-(4-pyridylmethoxy)isophthalate complexes were also reported [19], [20]. In the course of our investigation for coordination polymers of N-heterocycles and polycarboxylates [21], [22], [23], [24], a series of isostructural lanthanide coordination polymers based on 5-(3-pyridylmethoxy)isophthalate and terephthalate ligands were successfully synthesized and structurally characterized. These lanthanide complexes exhibit three-dimensional frameworks and emit strong luminescence. Herein, we report their synthesis, crystal structure, thermal stability, and luminescence.

2. Experimental

2.1. Materials and methods

All chemicals were of reagent grade and used as received without further purification. C, H and N microanalyses were measured with a Vario EL-III elemental analyzer. Infrared spectra were recorded with a Nicolet A370 FT-IR spectrometer using KBr pellets in 400–4000 cm−1 region. Powder X-ray diffractions were recorded at a scanning rate of 5° min−1 in the 2θ range of 5–50° on a Rigaku DLMAX-2550 diffractometer. Thermal analyses (TG-DSC) were completed on a Netzsch STA 449C thermal analyzer at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 in air. Luminescent spectra were measured on a Shimadzu RF-5301 spectrophotometer.

2.2. Synthesis of {[LnL(Bdc)0.5(H2O)2]·H2O}n [Ln = Ce(1), Pr(2), Nd(3), Sm(4), Eu(5), Gd(6), Tb(7), Dy(8)]

2.3. X-ray crystallography

The well-shaped single crystals of 1–8 were selected for X-ray diffraction study. Data collections were performed with graphite-monochromatic Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) on a Bruker Smart Apex-II CCD diffractometer at room temperature. Determinations of the crystal system, orientation matrix and cell dimensions were performed according to the established procedures. Lorentz polarization and absorption correction were applied. The structures were solved by direct methods and refined by full-matrix least-squares on F2 with shelxtl program [25]. All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically, and hydrogen atoms were located and included at their calculated position. The crystal data and refinement results are summarized in Table 1. The selected bond distances and angles are listed in Table S1 (Supporting Information).

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Description of crystal structures

All of the complexes crystallize as pure phases, as the results of elemental analyses, IR spectra and PXRD confirm. Since 1−8 are isostructural and 7 shows characteristic Tb3+ luminescence. So only the complex 7 is chosen to discuss the crystal structure in detail. Complex 7 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P1¯, with an independent Tb3+ ion, a L2− ligand, a half of Bdc2− ligand, two aqua ligands and one lattice water molecule in the asymmetric unit. As shown in Fig. 1a, the Tb3+ center is eight-coordinated by eight oxygen atoms from four different L2− ligands, one Bdc2− and two aqua ligands. The Tb–O bond distances are in the range of 2.275(3)–2.508(3) Å, which are similar to those reported for other Tb3+ complexes.

The carboxyl groups of H2L and H2Bdc are completely deprotonated. Both carboxylate groups of L2− anion adopt bidentate bridging coordination fashion. The pyridyl group of L2− ligand is uncoordinated. Each L2− anion acts as a tetradentate ligand to link four Tb3+ ions, forming a 2D [LnL(H2O)2]n network (Fig. 1b). Two carboxylate groups in Bdc2− anion both display a chelating fashion. Each Bdc2− tetradentate ligand links two Tb3+ ions via both chelating carboxyl groups. Thus, the 2D [LnL(H2O)2]n networks are pillared by Bdc2− spacers to generate a 3D metal-organic framework (Fig. 1c). Its space filling model is depicted in Fig. S1 (Supporting Information). Moreover, there are some face-to-face π–π interactions to stabilize the 3D structure. Two adjacent phenyl rings of L2− ligands are parallel to each other with a perpendicular distance of 3.43 Å. From the viewpoint of network topology, the L2− ligands can be treated as a 4-connected node, while the Tb3+ ion can be treated as a 5-connected node. This 3D MOF can be simplified as a (4,5)-connected tcs topological network with the Schläfli notation of {44.62}{44.66}, as indicated by the TOPOS software [26].

3.2. Synthesis and infrared spectra

Complexes 1–8 were prepared by the hydrothermal reactions of Ln(NO3)3 with H2L and H2Bdc in a molar ratio of 2:1:2 at 160 °C. The H2L and H2Bdc were fully deprotonated without basic additive. Four carboxylate groups of L2− and Bdc2− ligands adopt two types of coordination fashions, bidentate bridging and chelating fashions. It is noteworthy that pyridyl nitrogen of L2− ligand is uncoordinated. Generally, Ln3+ ion is “hard” acid and displays a preference to combine hard base with negative charge such as carboxylate O-donor compared with softer base such as neutral pyridyl nitrogen [12]. The most common coordination numbers around Ln3+ centers are 8 and 9 that is largely determined by the ionic radius and the steric requirement of ligands. Using same organic ligands, a series of Ln3+ ions often exhibit two or more types of coordination geometries with different properties due to lanthanide contraction [27], [28]. However, complexes 1–8 are all isostructural and adopt eight-coordinated number.

The infrared spectra of 1–8 (Fig. S2, Supporting Information) exhibit characteristic bands of L2− and Bdc2− ligands. The asymmetric and symmetric stretching vibrations of carboxylate groups appear at about 1540 and 1390 cm−1, respectively. The absence of strong bands near 1700 cm−1 indicates the complete deprotonation of carboxyl groups in H2L and H2Bdc. The C–H stretching vibration and the C–H out-of-plane bending vibration of phenyl groups appear at about 3085 and 750 cm−1. The broad bands near 3450 cm−1 are assigned to the O–H stretching vibrations of coordinated and lattice water molecules.

3.3. PXRD

Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) experiments have been carried out for eight lanthanide complexes to check their phase purity in products. The measured and simulated PXRD patterns of 1–8 are given in Fig. S3 (Supporting Information). The peak positions of the experimental and simulated PXRD patterns are in good agreement with each other, indicating that the crystal structures are truly representative of the bulk crystal products. The differences in the intensity may be owing to the preferred orientation of the crystal samples.

3.4. Thermal analyses

In order to study the thermal stability of lanthanide complexes 1–8, thermal analyses (TG-DSC) were performed for crystalline samples in the temperature range of 20–800 °C (Fig. S4, Supporting Information). Due to complexes 1–8 are isostructural, they display similar thermal stability. Hence, only the thermal behavior of Tb3+ complex 7 is discussed in detail. In the temperature range of 20–192 °C, a weight-loss of 9.6% (calcd 9.6%) is attributed to the release of two aqua ligands and one lattice water molecule with three endothermic peaks occurring at 79, 126 and 164 °C, respectively. The anhydrous framework is stable to 400 °C, and then rapidly decomposed with two strong exothermic peaks at 446 and 472 °C. The weight-loss of 56.73% (calcd 57.75%) in 400–700 °C is attributed to the release of L2− and Bdc2− ligands. Finally, the residue is Tb2O3 (found 33.67%, calcd 32.65%). The observed experimental values of complex 7 and other seven lanthanide complexes are similar, and are in good agreement with the results of elemental analyses and single-crystal X-ray structural analyses.

3.5. Photoluminescence

Generally, the luminescence can be increased if stable Ln3+ complex can be formed with suitable organic ligand that can transfer excitation energy to Ln3+ center (so-called “antenna effect”) [12]. There are two types of energy migration paths. One is ligand-centered absorptions followed by intersystem crossing S1–T1, T1–Ln3+ transfer, and metal-centered emission. Another path is the direct transfer of energy from the excited singlet state S1 to the energy levels of the Ln3+ ions, which is known for Eu3+ and Tb3+ [29], [30].

Because Eu3+ and Tb3+ complexes have particular luminescent nature [12], so we focus on the photoluminescent properties of both complexes. The emission bands of Eu3+ complex 5 excitated at 350 nm and the Tb3+ complex 7 excitated at 321 nm are illustrated in Fig. 2. Excitation of Eu3+ complex at 350 nm reveals five emission bands of the Eu3+ ion, which are attributed to the 5D0–7F0 (579 nm), 5D0–7F1 (592 nm), 5D0–7F2 (616 nm), 5D0–7F3 (645 nm), and 5D0–7F4 (698 nm) transitions [31]. The 5D0–7F1 transition is magnetic-dipolar transition, which is independent of the chemical environment surrounding the optically active center. The 5D0–7F2 transition is electric-dipolar transition, which is strongly affected by the variation of the ligand field strength and allows studying the site symmetry by luminescent spectroscopy [27]. As the 5D0–7F1 is clearly less intense than the 5D0–7F2 transition, it means the absence of inversion symmetry of Eu3+ site in complex 5, which is in agreement with the single-crystal X-ray analysis. The 5D0–7F2 (616 nm) transition is most intense one, which dominates the red emission of Eu3+ complex 5.

Similarly, excitation of the as-synthesized solid of Tb3+ complex 7 at 321 nm reveals five emission bands of the Tb3+ ion, which are attributed to the 5D4–7F6 (490 nm), 5D4–7F5 (545 nm), 5D4–7F4 (588 nm), 5D4–7F3 (622 nm), and 5D4–7F2 (641 nm). The 5D4–7F5 transition is most intense one, which dominates the green emission of Tb3+ complex. In the emission spectra of 5 and 7, the ligands do not appear emission. This means that the energy from ligands to Eu3+ and Tb3+ transition is very effective. The emission spectra of 1, 2, 3, 4, 6 and two organic ligands are added to Supporting Information. Only complex 4 shows Sm3+ luminescence (Fig. S5). Other four complexes display ligand-emission (Fig. S6).

4. Conclusions

In summary, we have successfully synthesized eight lanthanide coordination polymers based on H2L and rigid H2bdc ligand under hydrothermal conditions. Complexes 1–8 are isostructural 3D MOFs and display (4,5)-connected tcs topological network. Tetradentate 5-(3-pyridylmethoxy)isophthalate ligand coordinates four Ln3+ ions via two bridging carboxyl groups. The thermal stabilities of 1–8 have been investigated, which proved that the anhydrous frameworks are stable to 400 °C. Eu3+ and Tb3+ complexes emitting strong red and green luminescences demonstrate that the ligand-to-lanthanide energy transfer is efficient.

  • Structures and luminescent properties of two Tb(III) coordination polymers with aromatic dicarboxylates

    2020, Journal of Molecular Structure

    Two Tb(III) coordination polymers with ligands of two kinds of dicarboxylates were synthesized under hydrothermal conditions. The molecular formulas of the complexes were as follows: {[Tb2(3,5-pdc)(3,4-pdc)2(H2O)4]·2H2O}n (1) and [Tb(1,4-bdc)0.5(3,4-pdc)(H2O)2]n (2) (3,5-H2pdc = 3,5-pyridinedicarboxylic acid; 3,4-H2pdc = 3,4-pyridinedicarboxylic acid; 1,4-H2bdc = 1,4-benzenedicarboxylic acid). The two complexes were characterized by elemental analyses and IR spectra. And the structures of the two complexes were tested by X-ray single crystal diffraction. The crystal structures show 3,4-pdc coordinated with Tb(III) by oxygen atoms on the carboxyl groups. They connected the lanthanide ions to form 1D chain or 2D layer. 3,5-Pdc and 1,4-bdc ligands were both connectors in the complexes. They connected the 1D chain or 2D layer to further form 2D layer or 3D framework. The complexes emitted the green characteristic fluorescence of Tb(III) with the excitations of UV-rays. The absolute quantum yields (Фabs) of luminescence for the two Tb(III) complexes were also tested and the values of Фabs (1) and Фabs (2) were 42% (λex = 297 nm) and 53% (λex = 295 nm), respectively. The testing results of fluorescence lifetime showed the 5D4 excited states for the two complexes all had long lifetime and they were 0.819 and 0.901 ms, respectively.

  • Structural diversity, luminescent and magnetic properties of Tb(III)/Gd(III) metal–organic frameworks constructed by Tris(p-carboxyphenyl)phosphane oxide

    2018, Inorganic Chemistry Communications

    Two lanthanide metal-organic frameworks (LOFs), namely, {[Tb(L3−) (H2O)]·3CH3CN}n (1) and {(Me2NH2+)0.5·[Gd(L3−)(HCOO−)0.5 H2O]·DMF·4EtOH·H2O}n (2) have been constructed by tris(4-carboxyphenyl)phosphane oxide (H3L) under solvothermal conditions. Structural analyses indicate that complex 1 appears a 3D 6-connected {412∙63} net based on the binuclear {Tb2(COO)2} clusters. Complex 2 is also a 3D framework. Furthermore, the photoluminescence of 1 in the solid state and different solvent emulsions were studied, the results indicate that 1 shows characteristic green emission of Tb3+ ion and has better fluorescent recognition for sorts of organic solvent molecules and Fe3+ ion. Finally, the result of the variable-temperature magnetic susceptibilities suggests that complex 2 have antiferromagnetic behavior between Gd(III) ions.

  • Structural elucidation, EPR and photoluminescent property of a Ce(III) two dimensional coordination polymer with 4,4′-bipyridine and 2-sulfoterephthalate

    2017, Journal of Molecular Structure
    Citation Excerpt :

    In recent years, the outline and fabrication of lanthanide-based metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have created much attention due to their interesting architectures, fluorescence and magnetic properties [1–6].

    A new Ce(III) coordination polymer, {[Ce(stp)(bipy)(H2O)]·H2O}n (1) is afforded via the reaction of trivalent cerium nitrate [Ce(NO3)3·6H2O], 2-sulfoterephthalic acid monosodium salt Na(H2stp), and 4,4′-bipyridine (bipy). Based upon the versatile coordination modes of the stp ligand as linker and bipy as connector, the aforementioned compound is arranged in a 2-D network, as evidenced by single-crystal X-ray diffraction technique. In 1, the central Ce atom possesses on a distorted tricapped trigonal-prismatic coordination environment. The EPR measurement reveals that there exhibits a weak antiferromagnetic coupling between paramagnetic Ce3+ ions. The encapsulation of the Ce(III) CP disposes a moderate luminescent activity. Thermal analysis is also carried out to check the water loss of the molecule which is in accordance with the structural evidence.

  • Temperature and solvent controlled syntheses of six hydrous 5-(3-pyridylmethoxy)isophthalate Cd(II) coordination polymers

    2017, Inorganica Chimica Acta

    Six hydrous Cd(II) coordination polymers based on a scorpion-like 5-(3-pyridylmethoxy)isophthalic acid (H2L), namely {[CdL(H2O)]·2H2O}n (1), [Cd4L4(H2O)4]n (2), [CdL(H2O)]n (3), [CdL(H2O)2]n (4), {[CdL(H2O)]·H2O}n (5) and [CdL]n (6), were hydrothermally synthesized and characterized by EA, IR, PXRD, TG-DSC and X-ray structural analysis. Temperature and solvent play crucial roles in the preparation of the coordination polymers. Complexes 1–3 were prepared in a H2O-DMF (6:2) solvent system at slightly different temperature (85, 100 and 120 °C). Complexes 4–6 were prepared in different H2O-DMF solvents (6:3 and 8:2) and H2O-MeCN (3:6) solvent. These complexes possess different coordinated and/or lattice water molecules, and exhibit structural diversity and various topology nets. Complex 1 is a 2D double-layered coordination polymer. Complex 2 features a 3D porous framework with irl topology. Complex 3 displays a 2D double-layered network. Complex 4 is a 3D coordination polymer with sra topology. Complex 5 exhibits a 3D wave-like framework with sra topology. Complex 6 is a 3D coordination polymer with self-catenated hxg-d topology. 5-(3-Pyridylmethoxy)isophthalate adopts three types of coordination modes. These complexes exhibit higher thermal stability and emit strong luminescence.
