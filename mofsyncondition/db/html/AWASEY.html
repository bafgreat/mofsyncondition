Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) are a class of coordination polymers synthesized from metal ions or metal clusters and organic bridging ligands, and are generally characterized by their porous structures.1 The availability of a wide variety of metal and organic units and the ability to modify these building blocks allow for the construction of MOFs with pores whose size, shape, volume, and chemical functions can be tailored for specific applications.2–6

Structural sophistication and functions of MOFs can be further enhanced with the use of mixed metals,7via post-synthesis modification,8 or through building-block replacement9 that involves the change of key construction unit(s) of a parent MOF to afford new MOFs with different component(s) and properties. In this paper, we report a new approach to modify the structure of a class of heterometallic 3d–4f MOFs based on the coordination behaviour of alkali ions with O-based ligands. Specifically, alkali metal ions are inserted into a coordination environment formed by O atoms that are already part of the coordination sphere of two otherwise intimately bridged lanthanide centres. Insertion of the extra metal ion and the resulting new secondary building units (Scheme 1) lead to the production of completely new MOFs with significantly increased free crystallographic volume.

The study starts from the 1D chain complex {[Pr2(Hina)4(NO3)4(H2O)4](NO3)2}n (1), which can be readily prepared (Fig. 1; Fig. S1 and S2, Tables S1 and S2, ESI†). 1 crystallizes in the P space group and the asymmetric unit contains two Pr(iii) atoms with a {O9} coordination environment comprising two bridging carboxylates from two Hina ligands, one chelating NO3− and four terminal aqua ligands. It is worth noting that although the carboxylate group of Hina is deprotonated, the pyridyl is protonated, which prevents the structural extension from 1D to 2D/3D.

The uncoordinated nitrogen atoms of the pyridine groups provide the further extension of the coordination network if the nitrogen affinitive building units (i.e., periodic 0D, 1D, or 2D building units with 3d transition metal centres) were added. In this case, copper(i) iodide which demonstrates strong nitrogen affinity, flexible coordination modes and redox active reactivity was chosen to connect with the ina ligands. To our surprise the resulting products display a variety of porous structures as the solvents vary (Fig. 2).

When we use the mix solvents of formamide, isopropyl alcohol and acetonitrile, a 2D polymer {[Pr3Cu7I7(ina)8(HCOO)(CH3NO)4]·2H2O}n (2) is isolated (Fig. 2a and S2; Fig. S1 and S3, Tables S1 and S2, ESI†). It should be noted that the HCOO− ligand may be from an in situ oxidation reaction of formamide.10 In the structure of 2, the 1D lanthanide-carboxylate [Pr3(CO2)7] chain is twisted into an “S” shape, whereas the [Cu7I7] SBU is interlinked with a wheel shape and extends into 1D [Cu7I7]n chains. The 1D Pr-carboxylate chain and the 1D [Cu7I7]n chain are pillared by ina− ligands to form a 2D reticular network, which further stacks to a 3D porous crystalline solid with ca. 8% effective free crystallographic volume (Vvoid) upon the removal of guest solvent molecules.11

Compared to formamide, reactions in N-methylformamide (MMF) and N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF) result in two similar 3D networks {[Pr(Cu4I4)(ina)3(MMF)]·MMF}n (3) and {[Pr3(Cu4I4)3(ina)9(DMF)4]·DMF}n (4) (Fig. 2b, c and S3; Fig. S1 and S4, Tables S1 and S2, ESI†). This distinctive structural variation is mainly due to the change of the solvents. The formamide in 2 is only a terminal ligand, as well as the MMF and DMF in 3 and 4 are templates which play a significant role in the formation of 3 and 4. Because of this change, the [Pr(COO)]n and [Cu4I4] 1D chains in 3 and 4 are more straight and able to extend into three perpendicular directions along with the ina pillars, and the MOF channels are built around MMF and DMF as the solvent templates. If the terminal solvent molecules are excluded, there are two types of 1D void channels along the crystallographic b-axis (Fig. S4c and f, ESI†). As expected, the cavity shape and size of the two channels are also similar (for 3, ca. 5.8 Å × 8.5 Å and ca. 6.4 Å × 10.5 Å, respectively; for 4, ca. 6.2 Å × 8.2 Å and ca. 5.8 Å × 10.8 Å, respectively). The Vvoid are estimated to be 17% and 10% for 3 and 4, respectively.

The small porosity of 2–4 prompted us to seek ways for enlargement, which generally involves the use of dimension-expanded building blocks.12 For lanthanide-containing MOFs, the success by increasing the length of the organic linkers is inherently limited due to the flexible and generally poorly-defined coordination of the lanthanide ions; there is a high propensity for framework collapse when solvent ligands or guest molecules are removed.13 The other strategy of substituting single lanthanide ions for cluster-type polynuclear building units is also of limited use as such clusters are typically the elusive products of adventitious hydrolysis of the lanthanide ions.14,15

The approach of alkali ion insertion presented herein, different from previously reported strategies for structural transformation of MOFs, was born of a careful analysis of the structure of 4, and more specifically, the coordination environment of the oxophilic Pr(iii) ion. Between two adjacent Pr atoms lie eight O atoms from four different bridging ina carboxylate groups that form a cavity of square prismatic geometry; such an arrangement of O atoms is perfect for the accommodation of an octacoordinate Na+ or K+ ion as in the crystal structures of the biologically significant G-quadruplexes stabilized by such cations.16 It is thus tempting to envision the insertion of an alkali metal ion into the O-pocket in 4 and the production of a new MOF that manifests any accompanying structural changes. Unlike previous strategies, this strategy is not only able to effectively build highly connected SBUs, but also to control the structure of the compound by replacing the alkali ions.

Initial experimental validation of this conjecture was established by the Na+-incorporating MOF 5, {[NaPr(Cu4I4)(ina)4]·3H2O}n, which was obtained by using the same reaction mixture as for the synthesis of 4 but with the addition of NaCl (Fig. 3a; Fig. S5, Tables S3 and S4, ESI†). However, as detailed below, insertion of Na+ is not without a twist; the Na+ ion is hexacoordinate rather than octacoordinate as would be perfectly set up for by the aforementioned O-pocket (Fig. 3a). In hindsight, this result is entirely reasonable as hexacoordinate alkali metal ions are much more prevalent and likely favored than their octacoordinate cognates.17 This “twist” in the coordination of the inserted Na+ necessitates certain adjustments to the original Pr-ina coordination that subsequently lead to significant changes in the overall framework structure.

The Na+ ion is situated in an O6-coordination sphere of distorted octahedral or more accurately, trigonal antiprismatic geometry (Fig. 3a, top); these coordinating O atoms are from six ina ligands, each being chelated toward one Pr(iii) while bridging this Pr(iii) and the Na+ ion. The s/h ratio of the distorted octahedral geometry is about 0.88 which indicates an elongated structure.18 It is noted that these ina ligands are exclusively bridging in the structure of 4 before the Na+ insertion. Incorporation of the Na+ ion leads to a nearly linear arrangement of Pr–Na–Pr with an angle of 175.3° and a Pr⋯Pr separation of ca. 7.20 Å. On each of the Pr(iii) ions there is a fourth and unique ina ligand that only chelates the Pr(iii) ion but is not involved in the coordination of the Na+ ion (Fig. 3a, middle). Each sandwich-like [Pr2NaO6] unit is bridged to eight adjacent 4-connected [Cu4I4] clusters using its ina pyridyl N atoms to generate a (4,8)-connected 3D framework with size-uniform channels (Fig. 3a, bottom; window dimensions of ca. 11.0 Å × 7.0 Å). Interestingly, with the removal of the Na+ ion, the network can be simplified to a two-fold interpenetrated framework, each possessing a rare lonsdaleite (also known as hexagonal diamond) topology (Fig. S6†).19,20 As calculated by PLATON, the Vvoid of 5 at 53% is significantly higher than those of 2–4, which indicates the effectiveness of Na+ insertion in increasing the void volume within the framework.

Adding further support to this experimental design is the production of a K+-containing MOF ({[K0.5Pr1.5(Cu4I4)2(ina)5(H2O)]·x(solvent)}n, 6) that is structurally related to 5 (Fig. 3b; Fig. S5, Table S3, ESI†). Standing out in Fig. 3b is the SBU of [Pr2KO6(H2O)] that can be viewed as a cognate of the [Pr2NaO6] unit present in 5. It is however conspicuously different in its distortion from the much more regular coordination polyhedron in [Pr2NaO6], which is attributable to the size difference between these two ions (ionic radii Na+ 0.95 Å vs. K+ 1.33 pm).21 Corroborating are the bent arrangement of Pr–K–Pr at an angle of 141.9°, the sizably expanded Pr⋯Pr separation of 7.57 Å and a water molecule coordinated to the K+. With respect to the structure of 5, the “irregularity” in the structure of 6 can be easily understood as a compromise necessary to accommodate the larger K+ ion while maintaining a similar coordination mode/environment for both metal ions as observed in the structure of 5. Interestingly, a new 4-connected [Pr2(CO2)4] paddlewheel is formed and linked by ina ligands and two [Cu4I4] cluster units to generate a (4,4,8)-connected 3D network (Fig. 3b, middle). The channels in 6 are much larger with a rectangle-shaped opening of ca. 16.0 Å × 6.1 Å (Fig. 3b, bottom). With the removal of solvent molecules of crystallization, the Vvoid of 6 is estimated to be as high as 61%.

Recognizing the significant influence of the size of the alkali metal ions on the overall MOF structure, we have subsequently carried out the MOF synthesis with the addition of LiCl, RbCl, and CsCl under otherwise identical conditions. With Rb+, the next larger metal ion down the element group, structural determination by crystallographic studies was unsuccessful. The single crystals obtained were sensitive to air and readily lost their crystallinity accompanied by a colour change from pale yellow to blue. This observed instability may be a reflection of the difficulty in accommodating the larger Rb+ ion (ionic radius 1.48 Å)21 while maintaining the same coordination environments of both the lanthanide and the inserted alkali ions as in the cases of 5 and 6.

We submit that if the even larger Cs+ (ionic radius 1.69 Å)21 is to be used, a drastic structural change should occur to produce an MOF of a new structural type. This conjecture was subsequently verified by the isolation of MOF 7 {[Pr4(Cu4I4)(ina)8(NO3)(DMF)(H2O)10](NO3)3·x(solvent)}n with no incorporation of the purposely added Cs+ ion (Fig. 3c; Fig. S5, Table S4, ESI†). Clearly, the Cs+ ion is too large to achieve an analogous arrangement of [Pr2CsO6]. Instead, a new Pr-containing SBU of [Pr4(μ4-NO3)] comprising four Pr atoms connected by a μ4-NO3− group into roughly an isosceles trapezoid (Fig. 3c) is identified. The three different Pr⋯Pr sides are bridged respectively by one (Pr3⋯Pr4; bridging), two (Pr1⋯Pr4 and Pr2⋯Pr3; bridging), and three (Pr1⋯Pr2; one bridging and two bridging-chelating) ina ligands, each of which also uses its pyridyl N to coordinate a 4-connected [Cu4I4] SBU to form a (4,8)-connected 3D network with a channel opening of ca. 9.0 Å × 9.0 Å (Fig. 3c, bottom) and a Vvoid of ca. 59%. As a final note regarding our assessment of the size effect of alkali metal ions on the present heterometallic 3d–4f MOFs, no crystalline product was obtained when the smallest Li+ (ionic radius 0.60 Å)21 was attempted in the same capacity. In light of the outcomes discussed above with the use of Rb+ or Cs+, it is reasonable to assume that the much smaller Li+, as compared with the perfectly fitting Na+ for insertion, is similarly unconducive to the assembly of a new MOF that features Li+ insertion.

In summary, we present here a novel approach to systematically modify the structure of lanthanide-containing MOFs through formal insertion of alkali ions into otherwise perfectly stable parent MOFs. This approach, based on well-established knowledge of the coordination chemistry of alkali metal ions, is distinctly different from the existing strategies in that it relies on localized structural alteration upon insertion of an appropriately sized alkali metal ion to induce the overall structural changes in the resulting MOFs (Fig. 3). We demonstrated the pronounced size effect of the alkali ions with the production of a series of systematically altered MOFs. We note that the O-bridged arrangement of lanthanide ions with which the alkali ion-insertion and the assembly of new MOFs are achieved is prevalent in lanthanide coordination chemistry with a wide range of ligands that include simple carboxylates,22 functionalized carboxylates such as ina in the present work, amino acids,23 and β-diketonates.23a,24 The present approach should be readily extendable to these other ligand systems for the production of a variety of MOFs with novel structures and functions.
