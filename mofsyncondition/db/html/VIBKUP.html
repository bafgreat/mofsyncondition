As a major industrial chemical intermediate, propylene serves as a building block of an array of chemical and plastic products. The fluid catalytic cracking of petroleum hydrocarbons induces the concomitant production of both propane and propylene. Due to their associated similar physical and chemical properties (e.g., similar boiling points and difference in molecular size of only 0.3 Å), the separation of propane from propylene represents one of the highly challenging separations and is classified as having a “second level of separation complexity.”1

The current technology used for separating propylene from propane in industrial applications is the energy-intensive cryogenic distillation.2 Notable energy savings could be achieved by its replacement with an energy-efficient process. Therefore, a prominent axis of research is devoted to deploying alternative solutions based on adsorption, membrane-based processes, and employing porous materials as separating agents.

Conventional adsorbents, such as zeolites or ultra-microporous carbon, display low to moderate separation factors, as exemplified in the case of zeolites and carbon molecular sieves3–5 with contracted pore sizes. Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), a distinctive class of porous solid-state materials, have already demonstrated promise in gas storage and catalysis,6–12 and have been successfully employed in the separation of important commodities.13–20 The size and shape of the pore aperture are key parameters to be considered when targeting gas separations with a high degree of complexity, such as separations of hydrocarbon isomers, and propane/propylene in particular.21–24

The separation of propylene from propane using MOF sorbents has been attempted on different types of platforms to access a thermodynamic or a kinetic-based separation with a relatively low separating factor.25–27 Recently, our group isolated NbOFFIVE-1-Ni, the first MOF molecular sieve with the ability to fully isolate propylene from propane.28 However, its relatively small pore-channel size restrains its uptake performance to 1.4 mmol g−1 at 760 Torr and 298 K.

Accordingly, we sought to develop MOF sorbents for the separation of propylene from propane that combine both a high separating factor and a notable uptake capacity. Our strategy was to target cavity-based MOFs (instead of channel-based MOFs) with a small pore-aperture size. Based on our experience gained in the design and isolation of various highly-connected Rare-Earth-based MOFs (RE-fcu-MOFs,29,30 RE-gea-MOF,31 RE-ftw/kle-MOF,32 RE-pek/aea-MOF,33 RE-alb/shp-MOF34) with unique performances in separating different gas pairs, we decided to target a specific topology, ftw, that we envisioned to have the required structural features for such a challenging separation. Indeed, the employment of fumaric acid gave access to a RE-fum-fcu-MOF with a triangular window, 4.7 Å pore-aperture, which led to the molecular sieving of butane from iso-butane, a separation of first-generation complexity with a difference in size of about 0.5 Å.35 A careful analysis of the structure revealed that the triangle-shaped pore-aperture of the RE-fcu-MOF platform could not be further contracted by simply reducing the ligand length. Accordingly, we elected the ftw-MOF platform offering rectangular pore-apertures, and encompassing the Rare-Earth-based hexanuclear cluster identical to that of an fcu-MOF linked with tetratopic ligands. Previous work using MOFs with ftw topologies has revealed the hydrocarbon separation of butane and propane from methane via an equilibrium-based mechanism using geometrically expanded square tetratopic ligands such as TCPT, TCDPN, and TCDPA.32 Since the small pore-aperture size of RE-fum-fcu-MOF (about 4.7 Å) is sufficient for targeting paraffin/iso-paraffin separations, we selected two contracted tetracarboxylate ligands with slightly different lengths to target the ftw topology, biphenyl-3,3′,5,5′-tetracarboxylic acid (H4BPTC) and azobenzenetetracarboxylic acid (H4ABTC). The objective of this modification strategy was to reduce the pore-aperture size of the original TCPT-based ftw-MOF and to explore gas/vapor separations with different degrees of complexity, such as butane from iso-butane and propane from propylene.

Herein, we report the design and successful synthesis of two new ftw-MOFs, namely ftw-MOF-BPTC (1) and ftw-MOF-ABTC (2), achieved by employing our previously discovered synthetic protocol to isolate the RE hexanuclear molecular building block (MBB). The obtained materials, 1 and 2, were extensively characterized using single crystal X-ray diffraction, low-pressure adsorption, and breakthrough experiments. Our results revealed that the use of BPTC and ABTC ligands gave rise to pore-aperture sizes of 2.96 Å and 5 Å for 1 and 2, respectively. The use of a BPTC ligand (1) resulted in an ftw-MOF with a very narrow pore-aperture size that allowed only the adsorption of H2 and CO2 at 195 K. However, further tuning of the pore size using an ABTC ligand (a slightly larger linker) (2) led to the unveiling of a promising ftw-MOF with a remarkable performance in the kinetic-based separation of propylene from propane. Both 1 and 2 were found to be hydrolytically and thermally stable.

Solvothermal reactions between nitrate salt of yttrium or terbium, (Y(NO3)3·9H2O or Tb(NO3)3·5H2O), and tetracarboxylic linkers (H4BPTC or H4ABTC) in a N,N′-dimethylformamide (DMF)–H2O mixture, in the presence of 2-FBA (2-fluorobenzoic acid), yielded transparent, homogeneous cubic crystals, characterized by single-crystal X-ray diffraction (SCXRD) studies as |(CH3)2NH2|2[Y6(μ3-OH)8(BPTC)3(H2O)]·5.5(H2O)·(DMF) (1) and |(CH3)2NH2|2[Tb6(μ3-OH)8(ABTC)3(DMF)1.5(H2O)1.5]·1.75(H2O) (2). The phase purity of the bulk materials was confirmed by similarities between the calculated and as-synthesized powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns (Fig. S1, ESI†).

Compounds 1 and 2 crystallize in the cubic Im and trigonal Rc space groups, respectively. Because the two structures are isoreticular, they can be described as follows. Each RE3+ metal ion is surrounded by four μ3-OH groups, with the four oxygen atoms from carboxylate groups of four independent L4− ligands, leaving the ninth coordination site occupied by either a water or a DMF molecule. The adjacent RE ions are bridged via μ3-OH and deprotonated carboxylate groups in a bis-monodentate fashion, giving a [RE6(μ3-OH)8(O2C–)12] MBB (Fig. 1).

Each hexanuclear MBB is bridged through L4− ligands to produce a 3-periodic MOF (Fig. 1). Structural and topological analyses of the resulting crystal structure confirm that the topologies of 1 and 2 correspond to the expected ftw net, constructed from the bridged hexanuclear clusters, i.e., [RE6(μ3-OH)8(O2C–)12] MBBs, where the carbon atoms of the coordinated carboxylates, acting as points of extension, coincide with the vertices of the cuboctahedron vertex figure of the ftw net, and the L4− linker acts as the 4-c node.

Alternatively, 1 and 2 can also be interpreted as a kle and as an unprecedented (3, 12)-connected kxe net, respectively, when the L4− linker is described as two 3-c triangular nodes.

The structures of 1 and 2 encompass cubic cages with pore diameters of 9.46 Å and 9.6 Å, and pore-aperture sizes of 2.64 × 2.96 Å and 4.44 × 2.35 Å, respectively.

The thermal stabilities of the structures were investigated using variable-temperature PXRD diagrams, which revealed that 1 and 2 maintain their crystallinity up to 400 °C in vacuum (Fig. S2, ESI†). The water stability was also examined, as it is well known that stability in the presence of moisture is a key parameter for gas/vapor separations. The PXRD diagrams collected after immersing 1 and 2 in liquid water were found to match the theoretical one derived from a single-crystal structure (Fig. S1a and c, ESI†). These results revealed that both compounds exhibited exceptional hydrolytic and thermal stabilities, which can be ascribed to the strong metal–oxygen bonds (around 2.375 Å) induced by the high charge density (Z/r) of the lanthanide cations. Both 1 and 2 were found to be non-porous to N2 at 77 K due to the reduced pore-aperture sizes. Accordingly, CO2 adsorption isotherm was performed on 1 and revealed that CO2 can access the cages at 298 K, while a Type I isotherm is shown at 195 K (Fig. S4a and S5a, ESI†). The pore volume for 1, estimated to be 0.11 cm3 g−1 from CO2 isotherm at 195 K, is in good agreement with the theoretical value (also 0.11 cm3 g−1).

High-pressure adsorption measurements of CO2, N2, CH4, C2H4, C2H6, C3H6, and C3H8 were carried out on 1 to promote access of gas molecules into the cages. CO2 adsorbed quite easily, even at relatively low pressures. A minimum of 5 bars is required for N2, CH4, and C2H4 to reach an uptake of 0.2 mmol g−1, whereas C2H6, C3H6, and C3H8 need at least 8 bars to reach less than 0.15 mmol g−1 (Fig. S10a and S11a, ESI†). These results clearly show that the ftw-MOF constructed from a BPTC ligand cannot meet the pore-aperture size requirement for complex separations, even under high pressure.

Examination of the CO2 adsorption performed on the methanol-exchanged sample of 2 revealed a typical Type I isotherm at 195 K (Fig. S4b, ESI†), representative of a microporous material. The pore volume of 2 was estimated to be 0.18 cm3 g−1, in good agreement with the theoretical value (0.18 cm3 g−1).

The gas separation performances were evaluated by carrying gas adsorption isotherms of H2, CH4, N2, C3H6, and C3H8 up to 760 Torr (Fig. S6 and S7c, ESI†). Further analysis showed that a slight extension of the ligand led to the enhancement of the CO2 uptake across the whole range of pressure at 195 K (Fig. S4b, ESI†) and at 298 K (Fig. S5b, ESI†). Although the Qst as a function of CO2 loading is almost linear (i.e. 27.7 to 26.0 kJ mol−1), indicative of homogenous binding sites over the full range of the CO2 loading (Fig. S5c, ESI†), 2 displays weaker CO2 interactions at low loading than other RE-MOFs.

In order to assert the size of the pore apertures determined crystallographically by using the cut-off adsorption of molecules with larger kinetic diameters, the adsorption of propylene, propane, 1-butene, and iso-butane were investigated on 2. The equilibrium uptakes of propane and propylene were similar, with uptakes of 2.46 and 2.30 mmol g−1, respectively, at 760 Torr and 298 K (Fig. 2a). Nevertheless, beyond the weak thermodynamic force driving the separation of propylene from propane, we discovered that propylene exhibits faster kinetics of adsorption than propane (Fig. 2b). Markedly, this indicates that the adsorption cut-off is close to the size of propane. Further analysis of the adsorption of slightly higher carbon-number olefins and paraffin on 2 showed that the uptake of n-butane was 2.7 mmol g−1 at 760 Torr and 298 K (Fig. S8, ESI†). However, no adsorption for iso-butane was observed, indicating a full sieving of n-butane from iso-butane, a performance similar to that of fum-fcu MOF (Fig. S8, ESI†). In order to explore the potential of 2 for separations of increased complexity, we examined the mixed-gas adsorption of propylene from propane. The column breakthrough experiment using the gas mixture C3H8/C3H6:50/50 with a flow rate of 6 cm3 min−1 showed that propane was not retained in the column; the breakthrough time of propylene was 10 min (for 1.49 g MOF) (Fig. 2c), which is equivalent to a 0.9 mmol g−1 uptake.

This result demonstrates that the difference in the kinetics of adsorption observed in the single-gas adsorption analysis translated into a highly efficient separation “close to molecular sieving” when using mixed-gas testing with a high propylene uptake. The breakthrough experiment carried out with a diluted gas mixture, C3H8/C3H6/N2:5/5/90, with a flow rate of 11 cc min−1 revealed an impressive retention time for propylene (40 min, 0.66 mmol g−1) at a low propylene concentration (Fig. S9, ESI†). The separation of propane/propylene was further confirmed by simultaneous gravimetric and calorimetric (TG-DSC) measurements for propane and propylene, respectively. In the case of propane, the mass gain due to adsorption was negligible compared to that of propylene (Fig. S12, ESI†).

In summary, we demonstrated how the judicious practice of the MBB approach, combined with reticular chemistry, results in a topological shift, fabricating a new, ultra-microporous MOF that can achieve separations of high complexity. The shift from fcu to ftw allowed the concomitant alteration of the size and shape of the pore aperture and, thus, access to the full sieving of iso-butane and the kinetic separation of propylene from propane. By adjusting the rectangular pore-aperture size induced by the ftw topology, using either the BPTC or ABTC ligand, we accessed ftw-MOF-ABTC, which has the ability to kinetically separate propylene from propane. This work paves the way for the design and development of a new series of ultra-microporous MOFs using RE-based MOF sorbents for the highly challenging second and third-level gas separations.
