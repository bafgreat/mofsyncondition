Introduction

There is currently considerable interest in metal–organic coordination polymers or metal–organic-framework (MOF) materials which have open pores or channels for potential applications in gas separation, hydrogen storage, catalysis, optical and magnetic applications.1 A common synthetic strategy is to construct these materials using a variety of organic ligands as spacers and functional metal ions as nodes. In this respect multitopic carboxylates have received considerable study due to their availability and potential for allowing for the tailored design of such frameworks.2 Pioneering studies by Yaghi and co-workers established the use of the 1,4-benzenedicarboxylate (1,4-BDC) group under hydrothermal conditions (> 140 °C, H2O) for the construction of lanthanide based coordination polymers such as Tb2(BDC)3(H2O)4 and Tb(BDC)(NO3)(DMF)2,3 and there are now several other reports on this class of material.4 As part of a program focused on the study of new multinuclear lanthanide complexes,5 we recently described the use of the 1,4-BDC group for directly linking two or more preformed Ln(iii) complexes (Chart 1).6 We have typically used relatively mild reaction conditions, such as refluxing MeOH, to achieve 1,4-BDC-Ln binding. Lanthanide coordination chemistry is characterized by high coordination numbers and flexible coordination geometries and structures often depend on a variety of factors such as ionic radius, reaction temperature, coordinating solvents and the nature of counter anions.7 For crystalline MOF materials it has recently been shown that reaction conditions can have a significant influence over the composition of the isolated material. This, in turn, can have a profound effect on important properties such as reversible hydrogen adsorption.8 We were therefore interested to see if Ln based 1,4-BDC coordination polymers could be generated under milder reaction conditions. We report here the results of our studies on reactions of LnX3·6H2O (X = Cl−, NO3−) with 1,4-H2BDC in refluxing MeOH in the presence of Et3N. Under these conditions we were able to prepare new framework materials which fall into these types: (i) 3-D, binary [Eu2(BDC)3(MeOH)4]·8MeOH (1); (ii) 2-D, Cl− containing, [Ln(BDC)(MeOH)4]+Cl− (Ln = Eu (2), Gd (3), Tb (4); (iii) 2-D, NO3− containing, [Ln(BDC)NO3(MeOH)2]·MeCN·H2O (Ln = Eu (5) and Gd (6)).

Although compound 1 has a formula analogous to Tb2(BDC)3(H2O)4 and also exhibits a 3-D-framework structure, the use of MeOH results in a different structural configuration for the Eu3+ ions and 1,4-BDC units within the unit cell.3 The resulting 3-D-MOF has pores or channels which are significantly larger than those found in Tb2(BDC)3(H2O)4. Compounds 2–6 all have 2-D, sheet like, coordination frameworks. For the chloride derivatives (2–4) the Cl− anion does not coordinate directly to the metal. In contrast complexes 5 and 6, which are formed from Ln(NO3)3·6H2O, retain NO3− which remains bound to the metal center.

Experimental

All reactions were performed under dinitrogen atmospheres using standard Schlenk techniques. Metal salts, 1,4-benzenedicarboxylic acid (1,4-H2BDC), triethylamine and other solvents were purchased from Aldrich and used directly without further purification. Infrared (IR) spectra from 4000 to 400 cm−1 were recorded on a Nicolet IR 200 FTIR spectrometer. Absorption spectra were obtained on a BECKMAN DU 640 spectrophotometer, excitation and visible emission spectra on a QuantaMaster PTI fluorimeter. Elemental analyses were performed by Atlantic Microlab, Norcross, GA.

[Eu2(BDC)3(MeOH)4]·8MeOH (1) and [Eu(BDC)(MeOH)4]·Cl·MeOH·0.25H2O (2)

Triethylamine (2 mL of a 0.1 M MeCN solution) was added to a solution of 1,4-H2BDC (0.017 g, 0.10 mmol) and EuCl3·6H2O (0.036 g, 0.10 mmol) in MeOH (20 ml). The resulting solution was stirred and heated under reflux for 2 d. The solution was then cooled to room temperature and filtered. The filtrate was separated into two equal portions: One portion was subjected to slow vapor diffusion with diethyl ether. Colorless single crystals of 1 were formed after 2 weeks. Yield (based on 1,4-H2BDC): 0.009 g (66%). Anal. Found: C, 35.69; H, 4.26. Calc. for C16H22O10Eu: C, 36.51; H, 4.21%. IR (DMF, cm−1): 3620(w), 3002(m), 2987(m), 1566(s, νas(COO−)), 1416(s, νs(COO−)), 1350(m), 1258(m), 1099(s), 860(w), 660(m). The other portion was subjected to slow evaporation by standing in air. Colorless single crystals of 2 were formed after 1 month. Yield (based on 1,4-H2BDC): 0.002 g (31%), mp > 280 °C (decomp.). IR (DMF, cm−1): 3536(w), 3477(w), 2931(m), 2857(m), 1545(m, νas(COO−)), 1439(w), 1389(s, νs(COO−)), 1256(m), 1096(s), 1061(w), 659(m).

Found: C, 29.99; H, 4.63. Calc. for C13H24.5ClO9.25Gd: C, 29.94; H, 4.74%. IR (DMF, cm−1): 3678(w), 3012(m), 2876(m), 1551(s, νas(COO−)), 1390(s, νs(COO−)), 1260(m), 1090(s), 1060(w), 659(m).

[Tb(BDC)(MeOH)4]·Cl·MeOH·0.25H2O (4)

The procedure was the same as that for 1 using TbCl3·6H2O. Colorless single crystals of 4 were formed after 2 weeks. Yield: 0.032 g (61%). Anal. Found: C, 29.22; H, 4.93. Calc. for C13H24.5ClO9.25Tb: C, 29.84; H, 4.72%. IR (DMF, cm−1): 3533(m), 2900(m), 1550(s, νas(COO−)), 1388(s, νs(COO−)), 1258(m), 1091(s), 1062(w), 659(m).

X-Ray crystallography

Data were collected on a Nonius Kappa CCD diffractometer with graphite monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at 153 K. Absorption corrections were applied using GAUSSIAN. The structures were solved by direct methods and refined anisotropically using full-matrix least-squares methods with the SHELX 97 program package.9 The coordinates of the non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically, while hydrogen atoms were included in the calculation isotropically but not refined. Neutral atom scattering factors were taken from Cromer and Waber.10

Results and discussion

Reaction of 1,4-H2BDC with EuCl3·6H2O (1 : 1) in refluxing MeOH in the presence of Et3N gave a mixture of the framework materials [Eu2(BDC)3(MeOH)4]·8MeOH (1) and [Eu(BDC)(MeOH)4]·Cl·MeOH·0.25H2O (2). Similar reactions provided the isomorphous Gd (3) and Tb (4) analogs of 2. Reactions of 1,4-H2BDC with Ln(NO3)3·6H2O under similar conditions gave [Ln(BDC)NO3(MeOH)2]·MeCN·H2O (Ln = Eu(5) and Gd(6)). The complexes have been structurally characterized by single crystal X-ray diffraction studies and the luminescence properties of 1 and 4 in DMF solutions were determined at 298 K.

Compounds 1 and 2 may be isolated in relatively pure form depending on the method of crystallization. Thus, slow diffusion of Et2O into the filtered reaction mixture provides 1 while 2 can be isolated by slow evaporation of the filtrate in air. Crystallographic data for all compounds is presented in Table 1 and selected bond lengths and angles are given in Tables 2–7. Compound 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P2(1)/n with one Eu atom, one and a half 1,4-BDC anions and two coordinated MeOH molecules per asymmetric unit. A view of the coordination environment for Eu is shown in Fig. 1.

The structure is based on pairs of Eu atoms which are bridged by four -CO2− groups of the 1,4-BDC dianions. This configuration is similar to that found in the classic dinuclear d-block acetates such as Mo2(OAc)411 with the exception that one pair of -CO2− units is bonded in an asymmetric tridentate fashion (Chart 1(b)).12 A third -CO2− group (from one end of a 1,4-BDC group) is bidentate and binds to only one Eu atom and completes the 3-D network structure. Each Eu3+ has two coordinated MeOH groups and is 9-coordinate. The Eu⋯Eu separation within the Eu2 unit is 4.010 Å. The Eu–O (carboxylate) distances range from 2.357 to 2.715 Å. The overall structure can be described in terms of a parallelepipedal motif with dimensions 13 Å ×11 Å × 11 Å and having an Eu2 unit (node) at each of its eight corners. A 1,4-BDC spacer unit is then located along each of the eight edges of the polyhedron. In contrast the solid state structure of Tb2(1,4-BDC)3(H2O)43 consists of a 3-D framework structure of approximate dimensions 5 Å × 6 Å × 10 Å. A single Tb3+ ion is located at each corner and 1,4-BDC units occupy the faces of the polyhedron rather than the edges as found in 1. The structural differences are illustrated in Fig. 3.

Compound 2 retains chloride although this ion is not incorporated into the 2-D sheetlike framework. We have also isolated and structurally characterized the isomorphous Gd (3) and Tb (4) analogs of 2. The structure of these materials contain one crystallographically independent metal ion in the asymmetric unit, one 1,4-BDC group and four coordinated MeOH molecules. A view of the metal coordination environment in 4 (Tb) is shown in Fig. 4.

As found in 1 there are two crystallographically equivalent Tb atoms in 4 which are bridged by four 1,4-BDC anions, arranged in the Mo2(OAc)4 paddlewheel fashion, with a Tb⋯Tb separation of 4.178 Å. In contrast to 1 this unit does not have further 1,4-BDC units providing a 3-D structure. Instead there are now four coordinated MeOH groups per Tb and the framework structure is a 2-dimensional sheet. The Cl− counter anion is not bound to Tb. The Tb–O (carboxylate) distances range from 2.297 to 3.050 Å. Although the directly bonded framework in 2–4 is 2-D there are additional MeOH⋯Cl hydrogen bonded interactions between adjacent layers (O⋯Cl− 3.041–3.129 Å) which results in a 3-dimensional open network (Fig. 5).

The porous channels formed by this supramolecular architecture have approximate dimensions of 11 Å × 11 Å and run parallel with the c axis and accommodate further molecules of MeOH.

Reactions of the nitrate salts Ln(NO3)3·6H2O with 1,4-H2BDC in refluxing MeOH produced the 2-D framework materials [Ln(BDC)NO3(MeOH)2]·MeCN·H2O (Ln = Eu (5) and Gd (6)) which are isomorphous. As found in the solid state for 2–4 the structures of 5 and 6 contain two crystallographically equivalent metal ions which are bridged by four 1,4-BDC units (Eu⋯Eu = 3.974 Å (5), Gd⋯Gd = 3.972 Å (6)). The overall 2-D framework structure is similar to that of 2–4 but with a bidentate NO3− anion replacing two coordinated MeOH molecules (Fig. 6). The metal ions are again 9-coordinate. The metal–O (carboxylate) distances range from 2.326–2.735 Å (5) and 2.329–2.741 Å (6). A view of the 2-D σ-bonded framework structure of 6 is shown in Fig. 7.

As found for 2–4 there are additional supramolecular interactions in the solid state structures of 5 and 6 which result in open 3-D framework architectures. In these cases the 2-D layers are linked by MeOH⋯O hydrogen bonds between coordinated MeOH molecules and NO3− anions (O⋯O 2.758 Å) (Fig. 8). The extended open channels which are formed by these interactions have approximate dimensions of 9 × 11 Å and run along the a axis. Guest molecules of MeOH are also accommodated within these pores (Fig. 9).

IR-Spectroscopy

IR spectra of 1–6 all show absorptions characteristic of the symmetric and asymmetric vibrations of coordinated carboxylate groups with peaks at 1416–1388 and 1580–1545 cm−1 respectively. There are no peaks typical of 1,4-H2BDC groups (1715–1680 cm−1) confirming that the 1,4-BDC groups are fully deprotonated.13 The IR spectra of 5 and 6 also contain two intense absorptions at 1299–1296 (ν4) and 1462–1460 (ν1) cm−1 assigned to the asymmetric stretching vibrations of the NO3− groups.14

Luminescence studies

The photophysical properties of 1 and 4 were studied at 298 K in DMF solution (DMF = dimethylformamide). At a concentration of 2 × 10−5 M 1 exhibited the emission spectrum characteristic of the Eu3+ ion (5D0→7Fj transitions, j = 0, 1, 2, 3 and 4). Under similar conditions compound 4 gave the emission spectrum typical of the Tb3+ ion (5D4 → 7Fn transitions, n = 6, 5, 4 and 3) (Fig. 10). Limitations in the resolution of the spectrometer used prevented us from observing further splitting of the peaks. For 1 the appearance of the symmetry-forbidden emission 5D0→7F0 at 580 nm indicates that the Eu3+ ions occupy sites with low symmetry and have no inversion center,15 in agreement with the X-ray crystal structure.

Conclusions

A series of six lanthanide-based coordination polymers constructed from 1,4-H2BDC have been prepared at the reflux temperature of MeOH, and structurally characterized. In all complexes the lanthanide ions are 9-coordinate. Each 1,4-BDC dianion bonds to four lanthanide metals through its two carboxylate groups which show bis-monodentate and tridentate bonding modes. 2-D and 3-D lanthanide-based metal–organic framework structures are formed with Ln2 units acting as nodes and 1,4-BDC anions as spacers. Complexes 1 and 4 show typical visible emission spectra for Eu3+ and Tb3+ ions respectively.
