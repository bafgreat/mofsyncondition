3D zinc(II) coordination polymers built up by triazole and benzene-polycarboxylate anions: Synthesis, crystal structure, thermal and photoluminescence characterization

Author links open overlay panel, , , ,
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.poly.2009.04.008Get rights and content

Abstract

Two coordination polymers of the formula [Zn5(tmaH)4(trz)2(H2O)4] (1) and [Zn3(bta)(trz)2(H2O)4]·2H2O (2) [tmaH3 = benzene-1,3,5-tricarboxylic acid/trimesic acid, trzH = 1,2,4-triazole, btaH4 = benzene-1,2,4,5-tetracarboxylic acid] have been synthesized and structurally characterized by X-ray single crystal diffraction analysis. Both complexes are 3D coordination polymers containing [Zn4(trz)2] units connected by benzene-carboxylate anions. In particular, an undulated layer containing 6- and 18-membered rings is outlined in the network of 1. Hydrogen bonds, involving the coordinated and lattice water molecules with carboxylate oxygen atoms, contribute to the stabilization of the networks. Their thermal stability was investigated by thermogravimetric analysis. The fluorescence spectrum of 1 features two peaks at 419 and 323 nm, originating from a π–π∗ intraligand transition and LMCT, respectively. For 2 a broad band at 410 nm is assigned solely as a π–π∗ intraligand transition.

Graphical abstract

Two new 3D hydrogen bonded zinc (II) coordination polymers have been synthesized using the benzene-1,3,5-tricarboxylate dianion or the benzene-1,2,4,5-tetracarboxylate tetra anion, with 1,2,4-triazolate. Both compounds were characterized by their X-ray single crystal structures, and by thermal and photoluminescence studies.

1. Introduction

Metal organic frameworks (MOFs) [1], [2], [3] have been an area of increasing interest due to their topology and potential application in the field of ion exchange [4], [5], [6], catalysis [7], [8], [9], luminescence [10], [11], [12] and gas storage [13], [14], [15], [16]. Among the variety of organic molecules used as linkers in the design of supramolecular networks, aromatic polycarboxylates (O-donor) and heterocyclic N rings are the most widely used ligands due to their rigidity in structure and flexibility in coordination modes [17], [18], [19], [20], [21], [22], [23], [24].

Several metal organic supramolecular structures based on tmaH3 [tmaH3 = benzene-1,3,5-tricarboxylic acid/trimesic acid] and btaH4 [benzene-1,2,4,5-tetracarboxylic], in the form of differently deprotonated anions, have been reported. The examples show an extensive application of these ligands towards transition metal ions with the aim to build architectures of high dimensionality [25], [26], [27], [28], [29], [30]. Polyaza heterocycle trzH [1,2,4-triazole] and its derivatives are very interesting ligands with N-donor centers, which can produce interesting networks with novel topologies [31], [32], [33], [34], [35], [36], [37]. TrzH can function as a neutral bridging ligand to two metal sites or as a bridge between three metal centers in the azolate form (trz−). By exploiting the ligating behavior of these two types (O- and N-donors) of ligands, interesting novel networks can be synthesized.

In this work we report the hydrothermal synthesis, structural characterization, thermal and photoluminescence properties of two new coordination complexes showing a 3D network, namely [Zn5(tmaH)4(trz)2(H2O)4] (1) and [Zn3(bta)(trz)2(H2O)4]·2H2O (2).

2. Result and discussions

2.1. Preparation of the complexes

Zinc(II) complexes 1 and 2 were obtained by reacting zinc(II)acetate and 1,2,4-triazole with benzene-1,3,5-tricarboxylic acid and benzene-1,2,4,5-tetracarboxylic acid, respectively. The pH of both of reaction mixtures were adjusted to five by adding NaOH solution. Both the isolated compounds are air-stable, less soluble in water and alcohols, but soluble in solvents with pronounced donor properties.

2.2. Structure description of 1

An ortep view of the three crystallographically independent Zn(II) coordination spheres is depicted in Fig. 1 and selected bond lengths and angles are reported in Table 1. The three independent metals present different coordination environments, Zn(1) and Zn(3) having distorted tetrahedral NO3 and N2O2 donor sets, respectively, neglecting a long Zn–O(carboxylate) distance of 2.271(3) Å for the latter. The Zn(1)- and Zn(3)-N(triazole) coordination bond lengths are between 2.000(3) and 2.024(3) Å, those with O-carboxylate fall in a larger range from 1.941(2) to 2.072(2) Å. On the other hand, the Zn(2) ion, located on an inversion center, presents an octahedral O6 chromophore, with Zn–OH2 bond lengths of 2.159(3) and 2.063(3) Å, and Zn–O(carboxylate) distances of 2.060(2) Å. All these values are within the range usually observed for Zn(II) compounds containing five-membered azaheterocycles and benzene-carboxylate anions [38], [39], [40], [41], [42], [43], [44]. Fig. 2 shows the centrosymmetric [Zn4(trz)2] unit and the bridging behavior of the triazolato ligand coordinating two Zn(3) ions spaced at 3.807 Å, while the Zn(1)–Zn(3) and Zn(1)–Zn(3′) distances are 6.070 and 5.956 Å, respectively. In addition, the metals are linked by carboxylate groups having sin-anti bridging behavior (intermetallic Zn(1)–Zn(2) and Zn(1)–Zn(3) distances of 5.048 and 4.353 Å). The described connections give rise to an undulated layer comprising 6- and 18-membered rings (Fig. 2). These undulated metal layers are further connected by bridging benzene-1-carboxylic-3,5-carboxylato dianions to form a 3D network (Fig. 3), which is reinforced by H-bonds involving the aquo and carboxylate oxygen atoms. Strong interactions (O–H⋯O distance of ca. 2.6 Å; Table 1S in SI) are measured for the H-bonds occurring between the carboxylic groups. Here the 1,3,5-tricarboxylate dianions show different behavior, one acts as μ3-bridging/monocoordinating, the other as μ3-bridging/chelating species (Fig. 1S in SI). It is interesting to note that the carboxylate groups involved in metal bridging are tilted with respect to the benzene ring by 39.6(2)° (C7/O1/O2) and 23.2(2)° (C16/O7/O8), while the other –CO2 groups are almost coplanar with the ring atoms.

2.3. Structure description of 2

The two crystallographic independent ions, Zn(1) and Zn(2), exhibit a N2O4 octahedral and a distorted N2O2 tetrahedral coordination environment, respectively. Fig. 4 depicts the crystal structure, while a selection of bond lengths and angles is collected in Table 2. The coordination bond distances of Zn(2) are almost comparable, being in the range 1.922(4) to 1.993(4) Å, the Zn(1)–O(1w), Zn(1)–N(1) and Zn(1)–O(2w) distances vary from 2.070(5) to 2.100(4) and 2.146(4) Å, respectively. The deprotonated triazole ligand bridges three metal ions forming an isosceles triangle, the base being defined by two Zn(2) ions spaced at 3.611 Å, while the two Zn(1)–Zn(2) distances are 5.993 and 6.170 Å. These values are comparable to those measured in the structure of 1. The crystal structure can be described as being built up of – Zn(1)(H2O)4 − [Zn(2)2(trz)2] – arrays running in the ac plane, which are linked by μ4-tetrabenzene-carboxylate anions (Fig. 5) to form a 3D architecture. Each carboxylate group is tilted by about 54.9(4)° with respect to the phenyl ring, having one oxygen atom coordinating a metal ion and the other acting as a H-bond acceptor for an aquo ligand. The conformation assumed by the carboxylate groups is likely attained for steric reasons in order to accomplish metal coordination and H-bond interactions. The described H-bonding scheme together with interactions realized by lattice water molecules and carboxyl groups contribute to the stabilization of the overall crystal structure (O–H⋯O distances in the range 2.640–2.836 Å) (Table 1S in SI).

2.4. PXRD study

The simulated and measured XRD patterns of compounds 1 (Fig. 6a) and 2 (Fig. 6b) match well, indicating that the solid structures are unchanged during the crystallization process.

2.5. Thermal study

The thermal decomposition profiles for 1 and 2 are shown in Fig. 7, Fig. 8, respectively. Compound 1 is thermally stable up to 40 °C. On further heating, the compound is dehydrated in two steps (four coordinated water molecules, mass loss ca. 5%, Calc. 5.2%) in the temperature range 40–250 °C. The deaquation temperature range is significant, with the presence of two types of strong hydrogen bonding of the coordinated water molecules (O–H: 2.6 and 2.8 Å). The deaquated species collapsed further upon heating as the Zn centers fail to attain a stable geometry after the removal of the coordinated water molecules. A rapid weight loss (ca. 9.8%, Calc. 9.9%) between 260 and 340 °C corresponds to the loss of the trz− ligand, and sharp weight loss (ca. 59%, Calc. 60.8%) between 350 and 630 °C is attributed to the loss of the tmaH2− ligand. Compound 2 undergoes a preliminary dehydration process between 80 and 130 °C, corresponding to the loss of a total of six water molecules in two steps (mass loss ca. 15%, Calc. 15.6%), indicating the presence of coordinated and non-coordinated water molecules. The subsequent step between 350 and 460 °C is associated with the loss of the bta4− ligand with a mass loss of ca. 36% (Calc. 36.2%). Finally the trz− ligand is lost (mass loss ca. 19%, Calc. 19.7%) between 725 and 770 °C.

2.6. Photoluminescence study

The luminescence study of the ligands and complexes has been performed in the solid state at room temperature. The two polycarboxylates i.e. tmaH3 and btaH4, give no fluorescence response, whereas the azole trzH shows an emission band around 370 nm with λex = 300 nm (Fig. 2S in SI). Compound 1 shows two peaks: a major peak at 323 nm and a weak emission at 419 nm when excited at 278 nm (Fig. 9). For compound 2 a bright fluorescence at 410 nm with a broad emission band has been found when the complex is excited at 320 nm (Fig. 10). During a comparison of the emission properties of the complexes with the trzH ligand, the red shifted band by 49 nm (for 1) and 40 nm (for 2) can be assigned as a π–π∗ intraligand charge transfer of the triazole ring. On the contrary, for 1, the 323 nm band, blue shifted by ∼47 nm, can be assigned as a ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT) transition. The probable reason for this is that the triazole ring is a deprotonated π-excessive system which is coordinated to the Zn(II) center by displacing one hydrogen atom. The coordination of Zn(II) with the trz− ring decreases the electron density of the trz− ring, which is responsible for the weakening of the strength of emission.

3. Conclusion

The reaction of the 1,2,4-triazole ligand and zinc(II) metal with aromatic polycarboxylates affords interesting polymeric architectures. In this work, the combination of the 1,2,4-triazolate anion (trz−) with the benzene-1,3,5-tricarboxylate dianion (tmaH2−) or the fully deprotonated benzene-1,2,4,5-tetracarboxylate (bta4−) with Zn(II) ions leads to two 3D coordination networks. The X-ray structural analysis of the compounds revealed that both the polymeric structures comprise centrosymmetric [Zn4(trz)2] fragments where each triazolato ligand bridges three metal ions. In particular, an undulated layer containing 6- and 18-membered rings is outlined in the crystal structure of 1. A photoluminescence study revealed that the red shifted band for both compounds is due to a π–π∗ intraligand charge transfer of the triazole ring, while the blue shifted band in 1 is due to LMCT. The synthesis of these compounds demonstrates that the proper combination of polycarboxylates and 1,2,4-triazole derivatives is an excellent way to synthesize interesting high dimensional networks/MOFs.

4. Experimental

4.1. Materials and physical measurements

All the chemicals for the synthesis were used as received. Zinc acetate dihydrate was obtained from MERCK. Benzene-1,3,5-tricarboxylic acid, benzene-1,2,4,5-tetracarboxylic acid and 1,2,4-triazole were purchased from SIGMA–ALDRICH Inc. IR specta for 1 and 2 were recorded in a JASCO FT/IR-6200 using the ATR technique in the range 4000–400 cm−1. Elemental analyses (C, H, N) were performed using a Perkin–Elmer 240C elemental analyzer. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) were done under nitrogen (flow rate of 20 mL min−1) with a Metler Toledo (SDTA 851e) analyzer in the temperature range 25–800 °C, with a heating rate of 10 °C/min. X ray powder diffraction data were recorded on a BRUKER AXS D8 advance instrument using Cu Kα radiation. The fluorescence spectra of the ligands and compounds were measured in the solid state at room temperature using a HITACHI F-7000 spectrofluorimeter.

4.2. Synthesis of {[Zn5(tmaH)4(trz)2(H2O)4]}n (1)

A mixture of Zn(CH3COOH)2·2H2O (0.5 mmol, 0.109 g), tmaH3 (0.5 mmol, 0.105 g) and trzH (0.5 mmol, 0.034 g) in a molar ratio of 1:1:1 was taken in deionized water (8 mL). The pH of the solution was adjusted to ∼5 with 1(M) NaOH solution. The solution was transferred into 23 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel and heated at 160 °C for 72 h. The mixture was allowed to cool to room temperature and the resulting colorless needle-shaped crystals were isolated by decanting the supernatant liquid, and were washed with deionized water, ethanol and dried in air. Yield: 75%. Anal. Calc. for C40H28N6O28Zn5 (1367.53): C, 35.12; H, 2.06; N, 6.14. Found: C, 35.09; H, 2.12; N, 6.20%.

4.3. Synthesis of {[Zn3(bta)(trz)2(H2O)4·2H2O]}n (2)

Compound 2 was prepared by a similar procedure to that described for the synthesis of 1. The ligand btaH4 (0.5 mmol, 0.127 g) was used instead of tmaH3. After cooling the solution to room temperature, colorless needle-shaped crystals were isolated by filtering the supernatant liquid, and were washed with deionized water, ethanol and dried in air. Yield: 60%. Anal. Calc. for C14H18N6O14Zn3 (690.45): C, 24.35; H, 2.62; N, 12.17. Found: C, 24.29; H, 2.80; N, 12.25%.

4.4. IR study

The IR spectral analysis of compounds 1 and 2 shows a broad band in the region 3200–3500 cm−1 [ν(O–H)], suggesting the presence of H2O molecules [45]. νas(OCO), νs(OCO) bands appear at ∼1620, ∼1424 cm−1 for compound 1 and ∼1635, ∼1455 cm−1 for compound 2, respectively, indicating the carboxylate is involved in the coordination.

4.5. Crystallographic data collection and refinement

Diffraction data collections were carried out at room temperature on a Nonius DIP-1030H system, using Mo Kα radiation, λ = 0.71073 Å. Cell refinement, indexing and scaling of all the data sets were performed using the programs denzo and scalepack [46]. The structures were solved by direct methods and subsequent Fourier analyses, and refined by the full-matrix least-squares method using shelxl97 [47] based on F2 with all reflections. Hydrogen atoms bonded to carbon were included at calculated positions, while those of water molecules were located in a difference Fourier map and refined with distance constraints. All the calculations were performed using the wingx System, Ver 1.70.01 [48]. Crystallographic data are given in Table 3.

  • Syntheses and characterizations of calcium and strontium based coordination compounds with the 5-(2-pyridyl)tetrazolate ligand, respectively exhibiting extended 1 D and 2 D structures

    2022, Journal of Molecular Structure

    Coordination compounds of calcium and strontium with 5-(2-pyridyl)tetrazolate ligand (2-PTZ) were obtained by the reactions of the respective metal nitrates with 2-cyanopyridine, 2-cyanoacetamide and sodium azide in aqueous solutions. Both the complexes were found to have similar composition i.e. [M(2-PTZ)2(H2O)4]n.2nH2O where M = Ca(II) (1) and Sr(II) (2). The products were characterized by FT-IR spectra and single-crystal X-ray diffractions. Their photoluminescences, bandgaps, and thermal properties have been studied. Single crystal X-ray diffractions revealed that both 1 and 2 have distorted square anti-prismatic molecular geometries wherein the two 2-PTZ anions act as bidentate ligands by coordinating via their pyridine-N and tetrazole-N atoms. In both the molecules six water molecules, of which four acting as monodentate ligands, and the remaining two in the lattices were present. The lattice H2O molecules significantly stabilized the structures through N··H··O hydrogen bonds with the 2-PTZ ligands in the structures. Compound 2 (with around 50% decomposition) shows better thermal stability than calcium-based compound 1(72% decomposition).
