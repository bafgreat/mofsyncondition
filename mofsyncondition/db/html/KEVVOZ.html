Introduction

The material level control of structural assembly and molecular synthesis can lead to new specific catalytic mechanisms and chemical reactions. Organonitriles have an important application in the field of organic synthesis,1 and are used in natural products, pharmaceutical syntheses, pesticides and dyes. The cyano (–CN) group is mainly introduced into organic compounds using toxic cyanide reagents2 such as KCN,3 NaCN,4 Zn(CN)2,5 TMSCN6 and K3Fe(CN)6.7 These cyanide reagents pose a serious threat to the environment, and even more so to human beings, in their production, transport and application. So, devising a way to introduce the –CN group in situ could reduce the use of toxic cyanide reagents. The activation of C–C bonds by transition-metal complexes is the key to introducing –CN in situ and has attracted considerable interest,8 especially for acetonitrile. The cleavage of the C–C bond of acetonitrile has been reported9 only sporadically owing to its stable C–C bond energy of 133 kcal mol−1. These reactions occurred under rigorous experimental conditions in which expensive organometallic catalysts were used in most cases, for example, [Me2Si(C5Me4)2]MoH2,10 [Pd(PPh3)4],11 [Pd(dba)2],12 [(PMe3)4CoCH3],13 [RhCl(cod)]2,14 [Cu(CH3CN)4PF6],15 [Pd(PBu3)2Cl2],16 [Ni(dippe)H]2,17 Cp(CO)2Fe(SiMe3),18 and [Cp(PMe3)Rh(SiPh3)(CH2Cl2)][BAr′4].19 Cu,20 Zn21 and Ag22 complexes have also been used to cleave the C–C bond of acetonitrile. Most of the catalytic C–C cleavage reactions with these catalysts were done under accidental conditions and lacked any guiding methodology.

In this paper, a one-dimensional copper(i) nanorod polymer [Cu(L)2·(CN)]n (1) (L = 4(3H)-quinazolone), was formed in situ by the C–C bond cleavage of acetonitrile. The in situ synthesis of 1 was analyzed using electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) spectroscopy and electrospray ionization mass spectrometry (ESI-MS) and its methylene blue (MB) photocatalytic degradation mechanism was revealed from density functional theory (DFT) calculations. The results provide direction for the in situ synthesis of –CN complexes.

Experimental section

Materials and methods

The titanium dioxide photocatalyst (P25, 80% anatase, 20% rutile, BET area ca. 50 m2 g−1) was kindly supplied by Degussa. The 4(3H)-quinazolone (L) and methylene blue (MB) were purchased from Energy Chemical (Shanghai, China). All solvents and chemicals were commercial reagents and used without further purification. The IR spectra were measured as KBr pellets using a Nicolet 5DX FT-IR spectrometer. The elemental analysis (carbon, hydrogen, and nitrogen) was performed using a PerkinElmer 240 elemental analyzer. The X-ray phase analysis was carried out using a Rigaku D/max 2500 X-ray diffractometer equipped with a CuKα radiation source (λ = 0.15604 nm); the tube voltage was 40 kV, the tube current was 150 mA, a graphite monochromator was used, and the 2θ range was 5° to 65°. The X-ray crystallography measurements were carried out on a Bruker Apex CCD area-detector diffractometer. The XPS measurements were performed on a Kratos Axis Ultra DLD system with a base pressure of 10−9 Torr. The scanning electron microscopy (SEM) was performed using a Hitachi S-4800 under the following conditions: Mag.: 1 KX, Signal A: VPSE, and EHT: 20 kV. The surface area of 1 was determined using the Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) technique; the apparatus (AUTO CHEMII 2920) was employed to determine the surface area of 1 using N2 as the probe gas. The thermal stability was tested using a Pyris Diamond TG-DTG Analyzer. The UV-Vis diffuse reflectance spectra were measured using a UV-2700 instrument from Shimadzu, Japan with BaSO4 as a reference. The electrochemical cyclic voltammetry was conducted on a CHI 410B Electrochemical Workstation using a Pt disk coated with a polymer film, a Pt plate, and a Ag/Ag+ electrode as the working electrode, counter electrode, and reference electrode, respectively, in a 0.1 mol L−1 tetrabutylammonium hexafluorophosphate (Bu4NPF6) acetonitrile solution. The electrochemical potentials were calibrated against Fc/Fc+. The concentration of MB in solution was measured using a UV-Vis 2550 with a 664 nm wavelength.

Synthesis of [Cu(L)2·(H2O)2·(NO3)2]

[Cu(L)2·(H2O)2·(NO3)2] was synthesized according to a reported method.23 A mixture of L (0.2 mmol, 0.0292 g) and Cu(NO3)2·3H2O (0.1 mmol, 0.0241 g) was dissolved in 10 mL of acetonitrile and stirred for 2 minutes at room temperature. The mixture was filtered and collected in a 50 mL beaker. The solvent was left to evaporate at room temperature for 2 hours, after which blue bulk crystals were obtained in 78.1% yield (based on copper).

Synthesis of [Cu(L)2·(CN)]n (1)

A mixture of [Cu(L)2·(H2O)2·(NO3)2] (0.0516 g, 1 mmol) and 10 mL of acetonitrile was sealed in a 23 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel autoclave. The mixture was heated in an oven at 130 °C for 24 hours, and then cooled to room temperature, filtered, and the filtrate was collected in a 50 mL beaker. The solvent was partially evaporated at room temperature for 1 hour, after which yellow needle crystals were obtained. The filtrate was sonicated for 30 minutes to obtain a nano powder of 1. Yield: 51% (based on copper). Anal. Calcd for C17H12CuN5O2: C, 53.45; H, 3.280; N, 18.25. Found: C, 53.47; H, 3.14; N, 18.35.

Synthesis of UiO-66-NH2

UiO-66-NH2 was synthesized according to reported method.24 A mixture of ZrCl4 (3.495 g, 15 mmol) and 2-amino terephthalic acid (2.715 g, 15 mmol) was dissolved in 115 mL of dimethylformamide (DMF) with the aid of ultrasonic vibrations. The reaction was heated at 120 °C for 24 h and then cooled to room temperature. The solvent was removed, and the solid powder was washed three times with DMF and methanol and dried at 80 °C for eight hours to obtain UiO-66-NH2 as a powder. Yield: 88% (based on p-phthalic acid).

X-ray crystallography

The X-ray crystallography measurements were carried out using a Bruker Apex CCD area-detector diffractometer (MoKα, λ = 0.71073 Å), and the structure was solved by direct methods using the Olex2 program25 and refined using the Olex2 program.25 Since the C and N atoms are co-located in –CN, they were refined through a disordered approach. The hydrogen atoms were placed at calculated positions and refined as riding atoms with isotropic displacement parameters. Photocatalytic experiments

Taking the photocatalytic degradation of methylene blue (MB) as an example, the photocatalytic performance of 1 was investigated and compared with that of UiO-66-NH2 and TiO2. The reaction conditions used were as follows: MB initial concentration C0 = 5 mg L−1, pH 3–9, dosage 50 mL; irradiated by UV light with a 175 W Hg lamp (λ > 330 nm, Toshiba SHLS-1002A), at the center of the position where the reaction bottle was placed, the irradiation intensity was approximately 35 W m−2, as determined by a UV irradiation meter (Model UV-A, Photoelectric Instrument Factory of Beijing Normal University); 250 mL round glass container with circulating water jacket, temperature (T) = 25 °C; the weights of 1, UIO-66-NH2 and TiO2 were 50 mg, respectively. The concentration of MB was measured using a UV-Vis 2550 at a wavelength of 664 nm. Results and discussion

The cyanide groups (–CN) in the structure are generated from the cleavage of the C–C bond of acetonitrile under solvothermal or hydrothermal conditions (under a relatively high temperature of 120 °C and autogenous pressure), under which the reduction of the Cu(ii) center to Cu(i) accompanies the reaction.20 The mononuclear copper(ii) complex [Cu(L)2·(H2O)2·(NO3)2], was prepared23 by the reaction of L and Cu(NO3)2·3H2O in acetonitrile. When the mixture of [Cu(L)2·(H2O)2·(NO3)2] and acetonitrile was heated under hydrothermal conditions, the result of this catalytic reaction was the introduction of –CN in situ to obtain polymer [Cu(L)2·(CN)]n (1). The electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) spectrum of a powdered sample of 1 at 173 K shows valuable information about the electronic configuration of copper (see ESI Fig. 1†). In the range of 150–550 mT, no signal was observed, indicating that no single electron is present in 1. To gain further insight into the evidence that Cu(i) is present in the polymer, X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) measurements for 1 were also performed (see ESI Fig. 2†). This did not provide valuable information about Cu(iii) ions, but further eliminated the possibility of magnetic impurities that have a 3d8 configuration. Only Cu(i) signals were observed: Cu(i) has a characteristic peak at 932.8 eV with no satellite peak.

X-ray crystallographic analysis24 reveals that the two adjacent copper(i) ions in 1 are bridged by a cyanide anion and the coordination environment of Cu(i) in 1 is shown in Fig. 1a. The asymmetric unit consists of one Cu(i) ion, two 4(3H)-quinazolinone ligands, and one cyano (–CN) ion. The Cu(i) ion in 1 is coordinated by two nitrogen atoms from two different 4(3H)-quinazolinone ligands (N(1), N(1A)), and one carbon atom and one nitrogen atom from two cyano (–CN) groups. The C–N distance is 1.146(5) Å and the Cu–N distances fall in the range of 1.918(2)–2.343(2) Å (Cu(1)–N(1) = 2.343(2) Å; Cu(1)–N(3) = 1.918(2) Å), and Cu(1)–C(9) = 1.918(2) Å. The bond angles of N(1A)–Cu(1)–N(1) and N(3)–Cu(1)–N(1) are 113.89(11)° and 90.68(9)°, respectively. These bond angles and distances are comparable to those in other cyanide bridged copper(i) complexes.20 The –CN groups in 1 were in situ generated, and the FT-IR spectrum (see ESI Fig. 3†) of 1 showed a strong peak at 2109.16 cm−1, which is characteristic of a CN vibration, and falls within the normal range of complexes20–22 containing –CN, confirming the presence of the –CN group. The elemental analysis also showed that the contents of C and N in 1 are close to the theoretical values (the relative errors of C and N are 0.037% and 0.545%, respectively). The –CN bridges and the Cu(i) atoms form a [–Cu–CN–Cu–CN–]n one-dimensional chain structure (Fig. 1b) and the SEM characterization also indicates the presence of this chain (Fig. 1c). Although there is Cu(i) in 1, it is very stable. It can exist in solutions of pH 3–9 for 12 hours under 175 W high-pressure mercury lamp radiation (see ESI Fig. 4†) and withstand 225 °C (see ESI Fig. 5†). This stability is due to the presence of N(2)–H(2)⋯O(1) hydrogen bonds (Fig. 1d) and π⋯π stacking interactions (Fig. 1b) in 1. The bond angles of these hydrogen bonds are close to 180° (N(2)–H(2)⋯O(1) = 177°), and the distance of the π ⋯ π bonds is 3.6255(1) Å. The adjacent 1D chains are connected by hydrogen bonds, and 1 changes from a 1D structure to a 2D network structure (Fig. 1d). The surface area of 1 was determined using the BET technique and its specific surface area (see ESI Fig. 6†) was found to be 104 m2 g−1.

Several comparative experiments were conducted to illustrate the key factors that lead to the formation of 1, and the results showed that the reaction could be affected by the reaction temperature. When the reaction temperature was lower than 120 °C under hydrothermal conditions, the solution was colourless, meaning that [Cu(L)2·(H2O)2·(NO3)2] could not catalyze the C–C bond cleavage of acetonitrile. However, when the reaction temperature was maintained at 125 °C for 24 hours, the solution became yellow-green in colour, and the filtrate was collected and allowed to naturally evaporate to obtain an amount of 1 (yield: 11%, based on Cu). But, when the reaction temperature was above 130 °C, the solution became yellow, and more than a 50% yield of 1 was obtained after a reaction time of 24 hours. Therefore, the reaction must be carried out with acetonitrile as a solvent and temperatures above 130 °C are beneficial for the C–C bond cleavage of acetonitrile. The formation mechanism of the cyanide bridged one-dimensional copper(i) polymer in acetonitrile was also confirmed by ESI-MS spectroscopy using a Thermo Finigan LCQDECA XP ion trap mass spectrometer (see ESI Fig. 7†). Five signals attributed to [HL]+, [Cu(L)2·(NO3)2·(CH3CN)], [Cu(L)·(NO3)·(CH3CN)2]+, [Cu(L)·(CH3CN)]2+ and [Cu(L)2]+ were observed. In the formation mechanism of 1, it may be that [Cu(L)2·(H2O)2·(NO3)2] first loses two coordinated water molecules (see ESI Fig. 8†) to form [Cu(L)2·(NO3)2] in acetonitrile solution under 130 °C. [Cu(L)2·(NO3)2] has vacant metal ion sites, so the acetonitrile can then coordinate with it to form [Cu(L)2·(NO3)2·(CH3CN)]. Due to the instability of five-coordinated [Cu(L)2·(NO3)2·(CH3CN)], NO3− is lost due to the acetonitrile attacking the Cu(ii) atom, resulting in the formation of four-coordinate [Cu(L)·(NO3)·(CH3CN)2]+. Subsequently, after the loss of another NO3−, two-coordinate [Cu(L)·(CH3CN)]2+ is obtained. The Cu(ii) centre in [Cu(L)·(CH3CN)]2+ catalyzes the C–C bond cleavage of acetonitrile to form CH3 and (CN)2, and (CN)2 reduces Cu(ii) to Cu(i), resulting in the formation of [Cu(L)2]+. Finally, when the Teflon-lined stainless is opened, the solvent evaporates to yield [Cu(L)2·(CN)]n (1). The formation mechanism of 1 is shown in Fig. 2. The appearance of [HL]+ is due to the reduction of Cu(ii) to copper metal during the ionization process.25

UiO-66-NH2 is a very promising heterogeneous catalyst, which has a large specific surface area, good acid–base stability and visible light response. It has been studied for hydrogen evolution, pollutant degradation, CO2 reduction and for use in organic transformations.26 UIO-66-NH2 and TiO2 are often used as reference catalysts. The UV-Visible diffuse reflections (see ESI Fig. 9†) and cyclic voltammetry curves (see ESI Fig. 10†) show that the bandgap (eV) of 1 is 2.36 eV. The HOMO = −5.28 eV and LUMO = −2.92 eV of 1 can be calculated using the following formula:27EHOMO/eV = −4.44 – Eonset (Ox); ELUMO/eV = −4.44 – Eonset (Red). Therefore, 1 is a very good photocatalyst, which photocatalytically degrades MB, when compared with UiO-66-NH2 and TiO2 in solutions of pH 3–9. The amount of the photocatalytic degradation of MB is greater than that adsorbed (see ESI Fig. 11 and 12†). Among them, under pH = 3 conditions, the rate constant of the photocatalytic degradation of MB in time units are 0.0737 mg L−1 min−1, 0.0574 mg L−1 min−1 and 0.0144 mg L−1 min−1, respectively. The zeta potential vs. pH curves (see ESI Table 1†) of 1, UiO-66-NH2 and TiO2 show that their zeta potentials at pH = 3 are 38.52 mV, 27.51 mV and 18.84 mV, respectively. The quantum efficiency of the photocatalytic reaction is controlled by two key processes:28 (1) the competition between the e−/h+ composite and the captured process on the catalyst surface by organic pollutants; (2) the increases in both the electron–hole recombination time and the interfacial electron transfer rate lead to an increase in the quantum efficiency. The increase in the surface acidity accelerates the transfer of photogenerated electrons from the conduction band of the catalyst to the acidic center of the surface, which increases the rate of electron transfer between the interfaces and also inhibits electron–hole recombination. So, the greater the surface charge, the higher the photocatalytic quantum efficiency is. After the photocatalysis was completed, the powder of 1 was collected, and the PXRD patterns showed that its structure remained unchanged (see ESI Fig. 13†). To investigate the performance stability of 1, recycling experiments of MB decomposition at pH = 7 were performed, and the corresponding results (see ESI Fig. 14†) show that the efficiency of photocatalyst 1 was nearly 100% for the first cycle and remained above 98% even after five cycles.

The photocatalytic mechanism of 1 was studied by electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) in situ (see ESI Fig. 15†) under 175 W high-pressure mercury lamp radiation. The strongest resonance signal appeared at 322.9 mT and had a very large g factor of 2.21. Generally, isoelectronic ions in similar symmetry environments have similar g factors. A similar profile to that of the EPR signal assigned to the product of the chemical reduction of Cu(ii) was also observed for [Cu8O(tta)12](ClO4)2·2H2O.29 This indicates that the chemical oxidation of Cu(i) to Cu(ii) occurred in the process of photocatalytic degradation of MB by 1. To further investigate the decomposition process of the photocatalytic degradation of MB by 1, the energy structure of each atom in MB was calculated using the dmol3 package based on density functional theory (DFT) of the Materials Studio 5.5 software. According to the frontier orbital theory,30 atoms with higher 2FED2HOMO values are more likely to lose electrons and those with higher FEDHOMO2 + FEDLUMO2 values are more susceptible to ˙OH attack. The DFT calculation results (see ESI Table 2†) show that the N(14) position in the MB molecule has the highest 2FEDHOMO2 value, which makes it easier to lose electrons at this position. At the same time, the highest FEDHOMO2 + FEDLUMO2 values were found in the C(4) and C(7) positions of the MB molecule, which means that these sites are more vulnerable to ˙OH attack. This shows that the MB molecule easily cleaves at the C(4) or C(7) positions to form diphenylsulfoxide. The decomposition process of MB was also confirmed by ESI-MS spectrometry. Four signals (see ESI Fig. 16†) attributed to [C16H18N3S]+, [C16H22N3SO]+, [C6H9N2SO8]+ and [C4H6NO6]+ were observed. The ESI-MS results were combined with computational simulations, and the possible photocatalytic mechanism and degradation pathways are shown in Fig. 3.

In summary, we have synthesized a stable one-dimensional copper(i) nanorod polymer [Cu(L)2·(CN)]n (1) in situ by the C–C bond cleavage of acetonitrile. The C–C bond cleavage mechanism of acetonitrile and the mechanism of the photocatalytic degradation of methylene blue (MB) by 1 was analyzed using EPR and ESI-MS. In combination with previous studies,20–22 it can be found that the hollow metal ion sites in the complexes play a major role in the catalytic C–C bond cleavage, or temperatures higher than 120 °C if there are coordination water molecules in the complexes. Ongoing studies are aimed at elucidating the factors that enable this process. This method of using low-toxicity acetonitrile as a raw material to introduce –CN by metal ion-catalyzed C–C cleavage provides a new methodology for the in situ synthesis of –CN complexes.
