Introduction

In recent years, supramolecular chemistry of coordination compounds has attracted increasing interest due to their versatile structural diversity and their potential applications in different areas from catalysis to nonlinear optics.1 Since many of the bulk properties of molecular materials are dictated by the manner in which the molecules are ordered in the solid state, it is clear that the ability to control this ordering would afford control over these properties. Factors that play an important role in controlling the architecture of self-assembled species include: the metal ion, which has a more or less preferred and predictable coordination geometry; the ligand, which not only provides capability for selective metal ion coordination but can also provide potential interaction sites to generate desired non-covalent intermolecular interactions and accordingly, can direct crystal packing,2 and counterions,3 in charged complexes and the reaction conditions.4 Supramolecular chemists have a large toolbox of intermolecular interactions, such as hydrogen bonds,5 π⋯π stacking6 and secondary bonding interactions,7 which allow them to construct complex structures with predetermined connectivities from individual building blocks. Secondary bonding interactions (SBIs), schematically described as M⋯X, occur between a heavy element center and, for example, nitrogen, oxygen, sulfur and halogen atoms. This type of interaction is common in compounds of heavy elements (metals and nonmetals in the bottom rows of the periodic table) and can be regarded as a particular case of donor–acceptor bonds.8 Although SBIs have been known for a long time, progress in utilizing this type of intermolecular bonding to construct metallosupramolecular assemblies has only been made in the last few years.9 These studies reveal an undeniable contribution of such interactions to the organization and stabilization of the resultant crystal structures.

In order to further investigate the role of SBIs in the context of metallosupramolecular chemistry, a series of N-(2-halophenyl)-2-pyrazinecarboxamide ligands, L2-F, L2-Cl, L2-Br and L2-IScheme 1, carrying different halogen atoms in the phenylortho-position have been employed for the synthesis of mercury(ii) complexes. Twelve Hg(ii) coordination compounds, [(L2-F)–HgCl2⋯(L2-F)], 1, [HgCl2(L2-Cl)2]n, 2, [HgCl2(L2-Br)2]n, 3, [HgCl2(L2-I)2]n, 4, [HgBr2(L2-F)2]n, 5, [(L2-Cl)–HgBr2⋯(L2-Cl)], 6, [HgBr2(L2-Br)2]n, 7, [HgBr2(L2-I)]n, 8, [(L2-F)–HgI2⋯(L2-F)], 9, [(L2-Cl)–HgI2⋯(L2-Cl)], 10, [(L2-Br)–HgI2⋯(L2-Br)]n, 11, and [HgI2(L2-I)], 12, (uncoordinated ligand shown with a dotted line in the formula) have been prepared by the reaction of the ligand and HgX2 (X = Cl, Br, I) in a 1 : 1 ratio, Scheme 1. Evaluation of the supramolecular architectures of the mercury(ii) N-halophenylpyrazine amide coordination compounds

Structural analysis reveals that many of the differences in the structural motifs of the titled compounds can be attributed to the different halide anions and also the different halogen substituents of the L2-Xligands. So, in the following discussion, first, we will focus on the controlling effects of the coordinated anion on the structural motif of the resultant coordination compounds and then the effect of the different halogen substituents of the N-halophenylpyrazine amide ligands will be discussed.

Influence of the halide anion on the coordination geometry and supramolecular assembly

Anions may play different roles in crystals depending on whether they act as a coordinated anion or a discrete one.18 These effects have been studied by us and other research groups.3,19 Thus, the selection of the anion is very important in determining the topology of the coordination network. Interestingly, structural analysis of mercury(ii) N-(2-halophenyl)-2-pyrazinecarboxamide coordination compounds clearly show that by replacing the coordinated anions from chloride with bromide and iodide in each series containing the same ligand, the coordination geometry and structural motif of the resultant compound is dramatically affected.

In compounds 1, 5 and 9, where the carboxamideligand, L2-F, is similar, the coordination environment around Hg(ii) changes from pseudo square pyramidal in 1 to distorted octahedral in 5 and a pseudo seesaw in 9. As listed in Table 3, compounds 1 and 9 show dimeric and monomeric structures, respectively, while compound 5 has a 1D linear chain structure. For complexes 2, 6 and 10, which containing the L2-Clligand with HgCl2, HgBr2 and HgI2, the coordination sphere changes from distorted octahedral for 2 to a pseudo seesaw for 6 and 10. Also, the structural motifs in these compounds are quite different and the 1D linear chain in 2 changed to a monomeric structure in 6 and 10. The coordination geometries of the Hg(ii) centers in the HgX2 complexes of L2-Br are approximately similar considering the secondary-bonded L2-Brligands in the immediate coordination environment of the metal center in 11. The coordination polymers could be geometrically analyzed12 with the help of the angles ψ (a), ω (b), and θ (c), Fig. 13. The values of these parameters are summarized in Table 3 for the polymeric complexes synthesized here. Noticeably, approximately similar values of ψ occurred in 3 and 7, but ψ is distributed over a broader range of angles in 11. Also, unlike in 3 and 7, the two θ angles are further apart in 11, Table 3. These features, along with the highly distorted octahedral geometry of the metal centers in 11, confirm the deformation of [(L2-Br)⋯HgI2⋯(L2-Br)]n from an ideal dimensional state in which each metal center has near octahedral geometry, Table 3. For the HgI2 adducts with the L2-Icarboxamideligand, from 4 to 8 and to 12, the coordination sphere changes from distorted octahedral in 4 to distorted square pyramidal in 8 and T-shaped in 12. As listed in Table 3, compounds 4 and 8 show 1D linear chain structures, while compound 12 is a discrete mononuclear complex. Additionally, in all the isostructural coordination polymers2, 3, 4, 5, 7 and 11 reported here, infinite linear mercury chains (Hg⋯Hg⋯Hg angle is 180°) with different Hg⋯Hg intrachain distances from 3.887 Å to 4.430 Å are observed. Influence of the different halogen substituents of the L2-Xligands on the coordination geometry and supramolecular assembly

The chemical structure of the organic ligand, even a slight in the substitute position, can play an important role in controlling the supramolecular organization of molecular complexes.20 The principle of steric control over secondary bonding interactions and thereby molecular association has been nicely studied by Tiekink,21 Vargas-Baca,22 Johnson23 and others.24 Accordingly, in this work, some of the other differences in the structural motifs can be explained by the changing of the halogen substituent of the L2-Xligands while the anion remains constant.

Based on the same anion, 2, 3, and 4 are isostructural. Structural analysis of the HgCl2 complexes of the L2-Xligands reveal that the octahedral geometry around the metal ion becomes more distorted as the halogen substituent is varied from Cl to I. Comparison of the ψ, θ and ω angles in coordination polymers2, 3, and 4 also clearly implies this trend. On going from chlorine to iodine, the ω angle between the Npyz⋯Hg vector and the main plane containing the pyrazine ring of a non-coordinated L2-Xligand decreases notably and a somewhat broader range is observed for the ψ and θ angles, Fig. 13 and Table 3. In the case of isostructural 5 and 7, the same trend for ω is observed but the trends for ψ and θ are inconsistent with what we have found above and this is probably due to the poor quality of the crystal and therefore, the low precision of our data for 7.

The role of secondary bonding in the supramolecular association of mercury(ii) N-(2-halophenyl)-2-pyrazinecarboxamide coordination compounds

Understanding the role of secondary bonding interactions (SBI) is a current major challenge for the rational design of metallosupramolecular assemblies.7 These interactions are weaker than covalent or dative bonds but are strong enough to play a determining role in the resulting solid-state structures.7,8 Systematic investigation of closely related crystal structures is necessary to find the determining factors affecting the presence/absence of this type of interaction. By adopting this strategy, it has been revealed that by the judicious choice of metal-bound and/or ligand-bound substituents, it is possible to tune (turned on or off) secondary interactions and therefore, the ultimate supramolecular architecture.21f Accepting this, the question arises as to whether such synthons are robust enough and thereby, whether they can be used as a tool for engineering supramolecular assemblies? A number of attempts have been made to answer this question by using different structural units which can be assembled via this intermolecular interaction9d,e,21–24 but further studies should be done to clarify its inherent complexities. To make progress in this regard, the effect of the different halogen substituents on the phenyl ring of the phenylpyrazinamideligand and the different halogen anions on the coordination geometry and supramolecular architecture of twelve coordination compounds of mercury(ii) has been studied in this work. Our study reveals that Hg⋯Npyz intermolecular interactions were observed in five of the twelve mercury(ii) complexes reported here. It is interesting to compare the assembly of these complexes with respect to the growing steric bulk of the ligand substituent and metal-bound halide. Mercury(ii) complexes based on the L2-Fligand, [(L2-F)–HgCl2⋯(L2-F)], 1 and [(L2-F)–HgI2⋯(L2-F)], 9, show Hg⋯Npyz secondary bonding with 2.870(9) and 2.984(7) Å distances from the metal center, respectively. The defined φ angle (similar to the previously defined angle25) increases significantly from 12.02° in 1 to 14.27° in 9. This clearly shows how the Hg⋯Npyz SBI is weakened by increasing the size of the halide anions. The aforementioned SBI is also observed in the isostructural HgBr2 and HgI2 complexes of the L2-Clligand. The Hg⋯Npyz separation in [(L2-Cl)–HgBr2⋯(L2-Cl)], 6, is 2.919(7) Å (φ = 20.30°) and in [(L2-Cl)–HgI2⋯(L2-Cl)], 10, the analogous separation is 3.228(8) Å (φ = 28.89°), Tables 3 and 4. The geometrical analysis of 11 and its related isostructural coordination polymers were discussed above. As mentioned earlier, besides the Hg⋯Npyz distance, another contributing factor to secondary bond strength might be the φ angle, Fig. 13. The φ angle of the secondary bonded compounds investigated here varies between 12.02° and 28.89°, Table 4. In view of the above discussion, one can draw a general rule: the stronger the secondary bond, the stronger the trend for linearity will be. Cambridge Structural Database (CSD) analysis further endorses this notion. Fig. 14 shows a plot of the φ angle vs. Hg⋯N distances. This scattergram shows how interatomic distances between the Hg ion and ligand can vary substantially from a dative to a secondary bond interaction. The distribution of the data points clearly indicates that at shorter Hg⋯N distances, there is a strong tendency toward linearity and hence, the metal center tends to keep the secondary bond axis collinear with its primary bond. Also, the binding energies obtained from the DFT calculations with a correction for the basis set superposition error (BSSE) on two relative fragments (from complexes 1, 6, 9, 10 and 11) provide us with an opportunity to evaluate the secondary bonding interaction between the [(L2-X)–HgX2] unit and non-coordinating L2-Xligand. The selected fragments were cut out directly from the CIF data and are shown in Fig. S5, ESI.† The outcomes obtained from the DFT methods are listed in Table 4. The results in Table 4 show that the interaction energy of [(L2-Cl)–HgBr2⋯(L2-Cl)], 6, is greater than for [(L2-Cl)–HgI2⋯(L2-Cl)], 10, in good agreement with the geometrical and statistical analysis. For the two L2-F HgX2 complexes, 1 and 9, [(L2-F)–HgCl2⋯(L2-F)], 1, has only a slightly greater interaction energy than [(L2-F)–HgI2⋯(L2-F)], 9. These results also show that the HgI2 complex with two L2-Brligands in the coordination environment of each Hg center has the greatest interaction energy of the study set. Also, the decomposition behaviors of these compounds were investigated in a static air atmosphere from ambient conditions to 500 °C. Stacked TG curves of compounds 1, 6, 9, 10 and 11 are given in Fig. S6 (ESI†). The thermogravimetric analyses reveal that these compounds have identical single-step decomposition patterns. In these compounds, which contain no solvent molecules in the crystal lattice, continuous weight losses were observed after 219 °C, 238 °C, 221 °C, 233 °C and 182 °C for 1, 6, 9, 10 and 11, respectively, due to structural decompositions.

Conclusions

The supramolecular organization of mercury(ii) complexes based on L2-Xligands (L = N-(2-halophenyl)-2-pyrazinecarboxamide) using different halogen substituents on the ortho-position of the phenyl ring in the L2-X ligand framework and different metal-bound halides has been studied. This study clearly shows that halide anions and remote ligand substituents can lead to diversity in Hg(ii) coordination modes and supramolecular organizations. It can be anticipated that the metal-bound and ligand-bound substituent steric factors cannot be the only factor dictating the presence/absence of Hg⋯Npyz SBIs but it will certainly play an important role in this regard. In addition, although the variation in the size of the halogen substituent on the phenyl ring of the L2-Xligand occurs far from the metal coordination sphere, the findings indicate that remote ligand substituents are not innocent in the assembly process and should be considered when one is dealing with SBIs in these mercury(ii) coordination systems and similar systems. Finally, the cooperation between these steric factors goes so far as to overcome the tendency of mercury to adopt coordination polymer or secondary-bonded structures and instead, discrete supramolecular [HgI2(L2-I)] formed.

Experimental section

General

All the chemicals were purchased from Aldrich or Merck and used without further purification. The synthesis and recrystallization of L2-F, L2-Cl, L2-Br and L2-I and the mercury(ii) complexes 1–12 were carried out in air. The 1H NMR spectra were recorded on a Bruker AC-300 MHz spectrometer at ambient temperature in CD3OD. All the chemical shifts are quoted in parts per million (ppm) relative to tetramethylsilane. The infrared spectra (4000–250 cm−1) of solid samples were taken as 1% dispersions in CsI pellets using a BOMEM-MB102 spectrometer. Elemental analysis was performed using a Heraeus CHN-O Rapid analyzer. The melting points were obtained by a Bamstead Electrothermal type 9200 melting point apparatus and corrected. All the ligands were prepared according to the reported procedure.

Single crystal diffraction studies

For all the crystals apart from 3, the intensity data were collected on a STOE IPDS-II or IPDS-2T diffractometer with graphite monochromated Mo–Kα radiation (λ) 0.71073 (Å). The data were collected in a series of ω scans in 1° oscillations and integrated using the Stöe X-AREA26a software package. A numerical absorption correction was applied using the X-RED26b and X-SHAPE26c software. The X-ray data for compound 3 was collected using a Bruker SMART APEX-II CCD diffractometer equipped with fine focus 1.75 kW sealed tube Mo–Kα radiation (λ) 0.71073 (Å). The total number of images was based on the results from the program COSMO.27a Cell parameters were retrieved using the APEX II software27b and refined using SAINT on all observed reflections. Data reduction was performed using the SAINT software,27c which corrects for Lorentz and Polarizing effects. Scaling and absorption corrections were applied using the SADABS27d multi-scan technique, supplied by George Sheldrick. All the structures were solved by direct methods using SHELXS-9728 and refined with full-matrix least-squares on F2 using the SHELXL-9728 program package. All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. Hydrogen atoms were added at ideal positions and constrained to ride on their parent atoms, with Uiso(H) = 1.2Ueq. All the refinements were performed using the X-STEP32 crystallographic software package.29 Structural illustrations have been drawn with MERCURY30 windows. Computational details

DFT calculations were performed using the ORCA quantum chemistry suite.31 The local spin density (LSD) exchange correlation potential was used with the local density approximation of the correlation energy.32 Gradient-corrected geometry optimizations33 were performed using the generalized gradient approximation (Perdew–Wang non-local exchange and correlation corrections-PW91).34 The selected two fragments, Fig. S5, ESI,† were cut directly from the CIF data without optimization. Large atom TZP basis sets were used to ascribe all the atoms here. Scalar relativistic effects were taken into account by using the zeroth-order regular approximation (ZORA).35
