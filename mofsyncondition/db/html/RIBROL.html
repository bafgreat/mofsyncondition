Introduction

Oxygen evolution reactions (OERs) are involved in a wide range of electrochemical processes, for example, water electrolysis, chlorine evolution, electroplating, peroxide production, etc., and thus have drawn a great deal of continuing research attention.1 In particular, the production of hydrogen and oxygen from water electrolysis is a potential route to produce clean energy sources. However, the high overpotentials required in the OERs reduce the energy efficiency of the OER-involved electrochemical processes. Consequently, various electrocatalysts have been developed to minimize this overpotential.2 In aqueous acidic and neutral media, mixtures of oxides of ruthenium, titanium, tantalum and iridium are active catalysts.3,4 The most studied molecular OER catalysts have been ruthenium complexes,5 since the discovery of Meyer's original “blue dimer”,6 where two Ru ions are bound through oxo bridges and coordinated by polypyridyl ligands. First-row transition-metal complexes with coordination geometries analogous to those of ruthenium-based OER catalysts have also been investigated and they have shown catalytic activity.7,8

In recent years, there is great interest in metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) not only because of their intriguing variety of structures and topologies, but also due to their interesting properties and potential applications in ion exchange, catalysis, and optical, magnetic and porous materials.9 However, until now MOFs have been rarely explored as electrocatalysts for generating O2 from water.

Base on the situation, we synthesized a novel ditopic N-donor ligand, 1,4-bis(3-pyridylaminomethyl)benzene (L),10 in which the rigid pyridine ring and flexible –CH2–NH– coexist (Scheme 1). It is expected that the pyridine ring and –CH2–NH– moiety of L are stable toward oxidative deactivation under water oxidation conditions, and L is a good candidate for the construction of an electrocatalyst based on a MOF toward the OER in view of the long-term stability. Herein, based on L and two carboxylic coligands, adipic acid (H2adip), and 5-nitroisophthalic acid (5-H2bdc), two novel metal complexes formulated as Co(L)0.5(adip) (1) and Co2(L)2(5-bdc)2(H2O)2·H2O (2) are obtained via a hydro-(solvo)thermal technique in moderate yields. Their cyclic voltammograms (CVs), electrochemical impedance spectroscopies (EISs), controlled potential electrolysis (CPE) experiments, thermal stabilities and UV-Vis absorption spectra have been investigated.

Experimental

General considerations

All chemicals purchased were of reagent grade and used without further purification. 1H spectra were recorded using Bruker 400 MHz apparatus with tetramethylsilane (TMS) as the internal standard. The melting point was determined using uncorrected X-4 melting point apparatus from the Beijing Kaifu Company. C, H, N elemental analyses were performed using an Elementar Vario MICRO E III analyzer. IR spectra were recorded as KBr pellets using a Perkin Elmer spectrometer. The powder XRD (PXRD) data were collected using a RIGAKU DMAX2500PC diffractometer using Cu Kα radiation. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was performed using a NETZSCH5 STA 449C thermogravimetric analyzer in flowing N2 with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. UV-visspectra were measured using a HITACHI U-4100 UV-visspectrophotometer.

Electrochemical measurements

The electrochemical measurements were done in a three-electrode test cell. A saturated calomel electrode (SCE) and a platinum foil were used as the reference and counter electrode, respectively. The working electrode was prepared as follows: an acetone dispersion of 4 mg sample (1 mL) and 0.05 mL of nafion solution were deposited on a glassy carbon electrode and the solvent was dried by an IR lamp. The electroactive area of the glassy carbon electrode is 0.2 cm2. All the measurements were recorded in 30 mL of an N2 degassed solution at 25 °C. Cyclic voltammograms were tested in 30 mL of an N2 degassed solution containing 0.2 M phosphate buffer (pH 6.8) using a Shiruisi RST5200 electrochemical workstation. Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) measurements were performed with Autolab Frequency Response Analyser (FRA) software between 1 MHz and 1 Hz. The generated O2 volume during electrolysis was quantitatively and qualitatively analyzed using a GC instrument (Shimadzu GC-8A) equipped with a 1 m column filled with Molecular Sieve 13X using He as the carrier gas and a GC-MS instrument (Shimadzu GCMS-QP 1000EX), respectively.

The synthesis of Co(L)0.5(adip) (1)

A mixture of Co(NO3)2·6H2O (0.1 mmol, 0.029 g), H2adip (0.05 mmol, 0.007 g), L (0.05 mmol, 0.014 g), water (6 mL), ethanol (2 mL), NH3.H2O (0.4 mL) and DMF (0.1 mL) (DMF = N,N′-dimethylformamide), was sealed in a Teflon-lined autoclave and heated at 120 °C for 3 days. This was followed by slow cooling to room temperature. The resulting red prismatic crystals were filtered off (yield: ca. 45% based on Co). Elemental anal. Found: C, 51.71; H, 4.89; N, 8.06%. Calcd for C15H17CoN2O4: C, 51.74; H, 4.92; N, 8.04%. IR (KBr, cm−1): 3412(s), 3037(w), 2939(s), 2849(m), 1928(m), 1604(s), 1500(s), 1417(s), 1309(m), 1130(w), 798(w), 700(m).

The synthesis of Co2(L)2(5-bdc)2(H2O)2·H2O (2)

The synthesis of complex 2 was carried out as described above for complex 1 except that 5-H2bdc (0.05 mmol, 0.010 g) was used instead of H2adip (0.05 mmol, 0.007 g). The yield of the red prismatic crystals is ca. 35% based on Co. Elemental anal. Found: C, 53.36; H, 4.11; N, 11.98%. Calcd for C52H48Co2N10O15: C, 53.34; H, 4.13; N, 11.96%. X-ray crystallography

Single-crystal X-ray data for the three complexes were collected using a Bruker-APEX CCD area detector-equipped diffractometer using graphite monochromated Mo Kα (λ = 0.71073 Å) radiation at room temperature. Empirical absorption correction was applied. The structures were solved by direct methods and refined by the full-matrix least-squares methods on F2 using the SHELXTL-97 software.11 All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. All of the hydrogen atoms were placed in the calculated positions. The crystal data and structure refinements for complexes 1 and 2 are summarized in Table 1. Selected bond lengths and angles for complexes 1 and 2 are listed in Table S1 in the ESI.† The CCDC reference numbers are the following: 928986 for complex 1 and 928987 for complex 2.

Results and discussion

Crystal structure of Co(L)0.5(adip) (1)

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that complex 1 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P (Table 1). In complex 1, the asymmetric unit contains one Co(ii), half L and one adipic dianion. The crystallographically independent Co(1) is unsaturated coordinated and exhibits a slightly distorted tetragonal pyramidal geometry supplied by one N atom from one L [Co–N 2.065(3) Å] and four O atoms of four carboxylate groups from four adip2− [Co–O 2.013(3)–2.119(3) Å] (Fig. 1 and Table S1†). The crystallographically independent adip2− show a bis-bidentate coordination mode, and two Co(ii) ions are linked by four carboxylate groups from four adip2− to form a Co2 unit with a Co⋯ Co separation of 2.7876(10) Å (Fig. 1a). Different Co2 units are further connected by two strands of adip2− into a one-dimensional (1D) chain (Fig. 1a). In complex 1, the two pyridine rings of the crystallographically independent Lligand are parallel, and the dihedral angles between the phenyl ring and the two pyridine rings of L are 85.4°, indicating that the phenyl moiety is almost perpendicular to the two terminal pyridine rings. L acts as a μ2-bridge to connect the 1D chains to form a two-dimensional (2D) layer (Fig. 1b). As shown in Fig. 1a and b, each Co2 unit is connected to four neighboring units through two L and two pairs of adip2−ligands, thus the Co2 unit can be considered as a 4-connected node. Each L and adip2− link two Co2 units, and they can be viewed as a 2-connected node and are not counted as node topologically.12a Topological analysis12b of this complex reveals that it is a uninodal 4-connected non-interpenetrated sql/Shubnikov tetragonal plane net with {44.62} topology (Fig. 1c).

The electrochemical property of complex 1

The phase purities of complexes 1 and 2 were supported by the powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns of the bulk samples, which are consistent with the calculated patterns (Fig. S1†). Fig. 2 shows the cyclic voltammograms (CVs) of the 1-modified electrode (1-GCE) in an aqueous buffer solution (pH = 6.8, H3PO4–KOH, 0.2 M) in the range from −1.5 to 1.5 V vs. the saturated calomel electrode (SCE) at different sweep scan rates. For comparison, the electrochemical behaviour at the bare carbon glassy electrode (GCE) was also studied. As shown in Fig. 3, when the scan rate was 0.01 V s−1, an irreversible peak at +1.49 V vs.SCE was observed at the bare GCE, which coincides with the evolution of bubbles, which correspond to the generation of O2 from water. As we know, in a 0.2 M phosphate buffer solution (pH = 6.8), the theoretical potential for the O2 evolution reaction (OER) on a clean Pt electrode is 0.59 V vs. SCE, indicating that the overpotential (η) at the bare GCE for the OER is +0.90 V (Fig. 3).

When the 0.2 M phosphate buffer solution contains 4 mg H2adip, a quasi-reversible redox couple with a half-wave overpotential of −0.70 V (Epa = −0.32 V and Epc = −1.08 V vs. SCE, Epa: anodic peak overpotential, Epc: cathodic peak overpotential) was observed when the scan rate was 0.01 V s−1, which is probably ascribed to the redox of the CO bond of H2adip (Fig. S2†). It was found that the irreversible OER at the bare GCE was shifted to a lower potential (+1.45 V vs.SCE, overpotential η = +0.86 V) and the oxidation current was enhanced (Fig. S2 and 4†), indicating that the free H2adipligand can catalyze the OER. It is expected that H2adip might act as an electron-transfer promoter under the conditions.13

When H2adip was replaced by free L in the solution, a set of quasi-reversible anodic and cathodic peak overpotentials at Epa = −0.61 V and Epc = −1.09 V were found (Fig. S3†), which are probably related to the C–N bond of L. The irreversible OER overpotential at the bare GCE was shifted left (+1.40 V vs. SCE, overpotential η = +0.81 V) with enhanced current in the presence of L (Fig. S3 and S4†), inferring that the free L also can catalyze the OER.

When the 1-modified electrode (1-GCE) was utilized, a quasi-reversible redox couple with a half-wave overpotential of −1.07 V (Epa = −1.04 V and Epc = −1.10 V) was observed at the scan rate of 0.01 V s−1, which may be assigned to the Co-based redox of complex 1 (Fig. 2).14 The OER potential in the 0.2 M phosphate buffer solution is obviously shifted to a lower potential (+1.05 V vs. SCE, overpotential η = 0.46 V) which is accompanied with the markedly enhanced current, suggesting that complex 1 has a good electrocatalytic activity for the OER (Fig. 2 and 4).15 The electrocatalytic current grows larger with an increasing scan rate in the presence of complex 1 (Fig. 2). Complex 1 can lower the overpotential of the OER, which is also evidenced by the Tafel curve at 1-GCE in the phosphate buffer solution (Fig. S6†).

Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) is considered a powerful tool in probing the processes that occur at the electrode–electrolyte interface, and is often used as a complementary technique to cyclic voltammetry.16Fig. 5a presents the Nyquist plots for 1-GCE and the bare GCE. The impedance spectra of the electrodes were satisfactorily fitted with the modified Randles equivalent electrical circuit (Fig. 5d). As we know, the Randles circuit is one of the simplest common models used to describe the phenomenon of an electrochemical reaction at the electrode surface. Based on the assumption that the current that corresponds to the electrochemical reaction is treated as an additive to the double layer charging current, this results in the presence of an additional impedance, electron-transfer resistance (Rct). This model consists of an electrolyte resistance (Rs) connected in series with the parallel combination of double layer capacitance (Cdl) and Rct.17 As the electrode surface lacks homogeneity, such as that caused by surface roughness (Fig. S5†) or by non-uniformly distributed properties, the impedance spectra of the ideal polarizable electrode are deformed, so constant phase element (CPE) frequency is used in the model.17 As shown in Fig. 5c, the phase angles are not 90 °C, which are deformed from an ideal capacitive impedance.17

The Nyquist plot of a Randles circuit is a semi-circle arc. The value of Rs is estimated from intercept of this semi-circle at the real axis part at high frequency. The real axis value at a low frequency is the sum of Rs and Rct, indicating that the diameter of the semi-circle is equal to Rct.17Fig. 5b and c show the Bode plots for the circuit. Rs and the sum of Rs and Rct also can be observed from the magnitude plot in the high and low frequency regions, respectively (Fig. 5b).

As shown in Fig. 5a and b, the Rct value of 1-GCE is less than that of the bare GCE, indicating that the electron transfer is promoted in the presence of complex 1, which is related to the ligand and framework of the complex.13

As shown in Fig. 5a, the slope of the impedance curve in the low frequency region for 1-GCE is higher than that for the bare GCE, implying that the Rs value in the presence of complex 1 is more than that for the blank system,18 which is also proved by the Bode plots in Fig. 5b. However, the sum of Rs and Rct in the presence of complex 1 is less than that in the blank system (Fig. 5b). Therefore, the enhanced current of the OER at 1-GCE is probably related to the framework of complex 1, which promotes the electron-transfer under the conditions.13,17

A controlled potential electrolysis (CPE) experiment at +1.4 V vs. SCE (η = +0.81 V and current density = 1.65 mA cm−2) was also performed to provide evidence for the catalytic activity of complex 1. As depicted in Fig. S8,†1-GCE affords a robust and essentially linear charge build-up over time, with no substantial loss in activity over the course of 2 h, which shows higher charge build-up than the bare GCE under the same conditions (η = +0.81 V and current density = 0.32 mA cm−2) (Fig. S8†). Moreover, 1-GCE is stable. When the potential range is maintained at −1.5 to 1.5 V vs. SCE, the peak currents can be kept over hundreds of cycles. The stability of the electrocatalyst of complex 1 is also proved by the powder X-ray diffractions (PXRD) of the samples obtained after 1h-CPE and 2h-CPE, which are similar to that of the original compound, as shown in Fig. S1a,† though the morphologies of 1-GCE after 1h-CPE and 2h-CPE are a bit different from that of the original electrode (Fig. S5a†).

To estimate the Faradaic efficiency for O2 production by complex 1, the evolved O2 in the 2h-CPE experiment is characterized and proved by gas chromatography and GC-MS measurements. The generated O2 volume matches that calculated based on the amount of charge consumed, indicating that the catalyst operates at 96% Faradaic efficiency (see ESI†). Based on the amount of complex 1 used in the experiment, a turnover number (TON) of 2.44 mol of O2 per mole of catalyst is calculated.

The crystal structure of Co2(L)2(5-bdc)2(H2O)2·H2O (2)

In order to further investigate the relationship between the electrocatalytic property of the MOF toward the OER and its structure, another Co(ii)-based MOF was synthesized. Complex 2 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P with one Co(ii), one 5-bdc2−, one L, one coordinated water and half of an uncoordinated water in the asymmetric unit (Table 1). Two O atoms from the –NO2 group of 5-bdc2− are disordered over two locations (O5 and O5′, O6 and O6′) and their occupancy factors are 0.54(O5), 0.46(O5′), 0.75(O6) and 0.25(O6′), respectively. Co(1) exhibits a distorted octahedral coordination geometry supplied by two N atoms from two Lligands [Co–N 2.185(11)–2.190(11) Å], three O atoms of one monodentate bridging and one chelating carboxylate groups from two 5-bdc2− and one oxygen atom from the terminal aqua ligand [Co–O 2.062(8)–2.230(8) Å] (Fig. 6a and b and Table S1†). Due to the rotation of the flexible C–C and C–N single bonds, the crystallographically independent Lligand in complex 2 shows a different conformation from that in complex 1. The two pyridine rings of L are not parallel to each other in complex 2 (dihedral angle: 54.0°). The dihedral angles between the phenyl ring and the two pyridine rings are 68.4 and 54.0°, respectively (Fig. 6a). Two L ligands link two Co(ii) centers into a quadrangular cage with dimensions of ca. 12.0 × 10.6 Å (Fig. 6a). In complex 2, the 5-bdc2−ligand connects two Co(ii) ions via one chelating and one monodentate bridging carboxylate groups, thus the quadrangular cages are linked by two strands of 5-bdc2−ligands into 1D chain (Fig. 6b).

The different chains are linked by strong hydrogen bonds and π–π stacking interactions into a 3D supramolecular architecture (Fig. 6c). For example, H3–N3: 0.86 Å, H3⋯O4A: 2.491 Å, N3⋯O4A: 3.242 Å, ∠N3–H3⋯O4A: 146.28°; H5–N5: 0.86 Å, H5⋯O6′B: 2.342 Å, N5⋯O6′B: 3.199 Å, ∠N2–H2⋯O4: 174.77°. Phenyl rings 1 and 2 are constructed by C3–C8 and C11D–C16D, respectively. The centroid–centroid and perpendicular distances between the two planes are 3.793 and 3.657 Å, respectively (atoms with additional labels A, B and D refer to the symmetry operations: A x − 1, y − 1, z; B x, y − 1, z; D: x + 1, y + 1, z).

The electrochemical property of complex 2

The electrocatalytic activity of complex 2 toward generating O2 from water was also evaluated under similar conditions. As shown in Fig. 7, a quasi-reversible redox couple appeared at Epa = −0.90 V and Epc = −0.40 V when the scan rate was 0.01 V s−1, which may be assigned to the Co-based redox of complex 2.14 The OER potential at the 2-modified electrode (2-GCE) is shifted to 1.16 V vs. SCE (overpotential η = +0.57 V) which is accompanied with the enhanced current (Fig. 4 and 7), indicating that complex 2 also can catalyze the generation of O2 from water. Complex 2 can lower the overpotential of the OER, which is also evidenced by the Tafel curve at 2-GCE in the neutral buffer solution (Fig. S6†). For the free 5-H2bdc, when the scan rate is 0.01 V s−1, the quasi-reversible redox couple at Epa = −0.66 V and Epc = −0.62 V, and the irreversible peaks at −1.26 and −1.05 V in the CV may be assigned to the redox of the –NO2 group of 5-H2bdc (Fig. S9†). The OER potential was observed at 1.33 V vs. SCE (overpotential η = +0.74 V) and the oxidation current is enhanced compared with that in the blank experiment (Fig. 4 and S10†), indicating that the free 5-H2bdc also can catalyze the OER. In the presence of complex 2, the OER current was also proportional to the scan rate (Fig. 7). The EIS measurement shows that the Rct value of 2-GCE is less than that of the bare GCE (Fig. 5a and b), indicating that the electron transfer is also promoted in the presence of complex 2. The Rs value in the presence of complex 2 is more than that for the blank system. The enhanced current for the OER at 2-GCE is probably related to the framework of complex 2. The catalytic activity of complex 2 is also evidenced by the CPE experiment at +1.4 V vs.SCE (η = +0.81 V and current density = 1.07 mA cm−2), in which 2-GCE shows higher charge build-up than the bare GCE under the same conditions (η = +0.81 V and current density = 0.32 mA cm−2) (Fig. S8†) with the Faradaic efficiency of 95% and TON of 2.73 mol of O2 per mole of complex 2.

The stability of the electrocatalyst of complex 2 is proved by the powder X-ray diffractions (PXRD) of the samples after 1h-CPE and 2h-CPE, which are similar to that of the original compound, as shown in Fig. S1b,† though the color of 2-GCE after 1h-CPE and 2h-CPE changes compared with that of the original electrode (Fig. S5b†).

When comparing complexes 1 and 2, the overpotential of the OER at 1-GCE (+0.46 V) is different from that at 2-GCE (+0.57 V) (Fig. 2, 4 and 7), indicating that the two Co(ii) complexes possess different catalytic activities toward the OER, which is also evidenced by their different Tafel curves and 2h-CPE experiments. The different electrocatalytic properties of the two complexes are probably due to their different frameworks and different ligands in the structures. The detailed mechanism is under investigation.

Thermal stability

In order to examine the thermal stability of complexes 1 and 2, TGA was carried out. The samples were heated to 750 °C in N2. As for complex 1, no weight loss is observed in the temperature range of 30–310 °C, and the red curve shown in Fig. S11,† it is in good agreement with the crystal structure of complex 1, in which no solvent is included. Complex 1 shows one step weight loss in the range of 310–480 °C, which corresponds to the decomposition of the organic components (obsd, 76.4%; calcd, 76.2%) (Fig. S11†).

Complex 2 releases its uncoordinated water in the range of 30–120 °C (observed, 1.3%; calcd, 1.5%), as the blue curve shown in Fig. S11.† The loss of the coordinated water molecules (obsd, 2.9%; calcd, 3.1%) happens in the range of 120–180 °C. The dehydrated sample remains stable up to ∼270 °C without any weight loss. The decomposition and loss of the organic components (obsd, 81.0%; calcd, 81.2%) happens in the range of 180–600 °C (Fig. S11†).

UV-vis absorption spectra

The UV-vis absorption spectra of complexes 1 and 2 together with the free organic ligands in the solid state at room temperature are shown in Fig. S12.† As shown in Fig. S12,†L exhibits an absorption with a maximum at 306 nm in the range of 240–450 nm, which probably corresponds to the n–π* or π–π* transition of the aromatic ring.195-H2bdC displays one intense absorption peak at 300 nm and one shoulder peak at 345 nm in the range of 240–450 nm. As for the free H2adipligand, it exhibits a peak at 269 nm in the range of 200–320 nm, which probably corresponds to the electron transition of the CO bond.20

Complex 1 displays a complex absorption in the range of 240–800 nm with five intense peaks at 250, 324, 455, 570 and 730 nm (Fig. S12†). As for complex 2, it displays absorption with maxima at 253, 318, 520 and 634 nm, respectively. The absorptions of complexes 1 and 2 in the range of 240–450 nm are different from those of the free ligands, inferring that the absorption of complexes 1 and 2 may originate from the intraligand transition (ILCT), ligand-to-ligand change transfer transition (LLCT) and metal-to-ligand charge-transfer transition (MLCT).19 The absorption in the region of 450–800 nm for complexes 1 and 2 may be due to the visible d–d transition.

Conclusion

In conclusion, based on L, two Co(ii) MOFs formulated as Co(L)0.5(adip) (1) and Co2(L)2(5-bdc)2(H2O)2·H2O (2) have been synthesized. Complex 1 features a uninodal 4-connected non-interpenetrated sql/Shubnikov tetragonal plane net with {44.62} topology. In complex 2, two L link two Co(ii) centers into a quadrangular cage and different cages are further connected by two strands of 5-bdc2− to form a 1D chain. The two complexes both exhibit electrocatalytic activities toward generating O2 from water with lowered overpotentials and enhanced currents. The different electrocatalytic properties of the two complexes are probably related with their different ligands and different frameworks. Research into the detailed mechanism is under way.
