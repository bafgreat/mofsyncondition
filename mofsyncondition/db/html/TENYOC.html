In the past decade, there has been much effort devoted to develop novel low molecular-mass organic gelators (LMOGs) and to elucidate their nanostructures and new physical properties.1 Generally, the structure of a gelator is comprised of an apolar segment, such as π-system and long alkyl group, and a polar part, such as amide, urea, hydroxy, etc. The LMOGs self-assemble to form fibrillar networks (SAFINs) to immobilize solvent molecules and form supramolecular gels.2 The driving forces of such self-assembling processes could be specific or non-covalent interactions, such as electrostatic, dipole–dipole, hydrogen bonding (H-bonding), π–π stacking, van der Waals interactions and solvophilic–solvophobic balance. Recently, the use of coordination chemistry as a rational design route toward self-assembled gels with unusual functional properties has attracted considerable interest.3 Coordination interaction becomes a versatile tool for the construction of a self-assembled nanostructure. Since the first report by Shinkai and co-workers,4 a number of related studies have demonstrated that metal coordination is useful to impart new chemical and physical properties to the functionalization of metallogels.5

Although some triterpenoid based LMOGs6 and steroid based metallogels7 were reported, no terpenoid based metallogels have been discovered until now. In this contribution we present a novel and simple amphiphilic low molecular-mass gelator based on alkali metal salts of isosteviol-15-sulfonic acid derivative (4, Fig. 1), an in situ super gelator which can self-assemble into supramolecular organogels by water-assisted alkali metal coordination in different halohydrocarbonsolvents with phase- and solvent-selective properties at room temperature.

Isosteviol is a tetracyclic diterpenoid with a beyerane skeleton, obtained easily by acid hydrolysis of naturally available stevioside.8 Due to its relative low-toxicity, low-cost, rigidity and chiral frame, it has been noted for its applications in bioactivities9 and asymmetric catalysis studies.10 In our initial work, isosteviol-15-sulfonic acid 1 was prepared viasulfonation and salification (Fig. 2) of isosteviol. Firstly, the sulfonic group was introduced stereoselectively to C-15 of isosteviol to give compound 1, and then 1 was treated with sodium hydroxide or sodium carbonate through the normal acid–base reaction to give compound 2 (Scheme S1, ESI†). Surprisingly, it was found that compound 2 could also be formed immediately after treating compound 1 with common sodium salts such as sodium chloride or sodium sulfate, indicating that the easy formation of the weaker acid salt must comprise an assistance of the 16-carbonyl oxygen. Furthermore, treatment of compound 1 with potassium salts displayed similar phenomena whereas lithium salt, alkali earth metal salts as well as transition metal salts did not work under the same conditions.

Single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis of 2 (ref. 11) indicates that the Na(i) ion is in a distorted octahedral geometry (Fig. 3a) coordinated with six oxygen atoms that are from 15-sulfonic group, 16-carbonyl, two water molecules, one ethanol, and one carbonyl oxygen of the carboxyl group in another isosteviol molecule, respectively. With the assistance of the solvent molecules, the self-assembly of 2 led to 1-D right-handed helical supramolecular architectures (Fig. 3b). The coordination of the 16-carbonyl group to Na(i) ion should be the main cause for the easy salification of the sulfonic acid via an ion-exchange with the sodium salt.

So far, transition metal ion coordinated organogels have been reported, however, very little work on alkali metals has been known.7c,12 In view of the special amphiphilic structure of compoud 2 and its coordination behaviors, we were enlightened to probe into its gelation abilities. Compound 2 was therefore screened in both organic solvents and water. Unfortunately, no gelation phenomena were observed. Considering the unfavorable participation of the 19-carboxyl in the coordination, we blocked this group by esterification. Three new esterified isosteviol-15-sulfonic acids 3, 5 and 7, as well as their sodium salts4, 6, and 8, were synthesized through esterification, sulfonation and salification of isosteviol (Scheme S1, ESI†). As expected, we found that compound 4 was an excellent organogelator for halohydrocarbonsolvents. In addition, compound 8 showed poorer gelation ability due to its poor solubility in the halohydrocarbonsolvents, whereas compound 6 could not gel any solvents. Although the potassium salts9 could also gel these halohydrocarbonsolvents, the stability of the gel formed was inferior to that of the sodium salts.

The gelation ability of 4 was then screened for more than twenty common solvents by the inverse tube method via a heating–cooling process. The results obtained showed that 4 was capable of solvent-selectively gelling a number of halohydrocarbonsolvents reversibly (Table 1). In almost all the halohydrocarbonsolvents tested, such as alkyl halides, aryl halides, alkenyl halides and chloropropylene oxide (Table 1, entries 1–14), the gelation took place immediately after cooling the hot solution to sol–gel transition temperature (Tgel) at a minimum gelation concentration (Cgel) as low as 0.1% w/v (Fig. S1, ESI†), which can be classified as super-gelation.1c,13 In the case of trichloroethylene and 1,1,2,2-tetrachloroethane, the Cgel was somewhat higher than that of other solvents. Gel formation did not occur in less polar solvents, such as tetrachloromethane and 1-bromobutane (Table 1, entries 15 and 16), due to the poor solubility of 4 in these solvents even at a higher temperature. In addition, no gelation phenomena were observed in the halogenated solvents with other polar groups, such as ethyl bromoacetate and chlorohydrin (Table 1, entries 17 and 18). The mix-solvents of tetrachloride or 1-bromobutane with other gelable solvents, such as chloroform, could be easily gelated. Interestingly, the self-assembly of 4 could be achieved and form gels at room temperature after addition of the powdered gelator into the halohydrocarbonsolvents in hours. In contrast to many other gelators reported, the gelation of 4 could be completed without a heating–cooling process. Although some reports on solvent-selective gelation have been disclosed,14 selective gelation of halohydrocarbonsolvents at such a low Cgel at room temperature has been rarely reported.

Additionally, gelator4 can selectively gel halohydrocarbonsolvents from their aqueous mixture at room temperature and even efficiently gel a very small amount of organic phase from a large amount of water. An in situ gel formation process could also be expected, because compound 4 could form rapidly by treating 3 with common sodium salts. This was then confirmed by further studies: in situ selective gelation occurred immediately when adding compound 3 to the mixed system of a halohydrocarbonsolvent and an aqueous solution of sodium salt at room temperature (Fig. 4). Alternatively, by mixing the solution of 3 in halohydrocarbonsolvent with brine directly, the organic phase could also be gelated completely, leaving the aqueous layer uninfluenced. For the mixtures of two or more kinds of halohydrocarbonsolvents, the gelator exhibited similar gelation abilities. As far as we know, this is the first report about an in situ Na(i) coordination gelator with both solvent- and phase-selectivity at room temperature.

More significantly, by adding 3 to the mixed solution of the organic solvent and brine containing other common metal ions, such as Cu(ii), Zn(ii), Mg(ii), Co(ii) or Mn(ii) in a large pH range (pH = 2–12), the gel formation process progressed without any observed changes. The results suggested that the gel is not sensitive to other metal ions and pH. Nevertheless, when the gel was contacted with alcohols, acetic acid or DMSO, the fibrous network would breakdown in a few hours (Table S1, ESI†). Further investigation on the stability of the gels showed that most of the gels formed by 4 were very stable and no observable changes occurred after several months in a closed container. In contrast, the gels of trichloroethylene and 1,1,2,2-tetrachloroethane proceeded to decomposition in about 24 hours.

H-bonding has been shown to play an important role in the self-assembly of most LMOGs.2a,2c,15 However, water, metal ions as well as pH would affect the H-bonding formation and lead to the collapse of the fibrous network. Thus, phase-selective gelation of a certain kind of organic solvent accompanied with water is hard to achieve.16 Meanwhile, the gelation process for most LMOGs requires the common heating–cooling process or ultrasound.17 This greatly limits the applications of gels in water purification since the polluted water usually contains metal ions with different pH values. With this point of view, gelator4, with no response to water, metal ions and pH, may have the noteworthy potential in the purification of waste-water containing halohydrocarbonsolvents.

The aggregation mode and microscopic morphology of the organogelator were investigated by scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and atomic force microscopy (AFM) images in different solvents (Fig. 5). The SEM images of xerogels from air-dried chloroform, 1,2-dichloroethane and bromobenzene organogels revealed that 4 could self-assemble into nanoscale fibrous structures with regular fiber diameters of ca. 100–500 nm, which can be further confirmed by AFM images.

Deep insights into the gel structures were derived from the single-crystal structure of gelator4 (ref. 11). The results of the crystallographic analysis revealed that the asymmetric unit of the complex consists of two Na(i) ions, two sulphonate anions, two carbonyl groups from 4 and two kinds of coordinated water. As shown in Fig. 6a, Na1 is six-coordinated that is completed by three oxygen atoms from two sulphonate groups, one oxygen atom (O7) from a water molecule and two oxygen atoms from two 16-carbonyls. Na2 is seven-coordinated defined by four oxygen atoms from three sulphonate groups, two oxygen atoms from two water molecules, and one oxygen atom from 16-carbonyl. It is worth noting that the oxygen of one water molecule (O7) coordinated with both Na1 and Na2 while the other one (O8) coordinated only with Na2. For sulphonate groups, one adopts μ3-η1:η1:η2 mode and the other acts as μ2-η1:η2 mode. Finally, every Na ion was connected alternately by two sulphonate groups into the one-dimensional supramolecular structure (Fig. 6b). Almost perfect van der Waals interactions between the neighboring helices, with distances of the nearest carbons range from 3.639–4.077 Å (Fig. S17, ESI†), afford the 2-D and 3-D structure. Compared with the supramolecular structure of 2 (Fig. 2b), compound 4 self-assembled in different ways to form a 1-D structure with the hydrophilic coordination center convergent (Fig. 6c) and the hydrophobic groups emanative. This may be the dominant reason that only compound 4 can achieve gelation in organic solvents or mixed organo-aqueous two phase systems.

X-ray diffraction analysis showed that the diffraction pattern of the xerogel from chloroform and the powder diffractogram of the crystalline form of gelator4 were virtually the same (Fig. S14, ESI†), and the FT-IR of xerogel and crystal of 4 were the same, too (Fig. S13, ESI†). The results indicated that the molecular packing in the gelator fibers could be related to that in the bulk single crystals.

In order to further investigate the gel formation process, a variable temperature 1H NMR experiment was carried out on the CDCl3 gel of 4 (Fig. 7a). The broad signals at near δH 3.80 ppm that appeared in the gel state became sharper along with decomposition of the gel as the temperature increased (in steps of 10 °C). The chemical shift of the active hydrogen signal of water shifted upfield while increasing the temperature, which is due to the disruption of the coordinated structure. FT-IR spectra (Fig. 7b) further confirmed the role of water since both the IR spectra of the crystal of 4 and its gel in chloroform showed absorption at 3400–3600 cm−1 that can be related only to the O–H bonds of the two kinds of coordinated water since no other OH groups were presented.

Further study was carried out to demonstrate whether the gelation occurred in the absence of water. Therefore, the anhydrous gelator was obtained as a white powder by heating the crystal of 4 at 180 °C for 24 hours under vacuum. Although the anhydrous gelator could be dissolved in the dry halohydrocarbonsolvents under heating conditions, no gelation occurred after cooling down. More importantly, the organic solvent could be completely gelated by adding more than one equiv. of water relative to the anhydrous gelator. So, it is clear that water played a key role in the self-assembly of 4. Thereby, the hierarchical self-organization process of the supramolecular organogel could be reasonably deduced as shown in Fig. 8. The 1-D coordination supramolecular structure constructed by sulphonate anions, Na(i) ions and water assembled the 2-D structure by van der Waals interactions. And then the nanofiber was formed and halohydrocarbonsolvents gel was achieved finally.

In summary, we disclosed here the first sodium coordination super gelator4, an esterified isosteviol-15-sulphonate, with solvent- and phase-selective gelation abilities at room temperature. It was found that the novel amphiphilic gelator can selectively gelate most of the halohydrocarbonsolvents without observable interferences by water, pH value and other metal ions. In situ selective gel formation can also be achieved efficiently by adding the relative sulfonic acid 3 to the two phase system of halohydrocarbonsolvents and brine. Water was proved to be indispensable in the self-assembly of 4 into its fibrous supramolecular architecture. The aggregation mode and microscopic morphology, as well as the possible formation process, of the organogel were also described. Further studies on different isosteviol sulphonates with different ester or amide functional groups at C-19 as well as their potential applications in the purification of waste-water are currently underway.
