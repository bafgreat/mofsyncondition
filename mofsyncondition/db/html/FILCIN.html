Introduction

Considerable attention has currently been paid to the crystal engineering of coordination polymers owing to their fascinating structures and potential applications as functional materials.1 Despite the success in the construction of a variety of coordination polymers with interesting compositions and topologies, the control of polymer dimensionality is still a challenge in the field2 because the resultant framework is frequently influenced by many subtle factors such as medium, temperature, metal-to-ligand ratio, template and counter-ion.3 In a spontaneous assembly process, the structural information stored in both the organic ligand and the metal ion is read out by the coordination through their coordination geometry for the programmed system.4 Therefore, by the judicious choice of the favored coordination geometry of ligand and metal ion, it is possible to elaborate a specific architecture. In this context, the coordination convergence/divergence of ligand plays an important role5 because endo or convergent ligands, usually act as stoppers resulting in discrete or low dimensional species while exo or divergent ligands tend to form extended coordination structures of high dimensionality. A typical case is the bi-pyridine system. Whereas 4,4′-bipy usually produces 2D or 3D polymers,6 2,2′-bipy gives discrete molecular complexes (0D) or low dimensional (1D or 2D) frameworks if other suitable exo/bridging ligands coexist.7 Similar systems include, for example, the three isomers of pyridinecarboxylic acid.8

In this paper, we select three isomers of pyridine carboxylate N-oxide (PCNO): picolinate N-oxide (PNO), nicotinate N-oxide (NNO) and isonicotinate N-oxide (INO), bearing both carboxylate and N-oxide binding units, as the organic component interacting with the Ln–SO42− skeleton to construct lanthanide coordination polymers. Our aim is to understand how the dimensionality of the resulting lanthanide coordination frameworks can be manipulated by engineering the degree in the coordination convergence of the ligands. This approach is based on the following achievements and observations. First, designing coordination frameworks has focused mainly on the use of transition metal ions rather than lanthanide ions due to the inherently flexible coordination geometry of lanthanide ions.9 However, this flexibility of lanthanide ions promises rich and versatile coordination chemistry, and hence studies in this respect are increasing rapidly.10 Secondly, lanthanide polymeric complexes have been successfully obtained for benzene multicarboxylates11 or N-oxide12 ligand systems. Thus combining both carboxylate and N-oxide binding units should produce new lanthanide polymeric systems, and this possibility has been proved by our recent investigation on a new family of 3-D lanthanide sulfates of formula [Ln(INO)(H2O)(SO4)]n.13 Thirdly, recent studies by Rao and others revealed that the versatile coordination behavior of sulfate ion in conjunction with the flexibility coordination of lanthanide ions leads to a diversity of frameworks, and especially the sulfate itself is a good inorganic connecting tecton or exoligands.14 Finally, the three isomeric ligands we used in this work, PNO, NNO and INO, show a systematic variation in their coordination convergence/divergence (Scheme 1) because of the different disposition of the two binding sites, i.e., carboxylate and N-oxide groups. Here we report three new Gd coordination polymers [Gd(PNO)(H2O)2(SO4)]n (1), [Gd(NNO)(H2O)2(SO4)]n (2), and [Gd(INO)(H2O)(SO4)]n (3), which exhibit a systematic variation in the dimensionality of coordination structures, from 1D (1) via 2D (2) to 3D (3), demonstrating the influences of the coordination convergence/divergence of PCNOs upon the dimensionality of structures. The magnetic properties of compounds 1, 2 and 3 are also studied, as Gd(iii) is one of the most magnetically interesting ions in lanthanides.

Experimental

Materials and apparatus

GdCl3·6H2O were prepared by dissolving gadolinium oxide in dilute hydrochloric acid and drying it. Other chemicals were commercially available and used without further purification. Elemental analyses (C, H, N) were performed on an Elementar Vario EL analyzer. IR spectra were recorded on a Nicolet Magna-IR 750 spectrometer equipped with a Nic-Plan microscope. Magnetic measurements of compound 1, 2 and 3 were carried out on a QUANTUM DESIGN MPMS-5XL SQUID system. Diamagnetic corrections were estimated using Pascal constants15 (−153 × 10−6 cm3mol−1 for 1 and 2, −140 × 10−6 cm3mol−1 for 3), and background correction by experimental measurement on sample holders.

Preparation

All the compounds were prepared by a hydrothermal reaction. Typically for 1: the mixture of GdCl3·6H2O (0.30 mmol), MnCO3 (0.10 mmol), MnSO4·H2O (0.10 mmol), HPNO (0.30 mmol), H2O (6 mL) and ethanol (6 mL) were sealed in a Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel (25 mL), heated at 155 °C for 3 d under ambient pressure, and then cooled to room temperature. Colorless block crystals of 1 were harvested with an yield of 14% based on Gd. Similar procedures employing HNNO and HINO produced colorless block crystals of 2 and 3 in yields of 29 and 24%, respectively. Anal. for 1, Calcd. for C6H8GdNO9S: C, 16.86; H, 1.89; N, 3.28; Found: C, 16.82; H, 2.00; N, 3.24. Anal. for 2, Calcd. for C6H8GdNO9S: C, 16.86; H, 1.89; N, 3.28; Found: C, 16.58; H, 2.01; N, 3.22. Anal. for 3, Calcd. for C6H6GdNO8S: C, 17.60; H, 1.48; N, 3.42; Found C, 17.51; H, 1.28; N, 3.51%.

X-Ray crystallography

Diffraction intensity data for the single crystals of 1, 2 and 3 were collected at room temperature on a Nonius Kappa CCD diffractometer equipped with graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å).16 Empirical absorption corrections were applied using the Sortav program.17 All structures were solved by the direct method and refined by the full-matrix least-squares method on F2 with anisotropic thermal parameters for all non-hydrogen atoms.18 Hydrogen atoms of the coordinated water molecule for compound 1 were not included, and those for 2 and 3 were introduced by different Fourier maps and refined isotropically with constrains for the ideal geometry of water molecule with O–H distance of 0.96 Å and H–O–H angle of 105°. Other hydrogen atoms were placed by calculation positions. Detailed crystallographic data and structure refinement parameters are summarized in Table 1. Powder XRD patterns for 1–3 were obtained on a Rigaku D/max 2000 diffractometer at room temperature with Cu Kα radiation in a flat plate geometry.

Result and discussion

Synthesis

Hydrothermal reaction of Gd(iii), MnCO3, MnSO4·H2O and HPCNO in 3 : 1 :1 : 3 molar ratio in a mixture of water and ethanol (v/v, 1 : 1) produce three polymeric compounds 1–3 with the ratio of Gd : PCNO : SO4 = 1 : 1 : 1. We have observed in our previous study13 that the starting materials MnCO3 plays a role to deprotonate HPCNO ligand and MnSO4·H2O provides sulfate ion. The absence of manganese ion in the products reveals that there is a greater affinity for lanthanides to coordinate to the PCNO and sulfate ligands than the transition metals. Replacing the manganese sulfate by sodium sulfate or varying the metal salts to ligand stoichiometry, for example, the molar ratio of the starting materials in 3 : 1 : 1 : 6, yielded the same compounds. As in many hydrothermal reactions, the yields were not high, however, the powder X-ray diffraction patterns of the three products are in good agreement with the simulated ones based on the single-crystal structures (Fig. S1),† suggesting the purity of the products. The solid products contain no manganese ion, therefore we can safely presume that the manganese ion is still in the solution.

Structures

The ligands chosen for this investigation are three isomers of PCNO possessing carboxylate and N-oxide binding groups. As mentioned before, the three isomers have different carboxylate substitution positions on the 6-membered ring of pyridine N-oxide, leading to their different degree of coordination convergence, from convergent to divergent (Scheme 1). PNO, containing carboxylate group in the ortho- position of the pyridine N-oxide backbone, tends to bind metal ions in a convergent way, i.e., the chelate mode, thus facilitating the formation of low-dimensional framework. The second one, NNO, comprises a carboxylate group in the meta-position. Compared with PNO, the ligand shows more divergent coordination geometry, thus it has the potential for constructing a coordination polymer of higher dimensionality. In the last one, INO, the carboxylate group is located in the para-position, resulting in the linear and divergent coordination geometry. Therefore it can be employed as a good exo-dentate bridge to connect metal ions and produce network with high dimensionality. Indeed, due to the different degree in coordination convergence of the three ligands, systematic variation in dimensionality of the structures of the coordination polymers, 1 of 1D, 2 of 2D, and 3 of 3D can be obtained, indicating ligand-directed synthesis.19

[Gd(PNO)(H2O)2(SO4)]n (1)

From the viewpoint of coordination chemistry, 1 is a 1D chain-like polymer (Fig. 1). In the structure, each Gd(iii) ion is in a triply capped trigonal prismatic geometry, chelated by one PNO and two SO42−ligands, and further coordinated by one SO42− anion and two water molecules, and the three capping atoms are O5, O4A and O6B (Fig. 1a). The Gd–O distances range from 2.278 to 2.642 Å (average value 2.458 Å, Table 2), and the O–Gd–O angles from 53.4 to 148.8°.

The inorganic skeleton of the structure of 1 is Gd–SO4–H2O chain running along the a-axis (Fig. 1b). Within the chain, two Gd(iii) ions are bridged by two μ2-O bridges from two SO42− ions to form a [Gd2O2] rhombic unit. Each SO42− anion further chelates two Gd ions of adjacent [Gd2O2] units through its two pairs of oxygen atoms, linking the rhombic units into a ladder-like chain, with the [Gd2O2] units as the rungs and Gd–O–S–O–Gd linkages as the side-rails. The Gd⋯Gd separations are 4.271 Å in the [Gd2O2] unit and 6.351 Å between the units, respectively. Therefore, the SO42− anion adopts an η4,μ3-pentadentate coordination mode (Scheme 2a) that binds to three Gd ions. Due to the convergent coordination geometry, the PNO ligands, each of which chelates one Gd(iii) ion via its N-oxide oxygen atom and one of the carboxylate oxygen atoms (Scheme 1a), reside on both sides of the ladder chain, with the other carboxylate oxygen atom free of coordination. Therefore, the PNO ligand, as expected, acting as an endo-ligand, limits the further coordination of the chain, leading to a coordination structure of 1D, i.e., a structure with low dimensionality.

The chains are arranged along the b-direction interlinked through O–H⋯O hydrogen bonds between the coordinated water molecules and the sulfate oxygen atoms as well as the uncoordinated carboxylate oxygen atoms of PNO, thus producing a 2D hydrogen bonded sheet. These hydrogen bonds have O⋯O distances of 2.733 to 2.786 Å (Table S1).† The sheet is parallel to the ab-plane with pyridyl rings of PNO ligands projecting above and below it (Fig. S2a).† Furthermore, the adjacent sheets interdigitate to each other, giving rise to a 3D framework (Fig. S2b). There are two C–H⋯O type hydrogen bonds that reinforce the 3D structure,20 both involving C–H groups of PNO ligands of one layer and the sulfate oxygen atoms of the neighboring layers (Table S1). Therefore, without extending the coordination framework, the PNO ligand is involved in the hydrogen bonds that build 1D chains up to the 3D crystal structure.

[Gd(NNO)(H2O)2(SO4)]n (2)

The use of a more divergent ligand of NNO instead of PNO (Scheme 1) produced a 2D wavelike coordination sheet in the structure of 2 (Fig. 2). The Gd(iii) ion in 2 exhibits a square anti-prism coordination environment (Fig. 2a) formed by two N-oxide groups from two NNO, two carboxylate oxygen atoms from the other two NNO, two oxygen atoms from two SO42− anions and two coordinated water molecules. The Gd–O distances range from 2.329 to 2.518 Å (average 2.415 Å, Table 2), and the O–Gd–O angles from 61.9 to 149.3°. The inorganic moiety in 2 is a discrete dimer (Fig. 2b), in which two Gd ions are bridged by a pair of SO42− ions in a bidentate bridging mode (Scheme 2b). The neighboring dimers are connected by two NNO ligands with their N-oxide groups as μ2-bridges binding two Gd ions to give an infinite chain running along the b-direction. It is worth noting that the dimer itself has two additional Gd–O–C–O–Gd connectivities by other two NNO ligands with two syn–syn bridging carboxylate groups. The Gd⋯Gd separations are 4.323 Å within the dimer and 4.297 Å between the dimers. The chains, running along b-direction, are further linked along a-direction through the coordination connections of NNO ligands and Gd ions between the chains: the ligands with their carboxylate groups acting as bidentate syn–syn bridges in one chain are coordinated to pairs of Gd ions in the adjacent chains via their N-oxide groups as monodentate μ2 bridges, and vise versa (Fig. 2b). Therefore, each NNO connects four Gd ions, two in one chain through its bidentate N-oxide, and two in the other chain through its syn–syn bridging carboxylate group (Scheme 1b), creating a 2D coordination sheet.

Many hydrogen bonds are observed in the system, not only strengthening the coordination sheet but also helping the formation of 3D crystal structure. Within the sheet, there are (a) intra-chain O–H⋯O hydrogen bonding of the two coordinated water molecules with sulfate oxygen atoms (O8⋯O6A and O9⋯O7A distances are 2.902 and 2.774 Å, respectively, symmetry code: x, y − 1, z); (b) intra-chain C–H⋯O hydrogen bonding between the C–H groups of NNO ligands and sulfate oxygen atoms within the chains (C3⋯O5A and C3⋯O6A distances are 3.418 and 3.253 Å, respectively, symmetry code: x, y − 1, z); and (c) inter-chain O–H⋯O hydrogen bonding of one coordinated water molecule with sulfate oxygen atom (O9⋯O7B distance is 2.738 Å, symmetry code: −x + 3/2, y − 1/2, z). The 3D structure is generated by the connections of the layers via O–H⋯O (O8⋯O6C distance is 2.715 Å, symmetry code: −x + 1, y − 1/2, −z + 3/2) and C–H⋯O (C4⋯O4C distance is 3.030 Å, symmetry code: −x + 1, y − 1/2, −z + 3/2) hydrogen bonds between the donors from one sheet, the coordinated water and C–H groups of NNO ligands, and acceptors of the sulfate oxygen atoms from the neighboring sheets (Fig. S3).†

[Gd(INO)(H2O)(SO4)]n (3)

The employment of the most divergent ligand, INO (Scheme 1), resulted in a 3D coordination framework of 3, which belongs to the type II structure for middle lanthanides, reported in our previous work.13 The framework can be described as a two-fold interpenetrated “3D herringbone” framework. The coordination polyhedron around each Gd(iii) ion is a triply capped trigonal prism with three sites occupied by the oxygen atom of the N-oxide group of one INO and the chelating carboxylate group of another INO, five sites by oxygen atoms from three SO42− anions, and one site by a water (Fig. 3a). The three capping atoms are O2, O4 and O7C. The Gd–O distances range from 2.254 to 2.831 Å (average value 2.471 Å, Table 2), and the O–Gd–O angles from 52.5 to 152.7°. The inorganic moiety of 3, as depicted in Fig. 3b, is 1D ladder-like chain similar to that in 1, composed of the [Gd2O2] rhombic units. Within the chain, the Gd⋯Gd separations are 4.243 Å in the [Gd2O2] unit and 6.381 Å between the units, respectively. However, the chain of 3 is quite different from that of 1 when considering the exo- coordination property of INO in 3 in contrast to the endo- one of PNO in 1. In the chain of 1, the chelating PNO ligands inhibit the further coordination extension of the chain, whereas in the chain of 3, the ditopic INO ligand coordinates Gd ion with one of its two binding sites, the monodentate N-oxide group or the chelating carboxyl group (Scheme 1c), and keeps the other site to connect with other inorganic chains, thus increases the structure dimensionality. Each [Gd2O2] unit has four INO ligands that radiate from it in different directions, and the ligands of a chain link four identical and parallel ones to generate a 3D structure (Fig. 4a). Considering each metal ion as a node and the connection via sulfate ions in the c-direction, the global network is of “3D herringbone” topology, built from 2D herringbone layers with sulfate ions as interlayer pillars.

The 3D network is open. It has large rectangular channels of cross dimension of 14.4 × 10.5 Å, based on Gd⋯Gd separations, along the c-direction, and on the sidewall of the channel there are rectangular inter-channel windows of size of size ca. 10.5 × 6.3 Å, thus interpenetration is allowed. The total structure is two-fold interpenetrated (Fig. 4b). The channels of one framework are threaded by the inorganic chains of the other one, and the inter-channel windows by the INO ligands. In addition, extensive π–π interactions and strong O–H⋯O hydrogen bonds further stabilize the structure. The pyridyl rings of the two individual frameworks are stacked alternatively in a face-to-face and partially overlapping fashion21 along the c-direction, resulting in the π–π stacking interactions with interplanar distances of 3.15 and 3.18 Å. The O–H⋯O hydrogen bonds originate from the coordinated water of one framework with the bridging SO42− anions of the other one. The O⋯O distance and the O–H⋯O angle are 2.769 Å and 170°, respectively.

The three structures in this work clearly demonstrate the role of the coordination convergence/divergence of the ligands in controlling the dimensionality of coordination polymers. The three ligands have the same coordination sites, i.e., carboxylate and N-oxide groups, but they possess different orientations, i.e., different coordination convergence/divergence. PNO, the most convergent ligand among the three isomers, is not favorable for constructing high-dimensional frameworks. To the best of our knowledge, 3D frameworks with PNO as linkers are scarce, while 1D chains and 2D layers have been encountered when other exo- ligands coexist.22 In 1, PNO ligand acts as a bidentate ligand and forms a six-membered chelating ring using the N-oxide group and one carboxylate oxygen atom with Gd ion. Such kind of coordination mode is common in most metal–PNO complexes,23 capable of protecting metal sites from expansion of the polymeric frameworks, thus leading to the low-dimensional coordination polymers,24 as the cases of well-known chelating ligands, 2,2′-bipy, 1,10-phen, and picolinic acid.7,8 In case of 1 where PNO is used, although the SO42− anion acts as exo- ligands facilitating the formation of 1D Gd–SO42− ladder chain, the chain cannot further extend due to the coordination convergence of PNO, resulting in the low dimensionality of the final structure. For NNO, the meta- disposition of the two binding sites exhibits a more divergent coordination mode, which is favorable for bridging metal centers thus building frameworks of higher dimensionality. In most lanthanide complexes of nicotinate and nicotinate N-oxide, the carboxylate group is apt to bridge metal ions in bidentate syn–syn fashion to form a dinuclear unit.25 And these units are further cross-linked through the coordination of the N-oxide group.26 In 2, the carboxylate group also adopts the syn–syn bridging mode, reinforcing the formation of Gd–SO4 dimer, and the N-oxide group adopts μ2 bridging mode extending the dimer into a 2D layer. The comparison of the structures of 3 and 1 is interesting because both consist of similar 1D Gd–SO42− ladder chains as the inorganic skeleton, but the dimensionalities of the coordination frameworks are different: 1D of 1vs. 3D of 3. In 3, the two binding groups of INO have a linear and divergent distribution, which makes it a good spacer like 4,4′-bipy type ligands and isonicotinic acid.6,8 The INO ligand acts as bridge with chelating carboxylate group and monodentate N-oxide group binding two Gd ions of neighboring 1D Gd–SO42− ladder chains, connecting the chains into a 3D coordination network. Obviously, it is the three isomeric PCNOs that play the vital role in the formation of final architectures with systematic dimensionality variation of coordination structure, from 1D, 2D to 3D, by means of specific location and orientation of two coordination groups.

IR spectra and magnetic properties

The IR spectra of the three compounds contain characteristic IR bands (Table S2)† for deprotonated carboxyl and sulfate groups, these bands being in accordance with those previously reported.26,27 The Δν values, the separations between the asymmetric and symmetric carboxyl stretch frequencies, of these compounds, differ significantly from one another. For 1, the value of Δν is larger than 255 cm−1 reported for sodium picolinate N-oxide,28a indicating that the carboxylate group in 1 is monodentate.28b The Δν value for 2 is greater than that for 3, which provides evidence that the carboxylate group coordinates to the gadolinium ions in bidentate bridging and chelating modes in 2 and 3, respectively.28b Therefore, it is clear that the frequencies of the symmetric and asymmetric modes of the carboxylate group in the present compounds depend greatly on the coordination mode and thus the local geometry of the OCO unit. Compared with the N–O stretches at 1255 and 847 cm−1 for free pyridine N-oxide,29 the bands assigned to N–O vibrations of these compounds are shifted to higher or lower frequencies in different ways, consistent with the different coordination modes exhibited by the N-oxide group of different ligands. Several vibration bands of sulfate ions (ν1 and ν3) are observed in the range 940–1150 cm−1 for these compounds. Compound 2 possesses different vibration bands of sulfate ion in comparison to compounds 1 and 3, since the coordination mode of the sulfate ion in 2 is different from that in 1 and 3.28b

The temperature dependence of the magnetic susceptibility for the three compounds was measured in the temperature range 2–300 K, with applied field of 1 kOe. The plots of χMTversusT are shown in Fig. 5, where χM is the molar magnetic susceptibility per [Gd2] binuclear unit. The observed χMT at 300 K is 15.90, 15.85 and 15.73 cm3 mol−1 K for 1, 2 and 3, respectively, close to the theoretical value of 15.76 cm3 mol−1 K for two isolated Gd3+ ions (8S7/2).30 As the temperature is lowered, χMT values of 1–3 remain almost constant to ca. 20 K. Due to the similar ladder-chain structure, 1 and 3 exhibit similar magnetic behavior upon further cooling: for 1, χMT increases and reaches a maximum of 15.95 cm3 mol−1 K at ca. 5 K; for 3, the χMT increases rapidly to 17.07 cm3 mol−1 K at 2 K. Such behavior may be referred to the presence of a small ferromagnetic Gd⋯Gd coupling. The decrease of χMT for 1 as the temperature is further lowered to 2 K may be due to secondary effects such as inter-dimer or inter-chain antiferromagnetic interactions. For 2, χMT decreases rapidly as T is lowered further, this being indicative of antiferromagnetic Gd⋯Gd interaction.31

Conclusion

In summary, we have successfully prepared three Gd coordination polymers exhibiting a systematic dimensionality variation from 1D chain via 2D layer to 3D framework by the utilization of the three isomeric ligands, PNO, NNO, and INO, which possess different degree of coordination convergence, together with the versatile coordination ability of sulfate tecton. The results show that the choice of ligand structure, accomplished by adjustment of the location and spatial extent of the binding groups, would let us control the dimensionality of outcome frameworks. Further research on the PCNO ligand system may focus on: (a) the replacement of gadolinium with other lanthanide metals; (b) preparation and engineering of new organic–inorganic hybrid materials of different dimensionality by incorporating other inorganic tectons; and (c) exploration of the properties of these materials.
